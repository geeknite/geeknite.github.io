## What is Saboteur, and why should you care?

Saboteur is a set of cards that creates a short, tense, wonderfully chaotic mining expedition. Each player takes on a role: either a loyal miner or a saboteur. The miners aim to play path cards to connect tunnel segments and finally reach a gold card, while the saboteur tries to deliberately create dead ends or mislead teammates. The tension is deliciously high because you often realize you are on the wrong team only after the card has been played. It is a social deduction game that runs on simple rules but delivers elaborate misdirection and laughter.

The base idea is approachable for new players but cunning enough for seasoned table-scramblers. You play path cards to connect tunnels; tools and action cards add spice, like blasting a tunnel with a cave-in or stopping a saboteur in their tracks with a well-timed lamp or hammer. The pirate-level thrill comes from watching a trail of cards cascade into misdirection as someone you trusted starts to question their own loyalties. In short: Saboteur is the party game that feels like a heist movie without questions about why you forgot to bring a flashlight.

## How to play in under five minutes: a quick-start guide
- Shuffle role cards and deal one to each player face down. Do not reveal your role to others yet.
- Place the starting tunnel card in the center, then lay out a few path cards in a line that players will attempt to expand toward a goal.
- On your turn, draw a card, then either place a path card to extend the tunnel or play an action or tool card to influence the tunnel's direction or block an opponent temporarily.
- If a miner reaches the gold card, that round ends with miners scoring the gold; if a saboteur trips up the route, saboteurs win that round, and points are allocated accordingly.
- The first team to reach a predetermined score wins after a few rounds.

That’s it folks. The rules are simple, and the fun grows as the table dynamics become more and more booby-trapped with every misstep. As with many light strategy games, the charm is less in conquering a perfect plan and more in the chaos of watching a plan unravel in the most entertaining way possible.

## Teaching Saboteur: tips for new players
- Begin with a full explanation of roles without naming who has what. The moment someone reveals their role defeats a portion of the mystery and can make a round feel less exciting.
- Emphasize the non-verbal aspects of the game: the way players lean in, the timing of their card plays, and the quick glances that betray suspicious loyalties. A good group will be reading these cues as much as reading the cards.
- Start with a shorter session or a best-of-three tournament. It’s easier to manage the ebb and flow when you’re running a small sample rather than a marathon that risks fatigue and temper during the late rounds.
- Use house rules sparingly. It’s tempting to add extra roles or alternate win conditions, but Saboteur thrives on a balance between deception and cooperation that can quickly skew with too many variables.
