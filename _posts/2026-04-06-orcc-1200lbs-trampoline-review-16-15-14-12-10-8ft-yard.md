## Setup, maintenance, and long-term care
### Unboxing and assembly reality check
Even the most seasoned weekend warriors will admit that assembling a trampoline is a bit like solving a very loud puzzle. You’ll need a clean workspace, a helper or two, and a sense of humor. Read the instruction manual and then read it again, because you will probably realize you misinterpreted step three as you reach step six. Tools required typically include a set of sockets, a rubber mallet, and a whole lot of patience. The good news is that the frame is designed with bolt-in connections, which makes disassembly easier if you ever need to move houses, go on an epic road trip, or persuade a friend to store it for the winter.

### Safety checks you should do before every jump session
- Inspect the mat for signs of wear, holes, or suspicious suspiciousness. If you see a tear that could swallow your finger, replace it.
- Check the springs for rust or fatigue; if a spring looks questionable, replace the entire leg or consult the manual for the recommended maintenance procedure.
- Make sure the enclosure net is secure and the poles are firmly anchored to the ground with the provided anchors.
- Surface check: ensure the trampoline sits on level, non-slippery ground away from trees, fences, and anything you don’t want to bounce into at high velocity.
- Clear the area of objects that could become projectiles if someone underestimates their jump height.

### Cleaning and upkeep tips
A quick wipe-down with a mild soap solution and rinse is usually enough for the mat and padding, but avoid harsh chemicals that could degrade the materials. After heavy rain, let the frame dry fully before jumping to prevent rust or ice-skating style slips. Use a pool noodle between the net and the tube to prevent snagging if you want to avoid the “oops, my sleeve is in the net” moment.
