## Art and Theme: Parrillo’s Signature Touches
Lucio Parrillo is known for high-contrast, dynamic compositions, and a sense of movement that makes still miniatures feel like they’re mid-plot twist. In the Zombicide context, that translates to zombie factions with more personality and survivors who look like they walked right off a fantasy painting and into a post-apocalyptic alley.

You’ll notice two things as you flip through the art on various cards and the occasional character sheet:

- The palette leans on saturated reds, electric blues, and bone-white highlights. The result is a dramatic, cinematic look that makes the undead feel like they could star in a heavy metal poster—minus the stage dive.
- The sculpts themselves carry Parrillo’s signature anatomy exaggerations: broad shoulders, expressive faces, and that uncanny art-collection vibe that says, “this is art you can kill zombies with.” It’s not just tabletop armor; it’s a narrative invitation.

If you’re a fan of Parrillo’s existing work, you’ll spot the visual language instantly. If you’re not, you’ll still appreciate the way the art elevates the game’s mood from “shambling board game” to “epic fantasy vignette with a twist.” The thematic marriage is deliberate: the zombie threat is real, yes, but so is the sense of epic storytelling that Parrillo-style art invites you to pretend you’re a legendary hero, even while you roll a handful of dice and try not to knock over the snack table with your elbow.
