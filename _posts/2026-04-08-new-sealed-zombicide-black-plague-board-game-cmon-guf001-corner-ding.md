## Corner ding reality check: is it a big deal?

A corner ding is cosmetic by default, meaning it rarely affects gameplay. However, it can affect value, especially if you’re a collector who wants a flawless specimen for resale or museum-worthy display. In the sealed market, condition is a major selling point: mint-in-box (MIB) versus near-mint in box (NMIB) versus good-condition-with-a-story. Corner dings can lower price slightly, but they can also earn you a smile from the seller who’s faced with a price slashing that still lands you a pristine game on your table.

From a practical perspective, the interior components should be unaffected. The box literature might mention “corner ding: cosmetic only,” and that’s usually true. If you’re worried about moisture, humidity, or extreme temperature, the sealed status is a boon; you’ll still be protected by shrink wrap and the cardboard’s natural resilience. The real risk lies in long-term display: the still-sealed box could accumulate dust and minor scuffs if you’re constantly dusting your game shelf. But dust is the universe’s way of saying, “Let’s live a little.”

## Condition, grading, and how to shop smart

If you’re chasing a sealed copy with a corner ding, you’re likely dealing with a few grading considerations:

- Seal integrity: Ensure the plastic wrap remains intact and that no obvious resealing has occurred. If the wrap is broken, you’re not sealed anymore, regardless of the corner. This matters for resale value and for the thrill of a fresh unboxing.
- Box integrity: The corner ding should be documented in the listing, ideally with a photo. If the listing guarantees mint box but shows a ding in the photos, that’s a red flag or a challenge for your inner minimalist collector.
- Contents: In most cases, if the seal is intact, the contents are pristine. But always check the product page notes for any “box damage” disclaimers from the seller, especially if you’re buying from a marketplace rather than a direct retailer.
- Price delta: Expect the ding to bring a modest discount. The depth of the discount depends on market demand and the rarity of a pristine box. Knowledge of the current market is your friend; you don’t want to overpay for a cosmetic flaw, nor miss out on a great deal because you insisted on perfection.

For practical shopping tips, consider cross-referencing listings with other sealed copies of Black Plague. See how the corner ding is described in different marketplaces and whether the discount lines up with the risk you’re willing to bear. The best approach is to treat it as a premium-nerd negotiation: you want the best possible components inside, with the outside having more character than a musty old tavern.

## Maintenance, care, and storage tips

If you’re investing in a sealed copy with the intention of eventual unboxing or display, you’ll want to protect the box from environmental factors that exaggerate the ding or create new ones. Here are quick tips:

- Store upright on a stable shelf with other boxed games to prevent accidental pressure on the corner where the ding exists.
- Avoid humidity and direct sunlight, which can cause cardboard to warp and colors to fade. Zombicide art is too cool to let it disappear behind a yellow sunbeam.
- If you sleeve the box for display, choose a slipcase that accommodates the height and width without applying pressure to the corners.
- When you eventually open the box, do it with ceremony. The ritual of unboxing is half the fun—align your dice towers, line up your survivors, and let the chaos begin.
