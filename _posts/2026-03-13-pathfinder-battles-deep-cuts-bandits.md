## A closer look at the sculpts: detail, dynamic poses, and practicality

If you squint at the Bandits and tilt your head just so, you’ll notice two things: the detail is crisp, and the poses are pragmatic. The cloaks swirl with enough fold lines to give the painter something to grab onto, but not so much that you’re drowning in texture. The weapons are well proportioned to the figures, avoiding the common problem of oversized swords looking comically thick in a fantasy setting.

In terms of the actual details, you’ll notice little touches that show the care here: worn leather bands on belts, weathered boots with creases that look like they’ve seen a few campaigns, hints of chainmail that won’t require you to repaint the entire chain. The bandit captain figure tends to have a slightly more ornate belt buckle and perhaps a peaked hood or scarfish piece that distinguishes the leader from the rest. It’s enough to give cadres of five or six Bandits a sense of hierarchy on the table without needing a full miniature command squad.

### The painting challenge and opportunity

Bandits in the Deep Cuts line are a nice starting point for new painters because their color palettes are forgiving. You can lean into the classic earthy tones: browns, grays, and muted greens. Leather and cloth textures are the main event; metal is usually minimal, consisting of buckles and perhaps a dagger or a belt clasp. If you’re feeling bold, a bandit captain offers a chance to practice metallic highlights and a brighter accent color to set the leader apart.

Here are some quick tips to get the most out of the Bandits:

- Primer and base coat: A gray or white primer will set the stage. A matte black primer can give you a dramatic contrast if you’re aiming for a noir mood.
- Leather tones: Start with a base of leather brown, then apply washes to bring out depth. A dry brush of bone or tan on raised leather edges adds realism.
- Cloth textures: Use muted greens or blues; highlight edges with a lighter shade for a sense of weathered fabric.
- Metalwork: A quick steel highlight with a blue-gray wash can give the buckles and daggers that look without turning the table into a mirror showroom.
- Base painting: Don’t forget the base. A little texture paint, dry brush, and a dusting of static grass can transform a dull model into a live bandit that your players just love to hate.

Here’s the honest truth: unpainted miniatures are not for people who want instant gratification. They are for folks who want to dip their toes into miniature painting with a manageable, satisfying project. ### Painting plan and a sample colorway

Draft a simple color palette to start. Something like:
- Leather: Caliban Brown base, then Leather Brown shade, highlight Bone or Khaki.
- Clothes: Olive Drab or Moss Green base, wash with a brown ink, dry brush with lighter greens.
- Metal: Gunmetal with a blue-gray wash, edge highlight with silver.
- Skin: Tanned hide base, light flesh wash, subtle pink on cheeks if you’re feeling cheeky.

Now you can create a quick, cohesive squad that looks like they belong to the same gang rather than a random collection of shapes. If you’re feeling ambitious, you can plan a small conversion by adding a cape from another bandit sculpt and give your captain a unique belt clasp.
