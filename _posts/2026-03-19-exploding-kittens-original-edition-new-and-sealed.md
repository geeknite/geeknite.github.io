## What is Exploding Kittens Original Edition?

Exploding Kittens is a party-style card game designed for 2-5 players (though ambitious groups have pushed this with a bit of homebrew). It’s the brainchild of Elan Lee, Shane Small, and the Internet’s own The Oatmeal (Matthew Inman), who fused a chaotic idea with a product that feels like a high-stakes game night wrapped in a meme burrito. The core conceit is gloriously simple: players draw cards until someone draws an Exploding Kitten and is eliminated from the round unless they defuse it with a Defuse card. Defuses—the rare, desirable resource—are the moral core: do you risk a late-game gambit or save your last Defuse for a moment you’ll regret if you don’t?

The Original Edition features The Oatmeal’s iconic art and humor—bright colors, goofy captions, and cat visuals that land like tiny good-luck cat flashes in your brain. The rules are approachable for a casual party, yet there’s enough probability, bluffing, and zany chaos to keep a gamer mildly wickedly entertained. You don’t need a rulebook to play, but you’ll consult it anyway as you learn the delicate art of balancing risk, reward, and the urge to veto a sneaky, cat-themed bluff.

## A peek at the mechanics (without spoilers, we promise)

If you want a quick mental model: you’re assembling a hand of cards that can alter the fate of the draw pile. Your core objective is to avoid drawing an Exploding Kitten (the titular hazard) while using Defuse cards strategically to survive. The other action cards let you peek at future draws, force other players to draw, shuffle the deck, or pass the risk along to someone else. The pacing is built for party-style rounds: short, punchy, and full of those “Did that just happen?” moments as someone narrowly dodges a catastrophe by the skin of their teeth.

A quick taxonomy of core card types (in broad strokes):
- Defuse cards: lifesavers after an Exploding Kitten appears, often requiring you to plan ahead or bluff your way to survival.
- Exploding Kitten cards: the hazard you’re trying to dodge, unless you’re armed with a Defuse.
- Attack cards: shift the burden, forcing the next player to draw multiple cards.
- See the Future cards: peek at upcoming draws, useful for small strategic nudges.
- Shuffle/Skip cards: control the deck’s flow and carve out chances to breathe.
- The “funny” cards: rewards for banter, taunts, and perfectly-timed sarcasm that somehow tilts probability in your favor.

The pacing of a typical round is a dance: you draw, you react, you bluff, you pass risk along, and you watch chaos blossom as the top card lines up with a cute little hazard label. It’s chaotic in the best possible sense: you’re not composing a foolproof strategy so much as you’re guessing what your friends will do next and whether you’re comfortable watching the chaos unfold in real time.

## How to approach a sealed copy: practical tips

- Inspect the shrink wrap for rips or reseals. A torn wrap isn’t a deal-breaker, but it’s useful to know if mint condition is important to you.
- Check the box for moisture damage or warping. Card stock hates humidity as much as a bluff hates a strong counter-bluff. A warped box can hint at poor storage rather than nefarious tampering.
- Ensure the kit includes a Defuse card among the essential cards. You don’t need to memorize exact counts, but a quick card-type glance helps confirm you’re not accidentally purchasing a counterfeit deck (which, let’s be honest, you could still use for memes if it exists).
- If buying from a reseller, request clear photos of the top and bottom of the deck. Some sellers have a “mystery box” shipping vibe, and you want to know what you’re actually buying before it lands on your doorstep.
- Price expectations: sealed Original Editions can carry a premium, but it largely depends on market conditions, demand, and how many times your friends claim they need a “new” copy for game night and then never get around to opening it.

Buying sealed can be a joy if you’re building a retro shelf or nostalgia library. If you’re more about playing now, a sealed copy can be opened and enjoyed immediately by your gaming group. The ritual of cracking that outer shrink wrap, whispering jokes about cats with grand ambitions, and diving into a session that feels like a party with a dash of high-stakes luck is part of the charm.

## Gameplay tips and strategy (for the but-we-know-you-need-it readers)

- Play with a group that appreciates humor and doesn’t mind chaotic decisions. Exploding Kittens isn’t a deep strategic wargame; it’s a quick, social card game. Energy, timing, and a willingness to bluff are as valuable as any tactical decision.
- Protect your Defuse cards: you’ll use them to survive a bomb, but save some to counter others’ attempts to push risk your way. The moment you reveal a Defuse might be a turning point in the round, or it could be a brilliant bluff that makes someone else panic.
- See the Future is your friend, but don’t rely on it too heavily. It’s a glimpse into the near future, not a guarantee. Use it to string together a plan, then be prepared to adapt when the deck betrays you (it will).
- When you’re forced to Draw, you can use Attack cards to pass the duty along. Classic “pass the bomb,” but with comedic timing and a smile. It’s not just about surviving the next draw; it’s about exhausting your opponents’ confidence.
- Bluffs and misdirection are valuable: pretend you have a strong hand, causing others to misread the deck or misjudge your next blunder. The joy is in the theater of the moment—the small gesture, the sly eyebrow raise, the hand wave that signals chaos is coming.
