## What is Exploding Kittens Original Edition?

Exploding Kittens is a party-style card game designed for 2-5 players (though people have experimented with larger groups using house rules). It’s the brainchild of Elan Lee, Shane Small, and the Internet’s own The Oatmeal (Matthew Inman), who turned a chaotic idea into a product that feels like a high-stakes game night wrapped in a meme burrito. The core conceit is simple but gleefully chaotic: players take turns drawing cards until someone draws an Exploding Kitten, which eliminates them from the round unless they defuse it with a Defuse card. The Defuse cards—one of the few things you’ll hope you have when you’re tempted to peek at the top of the deck—are the moral center of the game: do you risk a late-stage gambit or save your Defuse for a moment you’ll regret if you don’t?

The Original Edition features the classic art and humor that defined a generation of micro-genre games: bright colors, silly captions, and enough cat visuals to trigger at least two dopamine hits per draw. The rules are simple enough for a casual party and complex enough for a gamer who secretly enjoys probability, bluffing, and the occasional nudge toward chaos. You don’t need a rulebook to play, but you will want to consult it a handful of times as you learn to balance risk, reward, and the occasional urge to veto a sneaky, cat-themed bluff.

### Box contents and approximate components

In a typical original edition box (sealed, of course), you’ll find a deck of cards broken into various action types, plus a selection of Exploding Kitten cards and Defuse cards. There are also some other action cards—See the Future, Shuffle, Attack, Skip, and more—that allow you to manipulate the draw pile, see what’s coming, or dodge a potentially perilous draw. The beauty of the deck is its lean, almost minimalist design: you get enough cards to fuel rapid-fire rounds, and enough variety to keep players guessing what comes next.

If you’re opening a new copy, you’ll notice the packaging is designed to give off that “brand-new, never-touched by human hands” vibe. There’s a satisfying heft to the deck, a reminder that this isn’t a throwaway game—it’s a social device designed to coax laughter and mild chaos from friends and family alike.

## A peek at the mechanics (without spoilers, we promise)

If you want a quick mental model: you’re forming a hand of cards that can alter the fate of the draw pile. Your goal is to avoid drawing an Exploding Kitten (the titular hazard) while using your Defuse cards strategically to survive. The other action cards let you peek at future draws, force other players to draw, shuffle the deck, or pass the risk along to someone else. The pacing is built for party-style rounds: short, punchy, and filled with moments where you’ll mutter “Did that just happen?” as someone dodges a near-miss by the skin of their teeth.

A few core card types you’ll see (in broad strokes, not an exhaustive list):
- Defuse cards: lifesavers after an Exploding Kitten appears. They usually require you to plan ahead or bluff to keep them in your hand.
- Exploding Kitten cards: the hazard you’re trying to dodge, unless you’re holding a Defuse.
- Attack cards: shift the burden, forcing the next player to take multiple draws.
- See the Future cards: peek at upcoming draws, altering your strategy by a few milliseconds.
- Shuffle/Skip cards: alter the flow of play and carve out opportunities to breathe.
- Various “funny” cards: rewards for banter, taunts, and perfectly-timed sarcasm that somehow changes the odds.

The pacing of a typical round is a dance: you draw, you react, you bluff, you pass a risk to a neighbor, and someone panics when the deck shrinks and the top card has a small, adorable danger label. It’s chaotic in the best way: you’re not calculating optimal strategies so much as you’re calculating what your friends will do next, and whether you’re comfortable watching chaos unfold in real time.

## How to approach a sealed copy: practical tips

- Inspect the shrink wrap for rips or re-seals. A torn wrap isn’t a deal-breaker, but it’s a thing to know if you care about mint condition.
- Check the box for any signs of moisture or warping. Card stock loves humidity as much as the next player loves to bluff. A warped box can indicate storage issues more than malice from the universe.
- Ensure the kit includes a Defuse card among the essential cards. While you don’t need to memorize exact counts, a glance at the card types helps confirm you’re not accidentally grabbing a counterfeit deck (which, let’s be honest, you’ll still use for memes if it exists).
- If you’re buying from a reseller, request clear photos of the top and bottom of the deck. Some sellers have a “mystery box” approach to shipping, and you want to know what you’re actually buying before it lands on your doorstep.
- Price expectations: sealed Original Editions can carry premium, but it largely depends on market conditions, demand, and how many times your friends claim to need a “new” copy for game night but then never actually get around to opening it.

Buying sealed can be a delight if you’re building a retro shelf or a nostalgic collection. If you’re more interested in playing, a sealed copy can be opened and enjoyed immediately by your game group. The joy is not simply in owning a new copy; it’s in the ritual of cracking that outer shrink wrap, whispering a few jokes about cats with inappropriate ambitions, and then diving into a session that feels like a party with a dash of high-stakes luck.


## Gameplay tips and strategy (for the but-we-know-you-need-it readers)

- Play with a group that appreciates humor and doesn’t mind chaotic decisions. Exploding Kittens is not a deep strategic wargame; it’s a quick-running social card game. Energy, timing, and a willingness to bluff are as valuable as any tactical decision.
- Protect your Defuse cards: while you might use a Defuse to survive a bomb, you’ll also want to save some Defuses to counter the other players’ attempts to push risk your way. The moment you reveal your Defuse might be a turning point in the round, or it could be a brilliant bluff that makes someone else panic.
- See the Future is your friend, but don’t rely on it too heavily. It’s a peek into the near future, not a guarantee. Use it to string together a plan, then be prepared to adapt when the deck betrays you (and it will).
- When you’re forced to Draw, you can use Attack cards to pass the duty along. This is the classic “pass the bomb” move, but execute it with comedic timing and a smile. Remember: it’s not just about whether you survive the next draw; it’s about exhausting your opponents’ confidence.
- Bluffs and misdirection are valuable: you can pretend you have a strong hand, causing other players to misread the deck or misjudge your next blunder. The joy is in the small theater of the moment—the way a hand gesture or a misdirection can shift the game’s momentum.
