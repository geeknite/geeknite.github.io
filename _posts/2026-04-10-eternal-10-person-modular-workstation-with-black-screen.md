## What is the Eternal 10-Person Modular Workstation with Black Screen?

The Eternal is a modular workstation designed to host up to ten people in a single, shared footprint while giving each user a personal zone for sensing focus, productivity, and occasional existential dread about posture. The centerpiece is the Black Screen, a large, central display panel that can be configured as a collaborative canvas, a wall-sized cue board for agile stand-ups, or a moody backdrop that makes your code look like it’s hiding from sunlight.

From a distance, the unit resembles a spaceship bridge meets open-plan office meets a Swiss Army knife of desks. It’s built on a robust steel frame, with modular work surfaces that can be added, removed, or rearranged depending on team size or mood. The black screen in the middle is a panel that can be used for presentations, video conferencing, or as a giant communal whiteboard when you’re in the mood to pretend you’re a modern-day Leonardo da Vinci of spreadsheets. The whole thing ships in modular chunks, which makes the unpacking experience feel like a reset button for your organizational chaos.

### The Black Screen: feature or theater?

The central “Black Screen” is advertised as a high-contrast, low-glare, energy-efficient panel. In practice, it acts as a few things at once: a shared monitor, a collaborative canvas, or a canvas that refuses to display anything at all and politely asks you to imagine your project. The red thread here is collaboration without shouting. If your team tends to talk over each other, the Black Screen can serve as a neutral focal point that keeps conversations on-track without someone shouting “Update!” every two minutes.

### Build philosophy and materials

The Eternal uses a powder-coated steel frame with modular laminated surfaces. Think Ikea-meets-Robotics: sturdy, modular, and capable of surviving the occasional coffee spill or interpretive dance during a sprint retrospective. The core modules are designed to slot together with cam locks and standardized cable channels so you can, in theory, rearrange the entire thing faster than you can spell “synergy.” In practice, you’ll probably spend a weekend planning different configurations and discovering a surprising talent for furniture jenga. If you’ve ever wondered what would happen if your team-building exercise turned into a DIY furniture project, this is your chance to find out without actually breaking anyone’s legs.

To punctuate the build with a touch of drama, here is a short shopping list you might find in the assembly guide: steel frame rails, panel hooks, anti-sag crossbars, a king-sized power strip, a ceiling-hung cable tray, and the Black Screen. The result is not a single desk; it’s a modular cloud you can physically walk into. It’s the kind of thing you buy when you’re tired of separate desks and want to unify your team under one ominous, glossy surface.

## Design and Build: a closer look

### Frame and materials

The frame is a straightforward steel skeleton with a powder coat finish in matte gray—very “professional gamer” by accident. The powder coating resists fingerprints, while the edges have a gentle radius to keep the office supply reps from getting PTSD every time someone looks at it sideways. The modular desk panels are formed from laminated MDF with a protective melamine overlay, designed to survive the inevitable sticker applications by kids and interns who still think a desk is a canvas for pop culture memes.

Cable management is the unsung hero of any large desk system, and the Eternal doesn’t disappoint. Hidden channels run along the inside of the frames, with pass-through holes that won’t make you weep into a cable spaghetti sculpture. The real magic is in the cable tray that runs along the top, which makes the chaotic energy of ten workstations look intentional and almost Zen when you’re not counting the number of power strips involved.

### The Black Screen: a centerpiece with purpose

The Black Screen is the pièce de résistance. It’s not a mere screen; it’s a design statement. In a room of ten people, a single focal point prevents conversation from dissolving into a dozen micro-threads. The screen can be used in a few modes: presentation mode (think hour-long demos), collaboration mode (digital whiteboard), or quiet mode (dark, unblinking, perfect for mood lighting and dramatic pauses). It’s also optional whether you want to plug in a thin client or simply pretend you’re in a sci-fi meeting where cats consult your roadmap. The size is generous enough to host screen-sharing from a laptop and still give each participant a sense of personal space—though “space” may vary depending on your office layout and seating plan.

### Ergonomics and user zones

A major talking point for any 10-person table is ergonomics. Each workstation is designed to give the user about 120 degrees of upright posture space, with a modest 2- to 3-inch vertical adjustment range for different chair heights. It’s not a replacement for a proper ergonomic chair, but it’s a respectable foundation. With ten people sharing a compact footprint, the design relies on personal space boxes rather than full separation walls. It’s the difference between a friendly, cooperative vibe and a chaotic, “who spilled coffee on my keyboard?” vibe. If you have a team that needs to stretch, you’ll want to consider adding a few mobile monitor arms and perhaps a couple of small privacy dividers to keep the peace when the sprint review turns into a heated debate about feature parity.

## Setup and assembly: how hard is it, really?

As any furniture aficionado will tell you, the true test of modular furniture is the assembly experience. The Eternal ships in modular chunks that require a screwdriver, a couple of hours, and a playlist that slaps just enough to get you through the middle part of the build.

### A practical assembly timeline
1) Unpack and inventory: count the rails, panels, cam locks, and the mysterious “extra bolt” that might be the key to the ultimate Slack channel’s morale.
2) First frame assembly: snap the frame together with the base rails. This is the moment where you feel like a tiny blacksmith forging a desk‑monolith for your office to worship.
3) Surface alignment: attach modular surfaces to the frame using the cam-lock system. If one panel doesn’t align, back out the lock, adjust, and pretend your life depends on precision engineering.
4) Cable management: route power and data through the pre-cut channels. A label maker is your friend here; your future self will appreciate the organization you thought of today.
5) The Black Screen installation: secure the central panel and connect the power/data rails. If you’ve ever wanted to feel like a stage manager for a corporate theater, this is your moment.
6) Final alignment and test: power up, check for wobble, verify cable slack, and measure for comfort. If there’s any wobble, tighten with a flourish and declare victory to an audience of none or your fellow team members depending on your mood.

In practice, most teams can assemble the Eternal within a long afternoon to a relaxed weekend. The real challenge isn’t the physics—it’s convincing your team to cooperate without turning the project into a “too many cooks” scenario. Pro-tip: designate a single lead assembler and a second as the “enthusiastic tester” who acts as the morale barometer, turning every minor adjustment into a small celebration.

### Setup tips for a happy office

- Pre-plan your layout: sketch out where the Black Screen sits relative to the central whiteboard (and your coffee maker).
- Use color-coded cable sleeves to keep track of which group sits where. This helps during change requests when mid-demo coffee spills threaten to become a feature.
- Invest in non-slip desk mats; you’ll thank yourself when everyone grants permanent residency to their keyboards without sliding seats.
- Document your configuration as you go. If your team ever splits, you’ll want to remember which module went where when you put it back together in a new space.

## Real-world usability: does it actually work?

Ten people around a central desk sounds glamorous until someone claims the chair has a backrest of doom. How does the Eternal fare in real life? In our tests, we observed several key dynamics:

- Collaboration boost: The central screen acts as a focal point, reducing the need to shout across the room. This is not just a vibes thing; it helps teams stay on the same page during live demos and quick stand-up sessions.
- Personal space balance: The modules create defined zones without slamming you into privacy walls. If your team values transparency and casual interactions, the Eternal delivers; if you’re in a “we need private code reviews” kind of setup, you’ll want to add light privacy panels at select stations.
- Maintenance: With ten people, cables multiply like gremlins. The built-in channels and robust cable tray help, but you’ll still want a weekly cable audit to keep the chaos from becoming a forest fire—metaphorically speaking. Proactive cable management is the unsung hero here.
- Acoustics: The surface material helps reduce vibrations from typing, but it’s not a soundproof habitat. If your team is prone to three or four simultaneous meetings, consider some acoustic panels or carpet squares to dampen the mid-level chatter.
