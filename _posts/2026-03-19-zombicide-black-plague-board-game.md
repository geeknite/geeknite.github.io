## What is Zombicide: Black Plague?

Zombicide: Black Plague is a cooperative tabletop game for 1–6 players (though six is a crowd, in the best possible chaos sense). You take on the roles of survivors—knights and other medieval misfits—who must cooperate to complete objectives while fending off wave after wave of zombies. The twist here is the theme: instead of modern-day survival, you’re marching through a grim, plague-ridden fantasy world filled with swords, axes, burned villages, and more catapult-worthy tableaus than you can shake a moy-en-axe at.

The core loop is simple on the surface and delightfully brutal once you start playing: each round, you activate your survivors, take actions to move, search for gear, or fight. Then the zombies spawn, surge toward the living, and you watch the board explode into a chaotic dance of combat and terrain effects. The tension comes from limited resources, the ever-present threat of a bad spawn, and the fact that your heroes can become truly epic if you survive long enough to level up and equip the right gear.

What sets Black Plague apart from the original Zombicide is the fantasy medieval flavor. The components lean into knights, magic, and siege warfare, giving you crossbows, maces, and armor that look like they belong in a crusade rather than a modern disaster movie. The art direction—bright, cartoonish, yet somehow menacing—lets you feel heroic (and a little silly) at the same time. It’s a big game with a big box and even bigger personality.

If there’s one thing CMON does well, it’s making a product that looks and feels premium. Zombicide: Black Plague ships with a mountain of minis, chunky game boards, and a vast assortment of cards, tokens, dice, and spiffy cardboard chits that somehow manage to be both practical and eye-catching. The quality control is generally excellent—you won’t have pieces snapping or cards warping in the way you sometimes do with budget games.

This is a game that wears its production value on its sleeve. If you want a co-op experience with a huge board, a ton of miniatures, and the satisfying weight of a product that feels substantial in your hands, Black Plague delivers in abundance.

## How to Set Up and Play (A Quick Guide)

Setup can feel like assembling a medieval siege engine, but fear not: it’s not as soul-crushing as it looks. Here’s the gist:

1) Open the box and organize the components. Sort the survivors, zombies, weapons, armor, and cards into sensible piles. Pro tip: designate a “casting director” for the first few rounds to explain the gear names and the various weapon types; you’ll thank yourself later when there’s no confusion about the difference between a longsword and a gentle, polite longsword.
2) Build the table-ready board by snapping together the modular tiles. The board layout influences spawn patterns, line-of-sight blocks, and the general sense of impending doom. Remember: bigger boards mean more opportunities for glorious, ridiculous, tabletop chaos.
3) Seed the zombie spawn deck and place the starting zombies. The starting set is small enough to feel manageable, but if your party is new, you’ll likely ramp up toward the end of the first session.
4) Give each player a survivor, load the starting equipment, and study the starting objectives. Some missions are stealthy and require you to move quietly; others require you to kind of trigger a big chain of events to finally slay a boss. You’ll see a blend of puzzle-solving and brawler action—perfect for groups that like strategy with a side of “oh no, a zombie is about to bite my mage” moments.
5) Roll the dice, resolve actions, and watch the board explode in a good way. The game runs in rounds; on each round, you’ll activate survivors in a certain order, perform actions, and then spawn zombies. The tension grows with every turn as the board fills with more undead and more opportunities for catastrophic heroism.

## Core Mechanics: How the Game Actually Works

At the heart of Black Plague is a simple but satisfying action economy. Each survivor has a number of action points (AP) they can spend on a turn: moving, searching for gear, picking up items, or attacking. The zombies don’t think; they just advance toward any nearby hero with a hunger for brains and glory. Combat uses a mix of dice rolls and weapon stats; more powerful weapons require you to be in a specific position or to spend more AP, which means you’re balancing risk and reward in real time.

- Action economy: You typically get 2 actions per turn, with some gear and class abilities granting extra actions or different effects. Positioning matters: line-of-sight, terrain bonuses, and “blocked” zones can decide whether you survive another round or become a statistic in someone’s post-game confessional.
- Gear and progression: Weapons like swords, axes, and magic staves are the lifeblood of your party’s survival. Armor reduces the sting of zombie claws. Treasure and equipment cards grant special powers or situational benefits. The XP system encourages you to push for risks that pay off later, rewarding players with new abilities and a stronger survivorship profile.
- Zombies and spawn traffic: The zed population isn’t static. After each round, new zombies spawn from the deck in varying waves. The more you advance, the more complex the board becomes, and the more you realize that a single misstep can cascade into a multi-turn disaster.
- Objectives: Each mission presents a set of objectives, from “eliminate a boss” to “collect three key artifacts” to “survive until the clock runs out.” Some objectives reward aggressive play; others reward careful planning and board control. The balance between risk and reward is what makes Black Plague exciting and occasionally heart-stopping.

The vibe is cooperative, but the game never shies away from letting you feel the consequences of your poor decisions. If you’ve ever yelled “I’ll just search again and risk the horde” and immediately regretted it, you’ll feel right at home here. The game teaches a brutal lesson: you’re strongest as a team, and the moment you split up to chase a personal glory objective, the board will remind you that this is not your single-player campaign—it's a living, groaning monster with a sense of humor.

The medieval fantasy setting isn’t just a cosmetic bath; it’s integral to the game’s identity. One of the achievements of Black Plague is how it blends lightheartedness with intensity. The jokes you’ll crack as a party—“we’re not so much adventurers as organized chaos with a blade”—are earned because the system invites you to improvise within a robust framework. It’s a game that benefits from a table full of players who can pivot, pivot again, and then deliver a clever plan that makes everyone cheer and then immediately regret the decision two rounds later when a boss spawns behind the party’s flank.

## Solo Play and Player Count Variations

While the box is designed for up to six players, the real magic happens when you dial the team down and lean into a full-on co-op narrative with a friend or two. Solo play is obviously possible with a strong solo variant (often published by fan communities or the publisher’s own expansions). In solo mode, you control multiple survivors—your job is to micro-manage multiple objectives and optimize your resource pool across more than one character, which is where the game shines in the brain-bending department. The bigger your table, the more you’ll feel like a general commanding a siege; fewer players, and you’ll feel like you’re running a tight, well-practiced strike team.

Time-wise, expect games to last anywhere from 60 to 120 minutes depending on the scenario, player count, and how many times your plan gets foiled by a luck-laden spawn or a poorly judged roll. If you’re playing with a noisy group, you may slide toward the 90-minute mark. If you’re chasing a tight, tense run, a 60-minute session can feel exhilarating for its pace. It’s not a “five-minute puzzle”; it’s a strategic workout with a generous splash of carnage.

## Strategy Tips for Victory and Survival

- Prioritize survivability first, loot second. Your initial moves should be about securing enough armor, weapons with good reach, and healing items. The moment you start chasing a shiny new sword with no defense, you’ll regret it on the next zombie surge.
- Control zones. Block doors strategically and use terrain to funnel zombies into bottlenecks. The more you can control the flow, the more you can manage damage and preserve your party.
- Coordinate reloads and actions. Don’t spread your best weapons too thin across the group. You want at least one strong ranged option and a couple of melee specialists for crowd control.
- Level up smartly. XP isn’t infinite; spend it on survivors who are likely to stay alive longer and contribute to the objective’s success. Sometimes a mid-tier survivor with a strong endgame build beats a hero who peaks early and then fizzles out.
- Don’t ignore search actions. Gear is the lifeblood of long survival. A well-timed search can yield a weapon upgrade or armor that changes the tide of a difficult round.
- Read the board state out loud. If the table is silent, it’s easy to miss a crucial spawn or a path that could save someone. Reading out loud keeps everyone engaged and helps with critical decision-making in chaotic moments.

## Expansions and Replay Value

Black Plague is famous for its breadth, and its expansions only compound the fun. There are several add-ons that introduce new heroes, new equipment, and new mission types. The expansions often scale in complexity, so they’re perfect for turning a single campaign into a recurring weekly event with increasingly elaborate setups and smarter zombie spawn logic.

If you’re aiming for freshness, you can mix in additional boards, more enemies, and new villain types. The replay value is strong, especially for groups that enjoy optimizing strategies and seeing how far they can push the “death-by-axe” moment before the next expansion is released.

## Comparisons: Black Plague vs. The Original Zombicide

The original Zombicide shipped with a more modern, urban vibe; Black Plague swaps the city for the castle walls and sewers for dungeons, while preserving the core mechanics that make Zombicide a staple of many game closets. If you loved the base game’s chaos and co-op teamwork but wanted something with more thematic punch, Black Plague scratches that itch. The art direction and miniature quality are upgraded in the fantasy setting, while the numbers and probabilities operate on familiar ground. It’s essentially a familiar engine with a different, more cinematic coat of paint.

That said, Black Plague inherits some of the same burdens as its progenitor: luck can feel decisive in the late game, and the scale of the box can be intimidating for a casual group. It rewards planning, but there are moments where a single unlucky spawn can turn a near-miss into a full-on wipe. If you’re chasing pure tactical chess, you’ll be thrilled; if you’re after a flawlessly consistent experience, you’ll want to discuss house rules or look toward the expansions for more control.

## The Geeknite Verdict: Who Should Buy It? Why? And When?

- You should buy if you want a strong, cinematic co-op experience with a big, bright table presence, a heroic fantasy vibe, and a gameplay loop that rewards teamwork and planning. It’s a fantastic party game for groups who like strategy but can handle a high-energy, sometimes chaotic vibe.
- You should not buy if you don’t have a dedicated gaming space, if you dislike heavy games with a long setup, or if you’re allergic to big box games that require a few evenings to complete a campaign. In those cases, there are lighter co-ops with similar themes that fit better with casual play.
- If you’re on the fence, borrow or watch a play-through first. The size and weight can be a commitment, but the payoff is real for the right group. And if you’ve got friends who adore fantasy, swords, and a little zombie mayhem, Black Plague will become a cherished staple on your shelf.
