## Introduction
If you were to cross a medieval fantasy setting with a zombie apocalypse and then squish it into a box with more miniatures than your budget should allow, you would probably end up with Zombicide Black Plague. CMONs GUF001CMN code might look like a shopping cart error waiting to happen, but this is not a glitch in the matrix. It is the doorway to hours of cooperative chaos where knights, priests, thieves, and magic folks sprint around a tiled dungeon dimension while undead gnaw at ankles with reckless enthusiasm.

Zombicide Black Plague is the second major spin of the original Zombicide concept, translating the franchise for fans who crave knights, goblins, and dragons a little less vampire and a little more decapitation. In this review, we will dive into the nuts, bolts, and brains of the game. We will discuss components, setup, gameplay flow, strategy, and whether a squad of square-jawed adventurers can survive a siege of shambling corpses wearing pointy hats and armor that squeaks with every step. Spoiler: it can be glorious chaos if you lean into it with the right crew.

> Quick note for the curious: this review is written for players who want narrative heft, tactical pacing, and a decent chance to win while still rolling dice like a caffeinated bard. If you are shopping for a solo puzzle instead of a co op dungeon crawl with a big box of plastic limbs, you might want to turn right back to the forest of misfit board games.

### Box code and packaging quirks
The GUF001CMN code is the behind the curtain geek badge of honor here. The box is big and heavy, which is a good sign because it means there is content worth spilling over your coffee table. Inside you will find a thick stack of nice punchboard tiles, a healthy squad of plastic heroes, a delirious number of zombie miniatures in various grotesque flavors, a heap of dice, and a pile of character cards and event decks. The art direction skews grimy and medieval in a way that makes you feel like you wandered into a dragon’s lair only to realize you forgot a lamp and your sense of self-preservation.

The quality of minis is notably good for a mass market production. The sculpts have personality, the faces are readable, and the weapons look like someone spent a tad too long in a blacksmith shop. You will likely want to basecoat or contrast paint if you intend to show this to your painting club, but the default mini quality is perfectly fine for a game night when you are mostly chasing the legend of who has the most efficient rush on a map.

## How the game plays
Zombicide Black Plague is a co op dungeon crawl at its core. A team of survivors roams a modular map of streets, tombs, and crypt floors, completing objectives while zombies spawn and pile up like an unenthusiastic avalanche. The game supports 1 to 6 players, with scenarios designed for different player counts, which is nice for a game night that occasionally forgets its RSVP. The turns are crisp enough to move quickly but complex enough to reward planning and teamwork.

### Turn structure at a glance
- Players choose a survivor character with a unique ability and a small set of starting items.
- On your turn you perform two actions. Actions typically include moving, searching for gear, opening doors, or trading items with teammates. You can also choose to end your turn and let the undead chorus begin.
- Zombies spawn based on the current scenario and a die or card driven event that dents your plans with a chorus of chattering teeth. The more you explore, the more corpses appear, which is a feature not a bug if your goal is cinematic chaos.
- The goal varies by scenario. It could be to collect specific items, reach a location, or survive a certain number of rounds. Some scenarios demand a more surgical approach while others encourage reckless heroism or a strategic stand in a plating of runic graffiti.

What sets Black Plague apart from the original Zombicide line is a thematic shift toward medieval horror and a more organic sense of scale. The walkers, fatties, and abominations feel heavier, and the maps have that labyrinthine medieval town vibe rather than generic post apocalypse alleyways. The result is a game that feels like it has less of a sci fi backbone and more of a fantasy siege engine behind its undead onslaught.

### Combat, morale, and the dice puzzle
We should be honest about the dice. The core mechanic of success hinges on rolling enough hits to kill a zombie while avoiding taking wounds yourself. There is a satisfying crunch to the dice, particularly when you roll multiple hits on a critical ladder moment and then watch the last zombie flip like a mishandled flag on a windy day. The morale system is implicit but present. Each character has health and a set of skills that can be spent to save a teammate or reroll a clumsy attack. The tension is real when you are low on health and the abomination is looming in a corner with a teethy grin and a “you cant outrun forever” vibe.

The tactical layer is where the fun lives. You often plan by zones, using the terrain to funnel zombies into kill boxes. The map’s tile system supports clever chokes, and the presence of doors creates two distinct phases per turn: the push through the current room and the manager of new zombies that spawn after the turn. It is a game that rewards space management and cooperative choreography. If you have a chess player in your group, you will hear the word acceleration used to describe how fast you can move a clutch of survivors from one side of the map to another while holding the line against a rising tide.

### Themed flavor and components in practice
The visual palette and the miniature sculpts lean into a louder, more theatrical vibe than many co op games. You can easily imagine a squad of knights and necromancers throwing themselves across a corridor, shouting the occasional heroic but impractical battle cry. The board tiles, doors, and objective markers read well at a glance, which is a huge plus when you have a table full of players who want to dive straight in rather than waste fifteen minutes interpreting the map. The knightly armor has character, the zombie types are varied enough to feel unsettling rather than repetitive, and the abominations evoke a sense of boss fight that makes you feel like you might actually win if you pay attention rather than just roll luck.

## What to expect in terms setup and upkeep
Setup time is non trivial, but not ridiculous. You should budget around 15 to 25 minutes for setup at the start of the night, especially if you are teaching players who are new to the franchise. The board tiles are modular, and there is a tendency for the map to explode into a sprawling network of corridors that could easily overwhelm a casual group. Take a moment to lay out a few standard encounter templates you enjoy first; this lets players learn the mechanics without being buried under six different scenario specifics.

During play, you will want a few different organizers on the table. There is a lot to manage: character decks, zombie decks, item decks, and the various scenario cards. If you are the kind of person who gets anxious about table fatigue or card misplacement, feel free to print a simple organizer or buy a small components tray. It helps a lot when you are mid fight and need to locate a key item or decide whether you want to trade an item with a teammate.

### Solo play and group dynamics
Zombicide Black Plague shines with a good group. The co op dynamic thrives when you coordinate zone control and execute a plan that relies on a little sacrifice to protect the more fragile members of the squad. Solo play is viable but can be a bit swingy; you can still enjoy the puzzle of managing tiles and equipment by yourself, but a group naturally provides the rhythm and humor that keeps the game alive through repetition.

If you are a gamer who loves to argue about the best move while someone else is rolling dice, this game will feel like a warm shower of catharsis. It scratches the itch for tactical thinking and cooperative problem solving, with the bonus of a terror quotient that makes you wince a little every time a zombie chains into a new room with a scream-worthy sound effect on the event deck.

## Comparing with other Zombicide editions
Zombicide Black Plague is part of the larger Zombicide family and sits alongside other editions that have experimented with settings and mechanics. Compared to the original Zombicide, Black Plague has more tactile weight and a fantasy flavor that makes the undead threats feel bigger and meaner. It also pushes players to engage more actively with terrain and map flow, rather than simply farming kills in an open space.

If you have dipped into Zombicide in the past and found the pace either too chaotic or too linear, Black Plague offers a well-balanced middle ground. The scenarios vary enough to maintain interest across multiple sessions. It is not simply a re-skin of a zombie walk; the medieval theme reshapes your expectations and the way you plan your routes and your trades.

## Strategy tips and recommended approaches
- Communicate early and plan a route map with your team. The order in which you activate survivors matters, because you will want to funnel zombies into kill zones where your strongest party members can deal efficient damage.
- Use your heavy hitters to clear corridors while a more nimble ally scouts ahead for loot and keys. Do not underestimate the value of a well placed search action in a room that looks empty but may contain a critical item or objective.
- Watch the zombie spawn pool. If you spend too much time in one area, the map wants to punish you with a flood of new enemies entering through every door. Keep a rhythm that balances search, move, and kill without allowing the map to dictate a panic mode.
- Manage gear wisely. Rare items can turn a bad turn into a turning point. Prioritize items that boost survivability and control the board state, like extra movement, damage modifiers, or ranged tools that let you thin the undead line before it arrives.
- Don’t neglect objectives. Sometimes you win by sprinting to the exit or collecting an item that triggers a win condition. It is tempting to chase loot, but sometimes you need to ignore treasure in favor of a clean escape route with allies who can still contribute on the final stretch.

## Expansions and replayability
If you want more of this deliciously grim co op chaos, there are several expansions and add ons that keep the party lively. The box content scales with player count, and a few expansion packs introduce new maps, new zombie types, and additional survivor classes that feel like a breath of fresh air after a dozen sessions. The re-playability here is strong, not because you get a new puzzle each time, but because the table antics and the occasional critical failure or heroic save create new stories every session.

For players who crave even more complexity, there are variant rules and custom scenarios that community members have cooked up. These are not required to enjoy the core experience, but they do give you a reason to dust off that zine, ball up your dice, and pretend you are a dungeon master guiding a small army of hopeful rejects through a siege against the undead in a medieval carnival of doom.

## Artwork, components, and production quality
The artwork is robust and atmospheric, leaning into gloom and gloomier with splashes of red to remind you that the zombies do not have a conscience. The map tiles hold up well under the table traffic, and the tokens for loot and objective markers are clearly marked. The steel and plastic feel sturdy, which matters when you consider the number of times you will reach across the table to grab a weapon card or confirm a kill.

There is a tactile satisfaction to moving a group of figures across a tiled courtyard, hearing the dice clatter, and turning a corner to face a new hazard. The theme lands with a satisfying crunch that makes this feel like a credible medieval zombie siege rather than a generic dungeon crawl with more swords than brains.

## The Geeknite verdict
Zombicide Black Plague is a strong entry in the Zombicide lineup for players who enjoy cooperative strategy with a strong theme and a sense of escalation. It rewards planning and teambuilding without demanding that every square inch of the map be memorized. It is not the easiest entry point for new players—there are many moving parts and a lot of reading to catch up on the first session—yet it pays dividends once you lock in a rhythm and start optimizing routes and trades.

It also delivers social currency. The nights when a teammate sneaks an extra item and quietly sacrifices themselves for the greater good become the kinds of stories you tell at meetups and around the table first. The balance between luck and strategy is well tuned enough that you feel satisfied with the win even if your plan was not perfect. And if your crew loves co op games with a bite, Black Plague will likely become a staple in your rotation.

## Final recommendation
If you want a boxed co op experience that scratches the itch for tactical planning, thematic siege vibes, and a table full of dramatic dice rolls, Zombicide Black Plague is a medicine for your gaming group. It scales from 1 to 6 players with a variety of scenarios that allow you to tailor the intensity. It is a buy with confidence for fans of the franchise and a strong recommendation for newcomers who want a big box game night with shared peril and collective triumphs.
