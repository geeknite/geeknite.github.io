## What is the Storyteller's Screen, anyway?

In City of Mist, as in many noir-infused RPGs, the screen serves two main purposes. First, it provides a barrier between the GM and the players, preserving some mystery and allowing the storyteller to flip a card with confidence while still looking dramatic. Second, it offers quick reference materials so the GM can stay in the moment rather than hunting for a rule in a rulebook that somehow got buried under a box of minis beside a half-melted candle. The City of Mist Storyteller's Screen is a three-panel affair, foldable for travel and robust enough to survive the occasional slap of a notebook or the occasional goblin-sized snack spill (hey, it happens). The art direction leans into the same moody noir aesthetic your city deserves: silhouetted skyscrapers, rain-slick streets, and hints of mythic iconography peeking through like a neon halo around a steam vent.

## Design and readability: the layout that actually helps

### The panels and their purpose
The three-panel setup is designed for a quick reveal of the essential City of Mist prompts. The middle panel is the spine, the vanishing point where you frame scenes and reveal the most important narrative beats. The left and right panels are your quick reference zones, typically dedicated to core moves, jurisdictional prompts, and scene cues. The exact layout varies slightly depending on the edition, but the logic remains: keep the most used bits front and center, and tuck the rest behind a mythic veil of art and color.

### Art direction and color theory
The art leans into the city as character: rain-slick blues, deep magentas for neon signage, and touches of gold for mythic iconography that hints at the heroic without shouting. The palette is suited to long table sessions; high-contrast panels ease recognition under low lighting and the occasional late-night gamer energy spike. If you enjoy a screen that feels like a companion rather than a simple divider, you will appreciate how the artwork communicates mood at a glance, not just at a second or third glance.

### Readability under stress
A good GM screen should vanish into the table so you can forget it exists until you need it. The City of Mist screen nails this in practice. The typography is legible without forcing you to squint; the font size is conservative enough to fit the most common moves without feeling cramped, yet bold enough to be legible from across the table for players who like to obstruct their own character sheets with habit and doodles. The inner print uses a clean, compact layout that minimizes the need to hold the screen in two hands while you narrate a skyline-shattering moment. It is a screen made for cold reads and hot takes.

## How it affects play: GM focus and player immersion

### For the GM: speed, clarity, and control
One of the greatest pains in any city-fueled RPG is the moment when a player asks, about the 9th round of a scene, for a reminder of a specific rule or a particular effect. The Storyteller's Screen alleviates a good deal of that anxiety by presenting concise prompts that you can glance at while delivering the next line of noir prose. The most-used elements—combat prompts, clue handling cues, and the Mysterious Character prompts—are easy to access, meaning you can stay in the moment rather than shuffling through a dungeon of books. The screen does not narc on you to your tablemates, but it does keep you accountable to the shared language of the City of Mist, letting you refer to the familiar terms instead of stuttering through a paraphrase.

### For the players: a shared mood, not a cheat sheet
While screens often feel like a barrier, this one is more like a stage curtain: it signals that a scene is about to unfold and invites players to lean into the mood rather than lean on the details. The ability to maintain the mystery of the city while still offering players clear landmarks (stats, moves, and story prompts) helps keep engagement high. The aesthetic also helps: a good screen reduces the sense that you are playing a game about myth and street-level crime and instead makes you feel like you are stepping into a world where every streetlamp hums with potential. It is not magic on a page; it is mood on a board.

## The practicalities: setup, use, and table impact

### Setup and placement
Setting up the screen takes less time than ordering a cup of coffee with complex foam art. You unfold the panels, align the edges, and you are ready to narrate the opening line of the night: the city is always listening. The weight of the screen helps keep your notes in place so you can lean in and exact your line of noir prose without the paper scurrying away. If your group is mobile, the fold-and-stow design makes it easy to transport in a messenger bag or a backpack. The real trick is remembering to tilt the middle panel toward yourself so you can see the inner references without doing a dramatic screen spin that collapses the dramatis personae into a chorus of laughter.

### Interaction with moves and prompts
City of Mist players are used to the interplay of myth and earthly trouble. The screen’s prompts align well with the game’s rhythm: you set the scene, players declare a move or a response, and the screen gives you a quick anchor for the next action. There is a tactile pleasure to being able to point at a prompt and say, in the cadence of a film noir voiceover, Okay, this is where we resolve that clue. It streamlines the process without feeling like it is forcing a rules check at an inopportune moment. The result is a smoother flow that preserves the energy of the city rather than pausing it for a page-turn moment.

### Durability under heavy table gaming
We tested the screen through a full evening of city-lore immersion, which included spilled coffee, a parade of snack wrappers, and a minor tabletop disaster involving a cat that found itself magnetically drawn to the table. The screen stood up to the chaos with minimal wear. The matte finish resisted glare from the fireplace and the neon glow; the panels did not warp under the heat of heated debates about who is myth and who is menace. If you run one-shot adventures or full campaigns, this piece should hold up to your table’s reckless enthusiasm for dramatic storytelling and dramatic gestures.
