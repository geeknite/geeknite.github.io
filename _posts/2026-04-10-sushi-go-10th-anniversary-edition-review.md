## How does the 10th Anniversary Edition differ from the base game?

The core mechanics of Sushi Go! remain intact: players draft a card each turn from a hand, pass the rest, and collect sets that score according to the dish types. - Streamlined setup and improved readability: The updated card art and clearer iconography reduce the time it takes to teach the game to new players and speed up the “one more round” factor for veterans who are already familiar with the table talk and scoring quirks.
- If you’re a completionist, you’ll enjoy the little extras: some editions include bonus cards or optional variants that tweak scoring paths. These aren’t dramatic departures from the core experience; think of them as spice racks that let you tailor the flavor to your crowd.
- Handling and durability: The increased card stock and improved coatings make the set feel more durable, which matters if you’re the kind of group that plays while eating or while your cat insists on sitting on the rulebook.

Bottom line: it’s the same heart with a shinier face. If you loved Sushi Go! before, you’ll appreciate the light, tasteful upgrades that don’t disrupt the rhythm you know and love.

## How to play (brief refresher for the curious)

If you’re new to Sushi Go!, the premise is simple and elegant: you’re a sushi chef collecting sets by drafting cards that move around the table in a loop. Each round, you pick one card from your current hand and pass the rest to the player on your left. After three rounds (or more on longer variations), you tally points from set collections like maki rolls, nigiri, sashimi, wasabi, and pudding. The trick is to balance short-term gains with longer-term scoring opportunities, because some cards align nimbly with others to maximize your total.

The Anniversary Edition doesn’t change that core. What it does is make the experience friendlier to teach, easier to distinguish, and more pleasant to look at while you’re counting the tiny piles of points in front of you. In practical terms, you’ll notice the following when you play:

- Card clarity: You won’t squint as much when spotting a three-fatty nigiri versus a two-fatty nigiri, which means you can make better trade-offs under pressure.
- Speed: The game remains lightning-fast. A group of four can finish a few rounds in the time it takes to decide what you want on a sushi night out with friends. Think of it as an easy win for busy households or after-work game sessions when you want something both quick and glamorous.
- Social flavor: The quick rounds encourage chatter. People rib each other about “yakitori diplomacy” or who’s the master of the wasabi bluff. It’s not just about scoring; it’s about the fun of the table and the stories built around every tiny decision.

If you’re teaching a group that includes kids, this edition rocks in two ways: the clarity reduces argument-paroxysms about who has what in the score pile, and the bright visuals keep little ones glued to the table as you explain why pudding is worth a surprising amount of points when your cards align just so.
