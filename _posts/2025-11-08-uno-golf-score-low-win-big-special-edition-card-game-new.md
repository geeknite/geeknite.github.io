## How to Play: Quickstart guide for the impatient gamer

UNO Golf Score Low is built on two pillars: a fast pace and a low score obsession. Here is a distilled quickstart so you can stop reading and start yelling at your friends about par values.

### The Basic Flow
1. Shuffle and deal a hand of seven cards to each player. The rest of the deck becomes a draw pile. A discard pile sits ready for wild and wacky plays.
2. On your turn, you must either play a card that matches either the color or the number of the top discard card, or you can play one of the golf themed action cards. If you can’t play, you draw one card.
3. The twist is the Golf Score mechanic. Each round you’re trying to lower your total score by shedding cards with lower point values and playing Par and Birdie cards to reset or reduce your standing. The par system rewards efficient shedding of cards rather than brute force removal.
4. When a player empties their hand, the round ends and scores are tallied. The goal is to finish with the lowest cumulative score across the agreed number of rounds. Yes, this is the kind of game where a single Birdie can swing the mood of the entire group from “laughing at memes” to “watching someone calculate depreciation.”

### The Golf Score Mechanic in Detail
Each Card carries a numeric value and a golf related label. Par cards set baseline expectations and can interact with the score pad in surprising ways. Birdie cards typically offer a modest score boost or reduction depending on context, while Eagle cards can dramatically tilt the math in your favor when you find yourself in a tight spot. Bogey cards are the mischievous tricksters, adding penalties that can derail even the most optimistic strategy, which means you’ll want to keep a Mulligan or two in your back pocket for those “my brain forgot to go to math class” moments.

The Score Pad is a surprisingly clever addition. It keeps track of each player’s running score across rounds, but it also includes space for a running tally of “golf vibes” — a rating you can use to describe how the game is going emotionally. If the table has a post-laugh glow after a Bogey chain, you can mark it and have a quick, comedic postmortem about your dream of becoming a golf pro who drinks only espresso shots and regrets nothing.

### Card Types and Effects: the lore of the deck
- Par: Stabilizes. Par cards can reset your hand’s momentum and give you a small relief in score. They are the light at the end of a lengthy prancing parade of numbers.
- Birdie: The gentle nudge. Birdie cards typically reduce your score by a small amount when played in the right moment, turning minor mishaps into triumphs you can brag about later.
- Eagle: The big swing. Eagle cards are your “go big or go home” moments that drastically cut or rearrange the scoring, often at the cost of leaving you vulnerable to the next turn.
- Bogey: The prankster. Bogey cards add penalties or force you to take a draw in a way that tests both your arithmetic and your poker-face.
- Mulligan: A safety valve. Mulligan cards allow you to redraw or reframe your score in a round, but you must use them with care, lest you become the person who always uses a Mulligan and thus never finishes anything.
- Hole in One: A legendary card that rarely appears and even more rarely satisfies everyone who witnessed it. When it appears, it can dramatically swing the round’s balance, to the joy and occasional despair of the table.

### End of Round: who wins and how
When a player depletes their hand, the round ends. Other players tally points from their remaining cards, and their scores are added to the round total. The aim is to keep your cumulative score as low as possible. The game typically runs for a pre-agreed number of rounds or until someone hits a target score, whichever comes first. The result is a blend of luck, math, and the occasional existential crisis about why you still can’t handle a simple card draw without theatrics.

### Strategy Primer: how to approach this with maximum vibes
- Early rounds favor card dropping and careful management. You’re not trying to slam all the low value cards at once; you’re setting up a future where you can play a Birdie and a Par in sequence for a controlled score swing.
- Late game demands disciplined risk taking. The table will be counting on you to avoid a sweeping Bogey cascade. Use Mulligans wisely, like a gamer who saves their ultimate for that boss you keep failing at twenty times.
- Observing opponents’ tendencies matters. If your buddy tends to catastrophize when a Birdie card hits the table, you can sequence your moves to press their emotional buttons while quietly sinking your score with a Par flush.
