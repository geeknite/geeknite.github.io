## Overview

Zombicide Black Plague is the darker, spikier cousin of your standard zombie-survival board game. It throws players into a swamp of plague-born undead, where you equip survivors with weapons that would make a D&D dragon sneeze, and then sprint around boards that look like they were designed by a caffeinated beekeeper. Friends and Foes expands this already chaotic playground by adding new survivor types, new zombie variants, and new rules interactions that freshen up co-op play without transforming it into a pure poker-night of who-can-muster-motivation-before-murder-horde-number-five.

The expansion arrives in a similarly satisfying CMON fashion: sturdy box, glossy miniatures, a handful of plastic bits that rattle pleasantly when you shake the table, and a rulebook that politely asks you to consider playing with a bigger table and more patience. The cover art keeps the same grimy-chic aesthetic, which is to say it looks like someone spilled coffee on a medieval tapestry and decided to call it ambience. If you liked the vibe of the base game, Friends and Foes keeps the vibe and turbo-charges the options.

## What's in the box

- A handful of new survivor figures, including some that diverge from the standard Jacks-of-all-trades and offer more specialized roles. Expect at least a couple of characters who feel like they were designed by a caffeine-fueled balance nerd with a penchant for dramatic poses.
- More zombie types: not just the standard legion but variants that require new tactics—because nothing says “fun” like trying to corral a dozen walkers while a hulking abomination shouts from the opposite corner.
- New mission cards and objectives that aren’t simply “kill everything and hope.” They add secondary goals, timed twists, and occasional misdirections that make you rethink the safest route—which is often the street with the least zombies and the most screaming, but who’s counting?
- Additional equipment and loot tokens to mix into the loot pool. These can swing a turn from “we might win” to “we might survive long enough to lose with style.”
- A slightly revised rule sheet that aims to reduce the number of times you and your table look at each other with the same fear you reserve for a ring-bound horror anthology.

The contents are not just more plastic toys; they’re more levers to pull when your group wants to experiment with risk and reward. The balance tweaks are subtle enough to keep the core experience familiar, yet they give you enough new tools to justify a second (or third) run in a single campaign evening.

## Thematic flavor and art direction

Zombicide’s theme leans into the grimy, overcast, urban-apocalypse vibe. Friends and Foes preserves that mood while injecting a little more human flavor through the new survivors. The ritual of choosing a character who can kite zombies with surgical precision or distract them with improvised traps feels like adding a few extra party members to your D&D party: more personality, more quirks, and more chances to mess up spectacularly in front of your friends.

The color palette and sculpt quality stay true to the Black Plague line. The plastics aren’t the lightest or most delicate in your collection, but they hold paint well, read clearly on the board, and survive the inevitable tabletop misadventure where someone uses a dry erase marker as a paint thinner (don’t ask me how I know this).

For fans of the artwork, Friends and Foes keeps the graphic identity intact. The card art is bold, the icons are readable from across the table, and the horror-fantasy mashup remains exactly the kind of mood you want when you’re scripting heroic but doomed last stands against the oncoming tide of undead chaos.

## Mechanics: what changes with this expansion

### New survivors and their specialty roles
The expansion adds survivors with distinct skill trees. These aren’t just re-skins; their special abilities incentivize you to rethink your standard play patterns. Some survivors excel at crowd control, some boost your offense in narrow corridors, and others unlock unique interactions with the new loot tokens. Expect more moments where a well-timed ability can turn a bread-and-butter horde into a manageable skirmish. The net effect is a few more decision trees per turn, which is exactly the spice that a veteran co-op game sometimes needs.

### Additional zombies and new threats
The new zombie variants in Friends and Foes introduce windows of vulnerability you can exploit—if you’re sharp enough to spot them. The more cunning undead types demand you to split your attention between objective progress and preventing a swift, fatal ambush. It’s a nice reminder that in Zombicide, it’s never just about how many zombies you kill; it’s about how many you keep from swarming your survivors in a single turn.

### New objectives and dynamic events
The mission cards in this expansion push you to balance speed with caution. Some missions reward you for planning around specific zones, while others punish you for getting confident and trying to sprint to the finish line. The dynamic events—twists tied to timer-like mechanics—introduce a pseudo-plot that can flip the entire board state in a single card reveal. If you thought the base game was a rollercoaster, consider Friends and Foes the seatbelt that makes that ride feel both safer and more terrifying.

### Loot and equipment evolution
With more loot on the board, players can construct more varied survivorship builds. You’ll see combinations that encourage riskier moves with compensating protection. The new items often have synergy with the new survivors, which creates an impressive amount of “one more turn” momentum without sliding into pure random luck.

### Rules learning curve and house rules
For some groups, adding new rules can feel like starting a new game. The good news is that Friends and Foes is designed to be friendly to players who know the base game well. The booklet provides concise clarifications and keeps core mechanics intact, minimizing table time spent on “how does this exact interaction work?” The result is a smoother onboarding for new players and a more exciting upgrade path for veterans.

## Gameplay experience: what it feels like at the table

In practice, Friends and Foes injects a playful chaos into your typical Zombicide round. You’ll find yourself juggling more ability combos, plotting a multi-turn plan that can pivot on a single die roll, and feeling that familiar tingle of dread when a reinforcement timer pings and you realize the horde might overwhelm you before you can complete the next objective. The expansion tends to reward coordination and risk assessment in a way that the base game doesn’t necessarily force on you every session.

If you love planning, you’ll enjoy the new survivor abilities that reward you for setting up favors with teammates. If you enjoy chaos, you’ll revel in the new zombie dynamics that push you to re-evaluate safe zones and retreat paths. If you’re a completionist who wants the entire Zombicide Black Plague experience, this expansion is a compact but potent addition that makes your investment feel justified on a long-playing evening.

One thing to note: the additional components do add table space requirements and create a longer setup. If your playgroup tends to have a shorter gaming window, you might find yourself muttering about “one more mission” as the night wraps around the clock. That said, the sense of momentum and the satisfaction of pulling off a hard-earned victory with the new rules is absolutely worth a little extended prep.

## Strategies and tips for maximizing Friends and Foes

- Diversify your survivor pool early. The new roles reward you for using complementary abilities in tandem rather than a single overpowered build. Build a mini-squad that covers crowd control, objective acceleration, and loot collection, and watch your odds of success rise as you juggle priorities.
- Map awareness is king. The new mission cards often hinge on controlling critical zones. Prioritize zone control in the early turns to deny zombies easy access to your survivors and to create bottlenecks that you can funnel the horde through.
- Don’t ignore loot variety. The new items aren’t cosmetic. Some of them unlock key synergies with the new survivors, allowing you to craft a narrative of “this turn I do the big, flashy move,” followed by “the next turn I clean up the leftovers with a precise poke here.” The payoff is a well-orchestrated chain that feels cinematic rather than random.
- Learn the new zombie quirks. Some variants surge in strength if ignored for too long; others become more dangerous when you split your attention. Read the cards aloud during setup so everyone knows the trap doors before they step through them.
- Consider scaling. If you play with more players, the expansion scales nicely—more survivors mean you can exploit more complex synergy networks. If you’re playing with a tighter group, the expanded rules can still offer meaningful choices without turning the game into a sprawling campaign.

## The big questions: Is it worth it?

- Is this expansion essential? No. If you already own Black Plague and are happy with it, Friends and Foes is a robust add-on that enhances variety without gutting the core experience. If you’re chasing more narrative hooks, better co-op dynamics, and a bit more crunch in decision-making, it’s a clear yes.
- Is it good for newcomers? It can be, but it’s a good idea to have at least a couple of run-throughs of the base game to understand the rhythm before introducing the expansion’s complexity. The extra rules complexity can overwhelm absolute beginners if you dump them in blind. A gentle onboarding with a couple of practice rounds is recommended.
- Is the component quality up to snuff? Yes. The new miniatures and cards match the look and feel of the original game and hold paint well. They’re not fragile, and you’ll appreciate the tactile variation with every expansion you add to your shelf.
- How does it impact balance? The expansion tends to push the game toward more strategic play rather than pure luck. It doesn’t completely flip the coin, but it nudges you toward planning, synergy, and adaptive tactics. If you enjoy balancing risk with reward, you’ll feel right at home.

Pro-tip: pair this expansion with a couple of friends who enjoy banter, because the moments where a perfectly timed turn decimates a doom-filled round are exactly the kind of memory you’ll want to retell at future gaming nights.

There are several Zombicide expansions on the market, each chasing a slightly different flavor of chaos. Friends and Foes stands out by leaning into supplementary mechanics that feel integrated rather than tacked on. Some expansions are pure add-ons that feel like new skins for old problems; others push the game toward a completely different sub-genre of co-op. This particular expansion sits in a comfortable middle-ground: enough new elements to diversify the game without fracturing the core experience. If you own multiple Zombicide sets, Friends and Foes provides a bridge to combine mechanics in a way that remains accessible and very much in the spirit of the Black Plague universe.

If you’re contemplating which expansion to grab first, this one earns bonus points for the way it respects the balance of the base game. It doesn’t try to reinvent the wheel; it gives you a better wheel, with a few new gears to jam in for good measure.

## Production notes and accessibility

The rulebook remains approachable for most players who know the base game. If you’ve never actually played Zombicide, you’ll want to start with the core set or a beginner-friendly session before trying to integrate a bunch of new components. The barrier to entry is a bit higher here, but the payoff is a more intricate, rewarding experience once you’ve wrapped your head around the new interactions.

From a accessibility standpoint, the game still relies heavily on color-coded icons and text on cards. For players with color vision differences, it’s worth verifying that the symbols are sufficiently distinct and that the board layout doesn’t rely on color alone for critical cues. As with most tabletop hobby games, a little pre-game setup with all players checking the iconography goes a long way toward a smooth session.
