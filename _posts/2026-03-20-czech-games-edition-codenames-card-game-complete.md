## How Codenames Card Game actually plays
### Setup
- Shuffle the agent cards and place a specified number face-down in a row? No, no, this is a card game, not a one-row magic trick. In Codenames Card Game, you deal a hand of clue cards to the clue-giver and lay out your own board of face-up cards that represent the agents you must identify. The number of face-up cards is usually fixed by the rulebook and can be spicy depending on player count.
- Decide teams and assign a clue-giver for the initial round. The clue-giver will work to link multiple cards with a single clue while avoiding the assassin or cards that belong to the other team.
- Flip, draw, and place tokens to mark the status of each agent card as your team claims them.

The setup time is snappy, ideal for newcomers who want a quick taste without belching a wall of rules at you. The card-based form factor means you can unpack and start playing in under five minutes, especially if your brain is already tuned to pattern recognition and weird wordplay.

### Card types and turn structure
- Clue cards: The clue-giver plays a clue card to indicate a number and a topic concept. For example, a clue like SLEEP 3 might indicate three agent cards that fit sleep-related words. The number tells how many cards you think you can safely identify with that clue.
- Agent cards: The face-up cards that players try to identify. Each has a word and a color-coded tint to indicate team ownership.
- Assassin card: Hitting the assassin ends the round in catastrophic fashion for the team that revealed it. The tension here is real and deliciously cruel—one misstep and you watch the cheers drift into groans.
- End-of-round tokens: Some variants use tokens to track progress, while others rely on simply identifying the final cards to win.

Turn flow is fairly straightforward: clue-giver states the clue and number, teammates discuss, and one player attempts to point to the cards that match the clue. The action resolves by flipping or revealing those cards and updating the board state. On a success, you stay in control and press your luck with a new clue; on a misstep, your opponents may pounce or you lose a turn, depending on the house rules you establish before the first game—the pre-game banter about rule minutiae is almost a ritual in Geeknite gatherings.

## Strategy tips and playstyle notes
- Start broad, then refine: A clue that hits five or six cards is fireworks if you are sure you won t hit the assassin, but don t be reckless. If you can safely identify a large, coherent cluster of cards, go for it. If not, shrink your ambition and nudge with a safer one.
- Know your team: Learn your teammates’ thinking patterns. Some players prefer literal connections, others enjoy loose associations. Tailor your clues to your audience to maximize hits and minimize ambiguity.
- Use the assassin as a strategic force: A clue that edges close to the assassin is a risk, but with a clever interpretation you can guide your team to victory while the enemy stares in disbelief. It requires a steady hand and a little showmanship.
- Word knowledge matters: The stronger your team’s vocabulary and cultural references, the more potent your clues become. But beware of ambiguous words that could trip up well-intentioned teammates. Ground yourself in shared experiences to minimize misreads.
- Manage tempo: The Card Game lends itself to quick rounds, which is perfect for a party vibe. If you sense fatigue or confusion creeping in, call for a quick break and let the room reset its energy. The best takeaways often happen in the short, intense rounds rather than marathon sessions.
