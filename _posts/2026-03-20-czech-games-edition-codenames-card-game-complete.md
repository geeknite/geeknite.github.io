## How Codenames Card Game actually plays
### Setup
- Shuffle the agent cards and lay out a board of face-up cards that represent the agents you must identify. Unlike the original, you don’t arrange a grid; you arrange a board of agents that’s ready to be interrogated by clue-givers.
- Decide teams and assign a clue-giver for the initial round. The clue-giver will attempt to link multiple cards with a single clue while avoiding the assassin and the opponent’s words.
- Flip, draw, and place tokens to mark the status of each agent card as your team claims them.

Setup time is brisk, which is perfect for newcomers who want a quick taste without drowning in rules. The card-based form factor also means you can be playing in under five minutes, especially if your brain is already tuned to pattern recognition and quirky wordplay.

### Card types and turn structure
- Clue cards: The clue-giver plays a clue card to indicate a number and a topic concept. For example, a clue like SLEEP 3 might signal three agent cards that fit sleep-related words. The number tells how many cards you think you can safely identify with that clue.
- Agent cards: The face-up cards you’re hunting. Each has a word and a color-coded tint indicating ownership by a team.
- Assassin card: If revealed, it ends the round in dramatic fashion for the team that drew it. The suspense is real and deliciously cruel—one misstep and you watch the room shift from exhilaration to groans.
- End-of-round tokens: Some variants track progress with tokens; others rely on identifying all the final cards to win.

Turn flow is straightforward: clue-giver states the clue and number, teammates debate, and one player draws a line to the matching cards. If the team hits, they press their luck with another clue. If they miss, the opposing team may pounce—house rules define the level of chaos introduced by that miss.

## Strategy tips and playstyle notes
- Start broad, then refine: A clue hitting five or six cards can be fireworks if you’re confident about avoiding the assassin, but don’t be reckless. If you can plausibly identify a large, coherent cluster of cards, go for it. If not, shrink your ambition and go for a safer spread.
- Know your team: Learn teammates’ thinking patterns. Some players crave literal connections; others love looser associations. Tailor clues to your audience to maximize hits and minimize misreads.
- Use the assassin strategically: Clues that skim the edge of the assassin are risky, but with a clever reinterpretation you can guide your team to victory while the opponents squirm. A steady hand and showmanship help.
- Word knowledge matters: A strong vocabulary and shared cultural references amplify clue potency. Be mindful of ambiguous words that could trip up well-meaning teammates. Ground yourself in shared experiences to minimize confusion.
- Manage tempo: Rounds are brisk by design. If fatigue or confusion creeps in, call a quick reset and reframe the energy. The best moments often come in short, intense bursts rather than marathon sessions.
- Be mindful of colorblind players: The color cues on agent cards are a core mechanic. If you’re playing with colorblind folks, establish a quick alternative indicator (e.g., symbols or text labels) before the first game to keep everyone in on the fun.
- Use variants to tailor difficulty: If the room is humming with energy, try a speed round or a two-team scramble to raise the stakes. If you need a calmer vibe, a cooperative or solo variant can focus the room’s attention without trampling it.
