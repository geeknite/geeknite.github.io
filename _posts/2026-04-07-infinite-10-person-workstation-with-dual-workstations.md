## The concept of infinite modularity
### Why modularity matters
A single, fixed desk for ten people collapses under its own ambition, but modular blocks? They scale like a well-behaved software project. You can remove a block for a stand-alone meeting, add a block for a bigger team, or rearrange the layout to support cross-functional standups. The engineering team behind this desk clearly read every blog about furniture design and then decided to do the opposite of what most do: make it easy to expand.

### The dual workstation concept
The two clusters offer privacy without isolation, collaboration without chaos. The distance between adjacent workstations preserves personal space, yet the spine creates a shared stage for the standups and demos. The result is a workspace that supports both deep work and quick alignment, a rare balance in modern offices.

The main frame uses extruded aluminum and powder-coated steel, with options for tempered glass surfaces and a selection of finishes. The colorways lean industrial, but there are subtle variations in surface texture to prevent the whole unit from looking like you’re staring at a high-tech fridge.

## Ergonomics and comfort
### Sit-stand flexibility
The desk supports height-adjustable legs, and you can install sit-stand mechanisms on both clusters. The key is to avoid creating a “rollercoaster of desks” effect where some people are permanently tuned to stand and others are stuck in a constant desk mode.

### Monitor and keyboard geometry
The two clusters accommodate multi-monitor setups with arms that extend up to 800–1000 mm. You can arrange monitors in a two-row matrix or a more relaxed L-shape depending on the tasks. Keyboard trays offer generous space and finger-friendly edges. The central spine provides an anchor for a shared numeric keypad or a compact keypad for those who cannot type without a curious flourish.

### Foot space and mobility
With ten chairs, legroom becomes a sensitive topic. The design ensures decent knee clearance for all participants, though you’ll want to avoid placing heavy equipment under some chairs that could impede movement. The floor area must be measured to prevent collisions during spontaneous sprint planning or mid-stand huddles.

## Technology compatibility and integrations
### Power and data management
The dual rails deliver power to each cluster and route data lines separately to avoid crosstalk. Expect a dedicated network patch panel for each cluster in larger configurations. If your workspace uses PoE devices or conferencing cameras, there are provisions for those as well.

### Monitor walls and peripherals
For teams that lean into collaboration, a central monitor wall is possible with optional mounting hardware. You can feed content across two clusters for side-by-side demonstrations, or keep them independent for secure testing.

### Audio and video for collaboration
Optional mic arrays and speaker bars can be installed for conference calls. The design minimizes echo and ensures that the person at the far end of your group feels present rather than like they’re listening to a conference call simulation.
