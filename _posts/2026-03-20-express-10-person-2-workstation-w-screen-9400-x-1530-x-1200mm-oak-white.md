## Assembly and Installation: From A to Z, With a Manual That Probably Has a Flowchart

No one truly enjoys the assembly phase. It’s the adult version of a LEGO build that comes with a warranty and fewer little plastic pieces that end up under the sofa for weeks. The Express system ships in modular components, designed for easier handling and a more predictable unpack-and-assemble flow. Expect a couple of professionals or a determined duo to get things standing with a couple of hours of careful alignment and a lot of “does this thing actually fit here?” moments.

### Pre-delivery prep
Before the forklift arrives (figuratively speaking), take inventory of the space. Measure doorways, ensure ceiling clearance, and map power locations. A floor plan drawing helps prevent the “Your chair is in the janitor’s closet” moment that happens when you misread a doorway clearance while carrying a 300-pound panel.

### Step-by-step assembly (highlights)
1) Unpack components and verify parts against the hardware kit.2) Attach legs to work surfaces and secure crossbars, ensuring level surfaces.3) Mount screen panels or screen arms if included, double-checking for secure fit.4) Route cables through the provided channels or under-desk trays.5) Connect power and test each workstation for stability and screen operation.6) Adjust height and tilt to match ergonomic targets.7) Final walk-through: confirm space between rows, ensure waste collection is accessible, and collect feedback from the team about the vibe.8) Optional calibration: run a mock standup with a few participants to assess line-of-sight and screen positions.

Latency in real life is not a thing you want in ergonomic design; you want predictability. The Express system is generally predictable—if you follow the manual and allow a slow, careful build rather than a frantic “we’ll figure it out on the spot” approach.

### Cable management and power planning
One of the unsung heroes of any large workstation is cable management. The Express system includes channels and trays designed to route power and data lines cleanly along the structural beams. A tidy cable solution not only looks better but reduces the risk of accidental unplugging during those intense brainstorming sessions where someone leans back in their chair with more enthusiasm than awareness. If you’ve ever tripped over a power strip that looked like a small black sculpture, you’ll appreciate a thoughtful approach here. Also, plan for surge protection and consider a centralized power solution if your space hosts more devices than a mid-range data closet.
