## In the Box and Build Quality
The Fortune Row figurines arrive in a compact blister with a glossy print of neon skyscrapers and rain-slick cobblestones. If you like your boards to tell a story before you roll a single die, this packaging delivers. The miniatures themselves are cast in resin (with a few metal bits as optional add-ons) and come on tiny bases that align with a 25-28mm standard, depending on the pose. The casting is clean; there are minimal mold lines (which means less sandpaper tears and more time to paint) and the joints are discrete enough that you can swap arms or heads if you fancy a Frankenstein-meets-Noir vibe.

The line favors dynamic poses rather than stiff stances. One of the figures leans into a cigarette-haze pose, another balances on the edge of a neon canal sign, and a third is mid-step, as if caught mid-chase. The sculptors clearly aimed for motion—something City of Mist has always leaned into: movement as narrative propulsion. That said, there are a couple of character choices whose silhouettes blur at the small scale; you might need to tilt your camera a touch to catch the details in a crowded scene. This is not a knock; it’s the nature of 28-30mm urban silhouettes in high-contrast lighting.

The bases are scenic but not overpowering: a mix of cobbles, a toppled hat, a scattering of casino chips as micro-art. The base art is a nice touch because it gives you a stage for storytelling without needing to build an entire diorama around them. If you want to push things further, you can pin a few of these to a custom base or resin-tabletop piece and call it a scene from Fortune Row as if the city itself dictated the plot.

Now, the materials: resin vs metal, weight, and handling. Resin is forgiving for beginners since it typically accepts a wider margin for paint and weathering mishaps. It also tends to be lighter, which means you can stage a crowd on the gaming table without your opponent's eye rolling at the weight of a small mobile home. The downside is fragility; resin can chip more easily than metal, and you should handle the figures with care when removing from their molds and priming. The five-character set we tested held up under gentle handling, and the joint areas were robust enough to withstand a few test pose adjustments. In short, the build quality sits comfortably in the “good-to-great” range for the price point.

### The Cast and the Character Archetypes
The Fortune Row lineup is built around archetypes that every noir city knows well: the schemer, the oracle, the enforcer, the trickster, and the chronicler of dark luck. Here are the quick profiles you’ll find in the set:

#### The Gambler of Fortune Row
A swaggering figure with a long coat, a sly smile, and a fist full of counterfeit coins. This sculpt celebrates risk, not just in-game mechanics but in narrative: every roll is a moral choice, every bet a possibility that the city might tilt in your favor or at least buy you a drink. The gambler's base features a few casino chips that are small enough to look cute but not so tiny that you’ll lose them behind your couch cushions.

#### The Oracle of Fortune Row
This one holds a crystal sphere that glows under certain light, hinting at a fortune and misfortune dynamic that City of Mist loves. The pose is calm but not passive; the figure leans forward, implying a willingness to share secrets—if you can keep up with the price of those secrets. The gestural detail on the robe folds reads well under a standard wash and dry brush technique.

#### The Street Prophet
Mid-stride, eyes forward, pointing toward a future that may be written in rain. The Street Prophet gives you a sense of urgency, which is essential for a tabletop moment where time is a currency you can burn on a single dramatic reveal. The base includes a rain-slick pavement texture that reads strongly at table distance.

#### The Shade
A cloaked figure with a wide-brim hat, half-hidden behind a shadow that seems to spill from the base like a noir fog. This figure is all silhouette and menace, perfect for scenes where someone’s secret is about to come out. The cloak’s folds are generous, offering multiple passable highlight colors without losing the creaturely silhouette.

#### The Mechanist
A workshop-stained tinker in mid-tooling pose. The Mechanist injects a little sense of tech-noir into Fortune Row, and its gear-laden appearance invites players to bring a few sci-fi elements into the city’s otherwise mystical vibe. The mechanical details are clean and readable, even at low light, which is a big plus if you run late-night campaigns online.

Taken together, these five miniatures cover a spectrum: evoke mystery, impulse, risk, and cunning. They don’t bend the city’s mythology to your will; they bend your storytelling to the city’s will, which is a subtle but powerful difference when you’re aiming for a long arc rather than a one-shot grind.

## Painting and Assembly Tips
If you’re a veteran painter, you’ll spot a handful of choices that will make these figures pop without sinking endless hours into them. If you’re new, fear not: you can achieve great results with a simple workflow that emphasizes contrast, edge highlighting, and a touch of weathering around the base.

- Prep and prime: A light gray or neutral dark primer will reveal details without saturating the model. If you want a more dramatic effect, a thin black spray across the entire model can do wonders for the noir vibe.
- Base colors: Start with a mid-tone palette. The Fortune Row figures reward a two-tone approach for clothing (outer coat and inner garment) plus accent metals. Use a warm gray for metallics and a cool gray for stone base details to create depth.
- Shading and highlights: Apply a thin wash to the recesses to bring out the folds. Dry-brushing the raised edges with a lighter shade creates a crisp edge that catches the table lamp. Don’t overdo it; the line thrives on moody contrasts rather than comic-book brightness.
- Freehand and signage: If you want to push the casino-chip motif, try a tiny dot pattern or a micro sign to simulate engraved metal or lacquer. The characters’ insignia can be freehanded or decaled depending on your trickery with micromesh.
- Weathering: A light rust wash on the Mechanist’s tools and a damp grime layer on the Street Prophet’s coat will give you a lived-in look that fits a city where every coin might be bad luck if you stare at it long enough.
- Sealing: A matte sealant helps keep the paint from rubbing away during table talk. If you’re streaming, consider a satin finish to keep the highlights readable on camera without washing out detail.

For players who want to swap poses between sessions, you’ll be happy to know the joints are robust but still compatible with pinning for the more ambitious conversion fanatics. If you like the idea of a “gambler with a brass knuckle” variant, you can mix-and-match parts from other kits—provided you’re comfortable with a few gaps that need filling. The result, however, is a set that can be painted to a range of levels—from quick tabletop quality to showpiece-grade display pieces.

- Faction cues: Each character has a vibe that communicates allegiance or motivation to the players at a glance, which is essential in a world where loyalties shift as often as a neon sign flickers.

For narrative flow, consider designing a scene deck or cue cards that reference each figure’s personality and backstory. The game’s fiction-forward approach plays nicely with physical miniatures because you can point to a figure and ask a player, “What does The Gambler bet on this turn?” That question alone can propel the session forward more reliably than a stubborn dice roll.
