### Painting and assembly tips
If you’re a painter who likes speed painting, this pack is a dream and a minor nightmare at the same time. Dream because you can batch-paint fifteen minis in a weekend and feel like a hero; nightmare because fifteen may require a little more coffee than your usual Sunday.
- Clean the minis first: a quick soak in mild soapy water and a gentle scrub will remove the releasing agents. Dry thoroughly before assembly or priming.
- Assemble as needed: the runners are usually snapped into standard gators; you’ll probably need a hobby knife to shave mold lines and a tiny file to smooth any rough edges.
- Prime in layers: start with black primer to catch shadows, then dry brush or airbrush lighter tones to give each figure depth.
- Color schemes: a common approach is a grayscale undercoat followed by washes for depth. You can do bone, torn clothes, and exposed flesh in distinct layers to avoid one dimensional look.
- Flesh tones: avoid pure pink; zombie skin tends to be muted with greens, yellows, and browns to mimic decomposition and grime.
- Clothing textures: shredded fabrics respond well to thin washes; use slightly watered-down colors to bring out the textures without pooling into creases.
- Highlights: finish with sharp highlights on teeth, eyes (if any visible), and sharp bone protrusions.
- Basing: a simple textured base with grit and static grass can make the whole group feel integrated with a dungeon or ruined city.

### Tabletop use and rules impact
The Zombie Runner pack shines on the tabletop. They’re not the tanky brutes; they’re the fast-moving blips you can barely track on a busy turn. In Zombicide Black Plague, runners usually spawn and chase your survivors, providing a fast-moving threat that can overwhelm you if you ignore them. Fifteen runners give you an immediate sense of danger in the early game and can shape the pacing of a scenario.

For new players, the key is to space them suitably so they feel like a threat rather than a mere obstacle. If your table is small, you may not want fifteen running at full pace down a single corridor; stagger them, split into two groups, or set up a multi-floor scenario where they ascend from staircases. You’ll get the best dramatic effect if you have multiple lanes, with runners pouring toward risk points—gateways, doors, and choke points.
