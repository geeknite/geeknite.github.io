## Setup tips and best practices
- Clear the area: Give the trampoline a few feet of clearance on all sides. If you’re near furniture, move things away. If you’re near a pet, prepare for interviews about “who owns the living room.”
- Check the ground: If you’re setting up indoors, choose a flat, hard surface or use a protective mat to save your floors and keep the frame balanced. Outdoors? A level lawn or driveway works, but you’ll want a ground cover to protect the mat from sharp twigs and stray lego pieces.
- Follow the sequence: The manual usually recommends attaching the safety net before the padding goes on. Mirror the step-by-step instructions so you don’t end up with a net that refuses to zip because a bolt is complaining about life choices.
- Tighten gradually: When you’re attaching springs, do a little at a time in a star pattern. It’s like giving a hug to a bear—gentle, balanced, and with a touch of trepidation for the inevitable squeak.
- Check regularly: After a few weeks of use, re-tighten bolts and inspect padding for wear. A quick maintenance check prevents mid-playtime drama and keeps you from sprinting to the hardware store at 7 PM on a Sunday.

## Indoor vs outdoor use: what changes, really?
Indoors, the trampoline becomes your kid’s personal gym—minus the overhead cable, plus the fun of being able to bounce and shout at the same time. Indoors, you’ll likely rely more on safety padding and floor mats to protect fragile floors from the occasional enthusiastic leap.

Outdoors, you get a larger sense of freedom. The weatherproof frame is designed to handle sun and rain, but avoid prolonged exposure to water on the jumping surface. Prolonged moisture can degrade padding and connect the dots toward a less-than-perfect bounce. If you want to leave it outside, consider a weather cover when not in use and ensure the ground is flat and clear of debris.
