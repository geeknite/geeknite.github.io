### The "NOS" and the "NEW" Conundrum

The label NOS NEW is a collector’s dream, conjuring visions of packaging that has never been opened, never been breathed upon by a curious toddler with sticky fingers. In practice, NOS means Mint on Card (or in some cases, New Old Stock), and NEW means the box remains factory-fresh enough to make you nostalgic for the smell of fresh paper. In this 1985 UNO deck, the real magic lies not in the gameplay, but in the packaging. The box may have a few scuffs, the plastic wrap may crackle a little with age, but the deck inside is often untouched—like a time capsule that has survived a dozen plus decades without ever being played. For some collectors, this is the dream; for others, it’s a reminder that life is messy and cards get shuffled into the annals of history.

## Condition notes: NOS-NEW in the modern world

- Mint condition: If the box shows no signs of wear, the shrink wrap is intact, and the interior cards aren’t bent, you have what collectors call a near-mint or mint deck. The downside of mint decks is the price, which can hop around with market demand and how often someone posts a new listing about it on a retro-gaming forum.
- Grading: Professional graders look at corners, surface sheen, and the evenness of color. For the layperson, the key is whether the deck can still be shuffled without creasing or tearing. A good shuffle is a sign that the cards haven’t spent too much time in a humid closet or 1990s gamer’s backpack.
- Common issues: If stored properly, you might not encounter major surface wear. However, if you find minor box creases, a light yellowing of the inner card edges, or a small amount of edge wear, these are normal aging signs and don’t necessarily ruin the experience. Collectors often value these imperfect but honest signs of a deck that has survived the test of time more than a pristine, unplayed deck kept in a climate-controlled vault.

## Care, storage, and minor restoration tips

If you want to keep your 1985 UNO deck looking as cool as the day it boxed itself into Woolworth, here are a few practical tips:

- Environment: Store away from direct sunlight, excessive humidity, and heat. A cool, dry closet is your deck's best friend. If you have a dedicated game shelf, consider keeping the box upright to minimize pressure on the deck.
- Handling: Use clean hands when handling the cards. Oils and sweat can accelerate wear on the card edges, especially with the older finish. For the rare moment you actually shuffle, consider a gentle riffle rather than a hard shuffle to preserve edge integrity.
- Display: If you plan to display, consider a light acrylic case. You want to keep dust away while letting guests admire the box art and the aura of vintage 80s packaging without actually opening the original seal.
- Cleaning: If you need to clean the interior, use a soft brush or a microfiber cloth very gently. Avoid moisture; the last thing you want is a sticky situation with a deck that refuses to slide properly.
