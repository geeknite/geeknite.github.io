## What is CODENAMES, and why is factory-sealed relevant in 2026?
CODENAMES is a word-based party game designed by Vlaada Chvátil and published by Czech Games Edition (CGE). The core idea is simple on the surface: two teams compete to connect their agents (cards in a grid) to their team-color keys via single-word clues given by their spymaster. The kicker? You’re trying to avoid connecting the opposing team’s agents, the innocent bystanders, and the dreaded assassin—who, if you draw them, ends the round immediately in a glorious, chaotic swing of the night.

## How CODENAMES actually plays: a quick setup and a first round walkthrough
CODENAMES is designed for two teams, usually separated by a board state that balances the puzzle and the social dynamic. Here’s a practical walkthrough to help you picture the first round, using a factory-sealed setup as the baseline:

- Setup: Lay out 25 Word Cards in a 5x5 grid in the center of the table. Shuffle and place the Key Card face-down so everyone can’t peek at the key unless you want to stretch the suspense. Decide which team will start—the starting team gets 9 agents on the board, the other team gets 8, plus the 7 innocent bystanders and 1 assassin scattered within the 25 cards. The rest of the color agent cards stay off to the side.
- Roles: Each team designates a Spymaster (the clue-giver) and field operatives (the guessers). Spymasters sit so their teammates can’t read their lips—this is a subtlety of the social contract that adds to the hilarity and occasional chaos.
- Turn flow: On a turn, the Spymaster gives one word as a clue plus a number. The clue word must connect to as many of the Word Cards as possible, without crossing into associated cards of the opposing team or the assassin. For example, if the grid contains words like “Star,” “Fleet,” and “Moon,” a clever clue could be “Space 3” to hint at multiple space-themed words.
- Guessing: Teammates discuss and choose Word Cards to flip over. If they reveal a red agent on a red-spymaster turn, they stay in play; blue agent cards are flipped but give the other team a moment of satisfaction and strategic misstep. Innocent bystanders and the assassin end turns in different ways: hitting an innocent bystander ends the turn with no extra effect, while hitting the assassin ends the game immediately and can be brutal or hilarious depending on your group’s temperament.
- Endgame: The game ends when the first team has all of its agents revealed, or when the assassin is flipped. The tension in that final couple of turns is where CODENAMES shines—the misdirection, the laughter, the frantic debates, and the occasional revelation that your clue “Animal 6” was too close to a real animal and your team still didn’t see it coming.

This is the heart of CODENAMES: a simple mechanism (one-word clues and a count) that yields wildly complex social dynamics and a lot of laugh-out-loud moments.

## Strategy basics: how to approach clues like a tactful wordsmith (without breaking your sense of humor)
CODENAMES rewards a few core strategic ideas, even in casual play. Here are some practical tips to elevate your game from “meh” to “wow, that was suspiciously clever.”

- Plan for your team, not yourself: A great clue works because it helps your teammates cross multiple relevant words in a single turn. Think about the grid layout and identify which words share a common theme that can be cleverly linked by a single word.
- The number matters: The clue number tells your team how many words you’re hinting at. If you say “Space 4,” you’re signaling that up to four words might be connected to that clue. If your team flips two words correctly and one is the assassin, you might want to adjust your future clues to be more precise.
- Avoid overly broad clues: A clue like “Food 10” might hit ten relevant words in an enormous grid, but it also runs the risk of hitting the other team’s words or the assassin. Precision is more valuable than sweeping generality.
- Read the table, not the box: A good spymaster pays attention to nonverbal signals and the dynamics of the room. If your team is whispering or hesitating, your clue choice can help or hinder them. Part of CODENAMES’ charm is reading a room as well as reading a word.
- The “innocent bystander” safety net: Having 7 bystanders means you have a buffer, but every bystander adds to the drama. If your clue nudges the team toward a bystander, you’re inflating the tension without scoring a single point. The best clues minimize the number of bystander cards hit while maximizing correct guesses.
- The assassin: This is where the game teaches you restraint. A misstep here can flip the night from a high-five to a groan. It’s a reminder that sometimes the best move is to ask for a clarifying second opinion within your team, or to choose a safer word with fewer overlapping connections.
