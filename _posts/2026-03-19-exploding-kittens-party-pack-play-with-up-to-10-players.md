## How the game plays when you have a crowd
### The turn structure (quick refresher)
A typical Exploding Kittens round is simple in principle but surprisingly nuanced in practice. On your turn, you play a card from your hand that does something—usually helping you dodge an exploding kitten, forcing an opponent to draw, or taming a wild card that changes the odds. The See the Future card, for example, lets you peek at top few cards to inform your next play. Defuse cards save you from kabooms, and there are several other card types that shuffle, peek, or rearrange the deck. The Party Pack adds a few extra cards to spice up the late-game chaos when the table is 8, 9, or 10 players deep.

The objective remains simple: avoid drawing the exploding kitten. When you draw an exploding kitten and you don’t have a defuse, you’re out of the round. The last feline-fearing survivor wins, which is usually enough to trigger cheers, groans, and the occasional overdramatic victory pose.

### Why the ten-player cap matters
The typical base version of Exploding Kittens is great for a handful of players, but the Party Pack wants to prove it can maintain tension, humor, and a sense of peril at scale. With up to ten players, you get a social dynamic that’s hard to replicate: the table becomes a living, breathing web of bluffs, misdirections, and accidental cat-related chaos. Table talk multiplies, alliances form and dissolve in seconds, and you learn which players are willing to sacrifice their dignity for the sake of a dramatic finale. It’s not subtle, but it is glorious.

## Rules, strategy, and quick tips for big groups
### Basic strategy you can actually use (even if you are not a card-slinging pro)
- Save defuse cards for the right moment. The instinct to play a defuse early is tempting, but holding onto it for a crucial moment can turn the tide when the last few players are sweating bullets.
- Pay attention to who is being bluffed. The social deduction aspect, while not explicit, is alive and well. If someone consistently underplays a card, they may be setting you up for a trap later.
- Don’t panic when you’re behind. A well-timed See the Future or a mass-attack card can reset the momentum and make a late-game recovery possible.
- Use the party’s energy to your advantage. In large groups, the social pressure to react quickly can push others to misplay. Stay calm, watch the table, and seize opportunities when they appear.

### House rules and optional twists for even more chaos
- The Midnight Snack Rule: allow a cheeky “meal break” card that resets the round mid-play for snacks and giggles—great for longer sessions.
- The Double Defuse: if two players hold defuse cards, they can combine to save someone who would otherwise be knocked out. This one multiplies the tension in the best way possible.
- Snark Mode: grant a player a chance to swap their hand with any other player once per game, but with a consequence that they must perform a goofy victory dance if they win the round. It works surprisingly well for keeping energy high.
