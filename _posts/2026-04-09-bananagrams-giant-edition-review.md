## Gameplay: how it actually plays out on the giant stage
### Basic rules refresher (for the giant crowd)
- Shuffle the tiles and deal a set number to each player. The exact number is adjustable by your household’s appetite for risk—more tiles means more opportunity for glorious disaster.
- Players race to assemble words using their tiles, spelling them left-to-right, top-to-bottom, stacking like alphabetic Legos.
- When someone uses all their tiles, everyone does a celebratory clack and the “peel” mechanic is activated (draw a new tile from the bag and continue).
- The game ends when the bag is empty and one player has used all their tiles. The person with the fewest tiles left is the grammar god of the room.

The giant edition tilts the pace toward a more physical, in-your-face experience. You’re not silently pondering the possible letter combos on a tiny board; you’re physically maneuvering a tile the size of a large cookie, often while three different people attempt to assemble a competing word formation in parallel. It’s chaotic, it’s chaotic, and did I mention how it’s chaotic? It’s chaos with the charm of a well-timed pun.

### Strategy: not all bananas are created equal
- Early-round aggression pays off. Start constructing short, high-frequency words that can be expanded later. The giants can adapt; your brain can too—if you give it coffee and occasionally a banana-flavored treat (for inspiration, obviously).
- Aim for flexible words. If you can pivot from “cat” to “act” or “tac” with minimal tile shuffling, you’ll secure psychological brownie points. In giant mode, word stability equals table stability; collapsing towers are for architecture students, not for family game night.
- Balance speed and correctness. It’s tempting to try to jam in a dozen long words, but a well-placed three- or four-letter tile cluster can be your secure anchor while you fearlessly peel your way to victory.
- Tile management matters. Distribute your vowels and consonants in a way that keeps future words accessible. The giant tiles will test your inventory management skills more than your math does; you’ll learn to treat the rack like a tiny space colony with linguistic needs.
