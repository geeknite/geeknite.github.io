The 18 red decks vs 17 blue decks
The math is undeniable: 18 red and 17 blue. On first thought, this seems like a trivial nuance, yet it matters in certain scenarios. If you are hosting a tournament with two teams, or you are teaching two groups with color-coded instructions, the imbalance can be a feature, not a flaw. It lets you run a small side quest like the red team vs the blue team, where the extras of one color can be used to seed the tournament bracket, or to keep a spare deck on standby for those moments when your magician friend instantly loses a deck mid-trick.

In practice, this distribution means you can conduct a mini experiment: give red decks to one group and blue decks to another, then line up a sequence of games to see if color fatigue or suit recognition plays any role in decision-making. The truth is, for experienced players this seldom matters; for kids or beginners, color variety is a gentle teaching tool that reduces confusion. And if you are a collector who wants to stage an elaborate color-coded display, this set gives you a neat reason to line up 18 red boxes in a row on a shelf and pretend you are curating a gallery of red decks. The aesthetics of red and blue are timeless, and the standard face design keeps things legible even when you have to recall which deck you used last night to perform a gravity-defying card flourish.

Stock, finish, and handling
The stock in these decks sits in the comfortable middle ground: sturdy enough to survive a weekend gaming session, flexible enough to allow a satisfying riffle. The finish is the familiar Bicycle air-cushion, which means you get a consistent snap when you shuffle, a slight resistance that signals you are about to perform some real card manipulation, and a comfortable surface that glides rather than claws. If you are a cardist, you will appreciate the control you get at the fingertip, and if you are a casual gamer you will notice that the deck does not fight back when you try to grip, peel, or drop the cards. Durability is the name of the game here; these stock choices are designed for repeated use, not one-off miracles, and they age gracefully.

Shuffling and card longevity
Shuffling these decks is where the real test begins. With 35 decks, you are not just shuffling a single powder keg of 52 cards; you are potentially shuffling an entire warehouse of 1,820 cards (yes, math matters here). The process of shuffling one deck in a friendly manner is one thing; doing it across 35 decks is another, but fear not, the general reliability holds. The air-cushion finish helps to prevent sticking, which means you can perform long sequences of riffle shuffles, bridge shuffles, and false shuffles without the stacks binding together. The longevity question is important: will these decks show signs of wear after hundreds of shuffles? Likely yes, but the kind of wear you get is typical of standard face Bicycle stock: modest edge wear on the pips, some mild corner wear, and a little bit of fade on the red/blue inks after many sessions. If you are running nightly magic shows or a weekend board game marathon, you can expect these to last longer than the typical trip to the thrift store.

Card design and readability

For readers who prefer alt-arts or jokers with flair, you might find that these 35 decks share the same classic look. Care and maintenance
Maintenance is simple: keep the decks in a cool, dry place away from moisture; keep them away from extreme heat; avoid bending the box too aggressively; do not leave them on the dashboard of your car on a sunny summer afternoon. The standard care routine for Bicycle cards is simple and repeatable: store in the box, shuffle moderately, and enjoy. If you spill something on them, you can wipe gently with a dry cloth; avoid solvents or water, as the air-cushion finish is not a fan of soaking. When not in use, you can line up the 35 decks like tiny soldiers and admire the color-coded discipline you have created for your gaming shelf.
