The Lost Zombivors Concept

Zombivores? Zombivors? The naming in Zombicide is almost as chaotic as its zombie design, and that is part of the charm. The Lost Zombivors line has always inhabited a funny space in the canon: survivors who were shipped with a deceptive air of mystery, like they forgot to pack their own limbs and decided the best backup option was to become a zombie’s snack. In Set 7 Unused, you are getting a slice of that character concept without any of the “heavily used” patina that usually comes with post-launch mishaps.

The characters included in this box are designed to slot into a wide range of base scenarios. They have unique ability tokens, starting gear, and little flavor tips on their character cards that remind you to laugh at the irony of their situation. The flavor text is the kind of tongue-in-cheek humor that Zombicide fans have learned to love: it nods at grim violence while delivering a wink that says we’re playing a cooperative game with and against fate all at once.

What you get and how it stacks up

- 4 to 6 miniatures depending on the exact core you own (the Lost Zombivors are not always a standard 6 per set, but this box leans into that variety). Each mini is a plastic sculpt with mid-level detail: enough to tell who they are, not so much that you need a magnifying glass to find their faces on the table.
- A handful of survivor cards, each with quirky starting abilities that can tilt a scenario from “oh no” to “we might actually pull this off.” The balance between power and flavor is the hallmark of Zombicide, and Set 7 Unused sticks to that tradition with a light touch of nostalgia. If you have played other Lost Zombivors sets, you will notice the thread that ties them together is less about raw power and more about improvisation under pressure.
- Tokens and cards for the weapons and special rules that come with this box. These components are the salt in the stew; they push you to plan, coordinate, and occasionally cheat a little toward survival. If you are the kind of player who loves the meta of how to stack weapons across turns, this set gives you more hooks to DTR (die the raid) in a satisfying way.
- A rulebook snippet and scenario ideas. The scenarios aren’t printed war and peace, but they are enough to get your group excited about trying a few custom missions that suit the survivors’ quirky profiles.

Gameplay: how these survivors change your game

The Lost Zombivors Squad in Set 7 Unused is designed to create new micro-stories in your games. The survivors’ skills are usually oriented toward fast clears, stealthy moves, and a few moments of dramatic survivorship when it looks like the party is about to be overwhelmed. The real joy comes from the synergy between these survivors’ starting gear and how you decide to allocate actions across a turn. You will find yourself debating whether you should loot a building or sprint toward a zombie horde, with the survivors’ different equipment leaning the debate toward one plan or another.

One of the standout design choices here is that the set does not push the player into a single style of play. If your table loves blunt force and heavy weaponry, you will still have that option. If you prefer a more craft-y, stealthy approach, there are tools in this box that reward careful planning and risk assessment. It is the kind of balance that makes Zombicide feel both accessible to newcomers and satisfying to veteran players. The Set 7 Unused box respects that balance by leaning into variability rather than forcing you into a single map or tactic.

Box contents, quality, and the repackability question

In terms of quality, the plastics are consistent with what CMON ships for most modern Zombicide expansions. The sculpts have that crisp look you expect from a current generation kit: clear silhouette language on each miniature, readable weapon outlines, and enough texture to differentiate clothing, armor, and gear at a glance. The color palette for the Lost Zombivors is slightly muted compared to the more saturated Gloomy Night lines, which works well for tabletop visibility. On a well-lit table, you will not be squinting to tell who is who—an essential feature when you have several survivors on one board and a dozen zombies converging from different directions.

Storage and transport is where the Unused packaging shines or collapses depending on your appetite for insert trays. If you own a big Zombicide core box with inserts, Set 7 Unused will slot into those trays with minimal drama. If you’re a minimalist, you may want to dump the contents into a zip bag and pretend you are an artful ne’er-do-well who collected action figures in the dark ages. The truth is somewhere in between: the box is not a behemoth, but the content density is enough to merit a dedicated storage plan so you do not end up with a choked hobby shelf and a rising pile of missing tokens.

Unboxing notes that matter for new players

For people who are new to the hobby, there are a few practical notes worth keeping in mind. First, the miniatures are small enough that you should avoid eating near the table; the temptation to nibble a tiny limb off a tiny hero is real, and we do not recommend encouraging it. Second, the tokens can be a little fiddly; keep a small tray and a loose-leaf sheet handy so you can manage the board state without the marbles of chaos rolling away. Third, the scenario ideas are a good starting point, but the box does not come with a full rulebook update. If you are playing with new players, you will want to reference the base game rules and the Lost Zombivors rules separately, so you do not accidentally introduce a misinterpretation that derails a mission before you roll a single die.
