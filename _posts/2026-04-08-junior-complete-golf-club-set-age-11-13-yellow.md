## Size and Fit for Age 11–13
The fit of junior clubs is half fashion show, half physics experiment. If the clubs are too long, your child will end up swinging from their kneecaps and wondering why the ball is going sideways while their hips are stuck like a broken compass. If the clubs are too short, they’ll be swinging with vanity rather than velocity, and the ball will still disappoint them. The sweet spot for ages 11–13 usually sits around a 37" to 40" grip length for the irons, with the driver shaft length varying by player height and arm length.

### Shaft Options and Weight
Most junior sets offer graphite shafts because they’re lighter and easier to handle for developing swing tempos. The Yellow Lightning Edition leans into a lighter weight than adult clubs but not so light that it feels flimsy. The flex is typically designed for beginner to intermediate juniors, offering some forgiveness while still allowing the kid to build a solid swing plane. If your child’s swing speed is notably faster than expected, some sets allow you to upgrade to a slightly stiffer shaft later on without abandoning the whole club set.

### Grip Size and Comfort
Grip diameter matters almost as much as the shaft length. A grip that’s too large can force the wrists into awkward angles; one that’s too small may cause the youngster to grip too tightly and lose touch. Junior sets often feature larger, softer grips compared to adult sizes. The Yellow Lightning Edition makes an effort to balance feel and feedback so beginners don’t feel like they are swinging bare wood sticks on a windy day. If you can test grip feel in person, do it; if not, check the manufacturer’s recommended hand size or reach out to customer support for a grip-sizing chart.

## Training and Drills with the Set
No review would be complete without practical drills that use the exact equipment you own. Here are a few simple, no-fuss exercises you can do with the entire set to sharpen fundamentals without turning golf practice into a chore:

- Tempo Tug Drill: Have your junior swing with a metronome setting, focusing on a smooth transition from backswing to downswing. Use a soft tee to measure inside-out path and encourage a centered strike.
- Lag and Release Drill: With the driver or fairway woods, practice a slow backswing and a controlled release to promote a shallow angle of attack and better carry.
- Short Game Station: Use the pitching wedge and putter on a practice mat and chip to a target on a small green mat. The goal is to keep contact consistent and avoid thinning or fat shots.
- Alignment Aids: Use alignment sticks or the built-in lines on the clubface (if present) to practice directional accuracy. The goal is to create a repeatable setup routine that your child can remember on the course.
