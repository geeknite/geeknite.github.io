## What is Phase 10? Quick recap
Phase 10 is a race to complete ten distinct phases, each a specific pattern of cards. The phases are fixed and must be completed in order: Phase 1 might be two sets of two, Phase 2 a set and a run, Phase 3 two runs, Phase 4 etc. On your turn you draw a card, try to lay down your phase from your hand, optionally add to existing phases on the table, and then discard. The first player to complete Phase 10 wins the round and moves on to the next phase or ends the match if you’re playing a longer session. It’s a mash-up of Rummy, Uno, and a tiny bit of Sudoku—without the terrifying mirror of actual numbers staring back at you.

In short: you’re balancing memory, probability, and a bit of social calculus about what your friends are likely to hold. The Brand New UK edition keeps the bones of the game intact while polishing the surface for a modern audience—nicer cards, clearer rules, and a touch more British humor in the packaging. If you’ve played Phase 10 before, you’ll recognize the rhythm; if you’re new, fear not: this is a learning curve that flattens quickly into a grin.

## How to play: the core loop
Phase 10 is deceptively simple to pick up, yet offers a surprising amount of depth as you play more rounds. Here’s the skeleton:
- Each player starts with a hand of cards (the Brand New UK rules preserve this standard).
- On your turn, you draw a card from the draw pile or the discard pile.
- If you can, you lay down your current phase using cards from your hand. You may add cards to your own laid-down phases on subsequent turns, as long as you’re legally completing your phase order.
- You discard one card to end your turn.
- If you have laid down your current phase, you advance to the next phase on your next turn.
- The round ends when a player completes Phase 10; that player wins the round, or you can play multiple rounds to determine the overall winner.
- Skip cards cause the next player to lose their turn; Wild cards can substitute for any card in a phase.

The UK edition’s improved instruction set helps a lot here, especially for new players. The rhythm remains one draw, one discard, one potential play of the phase, and one dramatic moment when you decide whether to drop a Wild early or hold onto it for a desperate finish.

- Phase 6: One run of four and one set of four
- Phase 7: Two sets of four
- Phase 8: One set of five and one set of three
- Phase 9: Two sets of five
- Phase 10: One set of five and one set of four
The exact numbers can vary by edition, but the idea is consistent: you’re chasing a specific configuration of cards, and the path to victory winds through a sequence of increasingly complex patterns.

## Strategy and decision-making: thinking on your feet
Phase 10 rewards players who plan ahead and adapt on the fly. Here are some practical angles:
- Phase awareness: Always know where you are on your personal ladder. The sooner you anticipate a Phase 7 or Phase 9, the better you can set yourself up with the right combinations and Wilds.
- Wilds are gold, but use them wisely: A Wild can rescue a near-finished phase, but you’ll often need it to salvage an even tougher one later. The UK edition’s daylight-clear icons help you see a Wild’s potential at a glance, which matters when you’re juggling multiple options.
- Discard discipline: Displaying fewer tells is often better. Don’t reveal too much about what you’re chasing by discarding obvious cards too early. The group that discards strategically often wins.
- Reading the table: If your opponents are close to finishing a phase, it can be worth delaying your own finish to disrupt their rhythm—especially when you’ve got a Skip card to turn their table-dominating run into a tentative hope.
- Turn economy: Some players try to maximize the number of cards they can lay down in a single turn, which minimizes the number of turns you spend waiting for your next shot. This can backfire if you’re forced to break up your phase to use a Wild at the wrong moment.
