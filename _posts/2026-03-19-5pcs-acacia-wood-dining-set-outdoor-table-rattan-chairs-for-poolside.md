## Materials and build quality
### Acacia wood: the unsung hero
Acacia wood is dense, relatively robust, and naturally resistant to termites and rot when properly treated. It’s not teak, but it’s a more budget-friendly alternative that still handles a sunny yard and occasional rain shower with a reasonable amount of grace. Expect visible grain patterns, some color variation between boards, and a countertop that will patina over months of exposure. The finish is typically a protective sealant or oil that enhances the grain while offering a sheen that catches light without shouting, Hey, I own a deck now.

### The chairs: rattan meets practicality
The chairs use a rattan weave over a sturdy frame. The trick with rattan is to check for tightness in the weave and comfort over longer sessions. If cushions are included, you’ll have an extra layer of cushiony support, and if not, you can swap in your preferred outdoor cushions with no drama. The chair legs are capped or finished to resist scuffing on concrete or wood decking, which means less worry about dragging the chairs across the patio while two neighbors pretend not to witness your mid-assembly chaos.

## Practicality around the pool
### Water and weather exposure
The poolside environment is not your living room—spritzes of water, chlorine, and sun are all in play. The set is designed to withstand occasional splashes, but frequent direct exposure to chlorine can affect finishes over time. It’s wise to wipe down the table and chairs after heavy pool use to prevent residue from building up in the weave or on the wood. Consider a cover or storage option for heavy rain days to minimize water infiltration and wood swelling.

### Cleaning and maintenance tips
A simple routine works wonders: wipe with a damp cloth, then dry. For tougher spots, a mild soap solution with a soft bristle brush does the trick. Avoid harsh chemical cleaners that strip the finish or dull the grain. Periodically oiling or sealing the wood with an outdoor-grade finish can help preserve color and reduce cracking. For the rattan chairs, avoid soaking the weave; quick dries are your friend, and if you store cushions, keep them dry to prevent mold growth. 

### Storage and life in the off-season
If you have a mild winter or a short off-season, you can leave the set outside with a protective cover. If you live in a climate with freezing temperatures, you might want to bring cushions indoors and cover the furniture. This is not a museum piece; it’s furniture designed for daily life, so treat it like a tool and not a sculpture, and it will reward you with years of service and occasional smiles at your own cleverness.
