## Setup and Maintenance: From Box to Bloom
Setting up the XXL is one thing; keeping it alive through spring, summer, and fall is another. Here’s a practical play-by-play for the busy gardener who’s also technically a gamer.

### Step-by-Step Setup Guide
1. Choose a location: sunny, open, and accessible to pollinator-friendly flora. A balance of sun and shelter is ideal.
2. Prepare the base: place on a stable surface and consider a small platform or bricks to keep it off damp soil.
3. Install the compartments: if modules are loose, secure them with small braces so they don’t rattle in strong winds.
4. Fill with appropriate materials: use bamboo tubes, wood blocks, bark, and pine cones in a random but balanced distribution.
5. Inspect for sharp edges and splinters: smooth rough bits that could injure tiny tenants.
6. Install close to nectar sources: flowering herbs and perennials that bloom in staggered timelines.
7. Monitor and adjust: after a season, move components to optimize occupancy.

### Routine Maintenance
Maintenance is minimal but important. A few times a year, inspect for signs of mold, rot, or insect overpopulation. Replace worn sections, especially if the wood has started to degrade. If a section becomes waterlogged, dry it out and consider reconfiguring the interior layout to reduce pooling. Cleanliness matters, but be careful not to disinfect like you’re sterilizing a lab. Insects appreciate a touch of chaotic charm as long as there are dry crevices.

### Inhabitants: Who Moves In and Why It Matters
Solitary bees, mason bees, and other friendly organisms are the main guests here. These species don’t swarm; they are efficient and generally non-aggressive. They prefer safe nesting cavities and are excellent for garden health because their larvae serve as pollinators while the adults gather nectar. Lady beetles, lacewings, and hoverflies are also welcome because they are nature’s pest management team. The presence of natural predators helps keep aphids and scale under control without resorting to chemical interventions. If you’re allergic to buzzes, this is a different kind of adventure—best enjoyed from a safe, silent corner of the garden with a coffee mug and a pair of binoculars.

## Hands-On Tips and Tricks
- Pair with nectar-rich plants: Install near flowering perennials and herbs that bloom in staggered patterns so bees have a steady food supply.
- Think in zones: Place the hotel in a sunny location, but consider shelter from afternoon heat in hotter climates. Morning sun helps attract early risers, while shade keeps late-day explorers comfortable.
- Rotate and refresh: Every season, consider rotating compartments and reconfiguring the interior to create new microhabitats. This helps keep the ecosystem dynamic and interesting for tenants.
- Avoid chemical interventions: When pests do show up, opt for non-chemical methods like physical barriers or encouraging beneficial insects. The goal is harmony between gardener and tenant, not a war of chemicals.
- Document occupancy: Take notes or photos of which compartments are most used each year. You’ll start to see patterns that inform future plant choices and placement in the garden.
