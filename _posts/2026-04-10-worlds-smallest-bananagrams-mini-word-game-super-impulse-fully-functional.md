## How to Play Mini Bananagrams
### Setup
Like its big sibling, the mini version uses a pool of tiles that you mix face down, then everyone grabs a rack of a certain number of letters. In the micro edition, the number of tiles is smaller, but the chaos remains. The rules are simple enough for a kid to grasp and elegant enough to keep a grown gamer from tweeting about your left-handed QBG practice (yes, that’s a thing I just made up).

### Turn structure and word-building
Players flip tiles from the communal pool and race to form a crossword-like grid of valid words, with all words interlocking. The objective is to exhaust your rack and then declare “BANANAGRAMS” (or simply win by having the fewest tiles left) depending on the house rules you decide to adopt during a heated coffee-fueled moment.

In mini mode, speed is the spice: you’re playing against the clock as well as your own capacity for anagrams. The smaller tile set means fewer potential letter distributions, which in turn makes the grid-building a little more deterministic and a lot more hilariously frantic as you try to squeeze a four-letter word into a space that clearly wants a six-letter epic.

### Scoring, variants, and house rules
Traditional Bananagrams uses a scoring style where tiles left on your rack count against you in the end. The mini version can adopt the same rule, or you can cheat your way into a wild variant by awarding style points for particularly clever interlocking, anagrams that spell odd ball words, or the most creative use of a Q without a U anywhere near it. The mini set invites creativity in ways that a larger board sometimes suppresses, because you’re forced to improvise with the space you’ve got—less room for vanity and more room for linguistic shenanigans.

The mini tiles are plastic, and they’re sturdy enough to survive the occasional clumsy carying-around moment but delicate enough that you probably shouldn’t use them as makeshift dice in a high-stakes game of craps with your cat.
