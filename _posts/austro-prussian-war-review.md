## Causes of the War: "It's Complicated" Status on Facebook

The Austro-Prussian War, also known as the Seven Weeks' War, wasn't just about who had the better lederhosen. It was a complex mix of political, social, and economic factors that led to the showdown. Here are the main reasons:

### The German Confederation: An Awkward Family Reunion

The German Confederation was like a family reunion where no one really liked each other. Austria and Prussia were the two major powers, and each wanted to be the head of the table. The competition for dominance was intense, and tensions ran high.

### Economic Rivalry: Money Talks, War Follows

Economic interests played a significant role. Prussia had been growing its economic power and wanted to control the German states for better trade opportunities. Austria, on the other hand, wasn't too keen on losing its influence.

### The Schleswig-Holstein Question: "Who's Babysitting the Kids?"

Schleswig and Holstein were two duchies that were the geopolitical equivalent of a hot potato. Both Austria and Prussia wanted control, and the disagreements over who should babysit these territories led to war. If only they had settled it over a game of rock-paper-scissors!

## Consequences: The After-Party Nobody Wanted

### Treaty of Prague: "Let's Just Pretend This Never Happened"

The war ended with the Treaty of Prague in August 1866. Austria had to concede defeat and agree to Prussia's terms. The German Confederation was dissolved, and the North German Confederation was established under Prussian leadership.

### Austria's Humiliation: The Walk of Shame

Austria's influence in Germany was significantly reduced, and it had to focus on its other territories. The Habsburgs, once the dominant force in Central Europe, had to come to terms with their diminished power.

### Prussia's Ascendancy: The New Head of the Table

Prussia emerged as the leading German state, paving the way for the eventual unification of Germany under the Prussian crown in 1871. Otto von Bismarck, the mastermind behind the war, solidified his reputation as a brilliant strategist and statesman.
