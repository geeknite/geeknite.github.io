## How it feels to play with it

### The rolling experience

The moment you drop dice into the top, you hear a satisfying clack that signals you are about to commit to a turn. The dice tumble, bounce, and slant, and then settle into a neat little pile at the base. The idea is to reduce table jitter and keep dice from rolling off into the void where snacks and stray meeples vanish. In practice, the tower does a solid job of guiding dice, but it does not eliminate rogue rolls entirely. If your dice are having a rebellious day, they will find a way to bounce beyond the tower—this is the nature of any dice-rolling ecosystem, not a bug in the CMON hardware.

What you gain is consistency. The dice lands near a predictable center, which reduces the amount of misinterpretation when you’re scanning for results and muttering about that critical hit you almost had but now have to pretend was a glorious miss to keep the table entertained.

### Setup time vs. payoff

Expect a few minutes to set up before your first turn. This is not a five-minute filler item; it is a gadget. If your gaming group values a quick start, you might want to install it and test a dry run before you gather the scoring drones and commence a nighttime campaign. The payoff is that gameplay can feel smoother, with fewer dice-tossing calamities and fewer moments where you realize you forgot to place tokens you just earned. If your table runs with a certain amount of chaos, the dice tower can add a theatrical calm, a small ritual that signals the transition from “let’s roll” to “let’s play the game” with a little style.

## Practical gameplay considerations

### Does it speed things up?

In a typical cooperative zombie skirmish, every second of table time counts. With a dice tower, you remove some fiddly moments: chasing dice that roll under cans, chasing dice that roll behind miniatures, and the occasional “is that a six or a sideways six?” moment that leads to a debate longer than a zombie’s memory of a human snack. In other words, you will move a little faster, and your table will look sleek while you do it. The downside is that a lot of the game’s flavor comes from dice chaos. If your group is very dialed into the chaos energy of Zombicide, a tower might feel a bit too orderly. It’s a trade-off you’ll feel on both the competitive and narrative levels.

### How it blends with other accessories

If you already own a dedicated dice tray, the tower will stack nicely in your play area. It does not obstruct your character boards or the initiative track, but you should verify there is enough clearance for someone leaning over the table to read a zombie’s statline without tipping the entire tower. We tested it with a standard 3x3 table and had enough room for a second player to lean in with a snack and a spritz without knocking the tower over. If you are playing a homebrew variant that uses multiple dice colors, you may find the tower’s interior channels a little crowded. Still, the airflow around the base is designed to prevent dramatic catch-alls; you’re not going to sneeze a rogue die into the furnace with the tower on the table.

### Randomness and fairness

Dice randomness is a conversation as old as dice themselves. The tower does not change the probability of outcomes; it only channels the dice in a predictable way. For true fairness in a game that often features a lot of luck-based outcomes, you want precision and consistency of die landing and a reliable stance. The CMON tower offers both. It will not magically convert a poor roll into a hero’s destiny, but it should prevent that critical roll from becoming a table-wide fiasco because a die refused to land in the line of sight of the player who needs to observe it.
