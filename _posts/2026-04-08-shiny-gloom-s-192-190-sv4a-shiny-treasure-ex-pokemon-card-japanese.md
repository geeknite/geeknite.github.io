## How to handle, store, and display
Card care is love, card care is life. The shiny surface invites fingerprints like a magnet market, so practice good dexterity.

- Handling: If you can, wear gloves when you’re pulling the card from packaging. The oils in skin can keep the holo from singing after a few hundred viewings.
- Storage: Use a Mylar sleeve with a rigid top-loader. Choose a binder page with enough margin to avoid sleeve-induced curling.
- Display: Lighting matters. Neutral white balance with a LED softbox yields the most flattering rainbow glints on the holo without washing out the artwork.
- Travel: If you’re taking the card to a convention or a local meet, consider storing it in a small card case to prevent accidental damage from enthusiastic high-fives and table flips.

## Authentication and fakes: how to tell, in plain terms
Counterfeits love the idea of glitter, so be skeptical when a seller’s listing looks too good to be true. Here are practical checks you can perform before parting with cash (or a trade deal that would put a smile on your face):

- Photo quality: Look for consistent lighting, check the holo’s reflection across multiple angles, and compare the border color to a known authentic print.
- Card stock and weight: Genuine Shiny Vault cards have a certain heft to the stock and a consistent feel across the sheet.
- Font and symbol fidelity: In Japanese prints, the font for the set symbol and the card’s numeration should match the standard pressings. A difference here is a common red flag.
- Edges and corners: A counterfeit often shows rough edges or inconsistent whitening. A mint-like edge is a good sign, but don’t rely on one cue alone.
- Authenticity tests with grades: If the seller can provide a grading certificate (PSA/CGC), that’s a huge confidence boost.

If you’re new to the hobby, start with reputable retailers who offer grading or authentication services, and always request multiple photos, including a close-up of the holo and the bottom-right numbers.
