## What is this game and how does it work in a sentence

Sushi Go is a fast drafting and set collection game. The core mechanic involves selecting a card from a hand and then passing the remaining cards to the next player, in a loop that happens a handful of times per round. Each card type contributes to scoring in unique ways, from simple point values on nigiri to multi-card combos like maki stacks and the classic two plate system for tempura and sashimi. The objective is not to munch your way through a menu but to assemble the highest scoring sushi spread by the end of the round or rounds.

In the 2-5 player version with eight plus on the age label, you get a tight, breezy experience. It is not a brain burner marathon; it is the kind of game that thrives on social timing, bluffing, and watching how others will race to finish sets before you do. In the tradition of many beloved party games, Sushi Go rewards both memory and risk management without ever drifting into heavy abstraction. Your advantages come from reading the table, predicting the choices of others, and exploiting the occasional misdirection that arrives when a round friend tries to explain why the egg nigiri belongs in their life more than in their stomach.

## How to play in 2 to 5 players: a quick run-through

### Setup
- Shuffle the deck and deal a hand of cards to each player. In a 2-5 player game, you will pass a portion of the hand around after each selection. The number of cards dealt per player and the number of passes per round remain consistent, keeping the tempo brisk.
- Place the discard pile near the center so everyone can riff on what was left behind, a small social moment where you pretend to consider a rational reason for passing on your chance to go for a perfect sashimi trio.
- Decide the number of rounds you will play. In the base game, you typically play three rounds, with points tallied after each round. End-of-round scoring happens after everyone has revealed their chosen cards, and the next round begins with fresh passes.

### Playing a round
1) On your turn, take a look at your hand and pick a card that seems most valuable for your current build. You will not only think about what you want but also what you can reasonably deny to the person next to you.
2) Place the chosen card face down or show it to your friends, whichever suits your table vibe. Then pass the rest of the hand to the left. The next player repeats the same action, and so on until all cards are claimed.
3) When all players have chosen, reveal the cards and score according to the card types you have collected. The scoring rules are straightforward but spicy. For instance, sashimi requires a set of three to earn points; tempura yields points for a pair; nigiri cards add their face value to your total, with some nigiri groups fetching bonuses beyond raw points.
4) After scoring, discard the round cards and start the next round with a fresh hand. The final scoring happens after the last round, with pudding cards adding special end-of-game points and sometimes turning the tide in a dramatic reveal that makes the whole table manager-style declare a winner.

### End of game and final scoring
The chaos subsides and you tally final scores. Pudding cards are worth points at the end of the game, so even if you were not racking up points through the rounds, you can still edge out a victory by sneaking a pudding win. The end result is a clean, small victory ceremony that can be celebrated with a chorus of laughter at clever plays or a groan at a well-timed passing move that stolen your would-be glory.

## Strategy tips: getting better without turning it into a math grind

- Prioritize early round information: Since you pass hands around several times, you can deduce what a particular player is chasing by observing their choices and the cards they keep or discard. A little memory goes a long way here. Do not overcomplicate it though; the charm is in quick decisions, not deep engine building.
- Balance immediate gains with long-term sets: A single high value card might be tempting, but if it blocks a necessary card for a bigger scoring combo, you might shoot yourself in the foot. The key is to find a balance between grabbing singles and completing small sets.
- Watch opponents for set up arcs: If you notice someone is pursuing a particular sushi type that requires three items, position yourself to grab the missing link or to pass the right cards away to slow them down. Light tactical disruption can be more valuable than a tenuous personal score spike.
- End-game pudding economy matters: Pudding is often overlooked in early rounds but can swing the final tally. If you sense you will not win on the strict sushi scoring, lean into pudding when possible. It is the quiet way to come from behind without dramatic plays.
- Adapt to player counts: With fewer players, you see fewer cards passed around each turn, which speeds up learning and increases misdirection opportunities. In larger groups, the mental math is replaced with a more social dance and a willingness to improvise.
