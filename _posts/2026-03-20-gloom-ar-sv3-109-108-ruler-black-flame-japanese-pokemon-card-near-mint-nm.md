### Condition: Near Mint NM explained
Near Mint NM is the sweet spot for many collectors. It implies the card has minimal wear: sharp corners (aside from the occasional gentle corner thing that happened during transit), no creases, crisp edges, and a surface free of major scratches. In practice, NM can be a little fuzzy on a Japanese print depending on how the card was shipped, stored, and handled. The best way to keep NM in pristine condition is to store it in a top-loader with a solid inner sleeve, then place it in a semi-flexible binder with acid-free sheets. Don’t jam it into a sleeve factory where it’s needed to survive a small earthquake — unless your life literally requires you to test the structural integrity of Pokémon cards under natural disasters.

## Visual and scanning experience (Display and care)
If you’re this close to a card with black flame drama, you’re likely wondering how to best display it without losing the edge. Here are practical tips to maximize the wow factor while preserving NM status.

### Framing and lighting
- Use a neutral, daylight-balanced light source to avoid color distortion. Avoid direct sunlight, which can cause minor fading over years.
- Consider a matte frame that reduces glare while highlighting the holo shimmer. The black flame art benefits from a softly lit angle so you can see the gradient properly.
- A small display stand that tilts the card at 10–15 degrees can make the flame appear to move with your peripheral vision, which is the printer’s idea of a practical joke on you.

### Sleeves, binders, and storage
- Top-loaders or soft sleeves with anti-glare properties should be the baseline. The aim is to protect the surface without dulling the holo sparkle.
- Store in a binder with acid-free pages. If your binder has a dark exterior, the card’s dark art will pop in a satisfying, gothic-tinged way.
- Keep humidity in check; too much moisture can warp corners and edges over time, even for NM-level cards.

### Digital photography tips for listings and social
- Use a lightbox or diffused lighting to avoid hotspots on the holo. A tiny white backdrop adds contrast without night-sky drama.
- Shoot from multiple angles: a tight shot of the arc line at the corner, a wider shot to capture the full art, and a macro view of any texture on the surface.
- For listing thumbnails, prioritize a clean, uncluttered frame that makes the flame motif the star of the shot.
