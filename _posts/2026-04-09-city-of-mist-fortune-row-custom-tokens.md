## What is Fortune Row in City of Mist?

Fortune Row is the kind of district that makes you believe in both luck and hubris. It’s where street-level myths wear designer labels and the city’s shadows politely pretend to be on your side until they’re not. In practical gaming terms, Fortune Row is a playground for social maneuvers, long cons, and enchanting misdirections. It’s a locale where characters’ hidden identities (Gonzo the Genius Thief, Madame Fortuna, or the Street-Sage) rub elbows with reality-defining moments: a card reading that predicts a change in the tide, a deal that changes a tag’s value, or a rumor that unexpectedly rewrites a scene’s stakes.

Custom tokens for Fortune Row let players and GMs lean into what makes this district shimmer: narrative leverage. Tokens are not a replacement for the core rules; they’re the glitter-dusted glue that helps the group remember who their characters are, what they want in this particular neighborhood, and how quickly a minor choice can spiral into a myth. We’ll cover design principles, a ready-to-use token pack with 20 options, and practical guidelines for weaving tokens into sessions—without turning your table into a spreadsheet of doom.

## Why Custom Tokens?

Tokens in Fortune Row serve several purposes:

- Narrative focus: Tokens remind the table what matters in a scene and what the players are actually risking or gaining. They’re compact prompts that trigger roleplay and quick decisions.
- Pace management: In a place as lively as Fortune Row, momentum can derail easily. Tokens provide a mechanical rhythm to moves, re-rolls, and scene transitions.
- Thematic cohesion: The right token design echoes the district’s aesthetics—fortune-tellers, gamblers, underworld figures, and the mythic entities that ride shotgun on every neon street corner.
- Player agency: Tokens reward creative use of tags, pushes, or Stunts (in City of Mist terms) and give players tangible incentives to lean into their character concepts.

Now that you’re sold on the concept, here’s a practical, two-part plan: first, a quick primer on token design principles, and second, a ready-made token pack you can drop into your Fortune Row sessions right away. And yes, this is fully compatible with your existing character sheets, as long as you treat tokens as optional yet delightful accelerants rather than mandatory checklists.

## Token Design Principles

Designing tokens for Fortune Row is a little like creating carnival prizes: you want something that feels exciting to claim, easy to narrate, and not so powerful that it breaks the vibe of the scene. Here are three cornerstone principles we’ll keep returning to:

### Thematic Alignment
A token should feel like it belongs to Fortune Row. If the token depicts a roulette wheel, a cryptic tarot card, or a brass dice cup, it should evoke the district’s mood: high-stakes glamour, risk, and a dash of supernatural luck. If your token is called “Fortune’s Eye” but looks like a boring spreadsheet cell, you’ve missed the point. Visual language matters.

### Mechanical Simplicity
Fortune Row tokens should be easy to use. In City of Mist, players already juggle multiple tags, powers, and Scene moves. A token should slide into the action without causing the table to pause for a mini-lecture on how many “Power dice” you have left in your pool. If a token requires a dozen steps to resolve, it’s probably overcomplicated for a session that’s meant to skim along the edge of chaos.

### Narrative Impact
The best tokens alter what’s happening in a scene, not just what’s logged in a sheet. A token should change the color of a scene, invite a character to reveal something about themselves, or shift the balance of power—however temporarily—so that the next scene feels different. That difference is what keeps Fortune Row feeling alive rather than a static map of “+1 bonus.”

## How to Use Tokens in Play

In a typical Fortune Row session, tokens can serve as optional add-ons for any move that involves negotiation, deception, or social maneuvering. Here are a few practical patterns you can steal (or borrow with pride):

- Scene Toggles: Place a token on the table at the start of a scene. At any point, a player may spend the token to tilt the scene’s tone toward a specific outcome (e.g., soften a confrontation, or push a misdirection forward).
- Tag Reinforcement: If a player uses a tag to justify their claim, they may spend a token to grant an extra tag on the next roll or move. This creates a resource-based economy of information and influence.
- GM Injection: The GM can reward players with tokens for clever narration, dramatic irony, or particularly stylish use of Fortune Row’s aesthetic. Tokens gain currency when players roleplay the district’s vibe, not merely when they roll well.
- Resolution Window: The token’s narrative impact should feel like a short XP bar: it’s enough to move a scene forward or widen a character’s personal arc, but it should not overshadow the central mystery of the arc.

A major advantage of token-based play is that it provides tangible stakes for roleplay moments. A token claims a scene, a conversation, a move—it's a literal reminder that in Fortune Row, every choice has consequences that echo beyond the moment.

## Sample Scene: The Neon Diner Heist

To show how tokens behave in a concrete moment, imagine a scene in the Neon Diner of Fortune Row: a con artist for a client of fortune, a hero with a hidden identity trying to keep it quiet, and a night that isn’t quite ready to end. The table uses three tokens: Gambler’s Coin, Sign of the Row, and The Deck of Favors.

- The GM sets the scene with a neon overhead buzz and the clatter of coin glasses. The Gambler’s Coin sits on the edge of the table, glinting with a stubborn sheen. The players describe how the crew attempts to secure a mysterious card from the Diner’s vault. 
- A PC offers a tag about their own mythic identity clashing with a local debtor, and the Sign of the Row is spent to gain a whispered advantage with the Diner’s owner. The GM uses The Deck of Favors to grant a temporary NPC ally—a bartender who owes a favor to the player’s past life as a thief.
- The scene hits a twist: a rival gang arrives, and The Last Bet Token is triggered in the climactic moment to alter the outcome. The GM allows one final swap of fate, creating a dramatic, high-stakes ending for the scene. The players feel the weight of Fortune Row’s environment—the place where luck isn’t purely random; it’s a living, breathing force you can trade with, bargain against, or sometimes offend with a flourish.

This is the essence of token-driven play: you take a moment, add a tangible object, and the narrative gets a new lever to pull. Tokens are not an extra rule to memorize; they’re a storytelling shortcut that says to everyone at the table: this is important, this matters, and we’ll remember this moment long after the neon stops blinking.

## The Community Angle

Fortune Row is a playground for creativity, which means your group will likely tailor tokens to fit their characters’ arcs. A few notes on making that work well in practice:

- Encourage player participation: ask players to propose a token that ties directly to their character’s ERAs (Establishing Relationships Aspects) or a pivotal memory. This makes tokens feel personal and embedded.
- Balance with GM discretion: tokens should help, not dominate. If a token becomes too influential, scale back its power or limit uses per session.
- Use tokens as narration prompts: you don’t have to enforce every token mechanically. If a player spends Gambler’s Coin to push a narrative in a surprising direction, let the scene adapt to the new path. The unpredictable nature of Fortune Row is its own reward.

Community-driven tokens can grow into a miniature ecosystem, where players collect, trade, and curate tokens that echo their characters’ unique paths through the district. It’s not just gear; it’s a living passport to the city’s myths.
