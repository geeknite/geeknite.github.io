## What is Throw Throw Burrito?

Throw Throw Burrito is a fast-paced card game by Expanding Kittens that pairs set collection with dramatic burrito-based chaos. The box includes three plush burritos, a packet of cards, a few dice (yes, dice), a scoring pad, and a whole lot of laughter in a compact size. The objective is to collect pairs of matching cards while dodging or initiating burrito-based chaos with other players. It’s a mash-up of memory, speed, and dodgeball without the commitment of a gym membership or the lingering chalk dust on your fingers.

### Box contents and what it feels like to own this chaos

- 3 soft burritos that feel surprisingly sturdy for the amount of therapy you’re going to need after the game. They’re designed for safe throwing, which means you will still have at least one bruise-free story to tell.
- A deck of cards featuring goofy animal illustrations, absurd pairings, and a few “attack” cards that force everyone into a burrito standoff.
- A rulebook that gently explains how to turn a social gathering into a ridiculous free-for-all in under five minutes.
- A score pad that makes you feel like you’re actually accomplishing something, even when your dignity is slipping away with each round.

If you’re curious about the components’ quality, the plush burritos are plush enough to cushion a mild elbow jab but forgiving enough that nobody needs a tetanus shot after a questionable strategy. The cards are nice and durable, which is essential given the number of times they’ll be slapped, slid across the table, or accidentally used as improvised fan blades during a particularly intense “Dodge!” call.

The art style leans into goofy, over-the-top humor—think goofy pets, neon colors, and bold typography that shouts, not whispers, at your competitive soul. The humor is very meme-y in a wholesome way: there are puns, exaggerated expressions, and a constant undercurrent of “this is all in good fun, please don’t throw real burritos.” The theme is not just garnish; it’s a design choice that encourages players to lean into chaos, be a little dramatic, and roast their friends with a wink. If you’re into Exploding Kittens’ irreverent humor, you’ll recognize the same energy here, but with a party-game twist that foregrounds physical interaction along with the card-based mischief.

## How to play: setup, turns, and techniques

Throw Throw Burrito plays best with a group of enthusiastic chaos agents who thrive on quick turns and loud declarations. Here’s a practical walk-through to get you from box to boom in under 10 minutes.

### Setup and layout

1) Place the burritos in the center of the table where everyone can reach them. If you’re in a crowded living room, designate a burrito pit in front of the couch—your future self will thank you for that strategic relocation.
2) Shuffle the cards and deal a starting hand to each player (usually 4-5 cards, depending on the number of players).
3) Decide the play order. In Geeknite fashion, you can implement a rock-paper-scissors showdown for the privilege of starting. Children of chaos welcome.
4) Set the score to zero on the score pad. Everyone starts fresh, but be prepared to accrue fast, because this game rewards speed and bluster over long-term planning.

### Turn structure: a rhythm you’ll love to hate
A typical turn consists of two parts: the card phase and the burrito phase. Players must juggle their hand, decide when to snap at a rival for a set, and determine whether to unleash a burrito blitz on the table.

- Card phase: Draw up to your hand limit, then play cards to either collect a matching set (two of a kind) or to trigger a “duel” or “attack” card against another player. Card effects often revolve around stealing, swapping, or forcing someone to reveal their secret weapon (their current hand).
- Burrito phase: If a player lays down a particular pair or meets a certain condition, they may invoke a burrito fight—this is the moment where chaos becomes performance art. Everyone slams a burrito into the center and prepares for a dramatic dodge or a risky throw across the table. Missed throws can lead to penalties, so the bets are high and the noise level is assuredly louder than your typical game night.

### The strategy layer: when to grab and when to dodge

- Speed versus safety: Some players aim to collect sets as quickly as possible, trading accuracy for speed. Others focus on defense, saving attacks for key moments when an opponent is about to win. The balance between offense and defense shifts as the burritos fly and the cards clack, and that tension is where the game earns its chaotic charm.
- Reading your crowd: The real skill in Throw Throw Burrito is learning when people will overreach. A confident bluff can bait someone into a reckless duel, letting you leap ahead without firing a single card. Conversely, you can sometimes bait your friends into a burrito-chucking trap by acting too confident, only to reveal a counter-card at the last second.
- Resource management: The deck rewards smart use of attack cards. Don’t waste your best moves on a safe round; wait for the pivotal moment when you can clinch a set and trigger a catastrophic burrito fight. Success hinges on a blend of risk assessment and a willingness to embrace glorious chaos.

### Endgame and scoring

As soon as a player claims a valid set, they score points. The tricky part is that other players can interrupt, steal, or force re-sets, so the endgame remains fluid. A few rounds can turn a narrow lead into a dramatic underdog comeback, or vice versa, in a hurry. The scoring pad gives you a sense of progress without becoming a sterile ledger; it’s more about bragging rights than about a precise victory margin, which aligns perfectly with the game’s humorous spirit.

## Theme and design: why this works as a party game

Throw Throw Burrito isn’t just a collection of silly mechanics; it’s a design that purposefully invites social interaction and energy. The physicality is not an afterthought but a core driver of the experience. You will find yourself shouting, laughing, and possibly throwing a burrito a little too enthusiastically. The game works on multiple levels:

- Social bonding: The action invites people to engage, tease, and coordinate (or deliberately mis-coordinate) with their teammates. You’ll create inside jokes, accidental alliances, and the occasional temporary feud—perfect for a group that wants memorable stories rather than quiet contemplation.
- Physical humor: There’s something inherently funny about red plush burritos sailing through the air like misguided projectiles. It’s safe enough to be light-hearted, yet chaotic enough to spark genuine laughter. If you’re a gamer who also enjoys a bit of slapstick, this is your zone.
- Accessibility: The rules are straightforward, and the rounds are short. New players catch on quickly, which makes it an excellent entry point for mixed-age groups or guests who don’t play a lot of board games. The fast pace keeps attention high and reduces the risk of boredom creeping in.

## Accessibility and safety considerations

The physical aspect of Throw Throw Burrito can be a source of joy, but it also calls for mindfulness:
- Clear a safe playing area: Keep drinks, fragile items, and precious electronics out of the burrito’s flight path. A few silly slips are expected, but a broken lamp is not ideal for your next retro game night.
- Respect personal space: Not everyone enjoys being hit by a plush burrito, even if it’s soft. Establish light-hearted consent and call-outs if someone wants to sit a round out.
- Children and adults: The game is appropriate for ages 7 and up, but parental guidance is advised for younger players on how to throw safely without aiming for faces or eyes. A simple rule: no aim for the head; burritos are for fun, not wrestling.

## Variants and house rules: remix the chaos
Many groups love to customize Throw Throw Burrito with their own house rules. Here are a few ideas that keep things fresh without breaking the vibe:
- The zero-draw variant: Start with 0 cards and each player must steal or complete sets using burrito-induced duels only. This increases chaos but speeds up the endgame.
- The duo duel: Two players team up against the rest for a handful of rounds. You’ll need to communicate without betraying your partner’s plans—great for teamwork, questionable for trying to outplay your own friends.
- The prop theme: Use protective gear like towels or light padding to avoid bruises on elbows during intense duels. It adds theatricality and reduces the risk of interfering with furniture.
