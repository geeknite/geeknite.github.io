Playing the game: quickstart guide
---------------------------------
Ages: 3 and up
Players: 2–4 (some versions allow more via house rules)
Playtime: roughly 15–25 minutes per game, depending on the number of players and how dramatic the Move cards get. 

Setup
- Shuffle the deck and deal 5 cards to each player. The rest of the deck becomes the draw pile in the center. If you’re playing with younger children, you can start with 4 cards to speed things up. 
- Decide who goes first: a quick rock-paper-scissors round works fine here, or you can simply let the loudest kid claim the first turn with confidence. 

How to play (basic rules)
- On your turn, you must play a card that matches the color or the number of the top discard card. If you have no valid play, draw one card from the draw pile. If that card can be played, you may do so immediately; otherwise, your turn ends. 
- Move cards: When you play a Move card, you perform the action indicated. The moves are designed to be quick and safe, and you’re encouraged to keep things light-hearted. For example, a Move card might say, “Touch your toes” or “Jump in place once.” If you can’t perform the move safely, you can skip the action with an agreed-upon safe alternative. 
- UNO-like rule twist: when a player has one card left, they must yell UNO to signal impending victory. We recommend clapping for the player who manages to say UNO without accidentally shouting, “I forgot to say UNO!” again. 
- Endgame: the first player to get rid of all their cards wins. The others tally up their penalty cards (or end-of-round cards) as needed for a lighthearted scoreboard. 

House rules and tweaks
- Short-swing mode: for younger players, reduce the number of cards dealt and shorten the game. This is great for short attention spans and simultaneous snack refills. 
- Non-competitive mode: celebrate everyone’s participation with a fun “team win” after the final card, rather than crowning a single winner. 
- Move card safety first: you can set a “no running indoors” rule for Move prompts; this keeps the game in the living room rather than the hallway. 
- Cooperative variant: you can flip the script and pair players to work together to finish the round, which is fantastic for teaching teamwork and turn-taking.

- Cognitive boosts: color and number recognition, as well as quick decision-making, get a workout as kids decide which card to play and when to trigger a Move. 
- Social-emotional growth: turn-taking, sharing space with siblings, and cheering on others all contribute to a healthier social experience around gaming. 
- Language development: calling out colors, numbers, and the actions on Move cards gives kids the chance to practice vocabulary in a context that’s meaningful and fun.
