## How to play (high-level, with flavor)

### The setup
The board is laid out, the cards are shuffled with a ceremonial breeze, and players choose their allegiance to chaos or order. The goal is deceptively simple: manage your resources, thread a narrative through your choices, and try not to collapse under the weight of glorious, shimmering decisions. The Limited Edition doesn’t change core mechanics; it enhances the feel. You still arrange cards into two streams, one ascending and one descending, trying to balance the playground of possibilities without creating a cosmic paradox that would embarrass your future self in a Saturday morning brunch.

### Turn structure and flow
Each turn is a compact dance: draw a card, play a card, and possibly trigger a special event that might reward you with extra points or punish you with a tiny penalty token that absolutely was designed to look cute but stings emotionally. The game emphasizes tempo rather than length, so you can savor moments of quiet triumph (you placed a card perfectly) and moments of panic (you realize you’ve misread the synergy and now everything builds toward an upside-down crisis).

### The scoring and tension
Scoring is not merely numeric; it’s a narrative. You’ll find yourself calculating not just what adds up but how the addition makes you feel about your own life choices. The Limited Edition nails this by ensuring card art cues lead your eyes toward clusters of potential combos that you, in a moment of heroic overconfidence, will chase only to realize that you’ve started an entirely new chain that may or may not be resolvable before the game ends.

### Solo mode and group dynamics
Yes, you can play solo, and yes, you will try to justify that you’re “teaching the game to a friend who isn’t here yet.” The solo mode is a surprisingly robust test drive that can teach you new strategies without the social cost of losing to your cousin for the seventh time in a row. In group play, the fun is collaborative chaos: players negotiate, bluff, and occasionally betray their own strategies in order to trigger a communal moment of, Did we just pull that off? It’s surprisingly satisfying when you jam a strategy together that looks ridiculous on paper but sings in practice.
