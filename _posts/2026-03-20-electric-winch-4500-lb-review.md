## Synthetic rope vs steel cable: the age-old nerd debate

Synthetic rope is the future you want if you care about safety, handling, and maintenance. It’s easier to handle on the ground, reduces the risk of severe injuries when the line comes free, and it’s much fluffier when you’re wearing gloves that feature cybernetic knuckles. The caveat: it pays to keep it dry, avoid chemical exposure that can degrade the fibers, and store it in an environment that doesn’t resemble a swamp. The rope’s diameter and length can influence the gear you’ll need for the job, such as fairleads, line speed, and a controller that can handle extended pulls without overheating. If you’re chasing heavy-duty rock crawling profiles, steel might be more appropriate for extreme loads, but for most trail rescue scenarios, synthetic is the safer, easier, and cooler-headed choice. ### Mounting tips for your bumper and frame

## Maintenance and care

To keep your winch singing for years, a few routine tasks go a long way:

- Inspect the rope for frays or kinks after every heavy use. Replace immediately if you see compromised fibers.
- Clean the winch exterior after mud and water exposure to minimize corrosion risk. A damp cloth followed by a light coat of protective spray is usually enough, not a lakeside spa day for your motor.
- Check the remote’s battery and test the wireless range in a safe area before you rely on it in a real rescue.
- Store the rope and the winch in a dry place when not in use. Prolonged exposure to sun and moisture can degrade synthetic fibers over time.
- Periodically test the entire system with no-load runs to ensure the brake and the spool are behaving correctly.
