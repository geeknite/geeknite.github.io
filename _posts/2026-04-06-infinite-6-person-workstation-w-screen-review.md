## Design and Build Quality
The Infinite 6 Person 2 Workstation is not shy about its size or its ambition. The unit measures a grand 5400 mm in length, 1530 mm in width, and 1200 mm in height. In human terms, that translates to about five and a half meters of desk per row and enough shoulder room to practice your posture without knocking elbows with your neighbor. The two workstations sit side by side, sharing a resilient central spine that provides structure and, allegedly, moral support when colleagues forget their headphones and decide to sing along to a virtual meeting.

Visually, the WtE Blk (white/black) finish is a clever two-tone that keeps the space feeling modern without drifting into the clinical coldness of a black hole of cables. The finish does a decent job resisting fingerprints and minor scuffs, though the real test is in the field where coffee drips and the occasional pasta sauce splatter try to masquerade as branding opportunities. The assembly team will appreciate color-matched hardware and pre-marked bolt holes that reduce the endless guessing game of which screw goes where.

Internally, the frame uses a steel backbone with a powder coat that feels robust to the touch. You’re not purchasing a delicate porcelain shelf here; you’re buying something that should survive a Monday morning sprint to the printer, a flood of post-it notes, and the occasional runaway rolling chair. The surface material is a practical laminate that resists scratches well enough for a busy office, though we recommend a protective desk pad if your team has a habit of wielding highlighters like swords.

## Dimensions and Space Planning
The footprint is the star here. At 5400 mm long, this unit can easily divide a large team across six person-specific work zones, each with its own screen alignment and keyboard tray. The central spine helps keep everything aligned and reduces the chance you’ll wake up one morning and notice your neighbor’s workstation has slid five centimeters into your map of personal space. The height of 1200 mm is generous for standing desk enthusiasts who want to alternate postures, though you’ll need a good chair with an adjustable height to keep leg posture comfortable.

One practical note: ceiling height matters. If your office has low ceilings or suspended lighting rigs, you might need to do some quick math and perhaps remove a sensor or two from the top rail. Also consider your HVAC routing. A long continuous desk can trap heat between your CPUs and the ceiling canopy—nice for a sauna, less nice for a productive Tuesday.

If you’re involved in space planning, the two-workstation design offers interesting opportunities for privacy without sacrificing collaboration. You can place the pair as a long working corridor or stack three workstations per side with a central path for movement. The modular nature means you could rearrange for workshops, stand-up sessions, or a very long gaming night if your team secretly runs a LAN party after hours.

## Ergonomics, Comfort, and User Experience
Ergonomics are the real test of any workstation, and the Infinite system tries to cover the bases: adjustable monitor arms for two screens per station, a wide keyboard tray, and a monochrome aesthetic that reduces visual fatigue. The screens themselves sit at a comfortable height, and the tension in the monitor arms feels adequate to hold position without the dreaded drift that makes you chase your display back to the center every few minutes. If you require ultra-adjustable monitor heights for a diverse team, you’ll be happy with the flexibility here. 

The keyboard trays are a pleasant surprise. They slide smoothly, with a soft-edge profile that prevents your forearms from taking a tactical skirmish with metal edges. The depth feels generous enough for full-sized keyboards and a mouse, with an extra inch of real estate for a notebook or a coffee mug that should be kept separate from electronics—because coffee and electricity are not exactly a compatible duo.

Chair integration is an important caveat. The table top height is a fixed feature here, so you want to couple this with an ergonomic chair that can adjust to the desk height. If you have a mix of short and tall colleagues, you may end up with a few “long-legged” moments at the ends of the rows. The solution is simple: use chairs with good seat depth and a strong lumbar support. In our test, the seating worked well enough for an eight-hour stretch, though extreme jumping jacks in between meetings? Not recommended. The human body is not a chair’s warranty card, even if the desk is an ecosystem of productivity.

## Cable Management, Power, and Tech Integration
Every large shared desk comes with a cable spaghetti incident waiting to happen. The Infinite system addresses this with a central trunking system that runs under the central spine and a side cable tray for each station. It’s not a magnum opus of cable artistry, but it does a solid job of corralling power strips, display adapters, and the occasional USB-C dongle. The result is a workspace that looks less like a hacker’s decade-old basement and more like a well-organized research facility. 

Power delivery is straightforward. The base model includes integrated power rails with USB-C fast charging in addition to standard outlets. It is not a magical portal to limitless energy, but it does reduce the “dying laptop breath” moments by keeping devices charging within easy reach. If your team brings a lot of external devices, consider adding extra charging ports or an extra strip in the central spine to minimize the scramble when one device decides to monopolize every outlet.

Routing cables through the central spine reduces the chance of a stray coffee cup short circuiting the entire floor. A minor nuisance is the occasional pinch point where multiple cables change direction; it’s not catastrophic, but you’ll want to ensure the cable bundles are not too tight or you’ll begin to hear the faint sound of a thousand tiny zip ties screaming for mercy. We recommend labeling cables by station and adding color-coded clips to simplify troubleshooting after the inevitable software update or hardware swap.

For display integration, the two screens per station are a win for multi-taskers. The dual-monitor setup makes it easier to cascade windows, compare data, or pretend you’re running a cockpit simulation while compiling a budget report. The screen arms are sturdy, with easy tilt and swivel adjustments. If you are a fan of portrait mode, you’ll appreciate the ability to rotate a display for coding marathons or reading long documents. Overall, screen placement is straightforward and forgiving, which is exactly what you want when your team is juggling timelines and brainstorming sessions.

## Assembly and Maintenance Experience
Assembling the Infinite 6 Person 2 Workstation is not a weekend DIY challenge unless you’re responsible for a large office renovation. Expect a couple of hours of assembly per side, especially if you’re meticulous with the alignment and want to thread every cable through the central spine without leaving a single rogue wire behind. The hardware is clearly labeled, the manual is fairly readable, and the pre-drilled holes align with minimal force. The included tools are basic, but you can use your own power screwdriver for speed—just don’t pretend you’re a master carpenter and attempt to drive a screwdriver like a mic stand while standing on a chair.

Maintenance is more about habit than tool-lust. The laminate surface cleans easily with mild cleaners, and the cable trays vacuum up nicely. If your team tends to enter the office like a tornado with a coffee cup, you’ll appreciate the lower maintenance vibe and the possibility of swapping panels or adjusting the layout without disassembling the whole thing.
