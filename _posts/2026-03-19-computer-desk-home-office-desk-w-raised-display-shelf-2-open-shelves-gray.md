## Features and Ergonomics

### Raised Display Shelf

This is the headline feature, and for good reason. The raised shelf places your monitor at roughly eye level for most users, which can dramatically cut down neck strain during long coding sessions or endless spreadsheet dives. The height is designed to accommodate most 24–32 inch monitors, with enough depth to sit a webcam or a compact soundbar on top without crowding the display. If you’re a laptop person who occasionally docks a monitor, there’s still room behind the monitor to tuck the laptop away—great for those times you want to watch a tutorial without your laptop wobbling like a ship in a storm.

If you’re into home studios or content creation, the raised shelf can serve as a mini-riser for a small microphone boom or a camera. It’s not a true studio rig, but it’s a portable, affordable way to give yourself a production-grade view without dramatically changing your layout.

### Open Shelves

The two open shelves offer convenient storage for peripherals, stationery, or a neatly organized collection of notebooks and doodads. They’re shallow enough to keep most items in reach while still letting you stack items without turning the desk into a clutter museum. Think of them as your “short-term storage zones”—things you need often but don’t want to go hunting for behind a monitor stand when you’re in the zone.

Pro tip: group items by frequency of use. Put the things you touch most on the left shelf and your longer-tail resources (manuals, spare dongles) on the right. It’s simple, it’s effective, and it reduces the “Where did I put that USB-C adapter?” moments you’ll have at three in the morning.

### Cable Management and Ventilation

Cable management isn’t glamorous, but it’s essential. The desk includes a rear edge channel and a back panel cutout to route cables to a power strip or hub. It’s not a full modular kabel labyrinth, but it keeps the visible cords in check and makes cleaning easier. Ventilation is decent for a desk surface: there’s enough air flow to keep a laptop from overheating under normal loads, assuming you’re not running a brute-force video render while your fan doubles as a spaceship engine.

Assembling furniture is part ritual, part test of patience, and part exercise in reading comprehension. The included hardware is straightforward: screws, dowels, and an Allen key that you’ll probably misplace five times before you finish. The instruction booklet uses color-coding that makes sense, and the steps are logically ordered to minimize backtracking. You’ll want to lay everything out, categorize hardware, and double-check the alignment before you tighten anything down too aggressively.

The process is pleasant enough if you go in with a calm strategy and a playlist that doesn’t tempt you to rush through the steps with a mental fog. Typical assembly time sits around 40–70 minutes depending on your toolkit, your patience, and how aggressively you want to future-proof the desk by over-tightening every screw. A few tips:

- Don’t skip the preliminary dry-fit (no screws yet) to ensure pieces line up cleanly.
- Keep track of small parts; a tiny bag of spare screws can save the day if you strip one early on.
- Use a torque-friendly screwdriver; you’ll thank yourself later when you’re not fighting a stubborn screw in a corner.

After assembly, you’ll enjoy a desk that feels sturdy enough to survive a small bouncy ball test, yet refined enough for a quieter, more professional home office vibe.
