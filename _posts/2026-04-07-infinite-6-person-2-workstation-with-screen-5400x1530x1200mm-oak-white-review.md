As with any large, multi-person workstation, the assembly process is the moment of truth. In practice, the assembly is a multi-person process. If you have a small team that’s used to building a set for a theater production, you’ll feel right at home. The good news is that once assembled, the desk tends to settle into its position and become the kind of thing you stop thinking about and start using—much like a good kitchen counter after you’ve cooked a dozen meals there.

## Ergonomics and usability for a multi‑user space

Space for six people implies a need for shared rhythm rather than individual reflexes. Ergonomically, this desk isn’t about every person having a personalized throne; it’s about minimizing friction when teams cluster, brainstorm, and annotate. Here are some practical observations:

- Height profile: The desk height is fixed, which is common for large, shared desks. If your team includes folks who vary in height or have specific accessibility needs, you’ll want to add adjustable chairs and perhaps a sit‑stand converter for each user, plus a sit‑stand mat in front of the desk so feet don’t hate life after a full day.
- Monitor ergonomics: With two workstations and a central screen, it’s wise to position primary monitors slightly angled toward the worker to reduce neck twist. A small monitor riser per workstation can be an excellent upgrade if each person is using a separate display.
- Keyboard and mouse placement: The surface is generous enough to allow for comfortable keyboard trays, but in a shared bench, you’ll love the ability to slide your peripherals to your personal sweet spot without bumping the neighbor’s elbow.
- Lighting and glare: The long plain surface can act as a light reflector. Depending on your office lighting, you may want to position the desk so that glare on the screens is minimized or add a subtle monitor hood for the central display.

In sum, ergonomics here aren’t magical; they rely on you configuring the space with the right accessories and keeping the awareness that a six‑seat setup is more about shared focus than one person’s perfect posture. If you’re building a creative lab, a software design studio, or a customer service hub, this desk gives you a canvas to design a people‑first workflow rather than a monitor‑centric fortress.

Cable management is where many desks of this size earn (or lose) their reputation. ## Maintenance tips for longevity

- Clean regularly with a mild wood-safe cleaner for the veneer to preserve the oak white finish. Avoid harsh solvents that can dull the surface or wear the finish prematurely.
- Check bolts and joints every few months. A loose joint on a 5400 mm long desk is not a problem you want to discover during a critical sprint review.
- Keep cables organized. This is not just for aesthetics; it reduces the risk of tugging power strips or unplugged devices during a crucial presentation.
- Consider protective mats for the surface in high-traffic zones. The desk’s surface can age gracefully, but a little extra care goes a long way toward resisting scratches and marks from everyday use.
