# UNO Attack! Electronic Card Game (Mattel, 2017) Review: Chaos, Cards, and Cardboard Fireworks

Welcome to the battlefield of family game night, where the coffee is lukewarm, the couch has seen better days, and a small plastic shooter is about to decide the fate of your evening. Today we dive into UNO Attack! Electronic Card Game, Mattel's 2017 edition that attempts to blend classic UNO spacing with the chaos of a slot-machine-inspired card launcher. If you thought regular UNO was enough of a cardio workout for your thumbs, this one demands sprinting reflexes, strategic yelling, and a surprising amount of patience for a game whose main goal is to empty your hand before the launcher explodes with confetti… metaphorically speaking.

## Overview

UNO Attack! is a spin on the tried-and-true UNO formula, but with a twist: instead of drawing from a plain deck, players may be hit with an electronic Card Launcher that can spit out 0, 1, 2, or more cards at anyone’s request (or worse, on its own whim). The 2017 Mattel revision refined the launcher for quieter operation and slightly more predictable randomness, which is basically the gaming equivalent of a polite dragon: still terrifying, but you can negotiate with it a little more easily than with the original chaos gremlin.

This game supports 2–10 players and is pitched for ages 7 and up. It is very much a party game that occasionally pretends to be a strategy title if your friends coerce it with a glare and a smug grin. The goal remains the same as classic UNO: be the first to discard all your cards. But in UNO Attack!, you’ll often win by pure luck, and sometimes by spectacularly overdoing your victory dance as a cascade of cards shoots out of the launcher.

## What’s in the Box (Components)

### The Card Launcher
The centerpiece of UNO Attack! is the electronic Card Launcher. It’s a plastic contraption with a red button that, when pressed, randomly dispenses 0–5 cards (sometimes more) into the player’s hand. It’s loud enough to wake a family of sleeping cats and visually dramatic enough to justify a dramatic squint or two. The launcher runs on batteries, which adds a delightful whiff of budget sci-fi to your living room—after all, nothing says “family bonding” like arguing about the power draw of a plastic blaster.

### The UNO Card Deck
This is your standard UNO deck with color-coded numbers, Draw Two, Skip, Reverse, Wild, and Wild Draw Four cards. The main twist is how often the launcher makes you draw more than one card, which can turn a calm game into a chaotic group therapy session very quickly. The 2017 Mattel edition refreshes some card art and inserts a few jokey card designs that nod to the launcher’s chaotic nature without crossing into the realm of pure gimmick.

### Rule Inserts and Player Aids
You’ll find a set of optional rules and quick-start guides tucked into the box. These are useful if you want to escape the classic “knees-grinding tension” of the base rules and try something wilder, like “every time someone says UNO, everyone else yells ‘Attack!’” (we’ll keep that as a suggestion, not a command from the launcher itself).

### Power and Durability
The launcher is plastic with a few moving parts that could become loose if you treat UNO Attack! like a high-stakes football game. In our tests, the device held up to the rough-and-tumble nature of family game night, though you’ll want to avoid slamming it on the table in a dramatic gesture. The cards themselves are standard UNO-quality: bendable enough to shuffle, stiff enough not to tear, and printed with enough color to resemble a small carnival.

## Setup and First Impressions

### Quick Setup
Setting up UNO Attack! is a breeze if you’re familiar with Uno, with one extra thing: the Card Launcher has to be placed within easy reach, and you’ll likely want to test a few cycles to ensure the batteries are fresh. The setup can be completed in under five minutes, especially if you’ve already pre-sorted your groups into “yellers,” “calculators,” and “you’re on thin ice” vibes.

### Aesthetic and Theme
The box art leans into the adrenaline of a game night gone slightly sideways, which is a vibe Geeknite fully endorses. The colors pop, the launcher glows with a nerdy neon red that says “we mean business,” and the card designs are as friendly as UNO cards can be while still making you question every decision you’ve made since kindergarten. The product doesn’t pretend to be subtle; it’s loud in the best possible way, which is perfect for a family game night where you want the kids and adults to have equal parts patience and panic.

## How to Play: The Core Mechanics

### The Turn Structure
- On your turn, you either play a card matching color or number or draw from the deck if you can’t. This is standard UNO fare. The twist is that the Draw action can be replaced by the Card Launcher’s spicy, unpredictable generosity.
- If you choose to press the launcher yourself to draw cards, prepare for the occasional stampede of tiny plastic missiles (okay, not missiles, but you get the idea). The launcher may instantly force you to draw 0–5 cards, changing your strategy on the fly. Your goal remains the same: get rid of all your cards first.
- Special cards (Skip, Reverse, Draw Two, Wild, Wild Draw Four) function as usual, but with the caveat that the launcher can pile on extra penalty when the random luck of the draw decides to be cruel.

### The Card Launcher’s Magic (and Mayhem)
The launcher can trigger after a draw, or sometimes randomly during gameplay. This unpredictability is what gives UNO Attack! its signature edge. The device adds a physical urgency that a regular UNO simply cannot replicate: you’re not just calculating the probability of drawing three cards; you’re actively preparing for the launcher to decide you need to “calm down” with a handful of new cards.

### Endgame and Winning Conditions
As always in UNO, you win by getting rid of all your cards. The presence of the launcher often creates dramatic finales: a tense countdown, a heroic last card that you almost played incorrectly, and a final reveal that leaves everyone yelling, in a good-natured way, about how chaotic a simple card game can become when technology is involved.

## Variants and Rule Tweaks
UNO Attack! isn’t shy about letting you tailor the chaos. Here are a few variants we found fun, safe, and occasionally sweaty:

### The Quick-Calm Variant
- Rule: When the launcher dispenses a total of 0 cards for a given player in one draw, that player must perform a celebratory mini-dance before resuming play. It’s silly, and the kids love it; the adults pretend they’re too cool for dances, and then cave after the second dance-off.

### The Stingy Launcher Variant
- Rule: If the launcher spits out more than four cards in one go, the player who drew has to tell a joke to diffuse the tension. The audience vote determines if the joke was legally funny or painfully dad-jokey. Either way, the laughter counts as “card play” in the long-term social scorecard of game night.

### The Cooperative Chaos Variant
- Rule: If any player runs out of cards, the entire group shuffles, and the launcher is allowed one “group reward” cycle where everyone draws a small number of cards to keep the chaos balanced. This is best used when a group has mixed skills in strategy and chaos tolerance.

## Pro and Cons: A Balanced Look

### Pros
- Engaging and chaotic: The launcher introduces a tactile, kinetic energy that ordinary UNO rarely achieves.
- Great for large groups: The game scales well from 2 players to a full party-night crowd.
- Encourages social interaction: The chaos invites cheering, groaning, and a surprising amount of storytelling around each draw.
- Replayability: The unpredictable nature of the launcher prevents the game from becoming stale after a few rounds.

### Cons
- Noise factor: It’s not a quiet game; expect some shouting, whooping, and occasional table-tapping of game-night nerves.
- Rule dependency: Some players get hung up on who actually drew what, especially when the launcher is involved. It’s easy to slip into “rules lawyer” territory, which can be charming or irritating depending on your group.
- Battery life: The launcher requires batteries, which means you’ll need back-up AAs if you’re hosting a marathon game night.
- Aging hardware risk: If you’re not careful with handling, the launcher’s mechanical parts can wear down faster than you’d like.

## Target Audience and Suitability

It’s a surprisingly good bridge between “clean, simple family game” and “let’s see what the launcher does this time.” If your family enjoys humor, competitive banter, and occasional mayhem, UNO Attack! comfortably lands in the sweet spot.

## Component Durability and Longevity
In terms of long-term use, UNO Attack! lives or dies by the launcher’s durability. The electronics are not fragile, but the mechanics can wear if you’re hammering it with high-intensity sessions. The cards are standard issue and should hold up to reasonable play; if you’re planning to slam this on the table as a daily ritual, you may want to invest in a stands or organizers to protect the launcher from accidental topples.

Aesthetically, the 2017 Mattel edition updates feel more modern and slightly sleeker than earlier runs. The color palette is vibrant but not overwhelming, which helps when you’re trying to read the color of a card mid-chaos. The cards slide smoothly, and the copying/duplication of Wild cards in the deck is a nice, subtle touch that keeps things interesting without becoming confusing.

## How This Stacks Up Against Classic UNO
- Speed: UNO Attack! tends to accelerate the tempo of play due to the random card spitting. Classic UNO can feel like a slow burn, while Attack often wants you to sprint toward the finish line.
- Skill vs. Luck: Classic UNO rewards a blend of skill and luck; Attack tilts much more toward luck, especially when the launcher micro-panic mode triggers. If you’re a control freak, Attack might challenge your sense of order.
- Social Experience: Attack dominates socially. The launcher creates a shared experience that makes every round feel special, even when you’re losing by ten cards.
- Replayability: Both have strong replayability, but Attack’s unpredictability means you’ll rarely feel like you’re replaying the same exact scenario.

In short: if you crave the traditional UNO puzzle but want more fireworks, Attack delivers. If you prefer a quieter, more predictable game with tighter strategy, you might lean back toward Classic UNO. If you want something that can handle big crowds with loud enthusiasm, it's hard to beat this version for party nights.

## Tips, Tricks, and House Rules
- Establish a soft-sell rule for the launcher: agree on a short “cool-down” period after a big draw to keep everyone from melting into chaos.
- Use a designated fan during the launcher cycles: a quick fan-out of cards helps the when-the-launcher-goes-berserk moments feel like dramatic reveals rather than frantic card-stepping.
- Keep a mental tally: a rough count of how many cards typically come out per draw helps you calibrate your strategy for the next rounds.
- Consider a quick rotation: rotate the launcher’s use among players to ensure everyone experiences the thrill and fear of the chaotic draw equally.
- Use the “two-lift” method: if someone tries to stack or manipulate the launcher, enforce a quick reset to keep gameplay honest and fun.
