## How Sushi Go! Works in a Nutshell (The Pick and Pass Part)
Sushi Go! is a card-drafting game, which is a fancy way of saying: you pick one card from your hand, pass the rest to your neighbor, and then reveal your chosen card. The next player picks one, passes the rest, and so on, until all cards are exhausted. Then you tally points based on the combinations you’ve assembled. It’s part memory, part tactical bling, and part “how many adorable combinations can we fit onto a tiny sheet of cardboard?”

Key mechanics to know before you dive in:
- Drafting loop: each round, pick one card, pass the rest. Your choices become a chain, and your neighbors’ choices ripple back through the table like tiny sushi waves.
- Card scoring: you’ll be chasing sets like “three nigiri” or “a pile of maki rolls,” while condensing the menu into something that resembles art class gone deliciously gourmet.
- End-game scoring: typically after three rounds (depending on player count), you’ll score and see who’s the most masterfully misinterpreting their own taste buds.

The “Pick and Pass” label emphasizes speed and social interaction—the reason it shines at casual game nights. It’s not a heavy strategy maze; it’s a confident sprint with a few fun decisions at each turn. The 2-5 player range makes it excellent for couples, roommates, and the “I brought a game, you brought snacks, let’s be friends” crowd. And yes, there’s a version with more content for bigger gatherings, but this base Sushi Go! remains a triumph of cocktail-party-meets-pantry-staple design.

## Practical Playthrough: A Quick How-To (For All Your Night-After-Work Sessions)
1. Shuffle the deck. Deal a hand to each player (the number of cards per player depends on your group size; the standard is three or four in a 2–5 player game).
2. Each player selects one card from their hand, places it face-down, then reveals it simultaneously.
3. The chosen card is kept by the player; the rest are passed to the next player.
4. Repeat until all cards are picked. Score the rounds according to the card combinations you formed.
5. Tally final scores and declare a winner who will probably still be wearing a hoodie with their gamer name on it.

There are a few scoring quirks to remember: most people forget the exact point values for each card type, so I recommend a quick skim of the scoring chart every few rounds to avoid the eternal question, “Wait, do I need three dumplings or four?” The official rulebook is short enough to memorize while microwaving leftovers, which is a plus in any post-dinner gaming session.
