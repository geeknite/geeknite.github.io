## How It Plays: Rules, Riffs, and the All Wild Variants

### Standard Flow, With a Twist
- The goal remains to discard all cards. Shouts of UNO still matter, but in All Wild there is a chorus of wild effects to navigate.
- If you cannot play a card, you draw from the draw pile as usual, but with All Wild you often have a second option to either bluff or draw more cards in a mini-challenge sequence.
- Wild cards still let you choose the color, but the additional effects can vary per card, sometimes forcing players heads up, sometimes turning the board into a color storm.

## Durability and Build Quality: How Well Do Two Packs Hold Up?

Two decks means you have two sets of cards that need to survive a little more wear and tear than a single deck would. The card stock is designed to be shufflable, but not too slick; you want the cards to hug each other during a chaotic shuffle rather than slip away with a smug little wave. The two-deck setup can cause more edge wear if you have a table full of enthusiasts who like to peel, twist, and riff on repositioning cards mid game. If you treat your cards like a tiny battle flag, you will probably be happier with a protective sleeve or a dedicated card box for each deck.

### Shuffling, Stacking, and Longevity
If you have two decks, you can do interesting things like stack a few wilds at the bottom to ensure a dramatic reveal, or you can alternate decks to test how consistent the color distribution appears over time. In my testing, the All Wild set held up well after several rounds, with only minor scuffs on a handful of cards. That is normal wear in a game night universe. If you plan a marathon session, consider a quick refresh with a hand wipe or a dedicated card clump to keep the deck from becoming a messy hazard and turning your living room into a tactile obstacle course.
