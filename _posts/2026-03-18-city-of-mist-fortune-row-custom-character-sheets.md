## What Fortune Row Changes About City of Mist
City of Mist uses a blend of Mythos and Logos to model heroes and their double lives. Fortune Row leans into that tension like a neon sign that keeps flickering on and off during a power surge. The setting is a place where con artists, insiders, and dream thieves rub shoulders with investigators, atypical detectives, and the occasional myth come to life. The result is a sheet that challenges you to represent two personas, allies and enemies, and a deck of Fate Scores that unlock story beats as you push the city toward or away from disaster.

Custom character sheets for Fortune Row do more than prettify fonts. They encode the narrative logic of the district into the math of rolls and the layout of your character sheet, so your sheet becomes a storytelling tool rather than a mere data entry form. If you treat your sheet like a character companion, you can offload a significant chunk of narrative scaffolding to the sheet itself. The right sheet makes it easy to see your hero’s factions, debt, favors, and the moral lines they will cross to achieve their goals. The wrong sheet will punish you with misalignment and cryptic scribbles in the margins that make your fellow players suspicious you doodled a plan to overthrow the GM’s authority through a multi-step, board-game-like scheme.

## Why a Custom Fortune Row Sheet is Worth Building
There are three big reasons you might want to build or customize a Fortune Row sheet rather than rely on a generic City of Mist template:

- Narrative clarity: Fortune Row thrives on a tapestry of relationships and myths. A well-designed sheet visually encodes the relationships and story hooks you care about most, so you can see your narrative threads at a glance instead of hunting through prose notes.
- Mechanical efficiency: The right layout maps Mythos, Logos, Clues, and Favors in a way that aligns with how you roleplay. You’ll find yourself making fewer token trips across the table to grab a piece of paper and remind yourself what you can justify with a single die roll.
- Campaign adaptability: Fortune Row campaigns often shift tone—from caper to tragedy to surreal myth. A robust sheet can adapt with you, providing sections for new factions, debts, or alliances you discover along the way without requiring a complete redesign.

If you want to save time, you can start with a solid base layout and then layer in custom sections as your group grows into the arc. The payoff is a character sheet that feels like a character’s second personality: practical when you need numbers, vivid when you want to tell a story.

## Designing the Fortune Row Sheet: Core Principles
Before you draw a single line on a new sheet, set a few ground rules. They’ll save you countless tweaking sessions later and keep your table from devolving into a crusade against the order of lines and boxes.

### Clarity over complexity
Fortune Row is rich, but your sheet should not be a labyrinth. Use a clear hierarchy: big, bold module titles; a two-column approach for Mythos and Logos; and a dedicated area for Debt, Favors, and Relationships. If someone can’t find a key stat in 30 seconds, you’ve failed your audience.

### Narrative anchors
Give each major section a narrative hook. Instead of generic fields for Strength and Agility, label them with terms that fit Fortune Row: Velvet Touch, Streetwise Grit, or Masked Intuition. For Mythos, you might list Iconic Forms, while Logos gets paired with Public Identity and Secret Power Verbs. The sheet should whisper the vibe of Fortune Row every time you glance at it.

### Modularity
Your sheet should invite additions. Leave blank corners with annotations like add a new faction here or a bespoke debt tracker there. Fortune Row evolves as players uncover new connections; your sheet should accommodate that evolution without requiring a full redesign.

### Visual rhythm
Fortune Row thrives on neon contrasts: dark backgrounds with electric accents, or an ink-on-vellum aesthetic for a noir vibe. Pick a color palette that makes categories pop: Mythos in a blood-red accent, Logos in electric cyan, Clues in amber. The right palette helps players navigate the dense information without needing a magnifying glass.

### Accessibility and readability
This is a place where sheet design can either invite people in or turn them off. Use readable fonts, appropriate line spacing, and high-contrast headings. If you’re sharing the sheet online, consider a printer-friendly mode and a high-contrast variant for dim tavern lights where Fortune Row games are often held.

## A Step-by-Step Build: Starting from Scratch
If you’re new to Fortune Row or City of Mist in general, here’s a pragmatic path to build your custom sheet while keeping the process fun, not fiddly.

### Step 1: Define your archetype and mythos blend
Fortune Row is a place to play with identities. Start by picking two central themes that represent your character’s Mythos and Logos. Do you lean into a high-society Mirage that uses masks to influence outcomes, or a scrappy streetwise Charmer who talks their way out of trouble? Write these down as the two pillars of your sheet. They’ll guide the layout and the word choices that fill the boxes.

### Step 2: Map the core sections to the sheet layout
A clean Fortune Row sheet often includes these core blocks:
- Mythos: your mythic identity, signature moves, and the form your legends take when you step into the spotlight.
- Logos: your everyday identity, public persona, and the skills you rely on to navigate the city without drawing down on your myths.
- Clues and Cases: a grid for clues discovered, cases connected, and how you’re piecing the mystery together.
- Conflict and Debt: a tracker for debts owed, favors granted, and how those bonds ripple through your relationships.
- Faction and Contacts: a roster of allies, enemies, and social ties that shape your choices.
- Gear and Tricks: items you can draw on in a pinch and a few flavor moves that set you apart.

Design the sheet so each block has a quick-glance summary line, a few lines for the dice-based mechanics, and a narrative prompt to keep the session from turning into a spreadsheet shopping spree.

### Step 3: Build the anatomy of moves and dice pools
City of Mist uses a dice-based system that encourages narrative flexibility. A Fortune Row sheet should reflect this by offering a compact method to track dice pools, triggers for special moves, and quick access to the effects of each Mythos or Logos decision. Use visually distinct boxes for the different pools (Mythos dice vs Logos dice) and consider a tiny legend that explains what each box represents. A clean legend is worth many late-night FAQs from players who forgot how many dice they rolled last turn.

### Step 4: Create variants and printer options
Provide at least two variants: a compact print-friendly version and a deluxe digital version. The print-friendly variant should trim margins, simplify the fonts, and reduce the number of decorative flourishes. The deluxe digital version can include tooltips, collapsible sections, and a sidebar with links to common moves. If you’re sharing the sheet in a community hub, consider packaging a PDF with editable fields for online play as well as a neat printable version.

### Step 5: Add a few example entries
People love seeing models in action. Add a small example character where Mythos and Logos are clearly at odds and a couple of sample debts and favors. The goal is to spark ideas, not to lock someone into a prewritten template. A good example helps players understand how a sheet can support their story rather than constrain it.

## Sample Layout: A Quick Visual Walkthrough
Here is a conceptual layout to illustrate the flow. Remember, you don’t need to reproduce this exact template; use it as inspiration to craft your own unique Fortune Row sheet.

- Header: Character name, Player name, Sheet version, Campaign arc.
- Right Column: Mythos – Iconic Form, Signature Moves, Current Boon/Curse.
- Left Column: Logos – Public Identity, Core Skills, Secret Power Verbs.
- Center: Clues and Cases – a grid with Case Title, Clue, Connected NPCs, Moment of Truth triggers.
- Bottom Section: Debt, Favors, Relationships – a compact ledger that tracks obligations and the consequences of breaking them.
- Footer: Gear and Tricks – essential items with quick-use tags and a space for creative moves.

This layout keeps the mythic drama visible while making the mechanics approachable. If you’re printing, consider a two-page spread: one for Mythos/Logos and one for Clues/Relationships. If you’re playing digitally, you can add interactive fields or checkboxes to speed up play and cut down on fiddly data entry.

## A Concrete Example: Fortune Row Character Sketch
Let’s conjure a quick exemplar to show how the sheet can sing when properly tuned. Meet Mira Kage, a Velvet Thief who moonlights as a public librarian—an evocative contradiction that fortune-tellers would call a paradox, not a persona. Mira’s Mythos is Night Silk, a form that allows her to weave shadows into paths of least resistance. Her Logos is Community Lantern, a public-facing identity built to gather people and information while keeping her true nature just foggy enough to avoid direct encounter with the mythic underbelly.

- Mythos: Night Silk – Signature Moves: Weave the Night (short-range shadow movement), Silk-Strike (stealthy takedown with a stylish flourish).
- Logos: Community Lantern – Public Identity: Mira Kage, Librarian; Core Skills: Streetwise, Persuasion, Investigation.
- Clues: A stolen manuscript, a coded flyleaf, and a whisper about a secret archive under Fortune Row.
- Debt/Favors: Owes a favor to a bookstore owner; returns favors with rare books and quiet doors.
- Relationships: A rival con artist who suspects Mira’s librarian cover; a mentor who runs the secret archive.
- Gear: A pocket mirror that reveals glimpses of truth, a lockpick set disguised as a bookmark.

This is the kind of synergy a good Fortune Row sheet should encourage. The sheet should let you drop in a Mira-like character and instantly see the theme threads: the dual identity, the community ties, and the mythic leverage at play in every scene.

If you’re crafting a sheet for Mira, you might also want to create a small, dedicated box for a “Moment of Truth” trigger that triggers when a clue closes a case or when a debt is repaid in a surprising way. It’s these small narrative mechanics that bring Fortune Row to life, and a well-designed sheet makes those moments obvious and delightful rather than accidental and confusing.

## Tools, Assets, and Practical Tips
If you’re a maker who loves to hyper-optimize your tabletop toolkit, here are a few practical tips to streamline production and sharing across your group.

- Use a consistent typography system. A strong pair of font choices—one for headings and one for body text—helps with readability in the heat of the moment. In Fortune Row, where mood changes are frequent, typography becomes a storytelling cue.
- Create a printable PDF and a digital editable version. Some players will want the tactile experience of a printed sheet, while others will prefer a filled-out, shareable digital file. A good approach is to provide both, but ensure the digital version exports cleanly to common formats like PDF or a fillable form.
- Build an optional “play sheet” for quick reference. Some players like to keep a very lean version of the sheet for on-table use. A two-page quick-access sheet with only the most frequently used fields can keep the table moving along when time is tight.
- Include a section for session-by-session notes. Fortune Row is a vibe-heavy game. A small notes area for session highlights or epilogues helps the group remember important moments without having to reconstruct them from memory later.
- Use external references to expand the world. If you want to connect Fortune Row with canonical lore beyond your table, add links to official sources and fan-made expansions. You can link to sources like the City of Mist official site for setting inspiration and to fan forums for ideas about how other groups handle the Mythos/Logos balance.

## The Geeknite Verdict: Is Fortune Row Worth It?
If you are running or joining a City of Mist table that wants to lean into the mythic noir aesthetic and you crave a sheet that supports storytelling as much as mechanics, Fortune Row is a strong candidate for your next project. The columnar approach, the two-identity tension, and the flexible sections for debts, favors, and relationships all align with how players want to narrate a character who exists in many layers. The best Fortune Row sheets feel less like a static form and more like a living, breathing character companion that you can customize as your hero discovers new powers, new debts, and new confidants.

That said, the success of your sheet depends on your group. If your players are happiest with minimalist, text-light sheets, you may want to pare down the extra fancy sections and keep it lean. If your table thrives on a lush, illustrated, playable novella vibe, you’ll want to push the aesthetics, add art prompts, and incorporate decorative elements that reflect your table’s mood. Either way, a Fortune Row sheet is a design challenge that pays off in session quality and a shared sense of mythic possibility.

As with any custom sheet project, start with a prototype, gather feedback after a couple of sessions, and iterate. The real magic is not in the layout alone but in how the sheet helps your players tell bigger, bolder stories without losing track of the small, human details that make a character feel alive. Fortune Row rewards creative players who can balance the glamour of myth with the grit of a street-smart identity. If you can pull that off, every session becomes a little carnival of neon, narrative, and new legends.

## Final Thoughts and Practical Takeaways
- Start with a minimal, clean baseline. You can always add flourishes later. The first version should be readable, navigable, and capable of handling a few sessions without needing a reprint.
- Prioritize two-column Mythos/Logos readability. The two personas are the backbone of City of Mist games and should be instantly scannable.
- Include a relationships and debts ledger. Fortune Row thrives on connections; a well-tracked web of favors will save you time and headaches when the plot thickens.
- Provide a quick-reference Clues/Cases section. You’ll save your players and GM precious minutes by summarizing key clues and case arcs in a single glance.
- Make it shareable. In a group setting, the ability to export or copy a sheet for a new character is worth its weight in neon-lit coin.
