## What is Polilla Tramposa?
Polilla Tramposa is a social bluffing card game designed for multiple players, where the central mechanic is lying with flair and then waiting for someone to call your bluff—or not. The moth in the title isn’t just a cute metaphor; it’s a symbol of the game’s tension between truth and fiction, fluttering through each hand like a wingbeat that might be the heartbeat of the next lie. The rules are designed to be intuitive, so you can spend more time performing and less time paging through the rulebook.

### Theme and Aesthetic
The art direction leans bright and cheeky with a cartoonish moth motif that signals “fun first.” You’ll see vibrant color palettes and humorous icons that communicate actions at a glance, so even if you’re juggling snacks and a conversation about a coworker’s cat, you won’t miss the moment you need to bluff or challenge a bluff. The Spanish naming lends the game a cultural flavor that makes the experience feel a bit more theatrical and a little less generic. It’s not trying to be edgy; it’s aiming to be memorable—and it succeeds.

## How to Play: A Quick Rundown
The goal is simple: accumulate mischief points through successful bluffs and clever plays, while catching your friends bluffing just enough times to keep the room honest (or at least laughing). Here’s the gist:

- Setup: Shuffle the deck and deal five cards to each player. Place the moth token in the center and decide on a target mischief score (for example, 6 or 8) that feels right for your group size. The game scales nicely: with more players, you can use more cards per hand and a higher target score to stretch the night.
- Turn structure: On your turn, you draw a card, then attempt to play a card face down while declaring a value or action that may or may not match the card you drew. This is where you start the story you want others to believe.
- Bluff or be called: Other players watch and listen. At any point, a player may call the bluff by saying “Polilla!” The card is revealed; if your declared content matches the actual card, the caller loses a mischief chip; if you bluffed (the card didn’t match your claim), you must lose a chip yourself. The mechanic rewards being convincing and punishing over-tyranny of truth.
- End of round: After a fixed number of turns or when a player hits the target mischief score, the round ends and the score is tallied. The first player to reach the target mischief score wins, though some play groups prefer a “best of three rounds” approach for drama.
- House rules: A lot of groups mix in “no-peeking” variants or add extra bluff cards to create more chaos. The rules are designed to be flexible so you can tailor the silly factor to your vibe.

### Round-by-Round Playthrough (Mini Example)
Let’s imagine a five-player game: Ana, Bruno, Carla, Diego, and Elena. The goal is to reach 6 mischief points first.
- Round 1: Ana starts by drawing a card and claims she’s playing a King of Vines (a bluff). She secretly played a lower card. Elena calls “Polilla!”. The truth comes out: Ana played a 4 of Wingbeats. She loses a mischief chip, Elena smiles and earns a point (or the group flips a mischief chip depending on your house rule).
- Round 2: Bruno wants to set the tone. He lays down a number of five cards in a single motion (a wacky multi-card flourish) and says something about a “moth banquet.” The field chooses to call or not; when the bluff is revealed, someone else pays the price.
- Round 3: Carla tries a different strategy—short, sharp statements with a confident body language. The group begins to see patterns: which players play with rapid-fire certainty, which players drag out the truth. It’s social science in card form.
- Rounds accumulate. By mid-game, relationships get a little heated; jokes become more pointed, and the table erupts when someone’s bluff is too good to be true and the revelation is hilariously mundane (like the card is a “Blue Moon Snack” instead of a “King of Vines”). The moth gets a bigger fan club each round as the tension rises and the laughter swells.

### Strategy and Etiquette
The elegance of Polilla Tramposa lies in balancing fear and gusto.
- Tell a story that’s almost true: Your bluff should feel plausible enough that others pause, but not so plausible that they’re convinced instantly.
- Watch micro-expressions: People often signal when they’re about to call a bluff; learn to read that edge and time your own bluffs accordingly.
- Use pauses to your advantage: A well-placed pause before naming a card or action can sell your bluff and create suspense.
- Keep it friendly: The goal is to entertain. If the table gets tense, switch to lighter variants or take a short break. The moth thrives on smiles, not scowls.
