## Introduction
City of Mist: Fortune Row is not just another pretty coffee-table book; it’s a love letter to the GMs who spend their nights turning rainy alleys into propulsive adventures and their players into walking, talking myths. The Deluxe Edition of the Fortune Row Art Book leans into that same energy, offering something that feels less like a product and more like a conspiratorial artifact you’d stash in your GM screen.

![Fortune Row Deluxe Edition Cover](/assets/images/city-of-mist-fortune-row-deluxe-cover.jpg)

### What is Fortune Row?
Fortune Row is a curated section of the City of Mist universe—a noir-inflected landscape where your heroes’ legends intersect with the city’s most crooked corners. It’s the kind of place you visit once and then you’re convinced your players will return again and again, each time with new hat tips on their badges and new dealers in the shadows. The art book aims to translate small moments into big visuals: a streetlamp catching rain, a velvet rope at a speakeasy, a fingerprint on a cigarette pack that might unlock a patron’s secret.

### Deluxe Edition: What’s inside?
- A hardcover art book around 110-120 pages (you’ll hear different numbers wherever you look; we’re going to call it “about a hundred-something pages” and smile politely).
- A linen slipcase with foil embossing that shimmers when you tilt it in a dimly lit gaming room.
- A set of nine premium art prints, each roughly 8x10 or 9x12, printed on heavyweight stock.
- A fold-out map panel of Fortune Row, showing key landmarks, alleyways, and the occasional occult doorway.
- A sticker sheet and a small bookplate sheet for your GM screen or cozy gaming nook.
- A certificate of authenticity for the true believers who keep their shelf inventory in order.

The design mass sits on a table that could double as a prop for a one-shot: the cover art is a smoky, moody composition—art deco meets street poetry. The spine has metallic ink that catches the light the moment your players lean in to squint at the tiny signature on the bottom corner.

### Art direction and style
Fortune Row’s art direction nods to early 20th-century illustration, with a modern digital polish. It’s not a slavish recreation of vintage magazines; rather, it borrows their mood—the high-contrast shadows, the chiaroscuro silhouettes, the uncanny balance of glamour and threat—and pushes it through a modern lens. The result is something that feels timeless, not derivative. The illustrations aren’t just pretty; they’re narrative beats: a portrait of a patron whose smile suggests you’ve just walked into a debt you’ll never quite pay off; a guard’s silhouette as a doorway to a secret; a neon sign that hums with a rumor about a "Fortune Row" figure who controls more than just a person’s luck.

### Notable pieces and why they matter to your game
The book is filled with pieces that spark sessions without hamstringing your players with too much lore. There are two or three pieces that would look fantastic on a GM’s mood board to set tone for a campaign arc: a double-page spread of a rain-soaked boulevard at midnight, a close-up of a character’s ring carved with arcane symbols, and a panoramic view of Fortune Row’s central plaza with flickering signage.

If you’re a GM who loves “show, don’t tell,” you’ll appreciate how many panels double as prompts. One illustration of a masked performer and their entourage hints at a covert pact that could become a session’s central mystery; another page shows a street vendor whose wares are more than they appear—perhaps the goods are psionic talismans in disguise.

### Layout, materials, and production quality
Deluxe Editions are often about how the physical object makes you feel when you’re holding it. Fortune Row delivers: the cover texture is a tactile treat—roughly linen with subtle embossing and a foil title that catches the light when someone near you looks away from a screen too long. The pages themselves are not thin newspaper stock; they’re a slightly heavier weight, with a matte finish that reduces glare during late-night table sessions. The paper color leans toward a warm ivory, which helps the artwork pop without fighting the ink. The prints included in the bundle use similarly premium stock, and the color reproduction holds up under close inspection; it’s the kind of art you can zoom into with a magnifier and still be impressed.

I should note: if you’ve ever opened a deluxe art book and found that it’s mostly “great art but the back cover flaps around like a loose leaf,” Fortune Row avoids that trap. The slipcase is snug but not suffocating, and the fold-out map doesn’t feel like it’s going to tear the moment you unfold it. The bookplate and sticker sheet are crisp, and the certificate of authenticity is a charming touch for collectors who keep track of their editions in a spreadsheet.

### How it informs the game: storytelling, not just decoration
One of the book’s strongest features is how it translates art into toolkits for play. You’ll notice that most pages aren’t just “here’s a pretty portrait” but “this could become a session anchor.” A character sheet for a Fortune Row patron is paired with a visual motif that can become a recurring symbol—an emblem you can reference on a character sheet, a map pin, or a prop in-game. The artwork invites improvisation: it’s the GM’s equivalent of rolling a die and saying, “That color palette suggests a mood; choose your encounter accordingly.”

If you’re a storytelling GM, you’ll appreciate the deliberate ambiguity. The art rarely spells out a single meaning; it suggests multiple possibilities, letting you decide which route your players should walk. The book avoids forcing a canonical interpretation of Fortune Row, instead offering a springboard for your own myths to take shape.

### How it compares to the standard art book
The standard art book (non-deluxe) gives you a scaled-down portfolio of images, a handful of concept sketches, and a few glossy pages that look terrific on a screen. The Deluxe Edition leans into the tactile and the immersive: more pages, more heavy stock, and the extras that create a “gift you’ll want to display” vibe rather than something you’ll keep in the shipping box. If you’re the kind of person who keeps a shelf of RPG paraphernalia to make your room look like a curio cabinet, this edition slots nicely into that decor. It turns your gaming space into a shrine to neon-lit myth, and that’s not a bad thing if your players expect a show.

### Space and accessibility for players
Fortune Row’s art-heavy approach is an asset for GMs who want to avoid heavy text and power through session planning with visuals. However, if you’re a player who adores lore dumps, you may find the book more satisfying when used as a collaborative prompt generator than as a direct source of long paragraphs of history. The layouts emphasize images first, captions second, and player-facing content third. That’s not a flaw; it’s the design ethos: inspiration before exposition.

### A designer’s note: what went right and what could be better
In any deluxe project, there are trade-offs. Fortune Row nails most of the core decisions: premium materials, consistent art direction, careful binding, and a coherent expansion of Fortune Row’s world through images. If there’s a place to improve, it would be in the density of accompanying text to anchor certain images to specific adventures. Some panels scream for a one-page GM guide beneath, a tiny “how to use this image in a session” blurb. If you’re the kind of GM who wants that, you could easily add your own notes in the margins or on sticky cards that live alongside your prints.

### Where to buy and what to expect price-wise
Official retailers and the publisher often run bundle sales of deluxe editions with some variation by region. Expect a price in the ballpark of a premium art book plus prints; not quite collector’s coffee-table-book pricing, but enough to make a GM pause and whisper, “Maybe I’ll justify it by running a Fortune Row arc.” If you’re a hardcore completist who wants every inch of this world mapped and celebrated, the Deluxe Edition is probably worth the extra scrimping and saving. If you’re a casual collector, you may still enjoy the tactile quality and the mood it creates—your gaming nights deserve a little more drama than a stack of loose sheets.

### Easter eggs and hidden references
The designers tucked in tiny nods to fan-favorite moments from earlier City of Mist runs. A background mural may reference a lesser-known patron who appears in a late-sprint adventure, while a prop in the foreground points to a side quest you can drop into your campaign. If you treat the book as a scavenger hunt, you’ll discover little prompts that pay off when you least expect them. It’s the kind of detail that makes long-running campaigns feel stitched together by shared memory rather than random encounters.

### Interaction with other materials in the line
If you’ve enjoyed the core rulebook and other art volumes, Fortune Row acts as a bridge between them. The mood and motifs echo the city’s broader mythos while offering a concentrated, location-based focus. It’s easy to slot Fortune Row-art inspired scenes into a longer arc that runs through several supplements. Think of it as a color palette you can pull out when you want a session to feel unmistakably neon-noir rather than vanilla-noir.

### How to use Fortune Row in your table setup
- Create a moodboard on your table using the included prints; rotate them as you shift scenes to help players “see” the setting.
- Use the fold-out map panel as a narrative map for a heist or infiltration sequence; reveal key locations as players succeed in clues.
- Place emblematic objects on the table—like the carved ring or the mysterious talisman— and weave them into your players’ backstories.
- Let the art suggest tone: if a particular image feels ominous, consider a more suspenseful or ominous encounter instead of a direct confrontation.

### The verdict: who should buy this edition?
- GMs who want a strong mood board for a Fortune Row arc.
- Collectors who prize tactile RPG artifacts and high-quality prints.
- Players who enjoy exploring a city’s mystique through images, even if they don’t read every caption.

If you want to maximize your return on time and money, this edition is best for groups who value inspiration and ambiance as part of the fight against a few stubborn dice. It’s not a necessity for every group, but it is the kind of object that elevates a possibly routine campaign night into something memorable—one you’ll still be talking about around the table months later.
