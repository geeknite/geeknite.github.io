## Surface quality, durability, and display tips
### Handling a holo with care
Foil cards are beautiful but sensitive. The surface can scratch more easily than matte counterparts, especially when shuffled aggressively or stored in pockets with zippers and keys. If you intend to show off this card, consider a soft cape or a top-load sleeve with an inner liner that minimizes friction. A soft microfiber cloth is your best friend for removing fingerprints that may accumulate during a long unboxing video or a particularly excited unwrapping at a local store.

### Lighting and photographing holo finishes
If you’re a content creator, you know the struggle: lighting that reveals the subtle rainbow sheen without turning the card into a glare trap. The recommended approach is to use diffuse light, a gentle two-point LED setup, and a lightbox if you’re serious about photography. Angle matters. A 20–25 degree tilt can reveal the holo’s micro-flakes while avoiding overpowering hotspots. A tripod helps, as does patience—because you’ll want to capture the moment when the card’s surface seems to glow with the confidence of a cat who finally learned a new trick.

### Grading considerations for holo cards
If you go the grading route, expect the grader to scrutinize centering, edge wear, and surface defects that can be aggravated by the holo finish. The surface inspection becomes a little art critique: you’re looking for the perfect fingerprint-free shine, a minimal edge roughness, and a surface that doesn’t show dull patches under standard lighting. For JP prints, you may find slightly crisper lines and more consistent color saturation, which is a boon when you’re aiming to maximize visual impact on photo posts.
