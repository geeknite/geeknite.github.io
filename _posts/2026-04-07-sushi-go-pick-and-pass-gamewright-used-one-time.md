## What Sushi Go is all about

Sushi Go is a drafting game built around a simple premise. Each round you start with a hand of cards. You pick one card to keep, then pass the rest to the left. When everyone has picked, the chosen cards are revealed and scored according to the card types you have collected. The process repeats for three rounds, with a fresh deck each round. The goal is to accumulate the most points by collecting sets of sushi cards that reward you for the right combos. The counts and scoring are straightforward, but the joy comes from reading the table and planning two steps ahead without turning the table into a strategy engine that requires a degree in combinatorics.

This used one time copy still feels fresh on the table because the core loop is so clean. There is no heavy setup, no ruler to memorize, and no appendix of 47 edge cases to survive. The theme remains bright and accessible, a factor that helps it age gracefully on many shelves. ## How to play in a quick primer

- Players and setup: 2 to 5 players. Each round, deal a hand of cards to everyone. The exact hand size scales with the player count, but the flow remains the same: you pick one card and pass the rest, keep one, and pass again until all cards in the hand are taken.
- The core loop: On your turn you take one card from your current hand and pass the rest to the next player. After everyone has chosen, all cards are revealed, kept, and scored. Then a new round begins with a new hand that has fewer cards, ramping up the tension while staying breezy.
- Scoring basics: Nigiri cards give points, sashimi rewards a set of three, maki provides the longest set for extra points, tempura yields points in pairs, and pudding scores at the end of the game. The exact numbers are small and the cadence of scoring is gentle, which makes the game accessible but still gives you something to think about as you place each card.

The three round structure ensures pacing that fits into a lunch break or a short game night. There is enough variety that repeated plays still feel fresh, but the overall footprint remains familiar, which reduces the intimidation factor for new players.

## Strategy and decisions you will actually make at the table

- First pick dynamics: In the opening moments, you are balancing two forces. You want a card that gives you a meaningful trajectory toward a good score, but you also want to shape what your neighbor can take on their next turn. Will you lean toward an early sorbet or a heavy main course of nigiri? The answer depends on your group and your appetite for risk.
- Passing as a weapon and a shield: Passing the right card can block a competitor from a highly valued combo or bait them into chasing something that is unlikely to pay off. This is the light element of interaction that Sushi Go offers without veering into hard negotiation or direct conflict.
- Memory and probability: As rounds cycle, you begin to predict what is likely to return around the table. If you notice that two strong sushi types just got taken by the person to your left, you might pivot toward a different path to avoid being stuck with a weak ending hand.
- End game pressure: In the late game, the pudding cards become a narrative pivot. The pudding score can swing a result from good to great or from tidy to middling. It forces you to weigh whether delaying for a possible flop is worth it when time is short and the table is watching you like hawks.

This is where the charm lies: the decisions are approachable but not trivial, and the social tilt keeps the table smiling even as you calculate probability like a caffeinated spreadsheet wizard.

## Card anatomy and trickier interactions

- Card types carry distinct scoring weights. Nigiri carry base points, sashimi require a trio for value, maki rewards based on the number of cards of the same type in a round where the longest run wins points, and pudding points are tallied at the end of the game. This arrangement yields a pleasant cascade of micro decisions across the three rounds.
- The rule of threes with sashimi means you often gamble on whether you can snag the trio before someone else clears it. The thrill comes from the moment you see that you might be able to pull off the combo if you commit to a specific path, even if your current hand is not ideal.
- Puddings offer late round swing but can also be a trap if you chase them too late and miss a bigger opportunity. This balancing act between early and late scoring presses players to plan ahead without forcing mind boggling calculus.
