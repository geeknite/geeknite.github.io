## The Sculpt and Materials
Fortune Row’s strength is in the sculpting—the characters have physique, silhouette, and posture that telegraph their role in a single glance. You’ll notice a mix of heroic stances and more casual, urban poses that capture the City of Mist vibe: a detective leaning on a lamppost, a street-smart rogue flashing a sly smile, a mystic with a cloak that seems to billow even when there is no breeze. The level of detail, from fabric folds to accessory rings, demonstrates a commitment to quality that should satisfy both painters and painters-at-heart who just want to display something that looks expensive without requiring a full studio setup.

Material choices appear to be resin or high-density plastic with a solid understructure. The weight is satisfying—enough heft to feel premium but not so heavy that you worry about bending a peg or stressing a joint. The miniatures are designed with a degree of modularity in mind, which means you can mix and match heads or props to create a handful of distinct silhouettes from the same base line. This is especially useful if you want to run a loosely connected Fortune Row arc that changes its membership depending on the city’s mood and the night’s fog density.

Edge detail on accessories and clothing is where the sculpt shines. You’ll notice micro-pearls on jewelry, tiny buckles on belts, and even minute stitching lines along coats. If you’re a paint devotee who loves to highlight texture, Fortune Row provides a fairly generous surface area to practice dry brushing and subtle shading. If you’re someone who buys minis to avoid painting, you’ll still enjoy the sculpt’s ready-to-go look, though a no-swatch base coat will still work better than arriving on the scene with a flat gray blob.

## Paint Quality and Cleaning
Paint quality is often the deciding factor between a display piece and a playable miniature. Fortune Row’s paint job lands in the “good enough for a display shelf” category with occasional moments of swoon-worthy detail. Expect crisp delineation on facial features and accessories, with shadowing that adds depth without going into the realm of heavy-handed shading. Some pieces benefit from extra touch-ups if you want a truly show-stopping display, but the overall finish is solid right out of the package.

The color palette is a mood mix—dark blues, charcoals, brass accents, and occasional pops of red or emerald to signal faction or identity. The contrast helps individual figures pop against a tabletop with fog effects or urban backdrops. If you plan to photograph your game sessions for social media, Fortune Row scales well under lighting setups designed for macro shots: you’ll capture the glint on metal studs, the sheen of leather, and the subtle wear on gloves that tells a story about each character’s past heist.

If you’re a picky painter who wants flawless skin tones or gradient capes, you’ll probably want to invest a little extra time with base coats and some gentle washes. The models respond well to dry brushing and selective edge highlighting, and the molds are forgiving enough for beginners to practice with without feeling like they ruined a rare resin figure. The only caveat is plastic seams in some of the joint areas. They’re not glaring, but you’ll want to take a hobby knife and a steady hand to tidy them up if you’re chasing a museum-grade finish.

### Maintenance and Care Tips
To keep Fortune Row looking their best, follow a few simple care steps. Wipe down with a soft brush to remove dust, avoid direct sunlight for prolonged periods (to prevent paint fading), and store in a cool, dry environment. If you accidentally knock a piece loose, a tiny dab of clear plastic cement can reattach it—just be sure to hold the part until the bond cures. And if you’re going to store multiple figures, consider a foam-lined case, which reduces chipping and minimizes the chance you’ll wake up to a tiny morning after of broken statues staring at you from the shelf.
