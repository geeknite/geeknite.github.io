Welcome back, fellow collectors and budget mages. What does All Rares Only actually mean in practice? It means your box will contain a lot of rares from various sets spanning two decades. Some will be modern and shiny, others will be older and a bit shy about chrome. There will be duplicates, yes, but not a mind-bending six copies of the same Korlash, Fear Argus. The vendor is advertising low duplicates; your mileage may vary, depending on how the seller handled their inventory and whether the warehouse was previously used as a break room for draft night drama.

### The Promise and the Pitfalls

When a listing screams All Rares Only, the imagination runs wild. We picture glorious mythics turning into memes, and every pack in the future will feel like it has a shiny rainbow in it. The reality is more like a curated chaos. You may encounter:

- A pile of rares that look like they survived a small card-ocalypse (edges fuzzy, corners rounded, but still presentable for playability)
- A few undesirable pips and some mythics that are not strictly valuable but still fun to own as conversation starters
- The occasional blank or double-sided misprint that will make your friends say Hmm and you say: yes, this is the content we ordered, not a misprint of reality

To illustrate, I opened a sample of the lot and kept a rough log of what I found: 3 Swords to Plowshares, 2 Llanowar Elves, a Dragon's Claw that looked like a dragon decided to take a nap on the card stock, and a foil from a recent set that felt more excited about shipping than being revered by collectors. The moral: expect variety, but also set aside space for the unexpected, like a friend who insists they play only Uro decks and somehow always ends up winning anyway.

### Components of the Lot: What’s Under the Hood

In a typical All Rares bulk partition, you’ll see:

- Rares from classic sets (Dominaria, Urza’s Saga era vibes)
- Reserved and unreserved Rare cards from mid 2000s to late 2010s
- A handful of older but not ancient rares that still fetch a price on some days
- No stamps of power or unbelievably valuable cards in most cases, unless the seller pulled a rabbit from the hat

The beauty of the bulk is its unpredictability. If you’re a gambler at heart, you’ll love this. If you’re a serious price researcher, you’ll treat this like a scavenger hunt where the prize is sometimes a $3 rare that could moonlight as a display piece. It’s not going to fund your retirement, but it might fund your next basic land flood party.

### Shipping, Price, and the Free-Shipping Mirage

Pricing on bulk rares is all about the math you don’t do at 2 am. Prices per card drop dramatically when you buy in bulk, but the real savings depend on shipping costs and how many duplicates you’re willing to tolerate. The listing often claims Free Shipping, which is true in the sense that shipping is folded into the price, or in some cases, you’ll see a separate shipping charge that seems to be the cost of a small spaceship. The trick is to compare shipping policies across sellers and to look for hidden costs like handling or insurance, which can sting like a goblin with a coupon.

### The Dupes Dilemma: Low Duplicates, High Drama

The pitch of low duplicates is appealing—but not always truthful. If you’re trying to craft a specific deck, duplicates may be a necessary evil. In all rares bulk, expect some duplicates; the goal is to minimize them to a reasonable level so you can draft or build around a few core cards without feeling like you’ve opened a box of identical clones. A practical strategy is to sort by set and rarity first, then by color identity, and finally by last played date in your collection—just kidding, you’ll probably just estimate based on fun and a bit of chaos.

### How to Sort a 750+ Card Lot: A Practical Guide

- Step 1: Create three piles — Keep, Sell/Trade, and Review. The review pile is where you put cards you pretend to learn about while you scroll through your phone.
- Step 2: Use a local inventory app or a spreadsheet to log the card name, set, and estimated value. Do not judge yourself for spending an hour on a spreadsheet; this is research, not a crime.
- Step 3: Sort by rarity and set area; prioritize rares that have modern uses or potential reprint value. You’ll be surprised how some cards that look boring on the surface can unlock synergy in casual formats.
- Step 4: Create a mini-deck from the Keep pile. It’s the perfect way to test the lot without committing your entire Evel Knievel energy to a full deck build.

### Value, What It Means Here, and Why It Matters

Value in bulk rares is a moving target. Some cards might be worth as little as a few cents, while others might have a surprising show-up in a midtier standard or modern deck. The trick is not to obsess about a single card but to look at the overall potential. If the total value of your keep pile plus potential trades and sellbacks covers the cost of the lot and leaves you a little extra for sleeves and a coffee, you’ve won the day. If your keep pile includes a surprise older rare or a foil that looks better in your display case than in your binder, that’s a win for your personal museum of magic.

### The Dupes Dilemma Revisited: Are We There Yet?

Duplication is not the end of the world, but it’s the part of bulk that tests your sanity. Some duplicates can be turned into seed values for budget decks, while others become permanent membership cards in your keeper pile for gifting to friends who showed up with a broken deck and a big smile. The trick is to keep duplicates at a minimum by isolating sets with high variance and focusing on a few standout rares. If you can identify a couple of kingpins early on, you’ve essentially won the “value discovery” phase of the experience.

### How to Improve Your Sorting Flow (Without Becoming a Card-Counting Hermit)

- Use color-coded labeling: Red for modern rares with potential playability, blue for vintage or foil-rich items, green for rares with valueless vibes but great art.
- Create simple checklists for each set: condition, center-ink, edge wear, and any anomalies (full-art misprints, borders cut oddly, etc.).
- Prioritize one or two sets you love and build a mini-collection around them. It makes the Keep pile more meaningful than a random assortment of ‘probably worth a dollar or two’ cards.
- Trade strategically at your local store or online. Don’t rush to trade away your best value; instead, plan a gallery exhibit of your top finds and let others tell you what they’d pay (and then surprise them with a fair counteroffer).
