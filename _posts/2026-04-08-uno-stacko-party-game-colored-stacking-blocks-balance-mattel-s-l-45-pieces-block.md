## How to play: the core loop explained

UNO Stacko’s rules are mercifully short, which is a blessing when you’re dealing with a room full of excited players. The core loop typically goes like this:

1) Build the tower with the 45 blocks. The blocks should be stacked in a stable, alternating pattern so that the upper tiers don’t immediately slump.
2) Draw an UNO color card (or use a house rule) and identify what color block you can remove without toppling the tower. The color rule adds a layer of strategy and a dash of luck to the usual Jenga-esque experience.
3) Remove a block of the appropriate color according to the card or rule. If you can’t safely remove a block, you either skip your turn or take a penalty depending on your house rules.
4) Re-stack the tower and continue. The goal is to be the last person to successfully remove a block without toppling the tower.

The nuance comes from the UNO twist. You might have to remove a red block if a red card shows up, or you may need to pick a block from a certain level, or there could be a wild card that gives you some power over the next move. The exact rules can vary slightly by edition, but the fundamental idea remains: balance, probability, and a healthy dash of psychology with your friends.

## A few house rules and variants to keep it fresh

Because UNO Stacko thrives on interaction, some simple house rules can dramatically change the vibe:
- Timed rounds: give players 60 seconds to remove a block; if the tower stays intact, they earn extra points. If it topples, they lose. This keeps energy high and decisions snappy.
- Card power tweaks: allow players to perform a block swap with another player after drawing a wildcard card. It creates dramatic shifts and strategic alliances that last until the next round.
- Cooperative mode: all players work together to prevent the tower from toppling for a set number of rounds. It’s surprisingly satisfying to outlast the tower’s stubbornness as a team.

You can implement these variations with minimal fuss, and they work particularly well when you’re teaching a new group how to play or want to spice up a recurring game night.
