## The Setup
### Box contents
The Duel Card Game trims the fat but not the flavor. Inside you’ll find:
- A deck of path cards that form tunnels and dead ends
- Action and sabotage cards that let you influence both your own path and your opponent’s progress
- A couple of hero cards that define objective tastes and victory conditions in a duel format
- A small set of scoring tokens and a treasure card to mark the finish line
- A compact rulebook that feels like a friendly nudge rather than a lecture

The whole thing is sized for a quick on-ramp, which is perfect for quick sessions between longer campaigns, or for people who want to introduce friends to the idea of table politics without making them sign a waiver.

### Setup for two players
1. Shuffle the deck and deal a starting hand to each player.
2. Place the draw pile within reach and flip the top card to start the tunnel chain.
3. Each player selects a role in secret and keeps it hidden until the moment of revelation, which adds a tiny dramatic element to early turns.
4. Put the treasure card at a fixed distance down the tunnel and prepare to race toward it while you attempt to misdirect and mislead your opponent.
5. Decide on any house rules you may want for the first few games. A lot of the fun in Saboteur comes from experimental play and friendly improvisation, so feel free to adjust the pace if you’re teaching a new player.

The setup is short enough to be done during a commercial break, which is useful if you’re playing with someone who insists that all games must involve a 40-page rulebook and a therapist on retainer.

## How to Play
### Turn structure
Turns in the Duel Card Game follow a simple rhythm:
- Draw a card from the deck
- Play one card from your hand to extend your tunnel or mess with your opponent
- Resolve any end-of-turn effects or triggers
The cadence is intentionally brisk, letting you feel the tension of the duel without needing to clear your calendar for an entire afternoon.

### Core mechanics
- Path building: You lay path cards to extend your tunnel toward the treasure. The openings must align, so plan your connections like a micro-scale railway system that somebody keeps rearranging mid-journey.
- Roles and motives: One player performs the hero role, pushing toward the treasure and keeping sabotage in check. The other player acts as the saboteur, peppering the path with detours, roadblocks, and the occasional cheeky trick. In this duel, deception is part of the toolkit and reading your opponent’s tells becomes a sport in itself.
- Action and sabotage cards: Action cards help you reinforce your position or repair a misstep, while sabotage cards push your opponent further from the finish. The balance between advancing and hindering is the heart of the game’s drama.
- Endgame pressure: The game concludes when a tunnel reaches the treasure or when the deck runs out. If both players reach the goal within the same cycle, a simple tiebreaker decides the winner, reinforcing that in a duel, every choice matters and nothing is ever truly safe.

This design keeps the energy high while avoiding the kind of analysis paralysis you get in longer, bigger Saboteur variants. It’s a duel, not a marathon, and the pacing reflects that intent.

### Strategy and tactics in a duel frame
Two-player Saboteurs learn quickly that each card has two faces: a utility function and a potential misdirection tool. Your job is to balance forward progress with strategic misdirection. A few guiding principles:
- Tempo control matters: when you push too hard, you risk giving your opponent a clean counterplay. Slow-burn approach tends to yield better late-game results.
- Bluff with intention: a fake plan can be more powerful than a real one if it makes your opponent waste cards.
- Resource tracking is everything: knowing what you’ve used and what remains helps you forecast the next five turns and plan accordingly.
- Read the table: in a two-player duel, your opponent’s temperament is a resource you can exploit. If they’re the type to commit early and overcommit, craft your strategy to punish that impulse.

### A sample round (illustrative)
Imagine a duel on a small table, lit by a lamp that keeps flickering and powering exactly the mood of the game. Player A draws a path card that looks promising but has a tricky connection. Player B plays a sabotage card that rotates a neighboring card, turning a potential long tunnel into a claustrophobic dead end. The players exchange a couple of action cards, a few quick discards, and a moment of tense silence where both players pretend not to notice the treasure card peeking from behind the next curve. The round ends with Player A nudging their tunnel closer to the treasure while Player B blocks a critical junction, forcing a fork that might be advantageous—or fatal—depending on the next card draw. The tension stays high, the table remains in one piece, and both players have the sense that one decisive move could swing the entire game in their favor.

## Accessibility and learning curve
This is not a heavyweight ruleset. The core turns are straightforward, and a few practice rounds will cement the rhythm. The trickier part, as with most stealthy bluff games, is learning to read your opponent’s tells and integrating that insight into your own plan. If you enjoy bluffing, deduction, and quick, punchy rounds, this game sings. If you prefer long, sprawling games with dozens of intertwining decisions, you might want to keep the base Saboteur on the table for a different night.
