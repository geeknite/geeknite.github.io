## What is Herd Mentality? A quick primer
Herd Mentality is a social guessing game built around prompts. Each round features a prompt that asks players to supply a word or phrase. Everyone writes their answer privately, for the most part, then all answers are revealed. The twist: you earn points when your answer matches another player’s. The more people who align with you, the better your score. The herd here is not a metaphor; it’s a literal crowd-sourced mash-up of opinions, jokes, and surprisingly strategic thinking about what other people will say.

The theme is intentionally silly and friendly, which makes it accessible to families and casual game groups. The cow pun density is high — you’ll hear more “udderly” puns than in a major league stand-up set — but it’s all in good fun. The game thrives on the shared experience: the moment someone blurted out something unexpected and the table erupts in laughter because, yes, you all heard the same bizarre thought.

## The 4-player sweet spot: does it fit a family of four?
The title promises “Big Groups of 4,” which is a cheeky way to say it scales nicely to a quartet without losing the party vibe. Four players is a paradox: it’s small enough to feel intimate and fast, but large enough that the herd’s answers can still diverge in entertaining ways. If you have a family that’s comfortable with banter and a bit of playful chaos, four becomes a surprisingly vivid social microcosm.

Pros of four players in Herd Mentality:
- Fast rounds keep the energy high and the tension low. You can squeeze in 6–10 rounds in an hour and still have time for snacks.
- Every round is a tiny social experiment: who’s going with the crowd, who’s bucking the herd, and who’s quietly plotting mischief with a sly, umbrella-worthy wink.
- The risk of one or two players dragging the table down is minimized because the reward for matching is distributed across the herd, not gated behind a difficult rule. It’s more about reading the room than reciting trivia.

Cons (aka, the potential caveats):
- Four can still feel like a small crowd when you want the room to erupt. The upside is that your group becomes a tight-knit, inside-joke machine; the downside is you may crave a third or fourth voice to amplify the hilarity.
- Some prompts land flat with just four people; you might miss that extra set of divergent opinions a larger group supplies. But if you lean into the sillier prompts, the laughs tend to compound rather quickly.

If you’re playing with fewer than four on a regular basis, we recommend rotating players between rounds or inviting a neighbor or two for “guest cows.” In Geeknite fashion, we celebrate flexible play: you’ll still get the social payoff, even if your herd isn’t technically numerous.

## Components, setup, and a quick unboxing
Herd Mentality doesn’t require a PhD in box-opening. It’s designed to be approachable for non-gamers and seasoned party-night pros alike. The components vary slightly by edition, but the core set usually includes:
- Paper answer cards or dry-erase sheets
- Pencils or tokens for answers
- A timer (or a trusty timer app on your phone)
- A deck of prompts that range from the art-grade to the unbelievably silly
- Score cards that resemble a bingo sheet, with dots or checkmarks to tally points

Setup is a breeze:
1) Shuffle the prompts and place them where everyone can see.
2) Each player gets their writing material and writing implement.
3) Decide on how many rounds you want to play (we suggest 6–8 for a standard game night). Some groups prefer “play until someone hits X points,” which is clean and keeps the tension weirdly satisfying.
4) Establish a quick host rule: no cheating, no sulking, and yes, you must read prompts with an overdramatic narrator voice at least once per game.

## How to play: a normal night in the barn
The core loop is simple, which is part of Herd Mentality’s charm. A prompt announces the round, e.g., “Name something you do before a big meeting,” and players privately write their answer. After everyone has written, each answer is revealed, and players vote (in most versions) or decide which answers they think match the majority. In the standard scoring, the more players who share your answer, the more points you win. If your answer is unique and nobody else thought of it, you get minimal points or none at all, depending on the house rules you adopt.

Key decision points:
- Do you tailor your answer to what you think others will say, or do you stay true to your own quirky path? The herd’s name implies you might want to lean toward consensus, but sometimes the rainbow-pun key is to trust your inner weathervane and watch everyone else copy you.
- How do you judge shared answers? Some groups count exact matches; others award only if at least two players share it. The latter reduces the “boring obvious” phenomenon but can make the game feel more chaotic in a good way.
- Time pressure: the timer ensures you don’t overthink your dairy-laden masterpiece. You’ll learn to write fast and lean, which is perfect for those of us who procrastinate daily but excel under mild panic.

Host tip: the best rounds happen when the host reads prompts with silly flair. Channel your inner game-show host, and don’t skip the dramatic pause after the prompt. The sound of your own dramatic sigh at the end of a sentence is proof you’re absolutely thriving in a dungeon of giggles.

## The “udder” chaos: printer-friendly prompts and house rules
One of the joys of Herd Mentality is how quickly you can tailor it to your group with simple house rules. Here are a few ideas that work well for a four-player setup:
- Match point cap: set a ceiling (e.g., 10 or 12 points) and end the game as soon as someone hits it, turning a marathon into a brisk sprint.
- Double-down rounds: every few rounds, allow players to swap one of their earlier answers with a new one, but you must keep the original hidden until reveal. This creates misdirection and late-game reveals that pay off in laughter.
- The “no repeats” modifier: once an answer has been used in a round, players cannot reuse it for the rest of the game. It forces creativity and reduces the inevitability of two cows singing the same tune.

Prompt curation is where you can truly tailor Herd Mentality to your group’s vibe. If your family loves pop culture, pepper the deck with timely memes and references; if you’re a family of folks who live for inside jokes about chores, throw in prompts that mine those daily experiences. The more you customize, the longer your herd stays entertained.

## How to run a knockout host session: tips for the ultimate game night
Here are a few host-specific tips to elevate your Herd Mentality session:
- Warm-up round: start with a soft, non-competitive prompt to get everyone in the mood. A few laughs at easy prompts help set the tone.
- Energy management: if you notice a lull, switch gears with a prompt that invites a story or a silly exaggeration. Stories are inherently entertaining and tend to keep everyone engaged.
- Props can help: a small prop bell or a gavel, even a pretend “judge’s wig” made from a paper towel crown, can add a playful ritual and a visual cue for rounds.
- Keep it moving: assign a timekeeper who nudges players when they’re taking too long to write. A gentle tap of the timer is often enough; you’re hosting a party, not conducting a lab experiment.
- Inclusivity: ensure everyone’s voice is heard. If someone is quieter, invite them to be the “round-robin writer” for one round to prime the pump.
