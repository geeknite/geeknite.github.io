## How to Play (In One Page or Less, If You Read Fast)

Skip-Bo is essentially a race against your own patience. The aim is to be the first to empty your stock pile. Each player starts with a stock pile of face-down cards; your job is to play through them by building on central building piles, in ascending order from 1 to 12. The wild cards act as anything you want—until they don’t—and that’s where the strategy (and chaos) come in.

### Setup
- Shuffle the entire 162-card deck. 
- Each player gets a stock pile of 30 cards (for 2-3 players) or fewer depending on house rules; draw four cards into your hand, with the rest serving as the draw pile.
- The center piles start empty; players aim to add cards in ascending order to the piles from 1 to 12. 
- Skip-Bo cards are wilds and can be used as any number to help you complete a sequence.

### Gameplay
- On your turn, you draw up to five cards in your hand if you have fewer than five. 
- You can play from your hand, or from your stock pile, onto the center piles or onto your own discard piles in order to reveal new cards, with the ultimate goal of finishing your stock pile.
- You can also place cards onto the center piles in ascending order. It’s not a race in a straight line; it’s a relay where you want to pass the baton as soon as possible.

### Playing a game
- The cards you play contribute to the growing stacks in the center.
- If you can’t or don’t want to play from your hand or stock, you discard a card to one of your own personal discard piles for later use.
- The game ends when a player empties their stock pile. That player wins.

### Strategy and tips
- Prioritize revealing cards from your stock pile early to speed up your own path to victory.
- Use wild Skip-Bo cards strategically; save them for when you truly need them to complete a difficult sequence.
- Don’t overcommit to a single path; flexibility matters. If you can fork a lead by playing a wild to one center pile and uncork another pile, do it.

### Turn-by-Turn Example

Let’s walk through a hypothetical hand to illustrate how a typical round might unfold. You’ve got five cards in hand after drawing, and your stock pile holds seven cards. The center piles are at 3, 7, and 2. You draw and decide to play:

A. You place a 1 from your hand onto the center pile that currently has 0 (as the starting pile). 
B. You then play a 2 from your hand onto the 2 pile, then a Skip-Bo wild to complete a 3 sequence across two piles.
C. You reveal two more cards from your stock pile as a new top card, showing a 5 and a 1. You place the 1 on a new 1 pile, then a 2 on the already established 2; your movement continues.

In the end, you discard one card into your own discard pile to keep more options open for your next turn. That’s the essence of Skip-Bo: a handful of cards, a handful of decisions, and a lot of people who think they’re about to win until something wild happens.

### Common Mistakes and Clarifications

- Not clarifying the starting piles: The piles in the center begin empty; the first cards placed into them must start from 1 or from any number when wilds are used to begin. 
- Misinterpreting wilds: Wilds can be used as any number, but they cannot be used to extend a sequence with two consecutive numbers that would require a third sequence to be completed unless you have the right wilds to fill the gap.
- Over-splurging with discards: It’s easy to fill your discard piles with cards you could have kept for later use; the trick is to keep as many options open as possible.
