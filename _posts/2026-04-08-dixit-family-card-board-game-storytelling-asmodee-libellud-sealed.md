## Gameplay: How a Typical Round Feels
Dixit is deceptively simple to learn but delightfully nuanced to master. Here’s the flow you’ll typically experience in a group of 3–6 players:

1) The storyteller picks a card from their hand and gives a clue—a phrase, a sentence, a song lyric, a riddle, or a cryptic whisper—anything that hints at the image on the card without giving it away completely.
2) Each other player selects a card from their own hand that best matches the storyteller’s clue and places it face down.
3) The chosen cards are revealed, and players secretly vote on which card they think was the storyteller’s. You gain points if your card is guessed by some players but not all; the storyteller also earns points if some but not all players guess their card.
4) The game moves on to the next storyteller, and the Chaos Engine keeps turning.

In the sealed edition, you can expect the same rules, but with the added charm of a box that makes your pre-round ritual feel like unboxing a treasure chest in a fantasy video game. The scoring tends to favor creativity—your job as a storyteller is to strike a balance between clever and accessible. If your clue is too obvious, everyone will guess your card; if it’s too obscure, no one will, and your points will dwindle like a candle in a windstorm.

What makes Dixit special in a family setting is the social contract: you’re not trying to “win” by a single metric so much as you’re trying to provoke shared laughter and creative thinking. The questions become less about who’s the smartest and more about who can tell the best story about a card that resembles a postmodern doodle with a crown.

Bottom line: Dixit remains one of the most approachable, art-forward storytelling games for families. The sealed edition is a nice option if you value presentation and a sense of occasion, but you’ll still be piloting the same narrative ship you’ve always known.
