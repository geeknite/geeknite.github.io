## Setup and learning curve
Set-up is the kind that makes you feel like you’re assembling a tiny, delicious meal rather than engineering a complex engine. In brief:
1) Shuffle the deck and deal a hand of cards to each player (the number varies by player count).
2) Each player selects a card from their hand and passes the remaining cards to the next player.
3) Everyone reveals their chosen card simultaneously, collects it, and repeats the process until all cards are exhausted.
4) End-of-round scoring happens, and you move into the next round with a fresh hand.

In practice, most players will be up and running within 5-10 minutes, including a quick teach. The rules are simple enough that a first-time gamer can pick up the gist in a few minutes, while a seasoned crowd will appreciate the layers of decision-making that emerge as the rounds pile up. The balance between luck (the cards you draw) and skill (choosing which card to pass and which synergy to chase) creates a lively tension that keeps everyone engaged without tipping into analysis paralysis.

## How the game actually plays (mechanics and strategy)
The core mechanic here is card drafting—a compact cousin of the big, sprawling drafting games that require a flight path to victory. In Sushi Go!, you draft one card each round, building combos with a few clear scoring rules. The most common strategies revolve around two ideas: collect high-value cards consistently, and chase special combos that offer big payoff when collected in pairs or sequences. 
- Nigiri cards: These are your base points. They come in different values (1, 2, 3 points depending on the type) and, crucially, can be boosted by Wasabi when paired with Nigiri: Wasabi triples the value of the next Nigiri you play, which can snowball if you manage to chain several rounds correctly.
- Maki cards: The player with the most Maki rolls at the end of the round gains a bonus. It’s a nice way to tilt score without needing to build perfect Nigiri stacks.
- Tempura: A pair grants substantial points; a single Tempura yields nothing. That makes the Tempura cards a classic risk-reward choice—do you chase a second Tempura later in the round or move on?
- Dumplings: The scoring scale increases with the number of Dumplings in your pile. It’s a classic “build into a bigger pile” mechanic that tends to give a mental map of whether you should double down or pivot away from Dumplings to chase a more stable source of points.
- Puddings: Pudding cards aren’t scored until the very end of the game. They reward long-term planning and are the kind of thing you only notice when you realize you’re a few pudding shy of victory, or you’re in the lead and want to protect that lead.
- Sushi with special effects: Some cards grant temporary advantages, forcing opponents to adjust their plans or rewarding the holder with a momentum shift.

The elegance here is the synergy. The more you play, the more you understand where to invest in a certain round. Do you anchor your plan by grabbing early Nigiri and hope to hit a Wasabi-laden combo later, or do you spread your bets across Maki, Tempura, and Dumplings to ensure you’re scoring points in multiple lanes? The beauty is that there’s no single dominant strategy every player should adopt. The best approach is adaptivity: read the table, notice what others are chasing, and pivot when you sense a scoring gap opening up.

## Teaching tips and player experience
- Start with a live demonstration: pick a handful of cards and walk through a couple of rounds slowly, explaining why you pick a card and what you’re watching for in opponents’ passes. Real-time examples help newcomers grasp the timing of Wasabi and Nigiri combos.
- Emphasize end-game awareness: remind players that Puddings score at the end, so sometimes you’ll slow-roll a plan to snatch Pudding cards rather than force a mid-round payoff.
- Keep rounds brisk: the 15-minute target is not just a number; it’s an invitation to maintain a tempo that energizes the table. If a table stalls, a quick reminder of the rule set brings everyone back into the fold without derailing fun.
- Encourage gentle mind-meld: Sushi Go! thrives when players gauge one another’s likely strategies. Subtle tells about which combos opponents chase can be a playful part of the social experience, as long as it stays friendly and light.
