### Print Quality and Condition Indicators

For a Japanese NM card, you’re looking for a few telltale signs. The surface should be free of scratches that read like a city street’s pothole map, the corners sharp (usually 7.5–9.5 range in NM), and the centering should be close to perfect, ideally 55/45 or 60/40-ish. In NM condition, you won’t see any whitening along the edges (or if you do, it’s minimal and not obvious at arm’s length). Color could pop a little more under fluorescent lighting; that’s normal. If you find cloudiness on the holofoil, that’s a red flag for misprints or moisture exposure—don’t worry, we’ll cover how to spot that later in the Care and Storage section.

## Grading, Care, and Handling

Grading is where the fun stops and the numbers start to hum. The difference between NM and MP (Near Mint to Mint) is not just the crispness of the corner; it’s the corner of a card that is so pristine you could frame it and pretend it’s a missing painting. Here are practical tips to assess NM:

- Surface: look for micro-scratches that only appear under bright light. If you can read the tiny scan lines across the holo, you may be flirting with MP territory, not NM. In NM, those lines should be there but faint.
- Edges: CW (corner wear) should be minimal. A dent or whitening along the edge hurts the grade more than a scratch on the holo.
- Centering: Ideally around 55/45 or 60/40; anything beyond 65/35 starts to push the grade toward LP (Lightly Played).
- Print quality: For Japanese prints, print alignment matters. A misaligned holo or miscut border—like a pizza slice—can be a red flag for a lower grade or a potential counterfeit hold.

If you’re buying from a seller who claims NM, ask for high-res macro photos and a trusted third-party grading note. For new collectors, remember: NM is a relative label, not a badge of absolute perfection. It’s okay to be picky; just don’t pay a premium for a card that’s only “almost perfect.”

## Handling and Storage Strategies

Proper storage is the unsung hero of preserving value. Here are recommendations that keep your Gloom AR looking as sassy as it did in the showroom:

- Sleeved protection: Use top-load sleeves with a solid inner sleeve. If you’re a minimalist, a standard card sleeve with a rigid sleeve will do. If you’re serious, invest in magnetic-rigid cases with climate control.
- Board orientation: Store upright in a dust-free binder with acid-free pages. If you must lay flat, ensure it’s not stacked under heavy items that could press the holo or corners.
- Humidity and temperature: Aim for 45–50% humidity and 65–72°F. Extreme conditions will cause warping or warping’s cousin: edge whitening.
- Handling: Clean hands or gloves, no eating while handling, and definitely no texting while flipping through your stack. Your Gloom deserves full attention.
- Display considerations: If you want to show this gem off, use a display with UV-protective glass to cut down on light-induced fading.
