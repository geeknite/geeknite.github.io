## How to play at a glance
Elimino is designed for quick rounds and even quicker table talk. You don’t need a PhD in card-chicanery to enjoy this, but a little strategic improvisation helps.

### Setup
- Shuffle the deck and deal each player a starting hand (the amount depends on player count). 
- Place the remaining deck in the center and flip the top card to start the discard pile. 
- Decide if you’re playing with any optional wild cards or house rules. The game is friendly to experimentation, which is essential for a family game night that doesn’t turn into a debate about rule interpretations.

### Turn structure
- On your turn, you draw if needed, then play one card that matches the current rule or color. If you can’t play, you draw again according to the house rules (often one or two cards).
- Some cards trigger effects: skip turns, reverse order, draw penalties, or wild card overrides. The exact text is a scavenger hunt of fun and occasional chaos, which is deliberate. Chaos? Yes. Confusion? Also yes. Enjoy both.
- The round ends when a player has one card left. In true chaos fashion, everyone yells a big “Elimino!” and the table’s noise level climbs to celebratory decibels.

### Scoring and victory
There are a few variants, but the simplest path to victory is: be the first to empty your hand. If you’re playing with scoring, you can assign points for the cards left in others’ hands, add bonus points for last card shouts, and declare a winner after a few rounds. The point here is speed, not arithmetic mastery. The game rewards quick reads and brave plays more than flawless math.

## Gameplay depth, strategy, and tips
Elimino emphasizes quick decisions and soft social manipulation rather than heavyweight strategy. Here are practical tips to raise your win rate without turning your family game night into a code-breaking session.

### Basic strategies
- Pay attention to the flow: if the current rule punishes you, pivot early to a card with a flexible effect.
- Use wild cards strategically to disrupt opponents who are close to finishing.
- Don’t always react to the loudest voice at the table; sometimes the quiet move is the best move.

### Multi-player dynamics
- In larger groups, the tempo can lag. Keep the pace alive by setting a soft timer or using a “one-card-per-turn” rule to reduce stall time.
- Teammate-style plays can exist in family settings where siblings temporarily teamwork to accelerate the going of the group—be mindful of fairness and good intentions.

### For younger players learning the ropes
- Practice with a “silent practice round” where players describe their plays without commentary. It helps kids articulate decisions and reduces confusion when real play resumes.
- Offer gentle guidance rather than authoritative instruction; let kids explore the many card effects and learn through play.
