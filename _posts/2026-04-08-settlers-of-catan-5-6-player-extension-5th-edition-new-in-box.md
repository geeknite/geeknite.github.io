## Setup and Game Flow with 5-6 Players
A typical six-player game is a different beast from the three or four-player chase you’re used to. Setup becomes a little longer because you need to arrange the board so every player can reach resources without starting a global brawl. The 5th Edition has improved the readability of the main board, but with six players you’ll still see a lot of edge-lane negotiation: the trade economy becomes the star; resources fly back and forth as players jockey for ports, ore-rich hills, and grain-laden plains.

### Step-by-Step Setup
1. Spread the main board sections to accommodate six players (the extension usually provides a larger or reconfigurable frame).
2. Place the six sets of color tokens and settlements around the board, ensuring no player blocks off another’s access to a starting spot.
3. Shuffle and deal starting resources according to the standard rules, but give each of the extra players a slightly longer starting phase so they don’t rudely steal their neighbor’s ports.
4. Decide on the turn order and begin trading. Expect longer turns as more players weigh trades and consider robber position.

## How It Changes the Game
The core game is not rewritten. The objective, resource production, and trading remain intact. What changes is the social dynamic:
- More players means more players at the table equals more negotiation, more deals that disappear overnight, and more dirty glances across the table when someone hogs a port for too long.
- The robber becomes more central as resource scarcity can occur more frequently in a six-player match, leading to higher tension and trickier negotiating politics.
- Turn length grows; the game can stretch to three-plus hours depending on the group, which is a premium if you’re scheduling a board game night after a long workday.

### A Balanced Look at Interaction
In a 5-6 player environment, trade is the currency of success. If you’re not actively trading, you’re not playing. The extension pushes players to adapt their typical strategies: blocking the best tiles becomes more critical, as someone else might jump into your previously safe spot.

## Real-World Play and Strategy for 5-6
Playing six players is a different rhythm. The expansion encourages more open ports trading and a greater emphasis on resource diversity. If your table has a heavy wind of rowdy gamers, you’ll love how the six-player mode amplifies banter around the table.

### Early Game: Setting the Foundation
In the early turns with six players, you need a plan that is robust but flexible. Start by surveying the board for resource density and identify the best port positions early. If you can lock in a couple of diverse hex types (brick, ore, grain, lumber, wool, and a couple of rarer resources), you’ll be better positioned to pivot when the robber shifts.

### Mid-Game: Negotiations and the Long Game
Mid-game, you’ll find the extension shines when players are weaving deals to control the corner tiles with ports. The extended negotiation window creates opportunities for master class bargaining and out-witted moves. A strong strategy is to claim a port early or a strong resource flow that lets you pivot to development cards or cities as the board evolves.

### Late Game: Closing the Deal
With six players, a single road to victory can be contested by several people. The best path to victory is to be flexible about the initial settlement strategy. If you can gather ore and grain simultaneously, you can push cities faster. Don’t be afraid to pivot to development cards if someone else is ramping up a city-building spree.
