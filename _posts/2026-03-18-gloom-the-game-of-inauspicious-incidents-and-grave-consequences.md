## Introduction: Where the Darkness Gets Funny
Gloom thrives on contradiction. It wants you to weep for your characters while also cackling at the absurdity of the misfortunes you’ve inflicted upon them. The game’s tone lands somewhere between a gothic novella and a late-night comedy roast, with a sprinkle of B-movie horror for good measure. The core premise is deceptively simple: each player controls a family and tries to drown them in misfortune, because in Gloom, the lower your family’s happiness score, the better you’re doing. The gloom, you see, is addictive. It’s the kind of game that rewards bad decisions with a giggle and then a groan—perfect for a group with a strong tolerance for melodrama and a weak tolerance for hope.

The design leans into theatricality and storytelling in a way that makes you feel like you’re directing a macabre soap opera rather than merely rolling dice. Cards depict unfortunate events—grim futures, cursed relatives, tragic romances, and surprisingly petty misadventures. The artworks lean into the melodramatic: oversized tears, exaggerated frowns, and enough gothic flourishes to fill a high school production of Frankenstein. The result is a product that doesn’t pretend to be a serious strategic sim; it’s unabashedly theatrical, and your table will respond in kind with banter, groans, and a chorus of “aww, come on!”

This is not a game to be played with a stony poker face. Gloom invites storytelling choices, puns, and the occasional mock-epic monologue about the tragic fate of Aunt Bertha, who, frankly, deserved better than a phantasmic polka-dotted umbrella. It is, in short, a celebration of storytelling through misfortune—and a surprisingly effective mechanic for encouraging collaboration in the most morbid way possible.

## Unpacking the Box: Components, Setup, and First Impressions
When you crack open the box, you’re greeted with the soft, slightly dusty romance of a game that has existed in the shadows of your closet for years and never quite left. The components are quaint rather than flashy: a stack of character cards (your families), a stack of positive and negative story cards (the misfortunes and oddities), and a set of overlay markers that are both essential and cleverly simple. The translucent story cards, which overlay onto character cards, are a masterstroke—think of them as “gloom overlays” that visually demonstrate how miserable your family has become without having to flip through a dozen separate trackers. The art style remains whimsical enough to keep it from spiraling into pure horror, which is crucial for a game designed for extended social interaction rather than a horror marathon.

Setup is mercifully quick: deal each player a starting family card, hand out a small handful of story cards, and give everyone a few clear overlays. Each player’s goal is to build a narrative of woe around their own family while avoiding excess misery on the others, all while trying not to reveal too much of their own strategy and thereby enabling others to crush them with cruel kindness. The physical setup encourages a quick start to the storytelling, a nimble shift from “what does grandmother do with a haunted dress?” to “how do we move mother’s misfortune to the parlor where it belongs?”

In practice, setup time is the difference between a table that’s excited to play and a table that’s already whining about the weather. Gloom’s design is very much about the social contract: you’re allowed to bookend your sentences with melodrama, but you should also be prepared for your friends to reality-check you with a perfectly-timed pun. If you’re the kind of group that enjoys theatrical table talk, you’ll feel right at home from step one.

## Mechanics and Theme: How Do You Earn a Sky-High Sorrow Score (Low Score Wins)?
The central mechanic of Gloom is the overlay of story cards on character cards. Each character has its own happiness score (a positive number), and each positive or negative card you play modifies that score. The objective is hilariously morbid: you want your own family to accumulate as much negative happiness as possible, while ensuring your opponents’ families are comparatively happier. The catch is that you score only at the end of the game, once all families have met an untimely fate, and the player with the lowest total happiness on their family cards wins. The game is a clever inversion of typical “most points wins” systems and serves as a constant reminder that sometimes the point of a game is to be the most miserable, not the most successful.

Story cards come in two flavors: negative events (misfortunes) and positive events (moments of mild relief, or “temporary bright spots” that might later be used against you). The overlay system means that the visible happiness of a family at any given moment is the sum of the base happiness plus all active modifiers from story cards. But because you’re constantly adding more cards to adjust the national mood of your lineage, the exact happiness score is fluid, which keeps the table engaged in a slow-motion tug-of-war of doom. You’re not only managing your own family; you’re wagering your opponents’ future misfortunes as well. A well-timed -6 to Uncle Petunia or a -4 from the haunted teapot can swing the table’s perception of who’s leading the gloom parade.

From a design perspective, Gloom nails pacing. Because players draw and play cards from hand, there’s always something to respond with. You can push a card onto your own family to accelerate its doom, or you can hoist an unfortunate event onto a rival’s line to derail their ambitions. The balance here is surprisingly elegant: you’re incentivized to cause misfortune, but you’re also incentivized to keep your own family in the running—both to avoid giving away your own strategy and to keep your rivals honest about their own doom-laden ambitions. It’s a subtle cocktail of malice and manners.

There’s a strategic tension between tempo and tone. Playing a doom-laden card on a rival may be satisfying enough to justify the risk of tipping your own hand, but you’ll often see players delaying the inevitable, either to maximize the visible misery on their own family or to time a late-game move that collapses someone else’s fortunes in a single dramatic moment. The game, like a good gothic novel, rewards anticipation and punishes vanity. The humor arises not from the gore or horror alone but from the social dynamics: you’re choosing not just cards, but moments of emotional dramaturgy for the table.

The “narrate the tragedy” mechanic is what elevates the game from a mere card-drawing exercise into a shared performance with a democratic, competitive edge. - Embrace the setup: A longer, more ball-busting first turn helps set the tone. Don’t be afraid to lean into the melodrama early, or you’ll end up with a poker-faced session that smells like a missed joke.
- Narrate with flair: The game rewards stories as much as outcomes. One well-timed flourish can convert an ordinary card draw into a legendary candlelit tragedy.
- Use a timer for round durability: Keep the pace snappy to prevent the mood from stalling. The moment the table senses a lull, someone will pull out a pun about a haunted teacup and the night snaps back to life.
- Rotate the “moral” of misfortune: If a card single-handedly torpedoes your own plan, roll with it. The group dynamic shines when you accept the chaos and tweak your strategy on the fly.
- Consider house rules for codifying boundaries: While improvisation is a feature, you can set friendly rules so that the nastiness stays within everyone's comfort zones.
