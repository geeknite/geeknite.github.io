## What is Saboteur?

Saboteur is a light-to-moderate trick-taking-style card game that blends set collection, bluffing, and route-building into a fast-paced dungeoneering theme. Players take on the roles of dwarves mining for gold, with a twist: some players are saboteurs, secretly trying to derail the mine while the rest try to reach the gold. The English edition from Z-Man Games brings clear card art, durable components, and a rules set that is easy to learn but hard to master.

## Components and Setup

In the complete English version, you’ll typically find a compact deck of path cards, action cards, treasure cards, and role cards. The miners need to carve a path from the starting tunnel to one of several treasure cards, while saboteurs attempt to insert dead ends, floods, rockfalls, and other nuisances that stall progress. The quality of the cards matters because in Saboteur, a worn corner can be the difference between a glorious tunnel and a heroic fish story about the one that almost made it.

The setup is part ritual, part poetry of chaos: shuffle the role cards face down and deal one to each player. Everyone looks at their own card secretly; the saboteurs cannot reveal themselves to the miners. Then lay out starting tunnels and a handful of treasure cards near the board. The rest of the cards form a draw pile. The first round is about to begin, and the dwarves are itching to draw a line in the path—literally.

If you want a tactile sense of the components, the card stock in the English edition feels punchy, with enough gloss to make you feel fancy while you're doing a plot about whether to trust the dwarf next to you. ## How to Play (A Simple Rundown for Miners and Saboteurs)

Saboteur plays out in a series of short rounds. Each round begins with the role distribution. After that, players take turns placing path cards in front of them to extend a mine from the start to a treasure card. Path cards include straight segments, left/right bends, intersections, and occasionally blocked passages. The trick is that you must place path cards so that they connect to an open route, not just willy-nilly place them with wild abandon.

Meanwhile, saboteurs can use action cards to hinder progress. They can block a path, derail a section, or insert a dead-end card that forces the miners to improvise a new route. The miners, on the other hand, can play action cards to remove a blockage, peek at another player’s role (to verify a suspicion), or bend the path back toward gold. The round ends when a treasure card is reached or if all players have placed cards and no path connects to treasure—a famous “almost victory” scenario. If the treasure is reached, miners receive gold and a round ends; if saboteurs cause the round to end without treasure, saboteurs gain points for their sabotage, which is the real win for them.

The meal of mechanism is simple to learn, but the table can become a riotous orchestra when several players bluff and misdirect. The English edition ensures language clarity in the cards and rules, which helps new players avoid misreadings that lead to a mistaken claim of victory before the last card is laid down.

## Strategy and Tactics

### Role-bluffing and Suspicion
A big part of Saboteur’s charm is the secret roles. If you’re a miner, you want to trust the people you’re sharing a mine with—but you will rarely get the full picture. If you’re a saboteur, you want to sow doubt with a well-timed flash of a dead-end card or a cleverly placed rockfall action card. The balance between deception and deduction creates a social dynamic that can feel like a board game version of a reality TV show, minus the commercials and with more pickaxes.

Smart players track who blocks passages, who takes extra turns in the mine, and who keeps drawing perfect path cards when a crucial intersection appears. Paying attention to what others discard or what actions they are signaling is often more valuable than the cards you hold. In the English edition, the artwork and icons are clear enough that you will not get stuck trying to interpret a symbol at 2am after two cups of tea and an existential crisis about your life choices.

### Path-building and Board Control
The path-building mechanic rewards planning and adaptation. You want to lay a route that can be extended toward treasure while giving you opportunities to leverage an action card later in the round. Saboteurs can insert dead ends that force miners to backtrack or pivot, turning a clean route into a snarl of tunnels where a clever misdirect can go a long way. The game encourages a nimble approach: expect the board to shift as cards are played, and be ready to pivot your strategy on a dime.

### Card Management and Hand Reading
Managing your hand is essential. You don’t want to be stuck with a bunch of action cards you’ll never use, or—worse—mess up your path by playing a card that blocks your own route. The English version helps with clarity, ensuring that you do not trip over ambiguous text. The tokens and scoring system reward strategic timing of plays and careful reading of the table.

How much to trust a rival’s claim about their own role? That is the heart of the bluff. Sometimes a saboteur will claim to be a miner to gain trust and pass along a critical action card. Other times, miners will insist that the person they’re about to trust is clearly sabotaging the route. The social deduction aspect is not the entire game, but it sure feels like a spicy topping that makes a twice-as-long game feel like a single, furious sprint.
