## How It Plays: Setup, Turn Structure, and Card Types
The core loop of Cards Against Humanity is straightforward, which is part of the reason it works so well as a party game. The host lays out a black card, players select from their white cards to respond, and the judge for that round (the person who pulled the black card in most cases) picks the best answer. The simplicity invites spectacle, improvisation, and a dash of trash-talking—hallmarks of CAH that make the wheel of jokes spin fast.

- Setup: Shuffle both decks. The host keeps the black card deck facedown, while each player draws a hand of white cards (usually around 10, depending on house rules). The host flips the top black card to reveal the prompt.
- Turn flow: The prompt is read aloud. Each participant plays one or more white cards that best fit or subvert the prompt. The judge collects the cards, mocks the contenders with a flourish, and then selects a winner. The winner keeps the black card as a point, and the next person becomes the judge. Rounds continue until you reach a defined score or time limit.
- Card types: Black cards are the prompts; white cards are the punchlines. In 2.0, you’ll encounter prompts that range from the profane to the absurd, and white cards that lean into sharp one-liners, pop-culture references, or cheeky UK-specific humor.
- Strategy vs. chaos: There’s a delightful tension here. You want to curate a hand that can fit multiple styles of prompts, but the best moments often come from those “oh no you didn’t” cards that bend the prompt in a surprising direction. The game rewards risk, timing, and a little shared context among players.
