## Painting and assembly notes
The Palanite Justicars are not the hardest models to assemble, but they are not the simplest either. The ornate detailing invites a careful approach, and the models benefit from a few deliberate steps:

- Clean and dry fit first: Check the connection points on each torso to the hips, then test the shoulder joints with the arms and optional banner pieces. A dry fit saves you from that moment when you realize you glued a sleeve in the wrong place and now you’re improvising with green stuff.
- Pin the important joints: If you plan to pose a squad in dynamic action, consider pinning the arms to the torso and the weapons to the forearms. The extra stability pays off when you pick up the squad after a long game and they feel like they could survive a hive collapse.
- Gap filling and cleanup: Some sculpts show fine lines around the ornate edges. A thin line of green stuff or plastic glue just enough to fill a seam makes a big difference once you prime the model. You don’t need to go full mock armor reproduction, but a clean edge reads well on the table.
- Base them to tell the story: A simple ruined street tile, a crack in the hive wall, or a blasted area here and there reads as a place where law and noise intersect. Base texture can be as simple as sandy texture with a few micro rocks or as elaborate as a tiny bit of resin ruin to suggest a precinct in disrepair.

Regarding paint schemes, the Palanite Justicars invite you to lean into contrast and clean, crisp color blocks. A classic look is a gold or brass trim on dark blue or deep red armor—colors that belong to the idea of imperial law rather than a street gang’s color identity. If you want to push the look even further, a two-tone scheme with lighter, almost white capes or banners can create a striking silhouette on a crowded board. If you prefer a more restrained aesthetic, a monochrome approach with muted metals and a touch of blue can yield a classy, “lawmen in the ruins” vibe that doesn’t shout but certainly asserts domination.

Metallic finishes pair beautifully with non-metallic metal (NMM) highlights—or the newer option, non-metallic spectrum (NMS), if you enjoy the gradient approach. The golds and bronzes pick up highlights nicely under standard table lighting, and you can experiment with weathering powders or a wash to push the age and grime into the fabric of your Underhive narrative. Weathering should be reserved for the occasional scorch or soot rather than a full rust belt look unless you want to craft a story about a particular hive sector that has seen better days.

## Rules, gameplay, and role on the board
Necromunda models are as much about narrative as they are about raw numbers, and the Palanite Justicars are designed to deliver a strong thematic presence while still offering practical utility in a game. In terms of in-game role, they typically function as well-armored, mid-to-heavy infantry that can anchor a frontline with solid shooting and credible melee options. Depending on your faction and the gang you’re building around them, they can serve a few distinct purposes:

- Fire support and area denial: With rifle options and decent leadership, Justicars can project firepower along a corridor or deny a chokepoint while your faster units maneuver for control.
- Close-range deterrence: The weapon loadouts and armor make them viable for brawling with single-target threats that threaten your flanks. A unit like this can punish aggressive melee squads trying to crash your lines.
- Narrative control: The model aesthetic creates a powerful storytelling tool. In a campaign, you can weave these minis into a plot about a law-and-order faction clashing with a rogue’s gallery of gangs who operate in the same hive, raising the stakes whenever you deploy them.

From a practical standpoint, their durability and firepower mean they are not the type of squad you want to leave out of a list. They can hold a position on a contested objective, weather an enemy’s first volley, and then apply discipline through disciplined fire or controlled retreat. They also pair well with units that can buff or protect them, or with leaders who can keep their order in the thick of a firefight.

Do note that meta shifts in Necromunda can change how you value these units. In a game where fast skirmishing crews thrive, a slower, heavily armored squad may feel less nimble than the speedsters. In more “line and ladder” oriented play or narrative campaigns where you want the aura of law enforcement to dominate the table, the Justicars shine as a thematic centerpiece with enough punch to justify their cost.

For conversion play, the presence of ornate details and multiple weapon options invites some creative builds. A few players have swapped standard weapons for more unique options or swapped out the cloaks for debris-laden capes to emphasize the “law in ruin” vibe. The beauty of Necromunda is that you can tilt toward narrative preferences, and Palanite Justicars are a robust canvas for that kind of hobby storytelling.

## Painting tips and quick scheme ideas
If you’re new to painting Palanite Justicars, here are a few quick ideas to get you started:

- Classic regal look: gold trim with deep blue armor. Use a rich metallic base, wash with a brown/sepia to bring out the engravings, then a bright blue for the plates. Highlight edges with brass or silver for a regal finish that pops under game lighting.
- Gilded ruin aesthetic: keep the base metal more brass with green-blue patina in the recesses. A weathered blue cloak and smoke-black accents can evoke a hive-leftover vibe—law in a city of soot and steam.
- Monochrome enforcement: dark armor with bright enamel insignia can deliver a sleek, modern look. A stark white cape adds drama while keeping the unit grounded in the grimdark feel.
- NMM or NMS approach: metallics on the armor are complemented by a gradient on the fabric; this helps the model catch light in dynamic poses while keeping a uniform tone across the squad.
- Basing: a ruined street with subtle mineral grit and small scorched bits is enough to push the narrative. If you’re feeling ambitious, a couple of broken brass plaques or a tiny police badge in the rubble adds a micro-story element you can reveal as the game progresses.
