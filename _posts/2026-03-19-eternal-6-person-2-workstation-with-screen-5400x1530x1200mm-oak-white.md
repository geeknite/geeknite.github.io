Pre-assembly considerations: space, power, and posture

First things first: you’ll need space. This is not a desk you slide into a cozy corner. It’s a desk you plonk in the middle of the room and pretend you own a small but sophisticated coworking space. Measure the room to make sure you have clearance for chairs, legs, and the occasional adulting moment when you realize you’ve invited two extra colleagues to the demo day. You’ll also need access to power outlets for the screens and any ancillary devices. If you live in a world where outlets are a scarcity resource, this may necessitate a cooperative planning session with your facilities manager or, worst case, a dramatic power strip situation that ends with a dramatic singalong of “There’s No Power In This House But There Is Power In This Desk.”

The assembly experience is where the fantasy either breaks or bonds you to the furniture for a decade. The Eternal system is modular, which means you’ll be connecting rails, aligning panels, and praying to the hardware gods that the two-minute diagram doesn’t require you to become an amateur architect. Expect a few hours if you’re doing it solo, a bit more if you’re assembling with a team that includes a person who insists on labeling every bolt by color. The upside: once assembled, the desk is surprisingly rigid, with a stability that makes you feel like you’re in a sci-fi control room rather than a design studio. The longer you use it, the more it starts to feel like a workstation that’s part of your life, not just a furniture piece you bought on a whim.

Ergonomics and the art of not turning into a pretzel

The design aims to keep your crew of six comfortable over long sessions, which is not a small feat. Ergonomics on a 6-person system is a bit like building a submarine that must accommodate six periscopes and one captain who refuses to wear a life vest. Here are the ergonomic highlights:

- Height adjustability on central zones so you can stand or sit depending on your mood or the last Zoom call you survived without saying “You’re on mute.”
- Width and depth allowances to support dual-monitor setups, with room for a small laptop, a notebook, and a snack stash that’s increasingly suspicious after the third bar of cacao. 
- Cable routing channels that look neat enough to host a tiny architectural model, which means fewer chances of tripping on cables while you walk around the desk pretending you’re in a clean-room lab.
- Aesthetic that doesn’t scream “obligatory office furniture”—oak white finish is modern without being clinical, and the wood grain gives your inner nerd a sense of warmth.

A seat for everyone, a screen for the team

The “with Screen” clause invites two possible interpretations: either built-in screens integrated into the workstation, or an elegant framework for mounting two large displays. Either way, the screen strategy is your gateway to either glorious collaboration or glorious chaos. If you go the built-in route, you’ll unlock a cohesive, professional look that minimizes the subwoofer-like hum of multiple monitor arms, but you’ll also be negotiating warranty terms with the vendor regarding screen replacement and service. If you opt for externally mounted displays, the system remains sleek and modular, with cable management that’s good enough to deserve its own TED Talk.

One of the standout design cues is the integrated tiered surface. The top-most layer is ideal for laptops and compact peripherals, while a mid-level surface hosts a keyboard and a secondary peripheral array. A lower panel hides power strips and network gear, which means your desk won’t resemble a chaotic IT closet at the end of the week. If you’re a neat freak, you’ll appreciate this; if you’re a creative type who believes “organized chaos” is a life philosophy, you’ll still find a place for your doodads and pencil towers.

Real-world usage: pros, cons, and the occasional reality check

Maintenance tips if you’re serious about keeping it sharp

- Wipe with a soft microfiber cloth and a mild cleaner; avoid harsh solvents that can dull the finish.
- Use coasters to protect the surface from hot beverages and questionable late-night pizza slicing rituals.
- Check the cable channels monthly for dust; a clean channel means better airflow and fewer overheating headaches for you to worry about.
- Inspect the modular joints periodically. It’s not sexy, but it’s essential if you want the system to stay sleeved in a comfortable, quiet workspace aura rather than turning into a creaky sculpture in your office.
