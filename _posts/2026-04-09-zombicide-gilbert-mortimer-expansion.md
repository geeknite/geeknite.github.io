## What is in the box and what it means for your table

The Gilbert & Mortimer Expansion is designed to slot into the Black Plague base game with minimal drama and maximum drama-queen vibes. It introduces two new Survivors, Gilbert and Mortimer, two scholarly types who probably spent more time in a library than a safe room, and a cadre of new zombies, artifacts, and scenario options that let you explore the darker side of plague-era fashion. The goal is simple: give you fresh ways to run your deranged band of survivors while still keeping the pacing brisk enough that your group stops arguing about the best place to put a panic token and starts arguing about who actually reads all the scavenged grimoire pages.

In the box you’ll find the two new survivors with their own special abilities, a handful of new encounter cards, some fresh mission tiles, and a handful of tokens that look suspiciously like they were designed by someone who learned the art of coin balance from a roulette wheel. If you are into the aesthetic of grimoires and alchemical glassware, this expansion will scratch that itch. If you like your zombies angry and your loot plentiful, you will also like it. If you dislike both, we still think you should keep reading because the humor level in this package is surprisingly high for a game about undead misfortune.

To help you picture the physical content, here is a quick overview of the major components:

- 2 new Survivors: Gilbert and Mortimer, each with a unique set of skills and a backstory you will pretend to remember during the third act of your next campaign.
- New zombie types and horde cards that tilt the difficulty curve a notch without turning the entire table into a battle of attrition.
- New artifacts and equipment that let your team explore more tactical options while still looking cool on the table.
- New scenarios and mission tiles that open up different paths to victory and, more importantly, different ways to lose gracefully.

If you want to see how this expansion proposals speaks to your current table dynamics, this is the moment to consider your group’s appetite for risk. Do you enjoy sneaking through the catacombs with a single slipstream of luck, or do you prefer walking into a flaming bakery of chaos with two extra survivors who may or may not save you by misinterpreting every instruction? Gilbert and Mortimer bring a vibe that is equal parts academic bravado and reckless gambit.

## Minis and production quality

The minis in this expansion continue CMON’s tradition of clean sculpts with enough personality to make you wonder if these two characters have a coffee addiction or a true moral compass. Gilbert looks like he would rather discuss the quadratic formula mid-combat than swing a blade, while Mortimer seems to wield a staff with the confidence of someone who has never been asked to leave a library. The sculpts are crisp, the bases are thematic, and the paint lines on the test sprues are intentionally messy to mimic the antique look. If you are a painter who cares about how your miniatures look at four feet, you will appreciate the potential here; if you are only concerned with game function, you’ll still enjoy the silhouettes and posing choices.

The box also includes tokens of various shapes that teach you nothing about ergonomics and everything about keeping your board tidy. The card quality is typical for Zombicide: sturdy enough to survive a few table flips but flexible enough to survive a table that might just be a tiny bit too excited about the dynamics of a zombie apocalypse. The artifact cards have beautifully illustrated icons that scream late medieval alchemy, which is exactly the vibe this game wants to project. It is not just a game about killing zombies; it is a reminder that sometimes knowledge is dangerous, especially when it comes with a +1 to your damage dice.

To give you a sense of scale, here is a little Jekyll-and-Hyde moment: the minis look good enough on a tidy table, but once you dump them on a cluttered kitchen island at two in the morning, you will realize that CMON’s design team has made them the kind of thing you want to carry around in a box just to show off when friends visit. If you are building a safari diorama for your kids, these minis are a nice addition; if you are building a competitive play setup, these minis bring a certain swagger to the table that can subtly influence your decisions.

## The gameplay shift and how Gilbert and Mortimer affect your strategy

The core idea behind the Gilbert & Mortimer expansion is to broaden your strategic palette without turning the game into a full-blown RPG session. The two new Survivors each have a unique set of abilities that reward clever positioning, smart resource management, and a willingness to take calculated risks. For players who enjoy optimizing synergies, this expansion is an excellent playground. For groups that prefer chaos and chaos-adjacent strategies, it still provides enough flexibility to inject entertaining chaos into each round.

Gilbert’s strength lies in support-oriented play: he can enable teammates in ways that feel rewarding but not game-warping. Mortimer, on the other hand, leans into the risk-reward calculus with abilities that reward daring moves and careful planning. The two characters don’t just offer new numbers; they push you to think differently about how you approach each wave of undead. That means longer planning phases for new players and a little more choreography for experienced ones who want to keep their fingers sharp and their dice luck non-fickle.

From a balance standpoint, the expansion feels like it sits between a light add-on and a medium-weight rebalancing of the core game. It doesn’t overhaul the base rules; it supplements them with new options that create alternative win conditions and slightly different risk curves. The new artifacts are a nice touch because they add moments of high drama—think of a last-ditch gadget that may turn the tide or backfire spectacularly. If your table loves power spikes that arrive at the right moment, Gilbert & Mortimer give you lots to work with.

For players who worry about game length, this expansion is generally friendly. It does not significantly extend the core play time per session; at most, it nudges an already tightly paced round into a slightly more thoughtful rhythm. It is a good match for experienced players who like to optimize and for newer players who appreciate a gentle nudge toward better decision making. If you are a purist who wants to keep the number of decisions to a minimum, you can add them in a controlled manner or even skip certain components without sacrificing the overall feel.

To illustrate the difference between base game balance and expansion balance, compare how a standard Black Plague round might flow with and without Gilbert & Mortimer. Without them, you will experience the classic high-tension wave management: allocate actions, plan movement, dodge the accumulation of zombies, and try not to run out of tools before you reach the last objective. With Gilbert & Mortimer, you lean into different lines of play: you may choose a more supportive approach for the first half of the mission, then pivot to a riskier, high-payoff strategy when the opportunity arises. The expansion rewards experimentation without punishing careful play, which is a rare and welcome balance in a game that is already wonderfully systematized for chaotic fun.

## How to run this on your table — setup, teach, and playthrough tips

As with any Zombicide product, setup can feel a bit ceremonial. The Gilbert & Mortimer expansion does not dramatically increase the friction of getting to the table, but there are a few good practices to ensure your first game runs as smoothly as possible. First, separate out the new components and identify them quickly. The survivors and their associated ability cards should be near your core reference sheet. Second, choose your scenario in advance. Some players like the idea of a cunning, artifact-rich mission; others want the adrenaline of a straight assault. The new tiles can be used to upgrade a preexisting scenario or to create a fresh course entirely.

Teach notes: remind players that the new survivors operate with a slightly different tempo. Unlike some base game survivors who have a predictable trajectory, Gilbert and Mortimer benefit from players who think a move ahead. As you walk through the abilities, emphasize how terrain and line of sight interact with their skills. A quick practical tip: run a dry run with no zombies on the board. Let players practice moving and using artifacts to see how the new rules feel before you drop the first wave of the undead into the learning room. You will thank yourself later when the actual game day arrives and no one is stumped by a confusing token.

Gilbert and Mortimer carry the signature vibe of Black Plague with a slightly more scholarly aesthetic. The artwork on the new cards leans into medieval alchemy and cryptic research notes, which creates a fun counterpoint to the grim mechanical chaos of the base game. The designers seem to be inviting you to roleplay a bit more, not just move models across a board. If you enjoy the flavor of a proper fantasy university, you will love the sense that these two scholars are not just helpers but participants in a larger story of survival.

The expansion’s art direction also plays nicely with the game’s color palette. This is not purely cosmetic; it helps keep your tactical decisions anchored in a world that feels cohesive rather than a mishmash of random bits gathered from a hobby store shelf.

If you already own several Zombicide add-ons, you may worry about compatibility.
