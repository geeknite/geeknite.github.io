## Synthetic rope vs steel rope: the great rope debate

Let’s get this out of the way, because it’s one of the most common questions in the forum: should you get synthetic rope or steel cable? In most practical off-road/ATV/UTV trailer recovery scenarios, synthetic rope wins the day for handling, safety, and ease of use.

- Safety: Synthetic rope floats in a way that remains flexible under load, with fewer sharp burrs if the line breaks. If the rope snaps, synthetic fibers tend to fray rather than whip with titanium fury. Obviously, you should never rely on a broken line, but the immediate hazard profile is lower with synthetic rope than with steel.
- Handling: Lighter rope means easier management around a trailer hitch, a winch drum, and a tree anchor. You’ll be able to coil and store it with less muscle and more sanity.
- Durability: While steel is famously strong, synthetic rope handles abrasion and heat differently. Rolling it through a drum, over a fairlead, and across a tree anchor requires less resistance and friction, plus you’ll notice less kickback if you need to free spool or reposition.

On the flip side, steel rope can be tougher in terms of heat resistance and longevity under heavy abuse (think long, continuous pulls in rocky environments). If you’re doing heavy, industrial-type towing with a lot of demanding loads, steel still has a place. But for a mid-range, all-around off-road recovery tool for trailers, ATVs, and UTVs, synthetic rope tends to be the sensible default—and that’s why Stealth ships with it in this line.

## Installation and compatibility: getting it mounted without drama

If you’re handy with a wrench, you’ll be fine. If you’re a hobbyist with a vague recollection of school shop class, you’ll still be fine as long as you methodically follow the manual and don’t rush. Here are some practical notes that help you avoid the “Ready, fire, aim” approach.

### Mounting pattern and bolt circle

Most 4,500 lb winches in this family share a standard mounting footprint—often around a 10 x 4.5 inch bolt pattern. Make sure your bumper, bumper plate, or trailer winch mount aligns with those holes. If you’re mounting on a trailer, check your stability under load, and ensure the mount grommets and bolts are rated appropriately for the expected loads. If it doesn’t align with your pre-cut holes, you may need to fabricate an adapter plate. It’s a one-way street to a happier recovery when you plan ahead rather than after you’ve sunk into a mud pit with your neighbors watching.

### Wiring and switch options

A clean installation means a tidy wiring job. Running a dedicated power line with a proper fuse and a switch is the baseline. The wireless remote is handy, but you’ll still want a safe, easily accessible switch inside your vehicle or on the bumper so you can engage the winch without squinting through the spray of rain. Use heavy-gauge wiring, make sure the ground is solid, and remember that a good ground is half the victory. If you’re wiring to a trailer, consider a separate battery or a dual battery setup for longer sessions, especially if your trailer sits unused for most of the year and the battery tends to sulk in the cold.

### Accessories and mounting add-ons

In many packages you’ll find a protective cover, a rope dampener, and sometimes a mounting kit that includes extra hardware. A dampener can be a nice safety-minded addition to prevent the rope from whip-lashing if you ever get an unexpected rebound during a snatch recovery. Safety first, even when you’re laughing at your own attempts to winch a stubborn log out of a ditch.

## Maintenance, care, and longevity: do the simple things well

To keep a winch like this performing well across seasons, you don’t need a garage full of exotic tools, only some sensible habits:

- Keep it dry where possible. If you’re in a rain-heavy environment, wipe down the motor housing and the external components after use to prevent corrosion.
- Use a protective cover when stored outside. The synthetic rope benefits from storage in a dry, cool place away from direct UV exposure.
- Inspect the rope for frays or nicks. Synthetic fibers can hide damage; you’ll notice if the line is wearing unevenly. If you see bright white fiber or fraying near the hook, consider replacing the rope or addressing the wear with a professional.
- Lubricate moving parts as recommended by the manufacturer. The goal is to maintain smooth operation and minimize drag during winching.
- Check the mounting bolts and hardware periodically. Loose bolts are the silent killers of recoveries.
