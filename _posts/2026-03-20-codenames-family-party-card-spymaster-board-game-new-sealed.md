## How to set up and play
Setup is the kind of thing you can do while someone else is picking a snack from the kitchen. Here is a quick run-through that captures the spirit without turning you into a full time rules lawyer:

- Shuffle the word cards and lay out a grid of either 4x5 or 5x5 depending on player count. The family edition keeps the grid compact so it fits on most tables without a flexing abacus.
- Separate the two teams and appoint a spymaster for each side. The spymaster sits so that their team can hear the clues but not see the grid directly if possible. This mirrors the classic tension of public performance and private chaos.
- The spymaster uses the key card to indicate which words are theirs, which are the other team’s, and which are safe. The other players try to guess the words based on a one word clue and a number that indicates how many cards the clue applies to.
- The turn continues until the team guesses the number of words indicated by the clue or until they hit an incorrect guess. If they pick a word belonging to the other team, that word is turned over and the other team gains momentum. If an innocent bystander is revealed, that turn ends, and play passes to the other side. If the assassin word is revealed, that ends the round immediately with a penalty, which is a dramatic moment for every family gathering.
- Alternate turns until one team reveals all of their words or until a predetermined stopping point if you are playing with a time cap.

The family edition keeps the rules approachable. The one word clue mechanic is preserved, which means your spymaster has to think in sneaky, metaphorical terms without starting a word that is too obviously obvious. It is a delicate balance of clever misdirection and reasonable vocabulary that kids can grow into with time. The learning curve is gentle, but not zero. As you play more rounds, you will notice patterns emerging in your table talk, and the phrases that get the best guesses without forcing anyone to reveal their own strategic secrets to the cat or the neighbor’s dog.

## Spymaster role and clue strategy
The spymaster has one primary job: give clues that connect as many of their own words as possible with a single, clever concept. The challenge in this family edition is balancing clarity with ambiguity. The one word clue can be a noun, verb, or another descriptive term, but must not be too obvious in a way that reveals the exact words to be guessed. The number attached to the clue tells the team how many of the words on the grid the clue could apply to. The trick for a good spymaster is to craft a clue that points to multiple words simultaneously while avoiding words that belong to the other team or to innocent bystanders. The family edition nudges you toward more family friendly associations, which can be a blessing when a clue about a dragon and a cupcake lands just right or a clue about a robot and a rainbow triggers a surprisingly accurate deduction.

A few practical tips for the spymaster:

- Start with a few safe crossovers. If you can hint at two or three words with a single clue, you gain momentum without risking a wrong turn.
- Avoid words that overlap with many others on the grid unless you want to escalate risk. The goal is precision and cleverness, not a flood of misdirection that confuses your own team more than the opponents.
- Use process of elimination. If you have a high probability join between two or three words, consider a clue that binds them all together. It is a risk, but a calculated one that can pay off with a big round win.

The spymaster role is where the game earns its legs. It rewards verbal agility, a little theater, and the patience to watch your teammates’ eyes light up as a misinterpreted clue morphs into a triumphant guess. It is also where the family edition shines, because it lowers the barrier to enjoy the process without needing to memorize a long rulebook.

## Strategy and tips for players
For the guessers, the key is inference and pattern recognition. You want to connect the clue to multiple words without accidentally selecting the other team words or the assassin. The family edition nudges you toward simpler associations, which is a good thing for casual players and kids who are still building language-based pattern recognition. A few practical tips for players:

- Listen for indirect connections. A clue like animal that points to both dog and cat shows clever cross-domain thinking and invites a clarifying question from your team leader.
- Use the process of elimination. If you suspect a word is likely not yours, avoid chasing after it in the hope that your partner or the spymaster will salvage the situation with a more precise clue.
- Pace yourself. Rushing can cause mistakes that derail the round. A short pause to think before guessing is often worth it.
- Reserve a couple of safe targets for a turn that looks risky. If your opponents are on a roll, you can carve out a victory by squeezing one or two sure bets into the mix.

Replayability is often a function of how many good words are in your vocabulary. The family edition tends to rotate word lists in a way that keeps phrases fresh across multiple nights of play. The art style and the kid friendly vocabulary help prevent fatigue as long as you keep the rounds short and focused on fun rather than a marathon that ends with everyone politely losing interest.
