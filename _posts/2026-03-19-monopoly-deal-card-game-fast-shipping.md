## H2: Components and setup

Monopoly Deal comes with a deck of around 110 cards, depending on the edition you snag. The deck includes money cards, property cards (in different colors), and action cards like “Deal Breaker,” “Just Visiting,” and “Rent.” Some decks also include house rules variants for extra spice. Here’s what to expect:

- Property cards in color sets: purple, light blue, pink, orange, red, yellow, green. Each color set represents a potential three-card monopoly when you collect all three of its properties.
- Money cards: These are used to pay rents and to fund your property collection. They double as negotiation chips and can be played as money to pay rent or as a stand-alone defense when you’re low on cash.
- Action cards: These are the real wildcards. They let you steal, swap, or charge rent, and sometimes doom your opponents in deliciously petty ways.
- The ever-popular “Deal Breaker” card (spoiler alert: it’s rare, it’s glorious, and it can end games in spectacular fashion).

Setup is simple: shuffle, deal five cards to each player (or four in a three-player game, per rules variations), place the draw deck in the center, and decide who goes first. In Geeknite terms: grab a snack, set up a timer, and get ready for economic disaster with a smile.

## H2: How to play (the quick version)

Monopoly Deal is turn-based with a few core actions per turn:

1) Draw two cards. If you start with a hand, you may draw up to five cards in hand, and then you must discard down to seven at the end of your turn (depending on house rules). This is the purification moment where you decide which cards you want to push into your destiny and which you want to ruin someone else with.
2) Play up to three cards from your hand. You can play money to your bank, place property cards in front of you, or use action cards to effect the board. You cannot play more than three cards per turn unless a special card or house rule says otherwise.
3) End your turn. Any money in your bank counts toward your own rent requirements, any unplayed property cards stay on the table in front of you, and your opponents begin their turns, plotting in earnest.

The rhythm is simple: keep a steady flow of cash, build your property portfolio, and use action cards to derail rivals. The magic lies in balance: you want to protect your future monopolies while nudging others toward bankruptcy just a little bit at a time.
