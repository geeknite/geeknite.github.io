### Acoustic Performance: Can You Actually Hear Silence?

Silence is not a feature you can see, smell, or taste, but you can measure it with ear drums and a willingness to believe. This pod promises solid isolation from ambient office noise. In practice, you should expect a noticeable reduction in typical open-plan office din: printers, bus-stop chatter near the elevator, and the occasional clunking of a stapler being used as percussion in a meeting that runs too long. The SE (sound isolation) and NRC (noise reduction coefficient) numbers vary based on how well you seal the door and how much the pod interacts with the room’s overall acoustics. Real-world tests suggest a usable reduction in outside noise sufficient for privacy in most standard offices, though it is not a submarine and will not erase the sound of a printer in full sprint mode across the hall.

The sealing around the door is critical. If you are placing the pod where the door meets a carpeted floor, you may get a tighter seal than over a hard floor, where the threshold can create a small friction that reduces the air-gap significantly. We recommend ensuring the pod sits on a level surface and using a draft stopper or seal kit if you sense any leaks near the base. The result is that voices inside stay inside, and voices outside stay outside, which means fewer colleagues wandering in mid-presentation asking if you want to try a different color palette for the slide deck.

For those who care about numbers: expect an STC rating in the mid- to upper-20s, with NRC values pushing toward 0.8 to 1.0 for typical configurations. In plain speak: you can have a phone call or a focused meeting without turning your neighbor into an unwilling audience member, which is a win for everyone except the open office ambience police who live for spontaneous coffee steam vocal performances.
