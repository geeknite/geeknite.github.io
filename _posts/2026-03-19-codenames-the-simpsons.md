## What this game is
Codenames: The Simpsons is a themed variant of the classic party word game. Two teams, red and blue, compete to locate their agents by clue giving. A Spymaster gives a one-word clue and a number, indicating how many cards on the board relate to that clue. The twist is that the 25 cards on the board feature Simpsons characters, quotes, episodes, and general Springfield-flavor prompts, not ordinary words. The goal remains the same: deduce your team’s agents before the other team does—with the constant risk of falling into the assassin trap or accidentally helping the other team with a clue that’s too clever by half.

The core tension of Codenames—connecting multiple target cards with a single cunning clue—gets a fresh coat of paint here. The Simpsons universe provides a filtered lens for a familiar mechanic: you’ll likely be thinking of a show quote you heard on a family night, a recurring gag from a classic episode, or a character’s iconic catchphrase. The result is a greater emphasis on pop-cultural literacy, and a little more pressure to avoid cryptic clues that require a PhD in Simpsons trivia.

### Playing the game: rules overview
The rules are intentionally simple, with a few elegant twists that make the Simpsons theme feel integrated rather than tacked on.
- Team setup: Two teams, red and blue, each with a designated Spymaster. The Spymaster has access to the full key card, which indicates which cards belong to which team and where the assassin sits.
- Clue giving: The Spymaster gives a one-word clue followed by a number (for example, "Marge 3"). The clue should relate to as many of the revealed cards as possible without pointing to the opponent’s cards or the assassin.
- Guessing: Teammates discuss and select cards from the grid. They can reveal up to the number given by the Spymaster plus one more guess if they feel confident. The catch is that revealing an assassin ends the game immediately, while revealing a neutral card ends that particular guess without awarding a victory.
- The aim: Reveal all of your team’s agents before the other team does. The tension arises from balancing risk and reward, much like choosing a family-friendly hide-and-seek strategy during a power outage in Springfield.

The Simpsons twist
The edition’s humor and flavor come from the way it leverages well-known moments from the show. Clues frequently reference famous lines or episodes without naming the character outright. For example, a Spymaster might use clues that evoke D’oh moments, “Monorail,” or a particular Simpsons catchphrase, which players will recognize instantly and respond to with joyful groans. On the other hand, a clue that’s too on-the-nose risks accidentally revealing key cards that aren’t on your side, tipping the other team off or triggering a cascade of laughter that disrupts focus.

### Memorable moments and how to leverage them
One of the best things about this edition is the shared cultural shorthand that the table brings. A well-chosen clue can unlock multiple cards in one go, especially if your team can connect characters through common archetypes (town prankster, greedy CEO, bumbling authority figure). The pitfalls are equally memorable—when a clue references a background character that only hardcore fans know, you risk a long, awkward silence as someone peers at the card and asks, “Who is that again?” In those moments, the table’s energy turns into a comedic chorus rather than a victory chant.

## Strategy and tips
- Build a repertoire of safe, broad clues that can apply to multiple characters. A well-chosen umbrella clue like "Springfield" or "Duff" can link several cards, while "Sax" or "Radio" might connect to episodes or backgrounds that appear on the board.
- Use cross-medium references carefully. The Simpsons edition rewards players who think across episodes, jokes, and iconic moments, but it punishes players who attempt to squeeze too much into a single clue.
- Pay attention to your team’s knowledge base. If you’re playing with non-fan friends or family who don’t know the show as well, you’ll want to hedge your clues toward more widely recognizable concepts.
- The Spymaster’s safety net: Don’t reveal the assassin by accident. It’s tempting to go for a flashy clue that links many cards, but the cost of misreading the board is high. The assassin can ruin a night in a heartbeat, so weigh the benefits carefully.
- Endgame tactics: When you’re close to victory, consider smaller, targeted clues to finish the job. The last thing you want is a misfire that gives your opponents a second wind.
