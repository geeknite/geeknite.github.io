The theoretical maximum is straightforward: each port can push up to 25 Gbps, so two ports equate to 50 Gbps in aggregate, assuming your switch, cabling, and storage can keep up. In practice, you’ll rarely see perfect full-duplex 50 Gbps throughput in every scenario due to protocol overhead, driver efficiency, and the underlying storage subsystem bottlenecks.\n\n- Link-level throughput: On a well-configured home-lab with a 25GbE switch and a fast NVMe/RAID storage pool, you can approach 24–23 Gbps per stream. In real-world file copies of large sequential chunks (e.g., large video files), you might see sustained around 22–24 Gbps per link after TCP/IP overhead and protocol handshakes settle.\n- Combined throughput: If you’re transferring multiple large files, you can amortize multiple streams across both ports, potentially approaching 40–45 Gbps of practical throughput across both links. That’s not a magical 50 Gbps, but it’s still serious headroom for backups, cloning, and live migrations.\n- Small-file and metadata-heavy operations: Expect decreased throughput due to SMB/SMB3 overhead and metadata lookups. In such cases, sustained throughput per port might drop to 6–12 Gbps depending on the NAS file system, caching, and CPU resources. That’s still better than your old 1GbE rig, and the latency improvements when dealing with large datasets can be noticeable.\n- Latency: 25G links can reduce latency compared to gigabit Ethernet in many scenarios. In home-lab tests, you might see sub-millisecond to a few-millisecond pings within a local network, which improves the responsiveness of VM migrations or live storage migrations during maintenance windows.\n\nAll of these numbers assume you’re interconnecting with properly provisioned hardware: a 25GbE switch capable of handling the speeds, a fast network path between the NAS and host, and a fast storage subsystem behind the NAS. If any of those are underpowered, your results will reflect it. The card doesn’t magically create bandwidth; it unlocks available bandwidth from the rest of the stack.\n\nIf you’re curious about real-world workloads, the best approach is to simulate your typical tasks: a backup to your NAS, a large media copy, and a handful of concurrent VMs accessing shared storage.
