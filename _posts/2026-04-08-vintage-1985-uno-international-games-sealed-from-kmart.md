### A Quick Note on NOS and Sealed Items
New Old Stock can be a mixed bag. The “NOS” label promises mint condition, but it also demands patience: the item has survived decades with its greenish, vintage glue intact, which can mean the seal will fight you at every turn if you ever decide to crack it. In our case, the seal is a physical handshake from the 1980s—firm, with a crackle of plastic that speaks of days when packaging was less about minimalism and more about making you feel like you’re about to unwrap a treasure chest. ### Authenticity and Grading: How to Verify You Actually Got the Real Deal
If you’re considering paying a premium for NOS, here are a few quick checks:
- The seal integrity: Look for a consistent, intact seal with no signs of tampering. A resealed item is less collectible and more a time-twister of potential re-wrapping tricks.
- Card stock and back design: Compare to period references. The 80s UNO stock should feel stiffer than modern versions, with slightly off-white card faces in places and consistent back patterns that match the era’s production techniques.
- Box art alignment: Check that the International Games branding is legible and that the artwork aligns with typical mid-80s print practices. Misalignment or typographic mismatches can be a signal of a later reprint or a counterfeit.
- Inner contents: If possible, confirm the deck has all 108 cards and that the color set matches the original palette. A missing Draw Four or a misprinted color border are telltale signs of a non-authentic run.
