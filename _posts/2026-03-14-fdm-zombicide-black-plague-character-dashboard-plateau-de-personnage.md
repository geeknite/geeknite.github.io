## Design goals and constraints
Before you print a dozen pieces, here is what we aimed for, refined through multiple print runs and a little trial by noise:

- Clarity over complexity: readable numbers, clean icons, and crisp typography even at arm’s length in a noisy gaming room.
- Compatibility: the dashboard must fit the original character sheet footprint, limb by limb, so you don’t have to rework your entire table just to accommodate it.
- Modularity: pieces should snap together with minimal friction and be easy to replace if a token is lost or a new effect appears in an expansion.
- Robustness: FDM parts can be a little brittle; we tested with PLA and PETG to see which materials stand up to weekly gaming sessions and the occasional tabletop tumble.
- Aesthetics: a look that blends the medieval fantasy vibe with clean lines and legible text that won’t clash with the base game art.

Secondary goals include easy post‑print finishing, minimal post‑processing, and the ability to customize with your own color schemes and faction palettes.

## Printing and material choices
Two main materials anchor the build: PLA for approachable printing and PETG for durability. PLA is forgiving — new players can print a neat dashboard with minimal warping. PETG adds resilience, especially if your gaming crew tends to maul the table edges or if you want something that handles finger oils better after a long session. A common trick is to print the main plate in a lighter color and use darker shades for the status bars so the information pops from across the table.

Layer height matters here. A 0.15 mm layer height gives you sufficient detail for icons without dragging print time into the realm of myth. 0.08 mm can be nice for supreme precision on tiny numbers. In testing, 0.12 to 0.15 mm is a sweet spot for a balanced print time and readable details. For infill, aim around 15 to 25 percent; too high and you add weight, too low and the piece can flex under the threat of a particularly aggressive roll.

Support structures should be minimized. Designing parts with self‑supporting overhangs reduces cleanup and keeps edges sharp for the die cuts that populate the board. A light chamfer on the outer edges helps avoid snagging on sleeves or rogue dice during a crit moment and keeps the piece safe from minor impacts over a campaign.

If you’re curious about filament choices, we’ve got a quick primer in our filament guide. For printers, a standard desktop FDM with a 0.4 mm nozzle and a stable bed is all you need. The result is a piece you’ll actually want to leave on the table rather than stash in a closet after the first session.

> Pro tip: print multiple small tests to dial in fit with the base board. A tiny misalignment can become a big headache when you’re trying to click the sub‑plates into place while a ghastly goblin yanks your attention away.

### The practical print checklist
- Choose PLA for a first pass; switch to PETG for durability after you’ve dialed tolerances.
- Print main plate first to establish base alignment; then print sub‑plates in batches.
- Keep orientation notes so you can trust the fit when you snap everything together.
- Consider a matte sealant for longevity and wipe‑down ease; it helps with greasy dice hands between rounds.
- Test fit with official tokens and reserve space for dry‑erase notes if your group likes temporary modifiers.

## The build process in a nutshell (expanded)
Print projects are never a clean, single‑night sprint. They’re survival expeditions with layers. Here’s a more granular take on what we did and what you can expect if you’re building this for a full squad:

1) Load the base model set and verify the scale against the base game board. A hair off and the snap‑fit will feel like a goblin’s riddle.
2) Slice at 0.12–0.15 mm layer height; enable a light cooling fan on the top layers to preserve edge sharpness for crisp icons.
3) Print the main plate first to establish baseline alignment. Allow to fully cool; this minimizes warping on subsequent pieces.
4) Print sub‑plates for wounds, energy, and status effects separately. Keep a print‑log with orientation clues.
5) Post‑process with light sanding on the edges and remove stray strings. Don’t overdo it or you’ll dull icon legibility.
6) Test‑fit with official tokens; confirm there’s space for dry‑erase notes if you use them.
7) Optional: seal with a matte finish for longevity and easier wipe‑downs after fat‑thumbed turns.
8) Assemble in a clean workspace. The sub‑plates should click with a satisfying resistance, not a heroic struggle.
9) If a piece feels looser than expected, reprint that component in a slightly adjusted tolerance; minor tweaks pay off in round two.
10) Add color accents to highlight actions and wounds; test on a spare piece first to ensure paint doesn’t obscure text.
11) Attach to the board with minimal friction; magnets or clips can help keep things steady on a crowded table.
12) Run a mock combat sequence without dice to validate readability under game lighting before you deploy to a live session.

This expanded procedure yields a dashboard that feels sturdy yet forgiving enough to handle enthusiastic finger‑tats from a table full of players.

## A practical print run and assembly tips (expanded)
- Start with a single character plate to verify scale, then print the rest in batches. This helps calibrate without getting overwhelmed by a mountain of parts.
- Use a low infill for outer shells but bolster inner structures around action point channels. You want stiffness without turning the plate into a brick.
- If you plan to use the dashboard on a crowded table, consider magnets or clips to secure the board to the base to prevent accidental nudges during tense moments.
- For a smoother finish, a very light sanding along outer edges followed by a quick matte clear coat helps reduce glare and improves longevity across many gaming nights.
- Label the sub‑plates with tiny doodads or initials if you’re running multiple characters to avoid swapping errors mid‑round.

### The gallery of fit and finish tips
We included a couple of real world notes from sessions: sometimes a tiny misalignment shows up as a stubborn gap between plates. Our fix is a small reprint of the misbehaving piece with a slightly looser tolerance (0.05–0.1 mm more clearance can shave seconds off snagging). If you are battling warping, consider printing the main plate in PETG and the sub‑plates in PLA or add a light bead of CA glue for the edges to keep things square during storage.

### Printing profiles we tested
- PLA, 0.12–0.15 mm layer height, 15–20 % infill for the main plate; 15 % for sub‑plates.
- PETG, 0.2 mm layer height for quick sessions that still look crisp; reduce cooling to avoid stringing on tiny icons.
- Support strategy: light supports only for overhangs on the outside edges; use dissolvable supports for complex internal geometries if your slicer supports it.
- Post‑processing: gentle sanding, a microfiber wipe, then a matte sealant for longevity.

## Maintenance and durability considerations
The dashboard is built to last but requires care. After a long campaign, wipe down with a dry cloth to remove chalky residue and dice oil. If you notice a looser fit after several games, a light reprint or a small adjustment in print settings will usually restore the perfect snap. For ongoing durability, consider sealing the surface with a thin matte finish. Heavy paints on the main surface are discouraged since the printed texture adds grip and helps characters stay legible from across the table. If you enjoy custom aesthetics, you can paint icons and numbers with a fine brush to match your army color; just test on a spare piece first to ensure that paint doesn’t obscure the text.

Storage is a thing: keep sub‑plates in labeled bags or a stackable organizer. A little care in storage prevents a chaotic reassembly moment after you pick up a sleepy goblin eye patch on the dice tower. If you want to keep things pristine for future campaigns, consider a simple UV‑resistant varnish to slow color fade on heavily lit tables.
