## What is the plateau de personnage
The plateau de personnage is a compact, well labeled, and highly legible stand that lives on the player board. It consolidates core status data that you normally track with small tokens and overlays. The idea is simple: if your character sheet has more icons than a sci fi control panel, you deserve a cleaner surface that tells you what matters in a heartbeat. The design here is crafted for Zombicide Black Plague, which means life points, action points, zombie counters, armor notes, and current effects all have home on a single plate. The color palette aligns with the original art style so you can identify a wound quickly, or a shield that is about to save your bacon from the next wave.

This is not a universal dashboard for every board game. It is tailored to the Black Plague cadence and the way players manage their teams across a campaign. It respects the two major play modes in Black Plague — a quick session and a longer, more narrative campaign. It also offers a version that integrates with the yellow and black tokens that come with the base game so the aesthetic is cohesive rather than a patchy mod cluster on the table.

## Design goals and constraints
Before you print a dozen pieces, it is helpful to outline what we aimed for:

- Clarity over complexity: readable numbers, clean icons, and crisp typography even at a distance of a few hands.
- Compatibility: the dashboard must fit the original character sheet footprint, limb by limb, so no one has to reframe the entire table just to fit the device.
- Modularity: pieces should snap together with minimal friction and be easy to replace if a token is lost or a new effect is added in an expansion.
- Robustness: FDM printed parts can be a little brittle if you push too hard. We tested with PLA and PETG to see what stands up to weekly gaming sessions and occasional surface abuse from enthusiastic players.
- Aesthetics: a look that blends the medieval fantasy vibe of Black Plague with the modern gamer gym vibe of clean lines and legible text.

Secondary goals include easy post print finishing, minimal post processing, and the possibility to customize with your own creature or faction color scheme.

## Printing and material choices
The prints you see here leaned on two main materials: PLA for ease of printing and PETG for durability. PLA is forgiving — if you are new to 3D printing, you can produce a pretty neat dashboard out of PLA with minimal warping. PETG adds resilience, especially if your gaming group tends to be a little rough with the table edges or if you keep the file on display and want something that resists finger oils. A common trick is to print the main plate in a light charge color and use a darker shade for the status bars so the information pops from across the table.

Layer height matters here. A 0.15 mm layer height gives you sufficient detail for icons without dragging print time into the days of yesteryear, while 0.08 mm can be nice if you want supreme precision for tiny numbers. In our test, 0.12 to 0.15 mm is the sweet spot for a balanced print time versus the readability of numbers. For infill you want something around 15 to 25 percent; too high and you add unnecessary weight, too low and the piece can flex when you reach for a spell card.

Support structures should be minimized. Designing the parts with self supporting overhangs reduces cleanup and keeps the edges sharp enough for the die cuts that populate the board. A light chamfer around the outer edges helps to avoid snagging on sleeves or bad dice tosses and keeps the piece safe from minor impact during the course of a campaign.

If you are curious about how to choose your filament, a quick primer is available in our sequence on [filament selection]({% post_url 2025-06-18-filament-guide.md %}). For printers, a standard desktop FDM is all you need, provided you have a 0.4 mm nozzle and a stable print bed. The result is a piece you will actually want to leave on the table rather than stash in a closet after the first session.

## The build process in a nutshell
The build is not a single evening project. It starts with slicing the files, then printing the pieces in batches, followed by a careful post print cleanup. Here is a simplified step by step guide to what we did:

1) Prepare the model set and verify the scale against the base game board.
2) Slice with a 0.12 to 0.15 mm layer height and enable a light fan for the top layers to preserve edge sharpness.
3) Print the main plate first to establish base alignment. Make sure to let it cool between prints to avoid warps.
4) Print the sub pieces for wounds, energy, and status effects. Keep track of orientation to ensure correct fit when snapping together.
5) Post process with light sanding on edges and remove any little stringing or stray filament nibs.
6) Test fit with official tokens and ensure there is room for OTC text or user notes if you choose to write on the plate with a dry erase marker.

This process yields a dashboard that feels sturdy and has a little give where necessary so you do not crack a corner during a frantic round of play. The design includes a removable sub plate for wounded tokens, an inset area for action dice, and a dedicated slot for armor or shield counters. It all fits within the silhouette of the original character board so your table remains consistent, even when players bring their fancy upgraded pieces to the table.

## Visual and tactile design details
The plateau de personnage uses a clean typographic scale that reads well under warm room lighting. The icons are designed to be legible at a minimum distance of three to four feet, which is critical when the table gets noisy. The color palette mirrors the base game art to reduce cognitive load; you do not have to memorize a new color code every time a zombie swarm spawns. The dashboard provides a clean micro-environment where information can be processed with minimal brain strain, letting you focus on strategy rather than counting cycles.

## Mechanics and usability
The usefulness of any dashboard comes down to how quickly you can access the right information without looking at the wrong thing. The plateau de personnage is organized into distinct zones:

- Health and wounds: large numerals for current HP and wound status with a dedicated indicator that pops when the count is low.
- Action points: a compact, color coded bar that shows the remaining actions for the turn.
- Effects and conditions: icons that are easy to read at the table distance and that clear with a quick glance.
- Inventory and armor: a small inventory strip that tracks armor points and carried items, reducing the need to flip a card mid round.

The layout is purposefully modular: you can move a sub plate to a new position if your group prefers a different table arrangement or if you own an expansion where extra tokens become part of the character sheet. The result is a dashboard that feels like a natural extension of the table rather than a foreign body glued to the board.

In play testing, the dashboard shaved several minutes off the first round of turns in a typical team. It is not a magic wand that turns mistakes into success, but it does help players recover from the initial shock of a zombie wave with a smooth mental model for how much you can risk on your next action.

## A practical print run and assembly tips
- Start with a single character plate to verify scale, then print the remaining characters in batches. This helps you calibrate the printer without being overwhelmed by a mountain of parts.
- Use a low infill for outer shells but bump up the internal structures for durability around the action point channels. You want a bit of stiffness without turning the plate into a brick.
- If you plan to use the dashboard on a crowded table, you may want to add small magnets or clips to secure the board to the base, preventing accidental bumps during a tense moment.
- For a smoother finish, a very light sanding along the outer edges followed by a quick spray of matte clear coat can help reduce glare and improve longevity across many gaming nights.

## Maintenance and durability considerations
The dashboard is built to last, but it does require some basic care. After a long campaign, wipe down with a dry cloth to remove chalky residue and dice oil. If you notice a looser fit after several games, a light reprint or an adjustment in print settings will usually restore the perfect snap. For ongoing durability, consider sealing the surface with a thin matte finish. Avoid heavy paints on the main surface since the printed texture adds grip and helps characters stay legible from across the table. If you are into custom aesthetics, you can paint the icons and numbers with a fine brush to match your army color. Just test on a spare piece first to ensure that the paint does not obscure the text.
