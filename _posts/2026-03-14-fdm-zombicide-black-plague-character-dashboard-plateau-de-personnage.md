## What is the plateau de personnage
The plateau de personnage is a compact, clearly labeled stand that sits on the player board. It consolidates core status data you normally track with a cloud of small tokens and overlays. The idea is simple: if your character sheet has more icons than a sci-fi control panel, you deserve a cleaner surface that tells you what matters in a heartbeat. ## Design goals and constraints
Before you print a dozen pieces, here is what we aimed for, refined through multiple print runs and a little trial by noise:

- Clarity over complexity: readable numbers, clean icons, and crisp typography even at arm’s length in a noisy gaming room.
- Compatibility: the dashboard must fit the original character sheet footprint, limb by limb, so you don’t have to rework your entire table just to accommodate it.
- Modularity: pieces should snap together with minimal friction and be easy to replace if a token is lost or a new effect appears in an expansion.
- Robustness: FDM parts can be a little brittle; we tested with PLA and PETG to see which materials stand up to weekly gaming sessions and the occasional table-top tumble.
- Aesthetics: a look that blends the medieval fantasy vibe with clean lines and legible text that won’t clash with the base game art.

Secondary goals include easy post-print finishing, minimal post-processing, and the ability to customize with your own color schemes and faction palettes.

## Printing and material choices
Two main materials anchor the build: PLA for approachable printing and PETG for durability. PLA is forgiving—newbies can print a neat dashboard with minimal warping. PETG adds resilience, especially if your gaming crew tends to maul the table edges or if you want something that handles finger oils better after a long session. A common trick is to print the main plate in a lighter color and use darker shades for the status bars so the information pops from across the table.

Layer height matters here. A 0.15 mm layer height gives you sufficient detail for icons without dragging print time into the realm of myth. 0.08 mm can be nice for supreme precision on tiny numbers. In testing, 0.12 to 0.15 mm is a sweet spot for a balanced print time and readable details. For infill, aim around 15 to 25 percent; too high and you add weight, too low and the piece can flex under threat of a particularly aggressive roll.

Support structures should be minimized. Designing parts with self-supporting overhangs reduces cleanup and keeps edges sharp for the die cuts that populate the board. A light chamfer on the outer edges helps avoid snagging on sleeves or rogue dice during a crit moment and keeps the piece safe from minor impacts over a campaign.

If you’re curious about filament choices, we’ve got a quick primer in our filament guide: [filament selection]({% post_url 2025-06-18-filament-guide.md %}). For printers, a standard desktop FDM with a 0.4 mm nozzle and a stable bed is all you need. The result is a piece you’ll actually want to leave on the table rather than stash in a closet after the first session.

> Pro tip: print multiple small tests to dial in fit with the base board. A tiny misalignment can become a big headache when you’re trying to click the sub-plates into place while a ghastly goblin yanks your attention away.

## The build process in a nutshell
The build is not a one-night sprint; it’s a micro-assembly project that rewards patience and a tidy workspace. Here’s a more detailed, pragmatic guide to what we did and what you can expect:

1) Prepare the model set and verify the scale against the base game board. If you’re off by even a hair, the snap-fit won’t click.
2) Slice with a 0.12 to 0.15 mm layer height and enable a light fan for the top layers to preserve edge sharpness. A cooler top layer means crisper icon edges for those nerve-wracking moments when you need to read status effects at a distance.
3) Print the main plate first to establish base alignment. Let it cool between prints to minimize warping.
4) Print sub-pieces for wounds, energy, and status effects. Keep orientation notes so you can trust the fit when you snap everything together.
5) Post-process with light sanding on edges and remove stray strings. Don’t sand too aggressively or you’ll nick the legibility of the icons.
6) Test-fit with official tokens and ensure room for dry-erase notes, if your group likes jotting down temporary modifiers.
7) Optional: seal with a matte finish for longevity and easier wipe-downs after fat-thumbed turns.

This process yields a dashboard that feels sturdy but still has a touch of forgiving give to avoid snapping corners during a heated round. The design includes a removable sub-plate for wounded tokens, an inset area for action dice, and a dedicated slot for armor or shield counters. It remains within the silhouette of the original character board, so the table’s visual language stays consistent even when you upgrade with fancy modules.

## Mechanics and usability
The usefulness of any dashboard is measured by how quickly you can access the right information without hunting for the wrong plate. The plateau de personnage is organized into distinct zones:

- Health and wounds: large numerals for current HP and wound status with a dedicated indicator that pops when the count is low.
- Action points: a compact, color-coded bar that shows the remaining actions for the turn.
- Effects and conditions: icons readable at the table distance and clear with a quick glance.
- Inventory and armor: a small strip tracking armor points and carried items, reducing the need to flip a card mid-round.

The layout is deliberately modular. You can move sub-plates to new positions if your group prefers a different table arrangement or if you add an expansion where extra tokens are used. The result is a dashboard that feels like a natural extension of the table rather than a foreign body glued to the board.

In play-testing, the dashboard shaved measurable minutes off the first round of turns in a typical team. It won’t turn a misclick into a win, but it does help players pivot faster after the first zombie maelstrom, providing a mental model for what you can risk on your next action.

## A practical print run and assembly tips
- Start with a single character plate to verify scale, then print the rest in batches. This helps you calibrate without getting overwhelmed by a mountain of parts.
- Use a low infill for outer shells but boost inner structures around action point channels. You want stiffness without turning the plate into a brick.
- If you plan to use the dashboard on a crowded table, consider magnets or clips to secure the board to the base to prevent accidental nudges during tense moments.
- For a smoother finish, a very light sanding along outer edges followed by a quick matte clear coat helps reduce glare and improves longevity across many gaming nights.

## Maintenance and durability considerations
The dashboard is built to last but does require some care. After a long campaign, wipe down with a dry cloth to remove chalky residue and dice oil. If you notice a looser fit after several games, a light reprint or a small adjustment in print settings will usually restore the perfect snap. For ongoing durability, consider sealing the surface with a thin matte finish. Heavy paints on the main surface are discouraged since the printed texture adds grip and helps characters stay legible from across the table. If you enjoy custom aesthetics, you can paint icons and numbers with a fine brush to match your army color; just test on a spare piece first to ensure that paint doesn’t obscure the text.
