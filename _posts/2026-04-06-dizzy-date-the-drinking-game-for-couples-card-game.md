## Overview
Dizzy Date is a card-driven game designed for couples to play a series of prompts, dares, and questions while encouraging playful vulnerability and a dash of competitive banter. The deck includes question cards, dare cards, policy cards (things you commit to trying), and a few wildcards to shake up the evening. The core idea is simple: you draw cards, complete the prompts, keep score (or not), and see how dizzy your date night can get before you start declaring truth over truth and truth or dare over dare.

The packaging is bright, with a cheeky font and illustrations that promise a night of laughter rather than a stern “we need to talk.” It’s the kind of game you would casually bring to a living room date night, a romantic game night with friends where you say, “We’re an adult couple with cool hobbies,” and your friends respond with a knowing nod and maybe a mic drop. The components feel sturdy, and the cards smell faintly of ambition and coconut-lime body wash, which is exactly the mood you want when you’re asking your partner, “Describe our first date in three emojis.”

## How to Play (The Quickstart)
If you’ve played a party game before, you’ll recognize this rhythm. Dizzy Date wants you to pull cards, respond, react, and either collect points or simply bask in the glow of shared laughter. Here’s the streamlined version:

### Setup
- Shuffle the deck. Place it in the center. 
- Decide on your scoring method: keep score and aim for a silly high score, or go full “no points, all vibes.”
- Pick who starts. The starter doesn’t get to pick a card; the other person does, if you’re going classic. You can also flip for it, which is a dramatic way to decide who will be the first to blush.
- Set the timer if you’re using timed prompts. If not, you can do a generous 60-second window and pretend you’re on a dating show where the clock is your love translator.

### Turn structure
1) The active player draws a card. There are several card types:
- Question Cards: Answer honestly. The diametric opposite of “lie about your ex” is “answer honestly.”
- Dare Cards: Something fun, a bit ridiculous, and usually harmless. These can involve silly outfits, quick truths, or small challenges.
- Policy Cards: These require each partner to commit to an action for a duration (e.g., “We will try one new cuisine this month”). These are great for long-term intimacy, but they can also be the most nerve-wracking if you’re still getting comfortable with ‘vulnerability’ as a hobby.
- Wild Cards: Spicy, goofy, or downright chaotic cards that wake you up from the usual dating-night snooze.
2) Complete the task on the card within the allotted time, if applicable.
3) Move the score marker or simply return the card and pass to the other player.
4) Repeat until you’ve either run out of cards or you decide to call it a night (which, let’s be honest, often happens after Card #42: “Do your best celebrity impression.”)

### Card etiquette and safety basics
- Consent is king. If a dare or question pushes into territory you’re not comfortable with, swap it for an allowed alternative (the deck usually includes swap cards or you can declare a “stop” and reframe the prompt).
- It’s okay to skip. If you’re not feeling a prompt, you can say, “Not today,” and grab a new card. The goal is connection, not punishment.
- Have a safe word or a side-object that signals, “We’re done with this line of play for now.” It’s not cheating; it’s protecting your date-night vibes.

### The twists that save the night
Design-wise, the game leans into tension-release humor. The prompts escalate gradually from light and silly to “you two are now a tiny marketing campaign for courage.” The transitions are smooth enough that you rarely hit a lull between rounds, and when you do, the silly prompts pick up the pace again.

## Variants and House Rules
No two date nights are identical, and Dizzy Date acknowledges this with a handful of variants you can mix and match:
- Speed Round: Set a tighter timer (30 seconds per card) and race to the finish for bragging rights.
- No-Repeat Night: If you’ve already answered a question, you can’t repeat it. The deck still has plenty of prompts; you’ll be surprised how much you haven’t discussed yet.
- Blind Card: Draw a card face-down, read it aloud, and then both partners respond before revealing. Adds a tasty layer of suspense.
- Luxury Mode: Add a “couples spa” or “romantic playlist” overlay; the prompts become more about shared experiences than personal revelations, if that’s your vibe.

The game also encourages customization: you can add your own prompts, swap cards you hate for new ones, or create a “forbidden topics” list if there are boundaries you don’t want to cross. It’s a flexible framework rather than a rigid commandment, which makes it friendlier for a wider range of couples, from newly minted relationships to long-time partners who still pretend to be on a first date every Friday.
