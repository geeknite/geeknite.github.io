## Overview
Dizzy Date is a card-driven game designed for couples to play a series of prompts, dares, and questions while encouraging playful vulnerability and a dash of competitive banter. The deck includes question cards, dare cards, policy cards (things you commit to trying), and a few wildcards to shake up the evening. The core idea is simple: you draw cards, complete the prompts, keep score (or not), and see how dizzy your date night can get before you start declaring truth over truth and truth or dare over dare.

### Card Types in More Detail
- Question Cards: The nucleus. They invite honesty wrapped in a wink. Expect prompts like “Describe your first kiss in three emojis” or “What’s a small thing I do that you wish I did more often?”
- Dare Cards: The spark. Think silly outfits, goofy truths, or tiny challenges like “Dance for 20 seconds with a kitchen utensil as your microphone.”
- Policy Cards: The commitments. These aim to move you forward as a duo (“We’ll try a new cuisine this month”) rather than a one-off stunt.
- Wild Cards: The chaos engine. Spicy truths, goofy dares, or sudden changes of scenery (bring a blindfold? Sure, why not).

## How to Play (The Quickstart)
If you’ve played a party game before, you’ll recognize the rhythm. Dizzy Date wants you to pull cards, respond, react, and either collect points or simply bask in the glow of shared laughter. Here’s the streamlined version, plus a few tips from the trenches.

### Setup
- Shuffle the deck. Place it in the center within easy reach.
- Decide on your scoring method: a silly high score that you pretend is meaningful, or no points, all vibes.
- Pick who starts. The starter can’t pick a card; the other person selects the card for them, if you’re playing “classic.” You can also flip for it, which adds drama and late-evening blushes.
- Set the timer if you’re using timed prompts. If not, a generous 60-second window is fine and you can pretend you’re on a dating show where the clock is your love translator.

### Turn Structure (The Core Flow)
1) The active player draws a card. There are several card types (Question, Dare, Policy, Wild).
2) Complete the task on the card within the allotted time, if applicable. If you’re skippable, skip gracefully.
3) Move the score marker or simply pass the card to the other player.
4) Repeat until you’ve run out of cards or you decide to call it a night (which, let’s be honest, often happens after Card #42: “Do your best celebrity impression.”)

### Card Etiquette and Safety Basics
- Consent is king. If a dare or question pushes into territory you’re not comfortable with, swap it for an allowed alternative or declare a safe prompt.
- It’s okay to skip. You can say, “Not today,” and grab a new card.
- Have a safety plan: a safe word or a pre-agreed signal that says, “We’re stepping back now.” It’s not cheating; it’s a maturity badge on date-night.

## Variants and House Rules
No two date nights are identical, and Dizzy Date acknowledges this with variants you can mix and match. Here are a few to try, plus a few more that could exist in a parallel universe:

- Speed Round: Tighter timers (30 seconds per card) to burn through the deck and earn bragging rights.
- No-Repeat Night: If you’ve already answered a question, you can’t repeat it. The deck still has plenty of prompts.
- Blind Card: Draw a card face-down, read aloud, and then both partners respond before revealing. Adds suspense and a little theater.
- Luxury Mode: Overlay with a “couples spa” or “romantic playlist.” The prompts become more about shared experiences than raw vulnerability, if that’s your vibe.
- No-Pressure Neon: A neon-glow version where you do everything with glow sticks and a playlist that’s so loud you can feel your teeth.

The game also invites customization: you can add your own prompts, swap cards you hate for new ones, or create a “forbidden topics” list if there are boundaries you don’t want to cross. It’s a flexible framework rather than a strict rulebook, which makes it friendlier for a broader spectrum of couples, from brand-new relationships to partners who’ve known each other long enough to preface every sentence with, “Remember that one time…?”
