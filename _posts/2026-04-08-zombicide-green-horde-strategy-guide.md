## Core mechanics recap (a quick refresher)
Zombicide games hinge on shared turn actions, loud noises, and the constant tension between securing a safe zone and salvaging gear. In Green Horde, you’ll:
- Move a group of survivors on a grid, using a set number of action points each turn
- Search for weapons and items to enhance combat efficiency and survivability
- Spawn zombies (and greens) based on the scenario and card draws, which creates dynamic board states
- Use doors and terrain to create choke points that you can hold against the advancing horde
- Complete mission objectives to win, rather than simply “kill everything” (though that helps)

The addition of greens complicates the math: greens are tougher, more unpredictable, and often tied to objective triggers that can force you to split up, retreat, or fortify a single corridor for a longer period. This is where the real strategy comes in: you need to balance aggression with caution, tempo with safety, and loot urgency with teammate welfare.

## Setup and map flow: getting your house in order
A good Green Horde run begins with setup discipline. Here are the essential steps and best practices:
- Choose your starting survivors with complementary abilities. A balanced crew often beats a “coolest hero” lineup, especially when greens are in the mix.
- Assess the board layout for likely choke points. The first few turns should invest in establishing a safe zone—one or two rooms with a door or two closed off, and a path to loot that doesn’t require sprinting into the teeth of the enemy.
- Place objective cards in positions that incentivize a deliberate push rather than a reckless sprint. If your objectives promote exploration, set up the map so exploration happens with a clear escape route.
- Shuffle and place the greens in positions that create a natural pressure line toward your safe zone. You want a map where the greens’ advance feels like a siege rather than a surprise ambush.

Once set up, your first turns should focus on mapping threats, collecting gear, and establishing a safe zone that you can defend without needing to repair a broken coalition after the first bad hit. The Green Horde loves to punish overextension, so early consolidation pays off.

## Strategy pillars: how to think about Green Horde
Three pillars form the backbone of a successful Green Horde plan:
1) Positioning and terrain control
2) Resource management (loot, armor, weapons)
3) Objective timing (when to strike, when to hold, and when to retreat)

Let’s break these down with practical examples and tactical checks you can apply at the table.

### Positioning and terrain control
One of the most valuable assets is space—the ability to control the line of engagement. Green Horde’s greens will crash into your formation in numbers, so you’ll want to:
- Create funnel points: narrow hallways or doorways where you can maximize your fighting power with a small squad.
- Use corners and walls to block line of sight or to separate weaker survivors from the main pack when necessary.
- Plan your loot routes so you aren’t zig-zagging across the map multiple times; you should be able to retreat to your safe zone without passing through high-risk zones more than once per round.
- Keep your ranged specialists behind cover. Green hordes can be particularly punishing if you expose melee-only heroes to a wide-front attack.

A classic mistake is trying to “clear” every room in one go. You don’t have to kill every zombie to win; you just need to collect the required objectives and survive long enough to extract. Read the room, not the room’s blood pressure.

### Resource management: loot, weapons, and healing
Green Horde encourages you to think in terms of resource budgeting. Don’t grab every shiny item if you don’t have the means to carry or use it. Key tips:
- Prioritize tools that extend your action economy (e.g., multi-use weapons, extra actions, or movement cards) over loot that looks flashy but doesn’t translate to survival.
- Healers and tank-types become incredibly valuable in longer campaigns; protect them and ensure they don’t get pinned in the first wave.
- Salvage from fights should be purposeful; every item you pick up should fill a gap in your current build or offer a critical upgrade for the next encounter.
- Don’t neglect the ability to block zombies by using environment: doors, tables, and other obstacles can save you more than a new blade.

Loot management is also about timing. A well-timed weapon upgrade before the late-game surge can mean the difference between a heroic exit and a tragic fan-fiction ending.

### Objective timing: when to strike, when to hold, when to retreat
The mission goals in Green Horde can reward careful planning more than sheer aggression. You’ll often see objectives that require you to reach a location, collect a token, or survive a round with a specific constraint. Key practice:
- Map out the objective path on the first turn. If you know where you need to be in the late stages, you can direct your survivors’ movement to minimize backtracking.
- Use the safe zone as a staging ground for the objective push. If possible, have a couple of survivors set up the final location while others deal with the front-line greens.
- If the scenario introduces “boss” greens or tougher elites, coordinate a two-phase approach: survive the initial wave, then pivot to the objective once the threat density drops.

If you’ve got previous experience with Zombicide, you’ll recognize a familiar pattern: the best plans are adaptable plans. You’ll need to pivot often as new cards flip, doors open, or reinforcements arrive.

## Survivor roles and ability synergies
Your team’s composition matters almost as much as your roll-of-the-dice luck. In Green Horde, you’ll want a mix of combat efficiency, map control, and healing potential. A few archetypes tend to perform well:
- The Frontline Guardian: a survivalist tank who can soak hits and hold a doorway while others reposition.
- The Scout: a speedy explorer who can grab loot and scout ahead without exposing the entire team.
- The Tactician: a support hero who can optimize action economy, hinder greens, or provide useful buffs to teammates.
- The Healer: essential in longer campaigns to minimize restocking downtime.
- The Sharpshooter: a ranged specialist who can thin greens from advantageous cover.

If you’re new, a balanced squad with at least one healer or support option and one capable frontline is a good starting point. As you gain experience with Green Horde, you’ll discover which combinations best fit your playgroup’s style.

## Combat tactics against greens
Greens aren’t your run-of-the-mill common walkers. They often require a more deliberate approach to combat, with emphasis on control and conservation of actions.

### Ranged vs melee: when to shoot and when to swing
- Use ranged weapons to soften crowds and create safe gaps for your frontline. Don’t be shy about trading a round of ranged attacks for a guaranteed next-turn advantage.
- Melee should be reserved for situations where you have a guaranteed kill or can secure a doorway without overexposing your team. Remember: a failed melee encounter may unleash a cascade of reinforcements.
- Positioning matters more than raw damage. If you can force greens to bunch up in a doorway, you can maximize the effect of area-control tools or splash-damage cards.

### Blocking and generating momentum
Momentum is the currency of success in Zombicide games. If you can chain actions so one survivor’s move creates a favorable alignment for the next, you’ll maximize your card draw potential and keep the pace steady.
- The plan: one survivor holds the line, another collects loot, and a third threads through to collect a crucial objective token. If the return path is clear, you can exit with minimal losses.
- Don’t forget the power of a well-timed quick-turn to reposition. If the greens surge, you want your group to pivot rather than freeze.

## Map and terrain utilization
The board is your friend—if you know how to coerce it into cooperating with your plan.
- Doors, corridors, and choke points are your best allies. Use narrow passages to partition enemies and limit the number of greens that can reach your heroes in a single turn.
- High-value loot should be placed in positions that reward careful work rather than reckless sprinting. If loot is in a hard-to-reach area, design a plan to secure it in stages without overexposing your team.
- Use terrain effects to your advantage. Some scenarios feature terrain that slows zombies or gives bonuses to ranged attacks; if you ignore them, you’ll regret it.

## Scenario variants and how to adapt your plan
Green Horde scenarios are rarely exactly the same as the base game’s. You’ll encounter variants that nudge you toward different objectives or encourage repeated playthroughs with slightly different constraints.
- Short missions: focus on rapid objective completion with a minimal risk approach. These are your warmups and practice runs.
- Long campaigns: plan for resource management and a slower pace, allowing you to accumulate critical upgrades before the final push.
- Boss or “end-game” encounters: coordinate your party so that the tank can hold the line while damage dealers do their best work from protected angles.

Adaptation is the name of the game. If you enjoy a challenge, run through a couple of quick missions to calibrate your group’s synergy before attempting a longer campaign.

## Practical playthrough snapshot (one turn sample)
- Turn start: The Frontline Guard holds the doorway, a Scout advances to loot a minor cache while the Sharpshooter picks a safe long-range shot at a small cluster of greens.
- Action economy: Guard uses two actions to pivot into a blocking position; Scout uses one action to move and one to loot; Sharpshooter uses one to shoot, one to reposition.
- Turn end: The objective token is now within reachable distance, but the team needs to defend against a second wave. The plan shifts from “kill all” to “kill enough, secure, extract.”

This is the rhythm Green Horde rewards: measured tempo and a willingness to trade safety for progress when the numbers align. If you’re improvising, you’re probably doing it right—just keep the team alive and the objective in sight.
