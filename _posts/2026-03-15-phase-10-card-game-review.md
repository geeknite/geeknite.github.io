## Phase 10 at a Glance

Phase 10 is a card drawing and discarding game with a built in ladder. Each round, players draw and discard cards until someone can lay down a phase from the current stage. Once a phase is laid down, that player has cleared that phase and is now working on the next one. The catch is that you have to complete the next phase in your own hand; you are not allowed to borrow someone else s patterns. The twist adds a little pressure: you must finish each phase in order, or you will have to try again in the next round.

## How the game flows

Phase 10 uses a fairly simple turn structure that even non-gamers can pick up in a single sitting. Here is the gist:

- The dealer shuffles the deck and deals 10 cards to each player. The rest is the draw pile, with the top card flipped to start the discard pile.
- On your turn you draw one card (from the draw pile or the discard pile) and then discard one card to the discard pile. The goal is to complete the phase you are currently aiming for and then lay it down on the table when you have it. When you lay down a phase, you move to the next phase on your next turn.
- Wild cards can stand in for any card in your phase; skip cards skip another player s turn and can dramatically alter the rhythm of the round.
- The round ends when a player is first to discard all their cards after laying down the current phase. The rest of the players tally up the points for any cards left in hand, and a new round starts with the next player as dealer.

In practice, the flow is a blend of rummy like hand building and a pressure cooker that scales with the number of players. Two players means quicker rounds and closer attention to what your opponent has, while six players can drag on as you watch the final few cards you need slip into someone else s hands as they chase their own phases.

## The phases and how the twist changes things

The heart of Phase 10 is the progression through ten phases. The exact list of phase patterns is fixed in the rules and tends to escalate in complexity as you go. What matters for most players is the rhythm: you aim to complete a phase and then upgrade to the next one, all while managing your hand and not letting the wilds ruin your carefully planned sets and runs. The twist comes from the fact that you must complete each phase in order; there is no jumping back a phase or borrowing from the future. If you fail to lay down the required pattern in a round, you still carry the cards into the next round, and the scoring penalty stagnates or grows depending on your hand. This makes Phase 10 feel less like a single puzzle and more like a marathon of micro-puzzles, with the end in sight but the next puzzle always looming ahead.

To give you a sense of the intensity, here are a couple of example patterns you might encounter in early rounds:

- Phase 1: two sets of 3
- Phase 2: one set of 3 plus a run of 4
- Phase 3: a run of 4 plus a run of 4 or a full house
- Phase 4: a run of 5

These are simplified examples, and the actual list includes a number of other patterns including five of a kind, and more complex combos as you progress. The exact list is included in the official rules, and you can find it on the official Mattel product page or the BGG page we linked above. For our purposes here, the key is to recognize that you will be juggling sets and runs, and you will use wild cards to substitute when a phase spelling would otherwise be blocked by a single missing card. The skip cards, which punish the player to your left or right depending on house rules, add a social dynamic that you do not get in a pure rummy session.

## Strategy and house rules that actually improve the game

Phase 10 is not a deep strategy game; it does not demand the sort of heavy planning that a chess fan would crave. However, there are practical decisions that can make or break your night. Here are a few guidelines we ve learned after many warm misses and near misses:

- Plan a phase ahead, but stay flexible. If you are close to completing phase 2 but the draw pile keeps giving you junk, it can be worth taking a few extra turns to adjust your hand. The goal is to minimize the number of cards you hold at the end of the round.
- Watch your opponents patterns. If you notice someone is chasing a specific phase, you may want to hold onto a wild card to foil their run or simply avoid discarding a card that would complete their phase.
- Use skip cards surgically. Skips can save a round for you if you can chain a lucky run of draws, but they also let someone else catch up if you deploy them poorly. The trick is to save them for moments when the round s momentum seems to be turning against you.
- Wilds are versatile, but don t over depend on them. They can shield you from a single bad draw, but overusing them can deprive you of the satisfaction of completing a phase with real cards. It s a balancing act in the same vein as using a cheat code in a video game: it s fun, but not always satisfying.
- Endgame awareness. The moment someone hits the top of the ladder with a complete phase, the table becomes a frantic energy bath. In these moments, timing matters more than raw luck. Some players will try to speed through a few early rounds, others will aim for a longer game that grinds the phases into a satisfying puzzle jam. Decide on a preferred pace and commit to it.

For families or groups that want something light yet engaging, Phase 10 hits a very comfortable sweet spot.
