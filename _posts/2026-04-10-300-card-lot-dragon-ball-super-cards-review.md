## Condition, Grading, and Authenticity
NM (Near Mint) is a slippery term in card collecting. It’s the difference between a card that has seen a few games and a card that you’d happily show to your grandma without fear of losing your pride. Here are practical guidelines to set expectations and avoid the classic buyer’s remorse moment:

- Look for corner wear: tiny nicks or dents on the corners are common in bulk lots. If most cards have sharp corners with only minor whitening along the edges, that’s a good sign.
- Check surface clarity: a NM card should have clean foil or image surfaces with minimal scratches. If you can catch a reflection of the room on the card surface, you might have a card that’s seen a bit more play than you’d guess.
- Edges and centering: a lot-driven watchword is consistency. If you see a lot of cards with similar centering and edge wear, you’re dealing with the same stock that could affect resale value.
- Authenticity: DBS cards use a layered approach to printing and holograms. If you’re uncertain about a card’s authenticity, seek a seal, holo stamp, or official Bandai watermark. If something looks off, it probably is. When in doubt, consult the community marketplace guides or take the card to a local card shop for a quick authenticity check.

Grading isn’t always practical for bulk lots, but it’s worth at least an occasional post-purchase audit. A quick multiple-card curl test (gently bend not more than a few degrees) can reveal if there’s any stiffness or warping. If the cards rise back to flat after a gentle press, you’re probably okay. If not, you may want to set those aside for different handling or potential return depending on seller policy.

### Authenticity Red Flags
- Off-brand logos or misaligned holo foils
- Faded or washed-out artwork in a supposed holo card
- Card stock that smells off or has a suspicious shine
- Inconsistent back designs or misprinted text
If you encounter any of these, document clearly with photos and reach out to the seller with a calm, professional tone. A lot like romance, refunds work best when you’re reasonable and specific about the problem.
