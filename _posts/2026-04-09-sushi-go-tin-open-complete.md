## Components: Cards, Cards, and More Cards
The core of Sushi Go! is, unsurprisingly, the cards. The Tin Version carries the base deck, which includes a variety of sushi cards: nigiri, maki, sashimi, tempura, tamago, and more. Each card type has its own scoring rules, and it’s the drafting mechanic that makes the entire experience sing. In short: you pass around hands of cards and pick one, then pass the rest to the next player. The process repeats until everyone has drafted a set of cards across several rounds. Then you tally scores, varying per card type, and the winner emerges with an artful, sometimes hilariously-barefoot onigiri victory dance—okay, maybe not a dance, but a celebratory moment nonetheless.

In the Tin Version, the card stock is mid-weight—sturdy enough to survive repeated draws from a central pile and a reasonable number of shuffles. ## How to Play: Rules at a Glance
Sushi Go! is a drafting game that’s famous for its speed and simplicity. The Tin Version preserves these core mechanics and the short learning curve that makes it a favorite for mixed-age groups.

- Setup: Shuffle the deck, deal each player a hand of cards (the number depends on player count), and place the rest in the center. Each player also has a score tracker if you’re not using the included pad.
- Turn structure: On your turn, you pick one card from your hand to keep and pass the rest to the left. You continue until all cards are drafted.
- Round structure: The game is typically played in three rounds. After each round, you tally points for the cards you’ve collected. The winner is the player with the highest total after all rounds.
- Scoring basics: Nigiri cards score points based on the type and the dim sum cards, like sashimi and tempura, reward micro-sets that multiply your points. Maki cards grant points for the largest group. The exact numbers aren’t the same across editions, but the principle remains: you’re balancing immediate gains with longer, building sets across rounds.

In the Tin Version, the rules sheet is concise, and the packaging design encourages a quick set-up. It’s the sort of game you can teach in under five minutes and start playing within ten if your group reads quickly and debates sushi strategy the way cats chase laser pointers.
