City of Mist has always been a game about legends in the real world: people with masks, rival conspiracies, and neon signs that double as plot hooks. When a new art book drops for Fortune Row, the city’s mythical underbelly gets a glossy finish and a bigger voice than a street preacher with a microphone and a sense of noir fashion. Fortune Row is a district known for its glitz, grit, and a certain kind of nightlife where every dream might cost a favor, and every favor might cost a dream. The art book acts as a reference guide that helps players and storytellers alike visualize the area’s mood, factions, and the price of glamor in a city that refuses to sleep. Strap in, because we’re taking a stroll down Fortune Row, where the art isn’t merely decorative; it’s plot, mood, and a few dangerous secrets wearing sequins.

## Overview
### What is this art book, and who is it for?
The Fortune Row Art Book (Reference Guide) is not a crunchy ruleset. It’s a visual companion designed to spark imagination and provide a stable, richly illustrated frame for the setting. If you’re a Keeper who wants a tangible sense of place to drop into sessions, this book is your friend who heckles you from the bookshelf with a velvet glove and a neon sign. If you’re a player who loves to conjure up a character by looking at someone else’s drawing, this is your playground—an atlas of faces, locations, objects, and moods that help you answer a very human question: what does this district feel like when the sun dies and the bill collectors arrive?

In the spirit of City of Mist, Fortune Row blends myth and metropolis. It’s about people who have big legends inside small lives and about the way a city can cradle a myth until it becomes inevitable. The art book compiles concept art, character portraits, locations, and micro-narratives that map the district’s tone: glamorous, dangerous, tempting, and just a little bit haunted. If the core rulebook gives you the rules of the puzzle, this reference guide provides the puzzle pieces in color, scale, and texture.

## Inside the Book
### The artwork as a narrative device
Fortune Row’s art is not there to decorate the page; it is a narrative device, a visual language that communicates what words cannot. You’ll find spreads that juxtapose glitzy storefronts with shadowed alleyways, neon reflections on rain-slick pavement, and portraits that capture the inner myth of a character—the mask they wear and the person beneath. The balance of light and shadow is deliberate: it mirrors the tension between performance and reality that defines the City of Mist universe.

The art style mixes noir realism with painterly flourishes—think watercolor neon and inked silhouettes, with occasional digital gloss to emphasize the modernity of the city. It’s a deliberate choice to evoke both the old-world myth and the new-world internet-age rumor mill where rumors behave like rain—getting everywhere, soaking into everything, and eventually hardening into legend.

### Layout, pacing, and design choices
The physical book is designed to be a quick reference as well as a gallery. It uses a modular layout so you can flip to a specific character, landmark, or faction and get a snapshot that’s immediately usable in a session. Page spacing is generous, which makes it easy to read from a distance if you’re GMing with a projector or a lamp that casts dramatic shadows on your dice. Typography leans toward elegance with a touch of street-corner graffiti flair—clear enough to read at the table, stylish enough to mimic the sign of a club that might swallow your money and your secrets in one go.

The book also embraces a practical approach to visual storytelling. Text blocks are concise, but the art is the star. You’ll notice short blurbs near certain pieces that offer prompts for session hooks, NPC attitudes, or a quick mood-check for players asking, “What does this place feel like?” It’s not heavy-handed; it’s a whisper from the page: you know the vibe now, you just need to act on it.

### Content highlights: locations, people, and objects
- Locations: Fortune Row’s landmarks range from opulent, over-the-top cocktail lounges to seedier sanctums where deals are brokered in hush and perfume. The art book provides a gallery of storefront signage, door textures, and interior design cues that scream “rich, powerful, and not to be trifled with.”
- Characters: Portraits capture a spectrum of archetypes—titled elites who have the city’s ear, streetwise informants who can vanish in a crowd, and mythic figures who blend into the crowd with a trick of their own smile. Look for expressions and micro-details (gloved hands, cigarette smoke, a ring with a symbol) that hint at a backstory beyond the page.
- Objects: Fortune Row is full of items that tell tales as they pass from hand to hand—sealed envelopes, a prop from a vanished underworld show, a mirror that reveals a different person when held up to the light. The book’s object cards give you quick prompts for scene seeds or loot ideas to drop into your next session.

## Art Style and Themes
### Color, tone, and motif
Fortune Row’s art leans into metallics and saturated jewel tones: golds that gleam like coins tossed into a wishing well, blues that feel like a midnight river, and pinks that hum with neon electricity. The contrast between warm skin tones and cool neon lighting creates a mood that’s both seductive and perilous. Thematically, the book treats glamour as a currency—something that can be spent, saved, or stolen—and the art uses light as a lie detector. When a character is under pressure, the image might tilt slightly, the color balance warming or cooling in response to the scene’s emotional stakes. It’s a subtle but effective storytelling trick that keeps players close to the mood without needing a long narration.

### Character design and mask aesthetics
Masks in City of Mist are about performance and identity. The Fortune Row art introduces a spectrum of mask aesthetics—from ornate, almost ceremonial designs to minimalist, modern silhouettes that suggest a more practical approach to performance in daily life. The portraits emphasize posture, gaze, and the small tells that reveal a character’s dual nature. You’ll notice recurring motifs—verses of poetry hidden in jewelry, symbols etched into cuffs, or a familiar silhouette that appears across multiple NPCs. These motifs function as easter eggs you can weave into your game for players who relish long-term world-building.

### Thematic tensions and visual storytelling
A recurring theme is the tension between opulence and danger. The art book uses composition to convey this: wide-angle perspectives that show the grand arch of a city skyline while a narrow door frame hints at what lies beyond. Reflections in glass surfaces often show a different perspective—what the character doesn’t want you to see, or what they fear their own reflection might reveal. It’s a clever design choice that translates well into table talk: you can describe a scene with the help of an image and let the players fill in the gaps with their own interpretations.

## How to Use This Book at the Table
### Quick-start uses for GMs
- Mood boards on demand: if you need a vibe check for a scene, you can flip to a page and describe the setting using the image as a backdrop. The art becomes your third player on the table.
- NPC quick-facts: portraits come with micro-blurbs that you can read aloud to quickly establish a character’s attitude, a few hooks, and the kind of trouble they’re likely to cause.
- Prop prompts: object cards translate easily into in-game props that players can interact with, trade, or steal. You can drop a prop into a scene with a sentence: “Someone slides you a velvet envelope bearing the symbol on the ring.”

### Session hooks and micro-scenarios
- Hook 1: The Velvet Signet. A surface-level luxury shop in Fortune Row is rumored to be a front for a mythic courier service. The signet ring someone wears in the portraits is actually a passkey to a hidden basement. The GM can orchestrate a chase through multiple floors, each revealing a different facet of the city’s mythic economy.
- Hook 2: The Neon Confessional. A balcony nightclub hosts a ritual that’s filmed for social media—only this “live stream” is real and emits actual magical energy. The players must navigate a crowd that believes the ritual is a performance and a real danger at the same time.
- Hook 3: The Mirror of Truth. A shopkeeper offers a mirror that shows the truth behind a person’s mask—but viewing it comes with a price. The players must decide how much truth they’re willing to bear and whether the truth is a weapon or a shield.

### Player-facing prompts
If you’re running a session with player-character creation or cross-pollination between a party member’s backstory and Fortune Row, the art book can offer quick prompts:
- “Describe your mask. Is it a symbol of power, a reminder of a debt, or a disguise you wear to hide the fear in your eyes?”
- “A piece of art shows a character who looks suspiciously like your own masked alter ego. What does that say about your path?”
- “You notice a recurring motif in the art—what in your life carries that same motif, and what would you do to claim it back or protect it?”

## NPCs and Factions: a Quick Reference
The Fortune Row reference guide can be used to populate a city block with purpose. Here are three archetypes that often appear in the book’s frames:
- The Velvet Executor: A power broker who dresses in opulence and speaks with velvet doors. They’re the sort who make deals with a wink and a blade behind the smile. They want to protect the district’s mythic economy and will purse-string any plan that threatens that balance.
- The Neon Archivist: A data-obsessed insider who records every rumor and every mythic event in a sprawling ledger. They’re helpful when you need information, perilous when you cross them, and fascinating when they’re the source of a clue that only reveals itself through a combination of art, ambiance, and deduction.
- The Glass Dancer: An enigmatic performer who blurs the line between spectacle and ritual. They wield attention as a weapon, and their art hides a deeper truth about Fortune Row’s undercurrents. Expect performances that reveal secrets and crowds that can be read as a living map of who controls which corners of the district.

Each of these NPCs is accompanied by a few visual cues and a short, GM-ready write-up. The artwork supports quick, on-the-fly decision-making, so you’re not spending half your session flipping through pages while your players flip the table in frustration (an unfortunate but occasionally necessary form of engagement).

## Comparisons: How It Fits with Other City of Mist Material
### With the Core Rulebook and other supplements
The Fortune Row Art Book serves as a companion rather than a replacement. If the core rulebook gives you the system, this art book gives you the soul of the setting. It complements mechanics with mood, tone, and texture. Where the core book explains how masks operate and what the consequences of a mythic event look like in rules terms, Fortune Row shows you how it feels to live among those myths in a specific neighborhood—the sounds, the smells, the glimmers on a rain-slick street. In practice, you’ll use the art book to craft a scene quickly and to sell your players on a moment that’s as cinematic as it is dangerous.

### Against other City of Mist art or reference guides
Fortune Row’s hallmark is its concentrated sense of place. Other art books in the City of Mist line offer broad palettes and generic vibes; Fortune Row narrows the field to a district with its own power dynamics and aesthetic. If you’re chasing a sense of “this is where legends shop and where legends hide,” Fortune Row is the destination. It’s not omnipotent in terms of gameplay value, but it excels at giving you something irreplaceable: a vivid, shareable setting that makes your table feel like it’s stepping into a club where the velvet ropes are also a plot device.
