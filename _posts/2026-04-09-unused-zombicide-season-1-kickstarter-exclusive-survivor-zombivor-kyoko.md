Today we dive into the unused Zombicide Season 1 Kickstarter exclusive survivor and its Zombivor counterpart, Kyoko. Kyoko arrives as both a survivor card and a Zombivor miniature. The survivor Kyoko is drawn with a balletic stance, katana like blade slung across her shoulder, and a look that says do not nap in a zombie apocalypse, unless you want to nap while defending a tile. The Zombivor Kyoko is the opposite: a zombified version with a penchant for snarling in a terrifying hoodie, a mohawk optional on the warground. This dual existence captures thematically what Zombicide does best: mix bright, cheerful miniatures with horrific consequences, and give you a reason to cosplay at the kitchen table.

## Background: Kyoko and the Seasonal Kickstarter Exclusive
Kyoko was originally released as a Season 1 Kickstarter exclusive, a time when backers were promised new characters and sculpts that would later become fan favorites or cautionary tales about stretch goals. The kit included two components: the Survivor Kyoko miniature, presented in a dynamic, wind up katana pose, and the Zombivor Kyoko miniature, a zombified counterpart that could roam the board as an undead antagonist or as a villain clone in creative scenarios. The community often jokes about the exclusivity as a badge of honor, a little like owning a limited edition gamer hoodie that smells faintly of cardboard and nostalgia. The alignment of survival and zombivation makes Kyoko a quintessential example of early Kickstarter era product design: bold sculpts, ambitious concepts, and a willingness to ship prototypes that feel finished, unfinished, and fabulous all at once.

## The Sculpt and the Pledge Drama
The Kyoko miniatures reflect the design language of late 2000s to early 2010s WIP aesthetics, with crisp lines and a tilt of anime inspired flair. The Survivor Kyoko features tight leather, a wind swept cape or scarf, and a blade that looks as if it could slice a thunderstorm. The Zombivor Kyoko adopts a more decayed, tainted look: torn fabric, exposed bone accents, and a posture that says wake me not, or else. As sealed Kickstarter exclusives, they survived the long arc of unboxing videos you never watched until you acquired them on the sly. The quality is typical for Zombicide: sturdy plastic that can survive a shelf life longer than some marriages, and a paint job that begs for a primer and color theory that makes your shelf look like a mini gallery.

## Card Mechanics and Play Style
The Survivor Kyoko comes with a card that channels the character into the core Zombicide ruleset: turn based actions, dice based combat, and a few luck based moments that feel equal parts thrilling and terrifying. The exact numbers may vary across print runs and the curious case of the early pre release kits, but the spirit remains: Kyoko thrives on mobility, precision, and a bit of risk. In practical terms, the card would likely grant a speed boost in early expansion rooms, a bonus to hit certain types of zombie, or a special action that can bypass one door with a quick sash of the blade. In the world of Zombicide, that could translate to a two or three step plan for clearing a tile while keeping a survivor in the game.

The Zombivor Kyoko, meanwhile, introduces a twist: the undead version has different movement, maybe weaker general stats but a unique trait like Feint or Frenzy that triggers when the team gets cornered. In some fan favorite homebrew games, Zombivors add a new layer of tension, where the party must decide if the undead Kyoko is a threat to them or an accidental ally, depending on who is controlling her. The dynamic is what keeps Zombicide players coming back to their coffee table with a smile and a hint of existential dread.

## Unused, Yet Revered
There is a special kind of joy in owning unused promo items. They exist as a microcosm of a hobby that can take up more shelf space than the actual game in play. The Kyoko survivor and Zombivor are examples of that era when marketing hype collided with gaming culture and produced something that looked too good to open. The packaging often included extra art, a glossy card layout, and a glossy stomach churning color palette that modern reissues might eschew in favor of leaner, more practical designs. For collectors, the value is twofold: the nostalgia factor and the potential rarity. If the kit never shipped widely, that scarcity is a vector of value that can drive price on secondary markets, especially among long term Zombicide fans who remember the old campaigns and the golden era of backers who believed the stretch goals would be more than a rumor.

## Value, Display and Collectability
The economics of unused Kickstarter exclusives are a curiosity. If Kyoko was never taken to the table in a community game night, is she still a playable piece? The logical answer in collector land is yes, with a smile, because the item remains in pristine condition, its plastic still smelling faintly of the factory and the risk that the piece could degrade if it is over-handled. Display wise, Kyoko makes a striking centerpiece: the Survivor on a shelf with a blade at the ready and the Zombivor perched in a glass domed case, or perhaps both on a corkboard like a display narrative of the first zombie apocalypse ever staged on a coffee table. The price, of course, swings with the general supply and demand for early Zombicide items, as well as the overall health of retro board game collecting. If you have a sealed kit, the value might increase as more time passes and interest in retro Kickstarter exclusives remains high.

## Practicality: Can Kyoko Hold Her Own in a Modern Game?
If you bring Kyoko to a modern Zombicide session, the question arises: will she be playable and relevant? The answer depends on your house rules and how much you love nostalgia. In official rules, older ex promo survivors are sometimes adjusted to fit new expansion scales. The Kyoko pair could be used as a fun one off character in a Season 1 Reimagined scenario or in a Promos Only mode that your group creates for a special event. If your group is performance driven, you may want to house rule her to have a fixed unique ability, something that captures her ninja vibe without overshadowing the more modern survivors. My advice is to treat Kyoko as a commemorative figure rather than a tournament ready asset; use her as a story beat and a challenge for players, rather than a raw power boost.

## Packaging, Quality and Handling
If your copy of Kyoko is still sealed, consider it a time capsule. The packaging would typically feature the Season 1 promotional art, a sticker with backer numbers, and maybe a note from the designer praising the early ambitions of the game. Handling such a piece should be done carefully: keep it in a climate controlled area away from direct sunlight to preserve the paint, if any, and to prevent the miniature from warping. If the kit has ever been opened but is in pristine condition, ensure you keep the contents intact and avoid heavy bending of the cards to preserve readability. In short, treat Kyoko with the reverence a small star deserves.

## Display and Hobby Suggestions
For display, you can craft a tiny diorama: Kyoko striking a heroic pose while the Zombivor lurks in the background, perhaps raiding a minifigure kitchen while a cardboard barricade stands in for a ruined storefront. It is a silly, charming setup, but it captures the essence of what a season 1 exclusive can do for a shelf. Hobby wise, you could prime and paint the Kyoko miniatures to more closely resemble vintage promo art, or you could embrace a Season 1 retro palette with mohicans and neon blades. If you want to get fancy, you could create a small display stand with a tiny plaque describing Kyoko origin, the intro text from the kickstarter page, and a short note about how the item ended up in your collection.
