## How to play (a quick primer for 2 to 6 players)
The core mechanic is simple: you aim to empty your stockpile by playing cards in ascending order from 1 to 12. In between, you’ll rely on Skip-Bo wild cards to fill gaps or to leapfrog trouble spots. The LB2S1 edition keeps this intact but offers a slightly clearer turn order than some of the older variants.

### Basic rules
- Each player starts with a stockpile of cards facedown; only the top card is visible.
- On your turn, you draw to a hand of five cards (or whatever the draw count says in the rules you’re using).
- You may play cards from your hand to form piles in ascending order on building piles. You can use Skip-Bo wilds to stand in for any number.
- When you can’t play, you discard; the next player goes.
- The first player to empty their stockpile wins the round; play continues for a set number of rounds if you’re doing a tournament-style night.

### Setup and turn flow
Setup is quick: deal out stockpile cards to each player, set up the central draw pile, and place your building piles within easy reach. On your turn, you have two main options: play from your hand to the building piles or discard strategically to set up your next turn. The wildcards offer flexibility, which is both a blessing and a curse—great for momentum, slightly scary for the corner-case rule-lawyers in your group.

### Winning conditions
In a standard session, the first person to run through their stockpile claims the bragging rights for that round. If you’re playing multiple rounds, you’ll keep score with tokens. The LB2S1 kit includes a simple scoring sheet to track who won how many rounds, which is perfect for those of us who enjoy pretending to be serious about a game that, in practice, becomes a laugh-filled report card for your friends’ decision-making skills.

## Playtesting: 2, 3, and 6 players in real life chaos
Skip-Bo thrives on social dynamics. The LB2S1 edition supports 2 to 6 players, which means you can invite your favorite gamer, your least favorite cousin, and that neighbor who always wants to borrow your charger. The variety of player counts changes the tempo in delightful ways.

### 2-player dynamics
When the table is two, the pace becomes almost suspiciously smooth, like a pair of comedians finishing each other’s sentences. You get a clear sense of who’s manipulating the pace and who’s bluffing about having a “definitive strategy.” In practice, two players tend to rotate through the same few gambits: one player aggressively cycles through discards to speed up the draw, while the other hunkers down, builds defensively, and prays the wild cards show mercy. The result is a tense but ultimately friendly duel where the winner gets a smug glow and the loser gets a new appreciation for dessert diplomacy.

### 3-player dynamics
With three players, the table gains a nice improvisational vibe. People block, steal, and occasionally attempt the ancient art of “accidental tailing,” where a misplay by one person becomes a teachable moment for the other two. The central draw pile becomes a shared resource, which means you’ll hear your friends mutter phrases like, “That’s mine now, you sunshine in a cardigan!” It’s chaotic, but it’s the brand of chaos that makes family game nights legendary.

### 6-player dynamics
The six-person table is where Skip-Bo becomes a social engine. It’s loud, it’s messy, and it’s glorious. With so many players, you’ll experience the classic phenomenon of simultaneous conversations about strategy and which snack is the best while some player is quietly sprinting to finish the stockpile. The LB2S1 edition handles this scale with a robust draw pile and a generous headcount for building sequences. The trade-offs: longer game sessions and more room for flimsy hand tremors as someone flushes a wild card to a perfect 12-stack and dramatically slams the table in celebration. It’s cinematic in the best possible sense.
