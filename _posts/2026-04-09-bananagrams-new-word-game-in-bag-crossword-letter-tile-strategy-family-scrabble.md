## Setup and rules: a quick-start guide

H3: Getting things ready
- Open the bag, scatter the tiles in the center, and shuffle them with a flourish that would make a magician blush.
- Each player grabs a certain number of tiles. In many family rounds, seven tiles per player is a comfortable starting hand; for speed rounds, you can go with five.
- Place the clue cards facedown or in a central pile, depending on whether you want a purely improvisational experience or a guided crossword-lite mode.

H3: The basic turn (same as Bananagrams at heart, with a crossword twist)
1. You have a timer running. When it ends, you must place all your tiles into a connected grid that includes at least one cross with another word.
2. You may rearrange tiles on your rack as you please between adds/removals; you can also exchange a few tiles if you feel your rack is going off the rails.
3. When you’ve used all your tiles and formed a valid grid, declare victory with enthusiasm and a shout that sounds suspiciously like banana-related victory music.

H3: The crossword twist (optional but recommended for extra brain spice)
- Draw a clue card from the deck. It will instruct you to place a word that intersects your current network in a particular way, e.g., “create a 3-letter vertical word intersecting the middle of a horizontal word.”
- You can opt to complete the prompt or skip it if you’re playing a freeform game. Completing the prompt adds a small bonus point or a strategic advantage, depending on your house rules.

H3: Endgame and scoring
- The player who uses all tiles first wins that round. If you want a stricter competition, assign points to each player based on tiles left in their racks. The fewer tiles, the higher your score, with bonus points for satisfying crossword prompts.
- In some family versions, you can tally a running score across several rounds, turning a casual game into a mini tournament night. It’s surprising how quickly kids and adults can escalate from zero to hero with a few rounds under their belts.

## Strategy and tips: because you’re not “that guy” at game night

### Tile management and the vowel conundrum
- Keep a balanced rack: aim for a mix of vowels and consonants so you’re not stuck with a rack of all vowels when everyone else is spinning out consonant-laden crosswords. If you’re staring down a vowel drought, don’t be shy about swapping a tile or two if your group allows it.
- Watch for common bigrams and trigrams: TH, HE, ER, AN, IN, ON, etc. They pop up in crosswords more often than not, and having a few ready-made building blocks can accelerate your grid formation.

### Crosswords on the fly: connecting the dots
- The real thrill is discovering how a word you place can unlock others. Try to place words that share a letter with multiple other words. It creates lanes of expansion and gives you options later in the round.
- Don’t fear short words; sometimes a fresh cross is built by a two-letter bridge like it or to get you across the middle of a cluster of longer words. Two- and three-letter words become your best tools late in the round.

### Timing and tempo: keep the pace lively
- Use the timer to your advantage. If you’re in a block, a short time pressure often forces a clever, out-of-left-field solution. If you’re ahead, you can slow down and savor a clean, elegant grid that shows you’ve got both vocabulary and spatial awareness.
- In family settings, a good rule of thumb is to allow kids to take the lead on fresh words but to encourage adults to help with cross-connections. It’s a teamwork vibe disguised as competition, which is exactly the right energy for family nights.

### Word quality vs speed: a healthy tension
- Don’t chase high-point words at the expense of grid coherence. The most beautiful moments in this game arrive when a long chain of short words collapses into a legitimate big-picture crossword. Your future-you will thank present-you for the clean, elegant grid you didn’t know you could achieve.
- If you’re stuck, lean into the cross prompts. They’re designed to nudge you toward rearrangements that you’ll be grateful for when the buzzer goes off.
