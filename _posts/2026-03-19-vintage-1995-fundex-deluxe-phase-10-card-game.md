## How to play: quick primer on rules, with a dash of nuance

Phase 10 is simple to learn but has enough subtlety to keep a seasoned group engaged. The goal is to complete ten distinct phases in order, with each phase describing a specific combination of cards you must lay down to end a round. A typical round starts with players drawing a card and discarding one until someone completes a phase. That player then moves on to the next phase while others try to catch up. The scoring works like this: points are tallied for cards left in hand when a round ends, with lower totals being better. The race to finish all ten phases while keeping a low tally creates a push-pull dynamic—blessings and curses with every draw card you hope will help you finish your current phase just as you see your rival slipping a little behind.

Key strategic levers include managing the risk of holding onto cards that would complete a phase in the long run, assessing what other players might be chasing, and deciding when to go for a high-risk, high-reward move versus a steady improvement of your own hand. The illness of the phase system is that it introduces a series of forced recalibrations. When you finally complete a phase, your attention shifts to the next one, which can require a different set of cards altogether. The difficulty isn’t in the rules themselves but in keeping a plan intact while the random draws remix your intentions. The Deluxe version plays that tension well with a satisfying space to slide into as you adjust your strategy with each cycle around the table.

### The ten phases in context
Phase 1 tasks you with two sets of three cards. Phase 2 adds a little run of four. Phase 3 might require a run and a set in combination, and so on. The final phase tends to test you with the most complex combination, testing whether you have learned to carve a path through the chaos. The exact sequences vary by edition, and while the 1995 Deluxe adheres to the core concept, the joy is in how your group negotiates the specifics. The beauty of the Phase 10 format is that no two games feel exactly alike, and a sealed edition from 1995 offers a sense of anticipation that a modern reprint does not always capture in quite the same way.
