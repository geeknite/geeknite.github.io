Gloom is not a game. Gloom is fate with a candle lamp and a deck of misfortune cards. In this review, we dive into the melancholy pocket of your tabletop library and unearth a game that makes your family quarrel over who killed Aunt Marigold and whether she was killed with a sarcastic sigh or a tragic sneeze. Brace yourself for a whirling carnival of gloom that somehow remains delightfully addictive.

## Introduction

If you are new to the concept, Gloom is a card game about making your miserable family members as unhappy as possible while keeping your own characters alive long enough to witness the heartbreak around the table. The goal is to have the gloomiest family with the lowest total prosperity when all characters expire in a melodramatic fashion. Spoiler: the happiest moment in any round is when your rival's character expires and you cheer, not with joy, but with a perverse sense of professional satisfaction.

### A quick elevator pitch

Gloom plays like a Victorian melodrama that somehow got into your living room and demanded a tepid cup of tea. Each player controls a family of eccentric, doomed souls, playing path of misfortune and misadventure cards to worsen others chances while trying to keep their own lineage from dying in unspeakable ways. The humor is dark, not because the game tries to be edgy, but because fate loves a dramatic punchline.

### Why Gloom stands out

The standout feature is not the doom, but the storytelling mechanic. Every card tells a mini-story with a cartoonish artwork that looks like a Victorian comic and reads like a tabloid of the afterlife. The art is playful but macabre, which means you can show it to kids and keep a straight face by muttering, its just a game, son, daughter, and then praying that the dice behave. This is not your average beige Euro game; it is a carnival of mood swings with cute characters wearing unfortunate expressions.

## What is Gloom?

### Components

The base game includes a handful of illustrated translucent cards, a small deck of transparent gloom tokens, and a rulebook that reads like a cross between a trading manual and a gothic novel. The quality is solid; cards are sturdy and the transparency of the gloom tokens adds a satisfying layer of depth to the visuals. The set is compact; you can shove it into a messenger bag and pretend you are a radical courier of misfortune on your commute.

### Setup and turn structure

Set up is brisk. Each player selects a family line and gets a deck that has their characters, a few positive cards to keep their hopes aloft, and a handful of negative events to unleash on others. The trick here is that you reveal gloom characters gradually using the transparency mechanic: you place your gloom cards on your family to cloak their hopeless fate from your opponents while maximizing the dramatic reveal at the right moment. The turn structure is straightforward: draw a card, play a card, pass to the next player. Simple enough to teach a first-time gamer while still leaving enough room for clever misdirection and cunning timing. The tempo of chaos accelerates as the table collects more gloom like a discount inventory.

### The gloom mechanic in practice

The gloom mechanic is the heart of the game. It is the moment when you realize that each character has a miserable outcome attached to their life, and your job is to arrange these outcomes to culminate in the most spectacular tragedy for your competitors. The actual scoring happens at the end, when the total prosperity of all players is calculated; the player with the lowest prosperity wins the game of gloom, which is not a friendly trophy but a moral victory with a dash of sarcasm.

## Theme and tone

Gloom does not pretend to be cheerful. It revels in the irony of human folly and the melodrama of a family that cannot survive without someone telling them how doomed they are. The art nails the Victorian goth vibe with pastel colors that somehow soften the doom. The tone is comedic, with a wink at the absurdity of family dynamics and the universal fear of being the one left holding the candle when the plot twists in the worst possible way. The humor can be dark, but it is never cruel; the game invites you to laugh at your own misfortune while you roast your relatives in playful banter.

## Gameplay Experience

### A stroll through tragedy, but with cards

In practice, Gloom is a balancing act. You want to invest in your family, but you also want to sabotage your opponents without breaking the game or crossing the line into actual family altercations. The translucent nature of gloom cards creates an interesting puzzle: you know what you are hiding, but only you know exactly how much gloom remains to be unleashed. This fosters a mental game about information management, risk, and timing. You will find yourself debating whether to reveal a positive event that benefits you and then use a negative event to ruin an opponent's day later on. It is a carefully choreographed carnival of doom that plays out across the dining room table.

### Solo play and group dynamics

Gloom shines in a group setting. The social dynamic is the real engine: teasing, accusations, and competitive banter fuel the experience. If you are playing solo, the joy of doom loses some of its spark; the game is designed to be enjoyed with friends or family who know when to swap a grim look for a grin. It is not that you cannot play solo, but the charm rests on the banter and the shared misfortune of a living room audience.

### Turning gloom into laughter

The humor in Gloom is a gift for the right table. It thrives when players lean into the melodrama and embrace the absurd. There will be moments where a card reveals that your aunt’s ghost died of an unfortunate sneeze and your rival laments the cruelty of the fates while you celebrate the perfect chain of events that left their family in tears. If your group can embrace the tonal balance, you will laugh with, and not at, the gloomy fate of the families involved.

### Rule tweaks and house rules

If you like to tweak, Gloom is friendly to house rules. A few players swear by a lighter gloom load to keep games moving on busy nights, while others love a longer session full of dramatic reveals. The rulebook itself is not exceptionally long, and it encourages flexibility when it comes to card interactions. A few minor changes can dramatically alter the pacing, and that is part of the charm. Remember that this game is a story first and a competition second; the best sessions feel like a short play with a messy, endearing cast of characters who ironically cannot escape their tragedies.

## Artwork and components quality

The artwork remains charming and unique. The translucent cards create a spectral effect when stacked, which looks extremely cool on a coffee table or a lazy susan. The quality of the cardstock is good, and the printing is crisp enough that you can read the tiny text on the gloom cards without requiring a magnifying glass. The chips and tokens are tactile and satisfying, and the whole experience feels premium without tipping into luxury price territory. If you are a visual person, the aesthetic carries the entire mood forward and makes the gloom feel real enough to justify the theatrics at your table.

## How to win friends and influence people with gloom

While the mechanics determine the winner, the real skill is social engineering. You will learn to read the table: who tends to overreact to a bad omen, who quietly builds an evil plan behind a smile, and who regards gloom with the seriousness of a murder mystery. The best players can orchestrate a sequence of doom cards so that the last act features an epic reveal; the worst players will attempt to cheat, then realize the rules are flexible enough to reward creative mischief and witty banter. In short: if you like strategy with a heavy dose of storytelling, Gloom scratches that itch with flair.

## Final thoughts and recommendation

Gloom is a rare breed of game that blends humor, tragedy, and strategy into one compact package. It rewards players who enjoy storytelling and social interaction as much as they enjoy tactical card play. It scales well with 3 to 5 players, and with the right mix of players, it becomes a mini stage show where the characters die in delightfully ridiculous ways. It is not a heavy sim, and it is not a chaotic party game that collapses under the weight of too many cards; it sits in a sweet, quirky middle ground that makes it a frequently revisited favorite on Geeknite.

Who should buy this:
- Players who love storytelling and dark humor with a hint of strategy
- Families looking for a game that brings out playful banter rather than arguments
- Casual gamers who enjoy quick setup with a high replay value

Who might skip this one:
- Players who require a strictly mathematical victory condition
- People who are deeply averse to sarcasm or humor about death, death-related puns, and the occasional cartoon skeleton

In short: Gloom is a delightfully morbid little gem that brings a smile to the table even as it drips with dramatic misfortune. If you want a game that sparks conversation and a little friendly misanthropy without crossing into cruelty, this is your darkly charming companion. 

## Design notes and playstyle tips

- Lean into the theater: a little drama goes a long way. The cards are designed to be read aloud with a flourish; embrace the melodrama and your opponents will follow.
- Manage information like a pro: concealment is half the fun. Use gloom cards to create awkward reveals that surprise others without tipping your own hand too soon.
- Pace matters: don’t burn through doom too quickly. The best sessions pace the reveals so that the last few rounds feel like the grand finale of a soap opera.
- Family dynamics matter: not every family is comfortable with a heavy dose of doom, so tailor the session to your group’s sense of humor. If a table crosses the line into uncomfortable, ease back on the theatrical gloom and focus on playful misfortune.

## Playthrough snapshot: a mini scenario

The table settles into a 4-player round. Player A lays down a positive event for their character, a little optimistic ghost who loves sunflowers. Player B counters with a doom card that makes Player A mental note of a family curse tied to their sunflowers. Player C slyly deploys a sympathy card that makes another player feel temporarily benevolent, only to trap them with a future gloom reveal. Then the first dramatic reveal occurs: Player D’s character dies not from a mortal accident but from the sheer embarrassment of mispronouncing a family ancestral name. Laughter erupts, the table revises their impressions of doom, and the score shifts in a way that could only happen in Gloom. By the end of the round, someone has a faint glimmer of hope, which is immediately snuffed out by a final doom card that perfectly lines up the last two turns. The sense of storytelling is electric; the game feels less like a set of rules and more like an improv session dressed as a tabletop card duel.

## Accessibility and table setup tips

If you want to keep things friendly for newer players, consider a quick one or two practice rounds before diving into full doom. Provide a one-page cheat sheet that summarizes the gloom tokens, the general flow, and the basic objective. The translucent cards can be a little fiddly, so a tray or shallow box to manage the gloom tokens is a nice touch. The game scales to a variety of group sizes, but you will usually get the best dramatic effect with three to five players. If your group tends to be quiet, encourage each person to narrate their family line with a dramatic flair to unlock the gag potential.
