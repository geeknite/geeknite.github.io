## Painting Plan: Monochrome Snow That Still Feels Alive
The challenge with a monochrome palette is not to flatten the model into a cardboard silhouette; the trick is to layer values so the figure reads as three-dimensional even without color. Snow adds an extra layer of narrative—your zombie is not experiencing winter; winter is experiencing the zombie. Here is the plan I used, which you can borrow or customize for your own models:

### Step 1: Prep and Prime
- Clean the miniature, removing any mold lines and flash that could muddy the grayscale. A good rinse in water, a gentle scrub, and a quick dry is standard practice.
- Prime with a neutral gray spray. The goal is a base that will reflect mid-tone values across the model, not a stark black or white that will skew your grayscale later.
- If you want extra bargain for the snow motif, you can prime with a cool gray and then mist a light blue over the upper surfaces to put a cool undertone in the highlights.

### Step 2: Establish the Midtones
- Base coat the main skin areas with a mid-gray that sits between the light and dark end of your grayscale. Avoid pure black or pure white here; you want something that reads as flesh with a hint of undead vitality.
- For the clothes, keep a slightly darker gray to push depth in folds and creases. This is where your snow contrast will later pop in a readable way.
- The M203 should have a metallic tone base to give it realism, so you may reserve a metallic base for that piece later in the process.

### Step 3: Build the Snow Layer
- The snow is not simply white. It carries cool blues and very light grays to sell the cold, glistening realism. Start with a thin white glaze and then gradually add a touch of ultramarine blue or a cool-light-blue wash along the upper edges and exposed surfaces.
- Dry brushing is your friend here. A light gray over the raised edges of clothing, gear, and the base helps the sculpt breathe. Then a final touch of pure white on the most raised highlights will catch light like fresh frost in the moonlight.
- Snow on the base should look natural, not like a frosting dump. A stippled mix of white with a dash of light gray on uneven terrain creates a believable road of winter fluff.

### Step 4: Bring the M203 to Life
- The M203 has mechanical lines that can read well in metallic or gunmetal. Choose a base metal tone, then pick out rivets and seams with a slightly lighter silver. A wash of sepia or brown over metal areas can simulate oil and age, which is essential for a zombie who has probably scavenged a few times and learned to use tools that were never designed for him.
- The muzzle and the stock can have a tiny highlight to suggest rounded metal catching light. Do not over-highlight; the goal is a believable weapon rather than chrome chaos.

### Step 5: Final Edge Highlights and Contrast Balancing
- A cool gray edge highlight on the armor plates, clothing seams, and the gun helps sell the grayscale look. The goal is to make the model pop against its snowy sheet without introducing color. Use a slightly brighter gray for the most prominent edges, leaving recessed areas in a deeper shade.
- Revisit the snow areas and boost the contrast where you want the viewer’s eye to go first. The brain tends to notice light, so the snow on the face, cuffs, and shoulders should have a bit more brightness than the rest of the model.

### Step 6: Base and Stationary Drama
- The base is where your story finally breathes. A ruined street corner with cracked concrete, a toppled sign, and snow drifts at knee height can tell a tale about what happened here. A few tufts of static grass, tiny rubble pieces, and a light wash on the rocks complete the scene.
- Consider a tiny spotlight effect by adding a drop of white onto the snow on the uppermost surfaces. It gives drama and makes the monochrome palette read like a film still rather than a toy.

> Pro tip: Keep a small palette of four grays (mid, lighter mid, dark, and near-black) and a cool blue for the snow. With monochrome, consistency is king. If you drift into a warm gray mid-tone, you’ll quickly lose the cold snow vibe you want to preserve.
