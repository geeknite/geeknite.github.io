## How to Play: The Basics with a Hogwarts Twist
If you know how to play Codenames, you basically know how to play this edition with two spherical caveats: you’ll be using HP-flavored words and you’ll be styling your clues to conjure connections within the wizarding world rather than generic associations. The core rules remain the same: two teams (red and blue), a Spymaster for each team, a grid of 25 word cards, and the goal to find all of your agents before the other team does. The Spymaster gives a one-word clue and a number indicating how many words on the board are tied to that clue. The teammates then guess those words. If they reveal the assassin word, the turn ends and the other team takes the stage. If they reveal neutral words, the turn ends but the words stay on the board for future turns.

In the HP Edition, the flavor is everywhere but the mechanics stay lean and accessible. You’ll be encouraged to use themes like spells, Hogwarts houses, magical creatures, famous artifacts, and well-known plot beats. The risk here is delightful: a clue that feels crystal clear to you might be interpreted in a totally different Potter phrase by a teammate who hasn’t memorized every spell from your fan fiction shelf. That friction is what makes the game shine. It invites witty misinterpretations, quick pivots, and the kind of collaborative giggles that only occur when someone shouts a perfectly chosen clue while others simultaneously realize they’re hilariously wrong—like a trio of students who realize they’ve been pointing to the same three words but for wildly different reasons.

A few practical tips for playing the HP Edition well:
- Embrace the themes, but keep it simple. One-word clues with a strong thematic twist work best, especially early in the game when the board is still a mystery map.
- Avoid obvious misfires. If you sense a potential assassin word on the board, don’t risk a clue that could cross into that danger zone just because you want to feel clever.
- Use multi-word associations sparingly. A well-chosen two- or three-word clue might feel magical, but it also invites overreach if your teammates aren’t on the same wavelength.
- Read the room. The tone of your group matters more than the exact words you choose. Some groups love long, extravagant clues; others prefer crisp, punchy associations. Adapt on the fly.

### The Card Layout and House Prefixes
The HP Edition’s word cards act as a curated stage for wizarding references. The Spymaster’s job is to lead the pack toward safe harbor while avoiding a few treacherous phrases that resemble the assassin word. There is a particular joy in seeing a word that sounds mundane in isolation suddenly become the keystone of a perfect Potter clue—like a mundane object becoming a magical instrument with the right wink and a lightning bolt of context.

### Turn Structure
A standard turn follows one Spymaster’s clue for their team, then a round of guesses by that team. If you guess correctly, you continue until you miss or hit the number limit specified by the clue. If you guess too many, you can unlock more of your team’s words, but you risk tipping off the other team to how you’re thinking. The assassin word is always the dreadful moment you never want to encounter unless you’re playing as the villain in a fan-made parody of a duel.

The HP Edition also introduces optional house flavoring: if your group is obsessed with a particular house, you can implement a house-based twist to one or two turns per game. For instance, if you pick Gryffindor as your house, you might assign a slight bonus to bold clues—ones that connect more words at once. If you’re a Slytherin faction, you may prefer cunning clues with higher risk but bigger payoff. It’s not official tournament rules, but it’s a delightful way to tailor the night to your audience.

## Strategy and Clues: How to Tilt the Odds in Your Favor
If you want to win with grace and minimal chaos, here are some practical strategies built from countless nights of analog wizardry:

- Build connections that are themselves plausible. The best clues feel inevitable once you hear them in your team’s conversation, but not obvious to an outside observer.
- Favor breadth over depth at the start. A clue that links three or four words is powerful, even if you have to sacrifice a tiny piece of safety.
- Use thematic bridges that work for your group. If your teammates love specific HP elements—like Horcruxes, Patronuses, or magical schools—lean into those once you know the board better.
- Save your big, risky plays for later rounds. It’s often better to secure a steady stream of safe words early on, then go for a dramatic swing when pressure is high.
- Beware of the assassin word. In HP terms, that’s the kind of word that can derail a round and draw groans from both teams. The sooner you learn to spot it, the better your odds become.

## Variants, House Cup Mode, and Longevity
One of the less talked-about joys of Codenames is how easy it is to add your own twists. The HP Edition is particularly friendly to light variant rules:

- House Cup Mode: each house gets a small advantage on a round or two. Gryffindor might grant a bonus for bold multi-word clues; Slytherin could earn more points for successful gambits with higher risk. Hufflepuff can offer a safety net in the form of a few neutral words that don’t criminally derail a turn. Ravenclaw users can grant quicker rounds with reduced penalties for incorrect guesses.
- Time-Boxed Duels: use a magical timer to create a crescendo moment in the last minutes, perfect for a party with a looming deadline for pizza delivery.
- House forfeit option: if a team is behind, they can choose to exchange one of their own words for a chance at a big comeback, adding a theatrical twist that HP fans live for.
- Themed Tournaments: host a mini Cup with teams representing different Hogwarts houses and perhaps even Hogsmeade vendors who supply fun house-motivated props and house-badges as awards.
