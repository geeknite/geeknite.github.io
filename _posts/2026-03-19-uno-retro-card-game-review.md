## How to play: quick-start guide

If you already know UNO, you know the rhythm. If you are new to UNO, you have a gentle education in how messy a card game night can be when people care about being the one to finish first. UNO Retro sticks to the familiar flow while celebrating the chaos with a touch of retro-cool flair. Here is a quick-start guide to get you up and running in under five minutes:

1) Setup: The dealer shuffles and deals seven cards face-down to each player. The rest goes into a draw pile. The top card is flipped to start the discard pile. If the first card is a wild card, you follow the deck’s instructions or reshuffle if you want to keep things simple for the opener.
2) Turn sequence: Players take turns in clockwise order. On your turn, you must play a card that matches the color or the number of the top card on the discard pile, or you can play a Wild card to change color. If you don’t have a playable card, you draw one. If the drawn card is playable, you may put it down immediately (in most groups, this is allowed but not mandatory).
3) Action cards: Skip keeps the next player from taking a turn. Reverse changes the direction of play. Draw Two forces the next player to draw two cards and skip their turn. Wild and Wild Draw Four let you choose the color and force the next player to draw cards (with the usual caveats about Wild Draw Four usage). UNO Retro doesn’t dramatically alter these basics, but the packaging’s vibe makes each action feel a bit grander, especially when someone saves the day with a well-timed Wild.
4) UNO and going out: When you have one card left, you must shout UNO. If you fail to shout UNO and another player notices, you draw two cards as a penalty. The aim is to be the first to discard all your cards.
5) Ending the round: The round ends when a player runs out of cards. Points are tallied, usually using the values posted on the cards, and the next round begins with a new dealer. Some groups prefer to play to a set score; others like to play a single, glorious duel that ends with someone triumphantly slamming the table and declaring it a night.

## Strategies and tips for UNO Retro players

As with any UNO edition, victory favors prep work and a little psychological chess. Here are some practical, non-snarky tips to help you maximize your odds while preserving the lighthearted chaos:

- Card counting intuition: even though you do not need to count every card, having a rough sense of what colors are likely to appear next helps you plan. If the table has burned through red cards, you may want to push into blue or green rather than forcing a safety draw.
- Hand management: keep a mix of colors in your hand whenever possible. If you can avoid being forced into a color with too few cards, you’ll reduce the chance of handing a powerhouse Wild to the player who has a rainbow hand but a single super-valuable color card.
- Use the Wilds strategically: Wild cards are powerful because they let you change color. Save them for moments when a color on the table is about to force you into a long, painful color run. Don’t waste a Wild on a color you are about to own; reserve it for a moment when you need to jam the brakes on someone with a multi-card engine.
- Reading your table: UNO is partly about psychology. Observe who claims to hate color-based games and who tries to memorize the exact card in everyone else’s hand. Use that information to plan your next move, or at least to troll with a tasteful unicorn sticker on your last card.
- The UNO Uno Rule: remember that shouting UNO is a social ritual as much as a mechanical requirement. A good-natured reminder helps avoid the penalty stacks and keeps the table friendly.
