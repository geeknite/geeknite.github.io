# Infinite - Beyond The Mind: Limited Tanya Edition on Nintendo Switch — Brand New, Probably Not Haunted by Your CRT

Welcome, fellow pixel-pilots and sanity-synthesizers. Today we dive into a party-sized brain sauna: Infinite - Beyond The Mind, but with a twist that would make a neon-lit Rubik’s cube wobble in its seat. The Limited Tanya Edition for the Nintendo Switch is here in all its cardboard-and-plastic glory, screaming softly into the void that you absolutely do not need a PhD in philosophy to enjoy it, but you might want one anyway to explain the ending to your cat.

![Infinite - Beyond The Mind: Tanya Edition art]( /assets/images/infinite-beyond-the-mind-tanya-edition.jpg )

If you’ve been geeking out about mind-bending platformers since you wore socks with holes in the toes and still called it fashion, you’ve probably seen variants of this concept before. But Infinite - Beyond The Mind aims to do something different: take the very act of thinking and throw a kaleidoscope at it. The Tanya Edition — the fizzy, fan-pleasing variant — stuffs a handful of physical goodies into the box, adds a character named Tanya who probably knows more about your past lives than your therapist does, and then asks you to press the A button with the confidence of someone who forgot to blink for 15 minutes.

In the spirit of Geeknite, we’ll break this down into the kind of sections that would make a librarian clutch a joypad in surprise: the setup, the brain gymnastics, the audio-visual candy, the creature comforts of the Tanya-verse, and whether the price tag invites a moral upgrade to your wallet. Spoiler: it’s complicated, like your favorite logic puzzle that won’t stop asking “are you sure?” as you try to sleep.

## What is Infinite - Beyond The Mind, anyway?

Infinite - Beyond The Mind is one of those games that exists at the intersection of puzzle-platformer, existential spa day, and arcade cabinet therapy. It tosses you into surreal rooms where gravity is a suggestion, time is a rumor, and physics are just suggestions your brain occasionally accepts with a shrug. The core loop is simple on the surface: solve a sequence of spatial/puzzle challenges to unlock the next mind-bending chamber. The twist? Each chamber redefines what “solved” means. Objects float, perception shifts, and your sense of self is politely invited to take a walk around the neighborhood while you chase a souvenir from the problem you’ve just created.

The Switch version leans into tactile delight with rumble that occasionally feels like a polite suggestion from a wild jellyfish, and load times that are brisk enough to keep your attention without becoming comedic relief. The game’s aesthetic is neon-soaked, with a synthwave soundtrack that could power a small city in the 1980s if that city happened to be in a dream you had after drinking far too much artificially colored juice.

If you’re wondering whether the game is “too clever for its own good,” the short answer is: yes, it is. The longer answer is that Infinite - Beyond The Mind bakes cleverness into the gameplay core, giving you moments where you realize a solution is less about brute force and more about changing your perspective, which is exactly the mental exercise you signed up for when you bought a game about bending reality. It’s not your typical action-thriller; it’s a contemplative arcade ride that occasionally winks at you and says, “you figured that out with your brain and a little bit of luck, good job.”

## Tanya Edition: What’s inside the Limited package?

The Tanya Edition doesn’t just throw a sticker on the case and call it a day; it attempts to be a souvenir of the mind’s endless curiosity, and it mostly succeeds. The physical extras vary by retailer, but common inclusions are a Tanya figurine that looks suspiciously like a character who knows all your best secrets, a compact art book that reveals process sketches of the mind-bending rooms (and a few rooms we’re pretty sure were drawn by a caffeinated octopus), plus a small enamel pin shaped like a cog that clicks satisfyingly when you twist its tiny gears in your fingers.

On the hardware side, the Switch version runs smoothly. The handheld mode is where Infinite shines, because the smaller screen makes the shifting perspectives even more disorienting in a delightful way. You’ll notice a bit more pop when docked, which is great for display on that one shelf you pretend is “curated.” The difficulty curve remains friendly for newcomers but will reward players who approach puzzles with patience and a willingness to experiment. If Tanya’s edition includes any physical booklet, it’s a quirky nod to the days when games came with extra bookmarks, maps, and the occasional cryptic riddle you could try to solve with a pencil and a steady hand.

Speaking of pencils, the in-game UI is minimalist but thoughtful. It gives you just enough information to figure out what your next step is, while stubbornly refusing to dump all the answers into your lap. It’s a design philosophy that feels kind: the game provides the stage, you improvise the performance, and the audience (your own brain) applauds when you don’t crash into a wall that you should have seen coming only after you’ve learned to look from a different angle.

## How the gameplay actually feels

### Core mechanics and brain-mending loops
The primary mechanic is not your run-and-jump platforming; it’s perspective-shifting. You’ll push and pull objects, rotate rooms, and swap between dimensions, all while a timer ticks away in the background like a friendly demon reminding you to consider alternatives. The puzzles favor aha-moments over brute force; there are never more than a handful of viable solutions to any given room, but the cleverness of those solutions can take a few loops to notice. It’s the kind of game that rewards deliberate experimentation: try something, fail, try something else that at first seems unrelated, and then realize you needed to do both in a sequence that would’ve looked at home in a backstage improv show.

The Tanya Edition’s added spice is a narrative through-line that sits lightly on top of the puzzle structure, enough to give you a sense of progression without breaking the dreamlike mood. Tanya herself is a playful narrative device, sometimes offering commentary on your choices, sometimes stealing the limelight with a well-timed quip. She’s the friend you want when you are stuck on a particularly stubborn room—someone who will nudge you in the right direction while letting you feel smart for figuring it out yourself.

### Pacing and learning curve
Pacing is the true star here. The game starts with approachable rooms that tease your curiosity, then gradually layers in more complicated spatial twists. If you’re someone who enjoys the “one more level” phenomenon, Infinite - Beyond The Mind is the perfect candidate, because the “one more level” concept becomes a philosophical question: how many levels can you solve before you realize you have learned something about the nature of perception itself? The answer, as with most good puzzles, is a satisfying number that leaves you grinning and a little ravenous for more puzzles.

### Difficulty spikes and accessibility
There are moments where the game teases you with a difficulty spike, but these spikes aren’t petty traps designed to humiliate you; they’re invitations to reframe your approach. Accessibility options are present but understated; the game doesn’t virtue-signal its approach to accessibility, it merely implements it with the same quiet confidence as a nerd who quietly powers through a D&D campaign with a note card and gamer fuel. If you rely on assist modes, you’ll still be able to enjoy a substantial portion of the experience, though the real joy comes from embracing the challenge and feeling your mental reflexes sharpen as you approach the endgame.

## Audio-visual craft: a neon dream and a whisper of synth

Visually, Infinite - Beyond The Mind is a carnival of color. Neon glows, prismatic shards, and backgrounds that seem to breathe as you move through them. It’s not simply pretty; it’s designed to alter your perception. The art direction invites you to consider shapes as possible pathways, colors as potential signals, and lighting as a narrative tool. The sound design pairs with this aesthetic brilliantly: a synth-heavy score that oscillates between soothing and chaotic light, punctuated by sound cues that hint at hidden solutions rather than loudly announcing them. It’s the audio equivalent of a wink from a friend who knows you’re stuck but wants you to feel clever when you finally break through.

The Tanya Edition’s added cut scenes or dialogue bits feel like an extra layer of seasoning on a dish you already enjoy. They’re not essential to gameplay, but they help anchor the player in a tiny, charming world where Tanya’s wit is a compass and your curiosity is the map.

## Story, tone, and the emotional arc

If you come for a story, you’ll get a light, enigmatic thread rather than a traditional narrative arc. Infinite - Beyond The Mind treats narrative as a mood, not a plot engine. It’s the kind of story where you learn about the world by solving its puzzles and occasionally catching a cheeky line from Tanya that reframes your purpose. The tone stays playful and smart, with a dash of self-aware humor. It’s the kind of game that understands gamers are part puzzler, part philosopher, and part snack connoisseur who wants a quick bite of meaning between boss fights—except there are no conventional bosses, just bigger, more intricate mazes where your brain is the hero and also the problem you’re solving.

Emotionally, the journey errs on the hopeful side. Even when you’re stuck, you’re tucked into a warm blanket of neon, a friendly chorus, and Tanya’s charismatic commentary. It’s not a tear-jerker, but it does manage to elicit a grin of satisfaction when you finally unlock a room that had been taunting you for 20 minutes.

## Performance on Nintendo Switch: a handheld romance with a neon horizon

Performance-wise, the Switch version is stable, pretty, and responsive. The game regularly runs at a solid frame rate in both docked and handheld modes, with occasional dips during the most elaborate multi-element rooms. The developers have clearly tuned the engine to accommodate the Switch’s memory and GPU constraints without compromising the core feel—this isn’t a “lower the resolution and hope no one notices” port; it’s a deliberate adaptation that respects the hardware while preserving the game’s sensory identity.

Battery life in handheld mode sits in the average range for action-puzzle experiences, which means you’re not draining a small city’s worth of energy every time you tilt the system. If you’re traveling, expect to reach a good checkpoint before you need to recharge, which is all you can reasonably ask from a puzzle-driven adventure when your bus is a moving tea kettle of noise and motion.

## The value question: is the Tanya Edition worth it?

Value is a tricky beast, as it always is with limited editions. The Tanya Edition adds tangible items that fans will want to display or collect, rather than purely digital whistles. If you’re a collector who adores the tactile thrill of opening a new box and finding a small extra to admire, you’ll find the edition pleasing. If you’re primarily chasing a mind-bending experience on the Switch, the core game remains the heart of the purchase, with or without Tanya’s accessory entourage.

From a critical perspective, Infinite - Beyond The Mind as a standalone digital release stands strong: inventive mechanics, a thoughtful learning curve, and a mood that’s both introspective and exuberant. The Tanya extras push it toward a premium tier, which is fair given the added physical value; however, price-conscious buyers may decide the digital edition suffices and the Tanya trinkets become charming but nonessential memorabilia.

If you want to compare, look at other indie puzzlers that tug at your sense of space and time, such as a few classic puzzlers that emphasize perspective shifts and delightful nonsense. The good news is that Infinite - Beyond The Mind doesn’t feel derivative; it feels like a game that has learned from its peers and refined the idea into a tighter, more cohesive package—with Tanya tagging along to keep you company during those late-night “one more puzzle” marathons.

Infinite - Beyond The Mind on Nintendo Switch, especially in the Limited Tanya Edition, is a game that understands its own nerdy heart. It invites you to participate in a playful mental experiment, rewards curiosity, and does so with a personality that feels like it was written by a board of enthusiastic puzzle-nerds who own an art-supply shop and a synth studio. If you thrive on brain-teasing, color-saturated landscapes, and a narrative voice that never takes itself too seriously, this one should be on your radar.

If you’re deciding whether to pick up the Tanya Edition, ask yourself two questions: Do I want to collect something tactile for a shelf that might someday become the shrine of my gaming pride?
