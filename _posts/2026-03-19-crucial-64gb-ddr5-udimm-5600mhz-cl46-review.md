### Overclocking and Tuning: How Much TLC Do You Give Your RAM?
DDR5 is friendlier to overclocking than DDR4 in many respects, but the real gains come when you’re willing to push beyond stock profiles and watch the system respond with a friendly beep rather than a cry for mercy. The Crucial 64GB kit ships with an XMP 3.0 profile, typically 5600 MT/s CL46, that many consumer boards will happily adopt with a single enable-and-reboot. Here’s what to expect if you like to push your luck (and your BIOS):

- XMP 3.0: The simplest route. Enable XMP in your motherboard BIOS, apply the 5600 MT/s profile, and let the platform set the voltage and timings. In most cases, this is the path of least resistance and often yields stable performance with near-peak bandwidth.
- Manual tuning: If you want to chase memory performance beyond XMP, you can try dialing in slightly tighter CL values (e.g., CL44 or CL45) at 5600 MT/s, with caution around voltage. Expect minor improvements in bandwidth with caveats about stability. Not every CPU/motherboard pairing loves the same timings, so be prepared for a small journey of trial and error.
- Power consumption: DDR5 tends to be efficient on a per-byte basis compared to DDR4, but at high speeds and large capacities, you’re still consuming more power than you would with smaller kits. If your motherboard has a decent VRM and your case cooling is quiet, the difference is typically negligible in everyday use.
