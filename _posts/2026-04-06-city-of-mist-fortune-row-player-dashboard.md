# City of Mist: Fortune Row - Player Dashboard

Welcome, fellow fog-chasers and neon-noir enthusiasts. If you thought City of Mist already had enough mood lighting to power a small moon, wait till you see Fortune Row not as a board game accessory, but as a full-blown player dashboard accessory—like a glittering control panel for your character’s mythos, secrets, and the existential dread that comes with choosing the right fate token at 2 AM. This review will break down what Fortune Row brings to the table, how it feels when you actually use it in a session, and whether your players will shout with joy or mourn the loss of a perfectly good unspent clue token.

> Pro tip: if your city ever becomes a live-action setting, Fortune Row could plausibly double as the city’s 80s-themed control room. You know, the one with more blinking lights than a rave in a power plant. It’s that level of vibe.

## What is Fortune Row? A quick primer
Fortune Row is pitched as a player dashboard for City of Mist, designed to track the essential bits of a character as they navigate the twists and turns of a mythic city. Think of it as the cockpit for a couple of super-powered-PI-styled detectives: you keep track of your Forte (your personal edge of chaos), your Mythos connections, your currently active Paths (or arcs), and, crucially, the tokens you’ll need to narrate the next dramatic beat without flipping the entire table into chaos.

The goal is to reduce table chatter about “who has what” and “where did that clue token go,” so you can actually roleplay and not tangle your sheets in a USB-powered tantrum. The dashboard has modular widgets, a clean color scheme (neon purples, electric blues, and a lot of dark space to pretend you’re in a club run by a particularly stylish deity), and a few snappy autoscripts that pretend to be helpful while also gently mocking your decision to take that Edge at 3 AM instead of breakfast.

If you’re curious about the general vibe, the city’s old beachfront club and a weather machine that’s never sunny on Fridays feel like the right parallel. Fortune Row aims to translate that mood into something you can glance at between dramatic monologues and the occasional dramatic roll of the dice that goes tragically wrong because you forgot to switch on the emotional access panel. It’s not a replacement for your brain; it’s a supplement that pings your brain with more neon than a cyberpunk wallet’s LED display.

### A note on compatibility and use
Fortune Row isn’t just a fancy bit of GM support; it’s designed to be compatible with existing character sheets and the City of Mist ruleset’s emphasis on mythoi, alter-egos, and the ever-present risk of becoming too self-important to survive a conversation with a rogue fortune teller. The dashboard is built to be non-disruptive: you can pull up a widget, glance at it for a moment, and still be exactly where you left the scene—unless you rolled a critical mistake, in which case, well, welcome to the dramatic beat.

If you’ve used other dashboards for RPGs, you’ll notice Fortune Row leans into that familiar, clicky, tactile feel—but with a nerdy wink. It’s not about replacing the thinking; it’s about speeding up the remembering. If your memory resembles a scrappy noir city, this will feel like installing a memory palace that’s wired to your actual memories (with less risk of a fire-breathing dragon downloading into your brain mid-session).

## Design and UX: form meeting function
Fortune Row’s UI is a study in “sleek meets practical.” The color palette leans heavily into the neon noir aesthetic: midnight backgrounds that allow your glowing icons to shine, and typography that radiates confidence even as your character makes questionable decisions. It’s the UI equivalent of a leather-bound notebook with a backlit header that says, in elegant script, “You are probably about to do something extremely reckless.”

From a user experience perspective, Fortune Row shines in two areas: discoverability and context. Discoverability means you don’t need to spend 15 minutes hovering over tooltips to find out what a token does. Context means the dashboard surfaces relevant bits of information at the moments you need them, not when you accidentally click the wrong tiny button and trigger an in-browser lunar eclipse.

The typical layout includes a central character card, side widgets for Fate/Tension/Paradox, a row of quick-action buttons, and a bottom panel with recent dramatic beats. There’s also a neat “Pathway” panel that lets you pin a current storyline thread and see how close you are to flipping the table in a super dramatic reveal. It’s all very “Sam Spade meets HAL 9000,” which is to say: clever, slightly ominous, and surprisingly good at helping you remember where your plot thread went.

One subtle win for accessibility: keyboard shortcuts and a responsive layout. This means you can realistically use Fortune Row on a laptop, a tablet, or that fancy 20-inch monitor you claimed was for “productivity.” It scales gracefully, so if you’re one of those folks who deep-clean their desk by flipping it over, Fortune Row will still look cool and function like a calm, starry night rather than a blinking traffic light in a submarine.

As a fun aside, the product includes a light sprinkle of micro-interactions that trigger when you complete a scene beat: a soft chime, a ripple effect on the panel, and a small flourish when you finally “resolve” a mythic thread. It’s not over the top; it’s pleasantly cheeky, and yes, it does make you feel like you are the lead in a neon-lit crime-drama voiced by a particularly witty narrator.

## Features deep dive: what’s actually inside Fortune Row
Here’s the good stuff, broken down into digestible chunks. If you’ve used any digital RPG dashboards before, you’ll recognize the structure, but Fortune Row has some neat twists that fit City of Mist’s vibe.

### The Fortune Ledger
At the core of Fortune Row sits the Fortune Ledger, a dynamic counter that tracks your available “fortune” points for the session. In City of Mist terms, fortune is the currency you spend to momentarily tilt the odds, pull a mythic strike, or force a dramatic reveal. The Ledger isn’t just a numeric bar; it’s a tiny narrative device that nudges you toward the big moments rather than the safe ones.

What I liked here: when you spend fortune, the dashboard shows a quick, one-line flavor caption. It’s the sort of UI flourish that makes you want to narrate the moment aloud. The downside: if you’re stingy and refuse to acknowledge the fortune spent, you’ll end up in a loop where the page is constantly telling you to “spend more fortune” until you cave and declare the coffee shop your sub-lieutenant. Either way, you’ll remember that an impulsive decision often yields the most memorable scene.

### The Player Widgets
Fortune Row ships with a suite of widgets: Mythos Link, Alter-Ego Status, Clue Tokens, and Dramatic Beats. Each widget is a little panel that you can customize, rename, and reposition. The Mythos Link shows who you’re connected to in the city’s tangled web—think: family vendetta, a mysterious benefactor, or the city’s ancient street-light maintenance cartel. The Alter-Ego Status reveals your current dual identity and the risks of exposure. Clue Tokens are literal tokens that you can drag around and assign to plot lines. Dramatic Beats include a log of recent narrative milestones and upcoming hooks.

The practical upside is obvious: fewer sticky notes, fewer post-its, fewer existential blank spaces on your character sheet. The downside: there’s a risk of over-structuring your session into a pleasing grid that doesn’t allow for the organic improvisation City of Mist players love. The cure is simple: treat Fortune Row as a guide, not a jailer. You want the dashboard to spark creativity, not suffocate it with too many checkboxes.

### Customization & Modding
Fortune Row isn’t a closed ecosystem. It offers customizable themes, widget re-sizing, and a couple of user scripts for power users. If you enjoy tailoring your experience to the exact shade of neon you have in mind, you’ll feel right at home. If you’re allergic to customization and prefer a “plug-and-play” approach, you’ll still find enough built-in layouts to get straight to playing. There’s something satisfying about choosing a layout like you’d choose a character class: you want it to reflect your vibe without demanding you to become a web designer overnight.

### Integrations and compatibility
The dashboard is designed to play nice with the standard City of Mist sheets. It doesn’t pretend to replace the GM’s tools or the table’s memory; it supplements those aspects. If your game uses a PDF or a Google Sheet for the core numbers, Fortune Row can happily co-exist, importing data or serving as a live companion screen for the players. The result is a coexistence that makes you feel like you’ve upgraded your long-lost high-school notebook to a futuristic journal curated by a benevolent trickster god.

## How it feels in actual play: table dynamics and pacing
When you drop Fortune Row into a session, you’re not just giving players a pretty dashboard; you’re changing the tempo of the table. The moment you show up and say, “Let me pull up the Fortune Ledger,” there’s a tiny impulse of social contract in play. People know you’re about to narrate, and the tension shifts accordingly.

Pros:
- Faster bookkeeping: tokens, clues, and relationships are visible at a glance.
- More dramatic beats: the Dog of Destiny (your party’s chaotic, always-hungry sidekick) has more opportunities to bark, and the pacing improves because you aren’t rummaging through a PDF mid-scene.
- A shared language: everyone uses the same labels and icons, which reduces cross-table confusion and increases the chance you’ll remember that your Alter-Ego just did something wonderfully reckless.

Cons:
- It can become a crutch if you rely on the UI for every decision. Sometimes the best moments come from forgetting to update a value and letting fate surprise you.
- For new players, the dashboard can be a little intimidating at first glance. It’s not a “quickstart” tool in the sense of “open the box and start rolling”; it’s a neuro-nerdy brain-hug that rewards time invested in learning its language.
- If your table lacks a dedicated navigator, someone might end up playing the dashboard more than the character. That’s not a tragedy, but it can steal the spotlight if you’re not careful.

In practice, Fortune Row shines when used as a collaborative instrument—players looking at their own widgets, the GM glancing at the board, and everyone else listening to how the next scene hook will twist the city’s fate. The mood is always “we’re in a neon-drenched city,” and Fortune Row is the best friend you didn’t know your role needed until tonight.

## Play session workflows: a sample night with Fortune Row
Here’s a rough playpath you can borrow if you want to see Fortune Row in action without turning your table into a tech support call.

1) Pre-session setup: quick character alignment. Each player updates their Mythos Link, Alter-Ego Status, and a “Tonight’s Beat” tag. This takes roughly 10 minutes and yields a treasure map of dramatic potential.

2) Scene 1: inciting incident. The GM sets the tone; players observe their widgets. Clue Tokens flicker as players decide which clue to invest fortune in to force a reveal.

3) Scene 2: complication. A new threat card is drawn. The Fortune Ledger nudges players to consider spending fortune to tilt the odds toward a personal stake rather than a group victory. The dashboard becomes a narrative mirror for the players’ choices.

4) Scene 3: climax. A dramatic beat is celebrated with a “flash of mythic power” moment. The dashboard’s visuals flash, the room lights do something dramatic (or at least the laptop brightness goes up), and everyone cheers or groans in equal measure.

5) Debrief: players update their last scene’s outcomes. The GM notes any shifts in Mythos links or Alter-Ego exposure. The table ends on a high-energy beat and a plan for the next session.

## Strengths, weaknesses, and a verdict you can actually use
Pros:
- Aesthetic that fits City of Mist’s mood and mythos perfectly.
- Lightweight but meaningful: you’ll use only what you need, when you need it.
- A move toward narrative control without stepping on the GM’s toes.
- Strong customization options for players who are particular about how their character story should look on screen.

Cons:
- Could overwhelm newcomers with too many widgets at first glance.
- The balance between “handy” and “over-engineered” can hinge on table dynamics; you’ll likely want at least one dedicated player to manage the dashboard during heavy scenes.
- Some players may prefer static sheets; Fortune Row encourages motion and interaction, which isn’t for everyone.

Overall, Fortune Row earnsGeeknite’s recommendation for tables that love mood and momentum. If you’re tired of clinking tokens and flipping chairs to track a single clue, Fortune Row offers a refreshing alternative that feels both thematic and practical. It’s not a replacement for good narration or sharp GM prep, but it is a very good assistant for those who want to keep the neon flowing and the plot unblocked.

## Final thoughts and call to action
Fortune Row sits at an interesting intersection: it’s a practical tool that also leans into the game’s narrative drama. If you’re running City of Mist with a group that prizes dramatic reveals and a slightly more formalized approach to fate, Fortune Row will likely become a trusted companion on your table. The modules are well-designed, the visuals are a mood, and the ability to customize ensures you won’t feel boxed in by a one-size-fits-all interface. It’s the kind of accessory that makes you want to lean back in your chair and mutter, “Okay, one more scene, let’s see how the city burns tonight.”

That said, Fortune Row isn’t for every group. If your players chase pure improvisation and hot, off-the-cuff storytelling with minimal prep, you might feel better off with a lighter toolkit. If, on the other hand, you enjoy a steady rhythm, a consistent language for discussing plots, and a dashboard that rewards consistent table discipline, you’ll likely fall in love with Fortune Row fast enough to forget that the neon ever blinked.

A few final notes to help you decide:
- If you value narrative momentum and want a shared space to track progress, Fortune Row is a win.
- If you want absolute minimal friction to start playing, there’s a bit of a learning curve, but it’s a curve worth climbing for the long-term payoff.
- If you’re a cosplay-detailed gamer who loves the look-and-feel of neon-noir, you’ll want Fortune Row in your bag as soon as possible.

So, is Fortune Row worth your table time? Yes, with the caveats above. It’s a stylish, clever, and surprisingly practical dashboard that doesn’t overshadow the story but amplifies it. It invites you to focus on character drama, not spreadsheet drama, and in City of Mist that’s a rare and welcome gift.

To sum it up: Fortune Row is a neon-lit neighbor you want at your table for a night of mythic mischief. It won’t write your plot for you, but it will help you remember where you left your last clue while you chase the next one around a city that forgets you as soon as you blink. If you’re ready to upgrade your session from “okay” to “oh wow, we’re living in a noir comic now,” Fortune Row is the upgrade you’ve been waiting for.
