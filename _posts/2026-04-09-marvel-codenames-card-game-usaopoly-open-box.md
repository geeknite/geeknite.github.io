Overview: What’s in the Box

The box print is bold, loud in a good way, and clearly aimed at fans who know their Avengers from their Wasp. Inside you’ll find a stack of double‑sided codename cards featuring Marvel heroes, villains, supporting cast, and a handful of teams that feel like they were borrowed from a cinematic universe rumor mill. There are also the standard Codenames spy cards: red and blue agent cards, a black assassin card, and some neutral agents. The art style sticks to bright, comic-book style portraits rather than the more painterly look of some other licenses, giving it a fast, readable feel across a crowded table.

A plug-and-play feature of this edition is that the iconography is designed to be highly legible at a glance. The grid is a 5x5 square, which is consistent with classic Codenames, and the color palettes help differentiate teams even when the table is a little noisy from chip bags, soda cans, and someone’s enthusiastic commentary about Spider-Man’s web shooters.

The rulebook is compact but complete. It assumes you’re already familiar with Codenames and gives you a Marvel-flavored refresher: one-word clues, two-word clues, and the occasional need for a clue that nods to a character’s power or catchphrase without collapsing into spoiler territory. The layout is clean, with examples that walk you through sample clue-giving and decoding moments, which is essential when you’re playing with a mixed group of players who know every line of the MCU and those who only know that Ant-Man is, somehow, a big deal.

Setup and Setup Time

As with most Codenames variants, setup is brisk. You lay out 25 hero cards in a grid, face up, with the grid orientation random every game. The spymaster receives a map that sets which heroes belong to red, which belong to blue, and which card is the assassin, mirroring the baseline game’s risk/reward system. The “open box” descriptor comes into play here: the box itself is easy to open, the cards are double‑sided and surprisingly sturdy, and the edge detailing helps prevent card-edge wear after repeated shuffles. If you’re opening this box for a first-time play session, be aware that some players will immediately adopt a “team captain” role, which is charming until you realize you’ve delegated all clue-giving to a single very enthusiastic person who uses words like “quantum arc reactor synergy” in a single breath.

Rules and Gameplay: How It Feels to Play

The core loop sticks to the familiar rhythm of Codenames: a spymaster offers a one-word clue plus a number indicating how many cards on the grid are tied to that clue. The teammates then attempt to guess the correct hero cards. In Marvel CODENAMES, the clues lean into Marvel character relationships, power themes, or cinematic moments without becoming literal spoilers. A one-word clue like “Web” could nod to Spider-Man’s web-slinging, while “Stature” might reference Ant-Man’s shrinking adventures and the idea of “small but mighty” heroes.

Turn structure is quick. A correct guess reveals a hero, which is good for the team; an incorrect guess ends the turn and gives the other team a fresh chance to push their luck. If the assassin card is revealed, the round ends immediately. This is faithful to the original but with a Marvel flavor that can influence clue choices in playful ways. For example, a clue that references a well-known team-up moment (think Avengers Assemble) can be a clever gambit, but you have to balance it against the risk of draining your clue pool if teammates are less fluent in the MCU or if spoilers start poking their heads in via side chatter.
