## What is Sushi Go and why does it fit a family reunion
Sushi Go is a light, fast-paced card drafting game. Players pass cards around and simultaneously draft the best combination of sushi cards to score points. The mechanic is simple: you pick one card from a hand, pass the rest to the next player, and repeat until all cards are claimed. The trick is that you are always planning several moves ahead in a microcosm of strategy, which makes it feel deeper than it looks while staying easy enough for kids to grasp. It is a perfect fit for family reunions because:

- It scales well in short sessions while keeping the fun intact. Most playthroughs wrap in around 15 to 20 minutes, which means you can run several rounds without turning into a real yak-itori zombie at the table.
- It accommodates a range of ages and attention spans. Younger players relish the cute art and the immediate feedback of scoring, while older players enjoy spotting minor combos and outmaneuvering a cousin who thinks they are a sushi connoisseur.
- The theme is light and charming. Eating with chopsticks, glee over sashimi combos, and the occasional playful banter about who has the best EDAMAME strategy are all part of the charm.

## Components and setup: what you actually get in the box
Sushi Go is intentionally compact. The base game is a deck of cards that represents different sushi items, each with its own scoring rule. Here is a quick tour of what you’ll typically find in a standard set:

- Nigiri cards (egg, salmon, and squid): These are your main scoring bites. They vary in points, with bigger combos delivering better value.
- Sashimi cards: Collect three of these for a big chunk of points; two alone are basically a polite shrug. Three sis in a row, and you light up the scoreboard. It is a small thrill, really.
- Tempura cards: A two-card set that scores if you collect both. It is the old reliable of the bunch — consistent but not flashy.
- Maki cards: These are the table-flankers; scoring goes to the player who has the most Maki rolls at the end of the round. The top Maki roll getter grabs points while the rest grin and bear it.
- Pudding cards: Pudding is the final boss in the deck. You stash these aside; they count at the end of the game and can swing the final tally in surprising ways.
- Chopsticks: A special card that lets you pick two cards on a future turn, effectively giving you a little extra drafting power if used wisely.
- Cherries on top: Okay, not literal cherries, but there are little bonuses and scoring twists sprinkled in a few editions. They spice up the endgame without turning the whole thing into a spreadsheet dungeon.

The art is endearing and clean, with a color palette that reads well at table distance. The cards are sturdy enough for a family night and the overall package is light on weight but heavy on smile-per-minute. The game shines on a standard dining table or a coffee shop table that is a bit too small for a serious board game night but perfect for casual play.

Setup is a snap. Shuffle the deck, deal a hand of cards to each player (the exact number per hand can vary slightly by player count), and you are ready to roll. The rules are short enough to memorize in a single sitting, but the experience is layered enough that players can discover new synergies as rounds progress. The most important thing to know about setup is this: pick a comfortable seat, claim a corner of the table as your own, and prepare to pretend you are a sushi chef negotiating with your relatives for the last bite of nigiri.

## How the rounds flow: drafting, scoring, and a pinch of strategy
### The drafting mechanic
In Sushi Go, you receive a hand of cards, select one card to keep, and pass the rest to the next player. Everyone does this simultaneously, making the drafting feel quick and almost cinematic as cards fly around the table. The excitement hits when you glimpse the next pass and realize you might maneuver for a particular combo while denying another player the piece of the puzzle they need. The tension comes from balancing short-term gains with longer-term combos. Do you grab a single strong card now or pass it and hope to set up a bigger payoff later? The joy of this mechanic is that it rewards planning without punishing you for a misstep too harshly.

### Card types and scoring logic in brief
- Sashimi: Three of these equals big points. Don’t chase a single Sashimi; chase sets. It is the classic I got two but I still need one more problem.
- Nigiri: These two- or three-card sets are your bread and butter. Nigiri values scale with the sushi type, and Wasabi cards can amplify a future Nigiri to ridiculous heights if you time it right. Pro players treat Wasabi as premium stock; pick it early, then wait for your next turn to unleash a big point haul.
- Tempura and Maki: The Tempura requires two cards for a safe payoff, while Maki rewards sheer presence. If you can accumulate Maki cards and force the end-of-round tally on your terms, you can swing the round even when your Nigiri score looks modest.
- Pudding: This is endgame fuel. Pudding cards do not score immediately; they accumulate until the end of the game. The most thoughtful players often hide these points until the last moment, adding a final twist to the tally that can surprise tablemates.

### A few strategic bones for families
- Track what others are collecting without appearing too obvious about your own goals. People dislike being telegraphed, but a well-timed nudge of a card can help you steal a set they are pursuing.
- Don’t chase a single mega-scoring combo if it costs you more immediate cards you can use now. Sushi Go is a game of tempo; sometimes skipping a bigger card now preserves a stronger future round advantage.
- Be mindful of the pudding game. If you see a couple players already collecting pudding and you have a chance to snag a few late, it can be worth focusing there for the endgame swing.
- Finally, use the chopsticks at the right moment. If you can draft two productive cards on a turn, you can turn a decent hand into a winning strategy. But misuse of chopsticks wastes a round and frustrates your tablemates, which is the opposite of what you want at a family reunion.

### Advanced notes for the eager table commander
If you want to elevate your game without turning the whole night into an academic exercise, here are some practical micro-tips:
- Plan a couple of rounds ahead by recognizing what you can assemble even if you pass away a strong card early. In practice, this means noticing which types of cards are disappearing from the deck and trying to secure a final set when possible.
- Keep an eye on your neighbor's pile during the draft to spot potential gaps in their strategy. You are not playing hide-and-seek with a deck; you are trying to anticipate what they might be chasing and season the table with a little competitive banter.
- Don’t be afraid to pivot. If you have a strong start but see your path for a future round blocked by the chips on the sushi board, switch to a different set of cards that can still yield points. The ability to switch gears quickly is what separates good players from great ones in this game.
