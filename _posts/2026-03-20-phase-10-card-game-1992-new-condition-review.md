## What Phase 10 Even Is (For the Curious Newcomer)

Phase 10 is a rummy-like card game where players compete over ten distinct phases. Each phase has a unique arrangement that players must complete with ten cards from their hand. The catch? You cannot move on to the next phase until you complete the current one, which means the cards on your table end up telling a little story about your decision-making skills—mostly about who you’re willing to trade a straight flush for a pair of runs when the clock is ticking. The core loop is simple: draw a card, discard a card, try to assemble a hand that satisfies the current phase, then go out and try not to giggle when your opponent bravely debates whether a set of four runs counts as four sets of three—spoiler: it does not. It’s as much about memory, planning, and bluffing as it is about luck, which is why Phase 10 has endured for decades and remains a staple in family game nights and geeky week-long game jams alike.

We’re focusing on the 1992 New Condition variant, which is less about the tiny design errors you usually see in modern reprints and more about that retro vibe that makes you want to dust off a long-forgotten table in your living room and yell about phase 7 at the top of your lungs.

## Gameplay Deep Dive: The Core Loops and the New Condition Touch

Phase 10 is a loop you’ll recognize from your cousins’ pick-up games and your own childhood sleepovers. Here’s the alley-oop of the experience, broken down for the uninitiated and the nostalgically-addled both:

- Setup: Shuffle the deck, deal 10 cards to each player, and reveal the first phase. The rest of the deck sits in a draw pile; the discard pile is that judgmental friend at the table who’s watching your every move.
- Turn structure: On your turn, you draw one card (from either the draw pile or the discard pile) and then discard one card. The art here is choosing whether to hold a potentially powerful card for a future phase or to dump it now and risk future regret.
- Phase completion: If you can lay down the required cards for the current phase (for example, a sequence of three runs or a set of four of a kind), you do so and move on to the next phase. You can be on a phase, then fall back to a previous phase if you’re out of opportunities at your turn. This is the emotional rollercoaster that makes Phase 10 so deliciously chaotic.
- Going out: If you discard your last card, you’ve “gone out” and end the hand. Hand scores are tallied; the person with the fewest points across all rounds wins.
- Scoring: Cards 1–9 are worth their face value, 10s count as ten, and the special phase cards contribute to scoring in ambiguous but exciting ways. The New Condition box likely doesn’t change the math, but it does change the way you feel about your own decision-making prowess.


### Ten Phases: A quick refresher (and a little bragging rights trivia)

Phase 1: 2 sets of 3
Phase 2: 1 set of 3 + 1 run of 4
Phase 3: 1 set of 4 + 1 run of 4
Phase 4: 1 run of 7
Phase 5: 1 run of 8
Phase 6: 1 run of 9
Phase 7: 2 sets of 4
Phase 8: 7 cards of one color + 1 card of another color? See, this is where people start to disagree about the exact phrasing—stick with the guidebook in your chosen edition.
Phase 9: 1 set of 5 + 1 set of 2
Phase 10: 1 set of 5 + 1 set of 2 + 1 run of 3 (or something close, depending on which print you own; this is why the New Condition edition feels bespoke)

If you’re reading this and thinking that looks like a lot of redos, you’re not wrong. The exact phasing can vary by edition, but the spirit remains: make the best possible use of the cards in your hand to hit the phase before your opponents do. It’s a puzzle with peer pressure and bragging rights on the line.


## Strategy Tips: Mastering Phase 10 Without Losing Your Mind

- Plan ahead, but not too far ahead: You want to lay down as many completed phases as possible, but if you’re fixated on a single configuration too early, you’ll miss out on simpler routes to victory.
- Watch the discard pile: The card you choose to discard can give clues about what your neighbors are collecting. If you see a lot of red in their hand, you might want to grab a color that minimizes your own competition for the next phase.
- Use Wild cards sparingly: They can be game-changers, but they also become a liability if you’re not careful about where you place them. A misused Wild can cost you a phase and a friendly dinner party.
- Don’t ignore the last card: If your hand holds a potential perfect ending, it might be worth finishing a phase early and using your last discard to protect your finish. It’s a delicate balance between forward momentum and defensive play.
- Adaptability is your friend: Phase 10 is a game of changing plans on the fly. The New Condition edition rewards players who can adapt to the draw and to their table’s mood.
