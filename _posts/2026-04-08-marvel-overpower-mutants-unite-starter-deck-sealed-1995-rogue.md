## Sealed deck experience in 1995 vs today
Sealed packaging in the mid-90s was a ritual. It started with the distinctive crack of the wrapper, the snap of plastic, and the slight give of cardboard that told you something magical had arrived. You could smell the paste and the ink and the faint hint of plastic that 30 years later still conjures up memories of local game stores with fluorescent lighting and a wall of boosters behind the counter. In a world of modern sealed products, the packaging tends to be sterile and mass-market; in 1995, it felt more like a treasure map printed on glossy stock. The Mutants Unite starter deck is the perfect artifact for that moment: a curated sampling of Mutant power, Rogue effect, and the first taste of the team synergy concept. The unsealing ritual is half performance art, half scientific experiment, where you never know if the cards will behave or if a stray foil will glitter like a dragon wing.

### Artwork and card design aesthetics
Artists in the mid 90s loved bold lines and high-contrast colors. The Mutants Unite deck carries that signature look: heavy black outlines, metallic highlights, and backgrounds that are a little too busy for a modern screen but absolutely iconic in person. Rogue, in particular, wears a look that balances danger and charm: the character's costume pops against the card's background, and the border treatment emphasizes the Mutant method in a way that feels like a cross between a trading card and a trading card's trading card. The art feels like a snapshot from a comic book inked in the era when Photoshop was still a rumor and digital coloring was a sticker on the printer. This aesthetic, though dated, has a charm that modern players often misplace when they throw away their time machine.
