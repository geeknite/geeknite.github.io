## What is Just One?

Just One is a cooperative word game for 3–7 players where one player is the guesser for each round, and the rest of the players are clue-givers. On each round, a secret word is drawn from a deck and shown only to the clue-givers. Each clue-giver writes a single word clue that would help the guesser identify the secret word. After all clues are written, the clues are revealed to everyone, but there’s a catch: if two or more clue-givers wrote the same clue, those duplicates are removed. The guesser then looks at the remaining clues and tries to determine the secret word. If they guess correctly, the team wgets a success token; if not, a token is lost. The round ends, and the next player becomes the guesser. The goal is to collect as many successes as possible before the tokens run out.

The genius of Just One lies in its balance between structure and freedom. The clue-givers must be concise (one word each), strategic (avoid duplicates), and, ideally, a little witty. The guesser reclines into the role of detective, trying to connect the dots from a handful of one-word beacons. It is cooperative, but not in the woo-woo kumbaya way—more in the sense that your group actually gets to participate, laugh, and occasionally squabble about whether the word was spelled with or without a silent letter. This is not a deep strategy game; it is a social engine that rewards creativity, listening, and the grace to admit when you’ve accidentally created two identical clues that cancel each other out.

### The tangle of clues and the elegance of design

One of the most delightful design choices in Just One is how it embraces a potential pitfall—duplicate clues—and turns it into a feature rather than a bug. Duplicates are not just discouraged; they are purged before the clues reach the guesser. This means clue-givers must think a little more creatively to ensure their clue stands apart from others, which can lead to surprisingly clever and funny clues. The tension between cleverness and accessibility keeps the energy high without ever feeling like a test you’re going to fail publicly.

From a design perspective, Just One benefits from that “one word” constraint that makes players lean into linguistic ingenuity. ## How to Play in a Nutshell

### Setup

- Shuffle the word deck and place it within reach of the group.
- Determine the number of rounds based on token count (the base game has a fixed set of rounds; expansions can modify this).
- The current guesser sits blind to the secret word and listens as the clue-givers prepare their clues.
- Each clue-giver writes a single word clue on a card and passes it to the facilitator (or slides it under the table, depending on your group dynamics).

### Round Play

1. The round begins with the secret word revealed to the clue-givers only.
2. Each clue-giver writes a one-word clue that would hint at the secret word.
3. Collect all clues, then reveal them to the guesser.
4. If two or more clues are identical, they are removed from the pool.
5. The guesser studies the remaining clues and tries to name the secret word.
6. If the guess is correct, you advance and mark a success token; if not, you lose a token.
7. Rotate to the next guesser and repeat.

### Scoring and Endgame

The objective is to accumulate a certain number of successes before your tokens run out. The tokens act as a life bar for the round. As the round count grows, the tension ramps up, and the room swells with laughter, groans, and the occasional “Of course that was the word!” moment. The endgame is light and satisfying: you’ll either taste sweet victory or end the night with a chorus of “Let’s try it again with different clues.” The best part is that even a near-miss round leaves everyone smiling because the clues, not the correctness, are what make the memory.

### The Guessing Experience

The guesser has a unique role that is both exciting and a little nerve-wracking. They are the audience, the solver, and the occasional scapegoat all rolled into one. The clues you’re given are a combination of hints, memories, and the cultural shorthand your group uses. The randomness of the clue pool adds an element of chance, but the shared context—the in-jokes, the wordplay, the way someone loves to stretch a clue to extremes—creates a memorable experience every time.

## Strategy, House Rules, and How to Improve Your Play

No two groups play Just One the same way, and that’s part of its charm. Here are some practical tips to maximize fun and minimize frustration:

- Embrace the one-word constraint. Short, precise clues often work best when the secret word is common, like “apple” or “robot.”
- Think in synonyms and associations. If the secret word is “rocket,” clues like “space” or “launch” can be effective, but be careful not to be too close to another clue that might be written by a different player.
- Avoid obvious or overly specialized clues. The goal is to give a hint that most players in the circle can interpret, not to confuse your teammates.
- Use cross-group humor. Some of the best rounds come from playful misinterpretations of clues. Laughter is not just encouraged; it’s the lifeblood of the experience.
- Pay attention to the accusing glances when duplicates show up. It’s always a good time to laugh at how someone’s clue echoed someone else’s, then pivot to a fresh approach in the next round.

## A Quick Round Walkthrough: A Concrete Example

Let’s walk through a hypothetical round to illustrate what a typical session might feel like:

- The secret word for the round is “galaxy.” The cue word is shown to the clue-givers only.
- Five clue-givers sit around the table. Each writes a one-word clue. The contributions include: space, stars, big, sky, and color.
- Clues are revealed to the group: [space, stars, big, sky, color]. Since all clues are unique, none are removed.
- The guesser examines the clues. With hints like space and stars, they might quickly infer “galaxy.” If they guess correctly, the team earns a token and moves to the next round. If they guess incorrectly, a token is lost and the round ends.
