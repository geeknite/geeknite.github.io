## The Miniature: What is the Orc Zombie from Zombicide Black Plague?

Zombicide Black Plague is a board game that brings the undead kitchen sink into one glorious corporate-approved mass of plastic and plastic-adjacent screaming. Fantasy miniatures nerds will appreciate the pose. It’s not a heroic, chiseled brawler—this is a zombie who forgot to die with dignity and instead joined a union for relentless shamble production. What makes the Orc Zombie stand out in the crowd of Zombicide minis is its torso bulk and the way the sculpt handles texture: ripped leather straps, cracked armor plates, ragged tunics, and a face that would make a banshee go, hmm, maybe I should take a bite of that snack instead. ## Painting and Assembly: From Mold to Masterpiece

Ah, painting minis. The moment you open a blister and see the little green friend staring back at you with a 90s grunge aura, you know you are about to embark on a small quest of your own. The Orc Zombie from Zombicide Black Plague is not a difficult paint job, but it rewards patience and a bit of color discipline. Here’s how to get the most out of this particular miniature without turning your hobby desk into a seance circle.

### Assembly and Prep
If your copy arrives in multiple pieces, the assembly is straightforward: a bit of glue on contact points, maybe a pin for extra stability if you plan on using it in a metal-heavy battle scenario, and a dry fit to ensure the arms don’t end up awkwardly behind the back. The neck connection can sometimes be a *little* fiddly because undead necks tend to be less reliable than their living counterparts. Pro tip: use a tiny drop of super glue or plastic glue and hold for a few seconds. If you are worried about alignment, a quick pinning job with a fine pin vise can save you nightmares later on the table.

For the painting stage, a fast undercoat of black or dark gray sets the stage for flesh tones and rotted armor. The skin often benefits from a base wash of a desaturated green or gray to push the bone and tissue into the realm of decayed. Layering natural skin tones atop the wash, with a few touches of sickly yellow or purple around wounds, creates that undead look you want without looking cartoonish. The armor can be treated with a metallic base such as gunmetal, followed by rust washes and dry brushing to bring out the texture. The torn fabric—think tattered leather and cloth—benefits from a midtone layer and then a couple of washes to simulate grime.

### Painting Tips from the Geeknite Goblins
- Start with a color scheme that pops on the table but remains believable as a zombie. Think muted greens for flesh, bone-white teeth, and dark metal for armor.
- Use a quick glaze for blood and gore. A thinned red glaze lets you simulate damp, meaty textures without going full gore-museum.
- Dry brush the hair and hair-like textures for a ragged, dead look. Even the hair on undead orcs loves a little bristle brush love.
- Base decoration matters. A textured base with gravel, tufts, and perhaps an accidental toe of a decayed sword gives your model a sense of place.
- Highlight edges of armor with a brighter metal tone to catch the light during game play, making it easier to read in the heat of battle.

## Dungeons & Dragons Compatibility: Fit, Flavor, and Function

The Dungeons & Dragons half of this fusion party wants to know if you can slip this mini into a standard 5e encounter without raising too many eyebrows at the table. The answer is yes, with some caveats. The Orc Zombie is roughly in the 28–32 mm scale range, which is a sweet spot for many official minis. It will pair nicely with human adventurers, half-orcs, tieflings, or any other character who appreciates a well-timed scream after witnessing an undead horde shuffle into range.

In terms of game use, you can treat the Orc Zombie as a basic undead or an improvised zombie variant with a simple stat block: it moves slowly, hits with moderate strength, and has a multi-turn life cycle that rarely ends before the party finds a clever way to dispatch it. If you want something a bit crunchier, you can adapt it as a CR 1/2 or CR 1 creature with a few unique undead traits: decayed resilience (advantage on saving throws against poison and fear), rotting reach (the jaw can gnash at anyone within reach), and a lingering aura that saps the will to live. In short, it makes for a fun, crunchy NPC or a low-level menace that can surprise a party expecting to blast through a corridor with a dragon in tow.

Balancing tip: if your table runs a high-skill party with a lot of mobility and crowd-control, the Orc Zombie can function best as a blocking unit. Place it at a choke point and use the fear factor to force the party to split their resources. If your group likes tactical combat, it also makes a decent trigger for a timed trap or a cinematic rescue scene where a square of space is opened for a dramatic escape.

## Base Design and Scenic Potential

The base is an integral part of the storytelling. A well-designed base can move a miniature from a simple token to a mini diorama element on your table. For the Orc Zombie, a base that suggests a cracked stone floor, a bit of dungeon rubble, and perhaps a stray pustulent fungus can make all the difference when you want to imply a dangerous environment rather than a generic tabletop plinth.

Here are some base ideas:
- Moonlit dungeon floor with random chits of bone and skeleton bits peeking through the cracks.
- Wall nooks with a few moss and damp textures to imply an underground ruin.
- A ruined arch or gate that provides a narrative moment, such as the point of entry for this orc to burst into your party’s path.

Basing is often where players remember a model. A few extra tufts of grass or moss can transform a plain round base into a story beat. It’s amazing how a tiny paint job on the base can turn a model from a decent figure to a centerpiece of a scene.

## Painting and Tabletop Tips: A Short Checklist
- Always start with a clean model. Remove mold lines, wash seams, and prepare the base.
- Prime in a color that will help you get your midtones down quickly. Black or gray undercoats work well for bone and armor tones.
- Plan your color palette around the lighting you want on the table. If your dungeon lighting is dim, choose subtler tones to avoid a washed-out look.
- Don’t overcomplicate the finish. You want the model to pop, not to overshadow every other miniature on the board.
- Test placement with a few different basing options before the final decision. A tiny change in base can alter the perceived menace of the model.
