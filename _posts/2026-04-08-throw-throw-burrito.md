## What is Throw Throw Burrito?
Throw Throw Burrito is a card-based party game that centers on collecting sets of burrito cards while dodging or throwing two foam burritos at other players. It is the kind of game that works best in a living room with friends who are down for chaos, not the kind of game you bring to a quiet library or a formal club meeting unless you want those rules rewritten in real time. The objective is to earn points by successfully forming sets of burrito cards and by avoiding being knocked off your rhythm by a well-timed throw. The deck features a mix of action cards, dodge cards, and burrito card types. Each round unfolds with a flurry of instructions that require quick hands, quick feet, and quick wit to outplay the person who just rolled the “Attack” card.

Design-wise, the game leans heavily on its theme. The art is bright, cartoonish, and, frankly, a little chaotic—perfect for a game about tossing plush burritos. The components feel sturdy enough for repeated use, though note that you will probably lose at least one burrito during the course of a full game session, which, to be fair, only adds to the charm. As a party game, it succeeds in delivering immediate satisfaction. The learning curve is gentle enough for newcomers to pick up in a single sitting, while still offering enough unpredictable moments to keep seasoned gamers engaged for several rounds.

## Components and Setup
The box content is a good example of “it looks like a party and you’ll use everything in it.” You get:

- Two plush burritos that are soft, squishy, and perfectly safe for all ages above preschool. They are the star of the show, capable of dramatic arcs when hurled with confidence.
- A stack of action cards that drive the chaos forward: draw more cards, force someone to dodge, or trigger a burrito throw moment.
- A deck of burrito cards in various flavors or colors, designed to be matched into sets during play.

Setup is fast: shuffle the cards, place the burritos within easy reach, and deal a handful of burrito cards to each player. The initial lay of the land invites players to start forming sets as soon as their turn arrives. The pace is friendly but ebullient, the kind of tempo that makes you think you might invent new workout moves to keep up with the rounds—because you know you will need the cardio if the burritos start flying in earnest.

The components are robust enough to survive a couple of “outside-the-box” throws and a handful of sticky comedic moments. The plush burritos are the kind of soft toys that do not hurt when they hit your shin or your knee—though they may stoke dramatic reactions when they ricochet off a bookshelf and display a spectacular bounce routine that deserves its own montage.

## How to Play: A Quick Overview
If you want the spark notes version, here it is: draw cards, collect burrito sets, and avoid getting hit by the other players’ burritos. But let’s unpack that a little so you know what to expect when you set up for a rainy Friday night of chaos.

- Each player starts with a number of burrito cards in their hand. The goal is to form a completed set of burrito cards, which earns points or victory conditions depending on the variant you are playing.
- On your turn, you will draw one or more cards from the deck, depending on the action cards currently in play. The deck moves fast, and you may feel compelled to rush your choices to avoid the looming burrito-related doom.
- The Attack cards are the mood setters. They force another player to either draw more cards or to deal with a burrito throw. It’s a mechanic designed to compress decision time and induce laughter, not to create surgical precision in strategy.
- Dodge cards offer a brief window to avoid a burrito or a punitive effect. Use them wisely, because a poorly timed dodge is as funny as it is painful for your pride.
- Burrito cards themselves are the loot. You collect them and try to assemble three-card sets or other win conditions the guidebook suggests. The actual victory condition varies by the edition and any house rules you decide to brew, which is where the game gains its longevity and roommates' tolerance levels start to be tested.

The cadence of turns quickly becomes a rhythm: draw, dodge, throw, laugh, repeat. The laughter comes not just from the physical comedy of a burrito flying through the air, but from the way players learn to anticipate the chain reactions and miscommunications that arise when several people are shouting at once about who has the best matching set. In short, it’s cooperative chaos with a competitive edge, a combination that is suspiciously effective at turning a group of strangers into a cohesive, ridiculous unit after a couple of rounds.

## Dividing the Chaos: Variants and House Rules
If you want to stretch Throw Throw Burrito beyond a single evening, there are a few variants and house rules that can extend the life of your burrito-throwing parties:

- Time Attack Mode: Set a timer and see who can form the most burrito sets before time runs out. It injects a little competitive pressure and makes every misthrow a potential game changer.
- Team Chaos: Split into teams and coordinate burrito throws and dodge cards as a unit. You’ll discover new levels of teamwork, often exposed through the laughter and the inevitable accidental tag-team collabs on the same target.
- No Mercy Round: In this variant, players must be hit by a burrito at least once during the round, which raises the odds of high-energy chaos and makes the social interactions even more entertaining.
- Theme Nights: Encourage players to embody silly personas—pirates, pirates of burrito, or even culinary critics who review every throw as if it were a review of a Michelin-star burrito experience. The props almost become accessories for your storytelling, which adds an extra layer of humor.

The beauty of these variants is that they are forgiving and additive. Throw Throw Burrito is built around social dynamics, not rigid strictures. The more you tailor it to your group, the more personal your humor becomes, and that is where the game earns its re-play value.

## Components Acknowledged: What Works and What Could Be Improved
What works well here is the tactile appeal: the plush burritos give a soft, forgiving feel to all movement. They are safe for indoor use and offer a pleasing weight for adding a bit of drama to a throw. The card deck is well curated for chaos, with a balance between offense and defense that keeps players on their toes. The rulebook is concise and humorous, which lowers the barrier to entry and accelerates the sense of belonging in the group.

Where it could be improved is perhaps in the variability of the win conditions. Some players may find the victory criteria to be too dependent on the luck of the draw, especially in larger groups where swings of fortune happen rapidly. A few additional modes or even a more transparent scoring system could help players feel like they have some agency beyond “we all rolled a crazy hand this round.” Additionally, while the chaos is the charm, there are moments when a misthrown burrito blocks a page in the rulebook or accidentally hits the snack table, leading to a brief pause for cleanup. A larger play mat or a designated burrito zone could make those moments less disruptive and more immersive.

That said, these are minor quibbles in a game designed for raucous laughter and shared memories. In the end, Throw Throw Burrito shines most in the right social context: a room full of friends who are willing to shriek with delight when a burrito whirls past a face and lands perfectly on a coffee table, or who cheer when someone manages to snag a matching burrito card against impossible odds. The setup invites chaos; the play sustains it; the aftermath—stories you’ll tell again and again—becomes the real treasure.
