## Overview
If you thought that Zombicide Black Plague was the peak of medieval zombie mayhem, the Green Horde expansion slides onto the desk like a slippery goblin on a reported-clean floor. It brings a new faction to the party, a heavier dose of chaos, and two agents who would rather be anywhere else than in a siege with undead chattering feet. Gilbert and Mortimer, two wildly eccentric survivals who somehow managed to miss every memo about staying alive, narrate this little adventure with a mischievous grin and more tea than is strictly healthy for a post-apocalyptic setting. This review dives into what Green Horde brings to the table, how it changes the pacing and tension of the core game, and whether your night of undead shenanigans ends with victory, a heroic last stand, or Gilbert accidentally trading his sword for a spatula and calling it a day.

For fans of the base game, the expansion promises more enemies, more cards, and more reasons to argue about line-of-sight at the table. For newcomers, think of it as Zombicide on a quest through a swampy, creeped-out fairy tale where every tree seems to be whispering, Do you really want to take the long way around, or the fast way through the fog? Spoiler: the fog has teeth.

To set the stage, we will follow Gilbert and Mortimer from their first clumsy setup through their best heroic moment. The tone stays playful, but the tactical stakes rise quickly when the Green Horde stomps into town with their own rules and agendas.

## What's in the Box
The Green Horde expansion comes with a lovingly chaotic assortment of bits that extend the base game without feeling like it’s trying too hard to outshine it. You get:

- A handful of Green Horde miniatures that look like they streamed onto the board after a long night of experimenting with glow-in-the-dark slime. They scale nicely with existing zombies and survivors, and there are enough models to populate a few new search-and-survive scenarios without feeling cramped.
- A deck of Green Horde encounter cards. These are the spice that keeps the game from becoming a predictable grind. They introduce new conditions, enemy behaviors, and occasional twisty objectives that demand quick thinking and even quicker finger-twitches on the card-draw button.
- A set of new equipment and combat cards. Expect some quirky gear that would frighten a dragon, or at least make you reconsider which weapon you actually want in your hands during a siege.
- New ally and monster tokens, plus a couple of oversized tokens for those “this is clearly a boss” moments.
- A double-sided tile that integrates into the existing board, allowing you to weave in the Green Horde with more cohesive map layouts. It’s a nice touch and helps avoid the impression of “new content, new chaos, same chaos.”
- A couple of mission sheets that show you how the Green Horde changes the win conditions, often forcing you to adopt an aggressive push or a more cautious, attritional approach.

And yes, there are new cardboard punch-outs that will get bumped around the table with Tangled Web energy. As always with Zombicide, the components are sturdy, the art is on-brand, and the minis fit neatly with your existing armies. The aesthetic remains the grimly comic medieval vibe that makes the base game so visually appealing.

### Thematic note from Gilbert & Mortimer
Gilbert, a tinkerer who wears goggles like a fashion statement and a smirk that implies he knows exactly how this is going to go wrong, narrates many of the rules interactions with Mortimer — a practical soul who would rather drink a potion than drink in a potion. Their banter sets a lighthearted rhythm to a game that can otherwise bolt into high-tension survival territory. It’s a reminder that the best, most ridiculous plans sometimes lead to amazing breakthroughs or spectacular misfires. Either way, the table wins the chaos lottery.

## Setup and First Impressions
Setting up Green Horde is a familiar exercise if you’ve played Black Plague, but with a few extra steps to incorporate the new faction into the clean, modular way CMON designs these expansions. You’ll lay out the base map as usual, shuffle in the Green Horde tiles, and then decide which encounter deck you’ll be using. The mission sheets are clear and quick to reference, though you’ll want to flip to them mid-game more often than you expect if you’re attempting to chase those dynamic objectives.

First impressions matter here because the expansion leans into tempo and pacing. The base game’s early rounds often feel like a sprint with occasional cool-downs. Green Horde accelerates the sprint signature by introducing relentless green forces that make you think about pathing sooner, not later. Gilbert and Mortimer often misread a map edge and march toward a “surprise” that is less surprising and more preordained by your dice luck. The laughter that follows is not just from the players’ misfortune; it’s also the sense that you are participating in something delightfully chaotic.

The first turn when the Green Horde appears can feel like a warm-up act that quickly turns into the main event. You’re juggling two objectives at once: survive the incoming greens and satisfy the mission’s win condition before the board becomes a cosplay of a bad dream. It’s fun, in the way a carousel ride is fun until you realize your stomach is doing a somersault it didn’t consent to.

## Mechanics and Core Systems (What Green Horde Changes)
Green Horde builds on Black Plague by adding a few mechanical accelerants that change how you plan turns and extra actions. The core structure remains the same: you manage a crew of survivors, gather equipment, complete objectives, and fend off the undead onslaught. What changes is the emphasis and the cadence.

- The Horde Cards: A new deck introduces special events and conditions that trigger with the presence of greens on the board. These can reward aggressive play or punish it with a sudden flood of adversaries. They’re a fun reminder that the game doesn’t owe you a victory; it owes you a story — and a good one is often messy.
- Green Horde Zones: Some zones on the map have unique effects when the Horde enters or passes through. These effects can escalate the threat or temporarily buff your team if you exploit the area correctly. Thematically, you get to see how the greens tighten their ranks and press their advantage, which is a satisfying mirror of your own squabbles with Gilbert’s experiments.
- New Combat Cards and Weapons: The expansion introduces gear that can swing battles in dramatic ways. You’ll occasionally draw something that feels overpowered and then realize the greens have a few tricks of their own to combat it. It’s a merry dance of risk and reward that keeps the table engaged beyond the initial few rounds.
- Boss and Elite Tokens: The Green Horde isn’t just a splash of extra minions. It requires a little more strategic response, especially when you’re facing large green units that can soak up a lot of damage before they start to cringe under your assault.

From a gameplay viewpoint, the pacing shifts enough to demand careful planning without stripping away the base game’s signature chaos. The expansions don’t force you into a one-size-fits-all approach; instead, they tempt you to experiment with different strategies. Gilbert’s and Mortimer’s antics become less distracting and more inspirational as you discover new lines of play that you hadn’t considered when you started the night.

### Thematic notes and design philosophy
Green Horde is clearly designed to be a force multiplier for the core experience. It doesn’t simply throw more zombies at you; it introduces a more dynamic rhythm that rewards adaptation. You’ll find yourself considering evacuation routes, the timing of recruitments for large heroic actions, and the way you align your objective with your own survival. The humor remains intact; it’s still a medieval dungeon of witty banter and ridiculous ideas that somehow keeps coming back to bite you in the best possible way.

## The Green Horde Faction: Rules and How They Feel at the Table
The new faction plays to several of the base game’s strengths: quick decision-making, dramatic turns, and the thrill of a last-minute save. The greens have a few core rules that make them distinct without making the entire game feel like a different system. For example, some Green Horde units may have special movement quirks or require players to respond to a specific order in which you resolve combat. The net effect is a cleaner, more satisfying encounter that still feels like Zombicide rather than a completely new title.

From a tactical perspective, the greens emphasize grid control and area denial. You’ll need to decide when to let the supply lines get bogged down by greens versus when you simply outrun the threat to complete the objective. This tension is the sweet spot where you will shine or crash and burn with a glorious, laugh-filled crash.

## The Miniatures, Artwork, and Components
The Green Horde minis are consistent with the Zombicide aesthetic: grim, slightly cartoony, and gloriously expressive in their poses. They sit well with base game minis and look right at home alongside your survivors. The sculpts are crisp, with enough detail to make painting a worthwhile side quest if you’re into miniature hobbying. The cards keep to the same design language as the base game, which makes the entire product feel like a coherent expansion rather than a separate add-on.

Artwork across the expansion is on-brand and thematically cohesive. The dank greens, the wan moonlight on torn banners, and the decayed wood textures create a mood that is equal parts eerie and goofy in the best possible way. The audio/visual impression when you open the box is exactly the moment you think: yes, this is going to be fun chaos.

## Difficulty, Balance, and Replayability
Green Horde nudges the difficulty curve upward, but not in a punitive way. It’s more of a gentle push that makes you adjust your plan earlier in the game. If you were breezing through with a triage kit and a dash of luck, expect to be forced into more careful decision-making and proactive planning. The new encounter cards and map elements create a few fresh headaches that feel fair and thematic rather than arbitrary.

Replayability is strong here because the greens’ behavior changes with different mission setups and board layouts. You can shuffle the encounter decks, mix in a couple of the mission sheets, and you’ve got a new flavor of chaos that will feel fresh after a few sessions. Gilbert and Mortimer’s banter becomes even more entertaining when you’re forced to improvise a strategy around the greens’ unpredictable moves.

## Strategy and Tactics: Practical Tips from a Geeky Table
- Prioritize flexible objectives: The Green Horde tends to punish rigid plans. Build your strategy around adaptable goals, not a single win condition.
- Positioning matters: The new zones reward smart positioning. Don’t cluster tightly unless you’re ready for a surge of green units to overwhelm your lines.
- Coordinate with your team: Use your survivors’ unique abilities to create synergies. The expansions are designed to reward player cooperation rather than individual glory.
- Manage your resources: The new gear cards emphasize risk management. Don’t blow a powerful weapon on a single zombie if it means you’ll be under-equipped for the next wave.
- Learn from Gilbert and Mortimer: Their commentary is a tonal cue that you don’t need to be perfect to enjoy the expansion. Embrace the chaos, but aim for moments when clever thinking turns chaos into momentum.

By focusing on flexible objectives, you’ll often squeeze out an extra round or two of life for your survivors, which is exactly the kind of vindication that makes a tabletop night memorable.

## Thematic Cohesion and Interactions with Other Expansions
If you’ve already got other Zombicide expansions, Green Horde slots in smoothly. The design team has done a good job ensuring rules interactions remain logical rather than contradictory. The Greens’ behavior often interacts with base game mechanics of spawns, searches, and room clearing in a way that encourages players to adapt rather than shotgun through every encounter.

If you’ve combined this with other expansions, we recommend running a couple of practice rounds first to feel out the new rhythms before hitting a full campaign. The last thing you want is a chaotic merge that makes the table feel like a medieval arcade cabinet from a parallel universe—though honestly, that would be kind of epic.

## Value and Longevity
For a mid-to-high tenner investment (depending on retailer and edition), Green Horde offers a meaningful increase in table time and excitement. It introduces enough new content to sustain several play sessions without overshadowing the core game. If you’re a die-hard Zombicide enthusiast, you’ll probably want this expansion for the long-term variety it provides. If you’re a casual player, this expansion can still be a fun centerpiece that revives your interest in Black Plague by shaking up how you approach each map.

As with most CMON releases, the longevity comes from the combination of new play styles and the social dynamism around the table. You don’t play Zombicide solely to conquer the map; you play to watch Gilbert and Mortimer improvise under pressure and somehow make it work—with occasional mishaps that still end up feeling epic.

## Final Verdict
Green Horde is a strong addition to Zombicide Black Plague, especially if you enjoyed the base game and crave a more dynamic, chaotic challenge. It preserves the core mechanics you love while injecting enough new rules, tokens, and miniatures to make each session feel new. The humor and banter of Gilbert and Mortimer are a welcome narrative coating that keeps the proceedings light-handed even as you’re staring down the blade of a green tide.

If you’re trying to decide whether to purchase it, here’s the TL;DR:
- Pros: Clear integration with base game, good mechanical variety, strong theme, enjoyable new missions, great table presence.
- Cons: May slightly escalate difficulty, which could frustrated some casual players if not balanced by teamwork.
- Bottom line: It’s a worthy expansion that adds depth without breaking the core experience, and it gives you a few more excuses to yell Gilbert and Mortimer across the table while pointing dramatically at a pile of zombies.
