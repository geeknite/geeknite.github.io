## The flaw: what we’re actually dealing with
Flaws in rugs don’t all come from the same place. Some are cosmetic quirks, some are from the factory floor, and a few are the result of a budget-limited design team trying to ship something that looks like a million bucks to people who spent a hundred. Here are the most common culprits you’ll encounter in a flaw-listed rug, and how they might manifest in Natural Terra:

- Dye variation: A slightly off shade in a pattern or solid area. Not a crime, more like a personality quirk. If you like organic, lived-in vibes, this can read as intentional depth rather than a misprint. 
- Weave tension: Tiny misalignment in loops or a minor wobble in the edge. Usually not noticeable from two feet away but certainly visible if you’re a perfectionist with a ruler.
- Fringing irregularities: Fringe might be a tad longer in one corner or unevenly knotted. It can be charming if you’re into the handmade look; less charming if you’re hunting for showroom-level symmetry.
- Pile height variation: Some patches may feel slightly thinner or thicker. This can create a “topographic” feel that some folks adore for the texture, while others call it a trip to the carpet mountains.
- Knotting or pattern misalignment: A small section where the geometric pattern stutters. This reads like a glitch in a video game, but with wool and a lot more warmth.

## Materials, durability, and maintenance: what to expect on a daily basis
Durability matters when you’re shopping for a rug that will see kids, pets, and occasional foot traffic from your in-laws. The Natural Terra rug uses a synthetic blend designed for stain resistance and ease of cleaning. Here’s what to expect in real life:

- Spill management: Blot, don’t rub. Use a clean, dry cloth to absorb the liquid, followed by a gentle dab with a mild detergent solution if needed. Avoid harsh chemicals, which can affect color and fiber integrity over time.
- Vacuuming: Regular vacuuming with a beater bar off (or a gentler setting) helps maintain pile integrity and reduces the chance of embedded dirt taking root like an unwelcome house plant.
- Fray risk: Fringes can snag on pets’ claws or on the edge of a coffee table. If the fringe is part of the installed design and you’re not skilled with rug repair, consider a heavy-duty rug pad to reduce movement and minimize snagging.
- Wear patterns: In high-traffic areas, you may notice consistent wear in the center or along the main walk path. That’s normal for a low-pile rug and can be mitigated by rotating the rug every few months to distribute wear more evenly.

Maintenance tips from the pros: keep a small cleaning kit on hand, blot spills immediately, and use a rug pad to reduce slippage and wear. If you have pets, trim nails regularly and consider a coffee-table fortress to protect the corners from unintended claws. The point is to preserve the rug’s life while keeping it looking intentional, not accidental.
