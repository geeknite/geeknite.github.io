## How to Play: Quick Overview and Rules Reboot

### Core Mechanic Recap

If you’re unfamiliar with Uno Flip, here’s the quick version: players take turns placing a card on the discard pile if the color or number matches the top card. The twist is that every card has a dark side that is active when a flip card is played or when the color demands a flip at the end of a round. The goal remains to be the first to shed all your cards, but the neon variant introduces extra stakes through flip cards, wild cards, and color change dynamics.

In Neon Colors, the deck splits into two visible halves: light-side colors and dark-side colors. The flip cards are still the star players, but the neon palette makes them more prominent at the table—physical cues become faster than mental notes. You’ll notice players blurt out color changes with a dramatic flair you didn’t know they had in them, often accompanied by a small victory dance or a theatrical sigh when they have to draw several cards.

### The Color Dynamics and Turn Flow

When a flip card hits the pile, everyone flips to the opposite side of their cards. The top of the new face reveals not just a color but a mood. The neon glow helps track whose turn is next more reliably, even in dimmer rooms. In practice, the neon colors create a rhythm: you play, the table flips, and the neon glow guides everyone’s eye to the new color, reducing the fumbles that sometimes plague standard Uno in low-light environments.

### The Rules Variants You’ll Likely Encounter

- Standard Uno rules plus flip: you must flip the whole deck to reveal the other color when a flip card is played or when a color-change effect is triggered.
- Draw-two, skip, reverse: continue to function as usual, but the neon palette makes these actions feel more dramatic due to the color pop.
- Final card shout: a fun optional rule where players can shout Uno when down to one card; in Neon Colors, the shouts feel more theatrical because the colors themselves scream attention.
