## Getting Started: Quick Play Guide in 10 Minutes (With Bonus Troll-Resistance)
If you want the fast lane to fun, here’s a quick-start that looks easy on the surface but is a sprint in the meadow:

### Setup
- Mix the base deck with the Recipes for Disaster expansion cards. The box supports 2-6 players, but we’ve found 4-8 to be the sweet spot for the table to feel lively and chaotic without tipping into a full-blown strategy lab.
- Each player starts with five cards. The aim is simple: avoid drawing an Exploding Kitten and end the round with the most points. Simple in theory, chaotic in practice.
- Shuffle in Exploding Kitten cards with the rest of the deck, and arrange a space on the table where everyone can reach for the recipe cards. Pro tip: designate a “kitchen counter” area that only exists for dramatic reveals.

### Turn flow
- On your turn you may play a card or draw. If you draw and reveal an Exploding Kitten, you’re out unless you have a Defuse card or a goofy recipe-based save. Defuse cards feel like magical insurance policies, but don’t sleep on the influence of timing.
- Recipe cards let you perform cooking-themed antics, like forcing someone to reveal their hand, swapping cards with another player, or introducing a blunder that negates another fancy move. It’s a kitchen theatre, and you’re the impromptu director.
- The expansion adds special recipe combos that trigger dramatic effects like a sudden dessert duel or a spicy sauce swap. It’s chaotic, yes, but the laughter is real—and the kitchen jokes are never-ending.

## Strategy and Social Play: How to Win in Style (Without Becoming a Villain Who Eats Defuses for Breakfast)
This expansion rewards social play more than cutthroat tactics. Here are some practical strategies, polished by years of watching friends pretend to whisk disaster from thin air:

- Read the table, then decide: The best moment to deploy a recipe card is often when someone is about to go on a winning streak. A well-timed force move can swing momentum and put a new mischief-maker into the lead. Timing is not just everything; it’s a friendly rebellion against competitive seriousness.
- Bluff and blurp: People love to guess what your hand contains. A confident bluff can deter others from calling your actions. The key is to stay consistently playful, not mean, and keep the room light. Your tone matters as much as your cards.
- Manage the defuse economy: Defuse cards are finite resources. Save them for moments that matter, or use them to trigger chains of reactions that advantage you in the long run. It’s often better to bluff and threaten than to actually risk your own safety by overusing defuses early on.
- Use the Disaster cards as momentum swingers: Don’t just punish; create a moment that changes the table dynamic. A well-timed disaster can spark a memorable clash between two players, filling the room with laughter and the scent of imaginary burnt toast.
- Embrace the party vibe: The point of this game is to entertain. If a round becomes too intense, switch to variants that emphasize storytelling or quick-fire questions. The more you lean into comedic energy, the less likely you are to hit that awkward lull.

### House rules and quick-start variants
- The Chef’s Randomizer: At the start of each round, draw one extra recipe card and discard one. The table must narrate an impromptu mini-sketch about the discarded card before resuming.
- The Showrunner Rule: After a disaster is revealed, the player who caused it must perform a two-line improvised monologue about their “culinary catastrophe” before the next turn.
- The Timed Turn: Use a 60-second sand timer for each hand once per game to speed up laughter and chaos. If time runs out, players must perform a silly action instead of drawing.
- The Kid-Glove Variant: If a player is visibly new to the game, grant them two extra Defuse cards at the start. This keeps the first session light and reduces early elimination anxiety.
