## How to play in a charmingly chaotic nutshell (with additional nerdy flavor)
Gloom invites you to tell stories by sacrificing cards to the altar of petty tragedy. Each family card has a name, a backstory, and a gloom value that climbs as the family endures misfortune. Your objective is to arrange your families so that the gloom peak occurs when they meet their melodramatic demise, all while trying to keep opponents’ gloom in check (they’ll do the same to you, obviously).

The play flow is accessible for a casual night yet deep enough for the puzzle-loving storyteller:

- On your turn, you can play cards to add to your own family or hamper someone else’s. Cards explicitly spell out their effects, which is a mercy when you’re worried about remembering a hundred arcane rules from a different game weekend.
- You manage two resources: the gloom value of your families and the negative tokens that accelerate doom. Planning is essentially arranging a funeral with a wink.
- Mood tokens are the secret sauce. When events trigger, mood tokens slide from neutral to tragic, occasionally flipping the table’s morale from “meh” to “I object to your metaphor.” This shifting mood can redefine who’s currently the table’s most devoted gloom connoisseur.
- When a family reaches maximum gloom and dies in a melodramatic, theatrical fashion, its card leaves play. The timing of these dramatic exits is what makes the game both gleefully morbid and surprisingly tense.

The Complete Collection shines here because expansions don’t just add cards; they add narrative strings you can weave into a continuous doom-ometer. The pacing remains brisk, with turns rarely stretching past a couple of minutes, enabling multiple micro-stories to unfold in a single game night.

### The rhythm of a turn (a closer look)
Turn structure is refreshingly tidy:

- Draw a card or play cards to advance your deathly agenda.
- Trigger any event effects from your cards or the table setup.
- Update gloom counters and mood tokens accordingly.
- Pass to the next player and resist the urge to sneeze at the stench of despair rising from the table.

In practice, you can weave a micro-story about a haunted teapot, a forgetful inventor, and a cat with nine lives that lives them dramatically nine times. It is storytelling with a timer, a dash of whimsy, and a suspiciously affectionate board game housecat watching over your shoulder.
