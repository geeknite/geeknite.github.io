## How to play in a charmingly chaotic nutshell (with some extra nerdy flavor)
Gloom wants you to tell stories by sacrificing cards to the altar of petty tragedy. Each family card has a name, a backstory, and a gloom value that climbs as the family experiences misfortune. Your objective is to arrange your own families so that the gloom is as high as possible when they meet their melodramatic demise. Meanwhile, you want to keep your opponents’ families from getting too gloomy because, well, watching others fail is half the fun.

The play flow is simple enough for a casual night and deep enough for a puzzler’s fever dream:

- On your turn, you can play cards to either add to your own family or hamper someone else’s. Cards tell you exactly what they do, which is great for avoiding the anxiety of interpreting a Legend of Zeldash version of a family saga.
- You manage two resources: the gloom value of your own families and the negative tokens that can accelerate doom. Planning is like arranging a funeral, but with more whimsy and fewer formalities.
- Mood tokens are the secret sauce. When certain events occur, mood tokens shift from neutral to tragic, and that shift can dramatically alter who is currently the greatest connoisseur of despair.
- When a family reaches maximum gloom and dies (in a heroic, melodramatic way, of course), its card is removed from play. The timing of these deaths is what makes this game both gleefully morbid and surprisingly tense.

The Complete Collection shines here because expansions do not just add more cards; they add more ways to string your gloom into a narrative that could convincingly sustain a small, dramatic web series about cousins and misfortune. The pacing remains snappy, with turns rarely dragging beyond a couple of minutes, allowing multiple cycles of storytelling to unfold over a single game night.

### The rhythm of a turn (a closer look)
Turn structure is straightforward:

- Draw a card or play cards to advance your deathly agenda.
- Trigger any event effects from your cards or from the table setup.
- Update gloom counters and mood tokens accordingly.
- Pass to the next player and try not to sneeze at the stench of despair rising from the table.

In practice, this rhythm means you can weave into one round a micro-story about a haunted teapot, a forgetful inventor, and a cat with nine lives that merely lives nine times as dramatically. It is storytelling with a timer and a slightly suspicious board game housecat.
