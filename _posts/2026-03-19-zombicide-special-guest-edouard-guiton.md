## Box Contents and First Impressions
The box is immediately a treat for the eyes. The cover art, which is where Edouard’s flair really shines, features moody lines, bold silhouettes, and zombies that look like they were sketched in a notebook during a particularly exciting lecture on color theory. Inside, you’ll find the usual suspects (survivor figures, zombie miniatures, cards, boards) but with the guest designer’s signature touch: more personality per tile, more character-driven zombie sculpts, and a surprising depth to the equipment cards that practically begs you to tell stories about your survivor’s backstory before rolling a single die.

Aesthetics aside, the components feel sturdy. The miniatures have the classic Zombicide pose language—dynamic, exaggerated, and ready to clatter into each other with a satisfying clunk when you finally nudge them into the wrong place. The cardboard tokens are crisp, the boards lay flat, and the rulebook—yes, that sometimes-dreaded rulebook—puts its best foot forward with clear diagrams, labeled steps, and a few cheeky asides that remind you this is still a game about having fun with friends, not studying for a physics exam.

You’ll notice a few new twists: a couple of fresh zombie types designed to feel both thematic and mechanically meaningful; a handful of new weapon cards that lean into Edouard’s aesthetic (think elegant blades that are almost too pretty to stab, but stab you will). There are also a handful of “guest diary” cards—these are narrative prompts you can use to spin a mini-campaign around your party’s misadventures. It’s a nice touch that signals the designers wanted this to be more than a add-on; it’s an invitation to a themed party where the zombies crash the door with a flourish rather than a dull, predictable march.


## Edouard Guiton: The Guest Designer’s Stamp
Edouard Guiton has a distinct visual language: clean lines, a moody noir vibe, and a tactile sense of energy in his sketched silhouettes. In the expansion, his fingerprints aren’t just on the art; they bleed into the mechanics in small but meaningful ways. For example, some survivor cards come with “favors” that you can call in by narrating a short heroic moment—think a comic book panel caption: “In this frame, you hear the whisper of a plan.” These moments aren’t strictly necessary to win, but they add a theater of story-telling to the session that many players will crave.

On the board, Edouard’s influence translates into spatial design cues. The new map tiles feature jagged streets and alleyways that feel like they’ve been hand-drawn from a notebook but realized with a modern sense of balance. You get a stronger sense of pacing as you snake around corners, searching for better cover and more efficient lines of fire. It’s not just art for art’s sake; it’s art that nudges you toward smarter positioning and more narrative tension.

The overall vibe is unapologetically stylish without sacrificing playability. It’s a rare quality: a guest edition that looks like a collector’s item but still behaves like a game you would want to bring to a coffeehouse game night rather than a museum. If you adored Edouard’s other projects, this expansion will feel like a warm glass of cider poured into a fancy glass: visually delightful, with a subtle bite underneath.


## Mechanics, Balance, and New Toys
Let’s get practical: what does this expansion actually add to the table beyond beauty and vibes?

- New Survivor Abilities: The edition introduces a few themed abilities that align with Edouard’s aesthetic. Some survivors gain boons when performing sharp, precise movements; others excel when choosing cover and maintaining line of sight to multiple targets. These aren’t misprints of a “cool idea” deck; they’re carefully integrated options that encourage different playstyles. If your group tends to play the same way every session, this expansion nudges you toward experimentation without forcing it.
- Fresh Zombie Variants: The new zombies aren’t just “two more sculpts.” They bring tactical considerations. Some variants test your ability to adapt to threats that aren’t just the old “shamble straight at you” pattern. One variant requires you to prioritize a multi-target approach; another demands careful resource management as it pressures you to split attention between multiple fronts.
- New Equipment and Cards: The equipment cards feel thematically aligned with the guest designer’s aesthetic. Some weapons are more flamboyant than practical, but they reward creative play. The cards come with little mechanical quirks that can swing a round in surprising ways, which is precisely the flavor you want from a Zombicide expansion: something new that still fits the game’s core drag-and-drop chaos.
- Campaign Staging: The guest edition encourages a structured mini-campaign arc, with escalating threats and a few narrative beats. It’s not a rigid campaign mode; rather, it’s a guided flavor that helps you build a night’s story rather than just a series of skirmishes. It’s perfect if your group wants an arc and a sense of purpose beyond “we killed as many zombies as possible before the board filled.”

Balancing is, as with most Zombicide expansions, a love letter to the players who obsess over how many walkers can fit on a single tile before you start to cry. The designers have done a commendable job here: the new elements feel meaningful, but not oppressive. The difficulty curve remains approachable for a mid-to-high-skill group, with enough headroom for table banter and last-minute strategies.

There might be a few edge cases where certain combos of the new cards can feel a touch overpowered if your group is particularly optimized. However, these are not egregious; they’re the kind of quirks you solve with house rules or a quick veto at the start of a session. In short: this expansion is playable, balanced enough for regular play, and offers a genuine sense of novelty that doesn’t destabilize the entire game system.

## Setup, Playthrough, and Table Time
Setup remains straightforward for seasoned Zombicide players. The new tiles are integrated into the standard map with a few additional rules explained clearly in the expanded rulebook. The clear takeaway: the guest edition does not require a half-hour prelude to figure out where the new pieces go, nor does it demand you memorize a separate curriculum to get started. It respects your time while still giving you something fresh to explore.

During the first playthrough, you’ll likely gravitate toward exploring the new spaces and testing the new weapons. The pacing benefits from Edouard’s design choice of giving players meaningful decisions at every turn. You’ll feel the pressure to pick a safe route versus a riskier maneuver that could yield a game-changing advantage—or a spectacularly entertaining betrayal by a zombie who suddenly rotates the board 90 degrees in your brain.

A quick walkthrough of a typical round with the expansion might look like this: you activate a survivor, choose a movement path that leverages cover and line-of-sight, declare an attack with one of the new weapons, resolve the dice results, and then react to the emergent threats triggered by the new zombie types. The flow remains familiar enough to not alienate veteran players, but the added options provide plenty of moments where you realize you underestimated the tactical nuance you can wring from a single turn.

If your group has a tradition of a monthly Zombicide night, this expansion is the kind of content that gives you a new chapter to project onto the same core rules you already adore.
