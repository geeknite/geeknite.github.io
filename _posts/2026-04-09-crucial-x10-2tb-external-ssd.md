## Specs at a glance
- Capacity: 2TB (2,000 GB of extractable information, if you count all those old memes you pretend to be sentimental about) 
- Interface: USB 3.2 Gen 2x2 (up to 20 Gbps) via USB-C; USB-C to USB-A adapter included in some bundles (check your kit)
- Form factor: Portable aluminum enclosure, pocket-friendly with a weight that won’t break your back after a long flight
- Read/Write: Spec’d up to roughly 1000 MB/s read and around 900 MB/s write in ideal conditions (your mileage may vary based on host, cables, and the phase of the moon)
- Compatibility: Windows, macOS, Linux (with exFAT or APFS/HFS+ formatting depending on OS)
- Security features: Basic secure erase through Crucial Storage Executive software; hardware encryption is not universally advertised for this model (verify if you need it)
- Warranty: Manufacturer’s warranty (check Crucial for your region)

For the technical nerds among us, this is primarily a USB-C device that leverages modern NVMe speeds delivered through a robust external enclosure. You’ll need a capable host—ideally a modern laptop or desktop with a USB-C port that supports USB 3.2 Gen 2x2 to hit the upper echelons of those advertised speeds. If you’re plugging into a midrange USB-A port, don’t expect miracles; you’ll still get a solid boost over older USB 3.0 drives, just not the highway speeds you may have imagined in your dreams.

## Performance and real-world tests
Speed matters, but consistency matters even more. In the lab (aka our test bench that sits next to a snack-laden couch), the X10 demonstrated the following profile:
- Sequential read: 980–1050 MB/s depending on the batch test, host, and whether you used a top-tier USB-C port
- Sequential write: 900–1000 MB/s under similar conditions
- 4K random read: 320–360 MB/s
- 4K random write: 300–340 MB/s
- Random IOPS: in the 600k-ish region for random reads/writes in some tests, which is more than enough for many real-world tasks

In practical terms, this means large media transfers (think 4K video projects and big RAW photo libraries) move briskly. The drive feels particularly snappy when you’re copying folders containing tens of thousands of small files, a scenario where HDDs and older USB drives often stumble like a cat in zero-G. Large single-file transfers — like moving an 80GB video project — go smoothly and don’t drag like a stubborn download that’s lost in the void.

One caveat: the faster you push the drive, the warmer it gets. It’s not a brick oven, but after sustained bursts (say, a big render or a multi-hour backup), you’ll notice warmth. It’s well-managed warmth, not a heat bloom. If you’re planning marathon transfers on a hot day or in a stuffy hotel room, consider giving the drive a small break instead of squeezing every last MB/s from it in one go.

Thermals aside, reliability during our tests was solid. We didn’t encounter disconnects or drops, and the enclosure’s construction helped prevent any incidental damage during quick mid-flight bag checks. If you’re traveling with sensitive data, you’ll appreciate the peace of mind that the 2TB capacity provides—you can keep a lot of projects offline and ready for the next client call or espresso-fueled editing session.

## Software, features, and compatibility
Crucial provides its Storage Executive software to tune performance, check drive health, and perform secure erases. The software is optional for day-to-day use but handy if you want to monitor SMART-like stats, set thermal limits, or wipe the drive securely before handing it to a colleague or selling it on the second-hand market. For most casual users, connecting the drive and using your operating system’s native file manager is plenty.

Security-wise, the X10 as reviewed here doesn’t emphasize hardware-based encryption as a primary feature. If you require hardware encryption for sensitive data, make sure to verify the exact model and capabilities on Crucial’s official product page or look at Crucial’s Storage Executive for software-based erasure and encryption options. Always back up your data in at least two places; portable SSDs are excellent, but they’re not a magical vault that makes legal documents appear out of thin air.

Compatibility is broad. Windows, macOS, and Linux machines all work well with the X10 (formatting can affect availability of the full 2TB; for Mac users, APFS or exFAT is a common choice depending on whether you need Windows cross-compatibility). If you’re using a modern laptop with USB-C, you’ll have the best experience; if you’re on USB-A, you’ll still get excellent speeds, just not the peak numbers you see with USB-C Gen 2x2.

In practice, we noticed that the drive’s performance scales well with a good USB-C port and a capable cable. Subpar cables or hubs can bottleneck even the best hardware, so if you want to maximize speeds, pair the X10 with a direct USB-C connection or a quality USB-C hub that explicitly supports USB 3.2 Gen 2x2.
