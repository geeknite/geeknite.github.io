## Design and Build Quality

### Blade and Geometry

The blade is a full length, robust design made from 7Cr13 stainless steel. In budget blade talk, 7Cr13 is the friendly budget steel that gets the job done without demanding a queen's ransom for sharpening equipment. It is corrosion resistant enough for everyday carry and can take a fairly decent edge with the right set of stones or a sharpening system. The geometry on this dagger favors practicality over flamboyance. The edge geometry is something like a robust working edge, well suited for slicing through packing tape, cleaning vegetables in the field, or slicing cardboard with a measured, measured swagger. If you are into hardcore WYSIWYG testing, you will notice that the edge holds reasonably well for light tasks, but you should not expect it to outperform your high-end steel kitchen knives. In short: it is a budget steel that gets the job done with a reasonable amount of discipline and polite maintainance.

For steel nerds, the 7Cr13MoV family is known for a balance between hardness and toughness. In practice, you can expect an HRC range roughly in the 50s for budget components. This means easy sharpening, decent edge retention for daily tasks, but do not expect it to outperform high-end blades. ## Field Testing and Real-World Use

This is where we separate marketing fluff from practical reality. The evaluation here covers daily tasks, occasional chores in intermediate environments, and the occasional cosplay-related test that we like to do for the sake of science and entertainment.

### Cutting Capability and Edge Retention

In practical terms, the Legionary dagger is not your kitchen knife. It is a tool built for heavier tasks and occasional piercing or chopping within reason. The 7Cr13 stainless steel accepts a sharp edge and holds it well enough for routine tasks like cutting cardboard, opening packages, slicing cordage, and other light to moderate tasks that a dagger might be pressed into during camping trips, survival simulations, or adult cosplay sessions. Edge retention for this steel is not on par with premium alloys, but it is respectable for its class. With regular maintenance — honing with ceramic rods, light stropping, and occasional oiling of the blade to prevent corrosion — you can keep the edge serviceable for a long time. If you push the blade into demanding tasks, you will notice the edge dulling a bit faster than you might like, but that is to be expected given the steel grade. A good practice is to treat this dagger as a reliable tool for daily tasks rather than a racehorse built for heavy chopping.

Durability here means how well the blade holds up to honest use and how well the leather sheath handles occasional dampness and heat.
