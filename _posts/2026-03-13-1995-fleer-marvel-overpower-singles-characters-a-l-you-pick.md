## The A-L You Pick Concept: A quick refresher and why it matters

OverPower’s charm lay in its knack for letting players assemble a crew of heroes and villains who could outsmart, outfight, and out-narrate each other in a single, glorious duel. The Singles concept—cards you could snag individually rather than as part of a booster pack—was a treasure hunt: you didn’t just want the strongest card; you wanted the right card for your personal theme, your playstyle, and the dramatic moment when your opponent realized you were about to pull off a combo they didn’t see coming.

Focusing on A through L keeps the discussion tidy, but it doesn’t strip away the chaos. It simply organizes the chaos into bite-sized nerd-snack portions. For the purposes of this guide, assume that you’re sampling a cross-section of iconic characters from both sides of the aisle who were commonly found as singles, or at least navigated with some expected synergy in the mid-90s meta. It’s also worth noting: in many playgroups, “you pick” meant a lot of interpretation and house rules—because the only thing more powerful than a character card was a player’s confidence in their ability to bluff an opponent about the power level of their collection.

## A group to anchor your strategy: A, B, C, D, E, F, G, H, I, J, K, L

Here, we’ll walk through representative characters whose names begin with each letter, talk about why they mattered, what they could contribute to a team, and what a reasonable “you pick” build might look like with them as anchors. This is not a universal roster—OverPower had a sprawling card pool, and your local playgroup might have leaned on different favorites—but it’s a solid map for navigating A-L Singles with a smile.

### A: Archangel, Apocalypse, Ant-Man, Angel

Archangel and Apocalypse are heavy-hitters with big-lift vibes; Archangel brings mobility and potent aerial tricks, while Apocalypse can impose threats that force your opponent to react rather than plan. Ant-Man adds trickery with size-shifting options, which in some game states translates into nimble hits and surprising blockades. Angel (Warren Worthington III) is more of a flavor pick but a valid one if your deck wants to emphasize flight, multi-angle assaults, and the melodramatic reveal of wings mid-battle. If you’re building around A, you’re chasing a theme: big presence with ability to pivot and disrupt.

### B: Black Panther, Black Widow, Blade, Bishop

Black Panther isn’t just royal flourish; his synergy in old OverPower sets often came from stacking a few quick, clean strikes and leveraging tech or leadership-like abilities. Black Widow adds a tactical edge with a hint of glass-cutting strategy—she’s the “hit-and-run” archetype in card form. Blade, the half-vampire hunter, embodies versatility—stamina under pressure and the ability to close in on a foe with a few clean blows. Bishop, an X-Men staple, could provide future-vision-style board control and synergy with energy-based attacks. A “B” lineup gives you speed, precision, and a few surprises to keep opponents off-balance.

### C: Captain America, Cyclops, Colossus, Captain Marvel

Captain America is the classic “lead the squad” card: morale boosts, leadership moves, and the courage to stand in the front line. Cyclops offers ranged control and disciplined firepower; Colossus provides bulk, presence, and a momentum swing when the timing is right. Captain Marvel (Carol Danvers, depending on your era) can bring a cosmic punch and flexible power usage. A C-team in OverPower is about strong, reliable centerpieces with slight edge-case options, letting you lock down space on the board while your other picks do the heavy lifting.

### D: Daredevil, Doctor Doom, Deadpool

Daredevil’s blind-sight style translates well into a reactive, counter-punch approach—great when you want to force your opponent into suboptimal spaces. Doctor Doom is a classic “go big or go home” spellcaster of sorts: he seeks to bend the rules to his advantage and impose fate on the arena. Deadpool is the wild card—often chaotic, funny, and surprisingly efficient when you lean into the humor and misdirection of the character. A D-strategy is all about daring plays, counterplay, and a few moments where the crowd of spectators (your friends) gasps at the improbable turn of events.

### E: Elektra, Emma Frost, Enchantress

Elektra is all about speed and aggression, a surgical strike artist who can close gaps with style. Emma Frost brings mind games and psychic support that can tilt the field in your favor. Enchantress adds mystique and magical misdirection—if your group enjoyed the magical side of the Marvel universe in OverPower, she’s a thematic fit for a mood deck with a dash of misdirection.

### F: Fantastic Four (team-flux), Firestar, Falcon

The F-bar in OverPower often meant “team synergy or versatility.” The Fantastic Four were a team archetype in many late-90s card games, offering balance and a built-in team vibe. Firestar gives warmth and speed; Falcon adds aerial pressure and mobility. A practical F-choices approach is to lean into flexible positioning and catch-your-breath turns that force your opponent to reevaluate their approach mid-game.

### G: Gambit, Ghost Rider, Goblin

Gambit is quintessential charm and cunning—edge-of-your-seat bluffing with the added spice of trick moves. Ghost Rider offers dark, motor-y momentum with a touch of fearsome aura that makes opponents hesitate. Goblin (Green Goblin variants) ups the chaotic factor, letting you push for unpredictable explosive plays. G-picks are about tricking the opponent into misreading your intent and then seizing the moment for a dramatic finish.

### H: Hulk, Hawkeye, Human Torch, He-Man (ok, not Marvel—just kidding, he doesn’t count here)

Hulk is your tanks-and-crash-bash archetype: big power, shock-and-awe presence. Hawkeye is precise, patient, and great when you can set up a multi-turn plan. Human Torch (Johnny Storm) adds burn-and-blast synergy, which can retire a foe if you manage your heat counters well. If you want a heavy hitter with a splash of flair, H has you covered.

### I: Iron Man, Iceman, Invisible Woman

Iron Man is mission-control—tech, upgrades, and the sense that you’re modifying your deck as you go. Iceman can freeze the tempo and provide a defensive backbone when your opponent tries to force a sprint. Invisible Woman layers misdirection and surprise, letting you control tempo in ways the other letters can only dream of. I-characters typically shine when you like long-term planning and a little flair with your defenses.

### J: Juggernaut, Jubilee

Juggernaut is about unstoppable force meets immovable object, which means you’re either meeting a wall or breaking through one. Jubilee adds a spark of energy and some clever burn mechanics—she can be satisfying in a deck that wants a flashy finisher. J-picks are about raw, decisive moments and a hint of chaotic fun that makes your opponent question whether they should have brought more countermeasures to the party.

### K: Kang, Kingpin, Kraven

Kang can bend space and time in your mental model of the game—great for decks that want to feel like they’re bending the battlefield to their will. Kingpin is a classic control captain, stopping or steering key actions. Kraven the Hunter adds precision and pursuit—great when you like a chase storyline in your game. A K-rotation is your pressure deck with a villainous vibe, driving your opponent to adapt or concede before the mid-game.

### L: Loki, Luke Cage, Loki’s clones (okay, that one’s a nerdy exaggeration for fun), Lilandra if you’re feeling fancy

Loki brings mischief and misdirection, a delightful antagonist in any card pool. Luke Cage offers sturdy presence and protection—perfect for a tank-heavy line. If you’re lucky enough to have a Lilandra or a similar option, you get a royal-blood vibe plus some space-ops flavor. L-picks tend to be about charismatic leadership and a little magical mischief that keeps your opponent guessing.

> Important note: your local set may have more or fewer options under each letter, and not every letter will deliver the same punch in the same meta. The goal here is to give you a flavor map you can rely on when you see a single you want to nab at a convention, a thrift store, or a friend’s desk swap.

## Character Spotlight: A few picks that consistently felt worth the gamble

### Archangel: The wings, the reach, the trickery
Archangel is a showcase of mobility and reach. In many OverPower games, being able to strike from a distance and still retaliate when someone closes in is huge. Archangel’s presence on the board forces your opponent to respect your aerial lane, and if you pair him with a second attacker who can threaten the same square, you create a pressure cooker that’s a nightmare to navigate. The card’s flavor text—grace in flight with a steel hide—also makes for excellent storytelling when you narrate your turns to your gaming group.

### Captain America: Leadership on a board
Captain America often acts like the team’s frontman. When you play him, you expect him to help align actions and set a tempo that favors your plan. A well-timed shield or morale boost in a critical moment can turn a trading blow into a decisive swing. The joy comes when you feel like you’re steering the entire field with a character who embodies “team first.” It’s the same energy you get from a well-timed captain’s speech before a big team challenge—minus the pep rally, plus a couple of creative card interactions.

### Gambit: Style and strike
Gambit’s charm is not just his trench-coat swagger; it’s the thrill of landing a move that feels inevitable once you’ve built up the right card synergy. He’s a reminder that the game rewards cunning as much as raw power. When you set him up with a couple of “hidden information” tools—whether from your deck or your opponent’s misreadings—Gambit can turn the table with a single, perfectly-timed gambit.

### Iron Man: The build-your-arc reactor approach
Iron Man is a thinking player’s dream: you start with a baseline setup, then, as the game evolves, you tinker, upgrade, and adjust your approach. The joy of Iron Man in OverPower comes from the sense that you’re not just dispatching threats; you’re curating a whole tactical environment around your suit’s capabilities. If you’re a deck-builder at heart, Iron Man gives you “portfolio management” vibes in a card game format—minus the quarterly reports.

### Loki: Misdirection embodied
Loki is pure misdirection in card form. When your opponent expects a strong front line, Loki can slip in a trick or two that forces recalibration. The key with Loki isn’t to lean entirely into chaos, but to weave misdirection with a credible threat so that the opponent never feels sure about their next move. It’s theatre in a deck, and that’s half the fun of the game.

## The mechanics you need to know for an A-L You Pick run

If you’re assembling a personal “You Pick” deck from A-L singles, here are a few practical tips that apply regardless of your chosen characters and the exact meta of your table:

- Balance power with board control: You don’t want to stack only high-damage cards if you’ll have trouble preventing your opponent’s big plays from landing. Find a comfortable mix of blockers, defenders, and pace-setters.
- Look for synergy in pairings: Some characters shine when paired with specific other cards that complement their abilities. If Archangel can threaten space, pair him with a support that punishes overextension.
- Don’t underestimate speed: Early tempo matters in many OverPower matches. If you can secure a couple of quick hits before your opponent establishes control, you’ll gain momentum that’s hard to reverse.
- Manage resources: If your singles come with extra effects that require energy or counters, plan ahead. A well-timed resource spike can turn a near-miss into a confident win.
- Narrate your move choices: The best nights aren’t only about who wins; they’re about how the story unfolds. A good narrative makes the table light up and encourages future game nights.

## Mechanics-and-memory: a quick cheat sheet for A-L singles
- A to L often gives you a cross-section of heroics and trickery, which means your deck might lean more toward mid-range control rather than a pure “all damage” vibe.
- Expect a mix of ranged and melee threats; a few flyers and a handful of ground-pounders help keep your board flexible.
- Expect some duplicates among the best singles; in a casual setting, you’ll often end up trading for two or three of your favorite picks, so plan for swaps.
- If your playgroup allows, you can spice up the zone control by using elevation and terrain-like effects that some non-core cards bring in; this adds flavor without breaking the core engine.

## Collectibility, condition, and value (1990s nostalgia with a price tag attached)
The value of OverPower singles isn’t purely a numbers game—it’s about the story they tell, the friends you played with, and the memories of discovering a “holy grail” card at a yard sale. That being said, condition matters for serious collectors. Mint or near-mint singles in crisp sleeves hold up best for long-term display, while a few well-loved cards with signs of wear still carry story-value that can’t be quantified by price guides alone.

If you’re scouting for A-L singles today, you’ll likely encounter a spectrum: some common picks that remain affordable, a handful of mid-tier cards that have a dedicated crowd, and a few hard-to-find gems that can fetch a premium on the right day. The joy of retro gaming is often less about the exact market value and more about the stories you’ll tell your future self about the night you pulled a rare from a thrift-store grab bag.

## Getting started today: where the nostalgia highway meets modern life

If you’re itching to recreate or build around A-L Singles, here are practical steps you can take, whether you’re playing solo reminiscence mode or gathering a lively group:

- Inventory what you already own: a quick binder scan can reveal which letters you’re strongest with and which gaps you want to fill.
- Decide your play style: do you want a heavy-hitter all-star lineup or a cunning deck built on misdirection? Your personal preference will guide which singles to prioritize.
- Search widely but smartly: local gaming shops, flea markets, estate sales, and online communities often yield the best finds for old OverPower singles. Don’t forget about listing photos—look for creases, edge wear, and surface scratches to gauge condition.
- Bundle and barter: singles are great for swap nights. Propose two-for-one trades or a small assortment deal to sweeten the deal for both sides—this is where the social fun of nostalgia trading shines.
- Preserve and display: once you’ve found your favorite A-L lineup, sleeve them, label them, and give them a home in a small, kid-friendly book or a repurposed photo album. It makes the collection feel tangible and approachable for future game nights.

## Final verdict: should you chase A-L singles for OverPower today?

If you’re asking whether an A-L singles collection is worth your time, the answer is yes if you’re chasing three things: memory, character-driven play, and the cross-genre joy of a board game that feels like a tactile comic book. The A-L subset is a great place to start because it represents a broad swath of character archetypes—leaders, tricksters, tanks, and aerial specialists—without forcing you into a single meta. You’ll find that some cards become emotional anchors: the ones that delivered a dramatic win, the ones you traded away regretfully but with a triumphant grin, and the ones that simply looked cool enough to frame on your wall.

If you’re a collector who values the “feel” of the era—the glossy art, the bold typography, the grainy photos of superheroes mid-battle—A-L singles provide a satisfying sampler. If you’re a player, you’ll appreciate the variety and the chance to craft a themed deck around your favorites. And if you’re both, you’ll enjoy the cycle of chasing a perfect combo and then laughing about how your “perfect” plan fell apart to a single unexpected card draw from your opponent.
