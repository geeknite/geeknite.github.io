## Features and Tech: The Screen Factor
### The W Screen Experience
The product name hints at something special: built-in screens. The Eternal 6 Person Workstation with Screen ships with two ready-to-go displays as part of the modular core. The screens are not gigantic cinema setups; think two 24–27 inch panels, calibrated for office tasks, code, memos, and the occasional multi-tab browser marathon. The screens sit on adjustable arms that glide smoothly; you can angle them to avoid glare or to stage a dramatic reveal of quarterly results during a stand-up meeting.

If you’re worried about screen quality, these aren’t budget office panels. They offer decent color accuracy, comfortable brightness, and reasonably punchy contrast for white slides that have too much white. In practice, you’ll likely swap these for nicer displays once you realize that the built-in panels are more about convenience than state-of-the-art cinema. Still, for a six-person station, two screens are a clever, space-saving compromise that reduces chair swiveling to fetch a monitor from the IT closet.

### Integrated Cable Management and Power
Cable management is the unsung hero in any shared workstation. The Eternal desk hides power strips and data lines in dedicated channels with magnetic covers. It’s not perfect—no cable management is. But it does a better job than most at keeping cables from acting like mischievous spaghetti on your carpet. The power distribution unit (PDU) is modular, allowing IT or facilities to swap in more robust options if you’re running six desktops, six chargers, and a small weather station on the same loop.

### Accessories and Customization
The modular system ships with pre-routed tracks, optional monitor arms, and even a couple of tool-less grommets for quick, upgrade-friendly customization. You’ll find attachments for a shared whiteboard, a central charging hub, and a small conference call speaker near the ends. It’s not all glitz—there’s a practical core here: you can reconfigure the desk layout without disassembling the entire thing. ## Setup, Assembly, and Real-World Use
### Unboxing and Assembly Experience
Let’s be real: furniture assembly is a rite of passage that tests your patience and your ability to maintain a neutral facial expression while muttering under your breath. The Eternal 6 Person Desk arrives in a few large boxes, each labeled with friendly icons that look suspiciously like a treasure map to a hex bolt treasure chest. The instruction booklet is clear—mostly—and the hardware is thankfully color-coded. The assembly is a two-person job for the core frame and the central console; one person can handle it solo with enough coffee and a YouTube tutorial about “how to fasten 8 screws in under 3 minutes.”

The key steps include anchoring the modular legs to the central spine, attaching the panel dividers, hooking up the power and data channels, and finally mounting the built-in screens. The two-hour estimate from the manual is optimistic for a first-timer; plan for a longer session if you’re trying to navigate the mystery of the three remaining screws that seem to be smiling at you from the hardware bag.

### Space Planning and Layout Options
Because it’s a six-person setup with two screens, you’ll want to think about the room shape and workflow. The Eternal desk can be arranged in a straight line, a U-shape, or a “two islands” configuration where two pairs of teammates share a screen, while others work on tasks that don’t require constant eye contact with the monitor. For stand-ups, the central panel acts as a shared hub, and you can position it to face the entire team so that every joke lands with maximum impact and minimum elbowing.

If you run a design studio or a game-dev squad, the layout becomes a creative instrument. The central core can host a shared reference board while the outer wings hold personal monitors. It’s almost like you’ve built a cockpit where every pilot gets a seat and the copilot gets the best view of the battery indicator.

### Ergonomics in Practice
We spent a week with a team of six using this desk, alternating tasks from coding to graphic design to video calls. The ergonomics hold up—though you’ll want to calibrate each chair’s height and the desk’s central panel to ensure comfortable wrist angles and shoulder relaxation. The built-in screens reduce neck strain by letting you align the screens to your line of sight instead of craning forward to see a distant monitor perched on a shelf. The keyboard trays are decently sized, offering room for a compact mechanical keyboard and a sensible mouse setup.

The challenge remains: with six people in close proximity, personal space is a precious commodity. It’s easier to stare at your own screen when the desk isn’t a double-decker bus with everyone’s knees sharing the same aisle. A little courtesy goes a long way—adjust your chair height, mind your elbow etiquette, and consider rotating seating occasionally to keep morale high.

## Maintenance, Durability, and Cleanliness
### Daily Care
Oak White finishes look stellar when they’re clean, but they show fingerprints and coffee mug rings with the same enthusiasm of a caffeinated raccoon. The recommended cleaning regimen is simple: a damp microfiber cloth, a mild wood-safe cleaner, and a gentle wipe-down of the screen bezels. Avoid abrasive agents that pretend to be coffee grinders but are actually sandpaper in disguise. The built-in screens are sealed from dust to a reasonable degree, but your desk should be a regular victim of keyboard crumbs and dust bunnies in the corners. Vacuuming under the desk every few weeks is a good practice; trust us, you don’t want a dust bunny uprising when you’re presenting quarterly results.

### Durability Checks
The dual-workstation module holds up well under repeated adjustments and the occasional “team huddle” of laptops. The metal frame is sturdy; there’s a reassuring heft to the keyboard tray when you slide it out. The cable channels stay closed, even with all five chargers and a power strip demanding their due. If you’re planning to move office furniture, ensure you have a two-person lift or a proper dolly—this thing isn’t a featherweight champion, but it’s also not a boulder you can roll with a single hand.
