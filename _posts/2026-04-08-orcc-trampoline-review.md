Setting up a trampoline is a rite of passage—the adult version of assembling a big LEGO kit where the pieces are heavy and the instructions are written in the language of parental bribes. Here’s what to expect when you unbox an ORCC trampoline:

## Safety features and net enclosure: the guardrails of gravity
Safety is why many families choose a trampoline in the first place, aside from the ability to pretend you’re a gymnast while chasing after a runaway toddler. ORCC’s enclosure net plus padded rails provide a buffer zone that reduces the risk of a tumble off the edge. Here are the key safety features to consider:
- High enclosure height: designed to keep jumpers inside the play area as you bounce with enthusiasm.
- Zipper entry: a controlled way to enter and exit, reducing the chances of accidental escapes mid-session.
- Cushioning: the protective foam around the frame minimizes impact with the metal frame if a bounce gets out of hand.
- Ground clearance: proper installation ensures you won’t drill into the grass or the patios with your toes or head during off-axis hops.

Of course safety also depends on user behavior. The best safety policies include:
- No flips near the edge unless you’ve got a wide open space and adult supervision.
- Shoes off, socks on, nothing sharp in pockets that could poke through padding.
- A cap on roughhousing during windy weather; a trampoline can become a surprisingly good wind sail if you’re not paying attention.
- Regular inspections of springs and padding; replace anything worn or damaged before use.

## Durability, weather resistance, and daily life with a bounce
A trampoline is a long-term investment in your family’s wellness and your property’s resale value only if you actually use it. ORCC tends to emphasize durability and weather resistance, which matters when your trampoline sits outside year-round. Here are the big durability points:

- Powder-coated frame: designed to resist rust and corrosion in a damp climate or a neighborhood with high humidity.
- Weather-sealed padding: keeps the protective foam from absorbing water and soaking like a sponge after a rainstorm.
- Easy maintenance: removable safety pads allow periodic inspection of springs and frame, and you can wipe down surfaces with a mild cleaner to keep things hygienic.

In practice, a well-maintained ORCC trampoline will outlive cheaper models by several seasons. The key is a simple annual check-in: inspect springs for signs of fatigue, ensure the enclosure net is taut, and re-tighten any hardware that may have loosened with time and weather. If you live in an area with heavy snowfall, consider dismantling and storing the frame during the winter, or at least cover it to prevent ice buildup on the mats and nets—trust us, ice on a trampoline is a whole new level of physics problem.

## Maintenance and longevity: tips from the field
- Regular inspections: check springs, frame seams, and net anchors. Replace worn parts promptly to prevent a cascading failure.
- Cleaning routine: a simple wipe-down with mild soap and water; avoid harsh chemicals that might degrade padding or mat materials.
- Storage strategy: use a waterproof cover during rain, wind, or long gaps in use; keep the area clear of debris that could snag the net or padding.
- Weather considerations: extreme heat can degrade foam padding over time, while heavy snow can add weight to the frame. Plan accordingly.

Your trampoline is a long-term investment in family fun, but it’s also a neighborly investment in community aesthetics. A tidy, well-maintained setup communicates that you care about safety and space, not just your next big bounce.
