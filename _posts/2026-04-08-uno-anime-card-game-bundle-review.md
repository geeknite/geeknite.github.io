## How to Play (Basics)
UNO’s core loop is simple: color-match and number-match to get rid of your hand. The Anime Bundle respects that backbone but dresses it up with a few thematic twists. Here’s a quick primer so you can start games the moment the players sit down with chips, soda, and questionable life choices.

- The goal remains the same: be the first to get rid of all your cards and shout UNO. If you forget to shout UNO, you might face a playful penalty card, typically a mild stinging sensation of embarrassment.
- Turn order is clockwise, unless you’re playing with a house rule that makes everyone randomly switch directions like a spontaneous plot twist.
- On your turn, you can either play a card that matches color or number or use a special action card to shake the table in just the right way.
- The anime-themed action cards give you extra options like swapping hands (a friendly nod to team-based arcs) or forcing opponents to draw cards when the rivalry hits a climactic moment. These cards are intentionally not overpowered; they’re designed to provide a spark that keeps rounds interesting without tipping the balance too far.
- When you have one card left, you must shout UNO. If you forget, the penalty is usually one additional card drawn by you or the other players. Humor ensues.

What changes here compared to classic UNO are the occasional character-specific effects and a few new card types that nod to anime tropes. The rules stay approachable, making it friendly for families, college roommates, and the friend who always wins by memorizing the exact number of cards you’ve drawn in the last five rounds.

### Card Types and Variants
The deck introduces several new card archetypes that feel natural in the anime crossover world:

- Character Cards: These are face cards that feature a hero or villain from the anime-inspired roster. They trigger unique effects when played, such as letting you peek at the top card or forcing an opponent to skip a turn in a dramatic fashion.
- Signature Move Cards: Think of these as special abilities tied to the anime vibe. They can turn the tide in surprising ways, but they’re balanced so that no single card entry becomes a guaranteed win in one round.
- Mentor Cards: A limited number of mentor cards serve as rare draw replacements or hand protections. They’re like “Kirito’s Sword” moment in a board game form—fun to pull, but not game-breaking.
- Ability-Boost Cards: These act as mini power-ups, letting you redraw fewer cards than you’d normally draw or swap up to two cards with another player under a constraint that keeps the game moving.

In practice, these new types add a small strategic layer without turning UNO into a brain-scratcher. If you’re a person who thrives on deeper meta-games, you might appreciate how these cards reward memory and sequencing without requiring a PhD in card mathematics.

### House Rules and Variants
UNO has always thrived on house rules, and the Anime Bundle invites a few light adjustments rather than a complete overhaul. A few that work well here:
- The “Skip and Swap” rule: If you have a character card that allows you to swap hands, you can do so with a “mentor-charm” twist if you reveal a certain combination of cards.
- The “Power-Flip” variant: If the draw pile reaches a certain length, you can declare a dramatic anime-style power move that reorders the top few cards in the pile. It creates a memorable moment without derailing the core loop.
- The “Tag-Team UNO” rule: In teams of two, players can share a single hand and coordinate their plays. This is a blast for party nights and makes you feel like you’re in a squad anime arc rather than a one-person hero saga.
