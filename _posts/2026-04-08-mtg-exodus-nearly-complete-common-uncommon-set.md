## Exodus in Context
Exodus was released toward the tail end of the Ice Age era, riding the wave of artifact synergy while leaning into the multicolor strategy that defined so many casual decks of the time. It wasn’t the flashiest set in terms of rare chase cards, but it mattered a lot for players who loved tweaking lists, testing color combinations, and maximizing synergy between artifact support and late-game plays.

For collectors, Exodus provides a fascinating snapshot of late-90s MTG printing: card stock that feels sturdier in some cases and a design language that sometimes looks like it was sketched during a lunch break by someone who really loved color pairs and mana acceleration. The Commons and Uncommons tend to reflect the core mechanics of the set—ramp, removal, card draw, and cheap threats—without the drama and price tag of the big rares. It’s the good-neighbor option of MTG collecting: not the loudest, but always there when you need a dependable pickup.

In a budget-minded collector’s plan, the commons give you creatures and landscapes to build a coherent early-game plan, while the uncommons push into threat density and control elements that can keep games interesting without forcing you to buy into a rare-price escalation.

## The Common/Uncommon Subset: Why It Matters
Why focus on commons and uncommons? Because these are the true “bread and butter” of MTG collection hobbies that are actually approachable for a real person with a life and a budget. Here’s why the nearly complete common/uncommon Exodus set is a gem:

- Accessibility: Commons and uncommons tend to be the least expensive cards in the set. You can build a significant piece of your collection without delving into the stratosphere of rare cards. It’s the collectible version of a comfortable hoodie—cozy, familiar, and useful in daily life.
- Playability: Those low-cost cards are often the most used in casual formats, budget cubes, or theme decks. A near-complete commons/uncommons collection can provide you with reliable options for a wide variety of colors and archetypes without breaking the bank.
- Local game-store gold: If you own a binder full of near-miss commons, you’re likely to see people digging through your collection to pull commons they’re missing from a trade pile. This increases the value of your set in practical terms: you can trade up for a few rares or a single standout uncommon that truly helps your deck shine.
- The joy of completion: The satisfaction of filling every last common and uncommon slot is underrated. It’s not about dropping $1,000 on a single chrome foil; it’s about looking at your binder and thinking, “Yep, this is a complete little map of Exodus in a way that makes sense to me.”

In short, the common/uncommon subset is the perfect blend of practical, affordable, and nerdy. It’s where most players can enjoy progress—equipment that shows your discipline and your taste without forcing you to mortgage your life savings for a single card with the same print run as a small country.

## What to Expect in Exodus Commons and Uncommons
Rather than listing card names (which is risky without a precise checklist and easy to misremember), here’s a practical map of what you’re typically prioritizing when building a near-complete Exodus common/uncommon collection:

- Early-game bodies: Expect efficient, two-mana or less creatures that help you stabilize the board or pressure your opponent. In common and uncommon slots, these are the bread-and-butter contributors to any forgiving multiplayer game. They’re not flashy, but they’re reliable and often underappreciated.
- Removal at budget costs: You’ll find a reasonable slice of removal spells that hit creatures or players without costing a premium. Some uncommons are strong enough to anchor a casual deck, especially when paired with solid hand disruption or targeted answers against a variety of threats.
- Card draw and filter: Exodus had cards that gave you extra draws or filtering options at a fair cost. In a near-complete set, these can be the difference between replacing a dead card and pulling the exact play you need to swing tempo.
- Color balance and mana efficiency: You’ll notice a lot of color pairs and a reasonable distribution of color identities. The set rewards players who can leverage mana efficiency, but it’s not so punishing that casual players can’t enjoy it.
- Artifact support: The artifact theme is a throughline of the era, and the common/uncommon slots often show the more practical, non-bling components of that theme. You’ll get artifact creatures, utility artifacts, and a handful of colorless options that slot into multi-color strategies with ease.

If you’re chasing a plan, a simple starter approach is to think in terms of a three-tier ladder: reliable early plays (commons), flexible or removal-oriented tools (uncommons), and a few standout rare-gap-fillers that you might snag later if you’re building a more ambitious deck. The commons set you up; the uncommons push you forward; the rares are the exclamation point you trade up to when you’re ready for the fireworks.

## The Card Quality and Grading Reality
When you’re building a near-complete common/uncommon Exodus pool, you’ll encounter typical printing quirks: white borders on some reprints, minor misprints, and the occasional misaligned artwork. The beauty of focusing on commons and uncommons is that you can ignore most grading drama. Your goal is a solid binder with evenly centered cards, minor corner wear, and clean borders. As long as you don’t mind a little wear on the margins (which is common for cards of that era), you can curate a collection that’s fun to flip through and pleasant to show off.

In practical terms, consider a grading mindset like this: for Commons, accept light corner wear and a few surface marks if the card is otherwise legible and color-true. For Uncommons, aim for better centering and fewer blemishes—these are the “workhorse” cards of your set, and they’ll earn more looks from potential trades and casual players than a battered common will. The game thrives on playable cards that look nice in a binder and play nicely on the table.

## Practical Tips for Building Your Near-Complete Commons/Uncommons
If you’re ready to embark on the near-complete Exodus commons/uncommons journey, here are some actionable tips to help you stay sane and get results without emptying your savings:

- Start with a checklist: Create a simple spreadsheet or binder page that lists every common and uncommon you want. Mark off what you have and what you need. It’s painfully satisfying to fill a checkbox with a firm “DONE.”
- Bulk buys for gaps: If you’re missing chunks, look for bulk buys or sealed product remnants that yield a bunch of commons/uncommons at once. You’ll be surprised how quickly those holes close when you stop chasing specific cards and focus on ranges.
- Condition-aware trades: When you trade, emphasize condition and the near-complete nature of your collection. People love someone who is clearly organized and has a clear plan. A binder full of sorted hits beats a chaotic pile of expectations any day.
- The art of discount pairing: If you’re buying both a common and an uncommon card that share thematic synergy, you’ll stretch your budget and create a cohesive mini-deck idea for casual play.
- Language and printing variants: Exodus exists in multiple printings and languages. If you’re language-flexible, you can broaden your pool without splurging. If you’re collecting strictly English, keep that constraint in your notes and trade plans.
- Use online catalogs for cross-checks: Keep a local copy of your checklist and use online catalogs to verify printing codes and set data. This reduces “oops, I already own this in a different border” moments during trades.

For many, the small-but-focused nature of this project makes it doable in a weekend or two of careful hunting. It’s not glamorous, but it’s incredibly satisfying to walk away with a binder that shows a clear, complete plan—each card telling a tiny story about your journey through Exodus.

## Deck-building and Playability: How to Use Your Near-Complete Set
A near-complete Exodus common/uncommon set isn’t just for show. It’s a practical tool for modern casual play and even for cube-style games with a tight budget. Here are a few ideas to get you started:

- Casual two-color synergy decks: Use the better common-uncommon spells to craft tight two-color decks that lean into artifact support and tempo control. You’ll be surprised how often the simpler cards deliver consistent wins when timed correctly.
- Budget cube seeds: Start a small cube with a majority of commons and uncommons to keep the cost down while preserving the core mechanics and fun of the block. You’ll still get meaningful games that feel like a pro draft with a fraction of the cost.
