## Design and Build: Why Synthetic Rope Matters
The debate between steel cable and synthetic rope has raged since the first rope met a tree in a lost tent city. The Stealth 3500 uses synthetic rope, and there are some practical reasons this choice changes your winching experience.

### Safety and handling
Synthetic rope is dramatically lighter than steel cable. Not only does this make it easier to coil and spool, but it reduces the risk of serious injury when the line under load snaps. That doesn’t mean you should be careless—gloves are still mandatory, and keep your face away from the line when it’s under tension. The rope also floats in water, which makes retrieving a line easier if you’re winching near a riverbank or a flooded campground where the creek has become a highway.

### Spool speed and heat management
A synthetic rope can be spooled without the same risk of whipping that steel cable has. This means you can spool and unspool more confidently, with less risk of kinking. The heat profile on a moderate 3,500-lb winch is manageable for typical two- or three-minute pulls, though you should avoid continuous max-load operation unless you’ve got a robust cooling setup or you’re in a water-filled moment of endurance.

### Longevity and care
Synthetic rope appreciates a bit of care. Rubbing across a sharp rock or a rough edge is bad news; a roller fairlead (as mentioned) is your friend. Keep the rope clean, inspect for signs of wear, and wash off mud periodically. When you’re done, coil it properly to avoid kinks, and store in a dry location away from direct sunlight to preserve color and strength.

## Installation and Fitment: A Guide for the Weekend Mechanic
If you’re mechanically inclined, you can tackle installation with a little planning. If you’re not, bring a friend who is. The goal is to mount a robust, safe system that won’t rattle your teeth on a rough road and then spill its guts in the engine bay.

### Preparations and mount selection
- Confirm your bumper type: ATV, UTV, or trailer hitch mount? The Stealth 3500 is designed to be versatile but requires correct mounting hardware.
- Check the bumper rating: You’ll want a winch rated for your vehicle’s gross vehicle weight and general usage. The last thing you want is a winch that looks strong but shakes itself apart after a single pull.
- Gather the mounting kit: the winch should come with a feet plate, side mounting bolts, and the necessary brackets. You may need longer bolts or a different pattern if your bumper is unusual.
- Electrical considerations: a dedicated fuse and proper gauge wiring to the battery are essential. This isn’t the place for thin wires and a prayer—the winch can draw current in bursts that far exceed what a spaghetti-thin lead can handle.

### The step-by-step process (high-level)
1. Disconnect the vehicle battery to avoid any shocking surprises. Safety first.
2. Remove any existing bumper hardware or winches if you’re upgrading from an older system.
3. Attach the mounting plate to the bumper with the supplied hardware. Tighten to spec and ensure there’s no wobble.
4. Mount the winch body to the plate and secure with the included bolts. Check alignment so the rope will spool straight and not rub a corner.
5. Route the synthetic rope through the fairlead and onto the drum. Tie on the hook with the proper knot or locking mechanism as per the manual.
6. Wire in the control box and remote. Use a proper fuse and a sturdy power source; a dull wire is a dangerous thing to listen to.
7. Test the winch at no-load height to ensure the rope spools evenly and the remote responds. Then load-test with a controlled, light weight to verify function.

### Electronics and safety checks
- Fuse sizing: confirm the fuse matches the manufacturer’s recommendation for the 12V system. It’s not a guess-your-way-through scenario; you’ve got a safety device for a reason.
- Wire gauge: thicker is safer. A typical installation uses a minimum of 6 AWG or larger for the main power cable; you can use heavier gauge if you’ve got a long run or you’re upgrading to a heavier battery.
- Grounds: a solid chassis ground is non-negotiable. A poor ground equals weak performance and possible electrical noise that ruins the remote’s mental stability.

## Safety, Maintenance, and Best Practices
Safety isn’t a sticker on the side of the winch; it’s a daily habit while you play in the dirt with a heavy, loaded mechanism.

- Always wear gloves when handling the rope. Synthetic rope can be unforgiving if it slides through fingers when under load.
- Stand clear of the rope’s path and avoid the direct line of pull when the winch is in operation. A rope under tension can whip and can cause injuries.
- Use a damping or line-cutting approach with a snatch block if you need to redirect the pull. It reduces load on any single point and extends your system’s life.
- Inspect the rope regularly for signs of wear, fray, or glazing. A small nick can become a catastrophe if it’s ignored.
- Keep the remote charged and accessible. If you’re counting on it to rescue the situation, you don’t want a dead remote when it matters most.
