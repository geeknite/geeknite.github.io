## Gameplay Mechanics: The Art of Removing and Replacing Without Losing Your Sanity
Jenga’s core loop is elegant in its simplicity: remove one block from a lower level and place it on the top, continuing until the structure becomes a narrative about your own nerve. The Classic set adheres to the standard rules—no cheating, no meddling with the laws of physics, and no pulling blocks from the top level unless you want to summon a one-man earthquake on your coffee table.

- Start with the tower in its original 18 levels, each level offset to create the classic staggered look.
- On your turn, remove one block from anywhere below the highest completed level. The removed block must be placed on top, completing a new level as you go.
- The level of difficulty increases as the tower rises; fewer blocks on the lower levels mean more dramatic sways and higher stakes.
- If the tower tumbles, you’re out, and the next round begins with someone else to claim the glory—or the comedic value of your misfortune.

Practical tips for first-timers: start by loosening blocks near the edges with small, deliberate twists rather than yanking with all your might. The edges offer the most leverage, but also the most dramatic consequences. If you’re playing with kids, encourage them to describe their “extraction strategy” aloud, turning a tense moment into a running commentary that rival sports analysts would envy.

## Durability and Maintenance: Keeping Gravity in Your Favor
Wooden toys age well when treated with respect. Jenga blocks are no exception. The wood finish holds up to dozens of pulls and returns, and the corners remain relatively snag-free after many rounds of accidental finger gymnastics. A few care tips to maximize longevity:

- Store in the original box or a dedicated organizer to prevent blocks from warping or misplacing.
- Wipe away fingerprints and sweat with a dry cloth to keep the finish looking neat.
- Avoid excess moisture; wooden blocks and water do not mix well, unless you’re reenacting a swamp biome from your D&D campaign.
- If a block starts to feel misshapen, set it aside for a week and let it acclimate to room humidity. It’s a tiny spa day for wood.

Maintenance is minimal, which is exactly what makes Jenga a couch-friendly staple. It’s the kind of game you can bring out after a long day and still feel accomplished about mastering the delicate balance between focus and chaos.

## Strategy or Chaos? The Eternal Debate You Didn’t Know You Needed
Is Jenga a game of strategy or chaos? The answer, like most good debates, is both and neither. Strategy shows up in block selection: removing blocks from the middle often destabilizes the tower less than the edges, and reading the tower’s wobble helps you avoid detonating a chain reaction. Chaos, meanwhile, enters the room in the form of friends who decide to celebrate with a clumsy cheer just as a block is being tapped free.

Here are some practical strategies that balance science with a pinch of theatrical flair:

- Start from the bottom but move toward the center. Edges are high-risk, but removing blocks from the middle of a level can give you more stable leverage for future moves.
- Observe the distribution of weight. When one side of the tower starts to tilt, you’ll want to avoid adding weight there and focus on blocks that rebalance the center of gravity.
- Practice your breathing and micro-movements. A shallow inhale, a slow exhale, and a smooth, deliberate pull can be more effective than any amount of brute force.
- Use a “tap and test” approach. Lightly tap blocks to test looseness before committing to a real pull. If a block moves easily, it’s probably your next move—if not, wait for a safer candidate.

Above all, remember that Jenga rewards patience and a little swagger. The swagger is optional, but the patience is not.
