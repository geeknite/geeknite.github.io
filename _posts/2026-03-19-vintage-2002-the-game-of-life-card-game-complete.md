### Card anatomy: what a life card actually does
Life cards are the heart of the experience. They come in different flavors: money cards, event cards, and action cards. Money cards are straightforward: they pay you or take money away. Event cards introduce mini-scenarios: you win a prize, you miss a bus, you win a silly lottery, or you get a chance to purchase something that matters later. Action cards provide short-term moves—like a quick boost on the track or a one-off event that changes which path you’re on for a turn or two.

The balance between luck and choice is intentionally skewed toward luck. You’re rarely far ahead because you made a perfect strategic choice. Instead, you win because your deck gave you the right mix of luck and timing, which is basically the universe’s way of saying, yes, you exist, and you deserve the jackpot you achieved by accident. This is comfortingly honest for a game about the existential dread of choosing a career path or a mortgage that spans decades.

### The rules in plain-ish English: a practical digest
- Objective: accumulate the most life value by the end of the track.
- Setup: each player gets a starting amount of life chips; the deck is shuffled; players place their markers on the starting space.
- On your turn: draw one life card, resolve its effects, and move forward a number of spaces equal to the value on the card or as dictated by the card’s instruction.
- Cash flow: money cards either increase or reduce your stash. Misfortune cards can hit you for a chunk of cash, which is where the game tests your willingness to embrace budgetary chaos with good humor.
- Endgame: once the track is exhausted, tally up life value from your chips and any unspent assets. The player with the highest total wins.

This is not a heavy strategic title; it’s a storytelling engine with tiny incentives to optimize, which means it’s approachable for non-gamers and party game fans alike. If you’ve got kids, grandparents, teenagers, and a dog who can sit still for 20 minutes without digging up bones, this is a surprisingly shareable bridge game for all ages.
