## How to play (a quick refresher for the seasoned shinobi)
UNO’s core is deceptively simple: match color or number, shout UNO, then try to dump your hand before everyone else. The Naruto edition spices up the meta with five ninja-flavored twists that keep things dynamic without requiring a degree in meteorology to understand the rules.

### Setup
- Shuffle the deck and deal seven cards to each player.
- Place the remainder face down to form the draw pile; flip the top card to start the discard pile.
- Determine who goes first via a quick scroll of fate (or simply the oldest player, if you’re aiming for speed and order). In many households, battle lines are drawn with who-can-yell-UNO-first, so be prepared for some theatrical posturing.

### The new cards and their ninja twists
- Chakra Boost (Wild Draw Four, Naruto-flavored): lets you pick the color to suit your strategy and forces the next player to draw four cards unless they counter with a suitable chakra move.
- Shadow Clone (Split Draw): instead of drawing a single card, you can split four cards between yourself and an opponent, effectively trading a few cards to shift momentum.
- Kunai Attack (Skip): a classic skip card with a ninja backstory that makes the next player lose their turn in a flash of shuriken-style flair.
- Rasengan (Reverse): a reverse card that changes direction and visually resembles a tiny Rasengan spinning on the card edge. The meta shifts in your favor with every Rasengan you play.
- Uchiha Power (Color Change): a wild that looks slick and delivers a dramatic anime pause before you reveal your chosen color.

These aren’t game-breakers, but they do add strategic depth. The key is to time your chakra moves when opponents are hovering near Uno-toast territory (one card left) so you can swing the mood with a well-timed Shadow Clone or Rasengan.

### Basic turn flow
- On your turn, you must match either the number or color of the top discard card, or play a wild card.
- If you can’t play, you draw a card from the draw pile.
- If you have one card left, you must yell “Uno!” (or risk a penalty if another player calls it first—but penalties here feel more like friendly taunts than actual punishment).
- The round ends when a player empties their hand. The other players tally up their remaining cards as points (the usual UNO rules apply, with Naruto flavor adjusting point values to resemble shinobi-style scoring).

As you might suspect, the theme adds a playful layer of strategy. It’s not just about color and numbers; you’re juggling chakra management, positioning, and whether your team’s defensive walls or opponent’s offense is better suited to a specific moment.
