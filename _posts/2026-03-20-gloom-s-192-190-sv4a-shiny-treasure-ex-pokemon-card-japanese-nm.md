### Condition, authenticity, and how to verify a pristine NM example
Authenticity matters more with rarities like SV4a Shiny Treasure ex than with ordinary commons. When evaluating NM, you should scrutinize three areas: corners, edges, and surface. Corner wear is the most telling sign; even a few micro-whites at the tips can drop condition from NM to near-mint or worse. Edges should be crisp, with minimal chipping or whitening along the borders. The surface should reflect a clean holo pattern or foil without major scratches or scuff marks that disrupt the art.

Authenticity checks include inspecting the card stock feel, the logo font consistency, and the foil pattern match to known authentic prints. Japanese prints sometimes have subtle differences in ink depth and gloss compared to English prints, so if you’re chasing originality, don’t rely on memory alone—compare to a verified reference copy or seek professional grading input.

### Care, storage, and display tips for NM Japanese cards
- Sleeves: use standard size card sleeves to protect from fingerprints and scuffs; consider matte sleeves if you worry about glare on display photos.
- Toploaders: place within a rigid top loader to prevent bending. Avoid glass display cases that trap humidity unless you have a humidity-controlled case.
- Binders: store upright in acid-free pages; avoid stacking heavy weights on top of a binder with this card to minimize edge damage.
- Environment: keep away from direct sunlight, heat, and humidity; the glossy surface is sensitive to thermal fluctuations which can warp the foil overlay over time.
