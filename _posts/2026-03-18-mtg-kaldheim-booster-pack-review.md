## Foretell and other set mechanics: what you should actually know
Kaldheim introduces Foretell, a game mechanic that adds a layer of timing and resource management to your games. The core idea is simple: you may exile a spell with Foretell from your hand by paying two generic mana on your turn. Then you can cast that spell later for its Foretell cost, typically at a discount over its normal mana cost. It’s a mechanic designed to reward planning, mana efficiency, and the satisfaction of catching your opponent by surprise with a spell that wasn’t in reach until you needed it most.

In practice, Foretell creates unusual draft decisions. You might draft a stack of early-curve spells with Foretell in mind, knowing you can deliberately “delay” the big payoff while your opponents race to catch up. It also encourages decks that run “two-speed” strategies: one wing full of inexpensive threats, and another wing ready to unleash discounted spells when the moment is right.

Beyond Foretell, Kaldheim leans into its Norse-inspired tribal themes—elves, dwarves, berserkers, and Valkyries appear across various card types. These tribal synergies matter in draft as well as in constructed formats, because the set provides several payoffs for players who lean into theme-building. There are also modal or double-faced card moments that reward attention to both sides of a card, a feature that makes the pack feel like a mini treasure hunt rather than a single card flip.

If you’re into longer-term planning, pack reveals, or synergistic deck-building, Foretell plus tribal lilt gives you a lot of creative space to experiment. It’s not just about pulling the biggest bomb; it’s about building a path from your first common to your late-game surprise that keeps your opponents guessing and your own playbook flexible.

## Draft psychology: opening a Kaldheim pack and staying sane at the table
Draft formats are like chess games played with cards and colorful art. In Kaldheim, the mythic and rare cards demand respect, but the real value often lies in what you can assemble from the commons and uncommons. Here are some practical tips to maximize your experience when drafting Kaldheim boosters:

- Respect Foretell but don’t lean on it as your only plan. You want a curve that flows despite the delay cards. If you ignore early drops entirely, you’ll stall out; if you over-index on Foretell cards, you’ll miss fast finishes.
- Be mindful of color balance. The Norse-inspired design sometimes pulls each color toward a particular type of aggression or ramp. If you force a three-color mana base too early, you risk your deck folding when you can’t hit your third color on turn four.
- Signal wisely. When you pick a strong uncommons or a standout common, you’re not just adding raw cards—you’re telling your neighbors what your draft strategy looks like. Listen to those signals and don’t worry about picking “perfect” for the table. The table will always adjust.
- Value vs. fun. Kaldheim’s cards are often designed to be fun to play, with the best experiences arising from interactions rather than raw power alone. If you draft with a focus on thematic synergy, you’ll likely enjoy more games and avoid the “beat-down ritual” burnout.

Those are the foundational heuristics. The actual experience depends on your local metagame and the other players at your table, but with these guidelines you’ll get more consistent games and fewer “I opened a bomb but had no support” moments.
