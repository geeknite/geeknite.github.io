### Painting and Customization: Getting from plastic to personality
Painting this mini is a fairly forgiving process, which is good news for hobbyists who want a tabletop-ready piece without spending weeks on details. Here’s a practical, no-fluff guide to get you a table-ready Orc Zombie that looks like it belongs in a dungeon and not in a museum.

1) Prime and base coat: Start with a solid anti-shine white or gray primer to give you a clean canvas. A base coat of green for the flesh is a good starting point. If you want a sickly undead hue, aim for a desaturated green with a touch of gray. For a more vibrant necromancer vibe, lean toward a green that has a teal or blue undertone.

2) Flesh tones and shading: Use a midtone green for the base and then shade into the recesses with a darker green or a brown wash. The key is to emphasize the musculature while making the undead nature obvious. A thin black wash can help define lines along the bones and armor edges without washing out the texture.

3) Armor and fabric: For the armor, choose metallic tones—silver, steel, or a patina bronze—and dry brush lightly on edges. If you want a more “curse-forged” look, you can aim for a blackened-metal effect with brown washes to hint at rust and neglect. The fabric parts should contrast with the metal; think muted earth tones to avoid competing with the glow of undead flesh.

4) Teeth, bones, and details: Teeth can be pale ivory; bones can be bone-white with a wash to bring out their cracks. For the eyes, a tiny dot of red or yellow can give a menacing gaze. You don’t need a big color budget here; small touches go a long way.

5) Finishing techniques: A matte coat is your friend. You don’t want your undead to look too glossy—unless you’re going for a “wet death” effect, in which case a gloss over the eyes or teeth can be dramatic. A light dry brush on highlights can lift the silhouette, helping the model pop on the table under a variety of lighting conditions.

For those wondering about speed-painting options, you can achieve a solid tabletop result in a handful of steps. If you’re pressed for time, you can skip heavy shading in favor of a systematic, layered approach that uses a few washes and a couple of dry-brush passes to achieve a respectable table-ready finish in under an evening.
