## What is UNO Flip Card Game and why it exists
UNO Flip is a reimagining of the old favorite that makes you switch sides—literally. The deck is two-sided: one light side with the familiar color cards and action cards, and a dark side that brings new rules when you flip the central deck or when a Flip card is played. The physical flip mechanism is ingenious in its simplicity. Flip a card stack, or flip the entire draw pile, and suddenly a game that felt predictable 30 seconds ago becomes a rollercoaster of strategy and chaos.

For fans of the original UNO, UNO Flip preserves core mechanics: you match either by number or by color, you play action cards that skip, reverse, or force draws, and you keep a running total of near-miss cards as the endgame approaches. What changes is the second side, which has its own color palette and action cards that completely reverse the expected flow of play. And yes, that means you can come from behind in a dramatic, flip-induced blaze of glory, or you can be totally owned by the Flip side if you blithely ignore the flips and keep churning through the light side like a machine.

## How to play UNO Flip: a quick refresher
UNO Flip follows the same basic objective as traditional UNO: be the first player to get rid of all your cards. But there are a few pivotal moments that set UNO Flip apart:

- Each card has a light side and a dark side. The flip changes the color and the available actions.
- There is a Flip Card in the deck that, when played, flips the entire draw pile to reveal the Dark Side or switch back to the Light Side depending on the current state.
- Action cards on both sides come with their own twists, so a +2 on the Light Side might be matched by a +3 on the Dark Side when rules flip mid-game.
- The game ends as soon as a player plays their last card; if that last card causes your opponent’s sagging morale to collapse, you win, and your table is forever grateful (or forever salty, depending on how your night goes).

Setup is straightforward: shuffle the two-sided deck, deal seven cards to each player, place the remaining deck in the center, flip the top card to begin the discard pile, and determine whether you’re starting on Light or Dark side by the initial flip mechanic. The Dark Side’s cards tend to carry more dramatic punishments, so expect a few dramatic gasps when a players’ plan is foiled by a sudden color mismatch or an empire-sized +4 on the opposite side of the board.

## Playing flow and strategy basics
- The core mechanic remains: match color or number. The flip side adds a new layer; players must anticipate when the Dark Side will take control and which cards will be most effective there.
- Flip cards are the key power-ups. A well-timed flip can swing momentum from a languishing table into a chaotic, closing-acts sequence—especially if you can force opponents to draw while you hold a handful of color blocks or wilds for the endgame.
- When you’re on the Dark Side, you’ll want to prioritize cards that keep you in the lead on that side. Color management becomes trickier because you’re navigating two different color wheels and the risk that a card that looks perfect on the Light Side suddenly becomes a misfire when the flip happens.

Play tips:
- Keep one or two wild cards in reserve for Dark Side turns; they’re your reliable rescue parachutes when your color pool dries up.
- Watch the discard pile trends. If the pile is heavy on a particular color, you’ll want to pivot early rather than wait for a crisis moment.
- Don’t neglect the social penalties. The game is not just about winning; it’s about keeping your friends and family engaged long enough to hear their sad misplays once the Flip Side takes control.
