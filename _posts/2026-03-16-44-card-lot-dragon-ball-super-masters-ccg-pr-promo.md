## Card quality and print details
### Card stock, foiling, and corners
The tactile experience matters as much as the visuals. In a 44 card PR promo lot, you’ll want to assess for edge whitening, centering, and corner sharpness. Promo cards often ride the line between premium print runs and standard production. You’ll see variations in stock thickness, foil density, and embossing that can hint at the production batch. Some cards might have micro-foiling that catches the light at increasing angles, while others play it cool with a more matte finish. If you’re aiming for either display value or tournament-ready play, note the balance of visual pop versus durability—the two often trade off in promo communities.

### Authenticity markers and serial numbers
Promotional cards sometimes include subtle indicators to distinguish them from the regular run. Look for age hints, small print details, or foil patterns that align with the era of the promo release. Authenticity checks usually involve close inspection under good light, optional grading services, and cross-referencing with known promo catalogs. If you’re new to this, start with basic checks: exam the card borders for uniformity, verify the foil’s consistency across similar cards in the same batch, and compare the text font with verified promos. For many collectors, the “feel” of the card—the weight, the bend, the gloss—can be a telltale sign of a well-preserved promo or a replacement card from a late-night swap meet.

## Care, storage, and authentication
### Sleeving and binder strategies
Storage matters. Cards that survive the test of time typically survive the test of handling as well. For a 44 card lot, invest in crystal-clear sleeves, rigid top-loaders for larger promos, and a quality binder with labeled dividers. If you’re displaying, consider a sleeve with a micro-cloth finish to cut glare while preserving color fidelity. If you’re stacking for transport, soft interior padding and anti-scratch sleeves help avoid micro-scratches on art-heavy promos.

### Grading and professional services
Grading isn’t mandatory, but it’s a reliable path to liquidity and market confidence. Seek services that specialize in CCG promos and have an established catalog for your specific card game. Provide high-resolution photos that capture holo brightness, edge condition, and any centering anomalies. A professional opinion can be the difference between a confident purchase and a post-purchase regret story you tell to your future self in the hobby shop queue.

### How to avoid fakes
Fake promocards exist, and yes, they can be quite convincing. When shopping this 44 card lot, here are quick checks:

- Compare foil patterns and text with confirmed promo references.
- Check for consistent card stock weight and finish across the lot.
- Verify that any promotional stamps or date marks align with known release windows.
- Request multiple angles for each card to check border consistency and corner sharpness.

If something feels off, it probably is. Trust your instincts and use reference catalogs as your north star.

## Buying guide: what to look for
### Photographs and scans
Ask for high-res images that show front and back, edges, and any damaged areas. A good seller should provide gloss-free shots under neutral lighting to reveal centering and edge wear. If you’re shopping online, request a 360-degree video of the entire lot to spot any subtle inconsistencies—the difference between a “great deal” and a “house suspicion” often hides in the last 5 percent of detail.

### Provenance and packaging
A solid provenance note adds value. If the seller can explain where the lot came from (a tournament prize pool, a convention drop, a special event), it’s a good sign. Packaging matters too: original sleeves, sealed or resealed boxes, or official promo packaging can all add to authenticity. If you can, ask for the exact screenshot or catalog reference that confirms the lot’s composition.
