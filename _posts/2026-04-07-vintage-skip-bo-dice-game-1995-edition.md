## How to play (the core loop)

Before we dive into the specifics, a caveat: there were multiple printings in the 1990s, and house rules proliferated as this game hopped from kitchen tables to garage games and into the hands of collectors. The core loop described here is faithful to the spirit of the 1995 edition and should give you a workable baseline to enjoy a first session. If your copy came with a slightly different rule sheet, you will likely discover a few adjustments in the margins that nonetheless preserve the essential rhythm.

### Setup
- Each player starts with a basic stock pool. Your objective is to deplete this pool before everyone else does. The stash size and how you draw new dice may vary, but the overarching goal remains consistent: minimize the number of dice you have to handle while maximizing the chaos around the table.
- The dice themselves are color coded to visually cue the run sequences you will attempt to assemble.
- A few face cards or reference sheets help you remind yourself of the action options, hand sizes, and how to use discard piles. The box notes may refer to “Skip-Bo style” sequences, but think of them as color-coded stacking with a dash of retro flair.

### Turn structure
- On your turn, you roll a subset of your dice. The exact number to roll depends on your stock and any house rules in play.
- You then use the rolled dice to form sequences or builds that align with the color-coded logic of the game. The rules tend to favor quick, incremental progress rather than long, intricate planning.
- You can set aside or discard a subset of dice to optimize later turns. The art of the game is balancing immediate gains with future possibilities, all with the clock ticking on social time.
- The round continues clockwise until someone clears their stock. Then the next round begins and the chaos resumes with possibly new winning thresholds.

### How to win
- The winning condition is typically to clear your stock pile, or to accumulate a certain number of points, depending on your chosen variant. In the plain vanilla experience, the first player to deplete their stock is declared the winner.
- The thrill comes from the social dynamics: gracious triumphs, mock despair, and the occasional whispered “we all know I shouldn’t have rolled that last die.”

### Quick tips for new players
- You are not obligated to use every die on a single turn. It is better to save a couple of dice for a future turn if the numbers currently align poorly.
- Prioritize completing small sequences early to reduce your stock quicker; this usually pays off in later turns when piles get tight.
- Don’t underestimate the psychological impact of a well-timed discard that blocks an opponent from completing a color run. It’s not just about math; it’s about table talk and timing.
