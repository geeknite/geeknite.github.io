## Painting and Conversion: How to Make It Your Own
### Step by Step Painting Approach
If you are new to the hobby or returning after a long coffee break with the hobby, the Orc Zombie is a forgiving canvas. Start with a primed base in a mid-tone green. A wet blend from forest to swamp yields a nice transition across the muscle groups. Build up the shading in the recessed areas with a darker green or even a blue-green wash to push the shadows deeper. The undead bone details can be done with a pale ivory, then fed with a brown wash to give age and grit. The torn tunic can be highlighted with a desaturated color in order to separate the fabric from the flesh without creating too much visual noise. A final dry brushing on the edges of armor plates and exposed bones will help the model pop under standard gaming lighting.

If you want a more cinematic approach, consider a triadic color scheme with a muted palette for the body, an accent color on the torn cloth, and a metallic hint on any remaining armor pieces. You can also add some subtle banners or trophy bits—like a pointy skull charm from a defeated villain—on the base to imply the miniature’s backstory on the tabletop.

### Color Palettes and Inspiration
Here are three quick palette archetypes you can experiment with. They’re flexible enough to accommodate your personal preference and your table’s lighting:

- Swamp Warlord: Deep emerald body, dark moss shadows, bone white teeth, tattered cloth in a swampy olive. Finishes with a dull matte to keep the undead vibe.
- Nightwatch Grunt: Slate greens with blue-gray shadows, bark brown tunic, bone highlights, and a hint of rust on any metallic surfaces if present.
- Fiendish Glory: Olive greens with yellow-green highlights, corpse gray for bones, and a rusty red accent on the torn cloth to imply a tragic past.

Whichever palette you pick, the key is contrast. The model reads better on the table when there is a clear path for the eye: silhouette first, texture second, details last.

## Practical Painting and Assembly Tips
### First Time Assembly or Repainting
If this is your first full repaint, do not fear the surface texture. You can strip and re-prime in a few quick steps. A good primer will bring out the lines and allow you to apply a new color palette without the paint pooling in every nook. A single coat of sea spray green or olive can serve as a nice base, followed by careful layering and washes to achieve the undead effect. Don’t forget to prime the bone sections with a pale base to create contrast for the teeth and bone. The torn cloth is your chance to add some splash color, but keep it restrained so the undead vibe remains intact. One thing we love about this model is how forgiving it is for speed painting, so you can get a tabletop-ready piece in a single session if you keep your brushwork clean and your plan simple.

### Accessories and Final Touches
Tiny details can elevate a model from good to memorable. Consider adding a small bit of green flock on the base to simulate moss or swampy ground. You can also craft a small torn banner using a scrap of fabric or paper and paint it in a color that ties into your campaign’s faction color. A couple of bone stickers or a little metallic rust on the weapon edge can add texture without making the model look cluttered. The goal is not to be flashy but to tell a story through the miniature’s appearance. Remember, the greater the contrast between the undead flesh and the bone, the more dramatic the look on the table will be under standard gaming lighting.

### Resin and Handling Tips
If the figure is resin, handle with care during painting and assembly. Warm water and a gentle scrub with a soft brush will help to remove any leftover mold lines, and a brief soak in mild soapy water can help reduce the risk of paint peeling later on. If you plan to glue multiple pieces, a tiny amount of super glue is usually enough; ensure you have a clean, dry workspace so you avoid accidental fusions of your own fingers.
