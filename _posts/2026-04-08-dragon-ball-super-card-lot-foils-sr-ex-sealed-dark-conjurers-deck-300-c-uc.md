## Condition, Grading, and Practical Inspection
### What to expect in a lot like this
When you’re purchasing a card lot that includes foils and a sealed deck, condition is everything. You’ll want to examine the listing photos for signs of wear: corner wear on foil cards, whitening on the edges, and any bending or warping in the sealed deck. If the listing mentions “C UC” (which could be shorthand for “Community/Uncirculated” or maybe a vendor’s own shorthand for condition), you should confirm with the seller what exactly they mean by that. The more responsible sellers will offer some depiction of condition, a return policy, and a transparent accounting of the number of foils vs. non-foil cards.

### Grading scale and how to verify
- Near Mint (NM): Cards look almost perfect, slight touches but no visible flaws that affect play or appearance.
- Excellent (EX): Light play wear, possible tiny scratches on foil or minor edge wear.
- Lightly Played (LP): Noticeable wear in non-critical areas and minor foil scuffing.
- Moderately Played (MP): Visible damage and more significant foil scuffing, but the card remains usable.
- Heavily Played (HP): Aged, scratched, bent, or severely edge-dented. These are often borderline for high-value foils.

For sealed decks, the concern shifts to seal integrity: is the plastic intact? Are there signs of reseal or tampering? A sealed deck that’s been opened, even if the booster packs inside are pristine, may not hold the same premium as a factory-sealed product. Confirm the seal status with the seller, ask for a photo of the seal or, if possible, request a video demonstration to ensure you’re truly buying a sealed deck and not a previously opened product re-sealed by someone’s grandmother with duct tape and questionable optimism.
