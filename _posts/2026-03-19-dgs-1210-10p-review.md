### VLANs and traffic segmentation
Smart switches like the DGS-1210-10P excel when you need to segment traffic for security or performance. VLANs allow you to isolate cameras from workstations, prioritizing critical flows (like VoIP) and preventing a glossy crowd of cameras from chewing up precious bandwidth. In Geeknite fashion, think of VLANs as the municipal zoning board for your data: keep the naughty stuff in one lane and the important stuff in another.

### QoS and traffic shaping
Quality of Service features let you define which traffic gets priority. If your office runs on VoIP telephones or a live video conference, you can reserve bandwidth and reduce jitter and lag. The UI guides you through basic QoS configurations without forcing you into a maze of options that would intimidate a newbie but charm a power user. The important part: QoS is there when you need it, and unobtrusive when you don’t.

## Setup Guide: Quick Start and Best Practices
1. Unbox, connect the power, and connect the two uplink ports to your core network or router.
2. Temporarily connect a PC to one of the PoE ports to access the web UI. Use the default IP (or let DHCP assign one, depending on your model) and log in with the default credentials.
3. Create a management VLAN if you want to isolate management traffic from user data. This is a sensible security practice that’s surprisingly easy to implement.
4. Enable VLANs for devices that must be segregated. Create at least two VLANs: one for voice/video (VoIP and cameras) and one for general data. Tie PoE devices into the correct VLANs to ensure they never bleed into the wrong lanes.
5. Configure QoS for VoIP and video traffic. Set a higher priority for voice and video to keep calls crisp and cameras responsive.
6. Finally, enable Link Aggregation if you have another switch in the stack and want more throughput to the edge. Make sure both ends support LACP and configure the aggregation group accordingly.

### Practical tips for different scenarios
- If you’re running cameras across multiple rooms, map each camera port to a dedicated VLAN and set QoS to prioritize camera streams.
- For VoIP-heavy deployments, set DSCP values in the QoS settings to ensure the voice packets get priority over bulk data transfers.
- If you plan to expand into a larger workspace, plan your uplinks and consider a stack with another switch to keep bandwidth available for critical applications.
- Keep a spare PoE budget in mind. If you plan to add more PoE devices later, you may want to distribute devices across ports to avoid hitting the budget ceiling on a single block of ports.
