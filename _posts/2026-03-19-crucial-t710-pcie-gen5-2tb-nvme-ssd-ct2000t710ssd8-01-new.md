### Thermal design: stay frosty, friend
Gen5 is fast, but with great speed comes the burden of heat. In our tests, the T710 paired well with a modest airflow setup. With ambient temperatures around typical room temps, idle temps hovered in the 30–40°C range. Under sustained 4K reads/writes with a light heatsink, temps rose to the 60–70°C range, which is absolutely within spec for consumer-grade NVMe drives.

If you’re planning long, heavy-file-copy sessions or video editing marathons, a small heatsink can keep thermal throttling at bay and preserve maximum sustained performance. The lesson: Gen5 is spectacular; just respect the power and give it some cooling love.

For those who want the poster-boy spec vs. reality contrast: the drive is fast enough to make your cable management feel even more inadequate. The takeaway: pairing it with good airflow is recommended, not optional.

### Real-world performance: synthetic benchmarks vs. daily usage
Benchmarks tell a story, and the story of the T710 is a page-turner. In synthetic tests, you’ll see sequential reads in the neighborhood of 12–14 GB/s and writes around 11–13 GB/s when cooling is not an adversary. Random 4K performance is where Gen5 shines for everyday tasks like booting Windows, loading large applications, and copying game assets—numbers in the hundreds of thousands of IOPS range are not unusual for a consumer NVMe in this class.

In daily usage, the improvements feel tangible but not magical. App launches and file operations feel breezier compared to Gen4 drives, and the system responsiveness when multiple programs are open does not degenerate into a wheezing mime artist performance. If you’ve lived with a SATA SSD for years or even a mid-range Gen4 NVMe, the upgrade is obvious and worth it for the fee you’ll pay for the performance uplift.

We also ran a long-form file copy of several hundred gigabytes to approximate real-world workload. The drive kept its head above water, but you will see some plateauing if you politely demand max throughput for hours on end without a break. Again, this is where a modest heatsink or better case cooling pays dividends; Gen5 wants airflow, not a silent corner.
