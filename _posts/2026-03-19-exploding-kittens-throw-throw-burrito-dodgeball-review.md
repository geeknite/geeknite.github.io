## In the box: what you actually get inside the NEW SEALED trio

First things first: the thrill of unsealing a triple-threat pack is unmatched. The glow of the shrink wrap, the crisp feel of the cardboard, the neon promises of “Never cry over losing your burrito again!”—it’s practically cinematic.

### Exploding Kittens

- A compact deck with a lot of attitude. Exploding Kittens is the absurdly simple, delightfully chaotic card game where your goal is to avoid drawing an exploding kitten card.
- Components (assuming you’re buying the standard edition): a deck of quirky cards, a few Defuse cards, and a handful of artful illustrations that make you want to adopt every kitten and name them after your internet-expert friends.
- How it plays: turns are snappy. You pass a card around, perform a silly action, and pray the kitten fairy stays away from your face. It’s basically “Russian roulette with a purring soundtrack,” except the bullets are five-point faces and the detonations are cartoon cats that instantly spark joy and fear in equal measure.

### Throw Throw Burrito

- A spin-off experience: physical play meets card-driven chaos. You’re throwing soft burritos at each other while simultaneously trying to collect matching cards.
- Components: a couple of plush burritos, a deck of cards with goofy art, a play area that’s large enough to accommodate enthusiastic shoves and dramatic sidesteps.
- How it plays: it’s basically a dodgeball-adjacent cardio session wrapped in a card game. You flip cards, shout “Burrito!” and sprint to dodge queued burrito projectiles. It’s a sanctioned chaos simulator that turns your living room into a makeshift dodgeball arena. If your HOA could support this, it would probably file a love letter.

### A Dodgeball Card Game

- The dark horse of the triple-threat. If you’ve ever dreamed of trading card stakes for athletic flair, this is the one to watch. It combines quick rounds with tactical moves that feel suspiciously like a tabletop version of dodgeball, but you’re using cards—so it’s safer for legal teams and less messy when someone inevitably trips on the rug.
- Components: a deck of dodgeball action cards, a few tokens, maybe a tiny score tracker, and art that screams “sports drama meets cartoon chaos.”
- How it plays: think quick-fire rounds, where each decision matters but the whole vibe is “we’re here to have fun, not to win the Nobel Prize in physics.” It rewards both bold plays and goofy misplays, which is exactly how party games should feel.

In short, the NEW SEALED trio promises three very different experiences under one cover. It’s like a gaming mixtape: one track is a cat wearing a bomb suit (Exploding Kittens), another is a slapstick burrito race (Throw Throw Burrito), and the third is a dodgeball-inspired card duel (A Dodgeball Card Game). They’re siblings in the same weird family, but each one brings its own rhythm to the table. Now, how do they actually perform when the shrink wrap comes off and the game night begins?

## In-depth looks: mechanics, pacing, and how clunky or smooth the trio actually feels

### Exploding Kittens: rhythm and risk without the heavy math
Exploding Kittens uses a simple “draw cards, play cards, avoid the bomb” loop. The math is light; the joy is in unpredictability. The Defuse cards—normally scarce—add a strategic layer, turning a near-loss moment into a gleeful, luck-sprinkled comeback. The pacing can feel breezy and fast, which is perfect for a 2–5 player setup (or more with expansions). The downside? If your table tense up over potential alliances, the humor can feel a tad mercifully cruel. You’ll hear a chorus of “you bastard” and “sorry not sorry” in quick succession, and that’s exactly what you want in a party game about exploding kittens and existential cat memes.

### Throw Throw Burrito: physicality as a feature, not a bug
This is where the game earns its chaotic reputation. You’ll move from brainy card play to sprinting and dodging. It’s light on setup; you can get a table of players engaged in minutes, which makes it ideal for guests who don’t want to study a rulebook while you refill the punch bowl. The burritos are harmless, the rules are forgiving, and the social friction—who throws what, who dodges what—creates story-worthy moments. The risk here is that real-life space matters. If your living room has low ceilings or a staircase in the path, you’ll be dodging more than cards; you’ll dodge minor furniture rearrangements. But that’s part of the charm. It’s a physical comedy show with a sane, portable deck of cards throwing the punch line.

### A Dodgeball Card Game: speed, strategy, and the playfully aggressive vibe
The third act of this trio introduces a slightly more tactical feel. You’re not just relying on luck or reflexes; you’re reading faces, bluffing confidence, and timing your moves with surgical precision—while still maintaining the ridiculous persona of a cartoon dodgeball coach. The game shines in rounds that feel quick and decisive, then relax into longer, strategic moments where you’re weighing a “risk now for a big payoff later” decision. It’s a game that rewards both speed and planning, which is a nice counterbalance to Exploding Kittens’ pure chaos or Burrito’s “let the chaos happen now” tempo.

In practice, the three together provide a broad palette: laugh-forward quick rounds, physical comedy in a safe space, and a hint of strategic depth that keeps the session from becoming a filler night. If you’re new to party games, this trio acts as a primer for how to balance variety—enough to satisfy different player tastes without leaving anyone bored.

## How to play a quick round (glimpse into the flow)
- Exploding Kittens: One-by-one turns, card plays with the aim to avoid drawing the exploding kitten card. Defuse cards provide an escape hatch. The rounds are short, which means you can escalate to a high-energy game night quickly.
- Throw Throw Burrito: Decide who starts; you continually draw cards and respond with the burrito throws when the card calls for it. The scoring is goofy—set collection plus penalties for near-misses. You’ll be sprinting, dodging, and laughing in a way that makes your neighbor’s cat think you’ve finally invented a new sport.
- A Dodgeball Card Game: Quick rounds with attack and dodge cards. The pacing is adjustable by how aggressively you bid for a knockout or guard the goal. It has a satisfying sense of momentum, especially when you pull off a bold move that changes the current leader’s status.

If you want a live example of a typical sequence, imagine a table full of people trying to read their next card while a burrito sails past, followed by a chorus of “nice throw” and “how did you even aim that?” That’s not just a game; it’s a memory being born at your coffee table.

## Practical considerations: space, noise, and family-friendliness
- Space: Throw Throw Burrito demands a bit of room. It’s not a microgame; you need enough space to dodge without becoming the neighborhood safety officer. Exploding Kittens and A Dodgeball Card Game don’t require as much physical maneuvering, so you can keep those within a tighter footprint.
- Noise: Expect laughter, groans, and the occasional shriek when a burrito smashes not-quite-you. If you’re in an apartment with thin walls, you might want to warn neighbors and use a plush burrito as a test dummy for practice throws.
- Family-friendliness: Exploding Kittens is relatively safe for older kids and up; Throw Throw Burrito is still pretty goofy, but you’ll want to supervise the more physical moments with younger players. A Dodgeball Card Game sits in a similar neighborhood—fun and silly, with just enough competitive edge to keep adults engaged but not mean-spirited.

These aren’t “solve the puzzle” games. They’re social instruments designed to create shared experiences. Here’s where strategy enters, in a way that won’t feel like homework:

- Reading the room: Exploding Kittens rewards players who can predict how risk-averse or risk-loving the table is. If you’re at a rowdy table where friends are trying to out-wit each other in a silly way, you’ll see engagements spike as players weigh the Defuse cards and the odds of survival.
- Timing the chaos: Throw Throw Burrito’s value is heavily dependent on timing. The right moment to unleash a burrito throw can flip the entire tone of the game. Your job is to maximize fun while maintaining a safe, friendly atmosphere.
- Resource management with a wink: A Dodgeball Card Game offers a more deliberate rhythm. You’ll want to weigh the payoff of an aggressive move against the risk of giving your opponents a clear advantage. It’s not heavy strategy, but it’s meaningful enough to give tabletop gamers a sense of tactical accomplishment.

If you’re seeking “deep strategy,” you’ll be pleasantly surprised by the occasional clever play that emerges from these smoke-and-mirrors experiences. If you’re seeking pure party chaos, the top two deliver with flair.
