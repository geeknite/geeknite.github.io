## Introduction
If you thought Zombicide was done with myths and legends after the last dozen expansions, think again. Zombicide: Troy & Evil Troy crashes into the Black Plague universe like a Trojan horse wearing a cape, a shiny helmet, and a penchant for turning heroic survivors into very dead art projects. Guillotine Games and CMON have delivered a box that smells like victory and gore in equal measure: two colossal miniatures, a few gimmicks, and enough chaos to turn any soccer mom’s attempt at lawn mowing into a heroic quest for survivors and something that vaguely resembles a spear-throwing Troy. In this review, we’ll dive into what’s in the box, how the new mechanics work, and whether Troy and his darker mirror Evil Troy deserve a place on your shelf (and, more importantly, at your table).

For completeness, let’s set expectations: this is not a standalone game. It needs a base Zombicide: Black Plague core set to run, and it plays best with a crew that enjoys a bit of theatrical chaos and humor as much as efficient turns. If your group is the twitchy, math-first kind, Troy will provide the adrenaline drip this hobby sometimes demands; if your group loves lore, you’ll get the mythic vibes of a city overrun with undead and a couple of mighty champions who aren’t afraid to make bad life choices in the name of victory.

## What’s in the box
The box is not shy about its purpose: it’s an expansion with a heavy emphasis on two larger-than-life characters and a few new rules that shake up the standard survive-and-slay tempo. Inside you’ll find:
- Two oversized, highly-detailed miniatures: Troy and Evil Troy. Yes, Evil Troy is not just a reflectively evil mirror; he’s a whole other set of abilities that can swerve a round from “quiet cleanup” to “wave the red cape like a living spoiler.”
- A new “Troy” character sheet with unique abilities that lean on leadership, battlefield morale, and occasional reckless bravery.
- A separate “Evil Troy” sheet with dark charisma, more chaotic triggers, and a willingness to break the fourth wall by declaring, “I am Chaos.”
- Several new zombie kits, including a couple of mythic variants designed to punish poor dice rolls and questionable moral choices.
- Cards for the scenarios, including mission briefs that let players experiment with new victory conditions and, inevitably, new ways to blame the person who brought “one last glass of wine” during setup.
- Tokens, boards, and the usual polyhedral dice, which you can never have enough of, because, yes, you’ll lose some to the couch or your dog who believes the dice are edible.

The components feel on-brand for Zombicide: Black Plague—solid plastic miniatures, sturdy cardboard tiles, and a color palette that makes the undead look gloriously cinematic rather than merely terrifying. The two big minis dominate the table presence, as any heroic narrative deserves when you drop a mythic Trojan horse into the middle of a zombie outbreak. If you’re a painter, you’ll have a field day; if you play straight out of the box, you’ll still feel like you’ve acquired a premium trophy to display during attempts to explain your hobby to skeptical relatives.

The box art and product photography do a lot of heavy lifting here. If you like your games to look cinematic from the moment you open the lid, you’ll appreciate the visual language, the dramatic shadows, and the sense that you’re about to launch a heroic, chaotic assault on the zombie horde. For some players, this visual heft translates into a longing for more narrative-driven play; for others, it’s simply a reminder to bring a couple of extra spoons for the inevitable laughter and chaos around the table.

## Miniatures and components quality
Let’s talk about the minis first because, in Zombicide, the minis are half the fun and all the glory. Troy is a hulking figure whose pose communicates “I have a plan and you’re about to witness it”—until the dice fail you and he ends up tripping over his own shield. Evil Troy, by contrast, has a silhouette that screams “I am Chaos in a helmet.” Both minis are extremely detailed, with textures on armor, creases in cloaks, and swords that actually look cinematic rather than toy-like.

The quality of the sculpts feels consistent with the best Guillotine Games/CMON releases. They’re robust, which is essential given the inevitable dice-chucking and miniature-bump operations that occur during live play. The sculpts are crisp enough to stand out on a modern table, yet balanced enough to blend with other Zombicide components without looking shoehorned into the set.

Card stock for the new cards and the scenario briefings holds up reasonably well. It’s not a premium linen finish, but it’s sturdy enough for repeated play and easy to shuffle. The new zombie variants—mythic, punished, and a few fresh walkers—offer enough variety to prevent boredom after a few sessions. If you are someone who buys expansions solely for painting opportunities, you’ll be in paint heaven here; if you’re someone who uses the minis as “honest-to-goodness gaming pieces,” you’ll appreciate the weight and presence they bring to the table.

## Rules, mechanics, and new twists
Troy & Evil Troy doesn’t reinvent Zombicide’s rhythm so much as it re-shifts the tempo with two key mechanisms: morale-driven leadership and chaos-driven chaos—aka Evil Troy’s thing.

### Leadership and morale (Troy’s specialty)
Troy introduces leadership-style rules that reward players who coordinate and protect allies. It’s not simply “kill or be killed” anymore; it’s about keeping the party together and leveraging the presence of a strong figure on the board. The rule complications come in two flavors:
- A morale track that encourages players to maintain a certain level of cooperation. When morale dips, attackers gain a slight edge, and you’ll probably hear the table groan as someone tips a cup to re-roll a die or two.
- An aura-like modifier that enhances allied actions when Troy stands near survivors. The aura is not game-breakingly powerful, but it makes you consider positioning with care, which is a nice change of pace for those who enjoy tactical chess on a zombie battlefield.

### Chaos and mischief (Evil Troy’s domain)
Evil Troy represents a deliberate “chaos engine.” He is designed to juice tension and unpredictability. His presence triggers chaotic card events and can flip the momentum in surprising ways. The net effect is a table that experiences rapid shifts in advantage—the kind of moments that become story-worthy: you’ll remember that one turn when Evil Troy did the thing you didn’t think possible and changed the outcome of the encounter.

The combination of these two mechanics means you’ll experience a shift in pacing. It’s not always smooth; there are moments where you’ll feel the dice bully you into a corner, but the overall arc is satisfyingly cinematic. The game likes to remind you that leadership is a thing and that chaos is a force, not merely the last resort when you’re out of good options.

### Scenarios and campaign structure
The expansion ships with several new scenarios that exploit Troy’s leadership and Evil Troy’s chaos. Some scenarios emphasize rescue and containment, others demand a more aggressive push toward a central objective that resembles a Trojan Fire drill rather than a calm skirmish. The missions tend to incentivize more aggressive play than some base-game scenarios, which is a nice change of pace for players who feel the base game sometimes becomes a bit too methodical.

There’s also a simple, but flavorful, narrative thread that runs through the campaigns—players discover fragments of lore about the ancient city, its curse, and how the two Troys can influence the outcome of the apocalypse. If you enjoy the idea of running a campaign arc where myth meets mayhem, you’ll feel right at home here. The scenarios provide bite-sized arcs that aren’t brutally long but still offer a sense of progression and consequence.

## How it plays with Black Plague
The synergy with Black Plague is where the expansion shines or falters depending on your group’s expectations. If you’re hoping for a wholly new game design, Troy & Evil Troy doesn’t deliver that level of overhaul. What it does deliver is a strong thematic layer that slots cleanly into the existing framework:
- You’ll continue to roll dice to activate zombie counters and survivors, with the same threat-level dynamics as the base game.
- The two new leaders influence how you plan rounds, forcing you to weigh risk and reward more deliberately than in a vanilla session.
- The chaos engine adds a level of unpredictability that can be stressful in the best possible way—perfect for players who enjoy a strong narrative imprint on their sessions.

If you’re primarily a solo player or a small group that loves tight, optimized plays, the expansion will still shine, but you’ll want to lean into the scenario design to maximize the narrative payoff. The randomness introduced by Evil Troy is not hair-pulling in a bad way; rather, it’s hair-pulling in a thrilling, “we did something dramatic and the table will remember this” sort of way.

For the solo players, the expansion can become a challenge twin-pack: you have to manage both the leadership aura and the chaos engine without a fourth hand. It’s doable, but you’ll want to tailor the scenario selection to avoid grinding your brain into zombie paste. It’s a different flavor of solo play, but one worth trying if you’re looking for a fresh spin on a familiar system.

## Paint, table presence, and storage considerations
One practical topic often overlooked in reviews is the physicality of the game in the long run. Trophy-worthy minis, larger bases, and the box’s bulk mean you’ll likely spend more time assembling, sorting, and storing the components.
- Storage: You’ll want a sturdy tub or a dedicated divider set to keep Troy and Evil Troy from reaching a mutiny on the storage shelf. The larger minis are not aggressively fragile, but the bases are wide enough that you’ll appreciate a bit of care when you lift the box lid.
- Painting: If you’re into painting, this is a great addition to your collection. The two larger minis are rich with detail, and the rest of the kit provides ample scope for shading and highlights. The textures on armor, weapons, and clothing are well thought out—an invitation to hobbyists who like to micro-optimize their color schemes.
- Table presence: The moment you pull Troy and Evil Troy onto the table, you’ll get a response from your group. It’s a mix of awe, fear, and grudging respect for the scale of the minis. If you’re a streamer or content creator, expect some camera-friendly moments as you orchestrate a dramatic showdown with a couple of undead hordes surrounding your heroes.

## Balance and difficulty considerations
Where some expansions risk tipping the scales too far in one direction, Troy & Evil Troy maintains a balance that’s approachable without becoming predictable. The leadership aura provides a steady, reliable boost to certain efficient strategies, but the chaos engine is always there to pull the rug out if you start thinking you’ve got the pattern cracked.

That said, you’ll want to pay attention to your group’s experience level. If you’re playing with a novice crew, this expansion can feel overwhelming at first, not due to complexity but due to the sheer pace of the events once Evil Troy enters the scene. For veterans who enjoy a bit more spike—where a couple of bad luck moments can unravel a round in satisfying fashion—the expansion is pure cake candy for the brain.

## Solo and co-op viability
As a co-op game, Zombicide thrives on teamwork. Troy adds a narrative dimension that encourages discussion and planning. The big challenge for solo players is maintaining a sense of momentum while juggling two separate leadership/chaos dynamics. It’s totally doable, but you’ll likely want to tailor the mission pool to emphasize smaller, faster rounds. The consequence? You regain the satisfaction of turning a chaotic turn into a cinematic moment without the table devolving into “one more reroll” chaos.

## Thematic and narrative flavor
Troy & Evil Troy aren’t just gimmicks; they’re designed to infuse the entire session with a mythic, slightly over-the-top flavor. The theme hits you in the best way possible for Zombicide: you can feel the mythic weight behind each action, and you get that sense of “great deeds in a bad neighborhood.” If you enjoy a pseudo-Greek myth vibe during your zombie apocalypse, you’ll find the flavor delightful. The pacing supports these moments, letting you bask in a few “heroic” turns before the tide turns and the zombies surge again.

The written materials do a good job of staying consistent with the Black Plague lore while giving Troy a distinct identity. Evil Troy, in particular, channels a chaotic energy that makes the story feel less deterministic and more cinematic. You’ll be tempted to narrate your own cinematic trailer as you play, which is exactly the kind of side-effect that makes Zombicide feel more like a role-playing experience and less like a pure math exercise.

## Flavorful comparisons: Troy and Evil Troy vs. other Zombicide expansions
If you’ve collected a handful of Zombicide expansions, you’ll already be familiar with the pattern: strong theme, good quality components, and a few rules that either enhance story or complicate the math. Troy & Evil Troy fits the typical expansion model very well, but with a few distinctive touches:
- The two big minis are more imposing on the table than many other villain or hero minis in the line, contributing to a stronger narrative presence.
- The dual-leader dynamic offers a new silhouette for player coordination that is more than a gimmick; it influences tactical decisions in meaningful ways.
- The chaotic events feel purposeful rather than random, which makes them easier to incorporate into a narrative flow without feeling like the game is trolling you.

Compared to some other mythic-themed expansions, Troy & Evil Troy lands in a sweet spot: it offers fresh mechanics without losing the core Zombicide experience. It’s an expansion that respects the base game's rhythm while nudging you toward more dynamic, story-rich play. If you’re chasing pure novelty without sacrificing table rhythm, you’ll likely find this expansion compelling and balanced enough to justify its price tag.

## Setup, learning curve, and playing time
Setup time remains reasonable, especially if you’re already fluent with Black Plague. You’ll need to separate two big minis, stack the new boards, and configure the scenario deck. It’s not a “minutes-to-finish” box, but it’s not an unwieldy behemoth either. If you’re the kind of group that enjoys a longer, cinematic session, expect to add a generous 15–30 minutes to your usual prep, primarily for the lore cards and the new token tracking.

Learning the new rules is straightforward for anyone familiar with Zombicide: Black Plague. For newcomers, there is a little more to absorb, but the teaching curve is not brutal. The rulebook is mostly friendly and well-structured, with examples that clarify the new aura mechanics and the chaos triggers. In practice, you’ll likely be running a few practice rounds to get your group on the same page; after that, the rest of the experience becomes smooth sailing with occasional dramatic interruptions courtesy of Evil Troy.

Playtime for a standard scenario sits in the 60–90 minute window for experienced players, with longer sessions available for more ambitious campaigns. The presence of the two larger heroes tends to push the round count upwards a bit, since there’s more decision space—especially if you’re prioritizing synergy and hero synergy. If you’re a streamer or a content creator who likes to narrate the turns with dramatic flair, you’ll appreciate the pacing variables that the new rules introduce.

## The definitive recommendation
So, is Zombicide: Troy & Evil Troy worth buying for your Black Plague experience? If you want a strong thematic lift, two spectacular minis that command attention, and a set of mechanics designed to produce dramatic, narrative-rich rounds, yes. It’s a solid addition for players who enjoy a blend of leadership dynamics and chaotic, cinematic turns. It’s not a must-buy for players who are happy with the base game or who prefer a pure, unaltered puzzle experience; and if you’re not a fan of heavy narrative flavor in an otherwise tactical game, you may find the aura/chaos dance a bit too theatrical for your taste.

Retaining a balanced approach, Troy & Evil Troy leans into what Zombicide does best: cooperative, chaotic, zombie-slaying fun with enough strategy to keep the brain engaged. It is a satisfying expansion that respects the core game while offering a fresh narrative angle that players can lean into or simply enjoy as a colorful backdrop to their zombie apocalypse. If you enjoy mythic, larger-than-life moments in your tabletop gaming, this box hits the sweet spot.

## Final thoughts and tips for getting the most out of the set
- Start slow: Run one or two short scenarios first to iron out the new rules and learn how Troy’s leadership interacts with the survivors’ action economy. You’ll avoid the cascade of missteps that can occur when your group tries to go all-in on turn one.
- Embrace chaos: Evil Troy provides opportunities for extraordinary table moments. Lean into storytelling and narration to maximize the fun factor when he shows up.
- Mix and match: The expansion plays nicely with other Zombicide expansions. If you have a few different packs, try a mixed scenario that uses Black Plague as a canvas and Troy as the narrative brush.
- Painting and display: If you enjoy the hobby aspect, you’ll love painting these minis. Use contrast paints or strong metallics to make the shininess of Troy’s armor pop under the table light.
- Storage: Plan your storage solution ahead of time. The box is not tiny. A simple, organized insert will save you headaches down the line and protect the big minis from nips and bumps during transport.

For readers who crave a quick takeaway: Troy & Evil Troy is an excellent thematic boost that doesn’t radically upend the base game’s structure. It elevates the drama, provides two memorable antagonists, and infuses a mythic flavor that many players didn’t know their Black Plague sessions were missing. If your gaming group is ready for a more narrative-leaning zombie outbreak and you want a tangible reason to yell “To the chariots!” mid-game, this expansion is your next purchase.
