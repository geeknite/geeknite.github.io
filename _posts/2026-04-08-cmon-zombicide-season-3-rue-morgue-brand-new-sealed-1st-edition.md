## CMON Zombicide: Season 3 Rue Morgue – Brand New Sealed 1st Edition Review

If you’ve ever opened a brand-new board game and felt the thrill of discovering a tiny plastic prisoner named Zombivor you didn’t know you needed in your life, then you’re about to get it in stereo with Rue Morgue. CMON’s Zombicide Season 3, Rue Morgue edition is a boxed set designed to transport you to a city full of undead jazz hands and the occasional ambulatory baguette. Spoiler alert: the zombies still aren’t impressed with your high-dexterity roll, but the box art might make you question your life choices.

Unboxing is the most sacred ritual in board gaming. It’s the moment you pretend you’re a zesty archaeologist discovering ancient treasure… and then you realize you’re just digging through a bag of cardboard tokens and plastic minis. This 1st edition sealed copy arrives with that new-game scent—a mix of cardboard dust, triumph, and the faint whisper of a sports drink that never existed.

The shrink wrap crackles like a tiny villain in a 80s cartoon, and as you peel it back you get a sense of the scale of CMON’s ambition: to condense near-endless dungeon crawling into a barrel of fun that can be consumed in a weekend and then expanded into a full-blown lifestyle choice. The Rue Morgue theme—nice nod to Poe, if Poe was more into zombies and less into the grim reality of editorials—lands with both a wink and a snarl.

First impressions matter. The box is sturdy, the artwork is bold, and the miniatures look like they came straight out of a high-budget cosplay convention rather than a play room. I’m not judging; I’m just saying it’s hard to go back to cardboard standees once you’ve hugged an over-detailed zombie that has a little more personality than your last two exes.

### Box contents and components

Inside the sealed lid you’ll find: a player’s handbook, a rulebook, a deck of new tiles, a bunch of zombie miniatures, survivor cards, and enough tokens to fill a small coffee shop with tiny plastic counters. The 1st edition Rue Morgue box includes:
- Survivor cards featuring new class styles
- A deck of special zombie tokens that behave like moody teenagers at a high school reunion
- Modular tiles that create the Rue Morgue district with a vibe you either love or fear
- A set of custom dice that feel satisfyingly chunky when you roll critical hits
- Plastic bases and stands that click into place with the confidence of a jazz musician hitting the right note

### The minis and the mold lines

The minis look fantastic; CMON has always had a knack for sculpting faces that look like they’re about to shout at you in a language only your gaming group understands. In Rue Morgue, there are new zombie variants that require careful planning and a little bit of luck with your dice. If you’re new to Zombicide, you’ll quickly learn that the minis aren’t just pretty; they’re a functional part of the strategy. The mold lines? Honestly, you’ll forget they exist as soon as you see your first survivor slice a zombie with a perfectly timed Ambush card.

### Cards, tiles, and rules

The new tiles provide a tight labyrinth of streets and alleys that force players to plan ahead. The rulebook is dense but approachable, and if you’ve played Season 2 or Season 1, you’ll notice a few fresh wrinkles that keep things exciting without making you reach for a glossary every five minutes.

### The first playthrough: setup and learning curve

Setting up Rue Morgue takes a little longer than you’d expect, mostly because you’ll spend a few minutes organizing your shiny new tokens into the correct color-coded stacks. Once you get into the flow of the game, you’ll find the learning curve isn’t steep, but the strategic depth is. You’ll want to plan your turns several steps ahead and accept that the zombie apocalypse is not a linear event but a chaotic, living thing that thrives on your mistakes.

### The gameplay experience

Rue Morgue brings a mix of cooperative chaos and tactical micromanagement. It’s not a “roll the dice and see what happens” game; it’s a game where your decisions cascade into a chain of consequences that can either be a triumphant explosion or a hiccup of despair. This edition adds new survivor classes that function like unique tools in your team’s toolbox. Some are tanky front-liners, others are nimble scavengers who can snatch objectives from under the noses of the undead.

If you’re the type who loves “one more round” nights, Rue Morgue offers enough tension to justify those extra cups of coffee. The zombies have new abilities that make the board feel more alive (or at least more aggressively undead). There are moments when a well-placed trap tile can turn the table, and there are other moments when you realize that you’re just one bad dice roll away from a disastrously dramatic death scene that would make your gaming group go “noooo!”

How does it compare to previous seasons? If Season 2 felt like a high-speed car chase through a mausoleum, Rue Morgue leans into claustrophobia, close-quarter fights, and the creeping dread of a city that doesn’t want to sleep—only shuffle. The balance remains accessible for newcomers but sneaks in enough advanced decision points to keep veterans engaged.

### Box versus table: value and longevity

The sealed status adds a nice sense of “newness” that you rarely feel with opened games. The investment pays off for collectors and completionists, but even casual players will appreciate the premium feel of the components. If you’re torn between picking up Rue Morgue or another Season 3 box, consider your local game group’s appetite for chaos. Rue Morgue leans into it in a way that feels like a party where the doors are thrown open and the guests are zombies with a taste for strategy.

Longevity is strong here. The modular tiles open up to create new layouts; the zombie variants provide enough variability to avoid stale repeats; and the survivor classes invite experimentation. If you’re thinking, “Will this sit on my shelf and collect dust?” the answer is: probably not, unless you own a shelf that doubles as a zombie hatchery. The real test is whether you’ll want to replay this scenario regularly, and the answer is—yes, you will, because there’s a certain thrill in mastering how to corner a zombie with a well-timed trap.

### Value in terms of price and resale

Brand-new, sealed copies hold value for a while because interest in Zombicide remains steady. If you’re a completionist or a collector, Rue Morgue is a no-brainer. If you’re a casual gamer who loves the occasional big box, it’s still worth considering, because the joy of unboxing is a ritual in itself, and the full kit offers hours of cooperative play with friends who defend your living room against the undead.

### How to play and what to expect on your first playthrough

During your first session, expect a bounce between planning and improvisation. There will be moments when your careful plan unravels in spectacular fashion, usually due to a zombie’s sudden spawn or a misread of a tile’s corridor. The beauty of Zombicide lies in this unpredictability. You’ll often find yourself improvising, pivoting, and sometimes improvising again.

To get the most out of Rue Morgue, you’ll want to:
- Set up a clear objective and keep it in sight
- Use the new survivors to coordinate attacks and extraction points
- Make smart use of traps and environmental hazards
- Manage your risk—sometimes it’s better to retreat, regroup, and come back stronger

Here’s a little aside on how the game handles “heroic” moments: a single good turn can save the day, but more often, it’s a sequence of small decisions that keeps you alive long enough to pull off that pivotal combo.

### The art, mood, and audio-visual feel

The art direction in Rue Morgue is moody and atmospheric, with a slightly noir vibe that fits the city’s undead theme. The miniatures are crisp and detailed, and the tiles convey a sense of claustrophobic urban sprawl that makes you feel like you’re playing in a labyrinth. The rulebook’s layout keeps things readable, and you can feel that CMON put a lot of care into making the experience immersive.

### A note on authenticity and seal integrity

Because this is a brand-new, sealed copy, you’ll want to check the seal for tamper evidence when you receive it. If you’re buying mail-order, ensure the shrink wrap is intact, and examine the packaging for any signs of wear. The sealed box provides an assurance that the product is untouched and unplayed, which is essential for collectors and store-level resellers.

### House rules and optional twists

One of the joys of Zombicide is that you can tailor the chaos to your crew. Rue Morgue plays beautifully with house rules: shorten or lengthen the rounds, tweak the zombie spawn rates, or allow certain survivors to gain extra abilities after meeting a scenario objective. These tweaks aren’t necessary, but they can significantly alter the tempo and keep long campaigns feeling fresh. If you’re into campaign hooks, consider creating a mini-arc that runs across multiple sessions, with Rue Morgue acting as a recurring “corridor of dread” that your players have to navigate.

### Final verdict and recommendation

So, should you buy CMON Zombicide Season 3 Rue Morgue? If you’re a Zombicide fan, a co-op enthusiast, or someone who enjoys a good puzzle wrapped in a zombie apocalypse, the answer is a confident yes. Rue Morgue brings enough new ideas to keep the brand fresh while maintaining the addictive core loop that has defined Zombicide since its early days. It rewards thoughtful planning, bold risk-taking, and, yes, a little bit of luck with the dice. The 1st edition is a nice collectible touch, and the “brand-new sealed” status adds an extra layer of excitement if you’re into the unboxing ritual.

Pros:

Overall: A solid addition to the Zombicide family that doesn’t shy away from chaos, while still delivering strategic satisfaction. If you like your zombies with a dash of puzzle-solving and a heavy dose of cooperative chaos, Rue Morgue is a win.
