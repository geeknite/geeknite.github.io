## What is UNO, anyway?

For the uninitiated (or the lucky one who slept through elementary game night), UNO is a color-coded card game with a stack of luck, strategy, and occasional rowdiness that leaves the living room feeling like a sports bar after a last-minute goal. The goal is simple: get rid of all your cards. But the catch is that each card has a personality. Red Skip? Extra dramatic. Yellow Draw Two? Passive-aggressive pressure. Green Reverse? The subtle way to ruin your plans for a quiet evening. And at the heart of it all sits the dear old Wild card, the one card that says, "I am the decision maker, and also I might be a party animal."

If you've never played, think of UNO as the card game equivalent of a group chat that occasionally yells, 'UNO!' and expects you to respond with a matching color or a wild move. It is chaos, packaged in cardboard, and it somehow works. The standard UNO deck comes with color-coded numbers, a handful of action cards, wild cards, and the occasional glare from the person who just drew six cards after you declared UNO for the third time in a row. Now, imagine all of that in one single deck labeled 108 cards. Yes, you read that correctly: 108 opportunities for you to misread the color, misjudge the strategy, and misplace your sanity.

## Rules and setup: getting to the chaos without losing your marbles

Setting up UNO with all cards included is essentially the same as standard setup, with a larger deck and a higher likelihood of accidental “how did we get here” moments.

- Each player draws 7 cards, and the rest become the draw pile. In an all-cards deck, you better have a clear floor area because the pile might attempt to take over your coffee table.
- The top card of the draw pile starts the discard pile. If it’s a Wild, you’re immediately forgiven for not knowing what color you’ll pick, because everyone’s about to discover your color psychology in real time.
- On your turn, you must match either the color or the number of the top discard card or play a Wild. The confusion increases with every wild card in play because the color you declare becomes the new reality, but not everyone is convinced your color choice is legitimate.
- Draw actions: when you draw due to a Wild Draw Four or Draw Two, you add to your hand’s chaos, not your dignity. The 108-card deck means more ways to accumulate cards you’ll pretend to play optimally later.

In all seriousness, a big feature of the all-cards deck is the glory of quick reshuffles. If you’ve ever watched a poker game and thought, 'That deck needs more drama,' UNO with all cards delivers. There’s always a moment where you realize you shuffled too well and now the draw pile looks like a cardstock avalanche. ## Card breakdown: what you’re probably going to encounter

The UNO deck is built from a handful of card families. With all cards included, you’ll meet the following comedians in card form:

### Number cards
- Colors: red, blue, green, yellow. The numbers range from 0 to 9. 0s are rare, 9s are dramatic, and 5s are the middle children who never know whether they’re liked or disliked.
- Function: These are the quiet cards. They simply remind you that you have to count to seven before you can do something dramatic. In all-card mode, you’ll end up counting in languages you didn’t realize you remembered in your sleep.

### Action cards
- Skip: The “you lose your turn” card. In large decks, it’s a party trick that becomes a bargaining tool at a family gathering.
- Reverse: Changes the direction of play. In a group of players who are all trying to memorize the turn order, this one is a sneaky way to confuse your friends as you circle back to your own previous move.
- Draw Two: Forces the next player to draw two cards. It’s the nicest way to humbly punish someone who played a color you dislike, while you get a free moment of thinking time (which you’ll waste with daydreams about snacks).

### Wild cards
- Wild: You get to pick the new color. This is the crowning piece of the UNO wheel, the moment when the player declares a color and everyone pretends to care.
- Wild Draw Four: The dramatic closer. It makes your friends question your honesty, but it also fills your own hand with potential gambits and misdirection. In all cards included, this card is louder, bolder, and more likely to ruin a perfectly good plan to end the game with a sly smile.

With all 108 cards, you get more of each card type than you’ll ever need—and more opportunities to over-think your strategy while you’re already at the mercy of random chance. The combination of many of each card means you’re always within one bad decision of a 12-card hand, which is the UNO equivalent of a small dominoes tower collapsing in your lap.

## Strategy for beginners (without spoilers for your future embarrassment)

- Learn what to hold: since you’ll be dealing with a bigger deck, identify two or three colors you’re comfortable with and try to keep at least one card of each in your hand to avoid getting stuck.
- Don’t panic on wilds: a Wild is not your friend or enemy; it’s a neutral tool that can help you steer a game that’s already spiraling. Use Wilds to set up your next turn, but don’t rely on them to do all the heavy lifting for you.
- Watch the draw pile: when the deck is large, the chance of the next card doing something useful is about the same as a coin flip. Don’t chase a specific card; chase a color or a plan that keeps you thinking three moves ahead rather than five seconds ahead.
- Bluffing and social dynamics: UNO is almost as much a social game as it is a card game. The real skill is not just the math but reading your friends’ faces as they disapprove of your color choices.

### House rules and variants (because we’re not puppets of the deck)
- Stacking Draw Twos and Draw Fours: some groups allow you to stack with a single color, stacking turns into a tenuous but glorious chain of misery. Others disallow it, preferring to keep the game moving. The all-cards deck makes this variant even more interesting because there are more Draw Twos and Draw Fours to chain.
- Seven-0 and extra rules: a personal favorite is the "Seven-0" rule, where every time a seven is played, players reposition, or every time a zero is played, players pass cards around in a new rotation. In big decks, this results in a carnival of confusion that lasts until someone yells, 'UNO!' for the fifth time in a round and the table forgets the original intent of the game.
