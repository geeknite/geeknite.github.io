## A closer look at the sculpts: Abomination, Runners, and the rest

### Abomination: the big boss that makes players reconsider sneaking back for the oven mitts
The Abomination in this set is intentionally larger and more imposing than your standard issue undead. It’s sculpted with a ragged, horrific silhouette that reads well from across the table and scales up nicely on a typical gamer’s Malm shelf. The textures are varied: clawed hands on a gnarly wrist, robes that look like they’ve absorbed a few centuries of plagues, and a head that tilts with a level of malice that suggests it forgot to blink during crucial turns. The paint job benefits from a contrast wash to bring out the creases and the sinewy detail in the robes. It’s not a statue; it’s a narrative engine with a swingy hit box, so to speak. If you’re into diorama-friendly setups, this guy begs for a dramatic base: a ruined courtyard with scattered bones and a splash of faux blood for dramatic effect.

### Runners: fast, furious, and fun to paint in neon if you want a chaotic vibe
Runners are the speedsters of this set. Their posture is dynamic, limbs slightly elongated to convey movement, and their expressions carry the gleeful menace of someone who is about to violate safety rules in a board game. Painting them gives you a chance to experiment with dusted edges, grime layers, and maybe a little metallic sheen on teeth and nails. The trick with runners is to use a subtle highlight on the edges to emphasize speed without overdoing it. If you want a color palette that slaps, go with desaturated earth tones for the bodies and a contrasting color on the eyes or teeth to make them pop under game light.

### Zombies: the reliable crowd that completes the tableau
Standard zombies in Black Plague eschew the modern trope of decayed fashion in favor of a more uniform punk of rot and decay. They’re designed to be the background chorus that makes the Abomination look even more impressive when it finally clambers into view. The sculpt lines are clear, making assembly optional if you’re not metal-friendly with glue, which is nice for beginners. The heads are varied enough to avoid monotony if you want to create a ragtag zombie mob painting project. When you stack these on the board, they naturally create the sense of scale you expect from a plague scenario: the Abomination looming over a pack of regulars, while the Runners cut across the edges of your line and threaten your flanks.

## Assembly and painting: getting them table-ready

The nine pieces arrive on plastic sprues that are typical for Zombicide minis. In practical terms, you’ll likely want to clip them from the sprue, tidy up any mold lines, and then wash them before painting. The Abomination’s bulk means you’ll want a sharper hobby knife for cleanup along its robe edges; the Runners’ limbs can benefit from a gentle file along the joints to prevent flaking paint from building up at the seams. If you’re a new painter, this set is forgiving enough to practice washes and dry brushing without feeling like you’re ruining a precious sculpture. The black and brown tones naturally lend themselves to muddy, plague-y vibes; a dash of red or sickly green on the eyes or wounds can do wonders.

In terms of technical approach, you can pop these pieces into a standard 28mm scale, but the Abomination will dominate the tabletop presence. Consider basing them on a textured 60mm base for the boss, or fashion a ruined stone base to hint at the medieval setting. A common base trick is to sprinkle crushed cork for rubble and use a matte medium to add a little sheen to the “wet” areas that imply recent gore. It’s not necessary, but it helps the piece read as dynamic under gaming lamps.

## Build quality, materials, and durability on the table

The plastic used for these minis is standard CMON fare: sturdy enough for table play, with the right amount of flex to survive occasional bumps during a heated clash. The Abomination’s mass can stress a small-base scenario if you’re not mindful of basing, but that’s a conscious design trade-off: more mass means more presence on the map. The Runners, being slimmer, tend to hold paint well if you seal with a matte spray. The Zombies are the most forgiving in terms of paint adhesion and are excellent for mobile mobs that suffer from less precise basing. In terms of durability, these minis shrug off light-to-moderate tabletop wear; if you have kids or a particularly enthusiastic gamer in your circle, consider a hobby-grade sealant to prevent chipping during long campaigns.

From a durability standpoint, there’s a soft spot with very fine details on the smallest parts. If you’re aiming for a clean, crisp finish, a light undercoat helps the subsequent layers breathe, and a couple of light passes with a fine brush can preserve the details on the abnormally slender appendages of the Runners.

### Painting and basing ideas to lift the set
A good painting strategy for this pack is to pick a unifying color for the mass zombies to keep them cohesive on the board, then give each runner and the abomination a distinct accent color to help players identify them quickly during tense rounds. A muted gray-brown wash across the zombies, with a dusty edge highlight, helps convey a plague-ridden world. The abomination can receive a deeper wash and perhaps a crimson highlight on its exposed flesh to signal its malevolence. For basing, a ruined courtyard look with broken flagstones and tufts of dead grass can ground the set in a medieval cityscape. If you’re feeling extra fancy, you can sculpt faint tracks on the base to imply the abomination’s heavy footfalls and create a narrative sticker across the base’s rim that reads like a broken entry plaque.
