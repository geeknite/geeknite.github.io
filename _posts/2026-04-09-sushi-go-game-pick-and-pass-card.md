## How the drafting engine works in practice
Sushi Go! is built on a simple, elegant premise: you draft a single card from your hand and pass the rest to your neighbor, continuing until all cards from the initial hand have been claimed. The round ends when the deck you are drawing from runs out. During scoring, you tally up points from each dish type and bonuses from special cards. The genius of the design is that every decision feels meaningful, yet the cost of a misstep is a quick round reset rather than a bruising defeat. You get to snatch a little set of dumplings, a row of maki rolls, or a tempting nigiri, and you watch the chips fall as players compare who has built the tastiest assortment.

The Wasabi card is a cheeky modifier: if you pick Wasabi, your next Nigiri card you collect will be worth triple its base value. This mechanic is a tiny strategic lever that can flip early rounds from a tight race into a gleeful sprint of scoring potential. It is not a universal game changer, but it is one of those design choices that makes you feel clever for noticing it, especially when you time it in the final moments of a round.

The scoring logic is robust but approachable. Nigiri cards are straightforward: set a baseline score which can be boosted by Wasabi. Maki stacks reward the player with the most Maki rolls at the end of a round. Tempura and Sashimi reward you for collecting pairs or triples, which motivates a mix of memory, prediction, and opportunistic grabs. Pudding is the most negotiable piece: it is an endgame kicker that influences the final tally rather than the round-by-round rhythm. If you are playing with family or younger players, you can provide a gentle nudge about pudding as a long game snack that pays off when the match wraps up.

The 2 to 5 player setup keeps the pace tight. In a five player game, you might see more dramatic cross-overs and the need to anticipate multiple turns in a row. In a two player setup, the pacing is consistently brisk and the bluffing element dissolves into a more direct cat-and-mouse mental exercise. The sealed edition handles player count with a simple shuffle and deal mechanism that nothing in the box complicates; you can adapt the number of rounds and the hand size on the fly, making it a flexible choice for all sorts of game nights.

In terms of accessibility, Sushi Go! is welcoming to newcomers without sacrificing depth for veterans who enjoy optimizing their card pools. The core action is intuitive: pick a card you want, pass the rest, score at the end of each round. The mental workload comes from balancing your current needs against what your neighbors might pick next. It’s a friendly, often silly, and surprisingly strategic little puzzle that rewards quick thinking and a willingness to read the table.

## The score sheet in your head: breaking down the scoring blocks
- Nigiri: Each Nigiri card gives you a small base value; if you snag Wasabi, the value of the next Nigiri increases. This creates a neat mini-puzzle where you weigh taking a single card now versus risking a different discard round.
- Maki: The player with the most Maki rolls at the end of a round scores a handful of points; the second most might also gain a smaller reward. This encourages you to monitor not only what you take but what others are likely to chase on their side of the table.
- Tempura: You need two Tempura cards to score a chunk of points. The probability curve of drawing two of the same dish adds a little tension to the drafting decisions, especially as the deck thins out toward the end of a round.
- Sashimi: Three Sashimi cards net you a decent payoff. The temptation to chase a lot of Sashimi can be strong, but it can also backfire if the round ends before you grind out the trio.
- Pudding: Pudding is the lingering reward. It sits on a separate track, contributing points at the end of the game rather than per round. This fosters a social calculus: you may pass on pudding early to avoid giving your opponents a chance to climb the endgame ladder.

Understanding these blocks helps you read the table and estimate risk. A typical round is a little calculus problem: what can you plausibly score from your current hand, what is likely to come next, and how much should you push toward a particular dish before the round closes? The answer often hinges on timing and a dash of luck — two things that Sushi Go! handles with a light touch rather than a heavy hand.

## Strategy and tips: how to actually win the lunchtime sprint
- Start simple, then layer in complexity: In your first few plays, focus on collecting a balanced set of dishes so you are not chasing a single path that might be blocked by a neighbor. As you get comfortable, experiment with Wasabi timing and the moment you pull a Nigi-rami card to maximize score.
- Watch the other players, not just the cards in front of you: The beauty of pick and pass is that you gain information as cards flow around the table. If a neighbor starts hoarding Maki, you can pivot to other scoring blocks and still come away with a strong final tally.
- Don’t ignore Pudding, but don’t worship it: Endgame pudding can swing the final numbers, but chasing it at the expense of immediate scoring blocks usually backfires. Build a plan that affords a Pudding payoff while staying flexible in the middle rounds.
- Plan for the late game: As the deck runs low, the probability of hitting certain cards increases or decreases depending on what was popular in the round. Savvy players often time their Wasabi bets to align with the endgame flow.
- Play for the table, not for the solo run: Sushi Go! rewards opportunities that come from the table dynamics. If you are playing with pushy jokesters or new players, subtlety emphasizing fun and quick rounds sometimes yields the best overall experience for everyone involved.
