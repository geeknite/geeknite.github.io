# Zombicide - Black Plague: A Geeknite Review

![Zombicide: Black Plague cover](/assets/images/zombicide-black-plague-cover.jpg)

Welcome, fellow geeks and dice-hoarders, to another expedition into the wild, undead-infested jungles of tabletop gaming. Today we’re crawling through the cobblestone streets of Zombicide: Black Plague, the medieval re-skin of the classic co-op zombie bash that somehow manages to be chaotic, charming, and terrifyingly loud in equal measure. If you’ve ever shouted at a table because your knight forgot to stab a zombie who was standing right in front of him, this review is for you.

## What is Zombicide: Black Plague?

Zombicide: Black Plague is a cooperative board game where 1–6 players take on the roles of survivors during a zombie apocalypse—only the zombies are less polite and more monolithic in their desire to eat your brains. The Black Plague version features a medieval fantasy setting with knights, archers, mages, and farmers who discovered that swordplay is an excellent way to cut down on brain-explaining. The goal in most scenarios is to complete specific objectives (retrieve an artifact, open a gate, rescue a survivor, etc.) and then escape or survive until the clock runs out. It’s not just about brute force; it’s about planning, dice logic, and the occasional moment of “how did we survive that?”

### Thematic lift

Black Plague uses a skin that makes the undead feel like they are storming a castle rather than a suburban mall. Armor clanks, magic sparkles, and chain mail seems to jingle every time a zombie staggers into a tile. The theme is pulpy sword-and-sorcery, with enough grimdark vibes to please fans of fantasy RPGs but not so heavy that it stops being a party game. It’s a mixed bag of dungeon-crawl vibes and siege-of-the-when-40k-was-a-thing energy, and that mix is part of the charm.

## Components and presentation

The base game comes with a metric ton of miniatures, tokens, cards, dice, and a board that looks like someone spilled a pot of gold over a medieval battlefield and then forgot to sweep up the corpses. Here’s the quick rundown:

- Plastic miniatures: survivors, zombies (walkers, runners, and some specials), monsters; they’re sturdy and pose well for photos for the #boardgamephotography crowd.
- Cards: equipment, skills, and scenarios. The card art is vivid, with a crunchy flavor that sells the fantasy setting without becoming a trading card nightmare.
- Dice: color-coded dice for combat and encounters. The dice are chunky and satisfying; yes, they make a satisfying clatter when you roll three doubles in a turn and immediately regret your life choices.
- Board and tiles: modular board tiles that allow for different map layouts every game. The layout, combined with the scenario cards, keeps things fresh across plays.

Pros here: the components feel premium for a board game of its age; the figurines look good on a shelf; the artwork and icons communicate rules quickly once you get the hang of them. Cons: the sheer number of pieces can be a lot to manage on a small table, especially if you’re absent-minded like me and keep losing track of which zombie tile is which type.

## Setup and first impressions

Setup is not the most trivial part of Zombicide: Black Plague, but it’s not a monster either. You’ll lay out the board, place spawn tokens, shuffle the equipment deck, and pick your survivors. The most important part of setup is this: decide if you’re going to play a single scenario or a short campaign. If you choose the campaign path, you’ll appreciate the way power-ups and injuries accumulate across sessions; if you choose one-offs, you’ll get a nice, tight experience that ends before your friends remember the Alabama-level heat on the table during July.

Early impressions: the core loop is crisp enough that you’ll want to keep playing, but enough randomness and variety that you won’t feel like you’re stuck in a long grind. The balance between planning and chaos is the motor that makes games like this sing. You’ll feel like a heroic band of misfits, valiantly misfiring crossbows and magic wands, while also realizing you’re probably being hunted by a zombie apocalypse that is always one wrong dice roll away from a TPK (total party kill).

## Core mechanics explained (without spoilers)

Zombicide: Black Plague runs on a few simple but interlocking ideas:

### Activation and initiative
Each survivor in turn order activates, performing actions like moving, searching, opening doors, or trading items with teammates. The zombie deck and spawn tiles drive the nerve-wracking pressure of the game: the more you advance, the more zombies spawn, and the board blurs into a chaotic swirl of red chits and pink dice as you run for your life.

### Combat and dice pools
Combat is dice-based. You’ll roll a handful of dice depending on your weapon and the situation, looking for hits while trying not to roll too many “misses” while a zombie bashes you with the next breath they can steal. Armor helps, as do ranged weapons and specialized equipment. The thrill is in landing a combo: you shoot a zombie with a bow, slice another with a greatsword, and your mage uses an AoE to clear a cluster—only for the last zombie to swarm your healer’s exact position because the board was feeling dramatic that day.

### Zombies and board pressure
Zombies in Black Plague aren’t tricky in mechanical terms; they’re tricky in timing. They’re relentless, and when the spawn gets heavy, you’ll feel the pressure like a tabletop pressure cooker about to explode. You’ll also marvel at the cinematic “soundtrack” of the game—the clamor of metal, the sizzle of magic, and the occasional scream that is probably yours when you roll three misses in a row. The zombies don’t think; they just push forward, clustering into dense mobs that threaten to overwhelm the party if you’re not careful.

### Survivors and equipment
Your survivors are varied and flavorful: knights, healers, archers, mages, and everyday folk who discovered that clubbing a zombie with a shovel is surprisingly satisfying. Equipment cards grant weapons, armor, and items that tilt the odds in your favor. The table will know you’re serious when someone wins the draw of the “Powerful Potion” and pretends to be its long-lost alchemist uncle.

## Scenarios, campaigns, and replayability

One of Black Plague’s strongest suits is how scenarios provide both a plan and a twist. Each scenario has a map layout, a set of objectives, and a unique spawn behavior. The replayability comes from two sources: the random board layout and the deck of equipment and event cards you’ll draw along the way. If you’re the kind of player who loves meta-challenges, you’ll enjoy trying to optimize survivor placements, timing your searches, and coordinating team actions to maximize efficiency.

Campaign mode, on the other hand, slowly threads through a story arc. As players complete scenarios, their survivors gain experience, unlock new equipment, and may gain new abilities. The longer you play, the more you feel like you the soap opera of heroes in a zombie-infested world: you forget the simple joys of a peaceful week and remember that every choice could be the difference between escaping a castle and becoming a zombie’s snack.

If you want a taste of the campaign feel without commitment, try mixing in a couple of scenarios from different campaigns. The modular nature of the game makes that approach approachable, and you’ll likely discover a new favorite combination of map layout and objective design.

## Experience, pacing, and table talk

The core experience is best described as energetic, occasionally chaotic, and deeply satisfying when the dice gods smile upon you. Pacing can swing from quick, sharp bursts of action to long, thoughtful planning turns where you debate whether to risk a search or wait for a better window. There’s a social aspect that is as important as the mechanics: teamwork becomes the glue, and the silly chaos of a failed plan becomes the punchline of the night.

The table talk is essential: you’ll coordinate, bluff, laugh, and sometimes groan together as a plan collapses under a poor spawn roll. That is not failure; that is the story you’ll tell at the next game night when you’re trying to explain why the tavern’s floor still smells like melted candle wax and victory smells like victory coffee and fear.

## Pros and cons (TL;DR version)

Pros
- Cooperative play with strong player interaction and shared planning.
- High table presence: miniatures and art make for great display photos and conversation starters.
- Re-playability through modular boards, varied scenarios, and equipment cards.
- Scales reasonably well from 2 to 6 players; group dynamics add energy or chaos, depending on your tolerance for chaos.
- Thematically rich and accessible to many players, including non-gamers who enjoy a vivid fantasy vibe.

Cons
- Setup and teardown can take a while, especially when you’re chasing tiny tokens around the table like a caffeinated raccoon.
- The rulebook can feel crunchy; some corner cases require a sticky note and a quick house rule to keep the game moving.
- Randomness can be punishing: bad spawn draws or a string of misses can end a round in minutes, which may frustrate if you’re chasing a perfectly optimized round.
- Space requirements: a big table is your best friend here; if you’re at a cafe table, consider a quick card game instead.

## Final thoughts: who is this for?

If you like co-op play, you’ll love the social dynamic of Zombicide: Black Plague. It’s a great pick for groups of three to five players who enjoy a mix of strategy, luck, and storytelling. Fans of fantasy and role-playing games will find the theme especially appealing, as will players who relish good-natured mayhem and “one more turn!” moments. It scales nicely for newcomers who want to learn the ropes of modern board-game combat without diving straight into the dense dungeon crawls of an RPG.

If your game group tends toward quick sessions with tight, linear objectives, this may feel like a longer commitment. If you’re the type who thrives on narrative arcs, character progression, and the thrill of coordinating a successful blind run against a horde, you’ll be hooked.

## Gear, expansions, and future-proofing

There’s no shortage of add-ons for Zombicide: Black Plague. The base game is already generous, but expansions introduce new survivor kits, new zombie palettes, and new map tiles that tilt the odds in surprising ways. The Green Horde expansion, for example, brings a taste of forested terror with different terrain and new synergy with existing gear. There are also standalone campaigns that mix in a variety of magical and monstrous encounters, letting your group blend fantasy and survival in new and exciting ways.

If you’re curious about the broader Zombicide line, there’s a family of games and spin-offs to explore, each with its own setting and creature collection. The core appeal remains the same: cooperative play, crunchy-but-not-too-cranky rules, and a heavy emphasis on teamwork under pressure. This is a game that rewards planning without punishing you for a single misstep: you’ll likely learn something from every run, even if it’s just “don’t stand there with your back to the zombie spawn.”

## A few practical tips (for smooth sailing)

- Start small. Pick a 2-player session to learn the mechanics, then scale up. The jump from 2 to 4 players is where the chaos becomes genuinely cinematic.
- Agree on a house rule for late-game spawn pressure. A little pre-agreed mercy can prevent the table from melting into a chorus of groans.
- Use a dedicated space. A big, clean table with proper lighting makes pictures look better, and you’ll thank yourself when you’re counting a dozen zombie figures without knocking over the board.
- Plan for downtime. This is not a fast game. Build your night around a few rounds, not just one long sprint; it’s a social experience as much as a strategy exercise.
- Have snacks ready. Glorious, crunchy, zombie-shaped snacks are optional but recommended; it boosts morale and makes the table feel like a camp after a successful foray.

## Notes on accessibility and inclusivity

Zombicide: Black Plague is approachable for newcomers to modern board games thanks to its clear iconography and the cooperative nature of play. The thematic elements can appeal to a broad audience, but as with any game centered on gore-adjacent undead, it’s wise to check with players about comfort levels with violence in a game setting. For players who want to ease into heavier strategy cons while still enjoying the fantasy vibe, a gentle introduction with a shorter scenario can be the perfect primer.
