Five Crowns is the kind of game that makes your family dinners feel like a mini board-game cafe, only with fewer baristas and more crown puns. If you like Rummy but want a regal twist, Five Crowns is your jam.

## What is Five Crowns?
Five Crowns is a rummy-style card game designed for 2-7 players. The deck is special: a 116-card collection across five suits, giving you more color and more chances to form melds than a standard deck can dream of. The rounds run from 3 cards per player in Round 1 to 13 cards in Round 11, so the game grows with your appetite for chaos and snacks.

In each round, players draft and discard to assemble melds—sets (three or more cards of the same rank in different suits) or runs (three or more consecutive cards of the same suit). At the end of a round, players tally the points in their hand; lower is better. The person with the fewest points after all 11 rounds wins the game. It’s simple enough for a family night, but intricate enough for seasoned gamers to enjoy a second or third game-night session.

## Components and Setup
- 116-card deck with five suits (imagine a regular deck, but five times fancier).
- A note: Five Crowns uses a standard rule-set with no jokers.
- A rule sheet (optional but recommended if your kids insist on deck choreography).
- Scorepad or app for keeping score.

To set up: shuffle the deck thoroughly (yes, the first round is the only time you’ll say “I shuffled enough.” That point will vanish quickly). Deal cards to each player: 3 cards in Round 1, then 4 in Round 2, and so on, up to 13 in Round 11. Place the remaining deck in the center as a draw pile, and flip the top card to start the discard pile.

## How to Play (In a Nutshell)
- Objective: form melds and get rid of all your cards by the end of a round.
- Turn structure: draw one card (either from the stock or the discard pile), then place one or more melds on the table (if you can) and discard one card to end your turn.
- Melds: three or more cards of the same rank in any suits (a set) or three or more consecutive cards of the same suit (a run). In Five Crowns, you’re constantly watching for opportunities to cluster cards into stylish, point-saving combos while dodging cards you don’t need like a true data-dump you can’t unsee.
- Ending a round: you can “go out” when you can fold your hand into melds plus a discard. If you go out, you’ll score zero or very low points for that round; if you don’t, you’ll accumulate deadwood points equal to the values of the unmelded cards.

### Scoring
- Card values: number cards are worth their face value; face cards are usually 10 points; Aces are 15? Not exactly. Most versions use each card’s value equal to its number, face cards 10, Aces 15 (or sometimes 11). We’re not playing for a math degree here, so pick one sheet and stick with it.
- If you go out, you can subtract some points by finishing your hand quickly, but if you’re stuck, you’ll face the scoreboard curse of lugging around high numbers.

### A Sample Round (Walkthrough)
- Round 3 (3 cards each): you’ll have a small hand and must decide which cards to meld early; risk is high because you have fewer cards to work with. You’ll likely use a couple of short runs, perhaps a tiny set if you pick up a matching rank across suits.
- Example hands: You have 3, 6, 7, 9 of different suits; your melds could be 3-4-5 in any suit (if you can draw a 4 and a 5) or 6-7-8 in a single suit (if you draw the right cards). The discard pile becomes your canvas and your opponents? They’re busy taunting you through memes and questionable jokes.

## Why Five Crowns Works For Friends & Family
- Accessibility: The rules are straightforward enough for new players to pick up in one sitting. There’s enough depth to keep it interesting for experienced gamers as well.
- Interaction: The discard-and-draw mechanic fosters social banter, trash talk, and friendly heckling without turning into a test of endurance. It’s a game-night monsoon of fun.
- Family-friendly: The five-suit deck brings bright colors and a regal vibe that appeals to kids and adults alike. It’s the kind of game you can teach in 10 minutes and still play for an hour or two.
- The pace: 11 rounds with escalating card counts keep the energy up. You start with something simple and end with something deliciously chaotic—like a well-baked pizza that somehow acquired a crown.

### Tips and Strategy for Beginners
- Learn to read the discard pile: If your opponent discards a card you’re after, it’s a hint that they might be trying to block you or that card will help you form a particular meld later. If you see a pattern, you can plan.
- Start with small melds: Early rounds reward you for small, clean melds rather than waiting for a perfect hand. The speed of escalation means you’ll want to unpack a few sets and runs sooner rather than later.
- Don’t overcommit: It’s tempting to go for a long-run strategy, but if you’re forced to maintain a long deadwood line, you’ll pay the price at the end of the round.
- Plan for the endgame: Remember that you’re aiming to go out with a clean hand. If you can see two easy melds, you should keep your options open to avoid getting stuck on your last card.

### Strategy for Intermediate and Advanced Players
- Card counting approach: Track which ranks are slipping away and which suits are overrepresented in your opponents’ hands. This isn’t about nuclear-level math; it’s about noticing patterns.
- Meld diversity: Balanced melds (two runs and one set, rather than three runs of the same color) often yield better results in the late rounds.
- The “block” tactic: If you’re behind on points, you might choose to avoid giving away specific cards by discarding less helpful cards, forcing opponents to draw more often.
- Endgame finesse: In the late rounds (Round 9 to 11), the desk changes; you want to look for quick combos that can be formed with the number of cards in your current hand. If you can see three-card sets without too many wildcards, you’ll boost your odds.

### Clarifications and House Rules
- Wild cards: Some households add a rotating wild-card mechanic to spice things up. If you like chaos, declare a wildcard for that round by turning up a card from the top of the deck; the rank of that card becomes wild for the round. All suits of that rank become wild, and you can use them in your melds. If you don’t want wild cards, just skip this rule.
- Extra player-friendly rules: If you have younger players or want to speed up rounds, reduce the number of cards in the early rounds or extend the endgame with shorter rounds.

### A Quick Round Demo (Step-by-Step)
- Step 1: Shuffle and deal three cards to each player.
- Step 2: Turn up the next card as the starting discard; the remainder is the stock.
- Step 3: On your turn, draw a card, decide whether to meld, and discard one card.
- Step 4: After each turn, check whether you can form a meld.
- Step 5: When a player discards their last card, the round ends. Players tally their deadwood; the player who went out scores zero for that round and the others tally their cards.
