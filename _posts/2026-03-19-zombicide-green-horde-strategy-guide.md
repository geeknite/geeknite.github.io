## Green Horde at a Glance
Green Horde is a fantasy-era spin on the classic Zombicide engine. The adversaries lean into orcs, goblins, and spell-slinging nasties rather than your standard shamblers. The objective cards can demand anything from wiping out a certain number of enemies to securing artifacts or sealing portals. The board is modular, which means your setup will look different every time you shuffle the map. That variability keeps the game fresh and ensures that no two campaigns feel identical, which is essential for the long-haul co-op experience.

What makes Green Horde compelling is not just the theme but the design philosophy: fun, fast, and a little feral. A single round can swing on a well-timed spell, a critical loot drop, or a miscalculated dash through a choke point that becomes a heroic sacrifice (or hilarious blunder). The game invites you to experiment with roles and tactics, to try combing spells with swords, and to learn that sometimes your best plan is your second-best plan executed with gusto.

## Core Mechanics Refresher
If you have played Zombicide before, Green Horde will feel familiar in its core: four actions per survivor per turn, a strong emphasis on cooperation, and a dice-driven combat system where luck interrupts your plans with comedic regularity. The map’s terrain changes the math: a wall or corner can turn a straightforward melee charge into a face-to-face standoff with a corridor-walking goblin, which is exactly the kind of moment your party rehearsed but never rehearsed well enough.

Line of sight matters. Some enemies can see and shoot across long lines, others require you to take a different route to reach their vulnerable flank. Fire, ice, and arcane buffs can tilt the balance in your favor, but you must manage cooldowns and mana (or mana-adjacent resources) with care. As the rounds progress, your plan should evolve from “how do we survive this wave” to “how do we extract the artifact and keep everyone alive.”

### Turn structure and action economy
Each survivor has a pool of four actions to spend per turn. You can mix and match moves, searches, attacks, and special actions granted by weapons or artifacts. Spells and equipment often provide strategic options that aren’t strictly about raw damage; a well-placed buff can turn a stubborn stalemate into a clean victory. The objective is to maximize the impact of each action while minimizing wasted steps. A classic pitfall is overextending a single hero, then watching the rest of the party crumble under a horde surge.

### Loot, equipment, and resource management
Loot matters. You’ll want a plan for what to loot and when to equip it. Early picks often include flexible weapons, armor that cushions hits, and a small selection of consumables for critical moments. Mid-game you start eyeing artifacts that transform the way you approach a board state, and late-game you want to ensure you have at least one plan for a super-charged turn that clears the last few waves. The trick is to balance immediate needs with long-term plans: don’t spend all your good loot on one round if you’ll need that item later to finish the objective.

### Teams and roles
Green Horde rewards thoughtful team composition. A balanced team typically includes:
- A frontline bruiser who can seize space and threaten multiple enemies.
- A ranged combatant who can pressure the horde from a safe distance.
- A spell caster or support character who can buff allies or debuff enemies.
- The fourth slot is flexible; choose a survivor who can adapt to mission-specific needs, such as healing or fast repositioning.

This balance isn’t dogma. You’ll learn through play that sometimes a chaotic, all-out offensive team can work if you catch the wave early and shut down the biggest threats. The key is to communicate and adjust on the fly rather than commit to a rigid plan that will quickly crumble when something unexpected shows up.

## Terrain, Positioning, and Board Control
Terrain is your best friend and your loudest enemy. Doors can become choke points; stairwells and narrow passages matter; high ground and lines of sight define which targets you can safely engage. A common approach is to anchor on a strong defensive tile with partial cover while you distribute ranged and mage support to cover the flanks. The enemy’s spawn pattern often rewards players who set up two or more safe zones, allowing you to rotate survivors through different roles without losing momentum.

Always look for opportunities to trap the horde into kill zones. A well-placed trap or an allied’s buff might be the difference between a clean round and a multi-turn dent in your party. If you can cut off a wave’s escape route, you not only survive the current turn but set up future rounds to be even more predictable and controlled.

## Looting, Inventory, and Synergy
Loot is not just loot; it’s a narrative device that lets your team translate raw numbers into tactical outcomes. Early on you’ll want to grab weapons that fit your role (melee for bruisers, bows for archers, staves or wands for spellcasters). Armor tokens reduce incoming damage; healing potions let you push through perilous rounds; and artifacts unlock special abilities that can swing a fight in your favor when used at the right moment.

Inventory management becomes a game in itself. Don’t hold onto everything forever; you will run out of turns and the board will punish you for hoarding. A practical rule of thumb is to equip items you will actually use within the next two turns or two waves. If you have a powerful artifact that requires two rounds to charge before it triggers, plan your turns around that guarantee rather than hoping for the best.

## Enemies and Special Encounters
Green Horde features a variety of foes: standard orcs and goblin skirmishers, plus specials that manipulate the battlefield in unpredictable ways. The goblin shaman might snipe buffs off your best character, while a hulking orc brute can steamroll a poor frontline if left unchecked. Special enemies often have predictable patterns once you observe them. The key is to control who they target and when you neutralize them, rather than trying to brute-force every wave.

Understanding enemy behavior helps you allocate your actions efficiently. If you know that a certain group will spawn behind you next turn, you might choose to retreat and re-engage from a safer angle rather than pressing a reckless advance that collapses your formation.

## Scenario Walkthroughs: Two Representative Maps
To demonstrate how these ideas play out in practice, here are two representative scenarios that illustrate typical decision trees. These aren’t official mission names; they are composites meant to show how you can apply the guide in real life.

### Scenario A: The Gatehouse Gambit
- Objective: Seal two gates and prevent the horde from flooding your camp.
- Setup: A corridor-heavy layout with two gates at opposite ends. You want to establish a secure axis while you approach one gate at a time.
- Strategy: Put a sturdy frontline near the gate to block the major breach path. Keep your ranged and spell users behind the front line, ready to punish any horde that tries to push through. If you have a mage, drop a zoning spell at the mouth of the corridor to slow down wave generation. Collect keys and objectives from mid-board tiles to unlock gate seals. A successful gate seal often buys you multiple rounds of breathing room and forces the horde to funnel into predictable kill zones.
- Pitfalls: Don’t overcommit one survivor into the gate corridor; if you lose your anchor, the other gate becomes vulnerable and the whole plan collapses in a single turn.
- Outcome: A well-executed Gatehouse Gambit buys critical time, enables better loot distribution, and dramatically lowers the risk of a horde sprint overwhelming your group.

### Scenario B: The Ember Keep Heist
- Objective: Retrieve a powerful artifact from a ruined keep.
- Setup: The keep has multiple floors and several stairwells. You’ll need to split the party strategically to gather the artifact while preventing the horde from swarming your position.
- Strategy: Assign one group to carve a path to the artifact, while the other group locks down the approach routes and clears the top floors. The artifact will empower one or two survivors with a game-changing effect; time its use for when you need to swing the tide in a crucial wave. Use summoning or teleportation effects (if available) to reposition and create an optimal cross-board attack window.
- Pitfalls: Splitting too aggressively without adequate defensive coverage invites a quick wipe. Keep the group’s healer within a few turns’ reach of both squads, or be prepared to pivot to a safer plan.
- Outcome: If synchronized, the Ember Keep Heist delivers a satisfying blend of tension, clever routing, and a sense of heroic luck that makes you forget about that one poor dice roll from earlier in the game.

## House Rules and Variants
If you want to tailor Green Horde to your group’s preferences, consider these light variants:
- Fatigue Meter: Track fatigue per survivor. After a certain number of rounds, you reduce the available actions or impose a small penalty. This adds a layer of resource management and makes long campaigns feel more tense.
- Hard Mode Boss Turn: On certain rounds, a powerful boss action triggers automatically. This forces players to adapt and avoid predictable patterns.
- Narrative Flourish: Allow players to craft minor flavor text for critical moments. It makes the storytelling aspect of co-op games richer and improves group morale after a spectacular failure.
- Quick Start Variant: For new players, reduce the number of starting minions and simplify the objective cards to make the first few games feel approachable and fun.

## Post-Game Debrief
After a session, take a moment to reflect. What worked well? Which decisions came back to bite you in the next wave? A calm debrief often yields more than a second replay does. Try to assign three things the group did well, three things to improve, and one bold idea to try next game. Humor helps—nothing breaks tension like a collective groan followed by a good-natured joke about the dice gods going on vacation.

## Tips for Players and Night-Long Play
- Communicate early and often. Let others know if you plan to move through a doorway, take a risky shot, or use a powerful spell that will affect the next wave.
- Balance is essential. Don’t hoard loot or use all resources in a single round. You will need to pace yourself as the board grows more intense.
- Don’t forget the terrain. Chokepoints are not just obstacles; they are tactical advantages if used correctly.
- Use the right tool for the job. If a particular artifact seems powerful but requires a few rounds to charge, plan its use for the moment when it will matter most.
- Keep morale high. In co-op games, the right mood is often your best asset; a good laugh after a tough round goes a long way toward keeping players engaged.
