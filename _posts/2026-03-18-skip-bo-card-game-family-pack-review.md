## How Skip-Bo Works (A Quick Refresher)
If you’ve never played Skip-Bo, or you’ve only heard it whispered in the same breath as “uno” at family reunions, here’s the long and short of it. Each player arranges a few stock piles face down in front of them. Then, players draw cards and attempt to play cards in ascending order onto the central build piles. The twist is that the deck includes Skip-Bo wilds, which can substitute any number to help you finish your stock piles faster—kind of like a cheat code, but legal and tabletop-adjacent.

The Family Pack doesn’t change the fundamentals: you still want to empty your stock piles first. Your turns hinge on drawing a certain number of cards (usually five), and then aggressively mutating your hand into playable numbers. The chaotic joy comes from competing for the same build piles, bluffing about your next move in a friendly way, and occasionally realizing you’ve just hoisted a colossal waste of a Skip-Bo wild that could have been used on the crucial run you were building. It’s competitive, fast-paced, and oddly satisfying for turning rational people into frenetic spreadsheet enthusiasts for 20-minute increments.

Skip-Bo’s replay value comes from the variability of each hand and the endless combinations of play orders. With two decks in the Family Pack, you’ll generate a greater number of possible runs. That, in turn, invites experimentation: should you dump lower-numbered cards first to clear your stock piles quickly, or should you reserve certain numbers for a future turn that could unlock multiple sequences?

Here are a few heuristic tips that tend to help in most rounds:

- Don’t forget the power of pressure: if you can subtly imply you’re about to make a crucial run, you can sometimes nudge others into suboptimal plays.
