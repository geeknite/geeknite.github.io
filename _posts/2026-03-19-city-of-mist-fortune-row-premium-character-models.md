## Build quality: the tangible truth behind the hype

### Sculpting detail and silhouette

Premium models live or die by silhouette clarity. Fortune Row delivers bold shapes. You can see the weight of the villain, the silhouette of the detective, and the subtlety of a reluctant ally even from a couple of feet away. The cloaks billow with cinematic intent; jackets and lapels tilt just so; bracelets and sigils catch the light instead of falling into the dreaded “blob with a gun” aesthetic. The downside? In some angles, particularly under bright lighting or macro photography, you might spot minor soft edges along the cape folds. It’s not a deal-breaker, but it’s worth noting if you’re aiming for museum-grade display pieces rather than tabletop heroes.

### Material, casting, and cleanup

Fortune Row uses a resin that’s firm but not brittle. The casting is consistent—no stray flash are these miniatures, which is a nice surprise for a premium line. Expect a minimal amount of cleanup, primarily nubs at sleeve cuffs or tiny feed marks along edge lines. A quick snap-off and a light sanding should have you ready for primer without the dread of losing an eye to a mini jackhammer with a Dremel on high speed. If you’re a new painter, you’ll appreciate that the surfaces accept primer evenly. If you’re a seasoned painter, you’ll enjoy the challenge of achieving a gradient on a cloak that looks like you consumed a sunset and then walked through it with a paintbrush.

### Poseability and assembly

Poses are deliberate but not prohibitively complex. You won’t need three-week assembly sessions to set your squad — you can glue, align, and place the models with a steady hand and moderate patience. The main fighters come with one or two alternative head options, which is a nice touch for differentiation without requiring a surgeon’s toolkit to swap. There’s enough room in the joints for some natural posing without sacrificing stability, which means you won’t have to hang the models from your ceiling to prevent them from toppling during a heroic pose. If you’re into dynamic dioramas, Fortune Row plays nicely with shattered street-light bases and weathering powders to evoke a moody dusk vibe.

## Paint quality and recommended approaches

Color strategy matters as much as the sculpt. Fortune Row figures lend themselves to a palette that emphasizes smoky blues, deep crimsons, and metallic highlights for sigils. If you’re comfortable with contrast paints, you can quickly push the characters to a high level of drama with a few deliberate washes and edge highlights. For painters who enjoy subtlety, you’ll want a restrained palette that avoids neon anime tones in favor of muted urban twilight. A few practical tips:

- Prime with a neutral gray to capture fine detail, then build up mid-tones.
- Use a glaze (blue-gray) over the cloak to emphasize shadow under an overcast neon city.
- Apply a controlled metallic edge on sigils or weapons to catch the light but not scream “cosplay weapon.”
- Finish with a matte varnish to preserve the noir atmosphere and prevent plastic glare in photos.

## Painting and assembly tips tailored for Fortune Row

- Prime in gray or white to reveal fine detail; avoid black primer unless you’re going for a heavy shadow effect.
- Basecoat in midtones that align with the row’s theme; you’ll save time with a deliberate color scheme from the start.
- Layer with midtones and then a wash to bring the recesses to life; a blue-gray wash on the cloaks works wonders for a moody, rainy-night feel.
- Dry brush metallics sparingly on sigils or jewelry to add a bit of pop without overwhelming the piece.
- Seal with a matte varnish to preserve the look if your table sees frequent handling or the odd spilled beverage.
