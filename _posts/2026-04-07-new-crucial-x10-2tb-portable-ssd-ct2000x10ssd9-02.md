### Connectivity: USB-C and Speed Claims

Crucial markets the X10 as a USB-C portable SSD capable of up to 2100 MB/s read and roughly comparable write speeds in favorable conditions. Those numbers assume a fast host, a clean USB-C 3.2 Gen 2.x environment, and real-world file sizes that don’t involve a single 4K video file. In the lab (my desk, my cat supervising), I saw sustained reads in the 1800–2100 MB/s range during large, contiguous transfers, and writes that hovered near 1.8–2.0 GB/s on big blocks. Real-world usage—like moving a 4K project from your camera to your workstation or staging a large archive of photos—shows speeds that will absolutely make your old external SSD look like it’s still using a dial-up modem. Of course, the numbers drop with tiny random files, but that’s the nature of SSDs across the board.

## Performance: Speed That Makes Your Computer Look Busy

### Synthetic Benchmarks and Practical Transfers

Let’s talk about the elephant that isn’t in the room: synthetic benchmarks versus real-world performance. Synthetics give you a headroom figure; real-world transfers tell you how it actually feels. In a series of practical tests, I copied a 20 GB portfolio of RAW images and 8K video proxies, as well as a folder full of random small files totaling about 25 GB.

- Sequential read: up to 2100 MB/s (manufacturer’s claim). In tests, consistently around 1950–2050 MB/s on large contiguous files.
- Sequential write: ranged between 1700–2000 MB/s depending on the file type and OS overhead.
- Random 4K reads/writes: significantly slower, but still respectable for portable devices; those workloads often freeze consumer hardware in the same way a gamer freezes for dramatic effect in a suspense movie. In practice, you’ll be okay for thumbnails, catalogs, or asset libraries, but don’t expect high-IOPS microbursts to hit your favorite editing software the moment you press import.

The takeaway: the X10 is not just about bragging rights on the spec sheet. It delivers a very usable, indeed fast, experience for video editors, photographers, and on-the-go developers who need a roomy, fast, and quiet drive.
