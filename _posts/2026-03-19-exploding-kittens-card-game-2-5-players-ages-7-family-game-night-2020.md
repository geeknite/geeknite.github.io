## What is Exploding Kittens

Exploding Kittens is a fast, silly, and sometimes viciously strategic card game that centers on avoiding drawing an exploding kitten card. The deck is a mix of kitten cards, defuse cards, attack cards, skip cards, and a handful of other playful tools that let you mess with your friends. The goal is simple on the surface: avoid exploding kittens while playing cards to manipulate the draw pile, force others into danger, and sometimes defuse your own misfortune with a defuse card. The 2-5 players version is the most social and chaotic of the bunch, and the 2020 family edition gives it a slightly more approachable aura with kid-friendly humor and bright visuals.

### Setup

Setting up Exploding Kittens is a ritual of anticipation:

1) Shuffle the deck. 2) Remove a number of Exploding Kitten cards equal to the number of players minus one. 3) Give each player a Defuse card to keep and then deal a few starting cards to each player. 4) Place the draw pile in the center. 5) Decide who goes first.

The rules are straightforward, but the joy comes from knowing that each round can swing dramatically based on what cards you hold and which cards your friends play. The 2-5 player cap makes it an intimate experience—sitting across the table and jockeying for control often feels more intense than it looks on the box. This is a game that rewards reading the room almost as much as reading the deck, and that is where a lot of the humor and chaos comes from.

## How to Play: A Quick Overview

Exploding Kittens plays like a ritualistic ritual of chaos. Each player has a hand of cards including attack cards (which can force others to draw multiple times), defuse cards (which save you from an exploding fate), skip cards (to dodge the inevitable moment of doom), and various other cards that manipulate the draw pile and players. On your turn, you can play cards or draw from the pile. If you draw an Exploding Kitten and you don’t have a Defuse, you are out of the round. If you do have a Defuse, you can play it to defuse the kitten and place the Exploding Kitten back into the deck at a location of your choice, adding a mini game of mind games to the chaos.

There is something delightful about the tension of whether to risk a draw or to flip a coin on who will be the unlucky soul to pull the kitten. The Attack and Skip cards create cycles that turn the table into a rotating carnival ride, and the Defuse cards act as lifelines that add a shimmer of hope. The item here is not just luck; it is the art of baiting your opponents into a bad move and then snapping back with a well-timed defuse if you are lucky enough to have one in your hand.

The rule set is intentionally light, which makes it easy for new players to jump in. The challenge is maintaining your composure while your friends push for the exact moment to drop a strategic move that screws over the person who just teased you with a perfect combo. The comedic payoff is the reason people return to Exploding Kittens after the first meltdown, as the memes and the mischief keep surfacing with new groups and new strategies.

## Strategy and House Rules

Exploding Kittens is not a deep tactical game, but there is a lot of room for clever misdirection and short-term tactical decisions. Here are a few tips that can help you tilt the odds, especially in a family setting where players of different ages are around the table.

- Read the room: In a mixed group, the best moves are often those that avoid escalating tension. If someone is about to snap, you may want to counter with a defensive move or a Skip instead of an aggressive attack.
- Use See the Future wisely: Looking ahead can prevent you from drawing a kitten, but it also reveals options for others. Weave your See the Future plays into a larger plan rather than using them as a quick peek to finish a round.
- Defuse with style: If you hold a Defuse, decide whether to use it now or save it for a later round. The timing matters, and the moment you choose to defuse can have ripple effects that shape the next few rounds.
- The last few cards matter: In the endgame, the deck can become a pressure cooker. You may want to hold onto a few defensive cards to lock in the victory, or go all-out if you realize the risk-reward is favorable.

House rules can also spice things up. For example, you could implement a mini rule where if a player uses a particular card to target someone with a cute hat, that player must tell a cat joke before finishing the move. It sounds silly, but it can add a little extra flavor and give everyone something to laugh about during the chaos.
