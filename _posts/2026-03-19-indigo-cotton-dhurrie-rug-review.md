## Material, construction, and why handwoven cotton matters
### The Dhurrie technique explained
Dhurrie is a type of flatweave rug with a history as old as the roads in an old caravanserai. Unlike pile rugs, which have loops or cut piles that stand tall, a dhurrie sits flat on the floor, offering a sleek silhouette and a lie-flat, no-trip design. The cotton fibers used for this rug are typically tightly woven, which gives durability and a crisp surface that cleans off with a quick vacuum pass.

Handwoven indicates craft in motion. Each rug has its own tiny irregularities—slight variations in weave tension, occasional knot slips, a line that’s a millimeter thicker here or there. When you buy a handwoven piece, you’re re-homing a moment of a maker’s day, usually with a smile and a cup of chai nearby. The charm is in the imperfect perfection, the human touch that mass-produced factories simply cannot replicate.

### Material breakdown and feel underfoot
Cotton is the midweight champion of rugs. It’s soft enough to be comfortable on bare feet but durable enough to handle daily traffic if you keep your expectations reasonable. Cotton is also naturally breathable, which means less sweating on hot days and reduced odor retention compared to some synthetic fibers. This rug wears well under chairs and coffee tables, and it doesn’t pill aggressively the way some low-pile synthetics can when you have a dog who loves dramatic sprints around the living room.

## Maintenance and care: keeping the indigo radiant
### Everyday care and stain management
Cotton rugs are relatively easy to care for. Regular vacuuming with a gentle setting helps remove surface dust and debris, and rotating the rug every few months keeps wear patterns even if your furniture creates a frequent traffic pattern. For small spills, blot with a clean cloth; avoid rubbing, which can push liquid deeper into the fibers. If you encounter a stubborn stain, a gentle mixture of mild detergent and water can be used to spot-clean, followed by air drying. Do not machine wash or tumble dry, since that can distort the weave and shrink the cotton.

### Special note on indigo colorfastness
Indigo can bleed a little initially, especially if the rug is new and you have light-framed furniture. A splash of color transfer is more likely in the first few weeks of ownership, so consider a lower-friction underlay and be mindful of placing white furniture or light-colored fabrics directly on contact until you’ve done a few passes with the vacuum. A quick test on a hidden corner can help you gauge colorfastness and your guilt when you spill coffee on it in a hurry.

## Buying guide: what to look for when selecting a handwoven cotton dhurrie
