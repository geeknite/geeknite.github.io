In Geeknite fashion, the Unhappy Homes Expansion for Gloom 2E is the cinematic sneeze you never knew your attic needed. ## Overview

The Unhappy Homes Expansion introduces a fresh architectural concept to Gloom 2E: the house itself becomes a character with moods, rooms, and consequences. The core idea is simple: you don’t just brood about your family’s misfortunes in isolation; you brood within a house that frowns upon you for streaming your own misfortune too loudly.

### What's in the Box

- 6 new Family cards, each with their own quirky negative “story” that stacks with others to create a houseful of sorrow
- 8 House Cards that generate new room effects, including the infamous corridor that traps you in reflective thought
- 6 Event Cards that force sudden remodels, or sudden levels of moodiness so dramatic your grandmother would approve
- 20 Mood Tokens (yes, the mood ring finally has a role in your life now)
- A new scoring overlay to track house-level gloom beyond individual families

## Rules and Setup

### Setup

Set up is more brain-friendly than you might fear. You shuffle the new Family cards into the gloom deck, lay out a House Card base, and grant every player a small handful of Mood Tokens to symbolize the house’s current mood. The new “House Mood” mechanic means you’ll be tracking a House Mood track as well as the traditional hand of gloom cards. It’s not fiddly; it’s just fussy enough to make you type a snarky comment to the rules and then forget to do something important like bidding for a coffin in week two.

### Core Mechanics (What Changes)

- House Cards interact with Mood Tokens to alter how gloom cards resolve.
- Family cards increasingly punish players for “interrupting the house’s vibe.”
- Event Cards can force a remodeling phase where you trade in gloom cards for new House Cards that shift the board state.

The net effect is a more dynamic play area without turning Gloom into a spreadsheet of doom. If base Gloom’s elegance is the baseline, you’ll appreciate the expansion’s architectural flair without sacrificing your favorite grim punchlines.

## Card Breakdown

### New Family Cards

Here are a couple to whet your appetite:

- The Grumbling Grandparent: When this card is in play, performing a move during the remodeling phase triggers one extra negative mood token on yourself, because nothing says love like a tiny spectral complaint in your ear.
- The Midnight Tenant: This family prefers late-night gloom and causes the House Mood to drop an extra point if you reveal gloom cards after 9 PM.
- The Re-Decorators: They love changes in décor; you must discard a House Card to keep them calm, or the house’s mood plummets twice as fast.

Each family has a unique interplay with house mechanics, ensuring that no two plays feel the same—even if your cat insists otherwise.

### House Cards

The House Cards are the star players. They introduce effects like:

- The Hallway That Walks: Movement is restricted; players must navigate a hallway that triggers a mood penalty whenever they step into it.
- The Portrait Nuisance: A painted family member becomes active, giving you a bad omen that you can’t ignore.
- The Conservatory of Condemnation: If you draw gloom cards when the Room is "empty," you suffer an extra mood penalty.

These cards combine with the Family cards to create a phase system that rewards planning and punishes adrenaline-fueled chaos.

### Event Cards

Short, punchy events that remind you that even a haunted house has to file taxes:

- Roof Leaks Humor: Draw two gloom cards; choose one to anchor to a Family card and other to the House Mood.
- Leaky Faucets of Destiny: The Mood Tokens shift positions; if you fail to track them, you’ll feel the mood swing twice as hard.

The Event Cards ensure you don’t loop in a comfortable rhythm for too long; they push you toward creative planning and, occasionally, a curse word directed at the design team.

## How It Plays With the Base Game

If you’re familiar with Gloom 2E, the expansion feels like adding a new city to a board game map. It doesn’t replace the old engine; it expands it. You’ll still be juggling gloom cards, aiming for the most tragic family, and trying to collect the most negative points before the clock runs out. The difference is that some of your gloom now has a home—literally. The house’s mood influences how gloom cards resolve, which means you’ll be thinking in two layers: what your hand does for you, and what the house is forcing you to do to keep its mood stable.

### Interacting with Other Expansions

If you’re playing this with other Gloom 2E expansions, the Unhappy Homes mechanic scales gracefully. The Mood Tokens stack with any existing gloom tokens, and the House Cards can be used as a common area for everyone—this makes for particularly chaotic—but also memorable—cabins in a shared gloom forest. ## Artwork, Components, and Theme

The graphic design remains consistent with Gloom’s signature style: lots of translucent colored cards, a melancholy color palette, and humor that glides just under the line of taste. The new Family cards feature slightly more ornate illustrations—think Victorian wallpaper meets mid-century modern gloom—while House Cards lean into architectural puns that would make your in-laws debate color palettes with you.

The physical components are sturdy; the Mood Tokens are satisfying to flip, and the new House Cards fit neatly into the base box. The only real snag is sometimes the box can feel almost too heavy for a casual session, so if you’re the kind of gamer who moves like a sloth carrying a teacup, you’ll appreciate the extra heft that says, “This box has opinions.”

## Strategy and Tips

- Plan with the House Mood in mind. Don’t chase a single Family’s velvet doom if the house mood needs a gentle push in a different direction.
- Stack Event Cards to create a comeback window. Timing is everything; you won’t win by outpacing gloom—you’ll win by outrunning the house’s mood swing.
- Use the new House Cards to block aggressive plays. If the corridor forces you to take a wrong turn, you’ll be glad you built a plan that uses that moment to your advantage.
- Balance short-term gloom with long-term scoring. The expansion rewards players who think two turns ahead, not the one who collects the most instantaneous tears.

If you’re looking for a straightforward “play it safe” style with minimal cognitive overhead, this expansion may feel a touch crunchy. If you love cunning planning and a few dramatic moments, you’ll find it a perfect blend of puzzle and punchline.

## Playthrough Sample: A Tiny Tale of Tiny Despair

Let’s imagine a four-player game, with a mix of seasoned gloomers and first-time crafters of attic dread. The Grumbling Grandparent and the Midnight Tenant pair off against the Re-Decorators and a fresh-faced family of young mischiefs. The Hallway That Walks becomes a highlight—when someone steps into it, the mood token slides backward, triggering a cascade of House Card effects. The Event Card for the round is Roof Leaks Humor, which pushes two gloom cards onto the table and requires the next turn to address a mood penalty and a decor change both at once.

One player deliberately holds back a gloom card, aiming to trigger a House Card at the right moment. A misstep, and the mood drops twice as fast; a careful turn, and the House settles briefly, but the Family cards start stacking gloom in a way that leaves all players giggling and groaning in equal measure. The game closes with a final tally that feels like a wedding between a saddest-lobby and a family dinner where the jokes are all about something that happened in the basement.

## Scoring and Endgame

Unhappy Homes keeps the base game’s scoring logic intact: you want to minimize your own gloom while maximizing negative points on your opponents’ hands. The twist is the House Mood track: if the house mood ends too low, everyone loses some points; if the mood stays reasonable, the players who exploited the mood mechanics will squeak out a victory that tastes suspiciously like victory but cheaper than a mortgage.

The final tally uses the standard gloom calculation plus a house-mood bonus, which makes the last couple of rounds feel like a dramatic, slightly melodramatic sprint to the finish line. This helps prevent games from dragging into an endless geriatric gloom festival where nobody dares to push the panic button.

## Final Verdict and Recommendation

If you own Gloom 2E and enjoy the two flavors most geeks adore—puns and mood management—Unhappy Homes is a strong addition. It adds a meaningful layer of strategy without burying you in bureaucracy. It’s not a huge mechanical overhaul; it’s a clever reframe of how to think about the board: the house has feelings; your families try to out-mourn the house without becoming utterly consumed by it. If you’ve found base Gloom to be lean and elegant, you’ll appreciate the expansion’s willingness to lean into character-driven storytelling, a touch of architectural whimsy, and enough chaos to keep you coming back for “one more round.”

If you’re a completionist who wants every wrinkle, this expansion is a no-brainer. If you’re new to Gloom 2E, you might want to start with the base game and its other expansions to find your preferred sweet spot before diving into this one.
