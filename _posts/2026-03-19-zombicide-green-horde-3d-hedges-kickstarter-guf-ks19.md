## Design and build quality

The 3D plastic hedges are a triumph of practical design. Texture work is crisp, enough to hint at leaves and branches without looking like a low-res plastic forest. The green hue is rich but not cartoonish; it reads as vegetation rather than a manufactured prop. The segments interlock with a little bit of force, but you do not need tools or brute force to assemble them. The joints stay aligned during play, which is a big win when you have more than two players bellowing about line of sight and ambush strategies.

### Texture and tolerances
The leaf texture captures the impression of real hedges without every leaf being sculpted as a separate mini. The result is a believable barrier that looks good on camera and on your old 1080p monitor as you narrate your tactical movements to friends who only half-listen. The edge lines are clean enough that you can paint the hedge with relative ease if you want to heighten the realism or simply differentiate hedges by color for a cluttered, lived-in look.

### Assembly tips
The connectors are designed to align with tile corners, enabling you to build curves and corners as you see fit. If you want to create a longer wall, stack spine pieces end-to-end and clip the segments together. For a quick setup, draft a layout on the tile map, place the hedges, and adjust your lines of sight by repositioning a segment. The modular nature makes it easy to adapt mid-game if your players decide to take a detour through the hedgerow instead of a straight shot to the objective.

### Magnet-friendly option
If you want to go full futuristic GM mode, you can embed small magnets in the base of each segment and use a corresponding metal mat or washers in your board tiles to keep pieces in place during dramatic table flips. This is optional, but hey, it is a delightfully nerdy upgrade for the serious terrain enthusiast.

## Gameplay impact: line of sight, movement, and balance

Terrain in Zombicide is a player character just as much as your survivors are. The hedges provide a tangible barrier that influences movement and line of sight. They are tall enough to obscure a direct line from some angles but not so high that you feel boxed in. In practice, this means players must plan routes, anticipate zombie spawns, and coordinate a strategy that makes sense both narratively and mechanically.

From a balance perspective, the hedges deliver what you want from a modular terrain kit: meaningful obstacles that shape the tempo of a mission without grinding it to a halt. A hedge wall may cause survivors to split their party, avoid a direct rush to an objective, or set up a flanking maneuver while the horde of undead tries to breach the hedge rather than sprinting straight through a single breach point. The result is a more dynamic board where terrain influences strategy as much as dice rolls do.

### Line of sight and tactical use
Because the hedges are three-dimensional, they cast believable sight lines. A survivor may be forced to poke around the hedge or use a ranged weapon at an angle rather than simply blasting through. The hedges are not so dense that you cannot see a model on the other side, but dense enough to reward careful planning and teamwork. For players who love optimizing cornering and flanking, this is a little playground, a tabletop puzzle that remains solvable and enjoyable game after game.

## Painting, customization, and care

The hedges accept paint well. A base coat with a couple of washes to bring out the texture, followed by a dry brush to highlight the leaf edges, yields a hedge that looks more realistic and less like a generic green blob. If you want your hedges to appear as an older, moss-covered barrier, a darker wash and some dry-brushed moss tones will sell that story nicely. If you want to keep things bright and cartoonish for a lighter mood, skip the washes and play with brighter greens and sharp highlights. Either approach works well and does not break the scale or the visual integration with Zombicide’s miniatures.

If you enjoy customizing your terrain, you can also apply light flocking to the base of each segment to imply deeper soil or a damp ground, enhancing the overall effect. The surface is rough enough that flock adheres well, but not so rough that painting becomes a slog. It is a sweet spot for hobbyists who want results without spending months on a single piece.

### Maintenance and transport tips
To keep your hedges looking fresh from campaign night to campaign night, use a matte sealant to prevent swapping colors from finger oils or from fingerprints that accumulate when you show off your new purchase. If you travel with your kit, consider storing the hedges flat in a padded box or a flat-foam organizer. Avoid stacking heavy items on top of them; even if the plastic is sturdy, constant pressure can deform curvature lines over time.

## How to use in your campaigns: scenarios and setup tips

- A hedge corridor can function as a pinched route that survivors must navigate to reach an objective, forcing coordination and timed actions. The horde spawns can appear from behind hedges, creating suspense as you wait for the right moment to unleash the next wave.
- A hedge maze scenario makes for a memorable, narrative-driven mission. You can hide an objective in the center of the maze, requiring survivors to backtrack and think creatively about how to reach it while the undead stagger through the greenery.
- Combine hedges with other terrain modules to create layered coverage. A hedge wall paired with shattered ruins or a wooden barricade creates dynamic sight lines and multiple shelter options, letting you stage multi-pronged ambushes or rescue missions with more dramatic room for missteps.
