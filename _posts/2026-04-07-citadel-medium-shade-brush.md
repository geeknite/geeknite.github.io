### What is a shade brush and why this specific one

Shade brushes are not your standard detail brushes. They are the midwives of shading, tasked with delivering controlled, smooth gradients in the narrow confines of armor panels, cloaks, and crevices where light is too fancy to travel. The Citadel Medium Shade Brush is sized to straddle the line between fine control and broad coverage. It is not a sculptor’s chisel nor a dry brush’s blunt instrument; it sits in a comfortable middle ground that makes you feel like an art director rather than a caffeinated goblin wielding a blunt instrument.

If you have used the standard base coat brush and then tried to glaze with a larger brush, you know the struggle: paint pooling in corners, bristles fanning out like a bad hair day, and the brush deciding your model’s fate by itself. The Medium Shade claims to offer a balance between bristle stiffness and tip softness that allows for credible shading without destroying your highlights in the process. In practice, it behaves much like a steadyhanded friend who occasionally stages a dramatic towel-twirling flourish before revealing your model’s new shadows.

### Bristles and performance

The bristle geometry is the core of a shade brush. The Citadel Medium Shade uses a mid-length, moderately stiff synthetic bristle configuration designed to hold a small bead of paint and lay it down smoothly. The tip remains relatively precise, enabling you to reach into ribbed armor panels without carving a trench across a flag decal. It handles shading with glaze-type consistency, meaning you can achieve subtle transitions without the dreaded chalky line that often accompanies over-diluted acrylics.

One caveat: shade brushes thrive on controlled pressure. If you bear down like you are trying to push a stubborn magnet into a magnetized sweater, the bristles will flatten and bury themselves in the paint. The key to success with this brush is feather-light contact and a willingness to back off the paint, wiping your brush clean, and trying again when you notice a harsh edge. For many hobbyists, this is the moment where you discover that shading is not a magic trick but a slow, polite negotiation with your model. The Medium Shade excels when you let it do the talking rather than you commanding the brush to forge shadows with the force of a negative attitude.

### Paint compatibility

The brush is designed for use with water-based acrylics, including the long-standing Citadel paint lines and many third-party shades that love a good spa day under a small lamp. When you work with shade tones or glaze-type pigments, you should notice better flow and a calmer drop-off at the edge of your shading. If you are into the popular contrasts or washes, the brush performs well when you apply lighter pressure to avoid pulling the paint across raised edges. The brush does well with thinning paint to around a glaze consistency, which means you should be prepared to do multiple passes if you want to push for deeper shadows on a busy miniature.

A common mistake is to expect a single pass to deliver the entire drama. The long form shading often requires two or three thin layers, letting each pass dry between applications so the shade pools in the crevices and highlights the underlying forms the way a stage light might on a carefully crafted set piece. The Medium Shade is forgiving in this scenario but not magical. It rewards steady hands and patient timing rather than speed runs through your paint rack.

### When to avoid or supplement

If you are chasing dramatic, high-contrast shading with heavy weathering, you might be better served by a smaller brush for micro-detail shading or a larger brush for broad glaze layers. The Medium Shade is not meant to be a one-size-fits-all tool. It shines in controlled shading tasks where you need precision but also want the efficiency of a brush that can hold a reasonable amount of paint without frequent reloads. If you are new to shading, you may want to practice on a few motto minis before launching into your prized squad. The goal is to understand how light falls on the model and how the shade helps tell that story without becoming the foreground character itself.

## Maintenance and care

Taking care of your brushes is a ritual, not a chore. Here are practical tips that keep the Medium Shade in top shape for a long campaign:

- Clean promptly after use with lukewarm water and a mild soap. This helps prevent pigment from drying in the bristles and causing stiffness
- Gently reshape the bristles after washing and lay flat to dry. Do not stand the brush upright while wet; water can creep into the ferrule and loosen the glue over time
- Avoid soaking the ferrule for extended periods. This helps prevent rust and bristle separation
- Dry thoroughly before storing. A little airflow goes a long way toward extending brush life
- Rotate brushes in your arsenal. Do not punish the same brush with every paint color, as this leads to uneven wear and bristle misalignment

## Techniques and tips

### Basic shading technique with the Medium Shade

- Load a small amount of glaze or shade onto the brush. You want a controlled bead, not a pool.
- Apply with light pressure along the recesses, following the natural contours of the surface. Let the paint settle into any crevices without forcing it into every nook.
- Wipe the brush after each pass to reduce excess paint on the bristles. You want a clean edge to prevent feathering.
- If you see harsh edges, apply a second thin layer after the first has dried. This helps you refine the transition without losing detail.

### Advanced shading approach

For players who enjoy gradient shading across larger surfaces, you can adopt a flood glaze technique. Start with a midtone across the panel, then apply the shade to the lower region of the surface with a gradual fade toward the top. The brush allows you to keep a gentle gradient without abrupt stops, which is essential for a realistic look on armor plates and clothing. The trick is patience and a light touch; the brush does not magically create depth by itself. Your color theory and brush control combined with this tool will yield moderate successes and occasional triumphant miracles.

### Clean-up and longevity tricks

- Clean the brush between color families to prevent muddy transitions. A quick rinse with water and a dab of mild soap should do the trick.
- For stubborn pigment in the bristles, soak briefly but do not soak long. Then rinse again and reshape.
- If you are storing for a stretch, keep the brush dry and upright with the bristles protected. A simple brush holder or a dedicated slot in your paint rack works well.
