## Design and Build
### Materials and Print Quality
The case relies on typical 3D-printed polymer hardware: PLA is most common for hobby printers, with PETG or ABS options if you want a touch more heat resistance or flex. The external shell is a simple, rectangular shell with rounded corners and a lid that uses a small interlocking ridge to create the snap-lock. In practice, print tolerances matter a lot here: if your printer’s calibration is a tad off, you’ll either get a lid that requires a small victory dance to close, or one that refuses to shut at all.

A good rule of thumb: print with a 0.2-0.3 mm nozzle, infill around 20-40% for the shell, and consider slightly larger clearances for the interior dividers if your filament shrinks a lot. The dividers themselves are the unsung heroes—these little PCB-like spoons keep Skip-Bo stock from wandering, and they allow you to customize the fit without a full-on internal overhaul.

### Snap-Lock Mechanism
The snap-lock is the star of this show. It combines tongue-and-groove geometry with a ridge that catches as you press the lid shut. It’s not a robo-detective mechanism, but it is functionally robust for travel: a gentle push, a click, and your deck is contained. The downside: if your print tolerances are a hair off, the lid can be either too snug (requiring two hands and a little elbow grease) or too loose (you’ll wake up to a lighter, happier deck on the hotel carpet).

### Durability on the Road
We subjected the case to a few weekend-style abuses: bouncing around in a duffel bag, a couple of accidental drops onto plush hotel carpet, and a humid hotel corridor moment where the door swatted the box in mild wind. The exterior held up, and the internal dividers stayed in place. In hot climates, PLA can soften a little; PETG would be a safer choice if you expect direct sun and high temps during long layovers.

## 3D Printing Perspective
### Material Choices and Print Settings
If you’re printing this at home, PLA offers ease and speed, but PETG or ABS give better long-term durability and heat resistance. A moderate wall thickness (1.2–1.6 mm) and a 20–40% infill produce a sturdy shell that feels substantial but not tank-like. The interior dividers can be printed separately and slotted into place for a modular, reconfigurable system.

### Print Time and Cost
Time is your biggest cost here, not money. A full shell plus interior dividers can take several hours, depending on printer speed and layer height. Filament cost is modest, and the experience of printing your own deck keeper is priceless in the same way that a DIY haircut feels like a personal victory even when it ends up uneven.

### Scale and Fit Considerations
If you’re a stickler for exact tolerances (and who isn’t), be prepared to calibrate your printer a touch. The goal is a snug but not painful fit; the lid should close with a definitive snap but not require brute force. If you’re sharing prints or buying from a maker, ask about tolerance specs and whether their dividers are sized for the precise card stock you use. A small misalignment with the dividers can turn a tidy tray into a jumbled mess after a single trip through a luggage carousel.
