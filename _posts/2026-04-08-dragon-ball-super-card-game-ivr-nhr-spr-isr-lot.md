## Foil quality and care: what to expect and how to keep it pristine

Foil quality is a tricky thing in modern card games. The finish can range from glassy to rainbow to heavy mirror without ever meeting your expectations of a perfectly flat surface. With this lot you should get a fairly consistent foil vibe across the 12 cards, with no visible white borders around the edges and minimal swirl marks that would give a corny effect during play. Real talk: foils look best when they stay clean and protected. Here is a short care guide that will help your cards stay visually intact for longer than your memory of a tournament win:

- Sleeves first, then binder pages. It sounds obvious but it is shocking how many disputes are resolved by the correct order of operations.
- Use thicker protective sleeves if you plan to transport these foils to events. The extra stiffness helps with shuffling and reduces the risk of edge wear when cards slide around in your bag like mischievous piccolo ships in a bottle.
- Avoid direct sunlight. Foils are reflectors by design and can burn like a tiny dragon if left on a windowsill long enough. Keep them in a cool, dry place and away from the glinting glare of sunlit shelves.
- Handle with clean hands. Oils on your fingers can affect the foil sheen over time. That is science and a terrible excuse to wash your hands before you count your chits.

The moral here is not to overthink it, but to respect the foil. Foil is a showpiece in your collection and a potential window into the aesthetic of your personal power level. If you care about your cards, you will care for the environment in which they live, which is basically a mini temple for your favorite dragon ball character lineup.

## How to sort and store: the binder to binder ratio

Storage is the unsung hero of any card collecting hobby. The right binder, the right pages, and the right organization can save you a morning of chaos when you realize you own 200 foil cards and the binder is of insufficient girth to handle them all. For this 12 card lot, here is a simple, practical storage approach that keeps things sane:

- Group by rarity. Start with IVR, then NHR, SPR, and ISR. It makes it easier to locate a target card when you want to show off or swap on a forum that still pretends to be relevant.
- Within each rarity, arrange by set or art, if you care about that level of nerdy detail. Some people love the art order; others just want to find their most powerful engine quickly. Either approach works as long as it is consistent.
- Use visible page sleeves so you can appreciate the foil glare without having to pull everything out. A good page will allow you to flip through like a high roller at a casino of chrome and myth.
- Label your pages. A tiny index card behind each sleeve or a simple printer-friendly list on the back of a page helps you remember which card is which when you resist the urge to reorganize your binder every other week.

A well organized binder is not just a storage solution; it is a display of your dedication to a certain level of nerdy perfection.
