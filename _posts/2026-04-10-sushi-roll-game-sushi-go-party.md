# Sushi Go Party! The Pick&Pass Card Game, Family Fun Party

Welcome, fellow geeks, to another food-tinged tabletop review where we pretend to be adults but secretly crave more plastic sushi than your local grocery store has ever stocked. Today we’re diving into Sushi Go Party!, the hit party-friendly cousin of the beloved Sushi Go! It’s a game about assembling the perfect plate of sushi by pass-and-pick drafting, with a menu that rotates like a very efficient sushi chef in a very small kitchen. If you’ve ever yelled “Corona roll!” in a crowded family game night and meant it, this review is for you.

## What is Sushi Go Party! and why should you care?

Sushi Go Party! is a card-drafting game from Gamewright that expands the original Sushi Go! into a bigger, more chaotic, more customizable party experience. The core mechanic remains simple and elegant: players start with a hand of cards, pick one, and pass the rest to the next player, repeating until all cards are taken. The twist? A rotating menu of mini-sets (the “party” aspect) each round, a larger table presence, and a mix of scoring cards that keeps players on their toes and often on the edge of a competitive food coma.

If you’ve played Sushi Go!, you’ll feel right at home. If you haven’t — welcome to a world where assembling nigiri may be more strategic than reading the dishwasher’s manual. The game scales nicely from 2 to 8 players, which means your Zoom game nights, family gatherings, and in-store demo events can all be transformed into a kawaii chaos of miso and miscalculations. It also includes a ton of variety thanks to multiple menu cards, so you’re not stuck with the same point salad night after night. It’s the gaming equivalent of rotating the menu at your favorite all-you-can-eat sushi joint and somehow walking away with a different order every time.

## How to play: the quick and the punchy

The gameplay remains faithful to the core Pick & Pass mechanic, but with enough mayo to keep it silky and fun. Here’s a condensed guide, with enough detail to get you started without needing a full ruleset on your lap during dessert.

### Setup

- Shuffle the menu cards (the round-specific scoring boards) into a stack and reveal one per round. The number of rounds and the menu cards chosen depend on the player count; more players means more variety and more chaos.
- Deal a hand of cards to each player. The exact number of cards per hand is determined by the round, but you’ll generally be passing a handful of sushi beauties around the table.
- Place the scoring track where everyone can see it. Pro-tip: keep a drink within arm’s reach, because you’re going to need it when someone steals your moment with the “Giant Salmon Nigiri for 12 points” and you realize your plate is now a sad, empty plate.

### The turn structure

1. On your turn, pick one card from your hand and place it face-down in front of you.
2. Pass the remaining cards to the left (the direction changes each round, adding more drama to your social life and less to your sanity).
3. Repeat until all cards are taken.
4. Resolve scoring for that round according to the active menu. There are multiple scoring patterns per menu, often rewarding combinations, sets, and sometimes a little sushi shenanigans that intentionally pits players against their friends.
5. Reset the hand to start a new round with a new menu board.

The beauty here is “easy to teach, hard to master.” The rules fit on a single kitchen napkin, but the decision tree expands as you see more cards. You’ll quickly learn to prioritise certain card types on certain menus, while ruthlessly denying your opponents the chance to collect a critical combo. It becomes a little cutthroat, but in the friendliest, most giggly way possible.

### Scoring gotcha and strategy spark

Each menu is a small puzzle. You might be chasing a set of maki rolls that reward volume, or a collection of nigiri cards that scales with color variety, or a round where you’re chasing a single “treasure plate” that weighs heavy in points but is deceptively hard to assemble. The strategy isn’t about “the best card” but about “the best card given the direction of the table and the current goal.” That dynamic, where you rarely know who’s building what and with which cards, is the source of much of the game’s social joy. You’ll watch players clutch their cards as the round closes, doing mental arithmetic that would boggle a calculator, a spreadsheet, and maybe a small octopus all at once.

### Variability keeps things fresh

Because Sushi Go Party! uses a rotating menu system with different round objectives, no two sessions feel identical. You’ll face a range of goals, from simple “collect three of a kind” to more intricate combos that require specific color matching or card-pair synergy. And because you can adjust the number of players and the menu cards included in a session, the replayability is quite strong. The moment you think you’ve mastered a pattern, the next menu throws you a delightful curveball and you’re back to recalculating odds mid-sip. It’s a party game that actually rewards attentive play without turning into a math competition—though there’s always someone who will attempt to do the entire score silently with their finger in the air like a bored wizard counting stars.

## The social flavor: why it plays well at family nights

Family-friendly and easy to learn, Sushi Go Party! is a stellar choice for mix-and-match game nights. It accommodates a wide range of ages. Younger players get a tactile introduction to planning and pattern recognition, while older players can flex their strategic muscles by optimizing routes and denying opponents the metadata on the board (i.e., what you are collecting and why you are collecting it).

The pick-and-pass element encourages interaction without heavy direct confrontation. You’re snatching cards, but you’re not drafting in a bloodthirsty manner. There’s enough humor in the mismatches of what people hope to collect and what they end up with, that the table stays light-hearted even as scores start to pile up in surprising ways. And let’s be honest: watching Uncle Joe realize he’s in second place but just discovered a three-point-maki-card chain is the kind of family drama that keeps the holidays interesting.

For some players, the pace is a near-perfect speed: quick rounds, quick laughs, and enough brain-work to justify a second helping of chips. For others, the turn-by-turn passing is a test of social patience, a gentle reminder that this is, in essence, a party-with-boards, not a pure strategy game. Either way, Sushi Go Party! earns its seats at the table with a cheerful mood and a buffet of cute sushi illustrations that make your brain say “yum.”

## Learning curve, accessibility, and teachability

The learning curve is gentle. A new player can typically grasp the core mechanics in a single go, with a short rundown of how scoring works on the menu board. The rest is just practice—watching how the table flows, noticing what others are passing, and adjusting your own picks accordingly. The most common stumbling block is understanding how a single choice on this turn will influence the end-of-round scoring on several different cards. But the rotating menus reduce the probability that any one strategy dominates, which keeps the game fair and fun for a mixed-age group.

Accessibility-wise, the icons are simple and the text is readable. The color palette is friendly and the cards are durable enough to handle the inevitable spills of a family kitchen (or the occasional napkin tossed across the room in a dramatic display of “I was this close to winning”). If you’re teaching kids, you can leverage the game to discuss probability, pattern recognition, and casual math without venturing into a formal arithmetic class—this is the best kind of disguised learning.
