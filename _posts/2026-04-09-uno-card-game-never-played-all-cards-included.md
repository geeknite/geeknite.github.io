What is UNO? A quick refresher for the uninitiated. UNO is a shedding-style card game originally released by Mattel. The deck contains 108 cards across four colors: red, green, blue, and yellow. Each color has numbers 0-9 (with one 0 per color) plus action cards: Skip, Reverse, and Draw Two. In addition there are four Wild cards and four Wild Draw Four cards. The objective is simple on the surface: be the first player to discard all your cards. On your turn you must match the top card by color or number, or you can play a Wild card to change the color. If you cannot play, you draw a card. Sounds straightforward, until the color-splitting, sneaky tactics, and the occasional betrayal come into play. UNO is part speed dating, part strategic misdirection, and entirely dependent on whether your best friend remembers to shout UNO in time.

The card cast and the rules in a little more detail. The four colors host numbers 0-9 and three action cards per color: Skip, Reverse, Draw Two. The 0 is singular per color; the digits 1-9 appear twice per color; this gives you a fairly generous pool of play options. The deck also includes four Wild cards and four Wild Draw Four cards. The Wilds let you change the color to one that best suits your hand, or frustrate the person who will need to draw five cards because you created a color parity nightmare. The Wild Draw Four is the dramatic closer: you can basically scud the color to something you know your opponent is weak against, and you force them to draw four cards in addition to the color change.

The All Cards Included approach invites house rules. Here are a few that come up in our testing:

- Draw Two stacking. If a Draw Two is played, the next player can respond with another Draw Two, accumulating the penalty. The round bogs down, but the tension spikes. In official UNO, stacking Draw Twos is often allowed in casual play; in many All Cards Included modes, players explicitly allow or disallow stacking depending on how chaotic they want the table to be.
- Draw Four counter-play. The official rules don't permit stacking a Wild Draw Four with a regular Draw Two, and you can only play a Wild Draw Four when you have no matching color in hand. In the All Cards Included world, some players allow countering with a Wild Draw Four or permitting a counter of any card to force the draw to extend. This is where things get spicy: it becomes a game of who can hold their nerve when the table erupts into a chorus of “DRAW FOUR!”
- Wild color-change rituals. Changing the color is a moment to shout, perform a quick jazz-hand flourish, or craft a micro-scene about your chosen hue. The color-change moment is a theatrical beat, a mini ritual that can reset the emotional climate of the table.

Strategy and tips for All Cards Included UNO. If you want to walk away with a smile and a sense that you still have control of the table, here are practical suggestions:

- Keep color distribution in mind. You want to avoid running out of a color at the worst moment. If you can, maintain a mix of colors in your hand so you can respond to color changes without panic.
- Save the Wilds for clutch moments. A Wild is not just a “color change” card; it's a strategic tool that can save or break you when you’re staring down a table full of potential chaos.
- Use Wild Draw Fours selectively. In casual All Cards play, Wild Draw Fours are definitive power plays. Use them to swing momentum, but be prepared for retaliation or a stalling strategy that forces you into a longer game.
- Read the table's color preferences. If two players are hoarding a specific color, you can attempt to force a color shift that leaves those players vulnerable later in the round.
- Master the art of the Reverse. In a four-player game, reversing direction twice can buy you a moment to wash the chaos away or set up a cunning trap for the next player.
- Don’t overlook the Zero-Card moment. In some variants, a Zero can reset the game or cause players to rotate hands or pass a certain number of cards; this can be a huge swing in the best/worst possible moment.
- UNO count matters. Always be mindful of how many cards your opponents hold. If a player is down to one card with a Wild Draw Four in their hand, you know the tension is about to spike.
