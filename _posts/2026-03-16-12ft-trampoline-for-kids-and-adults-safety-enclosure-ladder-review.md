## Assembly and setup: the Loony Lab of Legos
Whether you’re a DIY pro or a person who thinks a screwdriver is a fancy flavor of yogurt, assembly will require patience and a sense of humor. Steps:
1) Choose a flat, clear space. No, your dog doesn’t count as a measuring tool.
2) Lay out the frame pieces in a square, matching bolt holes; double-check alignment. The “measure twice, cut once” rule applies to bolts too.
3) Attach the frame to the legs, then secure the cross braces. The structure should feel sturdy with minimal wobbles.
4) Attach a spring to the jump mat, then gradually secure all springs to the frame. A helper can pass you springs, show you new dance moves, and tell you when you’ve pinned something wrong.
5) Attach the safety enclosure net. Zip up fully; test with a gentle bounce to ensure the zipper stays closed.
6) Install the ladder on the side. Test climbs with caution and supervision.
7) Anchor or stake the unit to the ground as needed. If you live in a windy region, do not skip this step.
8) Final safety check: ensure the pad covers all springs; check for loose hardware; verify no gaps or pinch points.

## Maintenance and care
- Regular checks: inspect springs, frame, net for wear
- Cleaning: gentle soap, water, and a soft brush; avoid harsh chemicals
- Lubrication: rarely required; if you hear squeaking, retighten bolts
- Storage: cover during winter or when not in use for extended periods
- Safety practice: always supervise children; remove jewelry and shoes before jumping; no flips unless trained and supervised

## Engineering notes: what makes the 12ft design tick
- Frame and spring count: a typical 12ft setup uses a dense grid of springs (often in the 60-80 range) to balance bounce and stability. More springs generally translate to a snappier feel; fewer springs yield a softer, more forgiving bounce. Both setups have pros depending on who’s using it and how often.
- Jump mat materials: the weave must tolerate repetitive flex without thinning. Expect UV-treated fabrics to resist sun damage for longer seasons, but even with protection, extended sun exposure will shorten life if you don’t cover it when not in use.
- Enclosure geometry: the enclosure isn’t just an add-on; it shapes the bounce arc. A well-designed net reduces the chances of contact with the frame edge, which is where a lot of injuries happen during wilder sessions.
- Ladder design: ladders can be a safety weak point if not anchored well. A sturdy ladder with slip-resistant rungs and side rails gives kids and adults a secure entry path without turning the climb into a circus act.
- Anchors and wind resistance: summertime gusts can turn a trampoline into a kite with a bounce. The included stakes and optional anchors are not glamorous, but they’re the difference between a calm evening and a runaway spring fiesta.

## Safety best practices and common myths
- Myth: A safety enclosure eliminates all risk. Reality: risk is reduced but not erased; supervision and safe practices remain essential.
- Myth: More padding means zero injuries. Reality: padding helps, but it is not a free pass to try risky moves.
- Best practice: teach jumping basics first, then progress to more advanced moves with supervision and protective gear if appropriate.
- Best practice: keep the area clear of furniture, pets, and loose toys that can become launch ramps for unplanned acrobatics.
