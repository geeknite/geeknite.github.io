## Painting Approach: A Friendly, Funny, Yet Practical Guide

Painting four exclusive survivors is not a sprint; it’s a jog in heavy armor, with a backpack full of tiny scenic rocks and a playlist that alternates between power metal and eight-bit dungeon crawl sounds. Here’s a practical, step-by-step approach that keeps things sane while still delivering the wow factor on the tabletop.


### Step 1:prep and Prime

First thing’s first: priming. White primer gives you the cleanest highlight transitions on light armor and bright cloth pieces. Black primer, however, can make the deeper recesses pop, especially if your color palette includes dark tones and strong contrast. For the Ultimate Survivors, I recommend starting light with white primer to establish crisp edge lines, then using a black wash later to bring out the detailed engravings.

- Materials: a good quality spray primer or spray-on primer, fine sandpaper for prep (optional but handy), x-acto blade for removing mold lines.
- Technique: a light, even coat from about 8-12 inches away. Avoid heavy builds; you want a thin veil rather than a tar pit.
- Common mistake: skipping the primer or spraying from too close. The result is paint that won’t stick, and a desperate gamer who must repaint the entire thing at 2 a.m.

If you’re new to the hobby, consider a primer color that matches your primary color path. For pale metalwork or light cloth, white is king. For dark cloaks and leather-heavy palettes, gray or black primer can simplify your shading work later on.


### Step 2: base coats and color theory

Now comes the fun part: color. The secret sauce here is planning a color path that reads well on the table under LED lights while still allowing for screen-to-tabletop legibility during long gaming nights. Here are some practical color schemes that look great on the Ultimate Survivors:

- Armor and metal: base with a cool steel gray, push with a mid-tone to a light metallic highlight, and finish with a subtle blue-gray wash for depth. If you want your steel to pop, a cold highlight (think icy blue) on the edges can create a striking, almost magical look.
- Leather and belts: rich browns with a touch of red-brown mid-tones. A bit of glaze helps the leather look worn, and a tiny bit of black wash into the crevices gives it that “sinned-in-the-bath-tub-of-ink” texture.
- Cloth and tabards: go for saturated colors that pop under a dim dungeon-lamp. Deep reds, forest greens, or royal blues look dynamic against metal and leather. You’ll want to pick a primary color for at least three pieces of clothing to create cohesion across the set.
- Wooden elements and weapons: natural wood tones with subtle grain lines. A dry-brush technique can reveal wood texture nicely. If your survivors carry big steel weapons, a few rust spots or aged patina can add character without looking cartoonish.

My recommended approach: pick a main color family (e.g., blues and silvers for a knightly vibe) and then pick two complementary accent colors. This keeps your four characters visually tied together but distinct enough to tell them apart on the board.


### Step 3: details, washes, and edge highlighting

Details are where the magic happens. You want to avoid overloading with too many washes in a single session, but a careful wash in the right places can deepen shadows and pull textures forward.

- Wash strategy: pick one or two washes (one for the metal, one for the leather). Use very thin washes to avoid pooling in the deepest recesses. Clean up with a fine-detail brush if you see blotches you don’t like.
- Edge highlighting: this is where your minis go from mid-table to centerpiece. A fine brush with a light highlight along the most protruding edges makes armor and weapons look painted rather than plated in a single color. Keep it subtle—this is not a highlighter contest; this is storytelling with color.
- NMM vs metallics: if you’re tempted to try non-metallic metal (NMM), you’re entering a deep, rewarding rabbit hole. For most players, a well-executed metallic base with a touch of blue or green wash can achieve a similar level of “steel” without extra complexity.

Practice tip: paint the eyes last. Zombie or not, a tiny white dot with a black pupil brings personality and life—or at least a convincing undead glare—to your miniature.


### Step 4: basing and final touches

The base is the stage for your mini’s walk through apocalypse-land. A basing scheme can unify the set or differentiate the characters. You can keep it simple with texture paint and dry pigments, or go full diorama with cork rocks, static grass, and little skull bits for flavor.

- Texture: use a texture paint or a base coat of sand. A light dry-brush of earthy brown then a wash in dark umber will give you a realistic ground surface.
- Debris: add a few small bits—gravel, tufts of grass, broken weapon parts—to tell a story about where these survivors have been.
- Edges: consider painting the bases with a subtle rim color to help the models stand out on crowded boards. A black rim is classic and readable, but a brown or dark gray rim can look chic on lighter bases.


> Quick pro-tip: spray your bases with a clear sealant after you’re satisfied with the paint. It helps prevent chipping during those tense late-game melee moments when dice decide to roll in a miniatures’ face.

## Maintenance: Keeping Your Painted Army Fresh

A couple of practical tips to keep them looking sharp through many nights of undead stamping:

- Sealants: use a matte sealant to reduce glare and keep colors vibrant. If you want a slightly glossy finish on metal bits, apply a selective gloss varnish only on those areas.
- Handling: wash hands before painting and avoid touching the model’s face or weapon edges to minimize oil buildup.
- Storage: store upright in segmented plastic trays to prevent chipped edges and misaligned bases.
