## How This Variant Differs From Codenames (Base Game)
Codenames Pictures shifts the vibe from wordplay to imagery. Instead of giving a one-word clue that ties multiple words on the grid, the Spy Master gives a clue that links multiple pictures. It’s basically Word Association 2.0 with more color, fewer vowels, and significantly more goats in hats.

- The grid stays 5x5, but the deck is pictures, not words. This means you’re interpreting iconography, which can be delightful, maddening, or both.
- The clue tokens haven’t changed dramatically in function, but the clues you generate are often more visual or thematic rather than linguistic. Your imaginary art-gallery assistant will be busier than your lexicon.
- The assassin card concept remains in play, but in pictures, the “trap” is less about a word that would confound you at a spelling bee and more about the sudden realization that your clue tied together three entirely unrelated penguin images but left you with deathly consequences.

## How to Play: Quickstart Guide
If you already know Codenames, you’ll feel right at home with this variant transplanted into pictures. If you’re new, fear not; this section will get you from box to board in under a dozen wine corks.

1) Set up the grid: Shuffle the picture deck and lay out 25 cards in a 5x5 grid. Each card is a visual clue stuck in the middle of your table like a museum label that forgot to be pretentious.

2) Pick your Spy Masters: One for the red team, one for the blue team. They sit on opposite sides of the table with a view of the grid and a shared brain that occasionally produces surprisingly accurate clues.

3) Clue-giving: The Spy Master gives a one-word clue followed by a number. The clue word should relate to as many picture cards as possible, with the number indicating how many cards in the grid relate to that clue. Your clue-giving muse might be a pop culture reference, an internal meme, or a smooth pun that somehow ties together a dragon, a bicycle, and a bicycle-riding dragon in the same sentence.

4) Guessing: The players on the clue side guess which cards the clue relates to. They can guess up to the number plus one cards, but they risk hitting a red card that ends their turn, a blue card that ends their turn, a neutral card that just gives them a tiny penalty, or the assassin that ends the whole round. The tension is delicious, especially when the clue is a stretch and the cards misalign with your expectations.

5) End of round: The round ends when either team identifies all their agents or someone hits the assassin. A new round begins with the other team’s Spy Master.

Pro tip: You’ll often find the most memorable moments come from the weirdest connections. A clue like “Pirates 3” might link a pirate image, a treasure chest, and a parrot that is somehow the same color as your team’s marker. It’s goofy, it’s chaotic, and it’s absolutely clutch when you pull it off.

## Strategy and House Rules
- Pick a theme for your clues. If you’re playing with a group of friends who love a recurring joke, weave that into your clues. The board will become a stage for your comedic misdirection.
- Use a timer for your clues if your crew loves breakneck pace. A 60-second time cap can keep the energy high and the jokes flowing.
- Embrace the chaos. The most amusing moments come from that one clue that somehow fits three images, including your teammate’s favorite meme and a random cartoon animal.

## The Experience: Social and Thematic Hook
Codenames Pictures capitalizes on the social dynamics that make board games shine: banter, collaboration, and the occasional moment of confusion when someone misreads a clue. The pictures give you something to talk about, expanding beyond the typical “word association” into something more visual and immediate. It’s a wonderful crossover for groups who want to play a party game that doesn’t rely exclusively on your vocabulary or your ability to spell.

## Comparisons and Recommendations
Here we weigh this against the original Codenames, as well as a couple of other CGE outputs like Codenames Dice or Codenames Duet.

- When to choose Codenames Pictures: If your group enjoys image-based humor, pop culture callbacks, and the joy of yelling out “That image is a cat wearing 3 hats” across the table, this is your jam.
- When to choose the base game: If you prefer wordplay, clever synonyms, and a strong vocabulary workout, you’ll likely reach for normal Codenames first. The pictures variant is a sister product, not a replacement.
- Player count and vibe: This game thrives in a social setting with at least 4 players, but it can skew chaotic in larger groups. If your group loves group jokes, you’ll find it shines with 6–8 players.

## Final Verdict
Codenames Pictures is a delightful twist on the classic word game, offering a playful, approachable gateway into more visual clueing.
