### Assembly time and tips

- Expect 1–2 hours for a first-timer; subsequent setups are usually faster.
- Clear a large space; you’ll need to lay out the frame pieces and stretch the mat without tripping over the dog.
- Have a second person for the front-end alignment; it’s a two-handed job, not solo.

Pro-tip: layout all parts before you start so you don’t end up with 17 screws of questionable size and an empty bag that looks suspiciously like a snack.

### First bounce and safety check

After assembly, do a quick test with an adult stepping lightly on the mat (in socks, not shoes). Check that the enclosure net is taut and the padding covers the springs completely. If you can wring a squeak out of the springs or feel any wobble, pause and re-tighten. The goal is to avoid the “ding-dong” moment where the frame shifts and your kid discovers gravity is a real thing.

## Space, Placement, and Indoor Use

Indoor trampolines require careful placement. You’ll want to avoid placement near walls or furniture that could become part of a hydraulic experiment when your kid decides to practice a mid-air spin.

### Room requirements

- Clear space: at least 6–8 feet around, 9–10 feet of headroom
- A vinyl or tile floor, or a rug with thick padding to reduce vibrations
- The ability to power wash or wipe down the mat for cleaning

### Setup tips for apartments and shared spaces

- Place near a wall to help tether any stray bounce, but keep away from corners
- Use a protective mat on the floor to minimize slipping
- Consider a foldable option if you’re short on space and a neighbor’s dog keeps barking

## Maintenance and Longevity

A trampoline is a long-term investment; you’ll likely want to keep it in good shape for years. A little maintenance goes a long way.

### Cleaning and care

- Wipe the mat with mild soap and water to remove kid-approved slime
- Inspect springs and frame for wear or rust, especially after rough play or heavy weather
- Check the net stitching for tears and fix promptly

### Storage and seasonal care

If you’re in a climate with harsh winters, consider a protective cover or moving the trampoline to a sheltered area when not in use. If you leave it in place, check for rust or corrosion and maintain the frame.
