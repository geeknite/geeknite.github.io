## Setup and first turn
### Setup
Shuffle the deck, deal a handful of cards to each player, and place the rest in a draw pile within reach. The number of cards dealt depends on player count, but you can typically expect a brisk pace. The rules lean into the idea that you can figure it out before your coffee gets cold.

### First turn
On each turn, you select one card from your hand and pass the rest to the left. Pick carefully or go full chaotic neutral depending on your mood. Reveal your card simultaneously (or pretend you are all so in sync your cards astrally align). Then score at the end of the round for the sushi types you collected in that round. The rounds chain together into a satisfying little sandwich of points.

## Core gameplay loop
### Draft and pass
The core mechanic stays faithful to Sushi Go: choose a card from your hand, pass the rest, and hope you drafted well. The fast play version nudges you toward quicker decisions with a lighter mental load. You are not building a diorama of strategy, just a tasty little combo basket that will either taste glorious or taste like burnt tempura. The speed keeps you from overthinking which card to take and instead you ride the wave of cute art and mild tension.

### Reveal and score
After each draft, you reveal your chosen card and set it in your personal plate. At the end of the round, you tally points for each sushi type. The scoring varies by type and sometimes by who ate the most of it. Wasabi cards, if included, spice up the end game; if not, you will still smile because the art remains delicious.

### End of round and onward
Once all cards are drafted and scored, reshuffle and deal again for the next round. In fast play mode you may have 2 to 3 rounds depending on the number of players and your tolerance for hunger metaphors. The game concludes when the deck runs out or when your friends shout for a rematch, whichever comes first.

## Scoring and sushi types
### Nigiri
Nigiri cards come in varieties like egg, salmon, and squid. They are simple scores: a single card can be worth a fixed point value, but the exact numbers depend on the edition. The clean thing is you generally want a few nigiri to anchor your score without letting the other players steal your thunder.

### Sashimi
Sashimi is a risk-reward type: collect three cards and score big. It can be satisfying to chase a mini-set, but the likelihood of missing one card pack adds a dash of spice and a reason to groan in public.

### Tempura
Tempura rewards two cards for a chunkier score. It rewards timing more than raw collection, which is exactly the kind of drama you want in a tiny game about fish and rice.

### Maki rolls
Maki scoring is a little like a mini competition within a game: whoever has the most Maki at the end claims points. The fast play variant still teases this mechanic, but with shorter rounds the advantage can flip in a blink.

### Dumplings
Dumplings scale in points as you gather more of them. They are the communal snack of champions, stacking quietly until a final reveal breaks into a chorus of applause or groans.

### Wasabi
Wasabi can amplify the value of a following Nigiri card, but if you miss a wasabi you still get something, which in a world of hungry game nights, is a win.

### End game shenanigans
In fast play you still want to balance your palate: avoid too many of one thing, chase a few small sets, and keep your options open. It is a little like dating in high school: you want variety, not a single perfect combo that makes everyone jealous of your taste.

## The fast play twist: how it speeds up your brain
The top line benefit is clarity without chaos. By trimming complex scoring lines, the game becomes a social, quick-fire negotiation of who takes what and who denies whom a particular card. The tension comes not from whether you will survive to the end of the game but from whether you can plan ahead enough to set yourself up for a couple of tasty combos before the deck collapses under the weight of your cravings. In practice, you will find that games last around 12 to 20 minutes, depending on player count and how loud the dinner table yells get.

## Strategy tips for beginners and veterans
### For beginners
- Prioritize a few safe bets: pick cards with immediate, small point values rather than chasing a fragile long-term plan. This reduces the cognitive overhead and keeps things fun.
- Keep an eye on what others are collecting. If you notice a potential block, seize the card you need to slow them down. It is like polite spite, served on a plate of sushi.
- Don t overthink the synergy: the fast play format rewards quick decisions and a willingness to adjust on the fly rather than constructing a master plan that requires 18 moves to execute.

### For veterans
- Build flexible combos. Look for cards that can slot into different scoring stacks rather than locking into one specific ceiling.
- Deny a rival when you can. If you can take a card they need while also improving your own score, it is a doubly satisfying move.
- The tempo matters. In a fast session, being the table who moves with the group makes the whole night feel like a high five in pastel colors.

### Tic tac toe your way to points
Think of each round as a micro-competition: you don t need to win every little battle, but you want to win the last one. Schedule your hero cards for the endgame when you can crush two or three scoring opportunities at once without tipping your hand too early.
