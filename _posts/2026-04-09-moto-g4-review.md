If you were alive in 2016 and had a fondness for totally reasonable hardware that pretends to be flagship-grade, you probably owned or lusted after a Moto G4. Motorola’s budget beast was the company’s answer to people who asked, “Can I have a legible screen, a day’s worth of battery, and a camera that won’t embarrass me in public for under $200?” The answer, most of the time, was yes. The Moto G4 wasn’t perfect, but it offered a vibe you simply don’t find in budget phones these days: a calm, no-drama user experience that didn’t pretend to be something it wasn’t. Fast-forward to 2026, and the G4 is less of a daily driver and more of a vintage smartwatch-battery-connector for your vintage nostalgia portfolio. Yes, it’s still usable for light browsing and social, but it’s no longer the device you’d recommend to your grandma as “the best thing since sliced toast.”

In this review, we’ll wander through the quirks and charms of the Moto G4, separate the romance from the reality, and help you decide if this throwback deserves a place in your gadget shrine. And yes, we’ll make jokes about the brick-sized battery and the preposterously optimistic 1080p panel. Let’s dive in.

## Design and Build: Plastic, but Pleasant

The Moto G4 sits squarely in the elegant-but-honest camp of smartphone design. It’s all plastic with a soft-grip polycarbonate back that feels light but surprisingly sturdy in the hand. The side rails aren’t metal here; this is not a phone that tries to pretend it’s premium when it isn’t. But the G4 nails the “uncomplicated daily driver” aesthetic: clean lines, a gentle curved back, and a comfortable 5.5-inch display that won’t wear your palm down after a full day of texting. The device measures roughly 153 x 76 x 9 mm, which keeps it pocketable for most adults with normal coats and not-terribly-tight jeans.

Weight sits around 155 grams, which translates to a phone you can forget you’re carrying until you tilt it to read notifications at 3:00 AM. The power button and volume rocker are chunky enough to feel mechanical, and there’s a small utility button on some variants (labeled “Moto” for the quick access feature) if you managed to snag a carrier variant with gadgety extras. While it’s not splash-proof or water-shielded like modern flagships, the G4’s micro-USB port is neatly tucked away and won’t scream for attention every time you plug it in in a hurry. For the price, it oozes a “no-frills reliability” vibe rather than a “look-at-me-with-this-metal-and-glass” attitude.

If you’re the kind of person who still uses a protective case, you’ll be happy: the phone doesn’t scream “slippery metal bar of doom” the moment you take it out of your pocket. It’s not a premium device by any stretch, but it’s deliberately built to survive the rough-and-tumble life of a budget phone owner. For fans of the era’s chunky, plastic-y devices, the G4 is basically a comfort food option.

And yes, there’s a sense of “this feels like a phone you can break and not scream forever” that makes it charming in a nostalgia-forward way. If you’re hunting for a modern feel, you’ll be disappointed; if you’re chasing a dependable, no-fuss daily driver that won’t doom you to “Android version roulette,” you’ll enjoy the G4’s honest bones.

## Display and Multimedia: 1080p, Decent Brightness, and the Daylight Dilemma

The Moto G4’s display is a 5.5-inch 1080p IPS LCD. It’s not going to win any HDR crowns or render you a vivid painting of the digital cosmos, but it’s readable, sharp enough for comic panels and streaming shows, and perfectly adequate for the everyday content most of us actually consume on phones.

In practice, you’ll enjoy crisp text and well-rendered icons. The color accuracy tends toward the warm side, which makes selfies and skin tones look approachable rather than clinical. The brightness levels are solid; you won’t have trouble reading the phone outdoors on a sunny day—though you’ll still squint a little if you’ve left the screen brightness to auto and you sprint under noon sun. Glare can be annoying, but nothing a quick manual brightness bump can’t fix.

Watching videos feels acceptable on this panel—sturdy 1080p playback with decent viewing angles. It’s not exactly “cinema in your pocket,” but it doesn’t feel like you’re watching through a foggy windshield either. Where things get flaky, however, are premium multimedia expectations: color shifts under extreme angles and the lack of wide color gamut means some vibrant 4K streams won’t wow you. If you’re deep-diving into color-critical photo editing or want to calibrate display luminance for professional tasks, you’ll wish for a more capable panel. For binge-watching a few episodes on the bus or scrolling through a news feed while you wait for coffee, it’s more than adequate.

Another practical note: the G4 lacks modern screen tech like Gorilla Glass Victus or high refresh rates. It’s a sturdy, plain-Jane LCD. No curve-flower OLED magic here, just a reliable display that gets the job done without drama. And that small, 5.5-inch real estate means one-handed use is surprisingly comfortable for many folks.

## Performance and Software: Calm, Capable, and Resilient

Under the hood, the Moto G4 ships with a Qualcomm Snapdragon 617 (actually an octa-core with Cortex-A53 cores) and options for 2GB or 3GB RAM depending on the market. It’s far from the most powerful lineup of its generation—but remember, this phone launched with a mission: deliver a smooth Android experience at a price that won’t require a mortgage. In day-to-day tasks, the G4 is perfectly serviceable: messaging, social, light gaming, and casual browsing glide along with reasonable speed.

What you’ll notice in practice is that heavy gaming or many tabs in Chrome can cause stutters and longer load times. It’s not a tragedy; it’s a reminder that you purchased a budget device in a world of high-refresh-rate behemoths. If you’re a power user who wants snappy performance for mobile multitasking, the Moto G4 will feel dated, and you’ll be constantly toggling between apps and closing background processes to keep things moving. But if your needs are light, you’ll be happy with the G4’s reliability.

Software-wise, the G4 shipped with Android 6.0 Marshmallow and many regional builds later received Android 7.0 Nougat through Motorola’s typical update cadence, though updates could be sparse and irregular depending on your carrier and region. In 2026, you’ll likely be running some aged Android version with limited security patches. The experience remains clean and near-stock compared to heavy OEM skins, which means fewer bloatware headaches if you found a mostly-unmodified software path. You’ll still get Motorola’s thoughtful gestures (double twist for flashlight, chop to cut to camera) which are tiny, but delightful touches that stand the test of time.

Battery life is one of the G4’s enduring virtues. The phone ships with a 3000mAh battery, which in 2016 was a sweet spot for long days of mixed usage. In 2026, you’ll still get a day of moderate use if you’re light on gaming and heavy on texting and streaming music. Expect a charge-run-late scenario if you’re constantly gaming or using the device as a hotspot. Motorola included fast charging on certain variants, but as with many 2016 devices, the charger isn’t universal and you’ll want the original adapter or a fast-charging alternative to keep pace with modern schedules.

Charging is standard micro-USB, not USB-C—this will feel dated to anyone who has become accustomed to the USB-C era. It’s a reminder that the G4 belongs to a transitional moment in mobile design when USB-C was just starting to become the norm and big, reversible ends were not a universal feature. If you’re into future-proofing, that USB-C point is one of the reasons newer devices from the same era felt more convenient; but for budget usage, the micro-USB port did its job without fuss.

## Cameras: A Respectable Snapper for Its Time

The Moto G4 features a 13MP rear camera with an f/2.0 aperture and phase-detect autofocus on some variants, plus a 5MP front-facing camera. In reality, this camera is good enough to snag everyday moments without giving you heartburn. In good light, photos lean toward natural colors with solid dynamic range for a budget device. In low light, you’ll notice the usual budget-phone struggles: more noise, softer detail, and a need to steady the shot for longer exposure times.

Video capture tops out at 1080p, which is fine for social sharing and quick clips, but you’re not getting cinema-quality footage here. The HDR-like processing is modest; you’ll get a bit more punch if you tap into portrait modes on certain variants or use software tools after the fact to boost sharpness. For selfies, the 5MP front shooter is adequate for video calls, though the field of view is not particularly wide and lighting will dramatically affect the final look.

It’s a good camera for sharing quick moments and documenting everyday life rather than crafting award-winning photos.

## Connectivity, Audio, and Day-to-Day Practicality

In terms of connectivity, the Moto G4 has the essentials: 4G LTE, Bluetooth, Wi-Fi, GPS, and a microSD slot for expansion. Yes, it’s a capable little ecosystem for everyday tasks. Audio quality from the single rear speaker is adequate for podcasts and videos in a pinch, though you’ll get clearer sound through wired headphones or a modest Bluetooth speaker. The device supports FM radio in some regions, which was a pleasant throwback feature for folks who like to listen without data consumption—especially during commutes and long road trips where streaming isn’t ideal.

The G4’s software experience isn’t loaded with heavy bloatware, which is a relief if you’ve ever dealt with carriers that insist you install a dozen apps before you can check your weather. It’s the kind of clean, approachable software that complements the hardware without fighting with you at every turn.

## Value, Longevity, and the 2026 Perspective

Here’s the crux: the Moto G4 was excellent value in its day. It offered a decently sized display, a reliable battery, expandable storage, and near-stock Android. Today, it’s a relic in a world of fast-charging, big-battery, high-refresh-rate smartphones. If your goal is a budget device that can perform basic tasks, the G4 still does that—with caveats: software updates are limited, apps may be newer and heavier than the hardware can natively optimize, and the camera isn’t competitive with modern mid-range devices.

But if you’re a collector, a tinkerer, or someone who wants a low-stress second phone, the G4 can be a charming choice. It’s a device you can treat as a long-running public service: it will dutifully load your messaging apps, fetch emails, and allow you to browse the web without making you cry about performance. It’s a retro-champion in the most wholesome sense: reliable, unpretentious, and a gentle reminder that not every phone needs to be a rocketship.

## How It Stacks Up Against Modern “Budget” Phones (And Why It Still Matters)

Let’s step outside the time machine for a moment. In 2026, the budget market is flooded with devices that pack bigger batteries, faster processors, and better cameras. The Moto G4, by today’s standards, is the picture of “classic budget philosophy”: do a few things well, don’t overpromise, and ship at a price that doesn’t feel like a lottery ticket. The value proposition isn’t about raw power; it’s about the experience: a beeline toward a clean Android journey with minimal bloat and a chassis that’s durable enough for daily life.

If you’re one of those people who sips tea and reminisces about the days when phones were simple and plastic was the new luxury, the G4 remains a charming artifact. It’s the gadget equivalent of a vintage console—not the most powerful on the shelf, but a reliable friend that brings a smile whenever you power it on and remember how far smartphones have come.

## Final Verdict: Should You Buy a Moto G4 in 2026?

If you’re reading this and thinking, “I want a cheap phone for basic tasks or a nostalgia piece,” the Moto G4 is a curious contender. It will still run essential apps, deliver a readable display, and provide a battery life you can trust through a regular day. However, if you crave modern speeds, the latest camera tricks, secure software updates, or long-term future-proofing, there are better options in the current market. The G4 is a decision that weighs sentiment against practicality.

For the curious, the G4 is also a fantastic fallback device for traveling, a spare phone for kids who need calls and a camera, or a “project phone” for hobbyist tinkering—like booting up an old ROM and exploring the era when Moto was still trying to decide if they should join the USB-C club or the last-gen micro-USB party.
