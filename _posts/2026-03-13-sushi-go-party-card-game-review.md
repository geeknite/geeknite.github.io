## How to Play (High-Level Rules and Flow)

Sushi Go! Party is a card-drafting game designed for rapid rounds and social speed. The core loop is simple: you look at your hand, pick one card to keep, pass the rest to the player on your left, and repeat until all cards in the hand have been drafted. Then you reveal your picks and score according to the current menu(s). The game ends after a fixed number of rounds, with the player amassing the most points declared winner.

### Setup

- Shuffle the sushi cards; this is your deck building magic. The party edition often includes multiple menus; each menu has its own scoring rules, which adds a delightful level of complexity without requiring a degree in culinary physics.
- Deal a hand to each player. The number of cards dealt depends on player count; the more players, the smaller each hand tends to be—keeping things snappy so you don’t drift into “board meeting at a retirement home” pace.
- Place the menu cards face-up in the center. These are the scoring cues you’ll chase (or avoid) each round.
- Decide winner conditions and number of rounds. Commonly, games run for 5–7 rounds in party mode, but you can tweak this if you want to simulate a sushi banquet that lasts longer than your attention span.

### Turn Sequence

1) Everyone looks at their hand and secretly selects one card to keep for the next phase.
2) Simultaneously, players pass the remaining cards to the left. This is the social test: can you mind-read the person next to you well enough to hoard the cards you think they’ll want to discard to you?
3) Repeat steps 1 and 2 until all cards have been drafted.
4) Resolve scoring for the round according to the active menus. You’ll see a mix of straightforward scoring (e.g., collect sets of the same sushi type) and more nuanced scoring (e.g., some cards give you points if you pair them with other specific cards).

This cycle creates the “creaminess” of the game: it’s quick enough to stay fun, but with enough variability that you’re not indulging in the same exact strategy every time. The inclusion of different menus means your choices aren’t just about grabbing the best immediate points; you also need to chase long-term, table-wide objectives while trying not to tip your hand too early.

### Scoring (A Practical Overview)

- Score cards individually: some sushi types grant straightforward points; others grant bonuses for sets or sequences.
- Menu-driven scoring: at the end of each round, the active Menu cards define which patterns earn you points. Some menus reward variety; others reward repetition; a few mix both to keep people honest.
- End-game scoring: once all rounds are complete, you tally your points from all menus. The highest total wins. There’s a satisfying moment when you realize your earlier gamble on a single weird card actually paid off big time; the entire table experiences a small, polite moment of awe directed at your card-chess genius.

### End of Round and End of Game

Rounds proceed in a fast loop, with players discarding their excess cards and revealing their scores for the round’s menus. After the final round, you do a final tally. The winner is the person with the most points. In practice, this can lead to a gentle, friendly feud about who cornered the market on egg nigiri, who accidentally sabotaged their own score by chasing a zany menu, and who had the most dramatic face when their draw finally paid off.

## Strategy and Tips (For Real-Life Humans Who Also Like Pizza)

- Read the room, not just the cards. The beauty of Sushi Go! Party is that it rewards social awareness as much as card management. If your table is a hive of suspicious glances and competitive smirks, you’ll want to lean into menu-driven strategies that exploit what others seem to value in a given round.
- Don’t chase a single card too hard. The game is about juggling multiple objectives across menus. If you go all-in on a single type, you risk missing out on other scoring channels that are easier to complete than you think.
- Balance risk vs. reward. Early rounds are often about setting up future rounds; you can take a risk now if it dramatically improves your end-game options, but you might also want to stay in a safer path that guarantees consistent points.
- Use memory, but don’t hyper-animate your brain. It’s good to remember what people passed you last round, but the drafting pace is so quick that you can’t rely on a full memory palace. Instead, pick a few signals you watch for from opponents and use them to inform your decisions.
- Watch your options. If your hand contains several high-scoring cards that benefit from set-building, you might delay taking one to prevent a table from locking in a counter-draft that destroys your plan. This is where the table chatter starts to feel like witty banter instead of a pure strategy meltdown.
- Don’t underestimate the simple combos. Some menus reward the boring-but-reliable cards in combination with more exciting ones. You can often build a respectable score by mixing a classic sushi set with a clever, under-the-radar card that your rivals ignore.

### Parting Strategy Tips by Web-Spell and Glitter (Lighthearted)

- If you’re at a crowded table, aim for three to four score channels. This makes your strategy adaptable, and it also ensures you aren’t forced into a single path that gets stomped by a table full of optimizers.
- In a pinch, prioritize cards that offer flexibility for future menus. Cards that can serve multiple purposes are the universal adapters of the Sushi Go! Party world.
- Remember that the fun isn’t only about points. The social glue—the playful glances, the mild cheating jokes, the mock-serious “I totally didn’t see that coming” reactions—matters just as much as your final score.

## Sushi Go! Party vs Sushi Go! (Original) – A Quick Comparison

Let’s set the stage for those who already know the classic: Sushi Go! Party is essentially the party-friendly evolution of the original concept. Both operate on the same core mechanic—draft one card per turn and pass the rest around—but Party introduces more menus and a higher player count ceiling, which translates into more dynamic scoring and more opportunities for table banter.

- Scope: The original is lean and elegant; Party expands the scope with multiple menus, giving you more scoring possibilities and more table talk.
- Player count: Party is designed to handle larger groups; the original is more comfortable with smaller groups. If you’ve got eight friends who all claim to be “gamers,” Party is likely the better fit.
- Accessibility: Both are approachable, but Party’s menus provide built-in hooks for players who crave a little extra strategy without requiring a full-blown strategy game. If you love “lite but with depth” design, you’ll appreciate how Party maintains speed while adding layers.

There are a few light house-rule options you can adopt to tailor the game to your table:
