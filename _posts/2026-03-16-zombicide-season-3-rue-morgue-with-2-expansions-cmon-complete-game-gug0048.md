## How it Plays: Quick Primer

Zombicide is a fully cooperative dungeon-crawling (but zombie-escaping) game designed for 2-6 players. You pick a crew of survivors, each with a special skill like “shooting while blinded by a shopping-cart hail” or “pocket-sized extinguisher.” The goal: survive the night, clear the streets, and complete scenario objectives.

At its core, Rue Morgue uses a modular board: grid-based streets with buildings, alleys, doors, and sometimes objective tokens. On your turn, you’ll move, search, loot, and fight. The zombie AI is a mix of predictable patterns and chaotic spawns, which means you’ll be cursing a lot but learning to count your shot opportunities.

Now, the two expansions: while we won’t reveal every spoiler here, know that they add more variety to both the survivor deck and the zombie deck, more mission layouts, and a few “oh-no-you-didn’t” moments that remind you that zombies are not your friends.

## Campaign Mode and Replayability

Rue Morgue shines when you play it as a campaign — a loose sequence of connected scenarios where your crew grows (or shrinks, usually in the grim way). The two expansions significantly ratchet up replayability: more missions, new enemy types that require different tactics, and new reward tokens that can tilt the balance between “we’ll survive this night” and “we’ll survive until our last coffee.” The campaign mode is not endurance testing in the modern sense; it’s more like playing a never-ending episodic TV show where every bad decision has consequences and you can hear the gurgle of a zombie in the next room.

If you’re new to Zombicide, you’ll want to start with simpler scenarios before attempting the complete Season 3 box. The rulebook is clear enough for experienced players, but the two expansions add enough new bits to make it feel fresh even for people who have played through Rue Morgue more times than they care to admit.

## The Expansions in Detail

### Survivors, Weapons, and Objectives

The new survivors come with abilities that will tempt you to try new strategies. One character might excel at closing doors behind him to corral the undead, while another can heal teammates in the thick of the action. Weapons and equipment are varied enough to keep the shopping list exciting but balanced enough to prevent the meta from collapsing into a “spam the best gun” routine.

The new objectives typically require teamwork and some clever positioning. You’ll need to coordinate to secure a critical location, or to escort a derelict vehicle through a gauntlet of undead. The tension rises when you realize that a misstep on the board can trigger a cascade of zombie spawns that makes your last turn look like a walk in the park.

## Theme, Tone, and Accessibility

Zombicide Season 3: Rue Morgue leans into a pulp-noir zombie fantasy, and it wears that aesthetic on its sleeve. The scenarios feel cinematic rather than purely mechanical, and the pacing often mirrors a suspenseful chase sequence rather than a “roll some dice and hope for the best” shootout. The two expansions reinforce that mood by injecting new narrative hooks and character hooks into the mix.

From an accessibility standpoint, Rue Morgue remains approachable for groups that have some familiarity with cooperative games. If you’re brand new to Zombicide or co-ops in general, expect a learning curve: you’ll be getting up to speed on grid-based movement, line-of-sight, zombie spawns, and the special abilities of your survivors. The expansions add a learning curve of their own, but they also introduce an enjoyable variety that often reduces the repetition of the base game.

## Solo Play and Difficulty

If you’re the kind of gamer who enjoys ripping through campaigns solo, Rue Morgue and its expansions are a mixed bag. The base game supports solo play, but the expansions increase the number of toys in the sandbox—and with more toys come more potential misplays. You’ll need to manage multiple heroes, each with a role, and you’ll feel the weight of every suboptimal decision as you scramble to complete an objective before the horde gets you.

Difficulty scales well. The expansions provide more challenging day-to-day decisions and more complex encounter layouts that require careful planning and execution. If you want a winter project, this is it: a campaign you can start and finish at your own pace, with enough tangents to keep you from getting bored.

## Value, Campaigns, and Longevity

Value hinges on how much you enjoy the Zombicide experience. If you’re in it for the long haul—thematic, campaign-oriented co-ops with heavy narrative interplay—Rue Morgue plus expansions offers many hours of play. You’ll be able to run multiple campaigns, try different survivor rosters, and explore various zombie loadouts. If you prefer shorter, self-contained scenarios, you’ll still find the two expansions a worthwhile addition for variety, but you may crave more bite-size missions.

The box design supports organized play. The inserts keep the map tiles and tokens relatively tidy, which helps when you’re about to launch into a chaotic round where every decision might be the one that saves or dooms your group.

## Strategy Tips and House Rules

- Focus on board control in the early rounds: securing doors, guarding critical intersections, and keeping a rotation of survivors moving rather than standing still except when forced.
- Coordinate fire support: avoid scattering your shots; concentrate your fire on the most dangerous zombie threats to maximize efficiency.
- Manage risk with objective priorities: sometimes it’s better to retreat and regroup than to chase a flashy objective with low odds of success.
