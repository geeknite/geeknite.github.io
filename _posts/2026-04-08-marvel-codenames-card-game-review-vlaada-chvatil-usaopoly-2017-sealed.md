### About the Designer and Publisher
Vlaada Chvatil is a designer who has made a career out of crafting clever tension between theme and mechanism. Codenames, his breakout party game, is the still-popular benchmark for team-based wordplay. USAopoly (the license holder for Marvel Codenames) is the company that tends to take a solid idea and wrap it in as much licensed artwork as possible, which is either a dream or a nightmare depending on how much you love Spider-Man and the color blue.

If you’re here wondering, yes, the 2017 edition is distinct from the original in terms of art direction and card flavor. The core rules are the same—two teams compete to identify their agents on a grid by giving one-word clues with a number. The Marvel twist adds a lot of flavor through imagery, character choices, and a few thematic touches that don’t change the core math of the game, but they definitely tilt the emotional axis toward heroic swagger.

## Unboxing, Components, and First Impressions
The sealed copy holds a promise: polished cardstock, robust cards, and a rulebook that signals it’s not shy about its Marvel license. Here’s what you’ll typically find inside a fresh box:

- A 5 x 5 grid of Marvel character cards (25 in total) with vibrant, comic-book style art
- Red and Blue agent cue cards (for the two teams)
- An Assassin card (they replaced the classic “Assassin” with a thematic stand-in—still a doozy to hit by accident)
- Neutral cards that neither help nor hurt a team immediately
- A compact rulebook that carries the same DNA as the original Codenames but sprinkled with Marvel flavor

- A small guide to card art and character references that Marvel fans will flip through just to catch a favorite moment

The moment you crack open the lid, you’ll notice two things: the art is bold and busy, and the text is readable from a distance—both good signs for a game that requires quick glances across a table. The cards themselves are sturdy, and the backs have a cleaner, more modern Marvel-infused look that will stand up to casual gaming sessions without feeling like it belongs in a collector’s cabinet.

### The Cards and Art Style
The art direction on Marvel Codenames leans into the superhero aesthetic without swamping you in too many details. Each card features a Marvel character, with a consistent portrait style and a small icon indicating alignment (hero, villain, or neutral). While the original Codenames relies on single words, this edition leans into Marvel’s pantheon to give you recognizable anchors for your clues. This is both a strength and a potential pitfall: it’s a blast for Marvel fans, but if your group is allergic to licensed IPs, you might find the theme a tad distracting rather than endearing.

The character roster spans across a spectrum of recognizability, from the mega-stars to the more obscure side-characters. That mix matters when you’re a Spymaster crafting a clue that tries to bridge two or three cards at once. Are you going to clue “Avengers” and hope that both Stark and Cap appear on the grid? Or do you chase something riskier like a hint that could lead to Spider-Man, Thor, and Loki because they all pull from a common source of puns and allusions? It’s a delightful exercise in guest-list curation.

## How to Play: Setup, Clues, and the Rhythm of a Game Night
If you’ve played Codenames, you know the bones: two teams, one Spymaster per team, a grid of 25 word cards, and the goal to identify all of your team’s cards first. Marvel Codenames keeps that architecture but swaps in Marvel-flavored tokens and art and uses Marvel characters on the grid instead of arbitrary words. Here’s the flow:

- Set up a 5x5 grid of Marvel character cards. Randomly determine which team goes first. The first team gets an extra cue card or some advantage in the standard rules (depending on the version of the rules sheet you find in the box).
- The Spymaster sits on one side of the table with a lookup sheet (which one? The one that lists the map of the grid to characters and the proper clue structure). The Spymaster provides one-word clues with a number: for example, “Assemble, 2” might be your way to hint at two characters who are Avengers on the grid. The number tells your partner how many cards on the grid relate to your clue.
- Teammates race to identify their own agents (red or blue) without hitting the Assassin or the Opponent’s agents. The assassin card typically ends the game immediately in a worst-case scenario—cue the dramatic music.
- The game ends when one team identifies all of their agents or hits the assassin by accident, which is a moment of supreme mild panic that’s perfect for comedic relief during later replays.

What makes Marvel Codenames a little different is the thematic layer, which can influence the way you construct clues. The Spymaster might be tempted to conjure a clue around a broader Marvel concept like “Infinity” to capture multiple characters who are part of the broader cosmic pantheon. But the higher the level of abstraction, the more you risk bringing in characters that aren’t actually on the grid. The balance of risk vs. reward is the name of the game here—the same balance that makes base Codenames so deliciously tense, but with a Marvel garnish.

### A Quick Rule refresher (in case you forgot how to be cunning)
- Clues must be a single word (though some expansions and variants let you bend this a little with hyphenated terms). The one-word clue should tie as many of your team’s cards as possible to a single concept.
- The number following the clue tells your teammates how many cards on the grid relate to that clue. A clue of “Spider-Verse, 3” might hint at Spider-Man, Spider-Gwen, and Spider-Verse—if those cards appear on the grid.
- Don’t pick the Assassin card. If you do, your team loses immediately. It’s like stepping on a LEGO at 3 a.m.—the most exciting kind of pain.
- You can stretch clues to cross into neutral characters or even the opposing team’s cards if you misread the grid, but that’s where the back-and-forth banter begins and the fun multiplies.

If you’re after a serious cognitive workout, you’ll want a heavier deduction game; if you want a pile of smiles and a few dramatic reveals, Marvel Codenames is a winner.

## Replayability and Variant Thoughts
Codenames, in its vanilla form, thrives on variety: you can constantly shuffle new word cards to create fresh grids, and you can modify the clue-giving rules or add house rules for a quick switch. Marvel Codenames inherits that DNA but spices it with the Marvel coloring. The grid cards are the same 25-card rhythm, but the characters you see can drastically affect your approach to clue-giving. A well-chosen clue that links two or three adjacent heroes can feel much more satisfying than the same two-word clue you’d drop in the plain version.

What about repacks and expansions? The Marvel license often encourages just one more accessory: new cards or a different roster for the grid. That’s good because it means your family who loves Marvel can come back to the table and experience a slightly altered grid with the same core mechanics. But don’t expect a revolution every time you crack open a new box; you’re still playing Codenames, just wearing a fancy cape.

## Strategy: Practical Tips for Spymasters and Agents
- As a Spymaster: start with broad categories you can safely link to multiple cards on the grid. The trick is to pick a word that doesn’t accidentally pull in an enemy card or the assassin. Marvel’s character roster provides plenty of thematic anchors—AIM for a villain cluster, or Avengers for a hero-laden row—but you’ll rarely hit a perfect three-for-three on your first attempt. Mastery comes from reading the grid and being stingy with your numbers. If your clue pulls in only one of your own cards but guarantees you don’t touch the assassin, you’ve still learned something valuable about the board’s layout.
- As a teammate: trust your Spymaster, but verify. It’s the same as keeping a secret identity: you want to believe their wordplay, but you’re the one choosing the concrete cards on the grid. If there’s any doubt, discuss the card visuals quickly and decide whether you’re willing to risk a misstep on a high-stakes clue.
- Thematic synergy matters: you’ll have better vibes and better guesses if you allow Marvel tropes to guide your thinking. A clue like “Stark Tech, 2” could capture Iron Man and Black Widow if they appear together on the board. If not, it can still be a fun exercise in bridging the gap between puzzle-solving and fan trivia.
- Don’t chase the Assassin—ever. A single misstep with the assassin flips the table into chaos, and chaos is not always entertaining in a friendly family game night. You’ll want to calibrate your risk appetite accordingly.

## Player Count and Table Time
Marvel Codenames is best with 4-8 players (two teams of 2-4). The more players you have, the brisker the rounds tend to feel, as the clue-giving and guessing pairs rotate more quickly and the cascade of ideas becomes more entertaining. A single session usually runs 30-60 minutes depending on how talkative your table is and how dramatic your reveals are. If you’re planning a party game night, Marvel Codenames slots nicely into the middle of the evening—between a light party game and a heavier strategy title—with the same energy and excitement you’d expect from a superhero team-up.
