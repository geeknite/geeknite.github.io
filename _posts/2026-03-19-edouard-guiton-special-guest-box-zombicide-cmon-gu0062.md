## Unboxing and First Impressions
Right out of the mailer, the Special Guest Box announces its presence with a confident little squawk of collector-nerd joy. The outer sleeve is glossy enough to attract fingerprints from a distance, which is perfect if you enjoy playing “Where did I leave that chrome paint bottle?” while the monkeys in your brain scream for a game. The packaging design leans heavily into Edouard Guiton’s aesthetic—big bold lines, a dash of neon, and enough character silhouettes to remind you that you’re about to assemble a small army of pixelated heroes and terrifying undead ahead of a long weekend.

Inside, the contents are ingeniously organized in a way that makes you feel you’re unboxing a boutique action figure line rather than a tabletop game expansion. There are not one, not two, but three trays carefully stacked with miniatures wrapped in protective foam. Each miniature is sculpted with Edouard’s flair: dynamic poses, exaggerated features for readability on a tabletop, and enough texture to make you wonder if you should prime before attempting to wash them. We’ll get into the painting potential in a later section, but suffice to say the sculpts invite your brush, even if you’re more of a dry-brush purist who believes color belongs in the box and not on the tabletop.

The accompanying material includes a signed postcard from Edouard—yes, a small, glossy card you can pretend is the most expensive, artisanal souvenir you’ve ever bought on a whim. He signs it in a manner that looks like a cross between a graphically bold signature and a barcode for “fun,” which is either charming or mildly unsettling depending on how much you love autographs on your hobby gear. There’s also a special tile and a handful of exclusive tokens that feel like you’re gaining access to a backstage pass for the Zombicide theatre troupe.

The unboxing experience, for better or worse, does not waste your time. It validates your impulse to buy with a tangible sense of “this is not just another reprint; this is a curated moment in your tabletop life.” If you’re into the showmanship of hobby shopping, you’ll feel right at home. If you’re a practical person who just wants to know if the minis can be used for a standard game night without major modifications, you’ll still feel seen. It’s a win in unboxing theatre, and the extra mile with Edouard’s branding gives the whole package a collectible vibe that can appreciate on a shelf—if you can resist the urge to display it like a trophy rather than a gaming utility.

### Aesthetic and Build Quality
The minis in this set are crisp enough that you can identify subtle features in natural light, which is a rarity in mid-range board-game minis. The materials feel sturdy, with a slightly heavier feel than typical cardboard-and-plastic boxes. The paint jobs on pre-primed pieces are tidy, which is pleasant if you’re impatient, but if you’re one of those painters who live for the challenging, blister-inducing paint sets that ruin your lunch hour, you’ll still want to prime and basecoat from scratch.

The new survivor figure—the titular guest box character—wears an outfit that is a hybrid of homage and whimsy: practical gear with a flash of stylized armor. It’s the kind of aesthetic that says, “I’m ready for a zombie apocalypse, but I’d also like to be photographed in a museum exhibit.” If Edouard’s signature touches translate well onto the model, you’ll be chasing the details with a brush to emphasize the fine lines around the face and the creases in the fabric. The rest of the crew, including a few new zombie variants, carry consistent sculpts that scream “Zombicide” while nodding to the guest artist’s influence. The result is a box that looks great on a table, photographs well on social media, and, if you’re the patient kind of painter, transforms into showpieces for your display shelf.

## Painting and Assembly Prospects
Let’s be practical: how much effort does this require? The short answer: not much, but with room for you to shine if you want to invest time.
- The survivor and several zombies are primed and sculpted in ways that make base coating straightforward. A simple color set will yield clean results without heavy underpainting or advanced shading techniques.
- If you enjoy layering, contrast paints, or washes, Edouard’s designs provide excellent opportunities for depth, especially in the leather, fabric, and armor elements. Expect to spend a little longer on your favorites to achieve that “gallery-quality” finish, but you won’t drown in a sea of edges and gnarly crevices.
- Customizing the base with a little texture or scenic bits can elevate the whole collection. A simple basing trick—grit, a splash of dry pigment, and a dab of matte varnish—can transform a mass of plastic into a cohesive diorama moment.

If you’re a new painter, you’ll be pleasantly surprised by the legibility of the details. If you’re a veteran who wants a challenge, Edouard’s pieces offer you a nice stage to test advanced techniques like dry brushing and selective edge highlighting. The box invites you to treat this as a weekend project rather than a quick drop-in during a gaming session, which is exactly what many hobbyists want from a special edition release.
