## Unboxing and first impressions
### What’s in the box?
The Everything Box is pitched as an “expansion” but it’s effectively a self-contained megabox of prompts that can be used on top of the base game or as a stand-alone deck for late-night chaos. Inside, you’ll find three hundred cards, a leather-thin artisanal hint of guilt, and the distinct aroma of late-night decision-making.

- 240 White cards (the prompts) and 60 Black cards (the prompts for the players), plus a handful of bonus cards designed to be wildcard punchlines. The ratio is intentionally chaotic: more prompts mean more opportunities to mispronounce a word you can’t pronounce at 2 a.m.
- Card stock quality is the typical CAH standard: sturdy enough to survive a giggle-attack, questionable enough to survive a coffee spill. If you’ve ever handled a CAH deck, you know the drill: a textured matte finish that handles sticky fingers with grace.
- A tiny rule card that claims you can “play with the base game” and “incorporate spectator prompts.” Translation: you can turn a normal game night into a full-contact improv show if your friends are into it.

The packaging is clean, almost suspiciously neat for a product designed to make you rethink where you’ve placed your sense of humor. The artwork on the cards remains in the same irreverent vein that CAH fans adore, leaning into the kinds of jokes you’ll either groan at or loudly declare as your new favorite insult. It’s audacious, it’s brash, and it’s probably going to offend someone at your table—probably you, at some point, which is part of the charm if you’re the kind of person who collects horror-comedy posters.

The card stock is decent: not the slick, premium gloss you see in some newer card games, but sturdy enough to flip and shuffle without invoking a midlife crisis about your card-shuffling technique. The edges show the wear of a well-loved deck rather than a dealer’s new release. If you’ve got a dog or a housemate who treats cards like Frisbees, you’ll be okay—these babies won’t crumble from a casual toss across the coffee table. This isn’t a “coffee-table coffee-table” piece; it’s a “table-broken-will” piece. It will survive your friends’ competitive banter and questionable boomerang jokes.

### Visual design and humor cadence
The Everything Box leans into the CAH aesthetic: clean text, bold prompts, and the occasional card that makes you question your own moral compass. The font is legible, the contrast is high enough to read in dim lighting (very important for late-night sessions), and the artwork—where there is any—leans into stylized minimalism rather than glossy gore. The humor cadence is a mix of pop culture references, edgy social commentary, and some old-school punchlines that remind you why the game has endured long enough to spawn a 300-card behemoth.

## How to play with the Everything Box
### Setup and compatibility
You don’t need to overhaul your existing CAH rules to use the Everything Box; it slots into the base game as a supplementary deck. The recommended approach is to designate a “wildcard prompt” round every few turns to keep energy high and avoid predictability. If you’re playing with newcomers, consider a shorter round limit so new players don’t memorize the punchlines by the second game.

Here are some practical tips:
- Shuffle the 300-card deck thoroughly before you start. A good shuffle adds suspense; a bad shuffle adds awkwardness that you can mine for material later. 
- Decide on whether to draw from the main deck or the Everything Box deck at each round. A 50/50 mix works for most groups.
- Establish guardrails before the party begins. The goal is fun, not trauma. If a card crosses a line for your group, set it aside or implement a quick-house rule to swap it out.

### The 17+ factor: a quick orientation
Cards labeled 17+ imply a mature, adult, unfiltered sense of humor. If your party includes folks who blush at PG-13 jokes, this expansion may feel like stepping into a red-lit comedy club. For many players, the 17+ threshold signals permission to push boundaries—without crossing into real-world harm. If you aim for inclusive humor, you’ll still find material here, but you’ll need to curate responsibly and choose triggers with care. The booklet that accompanies Everything Box is not a safety guide, but a reminder that you own the laughter you’re about to unleash.

### A few sample card dynamics (without spoiling your night)
- A white card might complete a prompt with a humor punchline that lands like a mic drop. If you’re quick on your feet, you’ll hear the groups erupt in recognition of the perfect pairing.
- A black card might compare two absurd concepts, forcing players to improvise a closing line that ties them together. The best returns are sharp, surprising, and somehow relatable to someone in the room.

## Content quality and design notes
### Card stock, finish, and durability
The Everything Box uses the standard CAH card stock: not too thin, not rock-hard. It shuffles reasonably well, with a satisfying card-snap that makes a satisfying sound when a round becomes a competition of who can deliver the zinger first. The printing is clean; the font remains legible even under a ceiling fan’s draft, which is to say you won’t have to fight with glare during a summer game night.

### Artwork and tone alignment
This expansion leans into topical humor, cultural references, and situational absurdity. It’s not meant to be subtle; it’s meant to be a mirror held up to your group’s humor preferences. If your friends love witty wordplay as much as they love pop culture callbacks, you’ll find this deck a reliable spark plug.

### House rules and customization
One of the great strengths of CAH is how adaptable it is to different crowds. The Everything Box amplifies this by offering more material to mix and match with your existing rules. Common house-rule strategies include:
- Setting a ‘soft-no’ line: any card you don’t want to see at the table gets a quick veto.
- Time-limited rounds: 60-second prompts to keep the energy high and the table from drying out.
- Spectator prompts: allow a non-player to contribute a prompt or a punchline, which can generate surprising winners.

### Interactivity and table dynamics
The 300-card expansion brings a more dynamic interplay to the table. You’ll see groups splitting into alliances or mercilessly self-deprecating. The humor tends to ignite when players recognize that a prompt aligns with shared experiences, or when a player unmasks a surprising cultural reference that somehow lands on the right brain synapse at the exact moment when everyone is expecting a cringe clapback. It’s not subtle, but that’s part of the charm.

### Compared to other expansions
The Everything Box isn’t just “more of the same.” It attempts to be a curated selection that can integrate with multiple CAH editions and even non-CAH prompts (with a bit of improvisational finesse). If you’ve already collected several expansions, you’ll recognize the extended universe that the 300 cards create. ### Storage and care
Storing 300 cards in addition to your base deck means you’ll likely end up with a larger shoebox or a dedicated CAH tote. It’s not a premium gym bag, but you’ll appreciate having a recessed space for small tokens, a timer, and those extra rule cards that you’ll inevitably forget you own until the moment you need them. Maintenance is simple: keep the box dry, and resist the urge to play in a kitchen during a spill-heavy dinner party.
