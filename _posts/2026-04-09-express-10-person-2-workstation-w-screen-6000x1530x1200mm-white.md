## Ergonomics and user experience
### Comfort at scale
Ergonomics are not a luxury here; they are a necessity. The two workstations are designed so that each user can adjust chair height and monitor position independently, while sharing the screen between them. This can be surprisingly ergonomic once you align monitor heights to eye level and set each seating position for a slight elbow angle. If you have team members with different heights, a compact footrest or adjustable chair is worth considering. The desk surface is smooth and consistent, which reduces friction during long hours of typing and brainstorming. The key is to empower users to find their own sweet spot without fighting the desk for dominance.
### Screen height, viewing angles, and glare
The central screen should be set to a height that roughly aligns with the upper chest level of an average adult when seated. If glare becomes an issue, consider a monitor hood or anti glare film. In conference mode, when the whole team is staring at the screen, brightness and contrast settings become more important. A consistent lighting scheme helps; you want to avoid policy meetings that rely on screen brightness to wake people up. A subtle reminder: blue light filters can help with eye strain during long sessions.
### Collaboration versus focus zones
One of the selling points of a large shared desk is the natural creation of zones. The central screen serves as a hub for collaboration, while each worker still has a personal zone that can be adjusted for privacy. Use screensaver and background lighting to denote zones and create a sense of order rather than chaos. In practice, teams quickly settle into a rhythm of rotating the main screen while maintaining personal space for individual tasks. The net effect is a hybrid of teamwork and personal responsibility that many offices crave.
