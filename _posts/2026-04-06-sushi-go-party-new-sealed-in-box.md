## What's in the Box

### Cards and Components
The core of Sushi Go Party is a rotating deck of dish cards and a menu board that customizes the rounds. The sealed box includes:
- A large stack of dish cards featuring sushis of all stripes: nigiri, maki, sashimi, pudding, and a handful of novelty items that vary with the menus. The art is crisp, the icons are legible, and the color coding helps you spot your favorites at a glance during the frantic pass-and-pick moments.
- Menu cards for eight players: Each round you choose a different set of dishes, with the menu dictating scoring. The Party edition often includes more menus than the basic Sushi Go, which means more ways to rack up points and more opportunities to draft into the perfect set.
- Scoring tokens or markers: Small tokens used to track scoring across rounds. You might not need them if you are comfortable with mental math, but the tokens add a tactile element that makes each scoring decision satisfying.
- Rulebook and quick-start guides: A compact, friendly manual that explains how to run a game in under 20 minutes for 2-8 players. The quick-start guide is particularly useful for groups new to drafting.
- Player references: Short cards that remind players of scoring rules for each dish. They are handy for new players who are still trying to memorize each dish's scoring quirks.

### Artwork and Card Stock
The card stock in Sushi Go Party feels sturdy enough to survive a few accidental shuffles, spills, and table scuffles. It is not as thick as deluxe card stock, but it is robust enough for regular game nights. The edges are clean, the corners are rounded, and the color saturation holds up after a few passes through the dishwasher -- metaphorically speaking, not literally.

The art style remains consistent with the Sushi Go vibe: cute, minimalist, and colorful. The cards are small enough to handle with a single hand, which is essential for a game that requires constant drafting while trying to keep track of your opponents' choices.

### The "Party" Flavor
Sushi Go Party introduces a menu system that allows players to customize each round. Instead of a single fixed deck, you choose from a set of menus that rotate. The result is not just a better party experience, but a more varied one. The variety makes repeat plays feel fresh; you can experienced players with different strategies.

### Setup Time and Play Length
Set-up time is relatively quick. You need to lay out the selected menus, shuffle the dish cards, and deal hands to players. In a typical 4-6 player game, you can expect your first round to begin within 2-3 minutes of the first page-turn. The actual play time depends on the number of menus included and how aggressively players draft. For larger groups (up to eight players), the rounds might take a touch longer, but it remains very approachable for party game nights.

The flow of a round is elegant in its simplicity: you pick one card from your hand and pass the deck along to the neighbor, and you keep your chosen card. After a predetermined number of passes, the round ends, and you tally points according to the menus. The next round uses a different menu, and the process repeats. The pattern is intuitive, but the variable menus create enough novelty to keep players engaged.

## Gameplay and Strategy

### Card Drafting Core
The heart of Sushi Go Party is the drafting mechanic. Each player starts with a hand of cards (the exact number depending on the number of players and the menus chosen). On each turn, you select one card to keep and pass the rest to the neighbor. The drafting speed is brisk, and the tension builds as you glimpse what the other players are discarding and choosing. The drafting mechanic is accessible and encourages real-time decision-making since you often need to weigh immediate points against future potential.

The party edition's menus provide multiple scoring triggers. Some menus reward you for collecting complementary sets; others reward you for aiming for high-looking combos; others rely on timing and order.

### Menu Systems and Scoring Variation
Each round uses a menu card (or a set of them) that dictates which dish types score. Some menus reward you for collecting patterns rather than raw counts. The novelty of the 'party' variant is that the menus can be swapped between rounds, providing a broader range of strategic options. This means that you might start with a score-heavy menu early and pivot to a more collection-based strategy later in the game.

One practical tip: keep a rough mental tally of which menus have been used and what you still need. The more menus you have in the rotation, the more you must track. If you prefer a lighter experience, limit the number of menus in play; if you want more challenge and variety, include more.

### Interaction and Table Feel
The game fosters social interaction without heavy conflict. Your choices steal points from others by depriving them of the cards they need to complete a set, which creates a polite form of competition. There is a playful competitive vibe rather than a cut-throat vibe. The table noise increases as players try to predict which cards you will take and adjust their own picks accordingly.

### A Note on Player Count
Sushi Go Party scales nicely from 2 to 8 players, though the tempo shifts with more participants. In tighter group sizes (4-6 players), rounds feel snappier and more decisive; with eight players, there can be a deliciously chaotic scramble that still remains readable and fun.
