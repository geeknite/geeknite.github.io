## What’s in the Original Edition (2015) and why it matters
The moment you crack open a New In Box copy of Exploding Kittens, you’re transported to a time when memes and tabletop games collided like two cats chasing a laser pointer. The original edition is a compact, colorful package that includes a 56-card deck and a handful of rules that feel both simple and mercilessly clever. The art, courtesy of The Oatmeal (Matthew Inman), is a chaotic blend of cute felines and daredevil chaos, which perfectly matches the game’s mood: lighthearted, a bit subversive, and absolutely not afraid of a good pun or three.

What does the deck actually contain? In the standard 2015 base deck, you’ve got:

- Several Exploding Kitten cards (the fatal, must-avoid hazard)
- Defuse cards (the get-out-of-explosion-one-time cards that let you live to see another round)
- A mix of utility cards like See the Future, Attack, Skip, and Shuffle
- Nope cards that let you counter other players’ actions, adding that delightful edge of social back-and-forth

The balance is deliberately lean: not too many card types, but enough variety to keep each round surprising. The Original Edition’s art style leans into the humorous, slightly chaotic vibe you’d expect from a game created for a broad audience: it’s just silly enough to be a crowd-pleaser, but sharp enough to satisfy the geeks who enjoy a clever mechanical twist.

## How to play (the quick-and-dirty rules you can teach in a coffee break)
Exploding Kittens is, at its core, a hot potato game with a lot of cat puns and a dash of luck. Here’s the barebones version to get you started without needing to wade through a tome:

### Setup
- Remove any Exploding Kitten cards and Defuse cards from the deck to form the base deck. Add 4 Exploding Kitten cards into the deck, then shuffle in enough Defuse cards so that every player who is dealt 1 Defuse won’t automatically die on their first flip.
- Deal 7 cards to each player (this number might vary slightly depending on the version you’re playing; the 2015 base rules typically land around this range).
- Place the remaining Defuse cards back into the deck and shuffle. It’s your job to not hit an Exploding Kitten on the very first draw, but let’s just say patience is optional here.

### Core actions and turns
- On your turn, you draw a card from the deck (unless you played an Attack card to force your opponent to take multiple turns immediately).
- You may play any number of action cards from your hand before drawing. These cards can do a range of things: skip your turn, see the future (peek at the top few cards of the deck), attack (force the next player to take extra turns), shuffle the deck, or counter other players’ moves with a Nope card.
- If you draw an Exploding Kitten, you must defuse it if you have a Defuse card. If you do, you discard the kit and continue; if you don’t, you’re out of the round.
- The last player standing wins the round. The fun (and chaos) ratchets up as numbers dwindle and each new draw becomes a cliffhanger with a cat-shaped banner screaming at you from the deck.

### Key card types (cliffs notes)
- Exploding Kitten: The doom tripwire of the entire round. Draw one, and you’re done unless you can Defuse it.
- Defuse: A lifeline. Each Defuse card lets you avert disaster once, and you can stash it anywhere in your hand after you use it? Not quite—keep it as a card in your hand, and you’ll hope you never forget you have it.
- See the Future: Preview 3 cards from the top of the deck. This one is the “spoiler” card that makes you look like a chess grandmaster for a moment—until you realize you’re still playing with cats and chaos.
- Attack, Skip, Shuffle, See the Future: Each of these cards reshapes the tempo of the round in deliciously selfish ways. Attack piles pressure on the next player; Skip lets you dodge a draw; Shuffle messes with your opponents’ expectations; See the Future is a friendly nudge that says, “We’re about to get weird.”
- Nope: A universal veto card that can stop almost any action, but not all. The social dynamics here are delicious: you bluff, you bait, you counter, and you laugh as someone tries to “Nope” your “See the Future” and ends up regretting life choices.

## How to play well when you’re playing with a feline-inspired randomizer
Strategy in Exploding Kittens isn’t poker-face-level depth, but there are still meaningful decisions hidden under the chaos. If you’re opening a NIB Original Edition with a bunch of friends who have mild to moderate cat allergies to danger and suspense, you’ll want to lean into these patterns:

- Use See the Future at the right moment. The knowledge you gain about two or three upcoming cards can be the edge you need to dodge an Exploding Kitten later in the round. Don’t overdo it; you want to appear confident, not like you’re a clairvoyant who just peeked into the deck’s assassination plan.
- Time your Defuses. In the early rounds, you’ll want to save your Defuse cards for a clutch moment: the moment when everyone else has already surrendered to the chaos and you realize you have a lifeline that can swing the match in your favor.
- Don’t ignore the socials. The social aspect of Exploding Kittens is where the “party game” label shines. The Nope cards enable playful taunts and strategic counters, but the real trick is reading your table. When someone pretends they don’t care about the game but slyly eyes your Defuse, you’ve discovered the social currency of this title.
- Attack to disrupt or to claim. If you’re behind, an Attack card can jolt everyone into a chaotic scramble, increasing the odds that someone else will explode—rebalancing your chances as you attempt to skate through the pandemonium.
- Embrace the humor. The art and phrases on the cards aren’t just decoration; they’re a social signal—an invitation to laugh, to rib your friends, and to remember that a game about potential kabooms doesn’t have to be a drag.

## NIB realities: is the Original Edition still worth it today?
Today’s modern tabletop market is saturated with spin-offs, reprints, and Party Packs. So why bother with the 2015 Original Edition in NIB form? Here are a few reasons we keep coming back to it:

- Simplicity that scales. The rules are short enough to teach in a single coffee break, but the interplay between cards creates enough micro-dynamics to keep seasoned players entertained.
- The art hits a nostalgic sweet spot. The Oatmeal’s visual humor translates well across generations, which makes this edition approachable for younger players while still entertaining adults who grew up with memes.
- It’s a reliable “gateway” game. If you’re mentoring new players to the hobby, Exploding Kittens Original Edition is a low-risk entry point that teaches turn order, bluffing, and basic probability without requiring a deep library of expansions.
- Collectibility with practicality. A sealed 2015 edition has sentimental value for fans who want to flaunt a piece of internet-era history on a shelf, and the NIB status adds a touch of rarity to the overall package.

Of course, there are caveats. The game can feel short, and the chaos can be polarizing—some players crave more strategic depth, while others relish the laugh-track chaos that erupts when a deck run goes haywire. If you’re a grump about “one more party game,” you’ll want to pair Exploding Kittens with something meatier. If you’re a sucker for memes and quick-fire rounds, you’re in your element.

For those who want to compare editions, look at the Party Pack and other expansions that came after. The Party Pack added more cards and larger group dynamics, which changes the tempo of the game significantly. The Original Edition, especially in a NIB state, remains a crisp, compact snapshot of the game’s origin and its early viral moment.

## Design, production, and the culture of cat-powered chaos
From a design perspective, Exploding Kittens was built to be accessible. The rules are lightweight enough to be explained in minutes, while the card interactions give you enough room to improvise and moralize about whether you would risk a Defuse for the glory of a perfect See the Future reveal.

The 2015 edition is a product of a mid-2010s design ethos: bright, punchy, meme-friendly, and a little bit chaotic. The box, cards, and art are cohesive; you feel like you’re stepping into a curated highlight reel of internet humor rather than a rigid game design. That’s not a flaw—it’s the entire premise. The game doesn’t pretend to be a brain-twister; it pretends to be a social experience with a dash of luck, which is exactly what the original backers likely expected when they pledged their time and money.

In terms of component quality, the Original Edition’s card stock and finish hold up decently after repeated shuffles and occasional slap-backs on a coffee table. If you’re opening a NIB copy, you’ll probably notice a fresh deck that’s brisk to shuffle and a box that snaps closed with a satisfying click—narrative cues that you’re dealing with something that has aged well and can live in a dedicated shelf space next to your other retro favorites.
