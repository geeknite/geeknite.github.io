Used - Very Good condition
The listing says used - very good. What does that mean? In the language of used media, it means the disc probably plays with minor cosmetic marks, the booklet is intact, and the hub hole has not melted from a car trip that was too hot. We test for major scratches and read errors, because in the age of optical drives, a single imperfection can become a gatekeeper to your enjoyment. On this copy, the surface shows slight scuffs, a couple of faint marks around the inner ring, but nothing that would cause skippage or audio dropout during a windy verse on Money or One Night.

Testing and testing again: how to evaluate a used CD
- Visual inspection: check for deep scratches, scuffs, or a warped edge. A warped edge can cause the disc to wobble in portable players and car stereos.
- Playthrough: listen for skips, repeats, or audible pops. If you hear a click every few seconds, it might be a fingerprint smudge or debris on the data layer. Gently wipe with a microfiber cloth.
- Cleanliness: a light cleaning can improve skip resistance, but avoid aggressive cleaning methods that could scratch the label. If the disc is particularly stubborn, consider a professional cleaning sleeve or a vetting service for used media.
- Verification: test across multiple devices if possible. If it plays flawlessly on a CD player but stutters on a laptop or car stereo, you have to consider the variability of drive quality.

Comparison with digital and vinyl
Digital purchases give you convenience; vinyl gives you ritual and warmth. CD remains a middle ground: portable enough to tote on a road trip, robust enough to survive a coffee-stained jacket, and more reliable than vinyl for a lot of listeners. Deluxe editions add the tactile value that streaming cannot replicate: a booklet with lyrics and album art that begs to be left unfolded on a coffee table, a console for your fancy headphones, a story that unfolds with each track.
