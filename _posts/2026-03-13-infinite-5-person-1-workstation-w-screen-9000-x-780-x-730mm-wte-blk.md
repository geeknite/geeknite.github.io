## Ergonomics and Comfort
Five humans around a single long surface is not your standard ergonomic setup, but the design makes the best of it. Each seat can be tuned for height, and the central screen is designed to be visible from multiple angles. If you have five people of varying heights, consider a monitor arm setup or a screen with a broad viewing angle. The key is to avoid the five-person 'eyes on the back of your head' syndrome that sometimes happens when people realize they’re not the sole focus.

Your chairs should be ergonomic enough to handle long sessions without turning into a post-apocalyptic couch. The desk height is adjustable on some configurations; if not, a set of adjustable chair heights and a small anti-fatigue mat can do wonders for posture. The surface is expansive enough to accommodate the standard office toolkit: keyboards, mice, notebooks, chargers, and a suspiciously large plant that keeps migrating toward the keyboard.

A central control hub is optional but recommended: think of a shared docking station or a dedicated keyboard tray to reduce cross-pollination of cables. The aim is not to create a five-person sausage, but to optimize collaboration without people stepping on each other’s toes or each other’s keyboards.

We also tested headset comfort and microphone reach; given the number of people involved, you will likely need a few long cables or wireless solutions to avoid the central mic chasing each occupant around like a curious golden retriever. In practice, a little planning and a few rehearsal sessions will help you determine the best seating rotation for the day: rotate after every design critique or after someone says the phrase synergy three times.
