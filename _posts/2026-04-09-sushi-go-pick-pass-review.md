## How to play in a nutshell

If you have played Sushi Go before, the mechanics will feel familiar. If you haven’t, you are about to discover one of the most joyful, bite sized drafting experiences in family gaming.

- Setup: Shuffle the deck and deal a hand of cards to each player. The number of cards depends on the player count, but the core idea is that everyone has a little buffet of choices in front of them.
- Turn sequence: Each player selects one card from their hand and places it face down in front of them. Then everyone reveals their chosen cards simultaneously. After revealing, everyone passes the remaining cards to the left (or right, depending on the round) and you repeat with the new hand.
- Scoring: At the end of each round, you tally points based on the sushi combos you collected. Nigiri cards provide fixed points, maki stacks provide points for the most in a round, and pudding cards provide end game bonuses.
- Endgame: After all rounds, you total the points and declare the culinary champion of your table. The hero card is the one who can assemble a sashimi of combos without messing up the flow.

The architecture is simple to grasp, and the real joy comes from the social interaction—watching the person next to you make a run for a particular maki card while you pivot to a different plan, all while trying not to disrupt the delicate sushi economy of the table.

Given its streamlined nature, the game shines with 2–4 players, and it remains a quick and breezy experience with 5 players if you must. The exact running time is kid-friendly: about 15–20 minutes per round, depending on your group’s enthusiasm, table talk, and how much you insist on letting the kids narrate the entire meal as if they are hosting a food show on a busy street corner.

A typical session rewards players who balance table talk with attention to what others are collecting.
