## What is Sushi Go! and why should you care?
Sushi Go! is a card-drafting game where players pass a hand of sushi cards around the table, choosing one card to keep and then passing the rest to the next player. The goal is to assemble the most delicious combination of dishes to score the most points by the end of three rounds. It’s fast, it’s thematic, and it rewards a certain level of “game-face” without demanding a PhD in calculus.

## How to play: a quick overview of the pick and pass mechanic
The heart of Sushi Go! lies in the pick-and-pass mechanic. On each round, players receive a hand of cards (usually seven per round, depending on player count). Each player picks one card to keep, then passes the remaining cards to the left. You repeat this until all cards are claimed. After three rounds (in the standard game), you tally up points based on the specific dishes you collected.

Here’s the flow in bite-sized detail:

- You look at your current hand and choose one card that you think will maximize your final score without giving your rivals the exact card you want next round.
- You place the chosen card face down on your personal display and pass the rest to the next player. The passing direction can vary between rounds, keeping everyone on their toes and preventing a single player from controlling the wheel of destiny for too long.
- When all cards in the hand are claimed, you score for that round according to each dish’s scoring rules, then you refresh the deck and start the next round.

The scoring rules are a delightful jumble of small sub-games within the main game. Some dishes reward you for collecting sets (like Sashimi or Dumplings), while others reward you for quantity (Maki stacks) or a bit of luck (Wasabi triples your point value for Nigiri). The result is a tapestry of tiny decisions that feel substantial without overthinking. It’s a game that rewards memory and composure over brute force.

To give you an example: If you manage to collect three Maki rolls in a round (three different Maki cards), you’ll gain a certain number of points, but if another player snags two of the same Maki cards, they might surpass you in a different configuration. It’s a little dance of risk and reward—like gambling with tiny edible chips, except the chips are adorable and non-destructive to your furniture.

## Strategy: when to go bold and when to go safe
Sushi Go! isn’t a heavy strategy game, but it does reward you for thinking ahead. Here are some practical tips to improve your results without turning the table into a spreadsheet:

- Know the card flow: A big part of the game is predicting what cards are likely to come back around. If you’re position one in a round, you might want to pick a card that denies a strong near-term option to the player on your left while still setting up a reasonable future pick for yourself.
- Collect sets strategically: Dumplings scale with the number you collect in a round, so sometimes it’s worth taking a single Dumpling early if you suspect you’ll be blocked on later rounds. Other times, it’s better to grab a Tempura or a Sashimi to secure a guaranteed point surge later in the round.
- Watch the table: Because players pass cards, your choices are as much about what others are likely to pick as what you want to keep. A few rounds in, you’ll start reading your opponents like a sushi menu with a side of poker face.
- Balance speed with precision: In a three-round game, you can sprint the first two rounds and tighten up your strategy in the final round. The sweet spot is learning when to sprint for scoring opportunities and when to nudge a rival out of a better combo.

If you’re playing with kids or newcomers, a lot of the strategy reveals itself naturally through repeated play. The games remain quick and breezy enough that it’s easy to explain and recover from a misstep without turning the table into a therapist’s couch. For more seasoned players, you’ll still find moments of satisfaction as you optimize your choices and push the table toward a satisfying finish.

## Scoring primer: what actually earns you points
The scoring in Sushi Go! is delightfully modular. Here’s a quick cheat sheet to keep you oriented during those tense “do I pick this now or hope it comes back” moments:

- Nigiri: Each Nigiri card is worth a base amount of points (usually small values), but Wasabi can multiply those points if you play a Nigiri on it later. Wasabi is a classic example of “if you hold this too long, you miss the moment; if you use it too early, you lose the multiplication potential.”
- Maki: The player with the most Maki rolls ends up with bonus points, and the second-most gets a smaller bonus. The numbers vary slightly by edition, but the concept remains the same: stack Maki and race to your personal finish line.
- Tempura and Sashimi: These are “collect X of Y” type bonuses. Tempura rewards you for two Tempura cards, while Sashimi rewards you for three Sashimi cards. It’s all about securing the right fractions of sets to maximize your end-game payoff.
- Dumplings: Sets escalate in value the more Dumplings you collect. It’s a simple but satisfying tension: fill a Dumpling suit to claim bigger rewards.
- Rolls and other bonuses: There are a few other cards that offer varied scoring, sometimes based on how many you have at the end of the round. The rules stay tight, but the flavor—delicious punning and bright icons—keeps things engaging.

As you can see, Sushi Go! rewards a combination of planning and opportunistic grabs. It’s not about “getting the most powerful card” every turn; it’s about curating a portfolio of dishes that will, collectively, maximize your end-of-round total across three rounds. It’s a clever balancing act that feels fair and satisfying, even when you’re suddenly sabotaged by the person who always ends up with the good two-card combos. If you enjoy a little math-y satisfaction without a heavy brain-tax, you’ll find this scoring system just right.
