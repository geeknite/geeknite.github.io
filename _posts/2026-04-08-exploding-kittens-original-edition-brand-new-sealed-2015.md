# Exploding Kittens Original Edition Review: Brand New Sealed Cat Chaos

In 2015 the internet did a strange thing. It introduced a card game with cats, explosions, and a lot of mock drama. The result was Exploding Kittens, a micro sensation that somehow found a home on every family table, dorm room, and late night dungeon crawl prep table. The post takes a deep dive into the Original Edition released in 2015, which ships in a brand new sealed box, perfect for nostalgia collectors and those who want to pretend to be a modern product tester.

The art by The Oatmeal is everywhere you look on the Internet, yet its appearance on a physical card deck still manages to feel playful and fresh. ## What is Exploding Kittens Original Edition

Exploding Kittens is a push your luck card game designed to be light in complexity but heavy in personality. In the Original Edition, you are not playing for points as much as you are avoiding catastrophe. Each hand is a mini drama in which you try to outsmart your friends and not draw the dreaded Exploding Kitten card. If you draw that card, you lose a life unless you have a Defuse card that can save you from disaster. The twist is that the Defuse cards come in handy not just to save yourself but to mess with the others. You can shuffle, peek, or preemptively unleash a catastrophe on your neighbor with a well placed See the Future card.

The kit is designed for 2 to 5 players, with 6 players possible using a two on two rule or passing the card deck around with the appropriate house rules. The Original Edition was the first major print run that made the game feel bright, zany, and not too serious. It took a concept that could have lived as a meme and turned it into a social experience that people want to re-create again and again.

### What's in the box

The 2015 Original Edition includes:

- A deck of 56 cards with bright, cartoon style art by The Oatmeal
- A nice, sturdy rulebook that feels almost like a meditation on cat chaos
- A few cardboard tokens for life tracking and card penalties

In total, you will get enough components for a handful of rounds designed to snap you into a party mood faster than a cat can knock over a glass of water. The nice thing about this edition is that the components are simple and robust; the cards are thick enough to survive a few enthusiastic shuffles and kid friendly enough to survive the occasional bend.

If you are a collector, you may be curious about the 2015 edition while you also seek out later expansions. The core experience, however, remains the same: you draw a card, you hope you do not draw an Exploding Kitten, and you try to use defense cards to survive the turn.

## How to play the Original Edition

### Setup and basic flow

To begin, shuffle the deck and deal a starting hand to each player. The exact number of cards dealt depends on the house rules, but a typical start is 2 to 3 cards per player. The remaining cards form the draw pile. On your turn, you take a card, then you may play any number of action cards from your hand to alter the course of the round. Then you end your turn and draw a single card if you have not drawn an Exploding Kitten already.

The core objective is simple: avoid drawing the Exploding Kitten card. If you draw one without a Defuse, you are out of the round. If you do have a Defuse card, you can use it to defuse the kitten and continue the chaos.

### Card types you will encounter

- Exploding Kitten cards
- Defuse cards
- Cat cards
- Attack, See the Future, Shuffle, and Nope cards

Defuse cards are powerful as they allow you to avoid losing a life. See the Future cards let you peek at the top few cards of the deck so you can plan your next move. Attack cards may force the next player to take an additional turn, while Nope cards cancel other players' actions. The combination of these cards creates a dynamic that is not about chess-level strategy but about reading the room and seizing the moment when your opponents hesitated.

### A quick play-through example

Imagine four players: A, B, C, and D. A draws an even mix of cards and tries to set up a scenario where B will inevitably draw a kitten. B uses a See the Future card to peek at the top three cards, and discovers that one of them is a Defuse. B decides to rearrange the top of the deck with a Shuffle card, hoping to move the kitten under a pile of harmless cards. D, feeling bold, drops an Attack to force C to take two turns in a row. By the time the round ends, another player is dangerously close to hitting an Exploding Kitten, and the table erupts in hysterical banter as Cat cards are played to tease, taunt, and occasionally defend the fragile psyche of a friend who insists that their luck is better than it seems.

## The humor and art in Exploding Kittens

The art by The Oatmeal is bright, bold, and retro-internet pretty. It has a vibe that feels like a mashup of a doodle on a notebook and a glossy trading card. The humor is cat-centric, with jokes that range from silly to quirky, and sometimes a little bit absurd. The humor is not always PG, but in a family friendly edition that is marketed toward kids aged seven and up, the jokes ride a fine line. For a younger audience, this requires a bit of parental guidance to avoid the more adult lingo or references that might appear in some later expansions.

## Age suitability and family play

### For a kid aged 7 and up

Exploding Kittens is marketed as family friendly and kid safe. The core mechanic is simple enough for a seven year old to grasp with one or two adults offering guidance. The concept of a popped kitten is a little dark, but in the context of a cartoonish style it becomes more silly than scary. The real issue is that the pace can be frantic, and the table can become quite chaotic if a group gets into a rhythm of rapid card playing and aggressive taunting. If you are a parent or guardian, you might want to set a few house rules at the start. For example, you can declare that cat puns are mandatory but personal insults are not allowed, or you can require that players read and explain each card text before playing it.

### Expansions and reprints worth considering

There are expansions and versions that add more depth to the core game. The art remains consistent with the original spirit, but you can increase player counts or alter the pace of the game with expansions. If you are new to Exploding Kittens, starting with the Original Edition makes sense to understand the core humor and core mechanics.

## Pros and cons

Pros

- Fast and chaotic; easy to pick up
- Laugh out loud humor that is accessible to a wide range of ages
- Clear and visually appealing art
- A nice balance of luck and small strategy
- Great value for party nights

Cons

- The chaos can become overwhelming for some players
- Not a deep strategic experience, which some players want
- May be less appealing to players who are not fans of cat jokes
- Card text can be a bit long and heavy for younger players without help

## How it sits within the bigger ecosystem

Exploding Kittens Original Edition is the gateway product for a brand that built a marketing machine around a simple concept. Over time expansions added more depth to the deck and more ways to wreck the game, which is both a blessing and a curse. If you want a deeper strategy or a longer experience, you might look elsewhere. If you want a quick, silly, group activity that evokes lively conversation and a sense of camaraderie, this is a great entry point.

Compared to other party games in the same vein, Exploding Kittens stands out because it is both self aware and highly approachable. It shares a similar energy with games like Uno or Fluxx in terms of ease of play, while still offering a more humorous and sometimes cartoonish flavor.

## Final recommendation

Overall, the Exploding Kittens Original Edition is a fast, funny, and wonderfully silly way to bring chaos into a game night. It is not deep in strategy, but depth is not the point here. The heart of it lies in the playful banter, the excellent art, and the way a simple misfortune like a kitten explosion can bond a table into a chorus of giggles. If you are looking for a light, social, and silly experience to share with family or a group of friends who enjoy memes and cartoon cats, this is a strong pick.

If the box is sealed and you value nostalgia, you will enjoy unsealing this relic and letting the cat chaos begin again. If you want a more strategic or longer experience, look at spins or expansions that extend the play time and bring in new mechanics. The core game remains approachable and casual, and that is its strongest trait.
