## Card specifics
### Condition NM and why it matters
Near Mint is a market term that means you can barely tell the card has existed outside a sleeve. A NM Gloom should have perfect corners, sharp edges, flat surface, and no printing flaws you can spot from arm's length with your face pressed to the card. We'll discuss typical defects to watch for: whitening at the edges, surface scratches, print lines, and centering issues. The Japanese print style often features crisp borders and deeper color saturation, which can make defects easier to spot but also makes the card pop in a way English print sometimes cannot.

### Set context and rarity
Shiny Treasure ex SV4a is the subset that loves to glow when the lights hit it just so. The S signature on the card stands for a special foil pattern that reflects light in a way that can only be described as a polite disco ball. This Gloom, wearing a Shiny Holo, belongs to a lineage of collectible runs that are sought after by both modern collectors and long time fans who remember the era when shininess was the ultimate punctuation mark.

## Print quality and condition notes
### Print lines, centering, and edge wear
Many NM cards in this subset carry a very fine micro-scratch on the holo layer that only appears under direct light. That is not a defect in the sense of a bad print, but a feature that sometimes catches the eye of the person who wants their Gloom to appear to be mid thunderstorm on a glossy lake. Check for uniform centering; a slightly off-center card can reduce market value by a portion, but in a holo like this, many collectors focus more on color saturation and holo coverage than perfect centering.

### Back and weight
The back of a Gloom is uniform, with a crisp echo of the set logo. If the back shows a fingerprint or a scuff line, that might indicate handling damage rather than manufacturing defect. This card should feel solid in the hand—no bending, no creases, no warping, and a back that shows consistent blue hues and the PTCG logo crisp as day.

## Market notes and value
### Grading and price ranges
The NM label does not magically unlock a treasure chest; it simply denotes that the card is in excellent condition for collectors. In the current market, the price of a NM Japanese Gloom 192/190 Shiny Treasure ex SV4a Shiny Holo can vary based on demand, the presence of the Hakuna Matata glare in the holo, and whether the card has population count peers. Expect a spectrum of prices from affordable to aspirational, with the true value often being driven by the holo's shimmer and the collector's desire for a near pristine example to top their display shelf.

As a Gloom card from a vintage era, this particular print is not likely to see standard tournament play in 2026. Its value comes from collection and display rather than deck viability. If you are a player who loves the nostalgia factor, consider a non holo reprint or reissued version for play, while reserving this shiny jewel for the cabinet.

## Comparisons and context
### English vs Japanese prints
Japanese print quality tends to emphasize color depth and border sharpness, while English copies may have different holo patterns and slightly different font typography. The difference in printing often gives Japanese NM examples a slightly richer field under light, which can translate to higher observed prices in some markets. If you collect across languages, this Gloom will sit nicely in a glass-front cabinet with a small halo of light.

### Other Gloom variants
Other Gloom variants—non holo, different sets, and alternate art versions—each carry their own story and value dynamics. This particular holo shines with a crisp color palette and the rare Shiny Treasure ex line, which gives it a wow factor that is hard to ignore on a well-lit shelf.

## How to store and display
### Sleeves and top-loaders
For a NM plush of this caliber, a high-quality semi-rigid top-loader with an inner sleeve is ideal. A semi-rigid holder helps prevent edge wear and keeps the mosaic holo from getting bent in transit. Consider double-sleeving for long-term storage if you plan to show it or move it frequently.

### Display setups
LED display cases that shine perpendicular to the card surface will catch the holo's glimmer and reflect a spectrum across your room. If you want to create a focal point, position it with a small light to highlight the thin border lines and the card's green hue, which matches well with dark wood shelves.

### Display text and signage
A small display plaque with the card's set name, rarity, and card number can enhance the viewing experience for guests who are curious about the Gloom line. A little context goes a long way to making the card feel less like a pretty object and more like a story told by light.

## Where to find this card in the wild
### Online marketplaces
Trading platforms can have this variant at varying prices depending on the time of day and how many collectors woke up on a Sunday with a coffee. If you want to avoid scams, search for authentic listing descriptions that mention the card number, set, holo type, and condition. Look for clear photos of the holo, the border, and the back.

### Local game stores and conventions
If you prefer to inspect the card in person, your local game store or a convention booth can be a good place to scan its surface with your own light. Bring a loupe if you want to be fancy and to confirm there are no hidden misprints that destroy the card’s glow.
