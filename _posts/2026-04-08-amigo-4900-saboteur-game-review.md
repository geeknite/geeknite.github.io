## What is Saboteur?
Saboteur is not a treasure map; it’s a social deduction card game wrapped in a dwarven twist. Players take on the roles of dwarves digging for gold in a network of tunnels. Some players are honest gold diggers; others are Saboteurs trying to derail the path to treasure. The goal is straightforward but the deception is delicious: be the team that reaches the treasure first, even if your own identity remains a secret until the right moment. The Amigo 4900 edition in the UK sticks to the core, with sturdy cards, a compact play surface, and a rulebook that politely whispers you’ll figure it out, captain.

## Components and Setup
- A deck of cards: roles, path building, action cards, and treasure cards.
- Gold tokens or treasure cards: the shiny goal you’re either protecting or plundering.
- Player tokens to mark roles and round status.
- A compact rulebook that somehow manages to be both concise and mischievous in tone.

Setup is brisk: deal each player two role cards (one real, one potentially fake), then hand out four path cards to each player. The rest is shuffled into a draw pile. You’ll assign starting points, determine the first player, and you’re ready to roll. In a tight 3- to 5-player game, the round whizzes by in about 15-20 minutes. With 6-10 players, you’ll likely want to pour a second cup of tea and prepare for a chorus of whispers, conspiratorial glances, and the occasional dramatic reveal.

### How a Turn Unfolds
Each turn you can do a few things, depending on what’s in your hand:
- Play a path card to extend the tunnel network toward the treasure.
- Use an action card to help your allies or hinder your enemies (think blocks, swaps, or reveals).
- Draw from the deck to replenish your hand.
The round ends when a path card connects to a treasure card, or when the pile runs dry. Then players reveal their roles, calculate who was actually digging versus sabotaging, and tally the treasure. The interplay between hidden roles and visible path-building creates moments that are equal parts tactical and theatrical.

## Roles, Bluffing, and Player Interaction
Saboteur nails the “hidden identity” mechanic without turning into a soap opera. You might be a steadfast miner one round and a crafty Saboteur the next—perhaps both, if your role cards allow it in a sneaky variant. The key is to read the table: who’s blocking the obvious route? Who’s laying down path cards that seem to help the majority, but actually funnel everyone away from the treasure?

For families, the humor is essential. Kids lean into the theatrics: whispering, pointing at suspicious players, and savoring the moment when a seemingly innocent path card unleashes a glorious chain of unintended consequences. For adults, Saboteur offers a delightful check-in with social dynamics: who’s overplaying their hand? Who’s silently sabotaging in ways that nobody notices until the round ends? The UK edition does a fantastic job of balancing light-hearted fun with enough tension to keep every player invested.

### Strategy for Diggers vs Saboteurs
- If you’re a Digger: play to create flexible routes. Don’t lock yourself into a single tunnel; you’ll be the one blamed when the Saboteurs cleverly block your favorite path.
- If you’re a Saboteur: sow doubt early, but don’t be overly obvious. A well-timed block or misdirection can derail a whole tunnel worth of treasure.
- Remember to exploit group dynamics: a large group means more players to misdirect, but it also means more potential allies who will pretend to be Saboteurs for a round to confuse others.

The beauty of Saboteur is that it rewards social acuity more than brute card counting. It’s not about memorizing a long rule set; it’s about reading the room and making a single, decisive move that shifts the balance of the round.
