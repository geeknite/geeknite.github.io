## The Guest: Edouard Guiton and the Art of Making Zombies Look Like People You Could Sacrifice a Third Cup of Coffee For
### Who is Edouard Guiton?
Edouard Guiton is the kind of designer who makes you squint at a zombie and think, I might invite that one to dinner. He’s known for sculpting minis that balance character and menace, a tricky equilibrium because you want your zombie to be threatening but not so scary you can’t bring them to family game night. In this Zombicide line, his stamp is unmistakable: elongated limbs with a touch of elegance, a hint of Victorian menace, and faces that manage to nestle between “I could buddy-cop with you” and “I will absolutely steal your last slice of pizza.” The result is miniatures that feel alive (pun intended), with shading that makes you wonder if the painter used actual physics to coax the light to do its job.

### What this Special Guest brings to the table
What makes Edouard’s contribution feel special isn’t just the textures or the dynamic poses—though those are welcome. It’s how his sculpts introduce personality into a typically anonymous mob of walkers and runners. You’ll find survivors with a swagger (even when armed with a makeshift bat), and zombies with a predatory elegance that catches the eye even before you’ve rolled a single dice. The line benefits from his ability to push the silhouettes just enough to differentiate a horde unit, which is essential for tabletop readability in the heat of a late-night zombie brawl. In short, Edouard’s minis don’t just fill bases; they tell tiny stories with every brushstroke.

### Design philosophy meets table-readability
The real magic here is how Edouard’s style translates to practical gameplay. In a game where a single misread silhouette can turn a tense moment into a comedy of errors, his work nudges the eye toward functional clarity. Survivors carry a swagger that hints at their backstory without needing a paragraph card on the table. Zombies pair menace with elegance, so you’re never quite sure if a walker is a slow threat or a cunning one—which is precisely what you want in a skirmish where speed and placement win the day.

## Unboxing and Component Analysis
### Box Content and Quality
This edition comes in a sturdy box, the sort that telegraphs premium without needing to go full luxury package. Inside, you’ll find several sprues featuring the Edouard Guiton crew along with standard Zombicide survivors and walkers. ### Cast Quality and Layering
The miniatures show a bit more raised detail than your average base set, which helps painting beginners with the dry-brush technique and veteran painters who like to chase perfect gradients. You’ll notice subtle feathering on cloaks, fine belt buckles catching the light, and a few decorative pouches that suggest storytelling as much as they do utility. If you enjoy the ritual of painting minis, this set gives you a lot of surface to practice on without crossing into the realm of “Too much detail for a board game.” It’s not Razor-Edge Glaive levels of complexity, but it happily toe-to-toe with the fine line between “paint-ready” and “demanding, but worth it.”

### Size, Scale, and Compatibility
Standard Zombicide scale shines here. The Edouard-signed crew aligns with your existing survivors and walkers without needing conversion chaos. If you’ve built a horde with the Black Plague or Green Horde lines, these miniatures slot right in. The bases are slightly larger in places due to dynamic posing, but not so much that you’ll feel like you need to re-base your whole collection. This is good news for hobby time rather than a weekend engineering project.

## Painting and Display Ideas
### Quick-Paint Walkthrough
If you’re chasing a quick but effective finish, start with a matte primer, then a shaded base coat to bring out the sculpt lines. The details on Edouard’s minis are forgiving enough for a beginner to achieve a satisfying look with standard craft paints. A two-tone leather belt or a weathered cloak can become a focal point without stealing the show from the rest of the model. If you want to push it, a subtle edge highlight on raised edges and a careful wash in the recesses will make the textures read well on the table. If you’re into more serious painting, you’ll find the faces particularly engaging, with tiny features that can be emphasized with careful glazing and a steady hand.

### Tabletop Aesthetics and Storytelling
These minis invite storytelling. When you place a survivor next to an Edouard mini with a swaggering pose, you don’t just see a square in a grid—you see a character moment. A survivor who has survived three waves might be standing taller, while a zombie that’s clearly too elegant to be a mindless pathfinder could be a mini-plot hook for your next scenario. The overall design invites you to tell a little story with each match—something we geeks do when we’re not counting how many times a dog-eared card has shuffled into oblivion.

## Painting and Display Tips (for the ambitious)
- Start with a mid-tone base on skin tones to avoid flatness; a subtle blue wash in the recesses adds depth without muddying the contrasts.
- Use a fine brush to pick out metallics on buckles and weaponry; small reflective spots sell the realism without going overboard.
- Consider a light dry-brush on cloaks with a gray or tan to avoid muddy textures and keep the character lines apparent on the table.
- For basing, add a touch of grit and tiny pebbles that echo the late-night urban ruin vibe you expect from a Zombicide encounter.

### Display Ideas and Shelf Talkers
