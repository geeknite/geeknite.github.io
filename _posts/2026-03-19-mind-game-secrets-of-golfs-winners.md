Golf pros frequently enter flow states during a round when every action feels automatic yet purposefully directed. How do they do it? The cognitive load theory provides a map:\n- Reduce extraneous cognitive load by eliminating unnecessary thoughts and distractions.\n- Increase intrinsic loads in a controlled way so skill learning remains anchored in the task.\n- Use external supports (rhythms, cues, and routines) as scaffolding to keep the brain in a flow-friendly zone.\n\nIn practice, this means things like:\n- A consistent pre-shot routine to cue the brain that it’s go time.\n- A simple, repeatable breath pattern to regulate arousal and attention.\n- A deliberate post-shot evaluation to learn without spiraling into self-critique.\n\nIf you want more on flow in sports, we recommend this accessible overview: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flow_(psychology).\n\n### Cracking the code: cognitive load on the course\nWhen your brain is juggling wind, slope, and the memory of that last missed putt, the risk of overload goes up. Do this for 60 seconds before your tee shot, and again after bad shots to recalibrate.\n- Visualization walk-through: On the practice green, visualize a target line seven feet in front of you. Step by step, rehearse the exact swing path, contact, and ball flight in your head, then execute with a clean swing.\n- Progressive exposure to pressure: Create a mini-challenge on the practice range—simulate a tournament pin position, but with your own set of consequences (e.g., a small bragging-right bet with yourself). The goal is to normalize the feeling of pressure and respond with calm, not panic.\n- Focus anchors: Pick a tiny cue (a breath, a foot pressure, a gaze point) and return to it whenever your attention wanders.
