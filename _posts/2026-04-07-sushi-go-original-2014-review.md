What Sushi Go! is (at a glance)
Sushi Go! is a quick, zippy drafting game. Each round you will pass a hand of cards around the table while choosing exactly one card from your current hand and then passing the rest on to the next player. The process repeats until all cards are played. The objective is to assemble the best combination of sushi dishes to maximize your points. It is level 1 friendly and level 10 satisfying, which is a sweet spot for families who want something that is both approachable and a little strategic.

How to play: the quick primer (with how it feels in real life)
Setup
- Shuffle the deck and deal a hand to each player. The number of cards per hand changes with the player count, but the principle is simple: everyone starts with a fresh set of options, and no one knows what their neighbor will take next.
- Place the remaining deck in the center as the draw pile for future rounds.
- Each player keeps track of their collected cards and prepares to draft them as the rounds begin.

Turn structure
- In each round, all players pick one card from their hand, then pass the rest to their neighbor. This happens simultaneously; there is always a moment of suspense when you reveal what you kept and what your neighbor took just before you pass again.
- After everyone reveals their chosen card, the round continues to the next card in the hand, and the passing continues until all cards from the hand are exhausted.
- The chosen cards go into each player’s personal tableau for scoring at the end of the round.

Scoring and end of round
- After all cards in a round are played, you tally points for the sushi you collected that round. The core pieces are simple: some cards give direct points, others give set bonuses or multiplier effects with Wasabi.
- The Wasabi card is a little sneaky meta-tactic: if you place a Nigiri card on a Wasabi and later pick up a Nigiri on that Wasabi, that Nigiri’s value is boosted. It rewards careful planning more than pure luck.
- End of round, you discard the used cards and prepare for the next hand. The rounds continue until the deck is exhausted, and you have built up a small stack of points from each round.

End of game and Puddlings (Puddings)
- The endgame scoring for Puddings adds a little sprinkle of strategic tension. Puddlings tend to be worth a set amount of points depending on your standing; the most Pudds get a larger payoff and the second most gets a smaller payoff. It provides a reason to sometimes chase Puddding cards instead of purely chasing Nigiri or Maki.
- When all rounds are finished, you tally final scores, including Puddings. The player with the highest total score is the sashimi-skip to victory message rolling in your head like a drum roll before a reveal.

Eating the 2014 edition: experiences from a used copy
- Pace and tempo: The game is designed to run in under 20 minutes with 2 to 5 players. The pace is brisk, with table chatter that ranges from light banter to strategic persuasion that your neighbor should not take the Dumpling you want next. It is the kind of game where you can keep a witty one-liner in your pocket for the right moment.
- Teachable in minutes: The rules are small enough to explain during a quick intro. This makes Sushi Go a perfect gateway game for families or groups with mixed gaming experience. It rewards observation and planning but never punishes the uninitiated with heavy math or months of rules to memorize.
- Replayability: While the concept is simple, the card combos create a surprising variety of potential scores. You will develop favorite combos and learn to predict what your friends are chasing. It leads to moments of delightful mischief, especially when you see a friend commit to a set that you can disrupt just by choosing a different card.

Tasting notes for different player counts
- 2 players: The dynamics tighten. You get a clearer sense of your partner’s or your rival’s preferred cards because there are fewer hands to pass around the table. It becomes a mental tug of war with a friendly face. The pacing remains quick, and you still get to enjoy the Wasabi trick and the occasional double-dip on Nigiri.
- 3-4 players: The middle ground of chaos and predictability. You’ll see more variability in what you can claim, but you’ll also spot more opportunities to exploit or defend against others’ plans. This is where the table chatter can run hot and the humor can flow freely.
- 5 players: The most social and chaotic scenario. Drafting becomes a little more of a social sport; you will cheer, groan, and sometimes outbid your own previous choices by accident. It’s the most dynamic mode for maximizing laughs and the most intense mode for near misses on a crucial card.

Strategy tips for the curious optimist
- Learn to read intentions: The best players in Sushi Go! are those who can infer what their neighbors are likely to take next. You’ll watch for patterns and take calculated risks when the payoff looks juicy.
- Don’t chase single cards: It’s easy to get fixated on a high-value piece, but sometimes a balanced spread of several midrange cards yields more overall points. This is a lesson in patience and pattern recognition.
- Use Wasabi strategically: The Wasabi trick is a classic power move. Early placement of Wasabi with a future Nigiri can yield disproportionate rewards for those who time it correctly.
- Endgame awareness: Puddlings and Maki count can swing final scores. Keep an eye on your tablemates’ potential Puddding counts and place a strategic emphasis on Maki cards if you suspect a close race for the top spots.

Practical considerations for a used copy
- Box wear: A little scuffing on the box is normal and doesn’t affect gameplay. If you find a corner ding that makes the lid not close, you can usually fix it with a light press or a soft insert. The key is readability and accessibility, not pristine packaging.
- Card wear: A used deck with a few edge nicks is not a showstopper. If the color or icons are obscured in places, you’ll want to test with a few rounds to ensure readability. If the backs are patently inconsistent, that’s a bigger concern.
- Rule sheet: Ideally, you want a legible rule sheet. If it’s worn, you can still navigate the core concepts by referencing the internet or a quick cheat-sheet you print from a reputable source. It still plays the same, just with fewer tears in your eyes when you explain the rules to a first-time player.
