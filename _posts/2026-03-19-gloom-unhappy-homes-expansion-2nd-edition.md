## Introduction

Gloom is one of those games you bring to a table and immediately declare the theme before anyone even shuffles: tragedy, misfortune, and a soundtrack of moans from the mentally exhausted players across the room. It’s a game that rewards storytelling more than precision, where the unit of measure for victory is not points but the dramatic descent of your family into worse and worse fates. So, naturally, Atlas Games released Unhappy Homes Expansion 2nd Edition, because what the base game needed was more gloomy houses, more despair, and apparently a few extra cards to remind you that your relatives are more likely to die of comedic misfortune than old age.

This review will dig into whether the Unhappy Homes expansion lives up to the gloomy hype, what it adds (and what it doesn’t), and whether the price of extra sorrow is worth it. If you’re hunting for a game that makes your lobby of friends laugh while crying on the inside, you’ve found your spiritual successor to rainy Tuesdays and awkward family reunions. And yes, we’ll keep things spoiler-light for new players, because the joy of Gloom is often in the reveal—so we’ll save some surprises for your own nightmare-filled table.

## What’s in the Box?

The Unhappy Homes Expansion 2nd Edition slides into the existing Gloom ecosystem with the same sense of stylish despair you’d expect from a Victorian mourning diary. The box typically includes a fresh mix of cards that slide right into the core game’s reuse-friendly design. Expect more Family cards, more House cards, and a handful of new Story and Event cards that bend the rules toward more chaotic storytelling instead of straightforward doom-mining.

- New House cards that tilt the board toward “home” life and its tasteful misery.
- Additional Family cards that create wilder family trees and more dramatic generational curses.
- Story and Event cards that nudge the table toward dramatic reveals at the worst possible times.
- A few updated rule clarifications for 2nd Edition, because nothing says “comfort” like a precisely defined rule about how a pillow might be cursed to float away during a funeral scene.
- A couple of tokens and markers that feel like tiny, elegant guilt-trips you can flip over to remind everyone that life is fragile and your dice have opinions.

Artwork on these cards maintains Gloom’s signature sepia-toned, hand-drawn vibe with a sprinkling of macabre whimsy. It’s the kind of art that makes you want to wear black while you’re telling your spouse you’ll still love them even after their doomed character dies—spoken with a smile that says you’re only half joking.

## How the Expansion Changes the Game

The base Gloom experience is all about making your characters as tragically miserable as possible while watching the other players do the same with their own doomed families. Unhappy Homes expands that space by giving you more levers to pull when you want to paint your doom with a broader brush.

### Mechanics and Flow

- The expansion adds new Home and Family cards that interact with each other in interesting ways. Some Home cards make it easier to attach a new misfortune to a family member, while others push the doom sideways toward a neighbor’s house, because why not spread misery?
- Story cards in this set lean harder into social misfortune. Expect prompts like “The PTA meeting reveals a centuries-old curse” or “A family pet turns out to be a familiar rather than a pet.” These prompt cards are designed to spark storytelling and let you weave a narrative that’s as sad as it is entertaining.
- The 2nd Edition clarifications help avoid “rule friction” at the table. If your group has a tendency to argue about whether a curse counts as a permanent or temporary gloom, these tweaks are likely to reduce the grumbling while increasing the storytelling tempo.
- The new House cards introduce meta-choices. Do you want to load your family up with a third heartbreak or gamble on a big, splashy misfortune that might derail someone else’s plotline? It adds a bit more strategic depth to the otherwise frantic, narrative-fired engine of Gloom.

If you’ve played the base game a handful of times, you’ll notice how this expansion nudges you toward longer, more dramatic sessions. The playtime can stretch as you chase the ultimate dramatic reveal, or collapse into a quick, chaotic round if your group is hungry for speed and punchlines.

### Balance and Tone

Gloom’s mood is inherently mixed: you laugh at the misery you’re crafting while slowly accepting your own impending doom. Unhappy Homes preserves this tonal balance but leans into “home life gone wrong” as a richer theme. It’s not darker for darkness’ sake; it’s darker with a wink, a nod to the melodrama of families, and a little poke at the idea that “the perfect home” is only a silicon dream in a world built on cards and misfortune.

## Component Quality and Production Value

The box design continues to whisper elegance with a touch of gothic flair. Card stock remains sturdy, and the card art is crisp enough to read the bloom of a misfortune before it blooms into full-on tragedy. The new cards fit neatly into the existing sleeves if you’re one of those people who sleeved everything (you know who you are, and we love you for it). If you’re playing with a larger table, expect a bit more card shuffling, which becomes a comedic ballet of players leaning in to read tiny fortunes while their opponents silently draft the best way to ruin everything.

I did notice that, in a couple of games, a card or two shifted out of focus in the glare of the table lamp. That’s more a lighting quirk than a production flaw, but it’s worth noting if you’re the “edge-of-the-table light snob” type who cares about the exact hue of melancholy in each illustration.

## Play Experience: What it Feels Like at the Table

Gloom thrives on the storytelling round. The Unhappy Homes expansion nudges you toward more story-rich, home-centered narratives, which means more dramatic reveals, more family hierarchies, and more opportunities for your ailing aunt, your demon-possessed bookshelf, or the neighbor who never learned to mind their own business. It’s chaotic, it’s theatrical, and it’s surprisingly cooperative in a way that only a table full of scheming adults can appreciate.

### Solo vs. Group Play

Gloom is inherently a group activity. The expansion scales well with more players, giving you the chance to witness a chorus of shrieks as someone’s husband’s curse merges with a dog’s misfortune and a house’s structural weakness. Solo play is less common for this game, but if you’re into storytelling monologues to an audience of plush toys, the 2nd Edition still delivers the same vibes, just with fewer opportunities to point fingers at real people for ruining a perfectly good doom card.

### Pacing and Table Etiquette

With more cards in play, pacing can swing from “snappy, quick doom” to “epic saga of gloom” quite easily. If you’re playing with a group that loves long, dramatic sessions, this expansion is a dream come true. If you’re playing with folks who prefer tight, quick games, you might find yourselves devising new constraints to keep things tight and comedic.

## Artwork, Flavor, and Theme Consistency

The Unhappy Homes expansion keeps the Gloom flavor intact. If you’ve spent evenings savoring the way a single card’s illustration can prompt a chorus of “oohs” and “ahas” as you read the text aloud, you’ll be pleased with the consistency. The new scenes lean into the “home” concept with stylishly macabre humor—an old-hand family recipe that curses dinner guests, a cursed heirloom that turns into a traveling ghost story, and a living room that’s less “cozy” and more “haunted by the prior owner’s failed pot roast.” It’s the kind of tonal balance that makes the table feel like you’re part of a shared, slightly haunted, evening of storytelling rather than a cold numbers game.

## Replayability and Longevity

One of Gloom’s enduring strengths is that each round feels like a different mini-play. The Unhappy Homes expansion adds more layers to this, which means a longer shelf-life for your nights of despair. The new cards introduce fresh chain reactions and new ways to combine misfortunes, so you can rebuild your family’s tragedy in new and surprising ways without retreading exactly the same gloom path.

That’s not to say you’ll never see repeats—some cards will inevitably reappear, and the core goal remains consistent: accumulate the most tears, the most dramatic sighs, and the most spectacular family eulogies before that last card is drawn. But having additional variations keeps the experience from getting stale, which, in the end, is exactly what you want when you’re trying to orchestrate a two-hour theatrical tragedy.

## Thematic Cohesion and Narrative Quality

What makes Gloom special is not just the mechanics but the way players narrate the misery. The expansion leans into “home life” as a setting for misfortune, which yields some of the most entertaining and ridiculous setups you’ll witness at the gaming table. A typical round becomes a cooperative-ish competition of who can spin a more entertaining, more tragic, more melodramatic arc for their doomed family—while still trying not to cause a total collapse of the table’s social fabric in the process.

If you’re the type who enjoys reading aloud with theatrical gusto, this expansion provides you with more fuel for your campfire of gloom. If you’re shy about performing, there are still plenty of dramatic moments to be had by simply watching your friends volunteer to narrate the most grievous fate for Aunt Gertrude and her cursed teapot.

## Comparison to Base Game and Other Expansions

Compared to the base Gloom, Unhappy Homes adds a more explicit home-centric flavor. It’s less about random general misfortune and more about a home’s creeping influence on a family’s destiny. In that sense, it complements the base game by giving you more avenues for a single family’s descent rather than scattering misfortunes across multiple households.

When stacked against other Gloom expansions, Unhappy Homes stands out for its emphasis on narrative richness and the opportunity to craft extended stories around a single household. If you enjoy power-scaling character misfortune and you’re in the mood to watch a single family crash-and-burn with a more domestic vibe, this expansion earns its place on the shelf.

## Pros and Cons (Quick Take)

- Pros:
  - Richer storytelling potential with home-centered themes
  - Solid 2nd Edition rule clarifications that reduce table friction
  - Beautiful production values that stay faithful to Gloom’s aesthetic
  - Good value add for fans of the base game who want longer, more narrative sessions
- Cons:
  - Adds more cards and setup time, which may not be ideal for quick sessions
  - Some players may prefer more variety across households rather than a single heavy domestic doom arc
  - If you’re not into storytelling, the expansion can feel like unnecessary fanfare rather than essential gameplay

## Final Verdict

Unhappy Homes Expansion 2nd Edition for Gloom is a welcome addition if you’re a fan of the base game’s storytelling engine and you want more home-centric doom to spill onto the table. It doesn’t reinvent the wheel; instead, it expands it, giving you more scenarios, more opportunities for narrative hijinks, and more chances to outdo your friends with the most melodramatic lamentations. The 2nd Edition improvements are thoughtful and practical, reducing the typical frictions that can slow down a fast-paced storytelling game while still preserving the signature “one more card” atmosphere that makes Gloom so endearing.

If you’re a newcomer to the Gloom world, you might want to start with the base game to learn the rhythm and then graduate to the expansions when you’re ready for richer stories and longer evenings. For longtime players who want something that nudges you toward the drama of domestic misfortune without losing the game’s fast tempo, Unhappy Homes 2nd Edition hits the sweet spot with style.

If you’re ready to double down on doom and drama while keeping a sense of humor about it, this expansion is your gateway drug to a night of theatrical tragedy with friends who secretly love that you ruined their characters—and themselves—in the most ridiculous way possible.
