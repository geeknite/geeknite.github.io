How to Play: The Quick and the Quirky
=====================================

UNO’s rules are delightfully minimalistic, but the social outcome is often maximal. Here’s the clean version and then a few common house rules you’ll probably recognize.

Basic flow
----------
1. Each player is dealt seven cards. The rest becomes a draw pile; the top card is flipped to start the discard pile.
2. On your turn, match the top discard card by number, color, or symbol. If you can’t, draw a card from the draw pile. Your strategy options multiply dramatically with Wild cards in your pocket.
3. Special cards inject chaos:
   - Skip: Skips the next player.
   - Reverse: Reverses play direction.
   - Draw Two: Forces next player to draw two cards.
   - Wild: You pick the color to continue play.
   - Wild Draw Four: You also pick the color but force the next player to draw four cards. This one is controversial (and often the source of late-night “is this allowed?” debates).
4. When you’re about to run out, you must shout “UNO!” to indicate you have one card left. Forget this, and you may face a penalty in some groups—usually a playful dare or a quick extra draw.
5. The first player to discard all cards wins. In some groups, there’s a final trick: counting down the remaining cards to see who is truly the spotless winner and who is merely the most dramatic one among you.

House Rules You Might Encounter
--------------------------------
- Stacking Draw Two and Draw Four: Some groups allow stacking, which means a player can counter a Draw Two with another Draw Two or even a Wild Draw Four. This amplifies chaos and extends the game, which is both hilarious and potentially dramatic.
- No-Match Penalty: If you can’t legally play, you must draw one card and say something silly like, “I’m not great at math, but I’ll count to UNO.” It’s a small ritual that makes the losses feel like character-building experiences.
- UNO Challenge: A side rule where you only win if you call UNO at the right moment and survive the final round without a sudden wild turn.

Strategy: When to Play Your Wilds and When to Hold Them
======================================================
UNO isn’t chess, but there’s a surprising amount of micro-strategy in a quick 15-minute round. Here are a few tips to help you avoid being that person who draws seven cards while perplexingly insisting you were “just testing the luck of the draw.”

- Save Wild Draw Four for strategic moments: It’s tempting to blurt it out as soon as you see a color you don’t like, but the best players hold it for a moment of tactical farce: when a rival is one card away from winning, or when you’ve just dumped a color that makes life unbearable for your closest competitor.
- Use the color control wisely: Wild cards let you steer the deck toward a color you can exploit. If you know someone has a vulnerability to a certain color, steer the game there.
- Balance speed and safety: Sometimes the best move is to keep your hand diverse. A hand full of mixed colors and numbers is a better shield against explosive last turns than a single-card avalanche waiting to happen.
- Don’t bluff too aggressively: UNO’s charm is in its bluntness. If you try to bluff and you’re not confident, you’ll end up with face cards on the floor and a defeated sigh from your friend group.
