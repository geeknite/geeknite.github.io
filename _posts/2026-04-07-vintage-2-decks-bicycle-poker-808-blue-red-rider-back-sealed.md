## Sealed Condition: What It Means for Value and Longevity

Sealed vintage decks carry an extra layer of romance for collectors. The plastic wrap is a shield against fingerprints, moisture, and the judgment of bored nephews who think everything old is junk. But here’s a caveat for the careful historian: seals can also be fragile. The more time passes, the more the plastic wants to shrink wrap itself into a pretzel. If you actually plan to open and use these, expect a bit more resistance than modern, mass-produced decks. The upside is that you don’t have to worry about the cards drying out or the color rubbing off—the Rider Back inks tend to stay vibrant and legible, even after decades of storage in a humid hobbyist’s closet.

## A Buyer’s Guide: How to Inspect a Vintage Sealed Deck
- Inspect the tuck box for bends, creases, or moisture damage. A perfectly flat, pristine box is a good sign.
- Check the seal integrity. A tiny tear near the opening is usually okay; a seal that’s fractured all around is a warning flag.
- Look at the inside of the tuck for discoloration or signs of humidity. The inside should be evenly colored and crisp, with no staining.
- Examine the cards’ faces through the wrap if possible. In some cases, vendors will photograph the faces; if the faces show obvious wear, you’ll likely encounter similar wear once opened.
- Verify branding and typographic accuracy. The 808 designation is a detail that faithful collectors will want; if it’s missing or unclear, it might be a counterfeit or misprint.

If you’re buying for display, you’ll want to search for mint condition, factory sealed items. If you’re buying to use for performance, you may accept a slightly imperfect seal with confidence that you’ll still get a pleasing shuffle. The key is to know your goal and adjust expectations accordingly.
