## Color theory, readability, and the psychology of neon
Neon colors, particularly a loud yellow, have proven to be attention magnets by design. In a room full of neutral tones, neon stands out; it’s a beacon that guides the eye. This deck leverages that principle not to overwhelm but to create a visual anchor on a crowded table. The retina responds strongly to yellow-green wavelengths (the so-called “lime” or “chartreuse” spectrum), and this deck taps into that instinct, turning your card table into a small stage where your cards are the star performers.

From a practical standpoint, the neon yellow back makes it easier to center your focus on your opponents’ hands without losing track of your own cards. It’s a design win for players who want to keep their outs visible and their tells possibly a touch more ambiguous because the bright color can distract from subtle hand movements—though not too much, because the crisp black pips and stark white card faces remain legible.

Humorously, neon cards can become their own “mood ring” for the table. If you bring a neon deck to a game night, you’ll hear the jokes: “Is that a traffic signal on your hand?” or “Careful, that color might be illegal in some tables at the neon district.” And yet, the neon deck earns its stripes by simply delivering stage presence: it tells the table that you’re playing with enthusiasm and a little style.
