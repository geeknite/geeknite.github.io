## Ergonomics for Four: Seating, Monitors, and Everything Between
The human factors department had a good day when they designed this thing. Four independent work zones mean four distinct micro‑environments inside one large unit. Each station has space for a monitor or two, keyboard and mouse, and a personal set of desk accessories—think a notebook, a cup, and the occasional phantom stale tortilla chip that always resurfaces after a project kickoff. Height, depth, and reach considerations are well balanced. The surface depth is comfortable for most 24–32 inch monitors, and if you’re the kind who dabbles with large ultrawides, you’ll still find ample space for a multi‑device workflow without feeling like your elbows are in the wrong dimension.

If your team runs hot on privacy, the screens offer a buffer that reduces side‑eye glare and gives you a respectable privacy envelope during calls. It’s not a complete sound booth, but it’s a noticeable improvement over open‑plan chaos where your neighbor’s screen glare is your morning alarm clock.

One of the better ergonomic touches is the ability to adjust each station’s monitor height and angle with relative ease. This isn’t a gym membership for your posture, but it does a legit job of letting you set a neutral head position, roughly 20–25 degrees of neck flexion, and a clean line of sight to the center of your primary display. If you’re the kind who dreams of a desk setup where you can alternate between sitting and standing, you’ll appreciate the option to add a compatible height‑adjustable under‑desk solution without sacrificing your four‑person arrangement.

For keyboard comfort, there’s a modest amount of space at each station. It’s not a home‑office banquet table, but enough to accommodate a standard full‑size keyboard and a comfortable mouse, with room to spare for a mug and a snack so you don’t have to choose between caffeine and carbohydrates mid‑presentation. The arrangement also lends itself to proactive posture: you can lean into the work without the risk of elbow contact with a co‑worker’s keyboard, which is not something you can say about most open desks in the wild.


## Screen Integration and Tech: The “Screens” in Cornerstation W Screens
Yes, the product name suggests screens, and yes, those screens matter. The four screen modules are integrated into the workstation as part of the modular layout. Depending on the configuration, you can opt for four distinct displays or a shared screen estate that spans multiple workstations. The result is a flexible, collaboration‑friendly environment that supports brainstorming while keeping the team from morphing into a single, blurry blob of floating monitors.

Cable management is a central design feature here. The screens, power lines, and data cables nestle in the spine and under‑surface channels. If you go with passive screens or lighter setups, you’ll find that the desk behaves like a well‑trained starfighter—responsive, precise, and ready for a hyperspace shortcut to productivity.

With proper care, the Infinite cornerstation can be a workhorse for years. The oak veneer is fairly robust against everyday use, though like any wood finish, it benefits from the occasional wipe and the avoidance of direct sunlight to prevent long‑term fading. The metal components are generally corrosion‑resistant and designed for frequent assembly and disassembly, should your team’s evolving workspace require reconfiguration.

Cable channels stay relatively clean if you commit to a monthly cabling sweep—this is less about aesthetics and more about reducing the risk of overheating from power bricks and tangled cords during a deadline sprint.
