## OG Display Boxes: nostalgia with potential value
OG Display Boxes aren’t just packaging; they’re a time capsule. They can be a selling point for collectors or simply a reminder of how the hobby looked back when sleeves were thicker, and foils were a big deal. Here are a few things you should look for if your bulk includes OG boxes:

- Box condition: Look for crushed corners, bent lids, or crushed foam inserts. A box that still looks presentable can fetch more than a battered one.
- Branding and labeling: Original branding and stamps add value, especially if the box labels align with a specific early set release. Collectors often value these more for display than for play.
- Inset feel and protective materials: Some OG boxes include inserts or protective liners that can help protect the packs during transit. Keep an eye on whether these are present and intact—they’re a nice bonus when they survive a bulk purchase.

Even if the boxes aren’t pristine, they still bring nostalgia and can be included in a bundle for a collector who wants to display their collection with a touch of retro charm.

## Sorting and organizing bulk: a practical, workable plan
A well-structured approach turns a chaotic pile into a functioning inventory. Here’s a robust workflow that many Geeknite readers use successfully:

1) Quick triage and top-level sorting
- Leave the cards in piles by obvious visual cues: foil vs non-foil, clearly rare vs not, and obvious misprints if you spot them.
- Do a light quick count to gauge your scale: how many cards in total, how many foils, how many duplicates, etc.

2) Color sorting
- Create five color stacks: red, blue, green, yellow, purple. The color wheel is your friend for deck-building. This helps you quickly assemble mono-color or multi-color decks as you experiment.
- In your storage, label bins or binders with color cards for easy access.

3) Rarity-based sort
- Separate likely-sellable rares and foils from bulk. Rares and foils can be set aside for resale or for higher-tier deck ideas.
- The bulk portion can go to a general table or tote for later use.

4) Potential keeps
- Make a keep pile for cards that appear to be in good condition and carry actual play value. The more you keep, the more your future deck ideas can grow.
- This is where misprints or unusual foils can shine. If you can verify the print run or variant with a community source, you might be able to produce a small but meaningful return.

5) Storage solutions
- Sleeves for good-condition cards or duplicates that you plan to use or trade.
- Binders for organized color decks or a deck-building backlog.
- Acid-free containers or protective sleeves for fragile cards or high-value items.

6) Documentation
- A small inventory sheet or spreadsheet helps you track what you kept, what you sold, and what remains in bulk. You’ll thank yourself later when you’re building a deck at the last minute and remember you kept a handful of “perfect-for-this” rares.

## Practical tips for maximizing your bulk experience
- Time management: Set aside dedicated sorting sessions. A marathon sorting session might be fun, but it’s better to chunk it into 2–3 hour sessions with a break to preserve enthusiasm.
- Documentation: Keep notes on what you’ve identified as valuable and what needs to be tested for deck viability.
- Community resources: The DBS community is active online. If you’re unsure about a card’s potential, post a photo and ask a local group or online forum for advice.
- Local market testing: If you’re considering selling, test the market with small bundles at first to gauge what buyers want and what they’re willing to pay.
- Cleaning tips: If cards feel sticky or grimy, you can gently wipe them with a microfiber cloth. Don’t use harsh cleaners; you don’t want to degrade the card stock or foil finish.

## Foils, misprints, and curiosities you might find
Foils are the shiny beacons in bulk that can coax a smile from any collector. A bulk lot might contain foils that are rare and valuable, or foils that are simply aesthetically pleasing but economically modest. Don’t confuse a flashy foil with a truly valuable card. Do your homework and check current market demand before pricing.

Misprints and oddities are the mythic beasts of bulk hunting. They can be valuable to the right buyer, but they’re not a guarantee. If you do find something unusual, verify with trusted sources and be transparent about its condition and origin when listing it for sale or trade.
