## What is Herd Mentality?
Herd Mentality is a social, quick-on-your-feet party game that thrives on collective thinking and humorous misfires. At its core, the game asks players to answer prompts in a way that they think will either align with the crowd or deliberately attempt to stand out from it. The tension comes from the split-second decisions about whether to chase the popular response or to gamble on a quirky, unexpected one that will still win you points if you’re right about the crowd, or at least right about what the crowd thinks the majority will pick.

This review focuses on the standard Herd Mentality experience augmented by the Bonus Cards expansion, which promises new twists to keep even veteran players on their toes. The premise is simple enough for newcomers to grasp within a round or two, yet strategic enough for those who enjoy analyzing social dynamics like a board game version of a data dashboard.

### The bonus cards variation
The Bonus Cards are an optional add-on that introduces extra layers of probability, risk, and cheeky misdirection. If you’re hosting a big gathering (the 4- to 20-player crowd), the bonus cards are a clever way to diversify the pacing. They prevent any one strategy from dominating every round and give the table something new to discuss between bursts of raucous laughter.

## How to play: a quick walkthrough
I won’t pretend that Herd Mentality is a 30-minute board game that only needs a single espresso shot to run. It’s a party game, which means the math of the table influences the experience as much as the prompts do. Here’s the general flow you can expect:

1) Set the table and explain the objective. The goal is simple: score points by predicting how the majority of players will respond to prompts, or by cleverly navigating the social dynamics so your answer resonates with the majority and you end up on the same page as the group.
2) Each round, one player reads a prompt. Players then write down a response on their personal cards or slips and keep them hidden for a moment.
3) All answers are revealed (depending on your edition, players may vote on a preferred choice or rank the responses). This is where the “herd” in Herd Mentality becomes visually literal as you watch what others think is the crowd-pleasing answer.
4) The group discusses the responses, often leading to ridiculous banter about why someone chose a particular cue—this is where the game truly shines: social chemistry, humor, and a little bit of bravado.
5) Voting or guessing on which answer the majority will pick follows. If you correctly predict the majority, you gain points. Some rounds award more points for being in a minority who still manages to influence the majority in subsequent rounds, reinforcing that some people are good at playing the table rather than playing the prompt.
6) If you’re playing with the Bonus Cards, you’ll encounter additional twists—perhaps a twist that forces players to reveal their card before voting or another that requires swapping answers under pressure.

For groups of 4 to 20 players, the core mechanic scales gracefully because the concept of a majority becomes deliciously murky as more voices join in. You’ll see patterns emerge: certain prompts become predictable in larger circles, while others explode into chaotic, hilarious outliers as people try to outsmart the crowd with brutally honest or absurd answers.

## Host tips: maximizing fun with big groups
If you’re planning a Herd Mentality night for large groups, here are practical tips to maximize fun and minimize chaos:

- Establish a soft rubric for scoring before the game starts. Clarify how many points you award for guess accuracy and what the implications of Bonus Cards are. A quick reference card at the center of the table helps keep everyone on the same page and reduces arguments.
- Rotate the reader role to ensure everyone gets a chance to influence the round. In big groups, rotating readers every two to three rounds helps keep energy up and avoids the “overbearing moderator” dynamic.
- Use a timer for prompts to keep the pace snappy. A 60-second window per prompt can be plenty; anything longer risks drift and fatigue. If the host timer is a nightmare to use, a simple phone timer works just as well.
- Leverage the social aspect. Encourage playful banter but set a positive tone so people feel safe to share bolder or sillier answers. The humor in Herd Mentality often comes from the absurdity of what people think others will say, so celebrate the goofy moments.
- Prepare a fallback plan for uneven numbers. If you happen to have 7 players but planned for 8, or if attendees drift in late, be ready to run a “mini-round” or a solo mode to catch everyone up without breaking the flow.
- Consider the room layout. In larger groups, seating should be arranged so that different clusters of players can compare their choices. It makes the seating arrangement a part of the show, increasing engagement and laughter.

The charm of Herd Mentality is in the creative, surprisingly relatable choices that people make under the pressure of being “right” in the eyes of the crowd.
