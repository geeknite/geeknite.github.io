## Display and Performance: IPS, 1 ms, and Enough Hz to Make a Sprinter Jealous

The big claim here is the 170 Hz refresh rate. In practice, you’ll notice a smoother motion in fast-paced titles and a more forgiving feel when you’re chasing a shot with a sniper’s patience. The IPS panel helps with wide viewing angles, consistent color from edge to edge, and a level of crispness that makes you wonder if you should stop playing and start graphic design instead. The 1 ms figure is usually a grey-to-grey spec rather than true pixel response; you’ll likely see variable results depending on how you enable motion blur reduction (if at all) and which gaming mode you select. In the real world, the difference between 1 ms and 4 ms is most noticeable in reflex-heavy games, and the AG275QXR holds its own, especially when you’re pinging a corner and trying not to ghost like a haunted monitor.

The panel’s resolution is 2560 by 1440, which is the sweet spot for many gamers who want crisp text and sharp assets without the GPU tax of 4K. If you’re upgrading from a 1080p panel, you’ll immediately notice more detail, better separation between darks and lights, and a sense of depth that makes games look more cinematic. The trade-off, as with any 2.5K display, is that holding high frame rates at max details requires a capable GPU. If your rig is more “mid-range enthusiast” than “rocket scientist,” you’ll still be rewarded by the smoothness, but you may not always hit 170 Hz at the highest details in top-tier titles.

A practical tip: for the pink edition, you’ll want to tune your color temperature to a warmer profile if you care about skin tones in cutscenes. IPS often has excellent color but can oversaturate skin tones when you push the saturation dial too far. Start with a standard sRGB or a calibrated display profile, and tweak from there. The good news is that you can get a great looking image without hunting for advanced color menus you only use once in a blue moon for a YouTube review. The bad news is that you’ll keep tweaking anyway because that’s just what monitors do to us humans.
