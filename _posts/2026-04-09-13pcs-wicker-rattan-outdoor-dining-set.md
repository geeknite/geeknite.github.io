## Design and materials: what to look for

### Frame and weave
The frame is the skeleton. Look for powder-coated aluminum or steel with a rust-resistant finish. Aluminum frames are lighter, easier to move for rearranging seating, and typically less expensive. Steel frames can be sturdier but heavier; if you opt for steel, ensure a good zinc or powder coating to guard against corrosion in coastal climates.

The weave around the frame should be tight and uniform. Common defects include loose strands, kinks, or gaps that reveal the underlying frame. A quick tug test (GENTLE, people) should show no significant give. A good set will have consistent pattern density and minimal fray at the joints.

### Cushions and fabric
Cushions are where comfort lives or dies. Look for cushions with high-density foam (at least 30–35 kg/m3) and a double-stitched seam to survive seasonal sun and casual spills. The fabric is crucial: solution-dyed acrylic fabrics are common for outdoor use because of colorfastness. Removable covers are handy; ensure zippers are robust and accessible for washing.

Test a cushion by sitting down and checking support. If you feel the foam compress too much or the cover sags before you sit, this may be a sign of cheaper materials. The good news: even mid-range sets can feel plush if the cushions are properly layered.

### Table and seating ergonomics
Ergonomics matter more than you might think. A dining chair should have a seat height of 17–19 inches (43–48 cm) from the ground, with a depth that encourages long conversations rather than back yoga. A table about 28–30 inches tall (71–76 cm) is standard; if you’re tall or short, you might want to test a demo chair in person before buying.

Ottomans or additional benches should complement the chairs, offering a place to rest feet or to slide into for a relaxed set of post-dinner chats. If your space is narrow, consider compact designs with thinner armrests and a shallower seat depth that still feels inviting.

### Color options and weave patterns
Many sets come in natural beige or gray tones to blend with a broad range of decors. Some brands offer darker espresso or whitewashed finishes for a more coastal or Mediterranean vibe. The weave patterns can vary from tight, uniform peels to more forgiving, hand-woven looks—each has its own personality. If you rotate furniture seasonally, lighter colors tend to show dirt more quickly but read as brighter, while darker tones hide grime but can heat up in direct sun.

## Comfort and practicality: seats, cushions, and layout

For many people, comfort is the deciding factor between I’ll survive and I’ll host a party. Here are practical tips to evaluate comfort without sitting through a two-hour furniture showroom.

- Cushion density and cover material: As mentioned, higher density foam and fast-drying fabric are your friends. If you can, choose cushions with removable, washable covers. Wash cycles on gentle and air-dry in shade.

If you live in a climate with cold winters or heavy rain, you’ll want to store cushions indoors during the off-season or invest in breathable storage bags. A good practice is to choose cushion covers with zippers and fabric that dries quickly after rain.

## Weather resistance and durability: sun, rain, and the occasional hail of lawn chairs

Outdoor furniture is a long-term investment. The wicker/rattan mix often handles sun and rain better than wood but is not invincible. Here are practical expectations:

- UV resistance: UV-stabilized wicker can resist fading for several seasons, but long exposure will lead to color change. If you live in sunny climates, plan for a color that hides minor fade.
- Moisture: Wicker is typically designed to shed water, but cushions absorb water. Cushion storage during heavy rain or winter is essential. If left damp, cushions can develop mildew. Clean and dry thoroughly before storage.
- Snow and freezing temps: The frame will be more forgiving than cushions. If you expect snow, consider storing cushions indoors and using a weatherproof cover for the table.
- Strength and weight: The weight of the furniture can impact wind resilience. Lightweight frames are easier to move but may require anchors in windy settings. Always ensure your set is stable and anchored on a solid surface.

### Durability indicators worth checking in your budget
- Welds and joints: Look for consistent welds or joinery. A few minor misalignments can be fine, but large gaps may indicate poor construction.
- Finish quality: Powder coating should be even and without obvious scratches. If you see bare metal at the joints, that’s a red flag.
- Fabric warranty: Check the cushion fabric warranty. Some brands offer 1–3 year fade resistance; others offer extended guarantees on colorfastness.
