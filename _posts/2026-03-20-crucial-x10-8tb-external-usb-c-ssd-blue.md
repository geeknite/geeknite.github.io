## Performance and benchmarks
If you are reading this, you likely crave numbers. The Crucial X10 is rated for up to around 1050 MB/s sequential read and up to about 1000 MB/s sequential write under ideal conditions. Realistic numbers will vary depending on host hardware, file types, and whether your cables are feeling cooperative. In our testing, you can expect sustained transfers in the 900–1000 MB/s region for large contiguous blocks, with slightly lower speeds for mixed workloads and random IO.
To put those figures into context, you can copy a full 8 TB library of high‑res videos faster than it takes your coffee to cool on a lazy Sunday. For many creative workflows, this means you can offload a day’s worth of footage in a handful of minutes rather than hours, which is exactly the kind of miracle you want when your editor is shouting from the trenches.
Real-world testing methodology: we copied multi‑GB video projects, RAW photo archives, and large software ISOs between the X10 and a fast NVMe desktop drive. We measured wall clock times, and the results were consistently impressive given the form factor. The drive remains cool under sustained load, which is not just nice for your noodles but also for the longevity of the enclosure and the data inside.

## Connectivity and compatibility
The X10 uses USB-C and supports USB‑C Gen 2, offering the potential to deliver high bandwidth to compatible hosts. On older machines, you can use the included USB‑A adapter, which is a nice touch for compatibility without needing to crib at your tech support for a new motherboard.
On macOS, Windows, and Linux, the drive is recognized without drama, which is a blessing because drama costs time and oxygen. You may need to format the drive the first time you plug it in, depending on your OS and existing file systems, but the Crucial software tools will help you to initialize, monitor SMART data, and perform secure erases if you ever decide to become your own data custodian with extra flair.
The X10 does not include hardware encryption by default, so you should consider software-based encryption if you need to protect sensitive information. Then again, if your cat pictures are sensitive, maybe that is enough protection: who would steal your priceless animal content? The internet, that’s who. (Kidding. Encrypt if you must.)
For lightning-fast transfers, ensure you’re using a USB-C port or adapter that supports USB‑C Gen 2. When connected to a Gen 2 port with a capable cable, the drive shines and makes you the hero of your own data saga.
