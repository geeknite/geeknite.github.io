## What Is Exploding Kittens Original Edition?
Exploding Kittens is a party card game designed by Elan Lee and Matthew Inman, illustrated by The Oatmeal, and published by the famous folks at Exploding Kittens, LLC. The premise is simple, the humor is punchy, and the core mechanic is shockingly easy to grasp: don’t draw an Exploding Kitten card, and defuse when necessary. It’s a game that thrives on quick plays, snappy banter, and a healthy dose of chaos when your friends decide to blink and forget they had two defuses left.

This post specifically highlights the Original Edition, the core set that started the whole craze. The deck is a smorgasbord of card types: attack, See the Future, favor, shuffle, nope, and, of course, the titular Exploding Kitten cards. The artwork is bright, cartoonish, and absolutely unpretentious about its silliness. And yes, we’re treating this as a New and Sealed copy, so we’ll talk about the physical packaging, card stock, and the unboxing dance that follows the first crack of tape.

## How to Play: A Quick Overview for New Buyers
Exploding Kittens is a simple game at its core, which is both its blessing and its trap for overthinking players. You’re trying to avoid drawing an Exploding Kitten card while using Defuse cards or other strategic plays to manipulate outcomes. The base rules are straightforward enough that newcomers can pick up the pace after a single round, but the strategic nuance—the timing of defuses, the savvy play of “Nopes” and “See the Future” cards, and the occasional betrayal—keeps the game lively for longer sessions.

### Core Mechanics in Plain Language
- Each player draws a single card on their turn.
- If you draw an Exploding Kitten, you must Defuse (if you have a Defuse card). If you Defuse, you shuffle the kitten back into the deck and continue; if not, you’re out.
- The deck contains a mix of action cards and defuse cards, allowing you to manipulate turns, peek into the future, force another player to draw, or cancel another player’s action with a Nope card.
- The last person standing wins. It’s that simple and that silly.

The clarity of the rules is a big part of why New and Sealed copies work so well in social settings. It lowers entry friction and invites quick rounds, which is precisely the pace you want when you’re trying to coax laughter out of a crowd that includes skeptics who swear they don’t like “kids’ games.”

## Practical Tips for New Buyers
- Inspect the outer box for any dents or moisture indicators if your region has humidity fluctuations. A seal can protect content, but the box can still get a little sad in transit.
