Comfort and ergonomics

Ergonomics end up being the true measure of a large, shared workstation. The Infinite system offers a few ergonomic levers: adjustable screens, a mix of fixed and adjustable desks, and enough legroom to stretch without violating a coworker personal space. It helps that the white aesthetic fosters a sense of calm and focus, which is extremely useful when collaborating on a feature that has ten opinions and a single deadline. The seats and chairs you pair with the unit are critical; the desk surface height ranges should align with a typical seated posture, with potential add-ons for standing collaboration if your floor plan supports it. The key with a big, shared unit is to avoid the trap of a one size fits all approach. If your team includes people with varying height ranges or accessibility needs, you will want to add adjustable chair arms, monitor arms, and, if possible, height adjustable work surfaces. The good news is that the modular approach makes these upgrades practical rather than impossible.

Screen integration and visual ergonomics

The central wide screen acts as a communal brain for the space. It anchors group discussions, shows live dashboards, and doubles as a presentation surface for quick demos. The screen sits at a comfortable eye level for most users with an adjustable track, minimizing neck strain during long collaborative sessions. If your team lives in software development, you may use the screen for live code reviews, while the peripheral monitors handle documentation and dashboards. The system plays nicely with Windows, macOS, and the occasional Linux workstation, which is essential for teams that have more flavors than a gelato shop. The screen quality is robust for a shared space; brightness and color accuracy hold up under typical office lighting, and the refresh rate or latency is sufficient for the majority of tasks. If you expect high-end CAD work or real-time 3D rendering on the communal display, you might consider pairing the system with dedicated GPU workstations and a separate display wall for heavy lifting.

Cable management and organization

If there is a weakness in large shared workstations, it tends to be cables. The Infinite system does a commendable job here. The built-in channels and clip systems keep power, data, and display cables from tangling into a modern day spaghetti monster. It is not a flawless solution — you still need to plan cable lengths carefully and avoid a tangle by leaving too much slack. But with the raceways and panel anchors, maintenance is easier than you would expect. A clean cable presentation reduces maintenance time and prevents accidental disconnections during tense sprint reviews. Spoiler alert: the IT folks will nod appreciatively when it all remains connected after a week of daily use.

Space utilization and layout options

Spacing is the defining feature when you move from a single desk to a 10 person layout. This system creates a sense of shared purpose while preserving personal space for focus periods. The modular segments allow you to create clusters for squads, or long rows for a linear workflow, depending on your space and culture. The footprint is large, but the payoff is a space that supports spontaneous collaboration and quick huddles. If your office architecture allows, you can also create quiet corners by adding acoustic panels or compact screens for more private conversations. The key is design your floor plan with future teams in mind, not just the current headcount. This system is scalable and ready to adapt, which is a major win in fast-moving environments.

Aesthetic and maintenance considerations

The white finish is stylish and modern, which makes the space look clean and professional. However, white surfaces show dust, scuffs, and fingerprints more readily than darker tones. Regular cleaning and occasional touch-ups will keep the aesthetic sharp. The material choices feel sturdy enough for daily office use, with a finish that resists minor scratches and glue marks from whiteboard sessions. If you operate in an environment with high traffic or construction dust, keep a maintenance schedule that includes a weekly wipe-down and a quarterly assessment of panel alignment. A little upkeep goes a long way toward preserving the industrial chic vibe this unit promises.

For some teams, that is priceless; for others, it is an extra line item that requires careful budgeting.

What kind of team benefits most

The ideal user profile includes mid to large sized teams that work on shared projects, require frequent cross-team reviews, and value a single source of truth during standups.
