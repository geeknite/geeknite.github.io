## Setup and Quick Start Rules

To begin, you pour the bag in the middle, give everyone a rack, and distribute a starter handful of tiles (the exact number is consistent with traditional Bananagrams and depends on the table size). The aim remains unchanged: use all your tiles to form a connected grid of valid words faster than your opponents. The new mechanics here are optional and aimed at maintaining momentum during longer sessions:

- Tide Peel: On your turn, you can declare a Tide Peel to swap one tile from your rack for a fresh tile from the bag and immediately place that tile somewhere on your grid. The strategic element is deciding where to place the new tile to keep your grid intact.
- Drift Dump: If you want to prune an awkward tile from your rack mid-game, you can perform a Drift Dump to drop a tile from your board back into the bag for a fresh redraw later.

As in the base game, you can redraw after a Peel until you’re satisfied with the shape of your grid. The booklet clarifies that these maneuvers are optional; use them to fit your table’s pace, your vocabulary, and your tolerance for chaos.

Rules quick-start: A quick skim of the rulebook reveals a few clarifications intended to help players with readability and timers. The Oceanic edition does not hinge on a dictionary crusade; however, it does remind players that real words are required for validity. ## Word Pool, Strategy, and The Ocean as a Metaphor

The core strategy remains a craft of tile awareness, word recognition, and spatial arrangement. Oceanic Edition expands the pool of accessible words with ocean-themed terminology, but you won’t be forced into nautical jargon to win. The rules still reward speed and revision as you go, and the larger tile racks reduce the need for frantic scrambling. In practice, you’ll find that you’re building longer word strings and more interconnected grids as you gain comfort with the new tile shapes and the calmer aesthetic that accompanies them.

Sea-salt aside, there’s a real tactical layer here: you’re not just racing to finish; you’re also shaping the board to defend against a late-game scramble from a rival who’s decided to “peel” every single tile and go for a monster grid. The Tide Peel mechanic is a gentle nudge toward strategic pacing, letting players trade one tile at a time rather than fizzing out in a chaotic flood of last-second letters. The Drift Dump acts like a last-resort pruning tool, and yes, it can be devastating if used against you at precisely the moment you’ve aligned a six-word collage across a corner of the board.
