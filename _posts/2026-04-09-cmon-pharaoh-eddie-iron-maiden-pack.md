### Painting Prospects and Techniques
If you’re a painter who loves contrast and layering, Pharaoh Eddie is a dream subject. The internal grooves offer natural shadows, and the crown and jewelry are perfect for metallics, bright gems, or ancient golds. Suggested approach:

- Prime in black or white depending on your scheme. Black priming helps with the deep shadows in the crannies of the armor; white priming makes the golds pop.
- Basecoat the skin on Eddie with a pale desert tone, adding a touch of blue-gray in the recesses for depth.
- Golds for the crown and armor look great with a base of metallic gold, followed by a wash (reikland fleshtone is common for skin; sepia or black for armor shadows).
- The decals can be applied sparingly to emulate lapis-blue inlays on the armor—think tasteful inlay rather than sticker chaos.
- Dry brush the raised textures with a light bone color to emphasize the reliefs and give a sun-baked look.

If you’re new to painting metallics, practice on a test piece first. The desertist aesthetic benefits from a weathered oxidized patina on some gold areas to give them “antique treasure” vibes rather than “brand-new bling.” The extra heads provide an opportunity to experiment with different headgear color schemes without repainting the entire model.

### Assembly Tips
A few practical tips to save you frustration:
- Dry-fit the components before gluing. The head, crown, and staff order can be a little fiddly, and you’ll want to ensure the alignment is right before you commit.
- Use a thin cyanoacrylate or plastic glue to avoid damaging fine details. A small needle applicator is your friend here.
- If you’re not planning to convert, you can keep the extra head as a display option and not risk the delicate neck pegs on heavy handling.
- Consider magnetizing the head or staff for easy swapping depending on your display vs. battle needs. (Magnetizing is optional, but it’s a great party trick.)
