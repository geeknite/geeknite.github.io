### The NM Mystery: What Does Near Mint Really Mean?
Near mint is the unicorn of card collecting: not quite pristine, but so close you could practically use it as a mirror for your latte art. In practical terms, NM for a pre-release lot means:
- Cards show only minor corner wear, slight edge wear, and no major creases or surface damage.
- No visible scratches on holofoils that would ruin the visual impact when you tilt the card under game-light. 
- No heavy marks, stains, or creases that would scream “this card was played heavily in a tournament” to a grading society.

The beauty of NM, of course, is subjective. Some players are ruthless in their expectations (they want every card to look like it just popped out of the pack with a fresh scent of cardboard sunshine). Others are more forgiving, focusing on playability rather than cosmetic perfection. The NM stamp in a pre-release lot often translates into “these cards could fetch a respectable resell price if you know the market tides,” but it’s not a guarantee—product shelf intrigue can be wilder than a DBZ boss fight.

### Condition and Quality: All NM, Please?
The label NM suggests the cards are in fabulous shape, but there are caveats:
- The cards should show minimal wear consistent with light handling. If you’re outside a store that’s had a hot summer and a heavy humidity swing, the edges might show waviness rather than actual scuffing.
- Holographic foils should have even foil distribution without obvious dents, misprints, or “blooming” patterns that scream “printer’s error.”
- Subtle print lines aren’t a disqualifier; those can be a result of the manufacturing process. Major creases or surface scratches, however, are a red flag that NM became NM-adjacent or worse—Doomed Mint (DM).

The point is simple: “NM” isn’t a magic spell—it's a good faith estimate. If you’re buying strictly for investment, you’ll want to inspect or request photos of the holofoils and the back of the card (to confirm consistent color and cardstock texture). If you’re buying for casual playing, you’ll accept a few cosmetic quirks so long as playability remains unaffected.

## The Card Tiers You’ll Encounter
Dragon Ball Super CCG has a tiering system that typically encompasses:

In a NM lot, you want the card shapes intact, the foiling even, and the print lines well-contained within the edges. You don’t want whitening along the card borders or obvious signs of re-cutting. It’s not a grading lab, but it’s close enough to feel like you’re auditioning for a card-collecting hall of fame.

## Authenticity and Quality Assurance
When you buy NM, you’re not just buying a stack of cards; you’re buying a promise: the cards will present well, perform in play, and not crumble under your gaze. Here are a few practical checks you can perform (or request photos of) before you hand over cash:
- Front and back consistency: Look for color bleeding, edge whitening, or suspicious uniformly-graded damage marks that could hint at post-production alterations.
- Foil integrity: Check for consistent foil distribution and uniform embossing. Uneven foiling can reduce the overall aesthetic and, by extension, resale value.
- Print quality: Watch for miscuts, misprints, or misalignment in the text. In a rare scenario, a card with fundamental text misprints could be unplayable, which defeats both your deck-building and investment goals.
