## What’s in the Fortune Row GM Screen?

The product ships as a standard GM screen with portrait-appropriate panels. The inner surface includes:

- Quick-Reference Combat/Verdict prompts: a simplified set of rules cues that you’ll reach for during tense moments. You won’t memorize every stat, but you’ll know exactly where to glance for the most likely resolution—great for keeping pace when the party is juggling multiple mythoi and the crowd is getting loud.
- Fate/Trophy stacks: a neat little index of how Fortune Points, Fate, and Clues interact in typical sessions. It’s the kind of thing you’d normally scribble on a sticky note, but the Fortune Row screen does the giving and the taking in a single glance.
- Narrative prompts: prompts designed to push scenes from “someone did something” to “this is how the mythic layer reveals itself.” These prompts help you guide the session toward the story you want without feeling like you’re forcing the players to follow a script. It’s a delicate balance, but Fortune Row aims for it.
- Clue and Location Lists: handy quick lists of common locations and clue types that players often chase in City of Mist games. You can refer to them without flipping through the core book, allowing you to maintain momentum.
- Optional rules reminders: the GM screen presents a handful of optional rules or house rules that you might be tempted to skip, but that you should consider for a more cinematic cityscape. It’s a gentle nudge toward a forgiveness-friendly mode of play where mistakes are part of the story, not a reason to pause the clock.

In short: Fortune Row is designed to reduce rule-meandering and accelerate table-talk, while still preserving the city’s flavor and its mythic-microstructure. If you’re the GM who keeps a dozen tabs open at once, you’ll appreciate having a compact cheat sheet you can actually use while you’re tracking the hourglass of danger in a crowded room.

## Build Quality and Practicality

Nothing kills a mood faster than a cardboard screen that buckles when you lean on it to take notes or prop a dramatic moment. Fortune Row uses a sturdy board with a matte lamination, which is forgiving during long campaigns and forgiving of accidental napkin shredding (we’ve all been there). The panels are aligned well enough that you can physically flip them without the glue letting go; this is not a flimsy product. The surface resists minor staining from spilled coffee or the accidental smear of a greasy finger; it wipes clean enough without showing wear in a few sessions.

The design is clever in terms of accessibility. The typography is legible, with a high-contrast palette that remains readable even if you’re far from the screen or wearing a dimmed lamp shade over your eyes to emulate late-night mystery vibes. The panels are sized to accommodate a finger’s width for flipping, which matters when you’re trying to flip to a reference in the middle of a tense scene without dislodging the entire layout.

One small gripe: the color-coding could be a tad crisper. Some sections blend into the background if you’re gaming in a low-light room with heavy red ambient lighting. It’s not a fatal flaw, but if you’re a mom-and-pop operation of a GM who relies heavily on color-coded cues to keep the party on track, you might want to consider a quick highlighter or a set of sticky notes to help the obviously efficient sections pop out more.

## Quick Reference and Storytelling Rhythm

The Fortune Row screen leans into a rhythm that suits City of Mist’s storytelling style: a table where quick calls-to-action replace long-winded rulings, and a narrative spark replaces a heavy rules lecture. In a system where players have mythic identities that are both powers and vulnerabilities, the GM screen’s prompts help you nudge the scene toward a mythic revelation without forcing a cutscene. It’s a subtle feature, but those are the features you remember when you’re eight sessions into a campaign and your brain has turned into a glittering newsroom of ideas.

From a design perspective, the balance between “reference” and “story prompts” is crucial. If a GM screen is all fluff and no utility, it becomes a prop. If it’s all stats and no flavor, it becomes a cheat sheet for someone who forgot what game they’re actually playing. Fortune Row attempts a middle ground that feels natural for this game’s tone, with enough flavor to spark ideas and enough utility to keep the game moving.

One of the more clever inclusions is the location/clue synergy. City of Mist emphasizes locations that feel cinematic: a club, a rooftop, a back-alley garage with a purpose. The Fortune Row screen presents quick entries for common clues tied to each location, which makes scene construction a little more cinematic and a lot less fiddly. If you’re the GM who tries to map visuals to the city on the fly, you’ll likely find this a helpful reminder rather than a constraint.

### Accessibility and Inclusivity in Practice
Fortune Row makes an effort to be accessible in practical ways. The bold typography helps players with vision differences pick out key terms quickly, and the layout is designed so that you’re less prone to misreading critical prompts during crunch moments. The color palette tries to stay legible in low-light rooms, but if your table has folks with color vision differences, consider adding a small legend on the table or using icons (stars, gears, footprints) to reinforce meaning. The design team’s intent is not to create an inaccessible product but to broaden usability without sacrificing the mood of the city.
