## The Core Rulebook: A Structure Built for Story, Not for Siege Engines
The book’s organization mirrors its playstyle: flavor upfront, rules that stay out of the way during the moment, and a quick-reference spine that respects your time at the table. It’s designed to be readable in a long train ride or a coffee shop scratchpad, not to punish you for not owning a binder made of premium leather and regret. The art direction leans into neon signs, rain-soaked streets, and colors that would get a vampire artist arrested for jaywalking. The result is inviting rather than intimidating—a book you want to flip through between sessions, not a door you slam behind you after just three pages.

### What the Chapters Are Doing For You
- Part I: Conceptual Foundations. You’ll meet Legends, Masks, and the city-within-the-city. It’s the philosophical and emotional primer: myths exist because people tell stories about them, and those stories become real when enough people believe.
- Part II: Character Creation. Build a Legend by selecting Mantles, Tags, and a mundane life. The core mechanic remains approachable: you don’t need a PhD in power taxonomy; you assemble a bag of Tags and decide how your Legend interacts with the ordinary world.
- Part III: Rules and Resolution. The dice come in here, but not as a burdensome grid. The Tag system is central: use Tags to generate opportunities, the GM uses opposing Tags to complicate things, and the story advances with momentum.
- Part IV: World Building and Campaign Tools. Cities aren’t blank canvases; they’re living organisms with neighborhoods, factions, and myths that collide in the night. Expect prompts, seed ideas, and a toolbox that helps GMs escalate tension without turning every session into a chase sequence.
- Appendices and Play Aids. Quick-start rules, sample characters, ready-to-run encounters—ideal for a one-shot or a curated evening that still feels substantial.

The structure is deliberately forgiving for newcomers and a bit investigative for veterans. If you’ve played games that hinge on tactical crunch, City of Mist nudges you toward storytelling first and mechanics second. The cleverness is in service to mood, not the other way around.

## Core Mechanics: How It Feels When You Roll the City Itself
The heart of City of Mist is a tag-based resolution system. You don’t juggle a dozen different dice; you stack Tags that describe who you are, what you can do, and what stands in your way. When you attempt something, you lean on relevant Tags. The result is narrative-forward: you succeed with a twist, fail with a complication, or pivot when a tag isn’t quite right.

### Tags, Legends, and Everyday Life
Tags are the heartbeat. A Tag is a two-edged blade: it can help you achieve a goal, or it can complicate matters in delightful ways. A Legend might be “Streetwise Detective,” “Nighttime Alchemist,” or “Mercurial Trickster.” The mundane life grounding the character matters because the city doesn’t wear masks; it wears the mask’s shadow in the protagonist’s day job, relationships, and ordinary routines.

What’s genuinely refreshing is how Tag use invites improvisation. There’s no need to memorize a spell catalog or a huge power ledger. You describe how your Legend tangles with the city’s mysteries, and the book nudges you toward cinematic language that keeps sessions dynamic without requiring a blockbuster budget.

### Resolution and Narration Style
Narrative cause and effect drives resolution. A successful Streetwise action might piece together a clue but lug in a rival gang; a Mythic Power might attract attention from a city official who has secrets of their own. The mechanics reward storytelling that feels inevitable, even when the outcome is unpredictable. You narrate; the GM fills in the gaps; the scene gains momentum instead of stalling on a chart.

The system is lightweight on purpose. You can stage a tense interrogation, a high-speed chase, or an intimate character moment with ease. It leans into descriptive storytelling and shared narration rather than exhaustive number-crunching.

### Character Creation: A Quick Tour Down Myth Lane
Creating a Legend is like crafting a minimal viable myth. Choose a Mantle (the domain of power), form a set of Tags, and define a mundane life behind the myth. The Mantle anchors you to the city’s bigger mythology, while Tags provide your toolkit for now—what you can do right here, what you can improvise in the moment, and what you should avoid unless you want trouble in the alley behind the bakery.

The Core Rulebook excels at guiding new players through a fast setup. It’s about voice, not spreadsheets. The Mantle and the mundane world interact in a way that makes a Legend feel specific and alive—the mask you wear and the possibility you chase. Noir fiction thrives on dualities, and City of Mist makes that tension feel earned, not manufactured.

### The GM’s Toolkit
For GMs, the Core Rulebook is more than a recipe of numbers. It’s a philosophy of framing the city as a living, reactive character. The City is a place with neighborhoods that hide secrets and factions that pull strings. The GM is a narrator building a web into which players step, heroic or flawed, and then realize the web has a will of its own.

The GM sections champion modular storytelling: drop a Legend’s backstory into a scene, twist a clue to reveal a larger conspiracy, or pivot to a personal drama that tests beliefs. This is quintessential noir: the mystery isn’t solved by a single clever tactic; it unfolds through a chain of moments where every choice matters.

## The Good, the Bad, and the Neon-Lit Middle Ground
There’s a lot to love: mood, storytelling emphasis, clever Tag-work, and the invitation to describe rather than compute. The Core Rulebook balances structure for quick starts with space for improvisation and personal flavor.

That said, a few caveats deserve attention:
- Rule drift in longer campaigns. Lightweight and narrative-driven systems can drift as groups interpret Tags differently or apply resolution rules at varying strictness. The book leans on guidance rather than rigidity, which can be either a blessing or a curse depending on your table’s temperament.
- Power scale and balance. Because Legends are built from Tags, you can end up with a spectrum of capabilities that feels uneven across players. The GM’s job is to lean into narrative consequences to preserve drama and fairness rather than rely on weighty numbers.
- Table aura and theme control. City of Mist thrives when the table commits to the noir mood. If players chase a sunny, breezy vibe, you may need to do some table governance to keep tone from tipping into camp or parody.

The book doesn’t pretend to be a perfect fit for every group, but it provides robust tools to tailor the experience to your table’s vibe.

## Running City of Mist: Practical GM and Player Tips
If you’re a first-time GM approaching City of Mist, here are some pragmatic tips to get into the action without dragging your night into a Rules Night of Doom:

- Start small. A single Legend, one Tag list, and a short Mystery can demonstrate the system and establish mood in a two-hour session.
- Use prompts, not rigid plots. Neighborhoods, factions, and myths in the book are designed to spark improvisation while staying anchored in mythic logic.
- Lean into the Mythic-Mundane balance. The joy comes from a Legend’s mythic power colliding with a day job, a neighbor’s secret, or a criminal network’s long shadow.
- Encourage players to narrate moments of truth. Let them craft the punchline of a Scene; you’ll often get richer storytelling than if you forced a strict sequence of events.
- Prepare a few universal hooks. The city’s overnight vibe loves the “what happened in the hood last night” approach, so hooks should be adaptable to multiple scenes.

For players, the core advice is simple: describe your Tag in the moment. If you’re a Detective who’s also a Trickster, don’t merely say you “use Streetwise to solve the case.” Describe how the pavement talks to you, how a clue shines with a half-remembered myth, and how truth slides away like a neon reflection in rainwater.
