## How to play and what’s new

UNO All Wild Family Party introduces a few twists that you will either love or loudly veto within the first two rounds. Here is a compact overview of what to expect when you set up for the first time.

### Setup
1. Shuffle both decks together and deal seven cards to each player, just like classic UNO. 
2. Place the top card of the draw pile face up to begin the discard pile. 
3. Decide who will be the scorekeeper if you are playing a longer session. 
4. Keep the new wild and party cards separate until you need them, then mix them into the draw pile as directed by the rules.

The setup feels familiar but with a nudge toward mischief. It sets the tone: we are here to have a good time, not to prove math genius caliber from the first draw. You’ll likely find yourself debating whether to shuffle again if a sequence of wild color changes hits back-to-back, which makes for excellent group chat fodder after the game ends.

### Core mechanics and the All Wild twist
The standard UNO mechanic remains: match color or number, draw when you cannot, shout UNO when you are down to one card. The All Wild variant adds two layers:
- Wild cards that change color are more chaotic because the color change can be triggered or forced by certain party cards.
- Party cards inject mini-games or penalties that gamify the human condition — embarrassment, bragging, and occasional strategic sabotage all packaged as a card instruction.

In practice, this means a turn can include: changing color, drawing extra cards, and completing a silly challenge in the name of the All Wild meta. The party cards provide prompts like sing a chorus of a jingle, tell a quick childhood memory, or swap hands with another player. If you are worried this will descend into chaos, you are not entirely wrong, but that is the intention behind the design. The All Wild system leans into the social engine rather than trying to suppress it; control is distributed and often hilariously imperfect.

### Strategy, chaos, and how to win
If you love tight procedural play with predictable outcomes, UNO All Wild will not disappoint you in a sterile way. If you love unpredictable overheating of family dynamics, you will probably adore it. The core strategy remains: minimize your card count while avoiding penalties introduced by party cards. The new mechanics reward players who can react quickly to changing color requirements and who are willing to embrace ridiculous dares for a shorter path to victory.

One practical tip: keep the wild cards ready for late game where a single color color-change can rescue you from a near-elimination state. The party cards are excellent at breaking ties or creating dramatic last-turn reversals, so use them to your advantage, but don’t rely on them to pull you through every round. The balance between classic UNO speed and party-induced chaos is where the magic hides. It rewards both quick thinking and a healthy tolerance for ridiculous dares. If your circle enjoys friendly sabotage and competitive banter, you’ll have a field day here.

## Practical tips for getting the most out of UNO All Wild
- Start with a soft introduction: two or three rounds with minimal party cards to acclimate your group to the new flow.
- Designate a 'party card captain' for the first few rounds to explain the dares to the group. After a while, the captain role can rotate.
- Use the color-change cards strategically in late rounds to set up your own winning window.
- Consider penalty caps: to avoid chaos turning into hurt feelings, set a cap on the number of party tasks per round or per game.
- Have a quick fun reward ready for the winner; a goofy trophy or a small silly prize adds extra motivation and keeps the vibe light.
- Rotate the party card density: sometimes run a lean game with few dares, other times go full chaos; read the room and adjust before the timer hits the boiling point.
