# Codenames Board Game by Vlaada Chvátil (CGE, 2016) – Brand New Sealed Party Game

When you think of a party game about spies, secret agents, and sneaky wordplay, your mind should probably land on the desk of Vlaada Chvátil. ## Overview
Codenames is a word game where two teams compete to identify their own agents on a grid of codename words. A spymaster from each team gives one-word clues that connect multiple words on the board. The team then tries to guess which words belong to their agents while avoiding the opposing team's agents and the dreaded assassin word. The sealed copy we’re examining here contains the classic word grid, a deck of agent cards, a timer (occasionally), and the satisfaction of starting a party game night with the subtlety of a glitter bomb in a library.

### What’s in the box
- A stack of codename cards with 25 words per standard board
- A tiny red and blue agent overlay sheet or counters
- A set of “neutral” and “assassin” cards
- A blobby guide to how clues work (which you will ignore because you know this already)
- A cardboard insert that will anger you when you try to recast the box for storage
- A tiny timer, if present in your edition (some sealed boxes save you from the tyranny of a ticking clock)

## Setup and first impressions
Unboxing a brand-new sealed copy is both thrilling and terrifying. The waxy smell of fresh cardboard fills the room, like a safety officer yelling, “This is totally safe for 4-8 players, probably.” You will appreciate the crisp card stock, the simple color palette (red team, blue team, neutral, assassin), and the fact that the box is designed to be repurposed as a tiny fortress for your regrets after you realize you’ve spent 90 minutes hurling words at your friends rather than clues at your colleagues.

To set up, you lay out the 25 codename cards in a 5x5 grid, pick a color pair to designate the starting team, and give the spymasters their clue decks. The official sealed product gives you a sense that you’re the CEO of a secret spy organization, minus the actual corporate benefits and with more caffeine. The moment the timer starts? You’ll hear a chorus of giggles, groans, and whispered, “Okay, what the heck does that even mean?”

### The core mechanics in a nutshell
Codenames is about encoding ideas with a single clue word and a number. For example, your spymaster might say “Ocean 2” and then your team must guess two codename words on the board that are connected to the clue “Ocean.” The catch: some words might be connected to the other team’s agents, some could be neutral, and one is the dreaded assassin. If your team guesses the assassin, the game ends immediately in a blaze of cardboard glory (or cardboard shame).

Here’s the crux: the strategy is not about guessing correctly; it’s about guiding teammates without tipping them off to the exact words. You learn to weigh degrees of proximity: does the clue “Ocean 2” include “Whale” and “Ship” or does it ruin you by also pointing to “Bank” because you’re thinking about an underwater vault? It’s a delicate dance of punny hints, shared mental models, and the occasional outburst of “No, that’s not it!” That’s the Magic of Codenames — the moment when your spouse, your best friend, and your neighbor collectively fail upward because you’ve chosen a clue that is simultaneously brilliant and catastrophic.

## Playthrough: a typical round
The game cycles through rounds where each team’s spymaster gives a clue, the field operatives guess, and chaos ensues. A typical turn goes like this:
1) Spymaster studies the card grid, scanning for connections that won’t blow up in their face. 2) Spymaster utters a one-word clue and a number. 3) Team members discuss, argue about whether a link exists, and point to codename words they believe fit the clue. 4) The chosen words are revealed; the team marks their progress. 5) If the assassin word is picked, the round ends dramatically in disappointment and a reminder that secrecy is tough on a Saturday night.

One delightful thing about a sealed copy is the potential for “new game smell” to become a running gag. If you’re playing with friends who have played dozens of times and think they’ve seen every permutation, the sealed copy can reintroduce the thrill of discovery as you discover which words people will connect to your clues more often than not.

### The team dynamics and social deduction flavor
Part of Codenames’ appeal is that it feels like a social deduction game in disguise. Your spymaster must reveal just enough to guide their teammates while keeping the other team from reading the same minds. The sealed box doesn’t change that core dynamic; it simply gives you a fresh palette to test your ability to craft clues. The trickiest part is knowing when to stop before you overshare. A clue that’s “Ocean 3” may tempt your team to guess three words, but if you misjudge, your team might stumble into the assassin or the other team’s words. The tension is delicious, and the laughter that follows is your warranty card for a wonderful game night.

If you’re the type who loves to mix in “double clue” rounds or “no assassin” rounds for kinder playgroups, your sealed copy will be the perfect blank slate for your experimentation.

## Taste test: humor, wordplay, and its crowd-pleasing nature
Codenames isn’t about heavy strategy; it’s about a shared comedic moment as you scramble to connect your team’s thoughts with a single word. You’ll see teammates pointing to words that couldn’t possibly fit the clue, and you’ll see the spymaster trying to salvage a doomed clue through a flourish of dramatic emphasis. The humor comes from human miscommunication—perfect for a party game where the goal is to have fun, not achieve a flawless victory.

The sealed edition is best enjoyed with a group that can appreciate puns, wordplay, and the consequences of overthinking. If your friends treat party games as a no-fun-zone, Codenames might not convert them into word-tacticians overnight. But with a little grace (and maybe a glass of something bubbly), you’ll witness social dynamics at their most entertaining: the gentle ribbing over a bad clue, the triumphant cheer of a well-chosen word, and the universal relief when your team finally guesses the intended words without triggering the assassin.
