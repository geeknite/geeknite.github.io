## Overview
Codenames is a word-based party game where you split into two teams, give one-word clues, and try to guess the agents on the table. The Original Game uses a 5x5 grid of codename cards; on each card, a word is printed. Some words matter to the red team, some to the blue team; one word is the assassin. The spymaster offers a clue like Oceans 3, and the teammates must pick cards that match the clue. It sounds simple, but it's a mental tug-of-war that requires prying open the other team's pattern recognition while concealing your own plan.

What makes it special: the tension, the misdirection, and the shared vocabulary quirks that arise when players discover each other's associations. It's the kind of game where you can laugh at your own ability (or inability) to connect the right dots. It works equally well in a noisy living room and a quiet cafe, where whispered conspiracies are the currency of the night. You will also learn a surprising thing: you are not the first to trap yourself with a single wrong link; someone else has lived your nightmare and somehow turned it into a legendary pun.

### History and design philosophy
Codenames began as a playground for language nerds and social fabricators. Vlaada Chvátil designed a game that rewards lateral thinking and relies on shared culture rather than heavy components. The genius of the Original Game is that it strips gaming down to a handful of words and a single rule: make a link, take a risk, and let the team decode you. The philosophy is clear: keep it accessible, keep it fast, and keep a touch of chaos to remind players that planning is often a spectator sport. In Geeknite's eyes, that is exactly what makes the game repeatable and endlessly re-playable.

### Setup and components
The original box contains 400 cards (200 red words, 200 blue words), plus a 5x5 grid card, a rulebook, a timer card that evokes a kind of spy drama, and colored tokens for keeping score. Materials quality is sturdy enough to survive repeated game nights; the cards shuffle well, the words are legible, and the grid is laid out with the minimal friction a spymaster needs.

The spyglass timer is more ambiance than engineering; it creates a sense of urgency without becoming a strict time pressure machine. Some groups have replaced it with their own minimalist timer, while others keep the original for nostalgia. The overall packaging is efficient: nothing feels extraneous, and yet nothing important is missing. It's a design that respects players' time and frees them to focus on the social puzzle at the center of the table.

### Gameplay basics
- Split players into two teams, designate a spymaster for each side, and create the 5x5 grid of codename cards.
- The spymaster sees a key card that marks which words belong to red, blue, the assassin, and bystanders.
- The spymaster gives a one-word clue and a number, like Ocean 2, pointing players toward two relevant words on the grid without tipping into the other colors or the assassin.
- Teammates discuss and select cards; the chosen cards are revealed to confirm if they belong to their team, to the other team, or to be lethal (assassin). The goal is to find all your team's words before the other team.

### Strategy and tips
- Clue quality matters more than quantity. A well-chosen clue that taps into multiple associations is worth more than a long string of random connections.
- Read the room: the best clues often come from common experiences the team shares rather than esoteric references.
- Balanced risk: sometimes a bold clue can swing momentum dramatically; other times a conservative approach preserves the turn order and reduces mistakes.

### Clue examples: what to aim for and what to avoid
- Effective clue: Ocean 3, pointing to Wave, Ship, and Beach if those are the corresponding words.
- Risky clue: a pop culture blast that requires perfect tacit knowledge of all players; it can backfire if even one teammate misreads the link.
- The assassin trap: Do not connect with a clue that can lure the team toward a dangerous choice; the payoffs may be catastrophic.

### Game variants
- Classic 5x5 grid remains standard; some groups riff on a larger grid or shorter rounds to accelerate the pace.
- Digital versions often add features like automatic card shuffles and built-in timers, but the social core remains identical.
- House rules abound: rotating spymasters per round, a shared pool of bonus words to boost action, or a timer that forces quick decisions.

### House rules and optional twists
- Timed rounds: limit each turn to 60 seconds; the timer keeps energy high and fosters concise clues.
- Spyglass variants: require spymaster to speak their clue through a mock spyglass for theatrical effect.
- Collaboration round: two teams work together to solve the grid within a single large time window, cross-pollinating ideas.

### Digital versions and expansions

### How to win: practical heuristics
- Early rounds emphasize discovery: focus on safe clues that reliably reveal multiple of your own words without pointing toward the assassin.
- Midgame: escalate with riskier clues if you have a clear sense of your teammates’ associations.
- Endgame: when only 2-3 words remain, the team’s confidence matters as much as the actual clue; the right guess can feel like a dramatic final cutscene in a popcorn film.
