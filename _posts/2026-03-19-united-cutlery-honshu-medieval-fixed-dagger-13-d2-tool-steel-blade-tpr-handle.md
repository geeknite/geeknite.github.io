### Design philosophy and specs

The dagger measures roughly 13 inches overall, a length that commands attention whether you’re showing it on a shelf or holding it in a cosplay duel. The blade is D2 tool steel, a composition known in knife circles for its hardness and edge retention. D2 is a high-carbon alloy that can take a sharp edge and resist deformation, but it is not stainless. Expect to wipe it down after use and oil it if you’re keeping it in a humid environment. Don’t be surprised if you see a little patina forming on the blade after extended exposure to moisture; that patina is a sign of character, not neglect.

The handle is a TPR (thermoplastic rubber) grip. TPR is the “squishy-chic” of the knife world, offering grip when your palms are sweaty after a long convention line and during staged combat rehearsals. It’s not a luxury wood grip that will age gracefully with time, but it is comfortable, grippy, and forgiving if your swipes are a touch enthusiastic. The combination of a full tang with a robust TPR handle gives the dagger a surprising amount of backbone. This isn’t a flimsy prop; it’s a real blade with a real backbone that can handle a bit of rough play in the right hands.

### The blade geometry and edge quality

The Honshu dagger features a double-edged blade with a spear-like point. The geometry is designed for slashing and puncturing in a scenario where drama is more important than culinary precision. The geometry looks symmetrical and clean; the factory edge is sharp enough to slice through paper with ease and carve a clean line on a test piece of cardboard. ### Maintenance and care (D2 care basics)

D2 steel, while robust, is not stainless; routine care is essential to keep it looking good. After use, wipe the blade dry, lightly oil the edge, and store in a dry environment. If you operate in a humid climate, consider periodic rust-preventive maintenance to keep pitting at bay. For the handle, wipe away sweat or moisture to prolong the life of the TPR grip. If the blade ever feels dull or nicked, you can rely on standard sharpening methods to restore the edge. The blade responds well to both traditional stone sharpening and modern steel sharpening methods. The key is consistency and avoiding excess moisture.

Maintenance steps:
- Clean blade with mild soap and water; avoid abrasives that could scratch the stone-washed finish.
- Dry thoroughly.
- Apply thin oil coating on blade, wipe off excess.
- Check sheath fit and belt retention; adjust if necessary.
- Store in a dry place away from corrosive elements.
