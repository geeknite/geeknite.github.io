## The Special Guest: Edouard Guiton
Edouard Guiton is inserted into this edition like a guest star on a super-hero team-up episode. We won’t pretend to know every design decision behind M71, but the feeling is that Guiton’s influence nudges the game toward sharper thematic beats without surrendering the core cooperative mechanics that fans adore. You’ll notice it in the way the scenarios unfold, with more emphasis on narrative beats and set-piece moments, rather than a dry sprint from A to B with a couple of zombies along the way.

## Components and Sealed Condition
Sealed is a double-edged sword. On the one hand, you get the satisfaction of “stepping into a fresh universe” with pristine components. On the other, you’re staring at a puzzle: Will you crack the plastic wrapper and risk marker misalignment, or will you keep the pristine aura by never opening the box? The M71 set gives you a robust kit: boards, tiles, dice, a generous assortment of miniatures, and a deck that can make you feel like a tactician and a gardener at the same time (because you’ll be pruning zombies with environment cards and plastic blades).

Durability-wise, the pieces survive the perils of hobby life: a sturdy box, thick boards, and cards with decent gloss. The miniatures are well-cast with clean molds; you won’t have to perform surgery on a sprue to decide which zombie is which. From a presentation perspective, the M71 set looks ready to go on a table in a cafe where you pretend to be cool while you argue about dice probabilities. The box remains sealed until you’re ready to play, which is the dream people tell themselves when they buy a new complete edition: “We will not open this until the stars align with a full day of rain and a cat demanding snacks.” If you’re after the aesthetic, you’ll be rewarded.

## Gameplay and Mechanics: What Works, What Isn’t
Zombicide’s core loop is simple on paper: coordinate with your crew, manage resources, slay waves of undead, and survive until the clock runs out or the last man standing raises the white flag. M71 follows that blueprint but with Edouard’s flourish. You’ll notice smoother turn sequencing, more meaningful mission objectives, and a tad more emphasis on positioning and line of sight. The result is a game that feels familiar enough to slip into your rotation, yet fresh enough to earn a few new “aha” moments.

### Solo vs Cooperative Play
As with the lineage of Zombicide, you can tackle M71 solo, though the experience changes. The solo mode scales to keep the challenge meaningful, which is essential because a lot of cooperative games lose their juice when played alone. The scenarios lean into a balance of risk and reward that feels fair, not punitive, which is a big win for players who like a challenge but don’t want to rage-quit after a bad die roll.

If you’re playing with a group, expect a tempo that rewards planning, talking through turns, and a little bit of healthy table talk about whether you should push your luck on a risky action or take a safer path that buys you time. The guest designer’s QA‑style touch shows here: tensions in play are resolved through smarter card text and scenario design rather than a dozen new rules, which keeps things accessible.

### Difficulty and Replayability
M71 doesn’t pretend to cure all board game ills, but it does a solid job of giving you a tower of variety without overwhelming you with complexity. The scenarios are modular enough that you can jump between adventures without feeling like you’re locked into a single story. The inclusion of Edouard Guiton’s design flavor keeps things interesting and makes repeated plays feel less like a grind and more like a cinematic montage.

Replayability is buoyed by a reasonable pool of encounters and a mechanics suite that rewards strategic planning without punishing you beyond reason for a single misstep. If you’re someone who loves the moment when a plan collapses and you improvise a brilliant save, M71 will be forgiving enough to let you savor the win without forcing you into a reset of epic proportions.
