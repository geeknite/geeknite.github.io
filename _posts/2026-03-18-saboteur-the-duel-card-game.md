A duel built on misdirection, not dice, where you try to out-bluff your best friend while pretending to be a trustworthy miner. The components are compact, the turns are tight, and the twist endings arrive like a plot twist in a sci-fi sitcom: abrupt, hilarious, and usually about someone bending the rules with a smug grin. It is the kind of small-box miracle that makes you whisper, This is exactly what I wanted at this time of night, when the fridge is empty and your ego is full.

## What is Saboteur: The Duel Card Game?

### Components

- A deck of polished, slightly brittle gold-chasing cards
- Role tiles for the two players: Miner and Saboteur (with id badges that say you’re either loyal or suspicious)
- A handful of action cards that let you build, sabotage, or bluff your way to glory
- A compact rulebook that you will misread at least twice because your caffeine level is unsustainably high

The artwork leans into the original Saboteur vibe: warm dwarven tunnels, beaming gold nuggets, and enough beards to start a lava lamp collection. The components feel sturdy, the cards shuffle nicely, and the little board/mat keeps track of where you are in the tunnel-puzzle. It’s not a luxury edition by any stretch, but it exudes character and a vibe that makes you want to play again immediately so you can rub it in your friend’s face with even more flair.

### Setup

Setup is a two-step dance: (1) decide who’s mining and who’s sabotaging, and (2) shuffle the deck and deal starting cards. It’s fast, clean, and you’ll likely explode into laughter or groans during the first few exchanges as you realize the game’s tempo is built on your friend’s ability to read your eyes and your hand’s willingness to betray them with a strategically placed card.

### How to play

Saboteur: The Duel flips the script on your typical two-player card game by weaving social deduction into the core mechanics. Each turn, you draw a card, play a card, and perform a quick action -- either to push your mining plan forward or derail your opponent’s plan with a well-timed sabotage card. The tension builds as you decide whether to trust your opponent with a bridge-building card or to hold it back, just in case.

#### Core mechanics

- Role assignment: At the start, one player is the Miner and the other is the Saboteur. Roles are known to both players in this duel, so deception centers on card play and reading tells rather than hidden identities.
- Card play: You’ll have cards that either advance the excavation (e.g., build a tunnel, place a lamp to see in the dark) or sabotage (blow up a section, mislead with a fake tunnel). Timing and bluffing are everything because you’ll often have the option to take a safe move or take a risk in hopes of a dramatic payoff.
- End condition: The Miner wins when the path to gold is established and safely traversed, while the Saboteur wins when the Miner is stopped or the sabotage deck runs out and you’ve made your point about who’s in charge of the tunnel.

#### Turn structure
Each turn consists of drawing up to a certain number of cards, playing one or more cards, and then resolving any end-of-turn effects. There’s a fragile balance between planning ahead and punting to the next shuffle. Guessing games tend to explode in your face in the most entertaining way possible, and Saboteur: The Duel capitalizes on that energy. You’ll be forced to make near-sighted decisions with long-term consequences, which is basically the existential crisis of every gamer after a late-night snack.

### Strategies and tips

This is where the deck-building meets therapy session. You’ll need to learn when to bluff, when to hold back, and when to let your chin do the thinking for you. The duel is as much about psychology as it is about card-counting, which makes it feel surprisingly heavy for a compact two-player jaunt.

#### Bluffing heuristics
- Don’t always reveal your plan. If your opponent suspects you have a safe route, they’ll waste sabotage on something innocuous, enabling you to slip by with a quiet victory.
- Use misdirection: play a card that looks like a breakthrough, but is actually a decoy that distracts from your real path. In practice, it’s like sending a decoy booby to the safeness of the tunnel while you slip through the emergency exit.
- Read the tells. This is a two-player game; you’re basically playing poker with a pickaxe and a lamp. If your opponent hesitates after drawing a card, they’re likely weighing whether to pivot their plan or commit to a risky play.

#### When to trust
- If your opponent has been sabotaging aggressively, it’s a good sign they’re trying to push a false narrative. Counter with a solid, safe play that keeps you within reach of the finish line while you wait for a moment to strike.
- If your opponent erupts with a big smile after a card reveal, it might be a controlled risk -- your opportunity to seize the initiative and land a killer combo.

### Expansions and variants
This is where a lot of two-player games go to die or flourish with a wink. Saboteur: The Duel Card Game has a few neat variants that you can incorporate as you and your opponent devolve into mustache-twirling trading of grins. The expansions add new sabotage cards, trickier roles, and even a night-mode option that adds a glow-in-the-dark vibe to the tunnels. It’s not mandatory, but it does a good job of opening up more ways to sabotage your friend without it feeling repetitive.

If you’re the kind of person who wants a longer game, you can pair Saboteur: The Duel with a short accessory like the official timer or a couple of house rules that extend the duel into a proper mini-campaign. You’ll end up arguing with your friend about the rules in a loving, gamer way that makes the whole experience feel like a cozy, nerdy tradition.

### The Good, The Bad, and The Ugly of Duel
- The Good: Short play times, tight interaction, and a vibe that makes you feel like a master of deception in a dwarven mine. It’s accessible to newbies, yet there’s enough nuance for experienced players to enjoy a few rewrites of their strategy.
- The Bad: If you’re not into bluffing or reading expressions, this game might feel a bit hollow. The tension can also depend a lot on the chemistry between players; if you have a friend who never lies or a friend who lies about everything, the experience changes in unpredictable ways.
- The Ugly: The occasional fake-route card can feel unsatisfying if you’re chasing a clean victory. You’ll hear a groan or two when someone pulls off a last-minute sabotage that changes the whole narrative of the duel. It’s not dramatic in a Game of Thrones sense, but it’s dramatic enough to bring out your inner stage performer.

### Playthrough sketches
If you want a feel for how a match can unfold, here are three quick playthrough sketches that illustrate different moods:

#### Playthrough A: The Quiet Tunnel
The Miner opens with a safe build, laying a straightforward tunnel that seems robust enough to carry the weight of a small orchestra. The Saboteur counters with a subtle misdirection card, signaling the audience that danger lurks where you expect nothing. The tension pulses as both players calculate the risk of continuing the path versus forcing a daring move that could pay off or blow up in their face. By turn five, a single well-timed saboteur card turns the table, and the Miner finds themselves scrambling to salvage a path that now resembles a winding rollercoaster through uncharted caverns. You both laugh, realize you were way too confident, and plan a rematch where the same moves would fail in a completely different way.

#### Playthrough B: The Chaos Sprint
This is the opposite mood: a sprint to the finish with a flurry of build and sabotage cards. The Miner goes pedal-to-the-metal, while the Saboteur replies with a counter-punch barrage that makes you wonder if you’re playing a game of chess or a duel in a tavern with a crowd. The path becomes a cinematic montage of near-finish lines, misdirections, and last-second card draws that bend reality. The ending is less about who wins and more about who can laugh hardest at the ridiculous, improbable sequence of plays that led there.

#### Playthrough C: The Mental Marathon
Here both players slowly chip away at the path, choosing to stall, bluff, and parry. The Saboteur feigns gentleness, presenting a route that looks safe but is actually a trap. The Miner, not to be outdone, counters with a long game plan that requires careful memory and a willingness to accept small losses for a big payoff later. It becomes a battle of wills, a battle of wits, and a battle of who can keep a straight face while the other one tries to bluff an obvious bluff. In the end, the duel is decided by a single card draw that lands exactly where it should and somehow feels fair and earned.
