## How to Play (a fast refresher or the uninitiated quiz)

Sushi Go! is a drafting game where players pick one card from their hand and pass the rest to the neighbor, building a quick set of sushi combinations for points. In the Collectors Tin edition, the mechanics are the same, but everything shows up in a more premium production. If you know the base game, the move here is to skim the cards like a hungry cat with a Bluetooth-enabled appetite: you want to snag the best combo while leaving your neighbor with a less-than-satisfying option.

### Turn structure and flow
- At the start of a round, each player is dealt a hand of five cards (or adjusted numbers if house rules apply).
- You choose one card from your hand and place it face-down on the table, then pass the remaining cards to the left.
- Everyone reveals their chosen cards simultaneously and adds them to their personal sushi collections for score at the round’s end.
- Rounds repeat until all cards have been drafted.
- After all rounds, you tally the points from your combinations (nigiri, sashimi, maki, dumplings, etc.). The highest total wins.

The core strategy is simple: chase the highest-scoring combinations early, but don’t ignore the potential of mid-round traffic to block your opponents. The Collectors Tin doesn’t change the math; it just makes the decision to take a card feel a smidge more dramatic because you’re deciding if the carrot sushi is worth risking your entire round’s potential. The game’s pace is brisk, typically finished within 15-20 minutes, which means you’ll get several plays in back-to-back before you realize you’ve been emotionally invested in a tiny cardboard bowl of arroz con nigiri.

To set expectations: Sushi Go! in any form benefits from a light touch and a little bit of chaotic assembly. The Collectors Tin adds shine but not bloat; it’s still the same core experience—fast, friendly, and whip-smart in a tiny box.

### Turn-by-turn sample (two rounds, simplified)
- Round 1: You look at your five cards and decide to take the maki roll that will give you a big score if you get a long chain, but you know your neighbor also loves maki and might nab the short stack you were eyeing. You pick, place it down, and pass.
- Round 2: The table heats up as everyone snatches the sashimi and dumplings to balance out scores. You time your dumplings to catch two or three for a nice fixed reward, hoping to offset a poor maki run you risked earlier.

The joy in this isn’t a grand strategy; it’s a mini-skrm (scream-ler) of choices: which card to take, which to pass, and how to keep your hand from turning into a chaotic scrapheap where you can’t find the card you were hoping for. It’s the kind of game that rewards several plays in a night because you quickly learn which opponents have the go-to curves for scoring and which players still count points with their toes.
