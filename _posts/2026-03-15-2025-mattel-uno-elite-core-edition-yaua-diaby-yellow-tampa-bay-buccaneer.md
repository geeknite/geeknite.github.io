## Gameplay Mechanics: Power Tokens, Elite Cards, and Degenerate Strategy
Let’s talk brass tacks: how does Elite Core actually change how you play UNO? In a word: it adds a layer of strategy without turning children into desert-dried philosophers waiting for a single “Skip” card to decide their fate.

### Core Rules Refresher
If you know UNO, you know the baseline: match color or number, play a Wild card to change color, deal with Draw cards, and try not to be the last person left holding a bunch of cards when you yell UNO. The Elite Core Edition adds three new layers:

- Power Tokens: Earn tokens by meeting certain in-game conditions (e.g., calling UNO exactly, chaining a specific number of compatible color plays, or successfully playing a Power Card). Tokens can be spent to activate elite options on your turn.
- Elite Core Cards: These are special action cards that resemble standard UNO functions but pack a diplomatic: “Negotiate” a color change with another player, “Lockdown” (skip someone’s play for a round), or “Swap Hands” (swap hands with a chosen opponent). The timing and strategy around these cards require you to read the room as if you’re plotting a heist while on a coffee binge.
- YaYa Diaby Flavor Cards: Small trinkets that grant tiny bonuses or penalties depending on the state of the game. They are not mandatory to use, but they add flavor and a little extra spice for the folks who like to narrate the game as if they’re sports commentators.

### Power Tokens: The Social Credit System of UNO
Power Tokens are the big novelty here. They function as a pool of “cool stuff” you can call on when the timing is right. For instance, you might spend a token to:

- Force a color change for yourself or for a target player.
- Cancel an opponent’s action card for one turn (within a limited window).
- Draw an extra card but immediately play a matching card to reduce risk in a dramatic, sword-swinging style.

Or you might save them for late-game moments when you’re staring down a wall of cards and the crowd is two “Draw Four” away from chaos. The tokens add a resource-management angle that makes late-game decisions more meaningful than simply “hope to draw a green card.”

### Elite Core Cards: The Spice That Doesn’t Break the Doorknob
The Elite Core cards come with unique text that governs their use. They won’t overhaul UNO’s core rules, but they offer tactical choices that reward careful timing. Some examples (paraphrased for flavor) include:

- Swap Hands: You and a chosen opponent exchange decks, potentially turning a lead into a disaster or vice versa.
- Force Swap: Pick a color, and everyone with that color must discard a card—fun when you want to clear a dangerous color from the table.
- Color Lock: Lock a color for two turns, making players’ color choices more predictable and, frankly, more annoying if you enjoy strategic micromanagement of your friends’ mistakes.

These cards require you to be mindful of when the group is enjoying the chaos vs. when they’re quietly plotting a revenge-run of their own. The balance feels right: enough influence to keep the game engaging, but not so much that it becomes a puzzle-hellscape where you need a manual to finish a single round.

## How to Play It at Home: A Quick-Start Guide for The Busy Geek
If you’re itching to start playing right now, here’s a compact starter guide to help you avoid the “Why are we fighting over a blue 7 again?” moment.

1) Set up: Shuffle the Elite Core deck with the standard UNO deck. Deal seven cards to each player. Place Power Tokens within reach. Decide who will start as the dealer.
2) Start playing: The earliest rounds use the familiar UNO rules. Match color or number, or play a Wild card to change color.
3) Introduce Elite Core cards: When you draw an Elite Core card, read the brief instruction aloud and apply its effect. If the rule requires a target, you choose a player with the least strategic advantage, or the one who has just shouted the loudest insult about your deck-building skills.
4) Use Power Tokens: Earn tokens by meeting simple triggers (like declaring UNO correctly or successfully chaining a color run). Save them for big moments when you want to steer the game toward your advantage.
