## Safety and ASTM Certification

This section digs into safety. ASTM certification is not a magic shield; it’s a baseline. The 7' trampoline typically includes padding over springs or springsless design, a safety enclosure net, and a non-slip ladder. But kids can be unpredictable—think sliding with a ball pit ball. Always place on a flat, non-slip surface and keep a supervising adult present.

From the safety box to your living room: ensure the enclosure net is intact. The mesh should be tight and without holes. If a ball slips through, or your toddler discovers Step 731: “Joke about the trampoline’s bounce,” you’ve likely found your problem. The recommended age range is typically 3–10 years, with weight limits around 100–150 pounds per user depending on exact model. Use your best judgment and check the manufacturer’s manual.

For safety nerds: read the official guidelines on trampoline safety here: https://www.cpsc.gov/safety-guidelines/trampolines. It’s not the most fun read, but it will save you from future regret. And if you want to compare with a more robust setup,{% post_url 'trampoline-safety-101' %} will help.

## Indoor vs Outdoor Use

One of the big questions: can this be used indoors? Yes, but only if you have a large enough space. A 7' trampoline requires clearance around and above; we’re not just talking about floor space but ceiling height as well. In a lot of apartments, a 7' unit will fit in a dedicated playroom or basement with enough headroom for a few bounces and a heroic victory dance. The slide could be a nuisance if placed near a wall, so consider relocating a few furniture pieces before assembly.

Deal with floor type: ensure you have a non-slip, non-carpet surface or add a protective mat underneath. If indoor, keep the area well-ventilated; kids bounce with all the energy of a caffeinated hamster. Outdoor use invites weather, which is both a blessing and a hazard. The frame coating will help, but you’ll still want to bring it inside during heavy rain or high wind to prolong its life.

## Assembly Tips and Maintenance

- Start with the frame: lay out the pieces and confirm you have all the necessary hardware. The manual helps; the warranty appreciates it.
- Use a helper: the assembly is a two-person job if you want to avoid wobbles and extra blisters.
- Safety first: pad the frame edges before you attach the jump mat; do not skip padding or you’ll regret it when someone’s knee meets metal.
- Tighten all bolts to the recommended torque; looseness leads to wobble and eventual creaks that make your neighbors call about noise.
- Check regularly: tighten bolts, inspect the springs, check the enclosure net. It’s not glamorous, but it’s essential.
- Cleaning: wipe down with mild soap and water; avoid harsh chemicals. The last thing you want is a chemical smell polluting your cool kids’ jam sessions.
