## Unboxing Experience and Assembly Tips

### Quick Assembly Tips

Most miniatures come on sprues that require snipping and perhaps a bit of trimming. Here are a few practical tips:
- Use a sharp hobby knife to carefully snip each limb and torso—don’t rush, or you’ll nick a joint you didn’t mean to touch.
- Clean up mold lines with a fine file or sandpaper; a little effort here pays off in cleaner armor and skin textures.
- If you’re planning to swap heads or limbs for customization, layout your pieces before glue to ensure balance and poseability.
- Prime before painting. Neutral gray or white primer will give you better color pull and easier shading.

### Storage and Organization

When you’re done painting, store the miniatures in a foam tray or compartmentalized case. The zombie horde is best managed with distinct trays per sculpt to prevent mix-ups and to keep track of which mutations you want to pull in a given mission.

## Painting Guide: Quick-start or Masterclass

### Quick Paint Scheme (Starter Friendly)

- Base coat: neutral gray or tan for skin, browns for clothing.
- Mid-tone: add a hint of green or blue to the flesh for a sickly look.
- Shading: a thin wash of dark brown or blue-purple into recesses.
- Highlight: edge highlight with a lighter skin tone on raised features.
- Finish: subtle dry-brush on clothing for texture, a touch of blood tone on exposed wounds.

### Advanced Techniques

If you’re chasing a more cinematic vibe, try gradient skin tones from gray-green to pale to bone, add selective metallic blood splatter for effect, and use matte varnish on non-blood areas to control shine. A gloss wash on exposed gore can give a wet, visceral look that reads well on camera.

### Thematic Suggestions

Use color palettes to match campaign factions or scenario mood. A city-wide outbreak might feature bleached gray skin and soot-stained clothing, while a lab outbreak could lean toward pale, jaundiced skin and sterile, blood-splattered lab coats—gives you a distinctive look for each encounter.
