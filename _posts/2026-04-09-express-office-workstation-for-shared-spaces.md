## Design details and specs
Here’s the low-down on what you get and what you don’t:

- Frame and surface: Steel frame, laminated top, and optional privacy panels. The top is sized at approximately 48 inches by 26 inches, which is roomy enough for a laptop, a monitor, and a cup of questionable office coffee without turning the desk into a two-person project.
- Height range: The primary desk surface ranges from roughly 28 inches to 46 inches high, enough to satisfy short folks, tall folks, and the occasional giraffe desk assistant who wears heels and claims knees as a service accessory.
- Weight capacity: The tabletop supports a typical dual-monitor setup plus peripherals with ample margin. If you’re mounting a small tower PC under the desk, you’ll want to check the clearance, but the design generally accommodates a standard PC case with a monitor arm combo.
- Cable management: A built-in channel and grommets help direct cables where you want them. It’s not magic, but it’s close—no more combat with a nest of cables taking your chair hostage.
- Privacy and collaboration: Optional privacy panels offer acoustic dampening and visual separation. They’re not soundproof booths, but they do a decent job of saying, “I am in a meeting with my spreadsheet.”
- Attachments: The system supports clamp-on monitor arms and a keyboard tray. The arms are adjustable for tilt, rotation, and height—great for multi-user days when someone wants a portrait mode monitor and someone else wants a landscape view for a spread of code.
- Mobility: While not a rolling desk in the sense of a full-on mobile workstation, the Express is designed for reconfiguration without brute force. The energy of moving desks in a shared space is part of the charm; this unit tries to minimize the chiropractic equivalent—aching back and a chair-ballet aftermath.
- Assembly: It’s not IKEA-level cryptic, but you’ll want to budget some time and have a friend or coworker around who doesn’t fear a hex wrench.

In shared spaces, ergonomic comfort is currency. The Express Office Workstation shines here by providing a flexible platform to adjust for different user heights and preferences. A few ergonomics notes:

- Monitor position: With compatible monitor arms, you can set monitor height and distance to reduce neck strain. The goal is to avoid the “upward glance from a reclining posture” style of work.
- Sit/stand rhythm: The range considers a typical office worker who alternates between typing and brainstorming. If you’re one of those people who forget to stand until 3 PM, the Express doesn’t nag you, but it does politely encourage a reminder.
- Privacy panels and acoustic feel: They help create a sense of personal space in an open layout. It’s not a cloister, but in a two-on-two stand-up meeting, you won’t hear the guy next to you debating the optimal nap time.
