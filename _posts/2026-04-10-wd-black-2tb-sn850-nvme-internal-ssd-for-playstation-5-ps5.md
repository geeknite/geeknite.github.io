## What is the WD_BLACK SN850?
WD_BLACK is Western Digital’s premium line of NVMe SSDs built for speed and gaming endurance. The SN850 is the non-X version of their high-end Gen4 drive, designed to squeeze as much speed as possible from PCIe 4.0 without breaking the bank. It’s available in several capacities, including 1TB, 2TB, and (in some markets) 4TB. The 2TB model is the sweet spot for most PS5 gamers: enough space to stash a decent library, decent sustained performance, and a price tag that won’t require you to remortgage your house.

The SN850 uses TLC NAND and a controller to deliver impressive sequential read speeds around 7,000 MB/s and write speeds in the 5,000 MB/s range, depending on capacity and cooling. In other words: it’s fast enough to feel like you’ve upgraded to near-SSD-console speed without actually buying a new console. For PS5 players, a fast internal SSD translates to quicker game installs, smaller texture streaming hiccups, and shorter load times between zones. It’s the spice rack you place next to your main dish, not the entire kitchen overhaul.

## PS5 compatibility and heatsink considerations
PS5 is famously fussy about M.2 drives because of thermals. The console’s internal storage management can throttle performance if the drive overheats, which is why Sony requires a heatsink on most M.2 NVMe drives or an actual PS5-approved setup. The WD_BLACK SN850 2TB is absolutely included in the care package of drives that can be used with the PS5, but you must pair it with an appropriate heatsink to ensure sustained performance.

### Heatsink vs non-heatsink: does it matter?
- Heatsink models: The heatsink is a bulky little chunk of metal that acts as a radiator. It keeps the SSD cool under heavy gaming loads and helps avoid thermal throttling. If you’re buying the 2TB SN850 for PS5, you’ll want a heatsink-equipped version or attach a separate heatsink. The PS5’s internal slot has plenty of space for a slim, integrated heatsink unit.
- Non-heatsink: You might come across a version without a heatsink. Those are cheaper upfront, but you’ll need to shop for a compatible heatsink if you plan to use it in a PS5. Mixing a bare drive with a heatsink could be a win, but it’s a dance you’ll want to choreograph carefully.

Thermals aren’t just about “the thing feels warm.” Sustained gaming sessions can cause thermal throttling that chips away at your real-world performance. The SN850’s thermal design, when paired with a heatsink, tends to behave nicely in the PS5’s internal environment. Still, if you push the drive to its limits with long gaming marathons, expect temperatures to climb and speeds to settle into a stable plateau rather than a sprinting firehose.

Endurance matters when you’re gaming for hours on end and updating patches that rewrite the same textures a dozen times.
