## The Baraja Española: numbers, suits, and face cards
### Suits and ranks
The Baraja Española features four suits: oros (gold coins), copas (cups), espadas (swords), and bastos (clubs). The ranks go 1 through 7, and then 10 through 12 for the face cards, but the naming varies a little by region and game. In many core games, the 8 and 9 are omitted, which gives the deck a distinct rhythm compared to the 52-card French system you’ll see in most American poker rooms. The ATB set sticks to the recognizable look of the Spanish suits with bold imagery and clean typography on the number cards. The court cards—Sota, Caballo, and Rey—carry the classic dignified look with decorative borders that whisper “old world gaming connoisseur” without needing a monocle.

### The jacks, knights, and kings
If you’re into the lore, the Caballo (knight) is a beloved figure in Spanish-playing culture, symbolizing chivalry and theatrical duel scenes. The Sota (jack) is agile and cunning, often playing a pivotal role in the scoring dynamics of Mus and other traditional games. The Rey (king) is regal and reserved, a reminder that you’re playing with cards that have a social as well as a gaming history. This three-deck configuration amplifies the drama because you have more Reys and Sotas to stake your fate on, or to curry favor with during a heated Brisca bidding duel.

### How to handle three decks at the table
Shuffling can become a bit of a party trick with three decks. Here are a few practical tips:
- Break it down: Shuffle two decks together, then merge the third in a controlled riffle. You’ll preserve card orientation and reduce the rate of misdeals.
- Use a card shuffler if your table is big or your hands are small. It’s not cheating; it’s tactical efficiency.
- For solo practice, practice shuffles with one deck first, then attempt a three-deck cascade. If you can handle the cascade, you’re ready for a living-room tournament.
- When dealing, consider dealing out from the top of the stack in a standard clockwise fashion to keep the flow predictable for everyone at the table.

### Handling etiquette and rhythm
Three decks increase the tempo of the night. People will demand “un nuevo mazo” after a tense hand, or declare a round over with a flourish that would make a magician jealous.
