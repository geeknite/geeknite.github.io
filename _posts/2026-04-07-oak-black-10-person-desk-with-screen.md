The typical footprint hovers around a long rectangular slab, roughly 10–12 feet in length (about 300–365 cm) and 30–34 inches deep (76–86 cm). This is not a desk; it’s a city block for your crew. ## Ergonomics and Comfort: Sit Up, Stand Out, Stay for a While

Ten people around a single surface presents ergonomic challenges. The top priority is to prevent arm cannons from colliding when someone stretches to reach the central display. The desk is typically paired with adjustable chairs and sometimes a modest height adjustability for the entire bench, enabling a few users to stand while others sit. The goal is to accommodate a variety of body types, from the spry project manager to the cream-joneled developer who spends more time code-surfing than socializing.

Key ergonomic considerations include:
- Keyboard and mouse placement for all ten seats. This usually means a narrow strip with central alignment and slightly recessed areas, so wrists don’t feel like they’re auditioning for a gymnastic team.
- Monitor height and distance. With screen arms running the length, you can set the top of each screen to roughly eye level for seated participants. For standing participants, you might need to provide some monitor tilting options or a quick desk height adjustment to avoid neck strain.
- Arm and elbow clearance. The central area should allow comfortable elbow movement without bumping into the person next to you or the stand-up desk version of a medieval jousting tournament.

Aesthetically, the oak top warms the room and the black edges give it a sense of grounded, confident design. In terms of user experience, the true test is whether people feel they can collaborate without feeling crowded, while also maintaining a sense of personal space for those moments when you need to pretend you’re listening to a long stand-up meeting and instead are planning your next snack run.

## Assembly, Setup, and the Great Hardware Hunt

As with most large, high-end furniture, the Oak Black 10 Person Desk with Screen ships in multiple boxes and arrives as a puzzle that tests both your handyman prowess and your soul’s tolerance for Allen wrenches. The good news: instruction manuals usually come with clear diagrams, color-coded hardware, and minimal jargon. The bad news: you will use more time than you think you should, you’ll swear you’ll never work with wood again, and you’ll discover you’ve started a small woodshop in your garage after your neighbor asks for help assembling a coffee cart.

Typical steps include:
- Verifying you have all components and hardware. It’s surprising how often the long, slim screws decide to hide under a label on the bottom of a box.
- Attaching legs, stages for screens, and any central cable troughs. Expect a few dozen fasteners; enjoy the satisfying clack of washers meeting wood.
- Installing screens and monitor arms. This is the moment where you realize your coworker’s keyboard is a little too loud, and the screens need to be perfectly horizontal to avoid audience fatigue during long demos.
- Cable management and power setup. A tidy desk is a sign of a peaceful mind; a chaotic desk is a sign of a mind that’s already ahead three projects and a coffee stain on the calendar.

If you’re working in a collaborative environment, consider staging a dedicated installation day with your IT team. The integration of power outlets, USB-C hubs, and HDMI matrices can be as complex as configuring a home theater system. The reward, though, is a clean, elegant solution that reduces desk clutter and increases the probability that your team will actually find the right cable without calling the help desk in a panic.

### Maintenance and Longevity
Oak is durable, but not immortal. Wipe the surface with a soft, dry microfiber and avoid harsh chemicals that could strip the finish. A monthly conditioning oil can keep the wood looking vibrant, while the black edges should be inspected for chips or wear. The screen system, with its moving parts, benefits from occasional lubrication and checks to ensure monitor arms stay balanced. If a panel begins to sag or a cable tray loses its grip, address it sooner rather than later—ten people noticing a wobble is not ideal for morale.
