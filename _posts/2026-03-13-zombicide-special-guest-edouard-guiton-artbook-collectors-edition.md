## Edouard Guiton: The artist behind the brushstroke revolution

Edouard Guiton is the guest whose presence elevates the project from “pretty art book” to “must have for the connoisseur of chaos.” His style—bold lines, expressive posture work, and a color language that can swing from neon to sepia in a single panel—gives Zombicide a distinct voice. If you’ve played the games and flipped through the base art book, you know that Guiton’s work steps out of the shadows, introduces personality into the undead, and makes every zombie design feel like a character with a backstory you’d actually want to read.

## Inside the pages: artwork, process, and a peek behind the curtain

The art book is arranged with a deliberate rhythm that feels like a well-paced campaign. You get a mix of concept art, interim sketches, color explorations, and final renders. The concept art reveals the bones of the characters—the skeletons of silhouettes you’ll recognize during a late-night solo play or at a crowded convention hall. The process pages are sprinkled with marginal notes that read as if Edouard is whispering into your ear, explaining why a certain zombie’s jawline needed extra menace or why the greens chosen for a particular faction pop under LED lighting.

One standout sequence tracks how a simple silhouette morphs into a fully realized character. You’ll see the initial blob of shapes, the exploration of silhouettes against grim backdrops, and the choices that lead to a final design that can be printed at poster scale without losing a single ounce of charm. The color palette section is a masterclass in how to evoke mood with color. The undead are not just green or gray; they are mood swings with bones—chilly blues for night shifts, sickly yellows for disease haze, and the occasional splash of hot magenta that says hey, we’re still having fun here.

There are pages where Edouard’s line work feels almost calligraphic, a reminder that sometimes the best zombie is the one that looks like it could have walked out of an after-hours mural. For fans who appreciate the craft, these pages are the good stuff: you can trace how heavy black lines create weight, how negative space carves out fear, and how tiny details like rib shadows or bone cracks carry a surprising amount of character.


## The art book in practice: layout, typography, and the reading experience

Layout is where art books either shine or stumble. In this edition, the layout respects the art. The pages breathe; the margins provide room for your eyes to rest after a sprint through a storm of zombie gore. Typography stays legible but playful enough to remind you that you are not just reading a field guide to the undead; you are riding a guided rollercoaster through a creative process. The inclusion of Edouard’s own notes—brief, insightful, occasionally witty—gives a voice to the images, turning pages into a conversation rather than a monologue.

The balance between image and caption matters here. Some publishers lean too far into show-and-tell, offering extended essays that flatten the energy of a painting. This book does the opposite: it provides concise captions and context, letting the artwork do most of the talking while the occasional note hints at the drama behind the scene. If you are the kind of reader who loves to pore over brushstrokes and lighting decisions, you’ll savor the micro-essays tucked at the bottom of some pages. If you’re more of a skim-reader, you’ll still get the gist without feeling overwhelmed.
