## Overview

Saboteur: The Lost Mines is a social-deduction, put-tunnel-down card game with a modular mine-building twist. The core premise remains faithful to the Saboteur formula: you’re a dwarf prospector, or you’re secretly not, and your job is to lead your team to a treasure while sabotaging the other team—without tipping your hand too early. The Lost Mines introduces a few spicy tweaks to the base game’s architecture: a modular mine layout, new roles and action cards, and a few clever rule shuffles that keep the game feeling fresh even after your tenth playthrough. It’s fast, it’s chaotic, and it’s the kind of game where you’ll cheer for a tunnel that almost works and groan at a tunnel that clearly does not.

The tone is light, the artwork is cheeky, and the tension arises not from heavy strategizing but from the social chemistry around the table. If you’ve run a game night with “just one more round” energy, Saboteur: The Lost Mines will slip right into that groove and then sprint past it to the snack table, only to return with another round and another plot twist.

## What is Saboteur: The Lost Mines?

At its heart, The Lost Mines is a diamond-dusted variant of the original Saboteur. Players take on roles that remain hidden for most of the game: miners who want to get to the treasure, and saboteurs who want to derail the path to treasure for the other side. The Lost Mines adds a modular board aspect via mine tiles that players collectively piece together, revealing a path toward the goal. This board-element layer increases replayability because each game can present a different topology—limiting the predictability that sometimes plagues card-only games.

One of the clever design choices is that the game doesn’t rely on a single victory condition. You can win by genuinely guiding your team to the treasure, or you can win by successfully tricking other players into pursuing a fictitious route. The balance of information and misdirection makes every round feel like a fresh episode of a prank show, with enough strategic underpinnings to keep heavy gamers satisfied but enough chaotic energy to keep casual players smiling.

## Components and Setup

- Modular mine tiles that form a connecting grid; the mine tiles come with built-in pathways and sometimes traps or bonuses.
- A deck of action cards that include build, dig, move, and sabotage options.
- Role cards that determine who is secretly a miner and who is sabotaging.
- Gold tokens and claim cards that indicate which team has reached the treasure and how much gold is claimed.
- Player aids and reference cards to remind you of what you can do on your turn.

Setup is straightforward but satisfying. Shuffle the mine tiles face down and lay out the starting area. Shuffle the action deck and deal a handful to each player. Everyone draws a role card (hidden). The Lost Mines’ modular approach means the table layout will be different every time, which is perfect for those who like to brag about “your mine is worse than mine” without actually lying—just a little white fibbing around table talk.

## Gameplay Mechanics: The Core Loop

The Lost Mines preserves the essential tempo of Saboteur: you play cards to move tiles, claim paths, and lay down tunnels, all while you’re trying to deduce who among you is rooting for the treasure and who is rooting for the path to collapse. Here’s a rough breakdown of how a typical round flows:

- setup phase: reveal your role, lay out the starting tile, shuffle decks, deal cards.
- action phase: on your turn you do one of several actions: build a tunnel (lay a tile that extends the mine), dig (remove or reposition threats or obstacles), place a tool card that helps you or hinders others, or drop a sabotage card to derail someone else’s plan.
- path reveal: as you connect more mine tiles, you progressively reveal potential routes to the treasure. Some routes will be blocked by traps or misdirected by saboteurs who have a nasty sense of humor.
- end condition: a team reaches the treasure and claims gold, or the last few tiles force a stalemate where nobody can salvage a winning route. In either case, scoring tallies the amount of gold secured and the sanity points preserved around the table.

The Lost Mines does a nice job of keeping the timing tight. Games run roughly 20 to 40 minutes depending on the table size and how cutthroat your group wants to be. If you’ve played other quick social deduction games, you’ll feel right at home with the pace and the occasional “how did you see that path?” moment that makes players gasp and then snort with laughter.

## Strategy and Tactics: Reading the Table Like a Map

Despite the lighthearted presentation, there’s genuine strategy to be found. The Lost Mines rewards players who balance deception with timing. Here are a few guidelines we recommend testing at your table:

- Play your role with discipline. The moment you reveal you’re a saboteur, teammates will adjust their trust. If you’re a saboteur, you’ll want to sow misdirection early but avoid being so obvious that you’re picked off in the first round.
- Build with intent. When you place a mine tile, think about future connections as well as present gains. A well-placed tile can open a path that looks promising but is actually a trap set for a rival team.
- Sabotage strategically. Don’t push sabotage too early; you’ll give away your identity and waste a valuable resource. Instead, deploy sabotage to create roadblocks that force opponents into less favorable choices when they’re most vulnerable.
- Read feeds of information. The art on cards, the way people talk about their choices, and even the order in which players draw cards all convey micro-signals. The Lost Mines thrives on social interaction—if your group is quiet, you’ll miss a lot of the fun.
- Endgame timing. As the mine tiles start to reveal a path to the gold, you’ll feel pressure to accelerate or slow down the route. Use that pressure to misdirect others or to force the conclusion before you’re found out.

For those who enjoy deeper strategy, there’s a satisfying tension between the immediate payoff of completing a route and the risk of tipping your opponents off. It’s not purely luck; skilled players can influence outcomes through careful tile placement and timely play of action cards.

## The Lost Mines vs Base Saboteur: A Quick Comparison

- Theme and tone: Both games lean into humor and misdirection; The Lost Mines leans slightly more on a puzzle-like, route-building vibe due to the modular tiles.
- Setup and table presence: The modular layout adds variety every time you sit down; base Saboteur remains card-driven with fewer physical tiles to shuffle.
- Player interaction: Both rely on social deduction, but The Lost Mines encourages more direct table talk as players discuss possible routes and motives around the same tunnel sketch.
- Replayability: The Lost Mines edges out a bit due to layout variability; base Saboteur is great but can feel a touch predictable after several plays.

If your game shelf has both, you’ll likely rotate between them to keep your evenings fresh. If you’re choosing one to start with, The Lost Mines is a strong all-around pick for a mixed group that enjoys both light strategy and social bluffing.
