## How to play
The Game Face To Face is designed to be fast and forgiving, with a flow that can handle a late-night fever dream of a gaming session or a casual brunch that spirals into improvised theatre. Here’s the gist:

- The game supports 2–6 players (though we recommend at least 3 for truly theatrical results). Each round features a designated judge who rotates clockwise.
- On a turn, a player draws two cards from the action deck and may play one card to trigger a challenge or response. The goal is to build the most convincing or entertaining facial expression to match the prompt of the round.
- The judge draws a prompt card and announces the challenge. Examples include “Show your smugest smug” or “Imitate your favorite robot who just found a banana.”
- Players respond by using their face cards and the drawn action cards to tailor their expression. The judge decides which player delivered the most convincing performance.
- The winner receives a smirk chip. The first to collect a set number of smirk chips wins the game. In practice, most groups end up playing for a handful of rounds and a lot of laughs, which is absolutely fine.

Turn structure is straightforward, which makes group management easier than teaching your cat to fetch your snack. There’s room for improvisation, but the game doesn’t demand you to become a professional actor. It revels in the spirit of “perform a little, joke a lot, and don’t take the blame for the bad lighting.”

## Mechanics and strategy
This game isn’t about deep strategic complexity or math-heavy calculations. It’s about reading the room, delivering a moment that lands, and gracefully accepting that someone will out-sass you with a glint in their eye. The main strategic levers are:

- Timing: Knowing when to unleash your best look is often the difference between a three-card round and a legendary moment that becomes local folklore.
- Group dynamics: If you have a table full of improv veterans, the game accelerates into rapid-fire performance. A quieter group might lean more into exaggerated expressions and the drama of a theatrical pause.
- Card synergy: Some action cards pair with certain prompts better than others. The joy is in discovering a subtle combo that makes everyone lose their composure just enough to mis-signal the judge.

In practice, the best players aren’t always the ones who can contort their face the most. They’re the ones who can pace their expressions, unleash a surprising twist, and then hand the round to a friend who delivers a perfectly timed line. It is, in a sense, a test of presence rather than raw acting chops.
