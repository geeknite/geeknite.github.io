## Overview

Sushi Go! is a fast, family-friendly card drafting game designed for 2 to 5 players and rated for ages 8 and up. The premise is adorably simple: you assemble a pseudo conveyor belt of sushi by drafting cards that grant points in various ways. The clever twist is that every round you pass your hand to the left, which means you have to predict not only what you will collect but what your neighbor will pick in the next pass. The game rewards speed, memory, and a touch of strategic mischief without devolving into serious math, which is exactly the vibe most households crave after a long day of chores.

The packaging in cellophane is a small ritual in itself. You peel it away, reveal a neat stack of glossy cards, and you can tell almost immediately that this is not a game that pretends to be bigger than it is. The aesthetic is bright, friendly, and absolutely designed to evoke a bustling sushi bar where the seats are a tad wobbly and the conversations are louder than the koi in the pond outside.

### The box contents
- 108 sushi cards across a variety of categories (Nigiri, Maki, Sashimi, Dumplings, Tamago, Inari, and more). Each card type has its own value or scoring condition, and the interactions between types are what make the game interesting.
- A tidy score sheet or an optional scoring tracker depending on edition. Some versions use tiny tokens; others rely on pencil marks.
- A compact rule sheet that covers the basics and then points you toward the more interesting decisions.
- A handful of quick reference cards per player that summarize scoring rules in a single glance.
- The box itself, designed to fit comfortably on most tables without hogging the entire dining room.

## How to play
Sushi Go! is deceptively simple. The core mechanic is drafting: you pick one card from your hand and pass the rest to the left. You repeat this until all cards are claimed. The cards you collect flow into your personal score pile, where they will later become points or set up combos that can swing the game in your favor.

### Setup and flow
- Shuffle the deck and deal the appropriate number of cards to each player. The exact number depends on player count; in typical 2- to 5-player games you will have a consistent hand size each round.
- Each turn, choose one card from your hand to keep and place it face down in front of you. Pass the remaining cards to the player on your left.
- When all cards in the round are claimed, tally scores for that round and discard played cards. Repeat for the next round with a fresh hand.
- After the final round, tally pudding cards for a final burst of points that can make the difference between victory and a tasty failure.

### Scoring highlights
- Nigiri cards score fixed points, but you can double their value if you pair them with Wasabi (a separate card that will amplify the value of your next Nigiri you place with Wasabi).
- Maki cards grant points to the player with the most or second-most Maki cards during that round.
- Tamago cards grant points based on how many eggs you collect across the round, with a predictable ramp up.
- Dumplings scale with how many you collect; more dumplings can yield a surprisingly chunky payoff later in the game.
- Pudding cards count at the end of the game and can tilt the final tally, especially if the lead is slender.

### A quick round walkthrough
Imagine a three-player table. You receive a hand of 10 cards. You pick one card that will advance your plan for a potential big score, then pass the rest to your left. Your neighbor looks at their hand and chooses a card that might disrupt your plan or help their own. The rest of the table follows suit, and before you know it, a mini-drama unfolds as everyone tries to predict who will end up with the most Nigiri or the clever combination that makes Wasabi sing. The rounds fly by; you’ll feel that rush of “did I just draft a near-perfect set?” followed by the sweet sting of “no, they just swiped the Wasabi on the last second.”

## Strategy and tactics
The beauty of Sushi Go! lies in the delicate balance between greed and foresight. Here are some practical guidelines that tend to help most tables befriend the high-score gods rather than anger them:
- Early reliability: Identify cards that have consistent value across rounds. For example, some Nigiri variants are always solid if you can pick them early and protect them with Wasabi later.
- Mid-round blocking: Don’t be afraid to take a card that would logically bolster a neighbor’s plan if it prevents their big payoff. The table will forgive you if you win a skirmish by keeping your leads intact.
- End-game awareness: Pudding can decide a tight game. If you’re behind, prioritize pudding cards in the final rounds to claw back or narrow the gap.
- Card flow reading: Since you see a significant portion of your opponents’ hands as you pass, you can infer what they are likely to pick next turn and time your own saves accordingly.
- Adaptability: If a critical card disappears from the deck, be ready to switch gears. The best players do not fall in love with a single strategy; they let it evolve round to round.

## Accessibility and scalability
Sushi Go! is very approachable for younger players while still offering enough depth to be satisfying for adults. It scales smoothly from 2 to 5 players, though the dynamics shift with each addition or subtraction in player count. In larger groups, the social interaction explodes in a friendly, chaotic way that is often exactly what you are hoping for on a game night. In smaller groups, the pacing becomes more controlled, which can be perfect for a quick, crisp session.

## Replayability and variations
The base game is a compact engine that lends itself to quick plays again and again. If you want to expand the shelf, Sushi Go Party! offers a larger, modular experience with more card variety and more scoring options, letting you tailor the experience for different groups. If you want to bring a twist to a session, consider house rules like a quota for certain card types in a round, or a rule that makes pudding more valuable earlier to keep pressure on late in the game. The nature of the drafting mechanic means even small changes in rules can produce a surprising shift in strategy and a fresh, dynamic feel.
