## Assembly and Setup
### Unboxing and initial impressions
When the box arrives, you’ll be tempted to adopt a “let’s just skip to the good part” attitude. Don’t. The assembly is typically straightforward: legs, table top, crossbars, and a handful of screws. You’ll want a clean workspace, a chilly beverage, and perhaps a friend who owes you a favor. The good news is that the instructions are usually clear enough for a weekend DIY hero to conquer without a degree in engineering.

### Time and tools
Most people can assemble the set in 1–2 hours, depending on whether your cat helps or sabotages your screwdriver. Basic tools—screwdrivers, an Allen wrench, and perhaps a rubber mallet—are enough. Don’t be tempted to over-tighten: wood can crack, and a stubborn bolt deserves mercy. Pro tip: lay out the parts first, then match each piece to its label like a logic puzzle for adults.

### Practical tips for assembly
- Check all pieces for minor shipping dings before you begin; a quick touch-up with wood wax can hide small scratches.
- Pre-drill holes if your kit lacks them, and avoid cross-threading by threading by hand at first.
- Allow the cushions to air out after unboxing to reduce that factory-offgassing smell. It’s not dangerous, just a little nose-tickling.

## Durability and Weather Resistance
### Exposure tests in theory
Outdoor furniture is an investment in your future outdoor hangouts, and part of that investment is trusting the elements to behave themselves. Acacia wood, when properly maintained, resists warping and has natural oils that help it survive sunlight and rain. Rattan weaving adds a layer of visual texture but can demand extra care to avoid sun bleaching and moisture damage. The cushions add comfort but can soak up rain like little sponges if left unmoved.

### Practical maintenance tips
- Apply a protective sealant or oil on the wood every 6–12 months to maintain color and water resistance.
- Cover the set when not in use or store cushions indoors during heavy rain or winter seasons.
- Clean the cushions with mild soap and water; avoid harsh chemicals that can degrade fabric over time.
