## Introduction: The Plague, The Princes, and The Two Dorks Who Won’t Stop Talking

If you’ve spent any amount of time wandering the apocalyptic bazaars of tabletop gaming, you’ve probably heard the name Zombicide. It’s the kind of game that makes you feel like you’ve eaten too much budget chocolate and then decided to punch a zombie in the face with a spoonful of medieval tech—it's chaotic, colorful, and somehow manages to feel epic without requiring a PhD in undead etiquette. Zombicide Black Plague is the medieval-flavored cousin that wears a hooded cloak and smells vaguely like roasted goblin and engine oil.

Enter Gilbert and Mortimer, two survivors who apparently majored in sarcasm and alchemy rather than swordplay. CMON dropped the Gilbert & Mortimer Expansion in a Kickstarter campaign that felt as caffeinated as a goblin on a caffeine binge. The promise? Two new survivors with ridiculous names, some shiny plastic friends, and enough new tiles to make your board look like a dragon decided to use it as a quilt. Since this is a review, you’re here for truth, goblin-level wit, and a verdict you can take to the tavern without getting kicked out for excessive charisma. Let’s dive into what the expansion actually delivers, how it plays with the base game, and whether you should back it or wait for the next dragon to sneeze on your wallet.

## Unboxing the Gilbert & Mortimer Expansion

### What’s in the box

The expansion arrives in a box that screams “I was a stretch goal and I’m somehow both proud and tired.” Inside you’ll typically find two survivor miniatures—Gilbert and Mortimer—each with unique sculpts that look like they were designed by people who read too many fantasy novels and watched too many rusted gears rust. Alongside the minis you’ll get survivor stat cards, a handful of new Gear tiles, a few new Spawn tiles, and an assortment of Objective cards to shuffle into your deck. If you’ve played Zombicide before, you’ll recognize the rhythm: you get enough bits to feel fresh, but not so much that your table turns into a mechanic’s convention.

### The minis: two clever-but-slightly-dorky heroes

Gilbert is your classic “bookish engineer” who somehow ended up holding a claymore and a backpack full of potions. Mortimer is the more flamboyant alchemist who insists that every problem can be solved with a well-timed splash of caustic charcoal and a label that reads, in glorious calligraphy, “Poison (for the Unsuspecting Zombie)”. The sculpts lean into their personalities with big hats, oversized goggles, and enough brass adornment to make a steampunk enthusiast jealous. They don’t look like your typical knightly hero, and that’s part of the charm. In a zombie apocalypse, you don’t need “Chiseled Bronze Warrior,” you need “That guy who can rig a trap and also pronounce the word ‘alchemical’ as if it’s a spell.”

### Cards, tiles, and little bits of chaos

The new Gear tiles expand your inventory with quirky tools: lanterns that reveal fog of war, vials that boost attack, and contraptions that might explode if you sneeze wrong. The Objective cards twist your early game goals into more rapid-fire micro-scenarios, forcing you to consider risk management in a way that makes you question every blanket of dice you roll. The new Spawn tiles open fresh lines of undead ambushes, ensuring that you won’t marathon the same approach to clearing a room and will instead embrace the chaotic joy of “let’s see what Gilbert and Mortimer do this time.”

### Aesthetics and production quality

The expansion matches the Black Plague base set in terms of color palette, plastic weight, and surface finish. The minis have the crisp detailing you expect from CMON, with more emphasis on mechanical gear than on aristocratic fancy. The cards are glossy but not sticky, the tiles snap into place with satisfying resistance, and the punchboard isn’t so fragile that you’ll need to treat it like a vinyl record while unboxing. If there’s a single gripe to be had here, it’s that the entire thing can feel a hair cramped if your play area isn’t large enough. But that’s a problem almost any Zombicide expansion has, except when you’re playing in a cavernous living room with a chessboard the size of a small car.

## How Gilbert & Mortimer Changes the Game

### Mechanic overview: team-up shenanigans

Gilbert brings a trap-building toolkit into the party. Mortimer brings alchemical recipes that can boost or debilitate like a battlefield magician who forgot to wear sleeves. The core concept is synergy: when Gilbert’s contraptions are used in proximity to Mortimer’s potions, you unlock two-way bonuses that feel flavorful without breaking the game. It’s not a gimmick that requires a corral of new rules; it slots into the existing action phase slots with minimal friction. You’ll often see players coordinate a trap with a post-trap alchemical buff, turning a dull corridor into a mini-boss encounter with minimal setup for the board master.

### Balancing and pacing: does it scale with the base game?

The expansion scales well because it leans into the core Zombicide rhythm: you’re fighting to activate, loot, and limp your way through an ever-growing pile of undead squads. Gilbert’s traps escalate the terror in a space-efficient manner, while Mortimer’s alchemical effects let you tilt the odds in a turn or two. The risk-reward balance tends to favor tactical play rather than brute force. If you’re a “throw more swords” player, you’ll learn to appreciate the cleverness of positioning and timing rather than the raw number of dice you roll.

### Table feel: speed, chaos, and the “aha” moment

This is where the expansion shines. The “aha” moment—where you realize you can chain a trap into a buff and then deploy Mortimer’s gadget to flip a near-lost combat into a clean victory—is exactly the kind of moment you buy Zombicide for. It’s the kind of tactile satisfaction that makes you forget you lost three rounds in a row to a rogue zombie that decided to spawn behind your back. The pace remains brisk; this isn’t a heavy euro by any stretch, but it’s a clever, fast, and hilariously cinematic piece of a long-running campaign.

### Thematic fit: does Gilbert & Mortimer feel at home in Black Plague?

Yes, they do. The medieval setting of Black Plague is already a pastiche of fantasy, folklore, and practical armor. Gilbert’s engineering mindset and Mortimer’s alchemy vibe slot neatly into that world, as if they wandered out of a dusty lab and into a plague-ridden castle with a backpack full of questionable contraptions. Their dialogue—what little there is, because everyone is too busy swatting undead to have a long chat—has a certain dry humor that is very “geek at a tavern after a long board game night.” It’s not Shakespeare; it’s party-friendly satire with a faint whiff of old parchment, which is exactly the right taste for a Zombicide expansion.

## Kickstarter Experience: Backer Perks, Delays, and the Joy of a Pledge Level

The Kickstarter for Gilbert & Mortimer came with the usual CMON charm—sleek trailer, glossy images, and a promise of stretch goals that felt like extra loot in a fantasy RPG. If you’ve backed a CMON project before, you’ll recognize the cadence: early birds, a handful of add-ons, and then a cascade of stretch goals that sometimes felt like a magician’s hat with a bottomless supply of rabbits. The experience wasn’t perfect, but it wasn’t chaotic either. The campaign page explained the backer rewards with a friendly tone, and the community updates kept the energy high without tipping into hype overload.

Backer perks were generous but sensible: extra dice, alternate sculpts, maybe a couple of comic short stories or a small companion card deck that makes you feel like you’re running a proper dungeon-crawl operation. If you’re the kind of person who loves collecting little trinkets, you’ll be delighted. If you’re just here for the gameplay, you’ll appreciate that you aren’t required to buy 18 different add-ons to enjoy the core expansion.

Delays happen in the Kickstarter ecosystem, and this one was no exception—no one’s fault, just the nature of mass production and international shipping. The important part is that the campaign owners communicated with the backers and avoided the dreaded silent update. Communication matters; it turns a potential sour note into a shared joke about “the plane is delayed, but it’s still bringing bread loaves of plastic goodness.”

 ## Components Quality in-Hand

The proof is in the punchboard. The tiles click with the same satisfaction you expect from a well-made tabletop game. The minis are sturdy enough to survive a few drops (or a table flip in a moment of hubris), and the painting quality is robust, if not museum-grade. If you’re picky about paint jobs, you’ll appreciate that some minor touch-ups are optional and easily doable. The cards are resilient and the art remains sharp after repeated shuffles. The only caveat is that, because you’re getting two new characters who rely on positioning-based effects, you’ll want to ensure your table is clear enough to map out lines of sight without turning the room into a chaotic art installation.

## Compatibility, Setup, and Play Time

### Compatibility with base game and other expansions

Gilbert & Mortimer are designed to slot into a standard Black Plague game without requiring a separate “expansion to expansions” rulebook. They’re not a standalone game; you’ll need Zombicide Black Plague as the base experience. The expansion does not dramatically alter core mechanics; instead, it adds new tools for players who want a deeper tactical layer. If you own other Zombicide expansions, you’ll notice some cross-pollination in zone control strategies and a more dynamic approach to objective completion. It’s a good fit for gamers who like to stack modules and watch how different synergies interact on a single crowded board.

### Setup and teardown time

Setup time for the expansion mirrors the base game with a few extra minutes to lay out the new tiles and set up Gilbert and Mortimer’s starter gear. Teardown is the same as usual: put everything back, stare at a pile of plastic, probably sigh, then vow to organize the hobby room properly next time. The only real nuisance here is the additional space the new tiles require; if your play area is a cozy apartment or a kitchen table that doubles as a dining room, you’ll want to move some furniture or enjoy a more compact “two-nomad’s-table” setup.

### Play time and player count

Zombicide Black Plague is typically friendly to 2–6 players, and Gilbert & Mortimer don’t change that range. The complexity bump is moderate, which makes it friendly for new players who want to experience two new survivals without drowning in a new rulebook. For veteran teams, the expansion provides a welcome challenge without turning the game into a math puzzle where you need a neon sign to track every buff. Expect a 60–120 minute game for a standard scenario, with variability based on the map size and the number of survivors in play.

## Thematic and Narrative Experience

This is where the expansion earns extra style points. The duo’s banter and the sense that you’re deploying gadgets in a plague-ridden medieval world adds a layer of character to the proceedings. Thematically, Gilbert’s trap systems feel plausible (in a fantasy-medieval sense), and Mortimer’s alchemy offers a splashy, cinematic flair to battle outcomes. It’s a good example of how an expansion can broaden the story without derailing the core narrative. You’ll find yourself narrating your turns with a mix of pseudo-scientific jargon and heroic bravado—precisely the flavor Zombicide intends to evoke.

## Value for Money: Is It Worth Backing?

Value is a tricky beast in the realm of Kickstarter board games. Gilbert & Mortimer offers two solid minis, some new expansion mechanics, and enough new content to breathe fresh life into Black Plague without requiring you to demolish your existing collection to afford it. Price-wise, it lands in the middle-to-upper range for CMON expansions, but you’re paying for a curated experience that emphasizes theme and tactical depth rather than gimmicks. If you’ve got a robust Zombicide table with a regular group, you’ll likely feel that the expansion pays for itself in a handful of sessions where the new tools unlock clever problem-solving rather than repetitive “kill more zombies” cycles.

If you’re a completionist who loves chasing every variant, you’ll also enjoy the extra pieces and the sense that your bookshelf is slowly becoming a museum of undead mischief. If you’re more of a casual player who buys a game to play it once in a while, consider the cost against your anticipated play frequency, because the expansion is best enjoyed as part of longer campaigns or recurring nights where your crew rotates between “try something new” and “stick with our favorites.”

## How to Integrate Gilbert & Mortimer into Your Campaigns

- Start small: try them in a single game to gauge whether you like their synergy before adding them to a weekly rotation.
- Pair with other expansions that emphasize setup variety: the more you diversify the tiles and objectives, the more the new duo will shine.
- Create “mini-story arcs” around Gilbert and Mortimer: give them a backstory you and your group can weave into the campaign, which makes their gadgets feel like plot devices rather than mere mechanics.
- Use the new objectives as pacing anchors: objective cards can nudge your party toward more aggressive or more meticulous play, which is great for balancing long sessions.
- Check table balance: if the group tends to overpower rooms quickly, you might want to dial up the zombie spawn rate in late game rooms to preserve tension.

## Final Thoughts: Should You Back It?

If you’re already a fan of Zombicide Black Plague and you enjoy the occasional moonlit night of cooperative zombie-splatting with a side of ridiculous gadgetry, Gilbert & Mortimer is a solid addition. It doesn’t reinvent the wheel; instead, it polishes and modernizes the wheel’s steering mechanism. The new duo brings personality, tactical depth, and a few “aha” moments that are the lifeblood of tabletop skirmishing in the zombie apocalypse. It’s a recommended add-on for players who crave varied scenarios, fresh strategies, and a dash of rogue-like chaos that doesn’t feel forced.

If you’re a casual gamer who plays Zombicide once every month or two, you’ll still get good value because the expansion adds enough variety to justify that occasional revisit. If you’re a collector who wants every CMON release, you’ll appreciate the design polish and the sense that you’re building a more complete Black Plague universe. And if you’re a die-hard optimist who believes that two nerds with enough gumption can outwit a horde of undead, you’ll love Gilbert & Mortimer for giving you a fun, flavorful way to do just that.
