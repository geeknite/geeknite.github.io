# CMON Zombicide: Special Guest Lucio Parrillo Expansion Pack – 2 Survivors & Zomb

If you’ve ever looked at your copy of Zombicide and thought, “Sure, the zombies are enthusiastic, but I could use a little more pizzazz in my apocalypse,” then buckle up, because CMON’s Special Guest Lucio Parrillo Expansion Pack is here to slap some glam onto your grimdark streets. This isn’t just a couple of shiny miniatures with a fancy painter’s signature slapped on the box. No, this expansion pack is a carnival of character, gore, and designer art that attempts to answer two very important questions: Can two survivors and a handful of new zombies actually change the cadence of a Zombicide session? And more critically, can they do it with style that would make any fantasy-horror crossover fan shed a single tear of both joy and regret for their bank account balance?

If you’re new to Zombicide or you just picked up this expansion on a whim, welcome to a game where you get to run around a miniature apocalypse while screaming, laughing, and occasionally interrogating a two-piece art exhibit on the tabletop. CMON has long excelled at giving players a visual feast: you’ll hear thank-you-notes from painters, you’ll see the art direction go on a joyride, and you’ll inevitably tilt your head and think, “That is not what a zombie looks like in any pre-2020 video game.” In this expansion, Parrillo’s hallmark style — visceral musculature, operatic gore, and a splash of supernatural chic — becomes the main event. The question is whether that style serves the game’s mechanics with equal flair, or if it simply looks like a blockbuster poster that forgot to bring the plot.

If you’re reading this rather than watching the latest trailer, you probably want the bottom-line verdict first: the Lucio Parrillo Expansion Pack is a delightful, sometimes silly, always stylish add-on that elevates the “wow” factor without breaking the core game’s balance. It’s not flawless, but it’s the kind of expansion that makes you want to stage a narrative one-shot starring your two chosen survivors and, yes, a few zombie variants that would not be out of place in a high-budget comic adaptation.

For the record, I’m reviewing this as a Geeknite columnist who has spent more evenings than is strictly healthy painting tiny limbs and arguing with plastic corpses about the nature of strategic movement. If you’re allergic to flamboyance or you prefer your zombie fiction served with a side of stoic grimdark stoicism, this may not be for you. Otherwise, strap in: the two survivors pack a punch, the new zombie sculpts pop, and the overall package invites you to inject a bit of theater into your next board-game night.


## What’s in the box?

The expansion isn’t a behemoth, but it doesn’t pretend to be one either. You’ll find two survivor miniatures (plus character cards), a handful of zombie figures with Parrillo’s distinctive flair, plus the usual assortment of cards, tokens, and a rule sheet that expects you to read it while wearing sunglasses because you just opened a box that looks like it belongs in a museum gift shop.

### The two survivors
The two survivors are the heart of any Zombicide expansion, and Parrillo’s pack gives you two who feel distinct from the usual scrappers and snipers you’ve been using since the game’s inception. Survivor 1 is a character who channels classic action-hero vibes: fast-footed, improvisational, and more likely to improvise a solution out of a broken bike chain than to follow a strict plan. Survivor 2 leans into the more cerebral, trench-coat-wearing strategist archetype: a character who stacks careful plans before stepping into the fray, letting their compatriot chase chaos while they compute the optimal lane of escape.

Each survivor comes with its own unique set of skill cards and signature items that pair well with the base game’s objectives. The design team did something smart here: the cards aren’t mere re-skins of existing abilities. They push players toward new playstyles without making old builds obsolete. If you’ve spent the last year chasing perfect card synergies, you’ll feel a pleasant nudge toward experimentation rather than a full-blown meta-shift. The two survivors also carry the Christie-Slaydon Factor that Parrillo’s art tends to accentuate: a little swagger, a little danger, and a lot of personality packed into a cardboard miniature that looks like it could command a squad of armor-clad adventurers in a promo poster rather than a random encounter on a city rooftop.

### The zombies and their variants
No Zombicide expansion is complete without a few new undead faces to keep you on your toes. Parrillo’s expansions lean into his signature grotesquerie: bulging veins, gnashing jaws, and a certain theatrical flair that makes you swear the zombies have better fashion sense than your last three players’ characters combined. The expansion introduces a handful of zombie models with distinct attack profiles and movement quirks, ensuring that even if you run a familiar scenario, the encounter feel is newly dangerous rather than stale.

If you’ve run the base game many times, the new zombies present a welcome challenge without crippling the game’s pace. They aren’t a runaway threat that makes you walk away from the table in defeat; they’re the kind of threats that force you to adapt your plan mid-encounter, creating the “aha!” moment that makes people grin rather than groan. The sculpts capture Parrillo’s cinematic intensity: you’ll stare at some figures and think you could frame a comic-book panel around them without any extra lighting or editing. This is one of those expansions where the miniatures’ allure is not just decorative — it subtly nudges players toward narrativizing their sessions.

The box also includes a small number of tokens and a couple of scenario cards that allow you to weave the two survivors into pre-existing campaigns or to design a mini-campaign around their personal arcs. If you’re someone who loves narrative insertions — that is, bringing a little soap opera into your zombie apocalypse — you’ll find these cards surprisingly satisfying. They don’t hijack the main game’s pacing, but they offer enough character flavor to make your play-throughs feel a touch more cinematic.

For the curious, the packaging also hints at future collaborations in the same vein. It’s not a formal promise, but the tone suggests CMON is leaning into a model where more guest-art expansions could surface, each with its own narrative hooks. If you’ve enjoyed the idea of a “guest artist” as a product line, this expansion is a proof of concept that they can do more with it, and do it well.

### Quality and components
The printer’s paper, the plastic basing, the paint jobs — all feel on par with CMON’s standard excellence. The sculpts carry Parrillo’s signature bold lines and dramatic muscle groups, which, for some players, can be a plus and for others a potential eye-roller. If you’re hoping for hyper-accurate miniature movie-accurate detailing, you’ll likely be satisfied; if you prefer the subtlety of less-extreme shading and more “grim realism,” you may need to do some post-painting cleanup to avoid a shelf that looks like a gallery of horror-historic figures.

The two survivors’ minis come with standees and a small set of multi-layered bases. The nesting of heads, gear, and weapons is clean and intuitive, which is a relief for players who want to dive into a game rather than calibrate models for thirty minutes. You’ll find that the survivors’ accessories are tailored to their archetypes: one carries a makeshift rifle and a field kit, while the other sports a pack of survival tools and a compact melee armament. The zombie variants, too, are well-sculpted, with enough individuality that you’ll recognize them on the table at a glance, even when their actions are masked by a flurry of dice and card draws.

If you’re a painter, you’ll likely enjoy Parrillo’s palette choices here. The color contrasts are bold, which translates well to the board’s tabletop lighting. The art direction invites you to imagine a glossy, cinematic zombie carnage, while the actual gameplay keeps you grounded in a practical, tactical exercise. It’s the kind of contrast that makes miniatures come alive in your imagination, which, in my opinion, is exactly what you want from a good Zombicide expansion.

### How it plays with the base game
The expansion slots into Zombicide without any need for house rules or awkward exceptions. The two survivors’ cards feel compatible with the core mechanic; their actions can complement or clash with other players’ choices, depending on your group’s dynamic. The new zombie types add fresh tactics to consider during each turn — not a devastatingly different set of rules, but just enough to demand a quick recalculation of your plan. It’s a subtle but effective way to keep long campaigns feeling alive instead of a glorious, repetitive waltz through the same map.

If you’re a campaign veteran, you’ll appreciate how the new scenario cards fit into ongoing narratives. They provide specific prompts that help you tie the two survivors’ arcs into your long-form game, and they also offer a couple of “one-shot” missions for nights when you want a quick, highly cinematic night. The balance remains intact; you won’t find yourself unable to rescue a survivor because the new content is too weak, nor will you be overwhelmed by an overpowered objective that shuts down the entire table. In short, Parrillo’s pack respects the core game’s rhythm while injecting a dash of spectacle and narrative momentum.

For those who track difficulty, the expansion lands in a comfortable middle ground. It’s not designed to dramatically up the baseline challenge, but it does introduce enough new threats and clever placement options that you’ll feel like you’re playing something new without needing to recalibrate every rule in your brain.

### Narrative flavor and thematic fit
This is the tricky part: does featuring Lucio Parrillo’s art and design sensibilities make sense within Zombicide’s world? My take is a cautious yes — the expansion treats its horror with theatrical energy rather than grim realism. The survivors’ dialogue options, their personal cards, and some of the scenario prompts lean into the cinematic horror vibe that Parrillo’s work often evokes. If you enjoy a game night where the table feels like a living comic book panel, this is exactly the kind of product you want on your shelf.

Now, if your group is more about grim practicality than storytelling, you’ll still appreciate the two survivors as functional choices. They don’t impose heavy narrative concepts on you; instead, they offer flavor that can be easily integrated into your existing stories, or simply enjoyed as a well-executed visual delight on the table.

### A few practical notes
- Compatibility: Works with the standard Zombicide line and, in most cases, with the base game’s campaign modes. If you run mixed factions, you’ll want to keep a mental note of the new survivor’s actions to prevent confusion.
- Assembly time: The miniatures are straightforward to assemble. If you’ve done a dozen Zombicide builds, you’ll glide through these in under an hour. If you’re new, give yourself a little more patience — the first build always takes longer when you’re still reading the cards.
- Paint-and-fit: Parrillo’s style translates well with standard hobby paint, though some players may want to tweak shading to keep the models coherent with their existing zombie horde color schemes. It’s not strictly necessary, but you’ll probably enjoy the results if you’re into tabletop painting.
- Value proposition: The pack isn’t cheap relative to a base expansion, but the two survivors’ cards, new miniatures, and narrative material provide enough replay value that it’s easy to rationalize as a long-term investment if you’re a storyteller at heart.

But for now, let’s talk about the two survivors’ personalities, because that’s where Parrillo’s artistry intersects with your table-time in the most delightful way.

## The two survivors in depth
The heart of this expansion is its two new characters. Each brings a distinct temperament to the table, and together they create a dynamic that can swing from cautious planning to headlong improvisation in a single turn.

### Survivor A: Cassia “Vibe” Calder
Cassia is the archetype you call when you want a survivor who can turn a messy situation into a clean exit strategy. Her card set rewards movement and bold positioning. She’s the risk-taker who wants to be in the middle of the action, not running the risk of being sidelined by a slow approach. Her equipment suggests a field kit and a light crossbow, which makes her a fantastic opener for players who want to secure a critical chokepoint before the second wave comes in.

Cassia’s personality reads as a detective on a horror movie set: cool, collected, and capable of improvising a solution on the fly. She’s not a pure front-line bruiser, but she’s not shy about standing up in a crowd and guiding the group into a survivable line of retreat. The card synergy encourages you to think in terms of zone control and dynamic repositioning — perfect for players who enjoy calculating the shortest route to survival while savoring the moment of a dramatic pivot when things go sideways.

### Survivor B: Milo Vance
Milo is the planner, the scavenger, and, crucially, the one who believes that a good defense can kill you as well as a rush of adrenaline can. His toolset emphasizes traps, beware-of-ambush mechanics, and resource management. In practice, Milo creates a counterpoint to Cassia: where she wants to stay in motion, Milo leans into securing a pivot point and turning it into a fortress for the party. He’s ideal for players who enjoy the meta-game of resource hoarding and chain-reactive plays where one careful decision tumbles into a cascade of smart, efficient outcomes.

The fun in Milo comes from how his skill-tree rewards you for thinking ahead and reading your board state several turns in advance. His survivors’ arcs can weave nicely into long-running campaigns, providing a narrative throughline that helps your group feel like they’ve got a reason to push forward with every map map reveal. It’s rare to see a two-survivor pack do this balancing act so gracefully: you get two distinct, playable voices in your party, both with meaningful contributions, and neither feeling like a stand-in for a more established character from the base game.

### Narrative integration
If you’re a fan of the storytelling side of Zombicide, Cassia and Milo offer excellent hooks for your next game night. A pair of survivors with complementary strengths is tailor-made for short, modular campaigns where each mission uncovers a little more about their backstory and the world around them. The narrative prompts included with the expansion help you seed an ongoing arc into your campaign without forcing you down a predetermined path. If you want to run a one-night “arcade of horror” session, this is your ticket.

## How to integrate this expansion into your next session

To make the most of the two survivors and Parrillo’s flamboyant zombies, consider the following practical approaches:

- Start with a one-shot mission to ramp up the feel of the expansion. Let Cassia and Milo be your anchor characters, and run a map that emphasizes choke points, rescue missions, and escape routes. Use the new scenario cards as a guide to craft the encounter’s beat-by-beat thrills.
- If you’re running a campaign, weave the survivors’ backstories into your ongoing narrative. Let a mission reveal a clue about Cassia’s past or Milo’s lost companion. It adds a layer of emotional stakes that can make you root for them as the table’s heroes.
- Pair the new zombies with a pre-existing map’s terrain to explore interesting tactical combos. The new variants excel in settings with tight corridors and elevated positions, so consider maps that feature rooftops, stairwells, or multi-level interiors.
- Use the two survivors to test innovative table-rituals: quick-start “pitch sessions” before a game where players describe their plan in three sentences, then execute. The combination of fast decisions and dramatic art makes such rituals feel motivated and fun rather than like a homework assignment.

If you want a concise pointer: treat this expansion as a narrative upgrade, not just a mini-battle expansion. It’s about telling a compact story while you roll dice, not simply about hitting a set of numeric benchmarks on a stat card.

## The Geeknite take: pros, cons, and a balanced verdict
Pros
- Strong visual and thematic integration with Parrillo’s art style, infusing the game with cinematic flair.
- Two survivals with distinct playstyles that enrich team dynamics and encourage experimentation.
- New zombie variants add tactical variety without clogging the gameplay with overly complex rules.
- Narrative cards give a light but meaningful flavor to campaigns and one-shot sessions alike.
- High-quality components that look and feel premium on table surfaces, inspiring both players and painters alike.

Cons
- Price is higher than some other small expansions, which may test your impulse control if you’re building a large Zombicide library.
- The flamboyant art style may be polarizing for players who prefer a more grim, subdued aesthetic. If you’re aiming for a “serious” apocalypse vibe, you might want to temper expectations.
- For new players, there’s a brief learning curve to optimize the two new survivors and to quickly incorporate the data cards into a campaign flow.

Bottom line: If you enjoy Zombicide and you crave a dash of theatrical horror and charismatic heroes, this expansion hits a sweet spot. It respects the base game’s rhythm, it provides two fully fledged survivors with meaningful playstyles, and it features the kind of art that begs you to stage a dramatic, cinematic moment on your dining-room-table battlefield.

If you’re on the fence, consider this: would you rather have a set of new tools that nudges your party toward a story-rich session, or a handful of extra minis that simply occupy shelf space? The Lucio Parrillo Expansion Pack stays closer to the former. It’s not a revolution, but it’s a delightfully flamboyant evolution of the Zombicide experience.

The Special Guest Lucio Parrillo Expansion Pack is a proper treat for fans of the line who also crave a touch of theatricality in their zombie fiction. The two survivors are memorable, the new zombies are flavorful, and the overall package is polished enough to feel like a real celebration of the game's enduring appeal.

If you’re ready to bring a little more cinema to your zombie apocalypse, this expansion deserves a spot on your table. And if you’re already planning a weekend session with friends who appreciate a good-looking mini and a hearty narrative, you’ll likely find that Parrillo’s touch makes all the difference between a routine game night and a memorable tabletop event.
