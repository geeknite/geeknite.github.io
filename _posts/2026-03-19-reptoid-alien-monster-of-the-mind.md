## Card stats and mechanics
The Reptoid arrives with a cost that feels fair for the engine it unlocks. In the base form, it is sturdy but not unbeatable, which makes it a sweet midrange option with late‑game teeth if you build around it. Here are the core numbers you should pencil into your deck diary:

- Cost: 4 mana (or energy, depending on your local meta labels) 
- Attack: 5
- Defense: 6
- Mind Power: 8

The mind power stat is the real star here. It governs the card's primary gimmick and its late-game viability. Mind power acts like a secondary resource that scales with the game’s tempo, letting you siphon control away from your opponent just when their board looks scary enough to make you consider therapy.

The mechanical nuance that elevates Reptoid above a throwaway beater is its ability package, which centers on mind control and disruption. It isn't merely a beefy creature to crash into your opponent’s face; it is a tool that reshapes the battlefield by poking at the quiet corners of your opponent's decision tree.

### Abilities and keyword interactions
- Telepathic Grasp (Active): When Reptoid enters the battlefield, you may look at target opponent’s hand and discard a card chosen by you. This is the kind of affect that makes opponents second-guess their mulligans and proposed turn plans. It is not a direct removal spell, but a cognitive disruption that blurs what your opponent sees as safe options.
- Mind Link (Passive): If you control a Neuronet companion card, Reptoid gains a smaller aura that reduces the cost of your next mind-based operation by 1. This is a frictionless way to accelerate the mind‑control engine, enabling you to chain plays if you draft a small synergy suite around neural themes.
- Hypnotic Coil (Optional): Once per turn, when Reptoid is in play, you may tap it to force a reroute of an opponent’s next draw. This can be crucial in a long game where you want to stall their resource economy and buy yourself a couple of tempo turns.

A lot of players will underestimate the Telepathic Grasp ability because discarding a card from your opponent’s hand is a non‑glamorous effect in a world of flashy battlecries. However, in practice, discarding a key answer (like a sweeper or a crucial counter to your combo) at the wrong moment can be more valuable than killing a creature outright. It is the subtle art of removing their next best play before they realize they needed it.

## Deck building and strategy tips
So you want to pilot Reptoid as the centerpiece of a mind‑control gladiator deck. Here are concrete pointers to help you avoid misplays and maximize the exhibit of your brain’s beauty in the arena:

- Core synergy: Build around a Neural Engine archetype. Reptoid thrives when you have a handful of mind‑pleasing spells or hardware that can stretch your mind powers into efficient plays. A typical engine includes one or two small utility cards that give you card filtering or additional mind effects, plus a couple of inevitables that win games when left unchecked.
- Early tempo vs late game: In the early turns, Reptoid is a good top‑deck if you can keep your curve smooth and avoid overcommitting. The thumb rule is to assemble a minimal board presence while you set up the mind‑control layering. In the midgame, the Telepathic Grasp is your primary weapon. Don’t be afraid to discard a nonessential card if it stymies your opponent’s momentum. In late game, your Mind Power should start to dominate, and your opponent will beg for a reset while you quietly close the game with a chain of mind‑based plays.
- Target prioritization: The Grasp ability is most valuable when you can remove answers to your own threats or remove cards that enable opponent combos. Prioritize discarding counterspells, removal that could threaten your board, or synergy cards that accelerate their own engine.
- Board interplay: Reptoid doesn't have to be the only heavy hitter. Pair it with creatures that punish delayed plays or reward you for maintaining a controlled battlefield. Cards that punish overextension or reward you for leaving your opponent with cards in hand are excellent teammates.
- Sideboarding strategy: If your local meta leans toward control, include a small clutch of cheap discard‑pacing options. If the meta tilts wrecking ball aggression, bring in a few additional threats and ways to extract answers from your opponent fast, since Reptoid’s strength lies in dictating terms rather than brute force alone.

The card rewards you for committing to a plan rather than hopping between disparate strategies.

## Playstyle variations and matchups
Reptoid is a card that plays well with a plan but suffers when the plan has too many moving parts late in the game. Against aggressive decks, you want to anchor the board with a few sturdy bodies while you pepper in mind disruption to delay their tempo. Against control decks, your primary goal is to identify the right moment to flip the board in your favor with a two‑card combo or a sequence of mind‑power plays that force your opponent into a corner.

In terms of direct matchups, Reptoid tends to perform best when the opponent relies on big, stacky combos that revolve around hand disruption or card draw. If they lean on a fast clock, your aim shifts toward resilience and denial: keep your opponent from having the precise tools they need, then finish with a mind power surge. If your opponent is heavy on removal and interaction, you will want to keep your threats relatively cheap to ensure you can flood the board with resilient choices while you maintain the upper hand through mind control.
