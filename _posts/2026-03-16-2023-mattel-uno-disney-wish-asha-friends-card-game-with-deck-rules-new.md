## How to play: rules and tweaks for Wish
For the uninitiated, Uno is a simple color matching game. The goal is to shed all your cards before anyone else. The basic actions you know and love are still there, but the Disney Wish branding introduces small twists to keep things fresh without reinventing the wheel. Here is the gist of the rules as they apply to this edition:

- Standard Uno play remains: players take turns matching a card by color or number to the top card on the discard pile. You can also play action cards to switch direction, skip a turn, or force a draw two situation on your opponents.
- Wish themed action cards exist and function like the originals but with a magical twist. Expect prompts that make players sing a line, tell a quick memory, or switch seats in a playful way. The exact actions are described in the rule sheet, so make sure to skim before the first showdown.
- Asha cards introduce a cooperative element during certain moments. When a certain combination appears, players may team up for a round where the goal shifts from being the first to shed all cards to reaching a shared milestone. This is optional and can be toggled off for traditional Uno style play nights.
- There is a little extra flavor text on many of the cards that helps you weave a story in roughly 10 second bursts. If you are the kind of player who loves dramatic narration during card play, you will get a kick out of this. If you prefer cold hard numbers, you can ignore the storytelling prompts and keep your head in the game.
- The last card rule remains: if you yell Uno and forget to shout it before placing your last card, you must draw two cards as a light penalty. The penalty text is printed on the rule card, but in practice we have all witnessed a chorus of playful heckling when someone forgets the chant.

What makes this edition more engaging than classic Uno are the little narrative prompts that occur during play. They are quick, usually taking 15 seconds, and they keep the energy high without derailing the core mechanics. This is not a storytelling game; it is a family game night where the story sparks are used to gentle humor rather than long winded epic sagas.
