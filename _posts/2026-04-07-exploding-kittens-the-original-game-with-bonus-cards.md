# Exploding Kittens - The Original Game with Bonus Cards: A Geeknite Review

If you’ve ever loitered around a table at a game night and wondered if catnip, chaos, and a tiny bomb could all somehow be a party, congratulations: Exploding Kittens is your spirit animal. This review dives into the original game—plus its Bonus Cards edition—and tries to unpack why a goofy art style and simple rules can conjure up more table talk than a D&D session with a misprinted map. ## What is Exploding Kittens, anyway?
Exploding Kittens is a push-your-luck card game created by Elan Lee, Shane Small, and The Oatmeal’s Matt Inman. The premise is gloriously simple: you’re trying not to draw an exploding kitten, which would typically end your run faster than a cat plot twist in a romcom. The deck includes a mix of defuse cards, detonation events, and a variety of goofy kitten-related mischief, all wrapped up in art that looks like it was doodled between naps during a particularly intense webcomic binge.

At its core, Exploding Kittens is about risk management with a dash of RNG and a lot of laughing-at-each-other. It’s one of those games that doesn’t pretend to be more complex than it is. The rules fit on a few sticky notes, which is both delightful and terrifying when your group’s memory capacity hovers around “did we shuffle the deck or did we just stare at the box for an hour?” The recipe works because it’s approachable for first-timers and still crunchy enough for veteran game-night veterans who enjoy proving that even the simplest decisions can be dramatic when you’re keeping track of three other players’ bluffs and your own rising panic.

## The Bonus Cards edition: what changed?
The Bonus Cards variant adds a handful of extra twists to the standard Exploding Kittens deck. Think of it as a flavor booster shot for your pub quiz night: more shenanigans, more potential misplays, and more opportunities to misread the table’s tone. The exact cards vary by edition, but common additions often include alternative pekoe of chaos: additional defuse-type options, sneaky stashing moves, and wild reversals that can flip a near-win into a spectacular pile of “how did we end up here?”

From a design standpoint, the Bonus Cards pack is a clever way to extend the core experience without overhauling the core loop. It keeps the game’s tempo snappy while giving repeat players a reason to reaccustom their strategies. You’ll quickly learn that a well-timed Bonus Card is less about “outsmarting” your opponents and more about leveraging a shared moment of confusion into victory. That’s the sort of win that makes you feel like you hacked the matrix with a feathered toy bird.

## How to play: a quick-and-dirty guide
If you’ve ever played Uno and felt like you learned a life skill along the way, you’ll be golden here. The rules are minimal, but the consequences are maximal in comedic effect.

- Setup: Shuffle the deck and deal a few cards to each player. The rest form a draw pile. In the middle sits the bomb—aka the exploding kitten deck card that turns a sunny afternoon into “who drew it?” chaos.
- The goal: Avoid drawing an exploding kitten card. If you draw one and don’t have a defuse card, you’re out. If you do have a defuse card, congrats—you’ve used it to… not explode. The defuse card is your get-out-of-jail-free card, usually paired with some wry commentary about your life choices.
- Cards you’ll encounter: Defuse cards (keep them!), Attack cards (pass your turn with a multiplier), Skip cards (end your turn without drawing), See the Future (peek at the top few cards), Shuffle (shuffle the draw pile), and various other antics that often reference internet memes rather than ancient strategic doctrine. The Bonus Cards add more flavors: temporary protections, surprise exchanges, and sneaky “well, that escalated quickly” moments.
- Turn flow: Draw a card from the deck at the end of your turn, unless you’ve played an Attack card—then the next person rolls the dice, literally, in their head. The DM of the table (the person keeping track of chaos) gets to watch as fate reveals itself one card at a time, while your group argues about optimal defuse usage and whether a See the Future was actually a bluff or just a very confident face.

The core loop is punchy and fast. Expect a game to wrap in 10-20 minutes, which makes it perfect for a chain of quick rounds if your group wants to experiment with different “what if we alter house rules” scenarios. It’s a great candidate for introducing new players to the hobby too because the rules are easy to grasp, the humor lands immediately, and the “oh no, I drew the exploding kitten” reaction is universal across families and colleagues who have realized they’re stuck in a meeting-turned-game-night at 6 PM on a Friday.

## Strategy, luck, and the social vibe
Let’s be real: Exploding Kittens is not a high-stakes brain-burner. It’s a social game. The strategy mostly hinges on reading the room, bluffing about your fear of the next draw, and managing your defuse cards like a seasoned card shark who’s also a cat person. The luck factor is high—this is not a chess match; it’s more of a frantic improv scene where someone’s pet may or may not be the real mastermind. The Bonus Cards amplify this dynamic by injecting micro-choices: do you burn a defuse now to bait a later blunder, or do you save it for a guaranteed catastrophe? The answer, of course, changes by the minute and by the group’s shared sense of humor.

A good playstyle example: you see the top three cards with See the Future, you don’t like what you saw, you quietly pass on an Attack and let someone else risk the fate. Then you watch the table implode when one of your opponents uses a Bonus Card to steal a future turn, only to walk into a double-attack chain that deletes the last reliable defender. It’s theatre, it’s chaos, and it’s exactly the kind of moment a table remembers long after the last crumb is swept up from the end of the night.

## Accessibility and group suitability
Exploding Kittens shines in mixed-age contexts. A kid can handle the simple draw-to-survive mechanic, while adults enjoy the witty card text and the occasional cheeky reference that lands with a knowing wink. It’s a gentle introduction to social deduction without the heavy “you must know the lore of a fantasy world to contribute” vibe. The humor is broad, a little silly, and a lot cartoonish, which means you’re less likely to see someone’s facial expression turn into a debate about “rule-as-written versus rule-as-expected.” It’s the kind of game that invites loud laughter rather than intense focus; it’s social lubrication for a crowd that wants to bond over ridiculous outcomes.

That said, if you’re gaming in a space where small, noisy groups with intense energy become disruptive, you might want to set expectations first. Exploding Kittens thrives in a tone of playful antagonism and lighthearted chaos; it can be less comfortable for folks who prefer quiet and deliberate strategy. Still, with a moderator or a clearly defined house rule, it’s easy to keep the vibe friendly and the laughter in bounds.

## Who should buy this game—and who should skip it
- You should buy Exploding Kittens if you like fast, social games that lean into humor and friendly chaos. It’s ideal for party nights, family gatherings, or after-work wind-downs where you want something quick, loud, and goofy.
- You should skip it if you want deep strategy, elaborate world-building, or you absolutely must have a clean, serious tabletop experience. It’s not designed to be your long-form gaming obsession; it’s designed to be your group’s “let’s laugh for a bit” card game.

Durability and ferry-crew style sessions aside, the game’s charm lies in how it makes people interact. The goofy art, the tiny bomb-cat imagery, and the sense of “we’re all in this silly moment together” are the real draw. It’s a social device as much as a game—the kind of item you reach for when you want a quick emotional punch, not a ponderous debate about rules interpretation.

It’s not about the number of cards you memorize; it’s about how loudly you and your friends laugh when someone pulls a risky move and somehow pulls it off or spectacularly misreads the room. If you’re hunting for a night-ender that wraps with a chorus of giggles rather than a solemn “we must discuss strategy,” this is a strong contender.

It’s not a life-altering game, but it’s a durable staple for gatherings where you want energy, not academic rigor. If you’re stocking a board game shelf for casual nights, this belongs in the rotation along with other light party games.
