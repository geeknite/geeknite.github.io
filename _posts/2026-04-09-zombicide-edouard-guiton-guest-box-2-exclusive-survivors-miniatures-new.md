## Painting and assembly tips

### Primer and base colors
- Start with a neutral gray or black primer to preserve detail. Light catching on edges will reveal the sculpt lines you want to accent.
- Use a mid tone for skin and a slightly cooler palette for clothing to keep the figures readable on the table during a game session.
- For metal and mechanical bits, a black wash followed by a metallic dry brush can help you achieve a worn look that reads well under game lighting.

### Highlighting and shading
- Edge highlighting on armor and weapons should stay restrained; a sharp line along the edge will make the model pop without looking comic bookish.
- Use a subtle brown wash for leather textures and a blue-gray shade for cloth, to keep the tones cohesive with the rest of your Zombicide terrain.
- A final touch of white or light cream on a small highlight can give life to eyes or reflective surfaces such as goggles or visor glass.

### Blending and basing
- For bases, a simple texture with a few stones and grit can anchor the figures. A bit of static grass at the edge of the base can give a sense of environment without going overboard.
- If you want to push the enviro into the mall or subway vibe, you can add tiny decals or sculpted debris to the base. Just avoid making the base too busy; you want the glances to settle on the survivors, not the scenery trap of your own creation.

### Assembly notes
- Dry fit first. The two main pieces should align cleanly, and a tiny amount of green stuff or plastic cement should do the trick for any gaps.
- Avoid over-squeezing glue, especially on smaller parts like hands or weapon grips; excess glue can obscure fine detail and leave visible seams.
- If your kit includes alternate weapon options, test fit these before final assembly so you can decide which loadout reads best for your table aesthetic.

## How to photograph and present your minis

When you photograph these minis, the lighting matters as much as the paint job. A soft, diffused light helps avoid harsh shadows that hide details in the folds and bracings. A single light source at about 45 degrees will help the face read clearly, while a second, lower-contrast light will reveal the texture of fabric and the grime on metal.

Use a neutral background with a bit of texture to give context. A white, gray, or sepia tone works well for narrative shots without overpowering the miniatures themselves. If you’re into tabletop photography, you can take advantage of a shallow depth of field to emphasize the faces and upper torsos of the two survivors while the battlefield blurs slightly in the background.
