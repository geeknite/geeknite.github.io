## Rules refresher: how to complete a phase and why it matters
Phase 10 is a game about completing ten distinct “phases” throughout the course of play. Each phase is a particular arrangement of cards, such as:

- Phase 1: two sets of three (three of a kind) in any color
- Phase 2: one set of three plus a run of four
- Phase 3: one set of four plus a run of four
- Phase 4: one run of seven
- Phase 5: one run of eight
- Phase 6: two sets of four
- Phase 7: one run of five plus one set of three
- Phase 8: one run of seven with a pair
- Phase 9: two sets of five
- Phase 10: one set of five and one set of two

Of course, this is a rough lane map; many editions swap numbers slightly, but the core mechanic sticks: you want to lay down your phase by combining cards in your hand to meet the exact layout. Players who finish a phase can “exit” that phase from their hand, laying it down for the table to admire and antagonize them about how lucky they got the Wilds on that run.

Every round you might draw from the deck or pick up the top card from the discard pile. You must end your turn by discarding one card, which creates a carefully curated soup of discarded decisions and near-misses for the next player. The Wild and Skip cards give the game an extra dose of strategic spice: Wilds can stand in for any card to complete a phase, while Skips delay an opponent’s progress. If you’re new, you’ll discover that the timing of using a Wild or skipping a turn can feel like a high-stakes, low-age-intensity chess match.

## Strategy: how to maximize your odds without becoming a full-time algorithm
Phase 10 isn’t about perfectly calculating every move. It’s about micro-decisions that compound. Here are practical strategies that work for most families and groups:

- Learn the phase order by heart, but accept that the deck can ruin your plan at any moment. Respect the chaos.
- Prioritize early flexibility. Wilds are precious; use them to unlock early phases rather than to salvage a single improbable run late in the game.
- Watch discards carefully. If someone is collecting a lot of a single color, watch that color; you may be able to block or misdirect them with a strategy reroute via a discard card.
- Balance risk and reward. If you’re close to completing a phase but need a couple of exact cards, a Wild might be worth a skip; or maybe you hold back and wait for a cleaner opportunity.
- Memory matters. Remember what has been discarded; a well-timed draw or a clever manipulation of the deck can happen when you remember that a crucial card hasn’t yet appeared.

A good rule of thumb is this: prioritize flexibility and tempo over perfection. The game rewards the bold, but not the reckless. If you’re playing with kids, teach them the difference between “I think this is the phase I’m aiming for” and “I know the phase, but I’m one card away and I do not want to give up my advantage.” It’s an excellent way to train early-level strategic thinking without turning it into a calculus exam.

## Variants, house rules, and how to hack Phase 10 for longer play
Phase 10 can scale from light party game to something with more bite by introducing house rules. Here are a few popular tweaks:

- Add a drafting phase: each player draws two cards at the start of a round and discards two by the end, creating more opportunities for dramatic phase completions.
- Implement a timer: give each player a minute to decide on a discard. If you’re gaming with a group that’s into fast-paced decisions, this one moves the table along and reduces analysis paralysis.
- Create a “deck reset” after a certain number of rounds to keep the game fresh. This helps when the same people keep hitting the mid-game stalemate and arguing about who has the best memory of discarded cards.
- Limit Wild usage: require a Wild to be played as a specific card in a particular phase, or restrict its ability to bend a phase for certain rounds to increase the challenge.

We’ve found that house rules are a fantastic way to tailor Phase 10 to your group’s sense of humor. Some groups enjoy wilder, faster variants that borrow from party games; others prefer a stricter, classic interpretation. There’s no right or wrong here—only a set of preferences that you can tune as you learn what your friends and family tolerate in a thrilling arm-length competition.
