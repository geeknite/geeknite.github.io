## How to play at a glance
Saboteur is a role-and-path game. Some players are secretly saboteurs; others are earnest miners. The objective is to be the first team to ascend a tunnel from the start to a treasure card. The catch? Saboteurs try to derail the tunnel-building, mismangling the path while miners push forward to claim gold.

### Setup
- Shuffle the role cards and deal one to each player depending on the player count. A couple of saboteurs slip into the mix; the rest are miners. The exact number of saboteurs varies with player count, which is part of the fun and chaos.
- Create a starting point and lay out a few initial path cards face down. Players will reveal them as the tunnel grows, one card at a time.
- Place treasure cards in a bag or deck, face down, and assign the corresponding tail-end goals. These are the prize—and the point swing—of the game.
- Decide on any house rules before you start. We’ll cover common variants later in this piece.

### Turn structure
On your turn, you typically perform two actions: play a path card or discard a card to draw, and then pass the turn to the next player. The wild card is the discovery: you may reveal or extend the tunnel by laying a path card that connects to the existing path network.

Key to the heartbeat of Saboteur is role secrecy. You won’t know who’s sabotaging until the end of the round, which creates a delicious tension all the way to the last card. The “killjoy” for saboteurs is that they’re not allowed to communicate their sabotage explicitly; they must use underhanded, clever misdirection—like intentionally playing a path card that looks right but leads to a dead end, or dropping a poor hint that your guess is wrong while your friends suffer.

### Roles and mechanics
- Miners: Their objective is to build a continuous tunnel from the start to a treasure card. They may share information by glancing at revealed cards, but they’re not allowed to reveal who they are.
- Saboteurs: Their job is to derail the tunnel by placing cards that bend the tunnel away from treasure or cause dead ends. They must avoid giving themselves away through obvious mistakes.
- Treasure rewards: When the tunnel reaches a treasure card, the round ends and the miners collect the treasure for points. If the main tunnel collapses or a dead end appears, anticipation tilts toward the saboteurs.

There’s a simple math to the endgame. The team that successfully tailors a path to treasure gains points proportional to the value of that treasure card. If multiple treasure cards are found in the same round, additional rounds can decide the ultimate winner. Saboteur’s clever modular scoring makes each round feel like a tiny, funny victory or a spectacular disaster—a microcosm of every “group project” you’ve ever endured with more drama and fewer spreadsheets.

### End of round and scoring
When a treasure card is connected to the start and revealed, the round ends and miners tally the points. Then all players redraw their roles and the next round begins. The number of rounds depends on the players and your preferred game length. A typical evening with 6 players might see 3–5 rounds, easily manageable within 30–60 minutes.

There are many ways to enforce a quick rhythm. Some groups implement a timer per turn (60 seconds), which speeds up discussions and increases tension. Others prefer a more relaxed approach with longer turns and a slower, almost theatrical, reveal. Either way, Saboteur rewards social play: bluffing, deduction, and a dash of chaos.

## Player count, game length, and how it shifts the vibe
Saboteur scales gracefully, but the feel changes with the number of participants.

### 3–4 players
With fewer players, suspicion becomes more direct. Roles are easier to deduce because you have fewer voices to juggle. It’s a compact, tense micro-game that still provides the core Saboteur experience: a private game of trust and betrayal with fewer lanes for misdirection. The rounds fly by, and the endgame hits you like a sudden treasure windfall or a devastating dead end—depending on whether you’re the miner or the saboteur.

### 5–6 players
The sweet spot for many of our readers. You gain more role diversity and more routes to misdirect your friends. The tunnel networks become denser, and the planning phase—where you weigh “I’ll place this card to advance a little” versus “this card will make the round end in a terrible fate for someone else”—is where Saboteur shines. Expect more laughter, more accusations, and more “Are you sure that’s a path card?” conversations.

### 7–10 players
Chaos reigns, but it’s the charming chaos that makes Saboteur so beloved for large groups. The abundance of players means more saboteurs to sniff out, more dead ends to build, and more chances for a late-game twist that upends an entire round after you were sure you’d won. It’s noisy and glorious, the kind of game where you want someone to hit a long chain of perfect card draws so you can watch a silver-tinned drama unfold in front of your eyes.

## Strategies: thinking like a saboteur without losing friends
Saboteur is a game of subtlety. Here are a few practical strategies to help you navigate the mine shaft of social deduction without blowing up friendships.

- For miners: focus on path continuity. Your number one goal is to build a contiguous tunnel to a treasure card. If a path card looks risky, weigh whether it creates a longer-term advantage or a short-term hazard. Coordination with other miners is possible, but secret roles mean you should not overtly reveal your strategies.
- For saboteurs: a balanced approach works best. Mix aggressive sabotage with quiet misdirection. Don’t always take the obvious path; sometimes a round-ending dead end may force others to reevaluate their plans and waste valuable turns.
- Reading tells: keep an eye on how players place cards. A player who frequently places “block” cards may be signaling distrust or a hidden alignment. However, don’t rely solely on behavior—game state matters more than any one player’s tells.
- Timing: the best saboteur moments often come late in the round when treasure cards are within reach. If you can push the tunnel to near-completion and then insert a tricky card that breaks the chain, you disrupt progress and heighten tension.
- Endgame risk: when a round nears the treasure, consider whether you should claim victory early or wait for a more favorable card configuration. This is where the social aspect becomes their ultimate weapon—players may negotiate, bluff, or try to salvage a bad situation with a renewed push at a second treasure card.

## Playing tips and house rules
- Short rounds: especially with younger players, you can use a timer (60 seconds per turn) to keep energy high and pace brisk.
- Transparent-skew variants: if your group prefers less secrecy, you can reduce the number of saboteurs or switch to a rule where all roles are known to every player after a round. It removes the bluffing element but keeps the puzzle-solving aspect intact.
- Solo or small-team variants: Saboteur is designed for party-style play, but you can adapt it for two players by alternating turns and using a “draft” of role cards to simulate multiple players. It’s not ideal, but it’s a handy emergency option when friends cancel.
