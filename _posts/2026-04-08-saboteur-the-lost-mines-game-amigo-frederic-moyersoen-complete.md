Saboteur: The Lost Mines is the kind of game that makes even your cat look suspicious when you shuffle cards. If you thought the original Saboteur was chaotic, The Lost Mines takes that chaos and dabs it with a sharper, more thematic twist. Designed by Frédéric Moyersoen and published by Amigo Spiele, this edition invites players into a world where dwarves dig for riches while an unseen saboteur tries to derail the entire enterprise with a sly wink and a well-timed joke.

![Saboteur: The Lost Mines]( {{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/saboteur-lost-mines.jpg )

## What is Saboteur: The Lost Mines?

At its core, Saboteur is a hidden-role, route-building card game. Players form a team with a secret purpose: reach the gold. Some players are saboteurs who want to sabotage the route. The Lost Mines introduces new mine-themed complications: additional path types, new sabotage cards, and secret objectives that can tilt the endgame. The tone remains lighthearted and cartoonish rather than grim, which makes the game approachable for newcomers and a fun party option with friends who enjoy a little psych-out.

## Components and setup

- Cards: path cards, action cards, role cards, and the special “collapse” and “secret objective” cards added by The Lost Mines.
- Tokens: gold tokens, ore tokens, and sabotage markers.
- The board: starting shaft, tunnel cards, and an end point.
- Rulebook and quick reference: a concise guide that speeds up teaching.

Setup is a little more involved than the classic Saboteur, because you seed the new elements and balance who starts as saboteur and who starts as miner. The “secret objective” mechanic adds a new layer of strategy and suspicion to early rounds.

### Card breakdown: Path, Action, and Roles

- Path cards create tunnels with bends, twists, and sometimes dead ends. The Lost Mines introduces new geometries to challenge your route-planning.

- Action cards let you influence the tunnel and players: move, block, swap roles, and, occasionally, force a redraw of some cards.

- Role cards designate miners or saboteurs; The Lost Mines adds specialized roles or modified effects to keep players guessing.

- The special cards can trigger temporary conditions, altering the endgame or the way sabotage works.

## Gameplay overview: turn structure and pivotal moments

A typical round flows as follows:

- Draw up and plan: Each player draws cards to reach their hand limit.

- Play phase: You play a path card to extend the tunnel or an action card to affect the board.

- Role dynamics: Saboteurs sabotage using dead ends and collapsed routes, while miners try to piece together a route to the gold.

- Endgame triggers: The round ends when the tunnel reaches the gold, or when sabotage collapses sections, invalidating the path.

The Lost Mines emphasizes dramatic reveals and quick decision-making, meaning you’re rarely stuck in analysis paralysis for long. It’s more about reading the table, deciding when to pressure a specific player, and recognizing the signs of potential bluffs.

### Strategic play for miners

- Read the room: Shared knowledge and hints from others help identify who is likely sabotaging or helping.

- Save critical cards: Reserve path and action cards for pivotal moments to shape the last few moves of the tunnel.

- Collaborative risk-taking: You’ll often need to commit to a particular tunnel path with a small team, balancing trust and risk.

### Strategic play for saboteurs

- Misdirection as a weapon: Saboteurs should create false leads—tunnels that seem promising but produce dead ends.

- Timing: Use collapse cards at critical moments to prevent a too-easy victory.

- Bluff and trust games: By appearing to align with miners, saboteurs can avoid suspicion, buying time for the endgame.

## Thematic integration and humor

The Lost Mines leans into a playful dwarven world. The humor remains light and accessible—think cartoon dwarves, witty text on cards, and a sense of chaos that’s more charming than threatening. The theme supports social interaction: players banter, call out suspicious plays, and enjoy the comedic tension of a misdirected mining expedition.

## Length, pacing, and replayability

- Player count: 3–9 players is comfortable. In smaller groups, the game tends to be more straightforward; in larger groups, you’ll get more misdirection and longer plays.

- Duration: Expect around 25–40 minutes for typical rounds. Secret objectives or larger groups can push the session toward an hour.

- Replayability: The new cards and objective cards provide variety, so your group won’t easily tire of the same path-building loop.

## Experience and teaching notes

- Learning curve: Moderate. If you know Saboteur, you’ll be in familiar territory; if not, there’s enough context to get you going quickly.

- Teaching approach: A quick one-page teach that covers roles, basic path-building, and how endgame triggers work is enough to start. A few example plays help illustrate how sabotage and cooperation interact.

- House rules: Some groups like to include timed rounds or alternate winners to keep things fresh.

## Pros and cons (summary)

Pros:
- Balanced mix of cooperation and betrayal
- Fresh mechanics that add depth without heavy complexity
- Accessible and humorous theme
- Good pacing for casual game nights

Cons:
- More complex than base Saboteur; new players may need guidance
- Endgame can feel slow with secret objectives in play
- Some players might prefer tighter constraints for a more structured game

## Comparison with other Saboteur entries

- Base Saboteur: The classic is simpler, faster, and very approachable, but lacks the extra strategic options found in The Lost Mines.
- Saboteur 2: Saboteur 2 builds on social deduction but shifts the dynamic; The Lost Mines remains closer to the original in feel while expanding the strategic possibilities.

## Matching the right night

- Game nights with friends who enjoy banter and bluffing will have the most fun with The Lost Mines.
- Family-friendly groups will appreciate the lighter tone and accessible mechanics.

## Visuals and components (deep dive)

- Card stock and design: The cards are sturdy with clear icons. The art is vivid but not overstimulating.
- Storage and layout: The box layout is practical; cards and tokens fit comfortably, and there’s little to complain about in terms of organization.
- Player aids: Quick references keep teach times low.

## Final thoughts

Saboteur: The Lost Mines is the kind of expansion that respects the core concept while injecting new ideas that reward clever misdirection and careful reading of opponents. It’s not a flawless reinvention, but it’s a well-balanced, entertaining package that should please both new players and longtime Saboteur fans.

## Final Recommendation

- If you want a lively, social game that rewards bluffing and careful observation, The Lost Mines is a top pick.
- If you’re new to Saboteur, start with the base game to learn the core flow, then bring The Lost Mines for extra flavor.
- For a mixed crowd or family-friendly nights, this is a great candidate to bring out when you want fast play and big laughs.
