Saboteur is a game about dwarves, diamonds, and dramatic acts of sabotage. The Lost Mines is an expansion that adds a new layer to the already chaotic tunnel-building circus, courtesy of Amigo and the master of misdirection, Frédéric Moyersoen. If you thought your group needed more masks to wear while pushing cards across a table, buckle up. The Lost Mines takes the familiar base game and throws in glittering fake gold, new dwarvish personalities, and enough mine cards to make a goblin feel morally conflicted about his career choices.

Below is the full review you did not know you needed but secretly wanted. We cover components, rules, theme, strategy, and how the Lost Mines actually plays with a full table of party-hungry humans pretending to be dwarves. We also sneak in a few comparisons to the base Saboteur, because context is everything, and a little drama never hurt when you are digging for gold under a mountain you might pretend to care about.

## What is Saboteur The Lost Mines
Saboteur The Lost Mines is an expansion that keeps the core rhythm of the original Saboteur intact. You still play as dwarves who must build tunnels to reach a hidden treasure, while some players secretly become saboteurs aiming to derail the expedition. The Lost Mines adds new role cards, new treasure cards, and a fresh set of tunnel cards. It also introduces a diabolic new twist in the form of a lost mine map that can be drawn mid-game, changing the path to the treasure in unpredictable ways.

If you already own the base Saboteur, this expansion plays as a modular add-on. It does not replace the original rules but augments them, turning single rounds into a marathon of clever ploys and last-second reveals. If you are new to Saboteur entirely, you can choose to start with the Lost Mines as a standalone, but most groups will benefit from first mastering the base game and then adding the expansion as a secondary layer of chaos.

## Components and production quality
The Lost Mines keeps the signature palette of Saboteur: bold dwarven faces, bright gems that glimmer with all the subtlety of a disco ball, and slightly wacky mining gear that somehow looks both cute and threatening. The components are sturdy. The new tunnel cards are a touch heavier than the original, which helps them shuffle smoothly and resist the inevitable table flip when a desperate player tries to push a mine card through a sealed tunnel only to realize the tunnel ends up blocked by a bossy goblin card.

New roles come with practical art cues that are easy to read at a glance, which is essential once the table erupts in hot takes about who is innocent and who is clearly digging the wrong way for the right reasons. The treasure cards have a nice mix of obvious riches and sly fakes, which is exactly what you want when you are playing a game about deception wrapped in a mining theme.

One minor quirk: the Lost Mines introduces a few more card backs and a handful of special action cards. If your play area is cramped, make sure you have a dedicated space for the draw stack and discard piles. Otherwise you will spend more time collapsing a fortress of dice bags than digging a tunnel. The rulebook is concise but not overly long, with clear examples and a handful of diagrams that would have made a young me appreciate diagrams in a game about cunning. The quality of the cards themselves is good, with a laminate that handles casual spills and enthusiastic high-fives without curling up like a crab. Overall, the production is up to the Saboteur standard and then some for players who savor a little extra sparkle in every misdirection.

## How the rules change with The Lost Mines
The core mechanics remain familiar. You have objective cards that point to a treasure and a need to connect the path from your starting point to the treasure while a saboteur tries to redirect the route. The Lost Mines adds new roles and a subset of map cards that are specifically designed to complicate tunnel-building. The changes feel additive rather than disruptive, which is a nice balance for a game that thrives on social dynamics.

Key additions include:
- New role cards that provide unique win conditions or special abilities. These add variety without tipping the balance too far toward any one player archetype.
- A set of mine map cards that can appear as the game progresses, forcing players to reassess their plans on the fly.
- Additional treasure cards that come with fresh art and a few quirky side effects that can reward bold plays or punish rash ones.

The setup time increases slightly due to the new deck, but the payoff is a more dynamic start-to-finish arc. You will see different opening strategies, as players weigh the risk of drawing a sabotage card early on versus attempting a quick push for treasure before the map reshapes the board. The Lost Mines also nudges players toward more long-term planning, a welcome shift for groups who felt base Saboteur sometimes rewarded spur-of-the-moment chaos too heavily.

## The table feel: interaction, tension, and humor
The humor remains one of Saboteur's strongest selling points. The Lost Mines extends this through artful new character poses and a few cheeky treasure names. The tension at the table grows not only from the possibility that a fellow dwarf is secretly undermining the tunnel, but from the creeping dread that a new map card could splinter your carefully laid route into a tangle of dead ends and glitter-dust regrets.

Interaction is still front and center. Players must read others, bluff convincingly, and time their reveals to maximize effect. Saboteur thrives on social deduction, and The Lost Mines doubles down. You will witness the proud moment when someone reveals a well-timed sabotage that benefits no one but the person who played it, including the saboteur themselves. That is the magic of Saboteur: the sense that everyone is performing on stage, even when the stage is a long wooden table with coffee rings and the occasional snack crumb.

## Strategy and player roles
So you want to win as a team or at least pretend to? The Lost Mines rewards multi-layer thinking. Here are a few strategic threads you can chase:
- Early commitment vs mid-game pivot: Should you push to complete a tunnel early, risking sabotage exposure, or lay low and let others draw attention while you reserve powerful cards for a late reveal?
- Balancing trust and suspicion: Saboteurs want to stay hidden, but sometimes a well-timed clue can mislead the table more effectively than any blatant lie. The Lost Mines gives you more opportunities to misdirect by introducing new roles with specific tells and abilities.
- Card economy: The expansion adds more treasure cards, which means more options for claiming victory. Use the new cards to force opponents into suboptimal plays or to close off routes they rely on. The key is to manage your hand without tipping your hand.
- Map disruption: The new mine map cards change the geometry of the board. A tunnel you counted on might become a dead end, turning your plan from a clean sprint into a slow siege. The best players treat this as a puzzle rather than a battlefield. Map disruption is not just about screwing someone; it is about bending the board to your narrative of cunning.

If you come from a highly aggressive Saboteur background, you will enjoy the Lost Mines balance. If you prefer a calmer, more calculated approach, you will discover a surprising depth in planning around the new map cards and role interactions. The designers did a solid job of weaving new threads into the old fabric rather than replacing the fabric altogether.

## Player count and pacing
Saboteur shines at the party-scale table, and The Lost Mines does not ruin that. In practice, the expansion plays best with 4 to 6 players. At four, you get quick rounds and sharper reads without overstaying your welcome; at six, you get the delicious chaos of more saboteurs, more possible misdirections, and more opportunities for spectacular comebacks. Ten players might feel a little unwieldy due to the increased downtime between turns; this is a game that thrives on interaction, and with more players there is inevitably more waiting for the table to decide who is allowed to draw a card, then who has to pretend to be surprised by a sabotage that everyone already saw coming.

The pacing is still crisp for a game that is basically a card-driven house of cards. The Lost Mines partially mitigates the hot potato feel of rounds by offering multiple paths to treasure and a few ways to stall a determined saboteur. You will have rounds where clever play locks in a route that looks unbeatable, only to have a new map card turn the table in a stunning, wryly comic twist. In short: the pacing remains one of Saboteur’s best traits, and this expansion preserves that rhythm while injecting a new pulse into the beat.

## Theme, art, and ambiance
The Lost Mines keeps the lighthearted dwarf fantasy vibe intact. The art has a genial cartoonish energy that makes cunning acts feel playful rather than grim, which is essential for a game that can become ruthlessly strategic. Thematically, the notion of lost mines and glittering gems resonates with everyone who has ever pretended to be more heroic than their card draw deserves. There is a celebratory mood to the visuals: you are not simply sabotaging a tunnel; you are putting on a show, and the audience — i.e., your friends — is your front row.

One subtle improvement in this expansion is how it handles fault lines between players. Because the new roles are varied and not all saboteurs are created equal, the social signals you read from other players become more textured. This means a skilled group can generate a narrative around each round, turning the game into a theater of suspicion in which everyone knows that their future self is going to blame their present self for the misdirection. It is the kind of meta-game that makes Saboteur feel clever even when you are losing.

## Solo and group play viability
Saboteur has never been a solo heavy game, and The Lost Mines does not force it into a new lane. You can try a solitaire variant with modifiers, but the expansion shines most when there are other players to joyfully misinterpret your motives. If you have a group that likes social deduction, you will enjoy the fixed cast of new roles and the additional pathways to victory. If you have a group that prefers heavy strategy solitaire-style games, you might find The Lost Mines a bit light without the social friction to keep the mind engaged. It remains perfectly serviceable as a party game, where the objective is to outwit the table in the most entertaining way possible.

## Comparisons to the base Saboteur and other expansions
Compared to the base Saboteur, The Lost Mines feels like a polished, slightly smarter cousin. It preserves the core identity while expanding the toolbox for both saboteurs and honest dwarves. The new map cards are a real standout. They add a tactile sense of improvisation that is missing in the original game, especially on long evenings when players begin to worship the sacred rule of the path and forget that the treasure should be a goal, not a hostage.

Other expansions for Saboteur tend to polarize groups between those who crave more chaos and those who want a more balanced experience. The Lost Mines occupies a sweet middle ground: it gives more options without steering the group toward a single playstyle. If you own additional expansions, you’ll enjoy how well the Lost Mines collaborates with them, creating emergent play patterns that feel both fresh and familiar.

## Replayability and durability
Replayability is the name of the Saboteur game. The Lost Mines adds new cards, new roles, and new decision points, which is essentially a meteor shower of reasons to replay. You will see different outcomes depending on who is the saboteur and how well the table reads the signals. With more role diversity, you will also experience more surprising outcomes where someone who is not the saboteur ends up performing a deceptive act that changes the entire table dynamic. The treasure and map cards have enough variety to keep games feeling new for a long time, which is a godsend for a game that thrives on social nuance.

Durability-wise, the cards are standard Saboteur quality: sturdy enough for frequent play with a decent shuffle. The art is resilient to wear from enthusiastic shuffles and the occasional spill of beverage. If you are a stickler for pristine canopies of table mats, you might want to sleeve the cards, but you will likely never feel the need to do so given the typical play surface and casual nature of this title.

## Final verdict and rating
Saboteur The Lost Mines stands as a worthy addition to the Saboteur family. It does not reinvent the wheel; instead, it polishes the wheel and adds a few new gears to the mechanism. If you enjoy the base game and crave more misdirection, more choice, and more dramatic table moments, this expansion is a strong buy. The new roles and the mine map cards provide enough variance to keep rounds feeling fresh without throwing balance out of the window. It is accessible to newcomers while offering enough nuance to satisfy veteran players who crave a little more tactical depth.

Rating: 8.3/10. It is not a flawless expansion, but it is a handsome, clever, and consistently entertaining addition to a game night. If you want a lighter, social deduction flavored experience that still scratches the itch for strategic tunneling and dramatic reveals, this is a top pick.

## Practical tips for playing The Lost Mines
- Start with a quick house rule about map cards: if a map card reveals a lost mine, the player who drew it must offer one small bargain to the table, such as allowing a single free peek or swapping a card with a neighbor. It keeps things light and reduces the sour mood when a tricky map rearranges the tunnel you were counting on.
- Use the new treasure cards to reward three different playstyles: fast risers who gamble on speed, cunning schemers who slow down the convoy, and opportunists who exploit the map reshuffles for maximum point gain.
- When in doubt, trust your gut. Saboteur is a social experiment. The Lost Mines gives your table a few more levers to pull. Use them with a smile, and if you fail, fail with flair.
