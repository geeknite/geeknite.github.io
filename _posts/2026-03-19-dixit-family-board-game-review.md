## What is Dixit? A Quick Primer
Dixit is a party game that rewards imagination rather than raw tactical ruthlessness. Each round, one player is the storyteller. They pick a card from their hand and utter a clue—a phrase, a sound, a whisper of a memory. The other players select a card from their hand that best matches the clue and submit it face down. The storyteller shuffles the chosen cards and reveals them. Players score points if their card is guessed or if the storyteller’s clue is matched with just the right amount of mystique.

The art on each card is lush, dreamlike, and open to interpretation. The sealed box you hold is not just a container; it is a gateway to countless evenings of giggles, gasps, and—if you are lucky—some surprisingly profound conversations about the strange places your imagination can visit.

## How to Play in 15-ish Minutes (Once You Get It)
Dixit is deceptively simple to learn, but it’s a game that reveals itself more as you play.

- Setup: Shuffle the cards and deal a certain number to each player (usually six). The storyteller for the round is chosen.
- The storyteller picks a card from their hand and gives a clue.
- Each other player selects a card from their hand that matches the clue.
- The cards are revealed and players vote secretly on which card they think belongs to the storyteller.
- Scoring: If no one or everyone guesses the storyteller’s card, the storyteller scores zero or more depending on the variant. If some but not all guess correctly, the storyteller scores points and the supporters also score.
- Next round: The role of storyteller rotates.

The details vary slightly by edition and number of players, but the core promise remains: a night of sharing strange, funny, and perhaps slightly embarrassing stories that somehow involve a teacup, a dragon, and a broomstick.

## Round-by-Round Dynamics: Balancing Clues and Guesses
One of the most delightful tensions in Dixit is the balance between giving a clue that is too obvious and one that is so obscure that no one will get it.

- If the clue is too obvious, players will guess almost everyone’s card, the storyteller’s included, and the point spread becomes lopsided.
- If the clue is too cryptic, only the storyteller’s closest fan will really know what is going on, which is great for the storyteller’s ego but bad for everyone else.

Seasoned players often develop a rhythm: a few rounds of playful misdirection, then a surprising, almost cinematic clue that ties together multiple cards in a delightful way. The best clues are those that spark a chorus of Aha around the table, followed by a chorus of what was that one again as someone insists their card matches a memory they barely believed in five minutes earlier.
