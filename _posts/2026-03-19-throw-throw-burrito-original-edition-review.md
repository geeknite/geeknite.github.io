## What is Throw Throw Burrito, Original Edition?

Throw Throw Burrito is a dexterity card game with a card-scoring twist and a physical element that sets it apart from your typical card-laying affair. The Original Edition—what we’re here to review—includes two plush burritos that players fling with reckless abandon, a deck of cards with matching combinations, and the promise that the player who collects the most points by forming matching sets will be crowned the burrito-throwing champion (or at least the last person still standing after the table erupts in laughter and chaos).

The core idea is simple: you draw cards and try to match them. Each match has a different value and a potential penalty, and when someone yells “Burrito!” you pause and sprint into a dodge-ball-esque moment of strategic chaos, while the burritos fly like caffeinated comets. The Original Edition is the classic setup that introduced players to the wild mix of memory, speed, luck, and physical silliness that has since become a hallmark of the Exploding Kittens line.

This is a game that thrives on group energy. It’s not the kind of thing you want to play with a silent room where the only sound is the ticking of a clock. It’s the kind of game that requires a little noise, a lot of elbow room, and a willingness to embrace chaos as a social lubricant. Thematically, it embraces a goofy, over-the-top vibe that fits perfectly with geeky gatherings: memes, pop culture references, and inside jokes bloom in the margins as players dodge soft burritos and hunt for match combos.

If you’re the kind of person who loves board games but hates the sterile calm before the big roll, Throw Throw Burrito is your chaotic rideshare to fun-town. It’s a game that doesn’t pretend to be serious. It’s here to deliver laugh-out-loud moments, some friendly taunting, and a dramatic crescendo that may or may not include a mild kitchen-ware brawl (okay, maybe not; but the possibility is absolutely there).

## Components: what’s inside the Original Edition box?

The Original Edition box is not a luxury champagne flute; it’s a sturdy, no-frills arena for chaos. Inside, you’ll find:

- A deck of cards featuring combos, matchups, and penalty triggers.
- Two plush burritos (soft, huggable, and entirely capable of causing near-misses and giggles).
- A rulebook that is mercifully short, which is a blessing for anyone who inhaled a burrito scent and forgot how to count to three without shouting.
- A few tokens or markers that help track points and round progression.

The cards themselves carry the visual signature of Exploding Kittens: bold, cartoonish illustrations with a playful sense of mischief. It’s a design language that says: we are here to entertain you, we’re not afraid to look silly, and yes, you’ll probably remember this image the next day when you’re trying to explain to your mom what a “kayak of kitties” is supposed to mean. The art supports the humor rather than overshadowing the game’s mechanics, which is a win in my book.

The burritos are soft, which is critical for safety and comfort. You don’t want a burrito that bites back; you want a burrito that sizzles with soft air and a moment of dramatic tension just before it is hurled. The design of the plush burrito is that perfect balance between novelty and practicality: big enough to feel exciting when you throw it, small enough to avoid causing physical injury or property damage in normal living rooms.

Now, let’s talk about the boîte de chaos in more detail: the card design. Each card has a clear illustration and a short, punchy effect. There are the “Skip” cards that force players to draw more cards, there are the “Banter” cards that encourage players to talk smack in humorous ways, and there are the “Match” cards that set up the scoring. The idea is that you’re constantly juggling two things at once: what cards you should collect for points and how to avoid being the target of a burrito hurled by a rival who is determined to steal your glory.


## How to play: a practical overview without spoilers

The game is designed for 2–6 players and moves quickly, especially once you shed the fear of missing your window to dive into the burrito blitz. A typical round involves drawing cards, selecting matches, and then resolving the action with a burrito toss or a quick attack sequence that is more performance art than anything else.

Setup: shuffle the deck, place the burritos within arm’s reach, and decide on a starting player. The goal is to accumulate matches that trigger points while avoiding the penalties that pop up when you’re the target of a “Burrito Barage” moment, which is exactly as chaotic as it sounds.

Turn flow: on your turn, you draw, you look for a possible match, and if you complete a match you gain points. The tricky part is that you cannot simply hoard cards forever; the game’s tension comes from the fact that other players will attempt to disrupt your progress by playing “Dodge” or “Block” style cards or by forcing you into the burrito battlegrounds when they cry out a round-ending buzzword. The exact mechanic is fun because it blends memory, speed, and a pinch of strategic risk-taking.

Burrito showdown: when a showdown is triggered, the burritos come out and the players have a rapid, short window in which to physically dodge and evade. Here is where the game earns its reputation: the room becomes a stage, players shout, and the sound of soft fabric colliding with air becomes the soundtrack of your game night. Safety is important, of course: keep the throws to the body of the person, not the face or the screen; the plush burritos are designed to be non-damaging, but it’s still wise to play with a clear space and a couple of soft-swaddling safeguards (and perhaps a friendly reminder to keep the throws gentle when your aunt is in the same orbit as the burrito cannon).

End condition: the round ends when a set number of matches are collected or when the timer says “time’s up.” The game then tallies points and resets for another round. The tempo is fast, the laughter is loud, and if you’re playing with a competitive crowd, expect some players to plan a strategic burrito ambush that would make a caped crusader nod in approval.


## The chaos meter: why this game thrives as a party title

One of Throw Throw Burrito’s strongest design decisions is its pacing. It never becomes a slog. The addition of the burrito-dodging mechanic ensures there is always something happening, and because everyone is frequently a target, no one can long hide behind a defensive posture. This creates a social dynamic that feels a lot like a chaotic, family-friendly competitive sport rather than a passive card game.

From a design perspective, the game uses social tension as its engine. People want to perform, to outsmart others, to time their burrito toss for maximum impact—without causing a real-life disaster on your carpet. The Original Edition nails this by balancing quick rounds with enough strategic choice to avoid turning into a total luck-fest. There is room for skillful play, but there’s also enough randomness to keep the vibe light and unpredictable. If you’re into the kind of party game where you yell, giggle, and possibly cry with laughter, Throw Throw Burrito is going to feel like a perfect fit.

## Practical tips: maximizing fun while minimizing frustration

- Clear space matters: a generous table or open floor area reduces the risk of accidental bumps or knocked-over drinks. A couple of soft baby gates or chair cushions around the play area can help keep things safe—and prevent a burrito from ricocheting into someone’s soda.
- Rotate the burrito bishops: if someone is a natural target and keeps getting shelled, consider rotating who handles the burritos. It’s not about “punishing” the target; it’s about keeping the game light and giving the whole group moments to shine.
- Set a time cap or round limit: for larger groups, a timed session can keep energy high and prevent fatigue from turning the fun into fatigue. The pacing remains dynamic even when you cap rounds, so you don’t end up in a never-ending loop of chaos.
- Have a quick debrief: after a couple of rounds, pause to applaud great plays and good dodges. A little positive reinforcement goes a long way in maintaining the party atmosphere and keeping hands away from actual bristle walls of the living room.
- Consider a safety baseline: remind players to avoid throwing burritos at the face. The primary target is the body, not eyes or ears. A little humor and a lot of common sense will keep the night enjoyable for everyone.
