## Overview

Codenames: Marvel is a licensed version of the classic party game Codenames designed for four or more players, split into two teams. The red team and the blue team compete to locate their agents among a 5x5 grid of word cards. Each card features a Marvel character name or a term related to the wider Marvel universe. The Spymaster for each team gives one-word clues followed by a number that indicates how many words on the board are related to that clue. The opposing Spymaster tries to stump the other team with clever connections, while the assassin word must be avoided at all costs. If you aren’t careful, you could inadvertently “kill” your own agents before you reveal all of them. It’s a high-energy mix of deduction, misdirection, and occasionally thunderous laughter when a clue hilariously misses its mark.

The Marvel edition keeps the familiar trapdoors and tension of the base game, but with a thematic glow that makes the clues feel like they belong to a shared universe rather than a generic soup of words. For Marvel fans, this is a gentle, caffeinated version of the universe you know—one where you might shout out a clue like Iron Man 3 and hope your teammates pick up the right 3 cards that reference Tony Stark, arc reactors, and tech that would make Jarvis nod approvingly.

## How to play Marvel: quick refresher
For anyone stepping into the game for the first time, here is a concise refresher that highlights how this Marvel edition sticks to the core Codenames rules while adding a thematic twist.

- The game is played by two teams: red and blue. Each team has a Spymaster and field operatives.
- The board contains 25 word cards. Each word card hides a Marvel relation: it might be a character, a concept, or a film element.
- The Spymaster gives one-word clues followed by a number. The clue word should connect to as many of your team’s word cards as possible without steering your teammates toward the opposing team’s words, neutral words, or the dreaded assassin.
- Teammates discuss and pick cards one by one, guided by the clue. If a team taps a card that belongs to their color, it’s covered with that team’s agent card. If they pick an opponent’s word, it ends their turn and turns the card over but gives control to the other team. If they hit a neutral word, the turn ends. If they hit the assassin, they instantly lose. The first team to reveal all of their agents wins.
- In the Marvel edition, Marvel flavor nudges the clues, but the mechanic remains the same. The clue should still be a single word, with a spoken number. Two-word clues are generally discouraged, since they blur the line between clever and chaotic. If your group loves Marvel, you’ll find the theme adds warmth without complicating the core rules. If your group is new to Codenames, keep to one-word clues and you’ll be fine.

## Strategies and tips
If you want to tilt the odds in your favor without becoming a clairvoyant god of deduction, here are some practical strategies tailored to Codenames: Marvel (Inglés).

- Know your deck but don’t memorize it. It’s tempting to memorize that the deck includes a lot of “A” words or “B” words or specific heroes, but a memory-based approach quickly collapses under pressure. Use flexible, general connections rather than hyper-specific ones that only work in certain boards.
- Favor one-word but broad clues. The rule is one word and a number. Use a clue that can feasibly tie to several of your own cards without pulling in too many of the opponent’s cards. A good clue often lands at the sweet spot: it’s evocative but not overly precise.
- Leverage Marvel synergy understandings. If you know your team is comfortable with a certain character or concept, you can anchor your clues around that. A clue like “Stark 3” could consolidate three Stark-related words on the board if your teammates see those cards and connect with Tony Stark.
- Mind the assassin. The assassin word is the kicker of any round. You can plan a clue that avoids leading toward the assassin while still hitting your own targets. It’s a balancing act: risk a little for potential big payoff, or play it safe and drag the game longer.
- Read the table. Often the best clue is a shared memory or a small in-joke. The people across the table will reveal their own biases and knowledge. Use those cues to tailor your clues in future rounds, adjusting your risk tolerance to fit the group’s mood.
- Use sarcasm and humor. This is a party game after all. A clever moment when a clue aligns perfectly with one teammate’s memory or a misinterpretation by the other side can turn the table into a chatty, laughter-filled arena—and that’s where the energy lies.
