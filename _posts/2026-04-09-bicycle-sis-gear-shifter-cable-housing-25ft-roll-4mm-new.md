## Installation: clean cable feeling

Installing new housing is a ritual, not a chore. Whether you are a veteran mechanic or a weekend tinkerer, there are a few universal truths: keep your cables clean, keep your housing ends square, and always test after a run to ensure you did not just create a more dramatic evidence of your own impatience.

### Tools you will need

- A cable cutter designed for housing and cables (avoid the paper-cutter hack, it will pretend to be a tool but will ruin your ends)
- A ferule crimping tool or a small set of ferrule pliers
- Cable cutters for inner cable
- A little bit of light grease or cable lubricant for smooth movement
- A clean workspace and a beacon of patience

### Step by Step install guide

1. Remove the old housing and inspect the inner cable for wear. If the inner cable shows blade-like wear or rust, replace both housing and cable. Do not monkey with damaged hardware; you are not a blacksmith in a very messy workshop.
2. Cut the new housing to the exact length required. It helps to route it along the frame to avoid sharp bends. Remember that housing should be trimmed to a length that does not cause tension on the entire system, but does not dangle like a broken Christmas light either.
3. Fit ferrules if your frame relies on them. Ferrules help keep the end of the housing from fraying while also providing a neat passage for the cable through the frame stops.
4. Thread the inner cable through the housing, ensuring the end cap is seated properly. A tiny dab of grease at the entry can help guide the cable through with less frustration.
5. Clamp the cable into the derailleur and the shifter, adjusting tension accordingly. You want a feel that is snappy but not jittery; excessive tension can cause shifting to jump and misalign.
6. Test the system by cycling through gears while pedaling. Listen for any rubbing or binding and inspect the housing ends for any uneven seating. If something sounds off, stop and reassess before you ride into the sunset with misaligned bragging rights.

### Common pitfalls and how to avoid them

- Kinking: The 25ft roll is long, but that does not mean you should feed it like a spaghetti dish. Make gentle curves and avoid tight radii. If the route requires a sharp bend, consider a small-radius guide or an alternative routing path.
- Fraying: Ensure ends are cleanly cut and properly seated into ferrules. Fraying can lead to the cable snagging and messing up even the best shifting plan.
- Contamination: Keep the inner cables clean during installation. Dirt acts like glue on a critical interface and will degrade performance quickly.
- Tension imbalance: Do not over-tighten. You want crisp responses, not a situation where every gear change feels like a mini workout for your wrists.

## Compatibility and versatility

One of the biggest selling points for any housing is compatibility. The SIS 25ft roll is versatile enough to support a wide range of derailleurs and shifters that use a standard 4mm housing. The key compatibility notes to keep in mind are:
- Inner cables should be standard 1.1 to 1.2 mm diameter. If your inner cable is significantly larger or older with burrs, consider replacing the cable while replacing the housing to maximize performance.
- Frame routing: Some frames with ultra-narrow internal channels may present a challenge. In those cases, a thinner or shorter run may be required, or you may need to use curved guides to prevent binding.
- Ferrule compatibility: If your frame uses ferrules, ensure the housing ends fit cleanly and clamp without play. Loose ferrules can introduce wobble and degrade shifting accuracy.
