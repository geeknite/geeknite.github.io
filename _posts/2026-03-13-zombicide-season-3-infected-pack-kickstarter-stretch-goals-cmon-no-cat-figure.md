Introduction: The Kickstarted World We Live In

Kickstarter has become the new friendly neighborhood vending machine: you put in some cash, pull the lever, and hope a tray of shiny plastic emerges, occasionally with a cat-shaped refund policy. CMON’s Zombicide line has been a reliable, if indulgent, source of tabletop charisma for years. Season 3’s Infected Pack promises a new wave of undead fun and a handful of stretch goals designed to coax your hobby budget into staying up late and making decisions you’ll regret on payday.

What we’re really paying for here isn’t just new zombies; it’s functionally a miniatures buffet, a new set of scenarios to run as you attempt to remember where you left your D20, and a reminder that you probably don’t actually need a new way to pretend you know how to herd the undead. But if your table demands more, the Infected Pack is here with spikes, teeth, and enough bling to justify a second mortgage on your gaming closet.

The No Cat Figure: A Tale of a Meme That Never Was

One of the most persistent rumors around this stretch goals cascade is the supposed “cat figure” that would ride into the apocalypse on a tiny, heroic cat-sled. The cat is the most gastronomically-laden meme of the modern tabletop era—cats as mascots, cats as survivors, cats as commanding officers—so the absence of a cat miniature in a Cat-Approved universe felt like a cosmic joke. CMON has released official statements in the past about cats that sit on shelves and watch you roll dice with the judgment of a thousand inner-monologues. The absence in this pack is… confusing, but also delightful in a way that only a room full of tabletop enthusiasts can appreciate.

Sure, it would have been adorable to field a feline ally who purrs as you roll perfect criticals, but the no-cat outcome is a reminder that the zombie apocalypse is serious business, and there’s zero tolerance for fluff that isn’t properly in scale with heroic cat-APR (ably Painted Results). So, while we may mourn the mythical cat figure, we still get a spectacular menagerie of infected that will gnash their way into your memory banks for the long haul.

What’s in the Infected Pack?

The core of any kickstarter expansion is not just “more minis”; it’s “more story per table.” The Infected Pack is designed to offer a batch of new zombies with distinctive mechanics, new survivors that mix up how you approach problems (or how you try to avoid the inevitable bite), and a batch of optional rules to shake up your campaigns. The aim is to give you fresh tactical headaches and the satisfaction of a well-painted horde sweeping across your board.

- New infected sculpts: Expect a few standouts—brutish brutes that punch your line, fast zomboids that threaten to flank you off your own chair, and the sly, cunning walkers that feel like they aren’t just mindless meat but rather tiny, judgmental bruisers with a plan.
- New survivors: Each survivor has unique quirks that matter in a skirmish where every action counts. You’ll want to draft a crew that balances range, melee, and the occasional “I’ve got a plan… sort of.”
- Thematic scenarios: A pack that expects you to think about objectives beyond “kill all the walkers.” Objectives can be as cunning as a cat meme that never existed—just kidding about the cat, not the cat-as-protagonist in spirit.
- Tokens, cards, and new terrain: The typical CMON treasure trove, enough to keep your table adding little inches to the board and making zany strategies feel plausible for more than two games in a row.

All that said, one needs to approach any stretch goal avalanche with a few practical brains: balance, quality, and value. The good stretch goals should feel like upgrades you’d actually want, not just re-skins that require a second mortgage to justify. The Infected Pack seems to push for that balance, offering new mechanical depth without turning the game into a full-on logistics exercise for your group. The big question is, do the new minis actually fit with your version of Zombicide? Are the sculpts distinct enough to justify painting time? Let’s take a closer look.

Sculpt and Production Quality: A Quick Reality Check

CMON’s production values in recent years have become part of the brand’s identity: heavy duty plastic, clean casts, and the occasional “favorite new zombie” who looks like they spent a wee bit too long in the meth lab of modeling (in a good way). The Season 3 infected have a few standout features:

- Poses that scream “I am not just a zombie, I am a threat.” Some walkers are still creeping along with that classic slow-burn approach, but there are others that seem to have an attitude, with arms flung forward as if trying to grab you by the throat and whisper, “you forgot to check your corners.”
- Details that pop under paint: Foliage, armor bits, and gore accents that become character when you apply a wash. If you like painting, you’ll have a field day. If you’re more of a “place primer and call it good” type, you’ll still appreciate the tonal integrity of the sculpts.
- Cast quality and uniformity: CMON often nails consistent sizing across a slate of figures, a critical factor for assembling multiple “zombie waves” without finding mismatched armor on a tall guy who isn’t supposed to be that tall.

In short: the production side is in line with the brand’s reputation. If you’re a stickler for uniform bases, clean seams, and the occasional dramatic spear-bearing zombie, you’ll enjoy the Infected Pack’s minis as much as you enjoy your favorite coffee grinder’s last ripple of steam after a long run on a Saturday morning.

Gameplay Dynamics: How the Infected Pack Changes the Table

Zombicide thrives on synergy and chaos—the two things that make board games feel alive without requiring you to purchase a gym membership for a character sheet. The Infected Pack introduces new dynamics that can be both exciting and punishing. Here are the core gameplay implications you’ll actually notice at the table:

- Mobility and threat assessment: Some new walkers create pressure from angles you weren’t expecting. This means you’ll need to think about line of sight and corner control in new ways. If you’ve been playing with Season 1 and 2 survivors, you’ll be pleasantly surprised by how the new mechanics nudge you toward more careful planning.
- Survivors with specialized quirks: The new roster isn’t just “more shooty dudes.” Some survivors come with situational tools that reward risk-taking and reward your squad for coordinating actions—perfect for those nights when your group is feeling hungry for tactical chess rather than Munchkin-level chaos.
- Objective layering: The new scenarios don’t simply ask you to survive; they challenge you to accomplish multi-step tasks while the undead push you to the brink. It’s a good reminder that Zombicide is as much about teamwork as it is about rolling dice and hoping for a critical.
- Pace and board density: With more figures on the board, encounters become denser. This can slow things down, especially if your group is juggling paint cycles and snack runs. If your playgroup likes quick sessions, you may want to field smaller boards or lean into the already-deadly-but-earnest pace of the expansion.

From a pure “fun factor” perspective, the new pack adds the kind of emphasis on careful positioning and boss-mika (boss-like mini moments) that keeps experienced players engaged while giving newer players something tangible to latch onto. It’s not just extra minis; it’s an invitation to practice new decision trees and to re-evaluate risk tolerance around each corner.

Value for Money: Are We Getting a Deal? (Yes, with Caveats)

Pricing on kickstarters is a careful dance between perceived value and actual reality. The Infected Pack sits in a tiered structure: you get the base pack with a handful of new miniatures, then stretch goals unlock mass quantities of additional zombies, weapons, scenarios, and accents. For many players, the enthusiasm is less about “is this a good deal” and more about “are these unique enough to justify the shelf space.” Here are some thoughts you’ll likely have at the table:

- If you’re a completionist: The pack can feel irresistible. You’re loading up a campaign that promises fresh aesthetics and a renewed challenge. The addition of new survivors means your old crew won’t be stuck doing the same thing time after time, and that sense of novelty is priceless when your weekly game night has become a tradition.
- If you’re cost-conscious: The incremental cost of the stretch goals can feel steep, especially if you’re not integrating every additional element into your campaign. You’ll want to estimate the number of sessions you’ll actually run with the new content and compare that to the price of a few alternative expansions.
- If you’re space-limited: Zombicide minis accumulate. The Infected Pack adds more models to paint and store. If your hobby space is a tiny desk with a lamp and three coffee cups, you’ll appreciate a well-planned painting schedule and a plan to rotate the display board. Alternatively, you can cash in the dopamine hit by painting a small subset of the new minis and leaving the rest for a future plateau of ambition.

Overall, value is a function of playtime and your love of the brand. The Infected Pack delivers a noticeable increase in table presence and narrative potential, which translates into long-term enjoyment if you’re willing to invest the time to paint and integrate the new figures into campaigns.

The Cat Conundrum: Why No Cat Figure Might Actually Be Great

Yes, we circle back to the non-existent cat figure with the same melodramatic fervor you reserve for late-night game-night snack debates. The absence of a cat figure is not a tragedy; it’s a narrative choice that actually makes room for other surprises. If there had been a cat figure, you’d have a museum-grade piece that would demand a corner-case painting plan, a dedicated rule interaction, and inevitably a different table dynamic as someone tries to “sneak” the cat into a zombie melee to gain a meta advantage.

The no-cat decision keeps the focus on the core zombie-driven storytelling and squad tactics. It’s a small price to pay for a cleaner retail value proposition and fewer “look, a cardboard cat” moments that can derail a campaign by half a game. In other words: the absence is the kind of quiet restraint you appreciate when your board is already a chaos-friendly canvas of plastic chaos.

Building a Campaign: Tips for a Smooth Integration

- Start small: Integrate 2–3 new infected figures into your first session to ease players into the new mechanics without overwhelming them.
- Map the objectives: Read through the scenarios and choose a campaign that emphasizes teamwork. The new stakes are meaningful when you can narrate why your survivors decide to split their duties instead of going full “lone wolf.”
- Painting plan: Schedule a slow paint-night for the new minis. A little patience goes a long way towards table presence and player buy-in.
- Balance the table: If your group has a dedicated power-gamer (the person who will optimize every interaction), consider rotating the MVP role to ensure everyone feels included and no one gets a free pass for “stat-checking” savviness.

Final Thoughts: The Infected Pack as a Tabletop Narrative Engine

The Zombicide Season 3 Infected Pack is not just a box of plastic. It is a design that invites you to rethink how you tell stories on the table. It gives you new tools to craft tense moments, bold pushes, and last-gasp saves that would be unbelievable if you weren’t playing a board game about surviving a zombie apocalypse. Does it deliver enough content to justify its price? If you’re in for a campaign with new mechanics, deeper co-op coordination, and a fresh coat of paint on old strategies, the answer leans toward yes—especially if you’re building a long-term Zombicide shelf with a plan for future expansions.

But if you’re the kind of player who jumps from one shiny thing to the next, there are legitimate concerns about value and storage. The decision rests on how much you love the world, the group you play with, and whether you’re ready to commit to painting and playing with all the new bits. If you want instant excitement with minimal friction, you might want to sample a few missions first, or join a local game night to see how well your group adapts to the new dynamics without committing to a full pledge.

Recommendation: Should You Get It?
