### The box condition and the myth of “mint” vs “vtg”
When you’re dealing with vintage open-box items, the term “2nd VTG” is less about a creek in Vermont and more about the quality grade the seller slapped on the product to justify the price. VTG, in collector language, is shorthand for vintage-grade, which usually translates into “used, but with love.” In this Pictionary set, the condition you’ll encounter is typically categorized as follows:

- Card stock: Expect some minimal edge wear, a few surface scratches from years of flipping and fanning, but nothing that makes the deck unusable. The print quality from 1987 remains crisp enough to read; you’ll recognize that font that screams “arcade-era charm.”
- Box: The lid may show scuffs or a bit of spine wear from being stacked at a garage sale or tucked away in a closet. Don’t expect pristine glue lines; you’ll likely see a gentle separation where the box once opened to reveal a world of doodling dreams.
- Markers and accessories: The marker, if present, may need a quick test run. It’s 80s plastic, which means it’s either a miracle of modern restoration or a stubborn stubborn thing that needs a fresh nib. If it’s missing, you won’t miss much—colleagues will still be able to read your drawings by squinting through a nostalgic haze.
- Pages and sheets: Score sheets and instruction cards can show yellowing or tiny creases, which is honestly part of the charm. It’s like reading a weathered map that leads you to a treasure trove of laughter.

Bottom line: 2nd VTG means “vintage-tinged nostalgia with workable parts.” It’s not mint; it’s an artifact that invites a story: the story of a family night that devolved into interpretive doodling, and the legend of someone who drew a shark so aggressively that the rest of the room swore they could smell the ocean. ## Nostalgia meets game design: how Pictionary 2nd Edition works in the 1980s and today
Pictionary has always been about time boxing, big drawings, and the occasional dramatic swoop of a marker. The 2nd Edition retained the core premise while embracing the era’s graphic design and word prompts that feel both timeless and terribly specific to the 1980s. Here’s how it plays out then and how it holds up now:

- Teams and turns: Two teams, a drawing player per round, and a timer that drives the energy. In the original 1987 rhythm, you had seconds to interpret a clue and shout your guess. Today, you can replicate that pace with a smartphone timer, but the 1987 version had a kind of raw intensity that digital timers can’t fully mimic.
- Word prompts: The clue cards push players toward everyday objects, phrases, and scenarios that are both universal and era-specific. Some cards still elicit “oh yeah, we all know that” moments, while others require a little guesswork and a lot of imagination.
- Drawing tools: The marker is a central character in the show. The better the marker, the more confident the doodle, the faster the guesses, and the funnier the misinterpretations. In an open-box scenario, you’ll sometimes find a marker that refuses to render a convincing pineapple for longer than a minute, which only adds to the chaos.
- Scoring: Simple and satisfying. The first team to complete a set number of correct guesses wins. It’s a race, a giggle, and a test of how well you can translate a crude sketch into a shared joke.

Today’s playthroughs often involve house rules—timers extended by a friend who loves a longer build-up, or “no erasing” rounds where every mistake becomes a performance piece. The classic 1987 edition rewards big, bold strokes and rapid-fire guessing; you get more laughs out of cartoons that look suspiciously like a friendly blob person than you do from pixel-perfect renderings. That’s not a flaw; that’s a feature. The joy of Pictionary is in the mutual embarrassment, not the art degree you didn’t quite finish.
