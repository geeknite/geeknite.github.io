# Monolith Editions Batman Who Laughs SW (New) - A Geeknite Review

In this deep dive, we peel back the glossy shrink wrap of Monolith Editions' latest stab at corporate nightmare fuel: a board game centered on the Batman Who Laughs. Yes, the character who looks like Batman’s grimace folded into a Spoilt Rotten Joker-ization. This is not just another superhero skirmish; it’s a compact chaos engine wrapped in a box that might as well be a safe for the most chaotic of gamers. If you have mild panic in your heart and a craving for dice that curse you in a friendly voice, strap in. We’re going to talk about components, gameplay, theme, art direction, and whether this new release is a keeper for your shelf of glossy bad decisions.

> If you’re new to the universe: The Batman Who Laughs is a DC villain hybrid who embodies the Joker’s chaos with Batman’s tactical genius. It’s a mash-up that sounds like a cross between a prank call and a heist movie, and the game tries to bottle that into a tabletop experience. Whether you’re here for story, strategy, or the absurd joy of seeing your self-proclaimed “master plan” get hijacked by a neon-green-clad nightmare, this release promises to scratch that itch.

## What this release aims to do

Monolith Editions has a track record for big-scale minis, scenario‑driven play, and components that feel thicker than your patience on a lazy Sunday. This new box—often marketed with a wink to fans of the Batman Who Laughs—claims to deliver a compact, fast-paced skirmish with a strong thematic throughline: you either align with or against the Laughs. The intent is to provide a quick-start experience that scales to longer campaigns, with a design that rewards clever play over brute force.

The box itself looks like it survived a marketing meeting where someone yelled, “Make it chaotic but collectible!” The art direction leans into neon-green accents, exaggerated villainous poses, and a map that looks like someone spilled coffee on a blueprint of Gotham after a windstorm of existential dread. In short: it’s loud in all the right ways and not afraid to lean into the silliness of its premise.

### The components: a closer look

What you get in the box matters as much as what you can do with it. Here’s a quick tour of the core kit:

- Miniatures: A handful of sculpts that range from delightfully grotesque to “this is what happens when you let a gremlin design a Batman villain lineup.” The Laughs himself is a centerpiece—statuesque, jagged lines, and enough menace to justify the price of admission. Some sculpts carry a lot of detail; others lean toward stylized caricature. Expect some assembly, depending on your edition. Loading the minis with basing material is entirely optional but highly recommended if you want your board to double as a tiny art gallery.
- Dice and tokens: A standard set of dice, plus action tokens, fear tokens, and a stack of “twist” cards that can flip your strategy faster than a villain can flip a coin. The color palette mirrors the box art: high-contrast blues, neon greens, and enough black to keep the mystery alive.
- Cards: Encounter cards, power cards, and a few scenario decks. The card art is punchy, with the Laughs’ grinning visage appearing on more card backs than you probably expected. The card text gamifies tension—cards come with both positive and negative side effects, encouraging you to weigh the risk/reward of every decision.
- Board/map: If there’s a hero side, it might be a map; if there’s a villain side, it’s a more abstract tile layout. The map is modular enough to offer a bit of variety, which is essential for replayability and for players who get bored after three rounds of clearing a single hallway while muttering heroic one-liners.
- Rulebook: A compact booklet that sometimes reads like a witty novel’s footnotes. It’s not the driest manual, but you won’t need a translator—no language barrier here, just a few game terms that demand a quick gloss.

If you’re a tactile person, the quality of the components lands somewhere between “premium” and “premium with a wink.” Some finish on the minis is sharp; other parts might require a pin vise or hobby tools if you’re particular about flawless assembly. It’s not a production slip; it’s a deliberate choice to keep costs plausible while delivering showpiece pieces that show up on camera well.

## Setup: getting from box to board in under an hour

As a general rule for a Monolith game of this scale, you’ll want to budget a modest block of time for setup. The Laughs aesthetic is worth it—the ritual of popping the cards, laying out the modular board, and snapping miniatures into their bases builds a little ceremony around the game. Expect 15–25 minutes to sort and assemble, depending on how meticulous you want to be about base textures and shading.

A few setup notes to accelerate your table time:

- Separate your tokens by type (fear, influence, power) and place the reference sheets within arm’s reach. The more time you save from hunting down “Which token is which again?” the more time you have for villainous banter.
- Pre-sort your scenario decks. The Laughs cards tend to rush in a way that feels theatrical; pre-sorting helps avoid last-minute scrambles to find the right card mid-boss-fight.
- If you’re playing with a new group, a quick 5-minute rules primer can save hours of “wait, what does this one do?” It’s a small investment with a big payoff.

## Core gameplay: how the turns flow and why you’ll either smile or cry

The game aims to capture the chaotic thrill of a Batman Who Laughs scenario while offering a digestible ruleset for casual play and a surprisingly crunchy layer for veterans. Here’s what you’ll typically experience on a normal turn:

- Phase sequence: A typical turn weaves through draw, action, and event phases with the occasional twist card interruption. The rhythm is steady, not breathless, which suits players who enjoy a bit of tempo without the frantic pace of a bullet train.
- Player actions: Movement, attack, defend, and using power cards. The Laughs’ deck adds a thematic twist by injecting “twist” events that can tilt the tide suddenly. You’ll feel the tension when you have a solid plan and the deck throws a curveball your way—the storytelling is where this game shines.
- AI/Enemy behavior: The Laughs and his crew are not mindless goons; they have a few autoplay rules that simulate a cunning adversary. The AI is not perfectly deterministic; it’s designed to feel like a plan that’s always one step ahead of you—sometimes literally one step away from disaster.
- Victory conditions: Generally framed as completing a narrative-driven objective before you’re overwhelmed by danger, while occasionally needing to trap or corral the Laughs into a string of bad decisions. It’s classic co-op stakes with a theatrical villain spice.

If you’re familiar with co-op skirmish games, you’ll notice the pacing tries to balance tension with playable safety valves: you’re encouraged to cooperate, but you’re never guaranteed a clean victory if you mismanage the threat track. The Laughs is not a “point-and-click” boss; it’s a dynamic antagonist that rewards adaptive play and memory of what the deck has shown you so far.

## Thematic integration: does the Batman Who Laughs hold court on the table?

Theme is the glue that binds a game like this. Monolith has leaned into the iconic imagery—Pause for a moment to admire how the Laughs’ mask seems to grin even when the art on the cards is grim. The balance between heroism and villainy is the canvas on which the game paints its story. The minions reinforce the mood: you’re fighting for Gotham’s soul, but the villain’s philosophy is too deliciously cruel to ignore.

The rules support the theme by rewarding cunning, long-term planning, and risk-taking. You’ll often find yourself triangulating between saving a fellow hero, stopping a local disaster, and keeping the Laughs from reaching a critical board state that accelerates a losing scenario. Thematic cues—sound effects you imagine when you flip a powerful card, those little “breathe in, punch out” moments—enhance the experience beyond a dry mechanical exercise.

However, as with many theme-first games, you might occasionally notice a mismatch between what the box photo promises and what you actually do on the table. If you crave ultra-tight realism in combat simulation, you may feel the mechanics take a few liberties for the sake of drama. If you prefer a lean, rules-light experience, you may wish for fewer card effects and more direct combat resolution. In either case, you’re not buying a pure wargame; you’re buying an atmospheric, story-forward experience with some crunchy bits you’ll learn to love (or lovingly mock).

## Mechanically: what actually works and what’s a toss-up

- Balance of luck and skill: Dice and card draws are the twin engines of chaos. The Laughs thrives on misdirection and timing, but you still have agency—the decisions you make with limited information become the backbone of victory or defeat. If you enjoy both planning and improvisation, you’ll feel right at home.
- Complexity curve: The core rules are approachable enough for newcomers, but there’s enough depth to satisfy veteran players who want to dissect combos and optimize routes to victory. The recommended player count is generally friendly to 2–4 players, with solo play possibilities that can scratch that itch when you’re flying solo against the nightmare clown.
- Replayability: With modular boards, a deck of unique encounter cards, and several scenario setups, you’re not locked into a single path. The Laughs can be confronted in multiple flavors—each run can feel distinct. The “twist” mechanic ensures at least one new trick each round. For people who hate replays getting stale, this is a strong argument in favor of this particular release.
- Table presence: The minis and cards look fantastic on camera and at the table. If you run a game night with friends who like to stream, the visuals are likely to attract chatter and, occasionally, mockery (in good fun). The aesthetic is a selling point that should not be underestimated; players often connect with a title because the art resonates with them emotionally and visually.

## Solo and co-op play: does it scale down well?

This release scales nicely for solo play, offering a robust challenge without needing a party. The Laughs becomes a formidable test of memory and planning, not unlike playing a chess puzzle with a toxic giggle attached. Co-op play shines when you have a stable group that enjoys bickering in a friendly, character-driven way—teams will discuss the best move, debate the ethics of “saving the city” versus “saving a character,” and occasionally sulk when someone chooses to hoard all the dice for a grand finale that never arrives.

One caveat for solo players: the AI can feel a tad predictable after a few games. This isn’t a fatal flaw, but it’s a reminder that you might want to throw in a few fan-made house rules or mix in another scenario deck to keep things unpredictable. If you’re into pure solo-perfect puzzles, you might find the middle portion of the game a touch forgiving. Still, the sense of accomplishment when you carve a path through a particularly nasty card chain is worth the effort.

## Components in practice: build quality meets practical use

The physicality of the components matters for long-term enjoyment. The miniatures, boards, and card stock all hold up under repeated play and transport. Storage is not a trivial issue—there are a dozen little tokens and several deck boxes to sort—but that’s the cost of a well-constructed game with enough personality to justify the box’s heft.

When you flip over a card and read a new effect, there’s a tactile satisfaction: the card stock has that satisfying resistance to the fingers, the corners are rounded enough to endure a few dozen table flips, and the iconography is consistent enough to keep misplays to a minimum once you’re familiar with the deck’s vocabulary.

If you’re a collector who likes to line up the minis for Instagram-worthy table shots, you’ll appreciate the design language here. The Laughs’ stylized look translates well into photos and videos, which is a small but nontrivial advantage for a game in today’s social-sharing culture.

## Comparisons: Batman board games you might be weighing against

- Batman: Gotham City Chronicles (Monolith): This is the heavier, earlier entry from Monolith that leaned into large-scale skirmish and cinematic boss-fight vibes. If you loved that system, you’ll likely enjoy how this newer release borrows some DNA but distills it into a more compact, fast-paced format. The new Laughs release is less about ridgeline-sweeping battles and more about tight turns, quick jumps, and a villain-forward narrative flow.
- Batman: The Board Game (unaffiliated publishers): Some entries in this space trade cavernous production value for streamlined play. The Laughs’ version, for its part, tries to find a sweet spot: a little more depth than a pure lightweight title but less heavy than the full Gotham City Chronicles experience.
- Other DC miniatures games: If you’re juggling a shelf of Justice League crossovers, this title slots in as a thematic, bite-sized piece that can slot into a casual night or be the centerpiece for a longer, drama-filled session. It’s certainly not a standalone investment for die-hard fans of one system; it’s a flavorful addition that brings a distinct mood to the table.

## Pros, cons, and who this is for

Pros:
- Strong thematic cohesion with engaging villain design.
- Balanced mix of luck and strategy; accessible yet offering depth.
- High table presence; great for content creators and show-and-tell at game nights.
- Excellent scope for solo or small group play.

Cons:
- Some players may wish for tighter mechanical alignment with a pure tactic-focused experience.
- The setup and component sorting can be fiddly for new players; not a “grab and go” in the purest sense.
- The Laughs’ AI, while entertaining, can feel slightly predictable after multiple plays, which might reduce replay satisfaction for some veterans.

Who is this for?
- Fans of Batman and DC lore who want a tabletop experience with strong mood and memorable villains.
- Couples, friends, and board game nights that enjoy narrative-driven skirmishes with a twist.
- Players who appreciate high production value and are not deterred by a touch of chaotic whimsy.
- Solo players who want a brain-wracking puzzle with a grimly gleeful antagonist staring back at them from the table.

Who should perhaps skip it?
- Players who insist on absolute realism and tactical perfection in every move.
- People who dislike chaotic card-driven twists in their strategy games.
- Groups that require ultra-tight timing and precise mechanical outcomes on every turn.

## Final verdict: is it worth your shelf space?

This Monolith Editions release brings a lot of personality to the table. It hits a sweet spot for groups that enjoy a bit of chaos clashing with clever, cooperative play. The visual language is strong; the narrative hooks are present and maintainable over multiple sessions. If you’re chasing a game that feels cinematic, thematically bold, and mechanically approachable, Batman Who Laughs SW (New) earns a solid space on your shelf and a solid rotation on your game night.

That said, it isn’t the most laser-focused tactical experience in the Monolith library. If pure chess-like simulations are your jam, you may be better served by other titles. But if you crave dramatic moments, witty banter, and a villain who makes you question your life choices while you roll another die, this is a strong recommendation.

To help you decide with more confidence, here are a few thought experiments you can use as you plan to pick up the game:
- Do you enjoy a game night that doubles as a show, with dramatic reveals and villainy as a spectator sport?
- Do you value component quality and artwork that makes you want to photograph every piece before you play?
- Are you curious about a system that lets you play multiple times with only minor rule adjustments, while still delivering fresh themes?

If your answer to those questions is a resounding yes, you won’t regret giving Monolith Editions’ Batman Who Laughs SW (New) a spin. It doesn’t pretend to be a perfect simulation of Gotham’s chaos, but it leans into the energy of the character with style, humor, and a bit of chaotic charm that only a villain dressed in neon-green can manage.
