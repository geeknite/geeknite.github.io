## A quick rewind on Marvel OverPower and the U PICK ONE concept

OverPower was one of the prominent Marvel CCGs that rode the 1990s boom in collectible card games. This was an era where set design often embraced bold color, heavy-handed typography, and the hope that players would accept a power curve that looked like a skyscraper next to a soap bar. The U PICK ONE mechanic is a fan-friendly promise baked into the card pool: when you draw or reveal a certain type of card, you pick one hero from a list to bring into action, as opposed to playing multiple heroes from a single card or relying on a fixed roster. The exact execution varied by card and booster, but the spirit was clear — you get a choice, you leverage it, and you hope the dice gods don’t vanish into the night.

Why 27? Because 27 is the kind of number that feels like a horse in a kids’ adventure cartoon — substantial enough to feel like a deck, but compact enough to be plausible to print on glossy cardboard in a tiny warehouse in the middle of nowhere. The U PICK ONE roster invites you to tailor your playstyle around a small, bespoke roster of heroes, each with his or her signature flavor. Some are ethical heavyweights with raw punching power; others are brainy tacticians who bend the board to their will. And some, honestly, are delightful oddities who exist to remind you that 1995 was not shy about bold design choices.

## The actual mechanics beneath the surface

The U PICK ONE concept is a light version of the player choice that can drastically affect the pace of a match. In practice, this means you might reveal a hero card and then pick one from a specific subset, such as your current team roster or a list printed on the card or booster. The payoff is that you feel you influence the battlefield more than the proverbial dice roll would allow. In many playthroughs, this choice becomes a mini-arc within a match — a chance to set up a combo or to block a particularly nasty threat from the opponent. The 1995 design team clearly believed in the power of decision points; the more decision you have, the more you feel like you are shaping the story rather than slavishly following a script.

A common critique from modern vantage points is the occasional feeling that power levels can be uneven in these early cycles. Some heroes can feel slanted toward offense with chunky numbers, while others can seem to operate more as utility or support. That chaos, though, is part of the charm. It creates memorable moments, like a last-minute pivot where a supposedly weaker hero suddenly saves the day with a perfectly timed pick from the U PICK ONE roster. The card art, meanwhile, runs the gamut from high drama to pastel chaos, a reminder that the 1990s did not fear a little garish glory when crafting a universe you could physically hold in your hands.

## How to actually play with the U PICK ONE roster today

If you want to replicate the feel of this vintage roster in a modern setting, here is a quick, antiquated rules-as-you-remember-it guide to get you started. Note that actual rules can differ by edition and print run, so treat this as a playful hommage rather than a strict reconstruction.

- Setup: Shuffle the 27 hero cards and lay them out in a 3x9 grid on the table, or any arrangement that makes a nice face of heroes. This is your U PICK ONE list — when you draw into this list, you pick one hero from the current face to enter your active lineup.
- Turn sequence: Each turn involves a draw, an action (attack, defend, or special ability), and a potential U PICK ONE moment if a card triggers that mechanic. The exact order of operations can feel rough around the edges, but that messy energy is part of the charm — it makes every decision feel heroic and a little chaotic in the best possible way.
- Combat: Resolve attacks by comparing power values, applying any special abilities, and resolving any defensive buffs or terrain effects you’ve built up. It is not a perfectly tuned chess match; it is more like a frantic game of chess played with action figures.
- Victory: First to a predetermined victory condition wins. The condition can be as simple as “defeat two rival heroes” or a more complex objective depending on the variant you’re playing. It’s a loose framework that invites improvisation.
