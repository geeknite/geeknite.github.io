## What is Modern Art about, aside from the pretty deck?

Modern Art is a knizia classic originally published in 1992, a game of auctions, art cards, and market dynamics that would make even the most stoic banker blush. Players bid for paintings by different artists whose value shifts as the market evolves. There are multiple auction rounds; money heats up the market; and the trick to victory is to be the person who can monetize the mood of the market more effectively than the other players. In this bilingual edition, you get the same core mechanics wrapped in a language pair that should help you navigate both English and Korean auctioneers. It is a game about cycles, trends, and the occasional re-issue of a painting by a fictional artist who becomes suddenly valuable because of a scandal involving a red square and a blue triangle.

Below we break down the core mechanics in plain, humorous terms so you can plan your strategy without needing a finance degree or a translator who speaks both Wall Street and art gallery slang.

### The core loop

- A round consists of a series of auctions for paintings that span a few artists. Each painting has a base price and, crucially, a card that determines how the market value shifts on subsequent rounds.
- During an auction, players can bid for the painting. The high bidder wins the painting and pays the amount bid. That amount goes into the market rather than to the selling player, which is the quirky twist of the game.
- The market track tracks how many paintings of each artist are for sale and how much they are worth. The more paintings by a single artist are sold, the higher their market value becomes, until it resets when another auction occurs.

The result is a game of devious calculus: you want to end up owning a lot of paintings by one artist, but you also want to avoid becoming the one who has to bid up the price so high that your last pennies are spent on a canvas you can barely justify in your living room. The fun is in the interplay of bidding, counter-bidding, and nudging your opponents into uncomfortable levels of expenditure.

Here is where the mystery floats like a half-dried paintbrush. The box declares a DiceTree, but the components in the unboxing appear to be standard for Modern Art. Dice as a mechanic is not part of the original, and this edition seems to lean more into a collector's dream with bilingual accessibility and a sealed, pristine NIB state. So what is a DiceTree? The most plausible explanations are:

- A misprint in the listing or packaging, where the seller used a chargen phrase for a different edition and the box claims something that does not exist in the game rules.

In the absence of formal DiceTree rules, we can treat this as an optional variant that you can test at home. If you want to implement a dice twist, you could introduce a small dice mood mechanic: at the start of each round, roll a die to determine the market tilt (bullish, bearish, neutral, or volatile). Then adjust bids or valuations accordingly. But be aware that adding dice to a Knizia auction game may violate the delicate balance of the core system and could lead to unpredictable chaos. For many players, the value of Modern Art lies in the bidding psychology and market pressure rather than dice-driven randomness. If you are into pure luck, you can simply treat the DiceTree as a collectible curiosity that adds conversation, rather than altering core mechanics.

## How to play: a practical walkthrough for the impatient optimist

This section is designed for players who have never played Modern Art or who need a quick refresh after a long day of coding and gaming at the same time. The rules are straightforward, but the interplay of bidding and market mood means you need to maintain focus, read your fellow players, and resist the temptation to overspend on a painting with a silly-sounding artist name.

### Setup and player order

- Each player receives a set amount of money. The amount can vary by edition, but the typical starting pool is reasonable for a five-to-six player game. If you have fewer players, you may adjust the starting funds as you see fit to maintain a balanced endgame.
- The market track is placed at the center of the table. The deck of art cards is shuffled and placed face-down on the board. A set of price tiles is arranged near the board for quick reference.
- The identity of artists is randomly distributed or revealed as per the house rules. In bilingual editions, artist names may appear in both languages for the sake of flavor and misinterpretation alike.
- The first round begins with a market mood that can be determined by a roll or a simple coin flip. This mood will influence bidding increments and perceived value for the first painting.

### The auction rounds

Each round, a painting appears for auction. The auction proceeds in a sequence of bids where players either bid higher or pass. The winning bidder pays the bid amount to the bank, not to the seller, which is a quirky but essential feature of Modern Art. The market track is adjusted according to the painting's artist and the arrangement of the market—if more paintings by one artist are sold, the price for future paintings by that artist tends to increase. The goal is to accumulate a portfolio of paintings that, when totalled by the scoring rules, yields the highest score. The trick is to manage liquidity and to anticipate how your opponents value a given artist.

### Scoring: the cold, rational endgame

Scoring in Modern Art involves tallying points for artworks owned by each artist. The market track influences the how many points each painting yields at the end of the game. The exact scoring rules can vary slightly by edition, especially in bilingual versions with added translation notes. The general principle remains: owning multiple artworks from the same artist is valuable, but only if you can survive the midgame battles without depleting your cash reserve. A common pitfall is to overbid early on a controversial painter that your opponents later devalue; those points may look attractive in the moment but can become a liability if you cannot hold onto them until the end of the game.

### Strategy and tips for this edition

- Read the room. The market mood shifts with each auction. Track how other players are spending and adjust your bids accordingly. If you sense that someone is planning a heavy investment in a particular artist, consider diversifying to keep your options open.
- Timing is everything. The endgame often hinges on who can hold the most valuable artist portfolio while staying solvent. Avoid the trap of chasing a few big paintings to the point that you cannot pay for the next round.
- Language-aware play. If you are playing with bilingual players, the rulebook becomes a live, practical tool. Encourage teammates to pivot to the language they are most comfortable with when negotiating bids. It is surprising how much micro-communication matters in a complex auction. If you are playing with someone who does not read either language fluently, consider a simple house rule: designate a single auctioneer to interpret terms for a round.
- DiceTree variant if you dare. If you decide to adopt a dice twist, keep it loose and fun. A single die can introduce a mood shift that makes players question their own math skills. If your group prefers pure strategy and measured bids, skip the dice and enjoy the central experience of painting price negotiation.
