# Introduction

If you’ve ever wondered what would happen if you mixed a high-stakes logistics treadmill with a poker night and a dash of World War II canniness, you’re probably describing Quartermaster General: WW2 Prelude from Ares Games. It’s the kind of game that makes you feel like a general, minus the paperwork, red tape, and the existential dread of potentially failing your country because you forgot to stockpile enough trucks. In the hands of a good table, it’s brisk, brutal, and surprisingly funny in the way only war games about supply chains can be. Yes, we’re about to dive into a 2k-word love letter to a game about railroad timetables and tank divisions. Strap in; this is geeky in the best possible way.

# What’s in the box

Ares didn’t stray from the feel that fans expect: a compact, modular box with bold art, plenty of cardboard chits, and enough cards to power a small startup’s marketing campaign. The WW2 Prelude expansion (the subject of this review) adds a new flavor without turning the base game into a separate component factory. You’ll find:

- A deck of Prelude event cards that seed the opening moves for both sides. Think of them as the pre-game jitters you get before a big presentation, only this time your jitters might trigger a German armored thrust or a Soviet logistic bottleneck.
- Logistics and supply tokens that deepen the tension around keeping your front-line units fed, fueled, and emotionally intact.
- A handful of new map tiles and counters that spice up deployment patterns and encourage more asymmetric opening gambits.
- A revised rulebook section labeled Prelude, because nothing says “historical immersion” like a subtitle you can underline with a highlighter.

As with the rest of the Quartermaster General line, the components are functional rather than lavish. This is a game that tells you to focus on decisions, not on assembling a diorama. The art remains bold and legible, which is essential when you’re trying to perform rapid mental arithmetic while your opponent flings a blizzard of cards your way.

# How Quartermaster General: WW2 Prelude plays

## Game flow

Quartermaster General is famously streamlined: two players—one Axis, one Allied (or variant setups)—compete to fulfill strategic objectives while staving off collapse. WW2 Prelude is a short, punchy way to get into the mood before the main event. The game progresses in turns that blend card play, action points, and global events. You’ll be planning several moves ahead, but the tempo is deliberately brisk enough that you can pivot when your railways start coughing up steam.

In Prelude, each side has a deck representing strategic options tailored to early-war conditions. On your turn, you draw a handful of cards and spend action points to execute orders (like “Advance Along the Rail” or “Urban Counterattack”). The Prelude deck adds an additional layer of variability: you aren’t just reacting to your opponent; you’re reacting to the clues the deck gives you about what the opening moves might imply. It’s like reading a chessboard with a weather report attached to every square.

## Card mechanics

Cards drive the game. They come with clear icons: movement, combat value, supply impact, and the occasional big-event card that can swing momentum. The Prelude expansion adds a small, clever wrinkle: some cards simulate the initial frictions of war—the miscommunications, the transport delay, the first samples of field tech, and the first major strategic misreadings that define early war. The advantage here is thematic. You’re rewarded for thinking about the logistics beyond “move units, roll dice.” The downside is that new players must wrap their heads around the fog-of-war elements without getting overwhelmed by too many edge cases.

The deck-building feel is tight, with a surprisingly good balance between predictability and randomness. It’s not a solitaire puzzle; it’s a two-player tug-of-war where both players have equally plausible but not identical routes to victory. Prelude nudges this further toward narrative: you get to craft early-stage strategies with a sense that you’re setting up a broader world war, not merely sprinting to a tactical checkpoint.

## The Prelude twist: new cards, orders, logistic tokens

Prelude shifts the early-game emphasis toward supply chains and timing. New orders emphasize movement corridors, the state of rail hubs, and the vulnerability of supply convoys. Logistics tokens drain or replenish your capacity to fund operations, making players think twice about “going all-out” in a single region. It’s a smart design choice because it brings a sense of weight to the opening phase without bogging the game down in a pile of extra rules.

This is where the game earns some of its personality. You’ll see moments where you think you’ve found a clean path to victory, only to discover that your opponent’s Prelude cue card predicted your move and set a counterplan into motion. The feeling is reminiscent of a good espionage thriller: not every move is a grand strike; sometimes the subtle shift of a supply line is the real game changer.

# Components and production

From a production standpoint, WW2 Prelude adheres to the standard Ares quality. The cardboard is sturdy, the counters are legible, and the insert actually helps you stay organized rather than becoming a jigsaw puzzle on day two. The artwork remains bold, with map zones color-coded to readability. The card stock is forgiving enough that you won’t feel like you’re about to lose a finger flipping through your deck at the wrong moment.

One minor quirk worth noting: the Prelude deck can feel a bit inconsistent in tone. Some cards read like earnest, historical what-ifs; others lean into the jokey, almost tongue-in-cheek “this is a game, remember?” vibe. It’s not a deal-breaker, but if you prefer a strict, sober historical texture, you might notice the tonal whiplash. The game’s humor is, fortunately, mostly in good fun and never undercuts the seriousness of the strategic decisions you’re making.

# Thematic and historical flavor

WW2 Prelude aims for a credible early-war atmosphere: the scramble for rail capacity, the bottlenecks in logistics networks, the tug-of-war over air superiority that’s still mostly aspirational. The theme lands most of the time, largely because the mechanics force players to contend with the same constraints the historical players faced—limited supply lines, fragile logistic networks, and the pressure of time.

That said, the “prelude” concept means you’re not getting a full historical simulation here. If you want a rigorous, year-by-year grind with extremely granular supply tables, this isn’t your lane. However, if you want a brisk, thematic two-player experience that captures the feel of early-war maneuvering and the delicate balance between offense and logistics, Prelude delivers in spades.

# Strategy and tactics

This is where the game shines. Prelude rewards long-term planning and flexible adaptation. A few rough strategic ideas to test at your table:

- Balance initiative with reserves. The Prelude cards tempt you to execute a proactive plan, but brainy play comes from saving some logistics power for a counter-punch when your opponent vents a blow anywhere else on the map.
- Control critical corridors. Rail hubs and supply lines matter more as soon as you start to push into contested zones. Deny your opponent their lifelines, and you’ll slow their momentum while maintaining your own.
- Read the deck. The Prelude deck teases with hints about what’s coming. If you can anticipate when a major logistic complication is due, you can time your push to co-exist with an expected disruption rather than fight it head-on.
- Maintain a split focus. The best players won’t simply chase one victory condition; they’ll press multiple conditions that the opponent has to defend simultaneously. That double pressure often forces suboptimal choices.

The overall takeaway: Prelude is a masterclass in tempo. It doesn’t give you a single canonical path to victory; it gives you a playground of viable routes and then nudges you toward the ones that fit the opening situation. That dynamic is what makes Quartermaster General enjoyable for repeat play.

# Solo and two-player experiences

Quartermaster General is designed as a two-player duel, but many groups experiment with “hot seat” or free-for-all variants. Prelude scales well in a head-to-head context: it’s short enough to play in a single sitting but dense enough that you’ll want a second round to test a different approach. The solo mode—if you’re the kind of player who likes grinding the same puzzle against the clock—can be enjoyable for practice, though it’s not the intended experience.

In co-op or team settings, the Prelude concept can be translated into a “team vs team” experience, but you’ll likely want to borrow some house rules to keep the turns aligned and ensure the logistics feel meaningful for both sides. The core elegance—fast decisions, meaningful trade-offs, and a ticking clock—remains intact in any variant you choose.

# Replayability and scenarios

Replayability is strong here because Prelude’s deck and logistical tokens create a fresh flavor in each run. The early-war setting means the map is frequently reshuffled in your mind as new tensions emerge. The variety of opening conditions makes it unlikely you’ll see the same first ten turns twice in a row, and that variability multiplies if your table experiments with different alliance setups or alternative victory conditions.

That said, if you’re chasing a deep, long-run campaign where every session advances a single grand narrative, Prelude is a standalone slice rather than the whole pie. It’s perfect for a quick night in the hobby calendar or as a prelude to a longer Quartermaster General campaign session. It’s also a suitable gateway for players who want to dip their toes into WWII wargaming without committing to a sprawling, heavy title.

# Art, components, and production quality

Visual design remains crisp and readable. The color palette helps you quickly identify front lines, supply hubs, and critical transport lines. Card art is punchy and iconic, with a style that fits the series’ tone: bold, legible, and a touch cheeky when it wants to be. The new map tiles inject extra flavor without making the play area feel crowded.

The physical components perform well under regular play. The tokens are big enough to handle without argument, the cardboard stock stands up to repeated shuffles and card flips, and the insert keeps the pieces aligned between sessions. If you value tactile satisfaction in board games, Prelude will scratch that itch without requiring you to sell a kidney to fund a deluxe edition.

# Value and price

Ares Games’ pricing for a Prelude expansion is reasonable in the context of a modular system like Quartermaster General. If you already own the base game and are looking for a flavorful, solid add-on to extend your play sessions, Prelude pays for itself with re-playability and swift, satisfying matches. It balances price with the experience, not over-promising an epic arc while still delivering a meaningful, distinct flavor.

# Comparisons to other editions and similar games

If you’ve played other Quartermaster General editions or expansions, you’ll notice Prelude is in the same family: accessible to newcomers, with a streamlined rule set, but enough strategic depth to reward thoughtful play. Compared to heavier WW2 wargames that lean into tactical simulation, Prelude sits closer to the “card-driven euro-wargame” spectrum: you feel like you’re shaping a war, not counting every casualty.

Compared to other two-player WWII cards-and-map games, Prelude often wins on tempo and clarity. It doesn’t bury you in procedural minutiae; it asks you to think about supply lines, timing, and risk in a way that is both accessible and meaningful. If you’re hunting for a fast, thematic, quick-play two-player experience, Prelude earns its keep. If you crave a longer, more granular treatise on WWII logistics, you might want to step up to something heavier and drop Prelude into rotation as a palate cleanser.

# Final verdict and recommendation

WW2 Prelude for Quartermaster General is a compact, confidently designed expansion that nails the balance between theme, tempo, and tactical decision-making. It’s ideal for players who want a brisk bout of strategy that feels consequential without pretension. New players will appreciate the approachable learning curve; veterans will enjoy the extra wrinkles to exploit, especially the logistics-oriented tension that gives the early game a distinctive edge. The Prelude addition doesn’t just pad the game’s length; it reshapes the opening act in meaningful ways, inviting careful planning and dynamic reactions alike.

In short: if you like clever card-driven play with a credible historical vibe and you want a game that plays quickly but sticks in your head, WW2 Prelude is a compelling addition to the Quartermaster General family. It isn’t a full-blown theater-wide simulation, and that’s a feature, not a bug—it’s a well-built prelude that sings when the curtain rises on the full show.
