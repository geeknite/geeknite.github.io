## Card anatomy and design decisions

### Patch window and patch quality

In this edition, the patch window is not a mere cosmetic rectangle. It’s sized to showcase a meaningful portion of the jersey fragment. You want enough patch to identify color patterns or team affiliation without needing a microscope to tell whether it is a sleeve or a shoulder. For a goalie like Comrie, you might see a patch segment that hints at the chest or arm stripes rather than a random dot. The patch’s color palette matters; multi-color patches tend to pop more in display, while solid blocks scream nostalgia for old-school team crests.

### The autograph placement

Autographs on ITG 12th Edition cards typically reside in a corner that balances visibility with the patch window. The ink color is chosen to maximize legibility against the background art and foil accents. A clean, evenly applied autograph indicates good stock quality and a factory-friendly production run, both of which are a relief to collectors who have seen signature overs, ink bleed, or ghosting on less refined releases.

### Card stock and finishing

ITG’s stock for the 12th Edition is sturdy but not bulldozer-thick. The finish tends to be glossy with a touch of foil that catches the light and, frankly, makes your desk lamp jealous. This isn’t the rougher stock of old-school base sets; it’s a premium feel that justifies the price of admission for those who like their collectibles to exude a sense of modern reliability while still nodding to retro aesthetics.

## Grading, condition, and display considerations

For anything with printed run numbers like 15/24, the condition matters as much as the rarity. Here are the big-ticket quality checks:

- Centration: Is the card centered evenly, left-to-right and top-to-bottom? A slight tilt can affect resale value and the visual appeal in a display.
- Corners: Are they sharp, or do you see micro-chips that suggest heavy handling? A 4/5 card will have minor imperfections, but you want to avoid major dings.
- Edges and surface: Any scuffing or scratches on the foil finish can dramatically impact aesthetic value. The panel with the patch should remain vibrant and unflawed by surface issues.
- Patch integrity: Is the patch cleanly integrated in the window, or does it appear to be miscut? A poor patch cut can reduce the patch’s visual impact and perceived value.
- Auto readability: The autograph should be legible without needing a magnifying glass. Blurred lines and ink bleed reduce the appeal.

If you are buying graded, a grade in the high-mid range might be acceptable for a 4/5 patch auto, but many collectors prefer raw copies for visibility and the ability to authenticate patches visually. Your display choice matters as well: a small acrylic display stand or a binder page with a white background can help the patch colors pop while protecting the surface from fingerprints.

## Authenticity and buying tips

- Request high-resolution images of the patch window and autograph; details matter. 
- Verify serial number (15/24) and ensure it matches the listing description.
- Ask for confirmation of the set and edition year to avoid misattributed cards.
- Check the seller’s reputation and return policy. A good return window is invaluable if the card doesn’t meet expectations on arrival.
- If possible, compare with known authentic listings from reputable sources to gauge typical pricing and presentation.
