## How to play (overview)
Phase 10 is a race to complete a preset sequence of phases. Each phase represents a specific combination of cards you must lay down to advance. The rules are simple enough for casual players to grasp in a single sitting, yet deep enough to reward strategic thinking as the rounds accumulate.

- Every round, players draw a card and discard one, cycling through the deck until someone completes the current phase.
- The current phase is the objective for all players in that round. Once a player completes it, they lay down their cards and move to the next phase on the next round.
- The round ends when the draw pile is exhausted, and players tally points for any cards left in hand. The faster you complete phases, the more you avoid the penalty of uncompleted cards.
- The game continues through 10 phases, with players trying to be the one to complete Phase 10 and finish with the lowest total score.

The Deluxe edition doesn’t reinvent the wheel in this section. It gently reinforces the core mechanics while providing a more robust setup so that the learning curve is lower for new players and the social friction is kept to a minimum. The feel is familiar enough for veterans and approachable enough for newcomers—a combination that explains why Phase 10 has endured long enough to appear on more tables than a stubborn cat on a keyboard.

### A quick tour of the phases
For those who want a mental cheat sheet, here’s a rough outline of what players typically aim for across the 10 phases. The exact sequences can vary slightly by edition, but the spirit remains the same:
- Phase 1: 2 sets of 3
- Phase 2: 1 set of 3 + 1 run of 4
- Phase 3: 1 set of 4 + 1 run of 4
- Phase 4: 1 run of 5
- Phase 5: 2 sets of 4
- Phase 6: 1 run of 3 + 1 run of 4
- Phase 7: 1 run of 4 + 1 set of 4
- Phase 8: 1 run of 5 + 1 set of 5
- Phase 9: 1 run of 5 + 1 set of 3
- Phase 10: 1 run of 5 + 1 run of 5

If you’re familiar with rummy-like games, these will feel like family-friendly cousins of those patterns. The challenge is not just forming the right combinations but doing so while blocking your rivals, reading their discards, and managing your hand to avoid getting stuck with high-point cards at the end of rounds.

To help you along, Phase 10 Deluxe includes a phase reference card so you don’t have to flip back to the rulebook mid-sentence every time you forget whether you can use a wild card in a run or a set. This small convenience improves the flow of play and reduces the “we’ve never seen the rule before” pauses that can kill a vibe during a tense moment.

## Strategy and play feel
### Core strategies
Phase 10 thrives on a few core strategic ideas:
- Plan ahead while staying flexible. The moment you lock into a single path, a single bad draw can derail you. A good player keeps a few potential phase lines in mind and adapts to the cards they draw.
- Watch discards and reads. Because you can often deduce what others are aiming for, you can anticipate their needs and sometimes deny them a key card with a well-timed discard.
- Manage your hand size. If you find yourself clinging to high-value cards late in a round, you’re inviting trouble. The Deluxe variant’s better score sheet helps you track this more cleanly, reducing the risk of accidental doom when someone says phase 9 and you realize you’ve been holding onto a runner card since Phase 3.
- Use wilds strategically. Wild cards are the glue that can help you complete difficult phases, but they’re also the cheapest target for your opponents to manipulate. A careful wild-card play often makes the difference between a smooth phase and a frantic scramble to salvage a loss.

## House rules and variants
### Common twists to spice things up
Phase 10 shines when you settle into a comfortable cadence, but many groups like to remix things to keep it fresh. Here are a few house rules that can add some flavor without breaking the core engine:
- Timed rounds: Use a gentle 60-second timer for each round. It adds urgency and reduces analysis paralysis. Note that this can also drive up the chaos factor, so brace yourself for a few dramatic endgame moments.
- Swap the phase order: Instead of playing strictly in order from Phase 1 to Phase 10, allow players to choose a phase if they’re the first to complete a different one. This can create interesting focal points and reduce the feeling that the early phases are too deterministic.
- Group scoring tweaks: If your group likes more forgiveness, reduce penalty points for leftover cards or grant bonus points for completing a phase by a margin of 1-2 cards. If you like competition, increase penalties for uncompleted cards or add a small bonus for the fastest completion of Phase 10.
- Variant deck: You can keep a few jokers or color modifiers out of the deck to alter the distribution of card values. It’s a way to balance risk and reward for a new crew.
