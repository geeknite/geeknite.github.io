Power draw is something to keep in mind. The WindForce OC variant sits in the mid-range of TDPs and typically pulls within the 180–210W range under load, with recommended PSU headroom around 500W for comfortable operation. Of course, this depends on case cooling, fan curve, and other components in your system.

Thermals are well-managed thanks to the three-fan configuration. Expect temperatures in the mid-60s to mid-70s Celsius under sustained gaming load in a well-ventilated case, depending on ambient temperature and case airflow. In practice, the card stays relatively cool, which helps keep the noise floor down as the fans keep their cool.

The WindForce OC’s fans offer a respectable balance of noise and performance. It isn’t whisper-quiet when under full tilt, but the noise is steady and non-intrusive—more like white noise you can tolerate while streaming your misadventures in a loot-based RPG.

## Overclocking and tuning
If you’re the kind of PC gamer who likes to squeeze a little more out of hardware, the WindForce OC edition invites a bit of headroom. Gigabyte’s own tuning tools—including the AORUS Engine software—allow you to adjust the power limit, clock speeds, and fan curves. Expect roughly an extra 5–15% performance headroom with careful tuning, depending on your silicone lottery and cooling. It’s not a monster overclock; this is a card engineered for reliability and moderate uplift rather than extreme sub-zero clocks.

A few practical tips for OCing:
- Start with a modest power limit increase (e.g., +10% to +15%), then stress test for stability and temps.
- Increase memory clocks gradually if your workloads involve high-resolution textures or memory bandwidth-limited scenarios.
- Fine-tune fan curves to balance temps and noise; a slight bias toward cooler temps often yields longer life and quieter operation.

Remember: every GPU sample is a bit different, and your mileage may vary. Don’t push it beyond your case’s airflow, or you’ll end up with a plush toy for your case fans.
