## Design and Build Quality
The HP TB Dock 120W G4 is not trying to be flashy; it’s trying to be dependable. The chassis leans into a practical, understated aesthetic with a mix of matte plastics and metal accents that don’t scream gaming rig, which is good for office environments and coffee shop credibility alike. Weight-wise, it feels substantial enough to stay put on a busy desk, yet not so heavy you’d fear it tipping over during a particularly enthusiastic keyboard-tapping session.

The ports are laid out with a sensible logic: upstream TB4, a mix of USB-A and USB-C, HDMI/DisplayPort video outputs, Ethernet, audio, and power passthrough. HP’s design language here is all about accessibility. Front-facing USBs mean you don’t need to belly-crawl under your desk to plug in a flash drive or a wired mouse, and the cable management groove on the back helps keep the bench from turning into a routing nightmare.

In practice, the dock’s build feels sturdy enough to survive the occasional laptop slams (not recommended) and the curious tug of a stray charging cable. It’s not a rugged expedition device, but for a modern workspace, it’s exactly the grown-up accessory you want on your desk—professional, not clownish.

## Setup and Compatibility
Setup is where many docks earn or lose hearts. The HP 120W G4 keeps things reasonably simple. You connect the upstream TB4 cable to your laptop, plug in the power supply if you’re not feeding power to the laptop, and you’re ready to roll. Windows and macOS users will be pleased to learn that driver wizards and plug-and-play behaviors are fairly predictable here. HP’s own documentation emphasizes driver support for Thunderbolt 4 features, reliable wake from sleep, and stable display output. In our tests, once the initial recognition happened, the dock behaved like a calm, helpful colleague rather than a moody intern.

One of the most delightful aspects of this dock is how forgiving it feels when you switch laptops across the TB4 ecosystem. On a Windows workstation with a 15-inch monster of a laptop and a macOS machine of similar heft, the dock handled the transition smoothly across reboots, with displays and peripherals reinitializing without drama. This is the kind of predictability that saves you from the “I swear I did everything right, but nothing works” meme mood—especially during a deadline.

Compatibility-wise, the 120W G4 supports laptops that can accept power delivery up to 120W, and most modern TB4 laptops will happily sip from it while keeping USB peripherals alive. If you’re pairing it with older Intel laptops that sport TB3 rather than TB4, you’ll still get working behaviors but with TB4 optimization not fully utilized. We also tested with a Linux laptop to check sanity with external displays and USB peripherals. Linux recognized the dock in most cases, though some early TB4 consumers reported that certain HDMI outputs could require manual EDID fiddling in rare edge cases. In short: it plays nicely with the majority of TB4-equipped machines; there may be corner cases if you’re mixing very niche hardware.

## Performance: Power Delivery, Video, and Peripherals
Power delivery is the headline feature here. HP lists up to 120W PD, which should be more than enough for most ultrabooks and some gaming laptops that aren’t chasing peak-watt performance on the daily. In our usage, the laptop charged at a reasonable rate when the dock was also feeding a handful of USB peripherals and a couple of external drives. It’s worth noting that actual charging speeds can vary depending on how many devices are drawing power through the dock and whether the host laptop is simultaneously running heavy workloads. If you pit a power-hungry laptop against a full array of external peripherals, expect the charge curve to show some flattening. This is normal in the world of docks—the magic 120W PD is a ceiling, not a guarantee of perpetual rocket-ship charging when you’re piloting a toaster-oven of a laptop.

Video output capability is another pillar of this dock. The HP TB Dock 120W G4 typically offers multiple external display options via HDMI and DisplayPort (depending on the exact revision in your SKU). In our setup, we ran two 4K monitors at 60Hz via DisplayPort and HDMI, which provided crisp images and a comfortable desktop workspace for productivity tasks, video calls, and the occasional game of a WebGL-based microgame during lunch. The efficiency of TB4 means you’re not constrained to a single high-res display; you can spread content across two or more screens while maintaining responsive input behavior. If you’re into high-color accuracy, you’ll appreciate the capable color reproduction on standard sRGB work tasks, and casual creative work will be pleasantly forgiving.

For Ethernet and networking, the dock includes a wired option that often yields more stable connections during VoIP calls or large file transfers. In the era of Wi-Fi 6E and 5G, a stable wired link can still be a life-saver when you’re delivering a presentation to a large audience or streaming multi-camera content. The combination of TB4 bandwidth, multiple USB ports, and a reliable Ethernet path creates a workspace that’s less about juggling dongles and more about diving into work without fiddly setup rituals.

USB peripheral performance is also solid. External storage, keyboards, mice, and webcams all behaved as expected. USB-C and USB-A ports offered adequate throughput for external SSDs, while still leaving some USB-A ports available for legacy devices. For power-hungry USB devices, you’ll appreciate the port density and the dock’s willingness to share power across connected peripherals. In other words, this is not a gadget that punishes you for plugging in a dozen accessories; it’s the kind of dock that pretends to be your friend when you’re juggling cables after hours.

### Ports and Layout: A Quick Reference
The exact port layout can vary by SKU, but the HP 120W G4 typically includes:
- 1x Thunderbolt 4 upstream (to host)
- 2x Display outputs (HDMI/DisplayPort) for external monitors
- 4x USB-A ports for peripherals
- 1x USB-C port for faster peripheral connections
- 1x Ethernet (RJ-45)
- 1x 3.5mm audio combo jack
- 1x 120W power adapter passthrough to laptop

If you’re counting on the dock to power multiple displays at very high refresh rates while charging a premium laptop at the same time, the hardware is up to the task—just don’t forget that cable management is still your friend, and the TB4 bandwidth is shared between all devices.

Here are a few scenarios where the HP 120W G4 shines, and a couple where it might not be the most optimal choice:
- Office workflow with dual 4K displays and a wired network: Excellent. You keep a clean desk and still manage a robust, multi-monitor setup. No annoying USB hub tantrums, no driver drama, just productivity in its most comfortable form.

If you’re pounding on the GPU for long renders, you might observe the laptop’s internal cooling competing with the dock’s thermals, but that’s the laptop’s problem, not the dock’s.

For gamers or workstation enthusiasts who require ultra-high refresh rates or specific high-end GPUs in a docked setup, it’s wise to check if the dock’s bandwidth is enough for your exact multi-monitor configuration. In most typical office workflows, you’ll be perfectly comfortable with what the 120W G4 offers.
