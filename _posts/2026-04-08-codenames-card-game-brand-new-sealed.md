## How to set up and start playing fast
Setup is mercifully quick. You gather two teams, assign the usual captain roles, and lay out the card backs in a neat grid. The grid is the stage, the words are the cast, and the clues are…the director’s whispered instructions that may or may not align with everyone’s expectations. In terms of time, you can realistically go from box to first clue in under five minutes if you and your crew are practiced pack rats at mnemonic wordplay. If you are a new group crossed with a group of friends who are convinced they can improvise, the setup may stretch to ten, perhaps twelve minutes as you sort out seating, snacks, and who gets to be the red team captain who has to shout the most chaotic clue of the night.

### A quick run through the rules
- Teams take turns with a single captain giving clues. The clue consists of a single word and an optional number indicating how many cards the captain intends their team to guess.
- The goal is to guess the words on the grid that correspond to your team’s cards while avoiding the others and, most cruelly, the assassin card which ends the round instantly in a dramatic, table-flipping fashion that is both exhilarating and terrifying.
- The other team’s words can be hinted at, but you do not want to go too far; the art of the clue is balance between precision and risk. A great clue can unlock multiple cards, a poor clue can trigger a cascade of incorrect guesses and laughter.
- The round ends when either team has guessed all of its words or when the assassin card reveals itself. Then scoring is tallied, bragging rights awarded, and it is time for another round because tonight is not about victory, it is about the stories you will tell your future self when you are a little older and a lot louder.

## Strategies and tips for winning without losing your mind
- Start with safe, close associations. In early rounds, keep your clues grounded in familiar connections that you know your team will recognize. A miss in the first couple of rounds will not ruin you if you keep your head and exploit the momentum of a few clean clues.
- Manage the grid like a word librarian. Map out which words belong to your team and which are risky. Keep a mental list of two or three anchor words that could plausibly connect to multiple cards so you have comeback options when the pressure builds.
- Balance risk and reward. If you have the assassin card lurking in a corner, do not be forced into a risky clue that ends the game immediately. It is a game of chess with words; think a few steps ahead and do not rush to claim victory.
- Read your teammates, not just the grid. Your clues will land differently depending on who is listening. If your group is more literal, you will want more precise clues; if they are playful and associative, you can push toward clever triples that nail three cards at once.
- Use the curse of knowledge to your advantage. We all know a word that seems obviously connected when you are thinking in your head, but not when you shout it aloud. Do not be afraid to misdirect, but do not let the misdirection become a total headache for your team.
