## What is Skip-Bo?
Skip-Bo is a card game from Mattel where players try to play all their cards in order from 1 to 12 on piles in the center. The deck includes wild cards and a stock that demands the kind of obsessive planning usually reserved for assembling IKEA furniture with the instruction manual missing.

Skip-Bo's core mechanic is simple: draw cards, organize them, and play them onto build piles. The twist comes from the fact that you often cannot play the card you want because your hand is stuffed with duplicates or because your neighbor's mind-reading gaze makes you question your life choices. The game has a lot of luck, yes, but also a surprising amount of strategy when you can count cards, manage the order, and bluff your friends by pretending you don’t see an Order 3 that is clearly in your own future.

## How to Play (in under 500 words)
Yes, there’s enough room for a full explanation here, but we will keep it tight and still capture the chaos.

Setup:
- Shuffle the tiny deck. Yes, you’ll laugh while you do it because the shuffling sounds like a quiet sneeze.
- Each player draws a small hand from the draw pile. The number depends on the rules you’re following; this mini edition keeps the core mechanics intact, just at a secret service level of scale.
- Center piles: you’re still trying to play cards onto build piles, in order, from 1 to 12.

Turn sequence:
- Draw phase: Draw up to your hand limit.
- Play phase: You can lay down one or more cards onto your build piles if they fit in order.
- Stock management: If you can’t play, you discard to a separate discard area and draw again on your next turn.
- Wilds and skips: The wilds function as versatile cards that can fit most places where a 1-12 slot exists, and you’re still trying to finish your stock pile before your friends finish theirs.

Objective:
- Get rid of all the cards from your stock pile. The first player to do so wins. In this edition, your victory is not just about who wins; it’s about who calls the round with a straight face while delivering epic taunts in a voice that sounds like a hamster wearing a hat.

Tiny Round Example:
- Player A has a 3 in hand; Player B has a 7; the current top of the central stack is a 2. They debate whether the micro edition allows for a 1-2-3 run. A nod to the rules confirms that yes, you can place a 3 on a 2 if you have a 1 to follow, and the tiny crowd roars in a chorus of squeaks.
- The moment of truth is always the last card: you reveal a Wild that can morph into the dimensions of anything. This is when the tiny crowd claps, and the table shakes with the thrill of a decision that matters in a world where card size is a life philosophy.

Maintenance tips:
- Keep the kit in a small pouch, not just for aesthetics but to prevent the card stash from becoming a lost arctic sea under the couch.
- Use a magnifying glass or a screen magnifier if you plan to play with older players who need to tilt the cards at a certain angle to see the numbers.
