## Setup

Set up is where the game earns its laurels as a quick family shredder (in a good way). You shuffle the deck, deal a hand to each player, and prepare for a cycle of drafting that takes only a few minutes to master.

Steps:
- Shuffle the deck thoroughly. It’s easy to do and surprisingly forgiving if your last round used the rigged dice you pretend to own.
- Distribute an initial hand to each player. The number of cards per hand depends on the player count and the edition you’re using.
- Each player looks at their hand, selects one card to keep, places it face-down, and passes the remaining cards to the left.
- Repeat until all cards from the hand have been claimed. Then reveal your chosen cards, tally any immediate scoring, and begin the next round.

The setup is quick; you can be digging into your first bite before the oven timer signals the end of the warm-up track. A little tip: keep a stack of sticky notes handy for quick scoring reminders (Wasabi multiplies the next Nigiri, Sashimi requires three cards to score, etc.). It’s not mandatory, but it reduces the moment of panic when you realize your Sushi Go! is not quite as spicy as you remember.

Two optional aids:
- A small post-it for quick scoring reminders (Wasabi multiplies the next Nigiri, Sashimi requires three cards to score, etc.)
- A timer for two-player rounds if you want to keep the pace tight and precise

## How to Play: The Draft-and-Score Dance

The core mechanic of Sushi Go! is deceptively simple: draft cards and assemble the best scoring combos. The rounds are short, which means you have to think ahead without losing your appetite for the next bite. Each round begins with a hand of cards dealt to every player. You choose one card to keep and pass the rest to the left. When all players have chosen, you reveal your selected cards and place them in front of you; these cards accumulate as part of your personal tableau. After three rounds, you tally your score.

Important cards and their rough scoring logic:
- Nigiri: Several varieties (like egg, salmon, and squid) with different base point values. If you find a Wasabi card, you can triple the value of your next Nigiri card when you play it.
- Sashimi: Typically requires three cards to score a few points, which means you’re looking for a three-card set and hoping your friends burn through the deck before you can complete them.
- Tempura: Usually requires two cards to score. It’s the kind of set that makes you feel clever when you finally pair the two cards and watch your points pop.
- Dumplings: The more dumplings you collect, the higher the value, often scaling non-linearly to reward drafting a bunch at once.
- Pudding: In many editions, these are end-game tiebreakers. They’re not part of the main score in each round but can become crucial when the final tally arrives.

Two-player flavor: In a two-player games, the drafting dynamics become a little more predictable and a lot more intense. Because you’re not passing to a large number of opponents, your choices often reflect more direct counters to your opponent’s intentions. The two-player variant tweaks the number of rounds or the card pool to maintain tension without dragging the game into a “bluffing duel” that lasts longer than a coffee break.

## Scoring, Endgame, and the Sizzling Finish

At the end of each round, tally the points for the cards you laid down. The layout is quick and colors pop off the table, making errors obvious in a playful way. The final scoring usually happens after three rounds, possibly including Pudding cards or other end-game modifiers depending on edition. If you’re playing with a friend who insists on counting every tiny detail with the pin-friend-taddle technique, you can politely remind them that the fun is in the drafting, not the quadratic equations.

Here are a few practical notes:
- Wasabi multiplies your next Nigiri, so time your big plays to maximize effect.
- Three Sashimi cards are your target for a solid payoff; if you’re unlucky, you’ll be chasing those three all game.
- Tempura pitches a nice payoff when you pair two cards; it can swing the end of a round where one player is just a little ahead.
- Dumplings scale with how many you pick up, making them a fun, sometimes counterintuitive strategic choice.

The final score is a mix of these components. You’ll learn to balance aggressive drafting with defense against others’ wins. The table talk—who took what, who’s chasing which set—adds to the fun. And if someone happens to grab a Pudding early, you’ll have a running joke about whether they’re “pudding up” a lead or merely padding a humorous endgame.

## Strategy, Table Talk, and a Little Geek Humor

Sushi Go! isn’t a heavy game, but it rewards sharp observation and quick math. Here are some beginner-friendly strategies to get you started:
- Early card recognition: Get a feel for which cards appear in the deck early on, and use your drafting to secure them when possible. A few rounds in, you’ll start to predict what the pool will look like.
- Diversify or focus: Do you want a broad spread of types to cover many scoring lines, or do you want to chase a single high-value combo? Both approaches can win, depending on your table’s behavior.
- Observe your neighbors: If someone consistently picks Wasabi for a single Nigiri and then never uses it, you might be better served by saving Wasabi for a stronger pairing. It’s not cheating; it’s reading the humor of the group.
- Timings for Wasabi: If you can align Wasabi with a high-value Nigiri you’re about to draft, you’ll maximize points. If your plan is exposed, rethink and pivot early.

In this process, you’ll also learn about the social dynamics of light drafting games: some players will try to force a bluff or misdirection (even if there’s no bluff in the rules). The humor of Sushi Go! often arises from how players talk themselves into good shots or jokes about who’s “sushi-nerd-spotting.” It’s all part of the fun.

## Two-Player Quick-Play Twist (Special Section)

Two players present an opportunity to refine your timing and your smack-talk in a compact battle. The drafting cycle remains, but the card pool is trimmed and the pace stiffens to a sprint. In practice, most couples or roommates enjoy two quick rounds with a twist: treat each round as a micro-match, and go straight into a sudden-death tiebreaker if needed. The key is to preserve the feeling of a conveyor belt while avoiding the table-turning standoffs that sometimes creep in when the table gets quiet and everyone stares into their pile of little cards.

## House Rules and Variants (More Flavor for the World’s Fastest Menu)

- Wildcard day: Add a new card that can act as any other sushi type for one round only.
- Time-crunch: Shorten each round to keep players on their toes; perfect for high-energy game nights.
- Team mode: Pair players to share a pair of eyes and a single strategy. It changes the dynamic and can be a blast when played with a larger group.

The approach? Keep it light, keep it fun, and don’t force a meta that kills the joy of drafting. Sushi Go! is at its best when players are laughing at the same silly puns that the card text occasionally offers. It’s not the place to chase a “perfect score” in a vacuum; it’s a social experience where we celebrate clever play and delightful anime-like food spectacle.
