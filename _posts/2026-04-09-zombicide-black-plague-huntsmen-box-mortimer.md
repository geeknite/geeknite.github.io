## What exactly is in the Huntsmen Box?

The Huntsmen Box for Zombicide Black Plague is pitched as a mid-level expansion that tacks onto the core game rather than rewriting its rules. Think of it as a “seasonal harvest” expansion: not essential to enjoy, but essential to feel like you’ve experienced the autumnal dread of a village overrun by knights with questionable fashion sense. Inside the box you’ll typically find: Mortimer (the eponymous Huntsman), a small cadre of Huntsmen figures, new equipment cards, mission cards, and sometimes store-bought-looking tokens that make you feel like you’re in a real, fully fleshed-out medieval apocalypse.

Mortimer is the star of the show, and rightly so. He’s not your run-of-the-mill survivor; he’s the guy who brings a pair of axes, a shrug that says “I’ve got this,” and a story about a horse named Latinus that you’ll never quite believe. Mortimer’s card set gives him special dice, movement tricks, and a few battlefield tricks that reward careful positioning and risk management. The Huntsmen—anyway you want to define them—are the box’s raison d’être in terms of adversaries and ancillary units. They’re the type of knights whose armor you can practically hear clinking in fear as they close in on your poor survivors.

Inside the packaging, you’ll also find new encounter cards, which are the lifeblood of these mid-campaign boxes. They’re designed to slot into your existing deck of mission objectives, offering fresh objectives, side-quests, and occasional boogeyman scares that make your survivors sweat-not-sweat. The art on these cards leans into the dark whimsy of the Huntsmen, with moody illustrations that look like they were sketched during a blackout in a candlelit chapel.

## Mortimer: The Huntsman who came to slay (or at least to punctualize the apocalypse)

Mortimer isn’t just a model with a quixotic beard. He’s built to reward a more tactical approach to Zombicide’s known chaos. In practical terms, Mortimer’s rules encourage you to think in terms of “zones of control” rather than “just walk forward.” His abilities typically revolve around two axes: tactical mobility and threat management. Mortimer can move quickly across the board, swinging his axes with a measured swagger that screams “I’m late for tea with a zombie aunt, but I’ll murder you first.” His presence can force you to rethink your standard approach to a cluster of walkers, especially if you’re trying to juggle multiple zones of threat.

From a design perspective, Mortimer is a win for players who like to feel clever without needing a doctoral degree in zombie epidemiology. The Huntsmen box gives Mortimer a set of cards that frequently reward you with extra actions, better line-of-sight options, and triggers that can chain into even beefier turns once you’ve set the board for a couple of rounds. In practice, Mortimer can become the hinge on which your entire siege turns—whether you stride into a choked corridor with a plan or improvise a chaotic, panicked sprint—tilt toward a controlled, satisfying victory.

Mortimer’s flavor text on his card is the kind of thing that makes your gaming group lean back and say, “We are now officially knights errant of a clearly dangerous, axis-aligned world.” The box doesn’t pretend you’re Victorian storytelling with pure moral clarity; it cheats with a sly sense of humor and a hint of grim practicality: this is a world where your best move is to stop relying on luck and start leveraging terrain, weapons, and order.

## The Huntsmen: What they add to the battlefield

What exactly are the Huntsmen? They are a set of adversaries that bring a different rhythm to the core game. The typical Zombicide battlefield thrives on a rhythm of walk, dash, and dice. The Huntsmen box adds a new tempo, a darker note that asks you to think about how to deploy your survivors against a group that’s more cunning, mobile, and, frankly, spookier than your average zombie melee train.

In terms of mechanics, expect new encounter cards that force you to consider safe zones, line-of-sight fog, and timing more carefully. The Huntsmen themselves bring a more knightly threat than the baseline shamblers, with armor that makes them harder to wound and moves that threaten to box your survivors in if you’re not careful. The synergy with Mortimer is where the box shines: Mortimer’s mobility and zone-control can help you corral the Huntsmen, turning a scary encounter into a puzzle you’re proud to have solved rather than a dice-roll disaster you pretend never happened.

As with most Zombicide packages, the Huntsmen Box comes with a mix of miniatures, tokens, cards, and sometimes a little cardstock plaque proclaiming “nobody reads the morale rules anyway.” The miniatures are crisp, with good panel lines and dynamic poses that make them look like they’re posing for a medieval action shot. The paint jobs on official minis are not included in the base kit, of course, but you’ll be thrilled to imagine Mortimer as a character who would absolutely pose for a character sheet while fending off a zombie apocalypse with one hand and a mug of ale in the other.

The artwork is moody, atmospheric, and a little cheeky—the sort of thing that makes you want to display the box on a shelf for “game-night bragging rights” while you quietly pretend to be an actual medieval planner making maps on parchment.

If you’re the kind of person who displays their game collection with loving detail, you’ll appreciate the box’s color palette and the way Mortimer’s poster-boy look ties into the overall theme of the expansion. The Huntsmen are designed to look like they belong in a forest clearing at dusk, and the art does a good job of selling that sense of place. It’s not just “minis on a table”; it’s a small theater production where you’re the director and the undead audience is getting increasingly grumpy.

## How it plays: pacing, strategy, and when to push the button

Zombicide’s core appeal is usually about turning chaos into a rhythm—like jazz with more skulls. The Huntsmen Box adds new tempo markers without turning your table into a calculus problem. Mortimer’s presence encourages players to consider line of sight, obstacle placement, and the timing of zone-effect abilities. That means you’ll likely see a couple more thoughtful turns per round, and maybe one or two “aha” moments when Mortimer’s abilities line up perfectly with a Huntsman ambush.

The box doesn’t completely upend your core strategy, but it nudges you toward more deliberate positioning. You’ll want to think about corridor control, choke points, and the timing of enemy spawns. Some players may initially resist the extra planning—after all, you bought a box to “roll more dice, not to count tiles.” But once the box clicks, it becomes a deeply satisfying experience: a little more methodical, a little more tense, and a lot more cinematic.

If you’re new to Zombicide or returning after a long break, the Huntsmen Box is approachable. It introduces enough new ideas to feel fresh, but it respects the core system you already love. The rules notes are clear enough that you won’t have to spend a game night doing interpretive dance to communicate the interpretation of a “grim reaper nod” from Mortimer’s card. It’s a solid bridge between the familiar and the new, with enough interactions to keep long-time players engaged without alienating newcomers.

### A note on difficulty and balance

One potential concern in expansions of this type is whether the new content scales your table’s difficulty in a way that makes the game feel unfair. The Huntsmen Box tends to raise the stakes rather than the randomness, which is exactly what you want from a mid-campaign expansion. Mortimer’s toolkit helps keep pace with the Hunters’ threat level. The result is a curated challenge that rewards good positioning and memory rather than sheer luck.

If your gaming group enjoys a bit of “who’s the boss” tension, the Huntsmen Box can become a recurring challenge. You’ll find yourself replaying scenarios to optimize Mortimer’s route and to test whether you can outmaneuver the Huntsmen before the clock runs out. It’s the difference between a frantic sprint and a well-planned siege—and that difference is deliciously satisfying when you pull it off on game night.

The Zombicide Black Plague Huntsmen Box with Mortimer is a solid, enjoyable expansion for players who want a bit more brain-bending planning on top of their usual zombie-slaying Tuesday. If you’re on the fence, ask yourself these questions:
- Do you enjoy the mid-game strategic shifts that require you to re-evaluate your plan on the fly?
- Do you want a thematic box that blends dark whimsy with solid mechanical depth?
- Are you looking for a fresh narrative hook to add to your current Black Plague campaign?

If your answers lean toward yes, the Huntsmen Box is worth adding to your shelf, your table, and your weekend planning calendar. It offers a robust package of new content, a flavorful centerpiece in Mortimer, and enough replay value to justify the purchase beyond a single session.
