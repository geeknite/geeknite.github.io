## Overview and box contents

Codenames comes with a handful of card decks, a grid board, and a handful of markers. The English edition keeps the same core components you know and love: 25 word cards laid out in a 5x5 grid, two-colored agent cards (red and blue), a single black assassin card, and a key card that tells spymasters which words belong to which team. There’s a rulebook that’s short enough to read during a coffee break and long enough to argue about during the last turn of the night.

- The Word Cards: 200-something unique words with a British humor twist? Not exactly; it’s more universal than that, but let’s admit it: some words will tickle cross-cultural funny bones in predictable ways.
- The Key Card: The secret sauce that determines which words belong to which team. If you’re the spymaster, you’re basically playing a two-backwards-Caps-lock cipher: you choose one clue that links multiple words and hope your team can assemble them before the other side.
- The Agents: Red and Blue agents plus the assassin. The tension rises not from the board, but from the fact that your clue could accidentally point to the other team’s words as well as your own.

## Playing the game (quick start)

The game is deceptively simple to teach and delightfully complex to master. At its core, it’s a word association puzzle that turns into a social deduction sprint.

- Setup: Shuffle the word cards and lay out 25 cards in a 5x5 grid. Pick a new key every round; one team starts with a number advantage, but that’s the least of your concerns when the clues begin to flow.
- Roles: Each team designates a spymaster who gives one-word clues plus a number. The number indicates how many words on the grid the clue relates to. For example, “Ocean 3” might point to three water-related words. The word must be a clue that links to multiple words and should be unambiguous enough for your team to catch, but not so broad that the other team can steal it.
- The Clue: The spymaster must give a clue that links to as many of their own words as possible, while avoiding the other team’s words and the assassin. The one-word limit is sacred; multi-syllabic oaths are optional but discouraged.
- Guessing: Players discuss and decide which words match the clue. They may guess up to the number given, plus one extra guess if they feel confident. Every wrong guess can end a round early if you hit an opponent word, an innocent word, or the dreaded assassin.

- Jekyll and Hyde moment: The more you play, the better you become at spotting subtle connections. But beware: a single misstep can give your opponents a swift victory, or worse, end the game with a tense standoff that feels like you just gambled your pride away at a sci-fi con.

- The “Duet” mode: Two players, one who takes both teams’ words? The mechanics are tweaked to keep play flowing while preserving the spirit of clue-crafting. It’s ideal for couples or two obsessives who want to outwit each other without a crowd.
- The “Pictures” variant: A different deck of cards with images instead of words. You still partner with your team and guess, but the anchors for your clues are more visual than verbal. This can be a fun change of pace, especially if your group loves artful misdirection.

## Mind games: clue craft and misdirection

Clue crafting is where the art and psychology collide. Here’s how to become a better clue-giver without summoning the spirit of your high school English teacher.

### Good clues vs bad clues

- Good clue example: “Technology 3” linking to “Robot”, “Laptop”, and “Virus” (assuming those are on the board and your team owns those words). It’s specific enough to be testable but broad enough to cover multiple target words.
- Bad clue example: “Thing 7” which is both vague and almost guaranteed to invite a cascade of random guesses. The spell is broken the moment a non-target word looks like a suspiciously perfect fit.

### The art of counting: linking exactly N words

A true maestro of Codenames can squeeze a clue that neatly ties together exactly N of their words, maximizing correct guesses while minimizing the chance of the other team stealing. It’s a tightrope walk: too few words and you waste turns; too many and you hand victory to your opponents.
