## Design and Build Quality: How Do They Handle the Apocalypse?

### Materials and Durability

The dashboards come in two core materials, depending on the edition you snag: a rigid laminated cardstock variant and a tougher acrylic version in some bundles. The laminated cardstock option offers a near-paper-thin feel but with glare-resistant coating that makes it legible under basement lighting and the occasional ceiling light sting. The acrylic version is a touch more premium, with a glassy surface that’s easier to wipe clean after a heroic misstep where a goblin’s spell slips across the table and leaves greasy fingerprints like battle scars. Either way, the edge rounding and corner safety feel deliberate, which matters when you’re clumsy and reach for another dice flip and end up finger-snapping into a zombie’s pride.

### Readability and Layout

Clarity is king here. The dashboards present a grid of zones: Action Track, Move Points, Wounds, Inventory, and a Hero Status line. The typography is legible from across the table, which is exactly what you want when you’re crouched over a board with 30 zombies on the doorstep. The color-coding is helpful without being gimmicky: red for wounds, green for health, blue for XP, and amber for action points. The overlay helps players who might otherwise squint and miscount the number of remaining actions or the number of times they have to heal a comrade before the municipal zombie-safety inspector shows up with a ledger and a stern look.

### Aesthetic Cohesion

Now, aesthetics may seem trivial, but in a game with a table full of chaos, a cohesive dashboard set reduces cognitive load. The four dashboards share a consistent design language, with subtle fantasy motifs and the same font across all four. It feels cohesive enough that you could place four of these in a row and your table would still look like a coordinated gaming outfit rather than a mismatched garage sale. Fred, Ross, Will, and Helen each have slight decorative flourishes tailored to their personas—without overdoing it—so you don’t feel like you’re playing with cookie-cutter gear.

## How It Changes the Game: Cognitive Load, Flow, and the Flowstate

One of the biggest selling points of a dashboard set like this is the promise of smoother turns. In Zombicide Black Plague, the turn sequence is a carnival of actions: move, search, sprint, loot, attack, end turn. Counting wounds, tracking ammo, and verifying which survivor has the next action can easily derail a table that’s already in survival mood. Here’s how the dashboards help:

- Quick reference: The action points and moves are visually segmented. You don’t need to flip through a rulebook mid-turmoil to confirm how many times you can reroll a die or execute a special ability.
- Memory relief: The wound counters and HP track across the dashboard reduces the mental bandwidth required to track individual counters scattered across the board. You can focus on choices rather than ledger-keeping.
- Coordination: With four dashboards, each player can own a dedicated panel, enabling faster communication and less friction when teammates coordinate a multi-turn plan.
- Flow maintenance: The dashboards reduce table clutter. Fewer counters on the board means you have a better sightline for strategic planning, which in a game about surging undead can be a rare luxury.

From a gameplay perspective, the difference is not revolutionary, but it is meaningful. It’s the kind of accessory that makes you feel like you’ve upgraded from a rental car to a sporty ride in terms of organization. The table looks more professional, players feel more in control, and that subtle sense of mastery can translate into stronger strategic decisions in the heat of the moment.

## Setup, Use, and Day-to-Day Nuisances

### Setup Time

If you’re the kind of person who likes a quick vibe check before a session, you’ll appreciate that these dashboards don’t demand a ritual. You pop them out of the packaging, slide the counters into their zones, and you’re ready to roll. The more you use them, the more you’ll appreciate their consistency. The only caveat is that if your group isn’t used to a dedicated dashboard per player, there will be a gentle learning curve about how to interpret the status line at the top of each card. After one extended game where you compare zombie counts and XP, you’ll be nudged into a routine that reduces chaos rather than adds to it.

### Maintenance

The laminated surface cleans easily with a damp cloth. If you accidentally spill the last Tonic of the goblin, it wipes away with a quick wipe. Acrylic versions can take a few more wipe-downs because their glossy finish can smudge slightly with marker residue. It’s wise to keep a microfiber cloth nearby. If you decide to use dry-erase markers, be mindful that some coating types can ghost after a few sessions; a gentle scrub with a non-abrasive cleaner and a fresh marker can restore legibility without harming the surface.

### Marker Etiquette and Reusable Counters

A lot of players like to accompany dashboards with separate dry-erase counters for wounds and XP. If your set includes markers, be mindful of marker colors — you don’t want a red marker in the XP zone, only to find it’s bleeding into the wound track mid-turn. The best practice is to assign a color scheme and stick to it for the entire game. Fred gets red for wounds, Ross gets blue for actions, Will gets green for health, and Helen gets amber for XP. It’s silly, but it’s that kind of silly that helps your brain stay on track when the undead are knocking at the door.
