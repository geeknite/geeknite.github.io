## What is Sushi Go! exactly, and what’s the Pick and Pass thing all about?

Sushi Go! is a light, fast-paced card-drafting game designed by Phil Walker-Harding and published by Gamewright. The premise is delightfully simple: you’re assembling a live-action sushi spread by drafting cards from your hand and passing the rest to the next player. Each round, you quickly select one card to keep and pass the rest—like a round-robin conveyor belt that only deals with edible happiness and a hint of strategic pedantry.

The core mechanic is card drafting, but Sushi Go! distills it to its most digestible form: everyone starts with a hand of 7 cards (in a two-player game, fewer cards are used, but the tempo remains crisp). You pick a card and pass the rest to your neighbor. The next player does the same, and so on until all cards in that hand have been drafted. At the end of each round, you tally points for combinations of cards you collected—things like Tempura, Sashimi, and Maki rolls pairings—while special cards provide quick points. The tempo is brisk, the tension light, and the theme is charmingly edible. The result is a family-friendly game that somehow doesn’t feel childish but rather delightfully mischievous in a friendly way.

For the sake of web-shares and cross-compatible humor, here’s a quick external primer: Sushi Go! keeps the play simple enough for newcomers to pick up in a single round, yet it sneaks in enough scoring nuance to reward careful attention. It’s the kind of game where a well-timed Nigiri can swing a round, and a failed Early Makizushi investment can sting in a middle round. The joy comes from predicting what your opponents might pass to you, and the moment you realize you’ve misread their intentions and now must pivot with a shrug and a smile.

## How to play: a quick, digestible walkthrough

If you’ve played any card-drafting game, you’ll recognize the flow. If you haven’t, fear not: this is a gentle onboarding that’ll have you drafting in less than 2 minutes after you’ve looked at the first page of the rulebook.

### Setup
- Shuffle the deck and deal a starting hand to each player. The exact number of cards depends on the player count, but the principle remains: you get a handful to choose from.
- Place the first player marker somewhere visible. In two-player games, you’ll adjust the hand size so the rounds stay brisk.
- Decide who will be the first to pick, or rotate the first-pick token around the table. The rotation of “first pick” is part of the game’s fun, because it creates a light social game of who snags which piece first.

### The draft and pass
- On your turn, examine your current hand. Pick one card to add to your tableau (your personal set of sushi you’re collecting for those sweet point bonuses) and pass the rest to the next player.
- Everyone does the same, until all cards have been drafted.
- After each round of drafting (which is usually 7 or 8 card passes, depending on player count), tally points for any completed sets or related bonuses. Then reshuffle the discarded or unscored cards and start another round.

### Scoring in a pinch
- Tempura bonuses reward a pair of Tempura cards. If you collect two Tempura, you’ll turn two points into a flash of victory when the round ends.
- Sashimi works similarly but with a triple: three Sashimi are worth three points if you manage to collect all three.
- Nigiri cards score points directly (1, 2, or 3 points depending on the specific Nigiri). But there are sometimes special rules (for example, wasabi triples the value of your next nigiri) that can make your palate—but not your brain—tingle with delight.
- Maki rolls are a little different: the player who has the most Maki at the end of the round gains a bonus, and in some variations that bonus can swing the final tally.
- There are other cards with simple, direct effects that can affect scoring in micro-shifts—these are the “puzzle pieces” that keep the game interesting without overwhelming you with a sandwich of rules.

### End of game and final scoring
- The game ends after a predetermined number of rounds (usually three or four, based on the player count and the edition you own).
- Tally per-round points, apply any end-of-game bonuses, and declare the winner. In a good night, you’ll see the leaderboards shuffle rapidly as the table’s fortunes shift with a single card pick.

## Strategy and nuance: good decisions that don’t feel heavy-handed

Sushi Go! is deliciously accessible, but there’s more beneath the surface than you might expect for a game that fits in your shirt pocket. Here are some practical takeaways that will help you do better without turning the table into a calculus seminar.

### Card priorities and how to think about them
- Tempura and Sashimi: These two sets create density in your scoring. The risk is that you’re committing to those combos with limited cards; if you miss them, your points may disappoint. The trick is to balance pursuing these combos with not over-committing early and letting better opportunities slip away.
- Nigiri and Wasabi: Nigiri cards are straightforward, but the Wasabi can turn a single nigiri into a surprising power move when you manage to pair them. The decision is whether to chase Wasabi at the risk of losing stock for other more valuable combos.
- Maki rolls and other bonuses: Consider your table’s passing tendencies. If everyone tends to pass away a lot of Maki, you may want to grab Maki early to lock up the bonus in later turns; if players are hoarding nigiri, you might pivot to the other combos to avoid being left with a few low-scoring cards.

### Reading the room and predicting passes
- The core skill in Sushi Go! is not calculating points in a spreadsheet; it’s predicting what your neighbors will pass to you next. If you notice a pattern (for example, a player in seat to your left tends to grab all of a particular type early), you can adjust your picks to block them or to set yourself up for a rack of points on the next round.
- Don’t be afraid to diversify your picks in the early rounds. The game rewards balance: a mix of small but steady point getters with occasional big scorers is usually more reliable than pinning all hopes on one big combo that may vanish on a single pass.

### Tempo and psychological dynamics
- Sushi Go! is a social game, and a lot of the fun comes from the small tells—subtle nods, a quick glance at the cards, the way someone smiles when they see a particular set. It’s light-hearted misdirection rather than a high-stakes poker night. Don’t take the game too seriously; it’s built to be shared, not hoarded.
- The 2-player version moves a touch faster, which makes it excellent for a quick two-hand duel or a “beat the clock” challenge when you have a time limit. With 3-5 players, you get more opinions and more opportunities for misreadings and comedic collisions of strategy and luck.

## The 2-5 player dynamics: how the game scales

Sushi Go! shines in its ability to accommodate a variety of player counts without feeling imbalanced. Let’s break down the practical implications.

### Two players: a brisk, heads-up sushi sprint
Two players creates rapid cycles. The cards rotate quickly, and you’ll feel the tempo intensify as you jockey for combos faster than your stomach can register a full meal. It’s an excellent way to practice your drafting instincts in a low-stakes setting. You’ll have to be more precise with your blocking, since you don’t have as much “body language” from the table as a larger group. The advantage is a sharper focus on optimal paths, which can be satisfying for players who enjoy a bit of “think fast, score fast” gameplay.

### Three to five players: the social engine of the game
With more players, the social dynamics become louder and more chaotic—in a good way. You get to observe more patterns, more passes, and more opportunities to cereal-kick your friends with that last-minute three-card combo you somehow pulled off. The game remains tight in this range; rounds stay short enough to avoid table fatigue, but long enough to keep the drama alive. The variance in receiving a strong starter hand versus a weak one keeps every session feeling fresh rather than monotonous.
