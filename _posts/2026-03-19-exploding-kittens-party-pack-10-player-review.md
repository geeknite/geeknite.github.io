## How 10 Players Changes Everything: The Group Dynamics Edition
Expanding Exploding Kittens to 10 players is like moving from a tricycle to a mini-van. The pacing changes, the tension multiplies, and the chance of seeing someone make a bold bluff that blows up in everyone’s face skyrockets. Here’s what to expect when you slot in a full house.

- More pauses, more micro-bluffs: With ten players, there’s always someone who has a Defuse card that they’re almost certainly not going to use when it matters most, or maybe they will, and you’ll be left to contemplate your life choices.
- Increased chaos during defusal moments: The Defuse cards feel precious and suspect at the same time. In a 10-player game, the timing of a Defuse can swing the mood from “we’re having fun” to “we are now performing a statistical experiment on risk tolerance.”
- Longer rounds, but often with bigger payoffs: The doom in Exploding Kittens isn’t just the bomb; it’s the dramatic reveal of what players do with the cards next. Ten players can extend a single round into a mini-drama where rivalries are born and snacks disappear.
- Table etiquette becomes a feature, not a bug: Folks learn to navigate the table’s geometry—who’s blocking the view of who, who’s got the Defuse card face-down, and who forgets to shuffle their deck properly because they were too busy smirking.

All this adds up to a game that rewards memory and misdirection almost as much as luck. ## How to Play: A Quick Guide for The Ten-Person Tango
Note that this is a streamlined overview. For the full rules, you’ll want the official rulebook; we also link to a couple of well-trodden community clarifications at the bottom of this post.

### Setup
- Shuffle all the cards and deal a compact starting hand to each player (the exact number depends on house rules, but typically 4 Defuse cards are spread out to give late-game hope).
- Each player takes turns drawing cards while trying not to trigger an Exploding Kitten. The Defuse cards can save the day—but you’ll want to keep them for the right moment.
- Place the Exploding Kitten deck and the Defuse deck in their own tidy piles. Keep a couple of counter cards handy to maintain a sense of balance.

### The Core Loop
1) On your turn, you do one action (or skip and draw, if you’re feeling especially bold). 2) Draw a card from the deck. 3) If you draw an Exploding Kitten, you must either play a Defuse card or suffer the consequences and hope someone else explodes before you do. 4) If a Defuse card is played, you get to place the Exploding Kitten back into the deck somewhere of your choosing, which is a deliciously devious moment for the table.

The action cards add spice—cards that force other players to reshuffle, peek at the top of a deck, or steal cards from someone’s hand. In a 10-player setup, those actions can change the entire row of outcomes in a single turn, which makes the game feel perpetually fresh even after multiple rounds.

### Ending the Game
The last player remaining who hasn’t drawn an Exploding Kitten (and not expelled by an exploding moment) wins. It’s a trophy that smells faintly of victory and popcorn. Historically, a 10-player game can end with a flurry of last-second defusings and a dramatic “I told you so” from the person who held the Defuse card for every other player’s misfortune.

## Strategy Notes: Shortcuts to Fun (Not Geniuses, Just Smiles)
- Save a few Defuse cards for late-stage explosions. In a 10-player game, the endgame often hinges on whether someone can survive the last couple of pulls with a secure Defuse reserve.
- Bluff inconsistently. Don’t telegraph all your moves. A surprising play or random card reveal can force others to reevaluate their own risk tolerance and potentially trigger misreads.
- Watch the rhythm. In large groups, players sometimes get tangled in conversation, giving you room to slip a crucial move past a cautious neighbor. Don’t overthink it—this game rewards social savvy as much as it rewards luck.
- Balance between offense and defense. Sometimes it is worth a mental tax to push other players into risky placements, but sometimes you want to hoard risk management for a later turn. The decision window is larger when there are more players to target and protect in equal measure.
