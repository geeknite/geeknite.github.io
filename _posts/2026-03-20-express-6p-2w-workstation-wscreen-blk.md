- In a straight-line configuration, leave at least 1 meter of clearance behind the seating line for chair movement and cable access. If you’re aligning against a wall, a 0.75 meter clearance behind the bench will suffice for occasional access to power and network hubs.
- A bridge or L-shaped layout can help with breakout zones. The idea is to prevent cognitive clutter by giving teams a natural anchor while keeping peripheral screens in sight.
- If you have a window wall, consider using lighter colored partitions at the ends to bounce light and avoid the tunnel effect of a dark desk spine.

### Screen integration and viewing comfort
The built-in screen option reduces the number of external monitor arms and the clutter that comes with a sea of screens. You’ll need to measure your typical monitor sizes and weight to ensure compatibility with the support arms. The goal is to keep the line of sight at roughly 20 to 30 degrees downward from eye level, with top-of-screen at or just below eye height. This is a standard ergonomic target that minimizes neck strain during long coding sessions or spreadsheet triage.

Pro tip: if your team is using video conferencing, place the screen so that everyone has a clear view without craning their necks. It’s a small thing, but it makes a big difference when you’re flipping between a design review and a sprint planning board.

## Tech and integration: screens, power, and data
### Screen integration considerations
The built-in screen option is a nice touch for teams that want to reduce cable clutter and unify the display experience. Ensure the display size fits your collaboration style. If you frequently share the screen with clients or cross-functional teams, you’ll want a display with reliable brightness and stable viewing angles. PDUs (power distribution units) integrated into the desk back can simplify plug counts, but you’ll still want to check the total amperage draw for all devices and avoid overloading a single circuit.

### Power and data routing
A well-planned power path prevents the dreaded “ghost reboot” during a sprint retrospective. Use a combination of surge protection and cable routing channels to separate data lines from power lines where possible. It’s not just best practice; it’s a sanity saver for IT and a comfort booster for the team.

### Monitor arms and keyboards
If you choose to customize beyond the built-in screen, monitor arms should be rated for the weight and size of your displays. Keyboard trays should offer adjustable tilt and comfortable mouse space without encroaching on the chair’s clearance. A practical approach is to allocate dedicated per-seat space for input devices and booking a nearby wall for shared peripherals to maintain a clean desk surface.
