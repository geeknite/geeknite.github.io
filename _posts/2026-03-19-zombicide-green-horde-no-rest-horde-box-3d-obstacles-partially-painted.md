## Introduction

Welcome to another glorious dive into the world of the undead with a thatch-your-own-beard level of enthusiasm for the grim and giggly. Today we dig into Zombicide Green Horde, a game that invites you to shed your civilized self and embrace the glorious chaos of green-skinned mayhem. This particular edition/collection bundle includes the base Green Horde core, the No Rest expansion, the Horde Box, a clutch of 3D obstacles, and yes, a batch of minis that are only partially painted because, well, life happens and the goblins of paintbrush frustration are legion.

If you know how to roll a die and occasionally scream, you probably know what Zombicide is about—cooperative play, tiny heroes with big ambitions, and a constant sense that the zombies are somehow always two steps ahead of your planning. The Green Horde setting flips the usual zombie apocalypse vibe: these aren’t city-destroying undead in black-and-white, they’re forest-dwelling, green-skinned, horde-brained mayhem with a bit of fantasy flair. It’s a weird mix of high fantasy and survival horror, served with a side of slapstick when your survivor trips over a hobgoblin’s club or a 3D obstacle block fits the wrong way and somehow becomes a tactical conundrum.

This review is a long-form love letter to the experience when you stack all the pieces together: the expansion content, the glorious Horde Box, the 3D terrain accents, and the joy (and occasional frustration) of painting minis that were not fully painted out of the box. Spoiler: when you blend it all, you get one of those tabletop experiences that makes you grin, grimace, and immediately plan a rematch so you can fix your misplays with the speed of a zombie scuttling toward a fresh snack.

> If you want the quick verdict up front: the No Rest and Horde Box expansions in combination with the 3D obstacles lift the game from a competent sprint through a swamp to an entertaining, sometimes ridiculous, and deeply satisfying marathon through a forest of chaos. The partially painted minis add personality, while reminding you that tabletop gaming is a hobby with a list of hobbies under its belt. There are rough edges—like any game that throws more plastic at you than a dragon in a candy store—but the upside is sticky, delicious zombie syrup.

## What's in the Box? Unboxing the Green Horde Medley

Unboxing is where the romance begins. The Green Horde box itself is a handsome, sturdy container that promises not to sneeze open in your arms at the worst possible moment. Inside you’ll find the core heroes, the zombified masses, and a handful of sceneries that say, I am a forest and I am here to complicate your day in the nicest possible way.

### The Base Game: Zombicide Green Horde

Green Horde keeps the core mechanism that made Zombicide famous: players control survivors who must work together to complete objectives while she-wolf zombies with big teeth (and bigger goals) advance along the board. The greenskins here are not just re-skinned walkers; they’re ambushers, pack hunters, and occasionally surprisingly cunning early-game strategic blockers. The base game gives you a handful of missions that emphasize terrain, chokepoints, and the occasional “we really should have brought more ranged weapons” moment.

### No Rest for the Wicked (Expansion)

No Rest is an expansion that spices up the late-game tension. It adds new spawn pools, stronger zombie variants, and a few quirky event cards that threaten to derail even the most confident plan. The name suggests a grim inevitability: your survivors will not catch a nap, and the undead aren’t exactly yawning either. It’s a good tension booster, especially when you’re in the middle of a long, grueling scenario and the next wave reveals a new twist that makes you wonder if you should have taken up knitting instead.

### Horde Box

The Horde Box is the kind of content that makes you feel like you’ve leveled up your apocalypse board. It adds more enemies, more objectives, and more variety to the zombie rosters. Expect bigger crowds, more special rules, and the satisfying moment when your newly minted survivor with a shiny new weapon actually uses it to devastating effect. The Horde Box can scale the game up to a degree that demands a stricter plan and arguably more snacks.

### 3D Obstacles

Then there are the 3D obstacles. These are the spice rack of the box—the crunchy bits that transform a map from a flat, boring battlefield into a multi-layered battlefield with elevation, cover lines, barricades, and the occasional trap that almost guarantees you’ll trip over your own feet. The 3D terrain adds both aesthetic charm and tactical complexity: walls that block line of sight, doors that actually require action to open, and barricades that convert a straightforward sprint into a careful, measured advance. They can either hamper your plan or save your bacon depending on how artful your placement becomes.

### The Partially Painted Minis

Let us not pretend you opened this box with a paintbrush in one hand and a coffee mug in the other. The minis arrive partially painted, giving you a sense of progress without imprisoning you in the moral complexity of painting 50 goblins while your cat stares at you with existential judgment. Partially painted minis offer a pleasing look on the table and a tiny bit of personal achievement. They also allow you to pick a stubborn color scheme you’ll return to for weeks, because painting is a journey, not a race. If you like the vibe of a living, breathing forest army with a few mismatched colors that somehow works, you’ll feel right at home here.

> Visual note: the base greens are rich, the accents pop, and the vibe you get from a partially painted board is something like a fantasy forest depicted by someone who enjoys a blood-pumping game but refuses to give up on their brushwork.

## The 3D Obstacles: A Tactical Love Letter

Terrain has always been the heart of Zombicide’s puzzle. With 3D obstacles, you’re pairing a tactile experience with the satisfaction of cutting off one more alley for the undead to wander through. The 3D walls and barriers are not mere décor; they’re real obstacles that force you to rethink your approach. Here are a few thoughts on how they shape gameplay:

- Elevation changes line of sight and line of attack. A survivor perched on a ledge can rain arrows or axes, while a zombie stack on the ground is suddenly a much easier target. This is not just a cosmetic flourish; it’s a mechanical nudge toward smarter positioning.
- Ambush points become possible. If you place a 3D obstacle correctly, you can funnel the horde into a choke point, turning a potential wipe into a fight you can win with the right combination of dice rolls and teamwork.
- Doorways and barricades create a living labyrinth. You’ll spend more turns planning how to breach a room or how to drag a wounded teammate through a restricted corridor. It’s not just about killing zombies; it’s about solving a spatial puzzle with friends.

Speaking of doors, the No Rest expansion interacts nicely with these pieces because it frequently changes when and where spawns occur, turning a previously safe corridor into a survival obstacle. The result is a game that remains tense without tipping into frustration—a balancing act that is not easy to pull off with multiple add-ons, but the authors here managed to do it with a wink and a well-timed scream when a zombie busts through a barricade you thought was rock solid.


## Painting Status: Partially Painted, Fully Enjoyable

Partially painted minis often spark a conversation that begins with, Should I paint these or not? Most players will tell you to embrace the partial vibe as a feature, not a flaw. It gives your table a unique personality and makes you feel like a general who is good at strategy but terrible at finishing squad color schemes. And there is something satisfying about a forest of green figures on a board that doesn’t look perfectly uniform.

If you want to level up your hobby game, you can always pick a few key units to finish first. Target the leaders or the most important models for your current scenario, then let the rest be that convenient mid-point between tabletop-ready and art project. The result is a board that feels dynamic and lived-in rather than perfectly polished—a vibe that fits well with a chaotic zombie apocalypse with a hint of fantasy whimsy.


## Gameplay Flow: How the Pieces Play Together

The Green Horde is famous for a relatively accessible rule set that scales up nicely with expansion content. With No Rest and the Horde Box, you’ll notice three persistent shifts in how the game plays:

- Wave pacing becomes more punishing. The No Rest rules ensure there’s no quiet night smoking a soggy cigarette by the campfire; the next wave will arrive with more ferocity, asking you to double‑down on your strategic planning.
- More objectives, more moving parts. The Horde Box brings additional mission goals that require you to split your focus between killing the undead and securing critical assets. This keeps the table lively and ensures every player has something to contribute besides rolling dice with dramatic flair.
- Terrain influences every move. The 3D obstacles create a dynamic battlefield where you must constantly re-evaluate line of sight, cover, and the speed at which you can traverse a map. It’s a puzzle that rewards careful measurement and planning and punishes recklessness with the sticky, satisfying pain of a bad dice roll.

On a day when you’re determined to win, these expansions reward teamwork and communication. The survivors aren’t lone wolves; they’re a small band of people who need to coordinate their shots, their reloads, and their movement to maximize damage while minimizing risk. The No Rest twist makes it feel like the board is always one step ahead—like a forest that refuses to let you rest, even when you find a safe little alcove behind a 3D log pile.


## Scenarios and Strategy: Making the Most of It

If you approach this collection with the right mindset, you’ll unlock some surprisingly satisfying strategies. Here are a few that work particularly well with the combined set:

- Corridor control: Use 3D walls to create funnel points. Position your melee survivors at choke points while ranged survivors pick off zombies at a safer distance. This is classic Zombicide strategy, but the 3D elements make it feel fresh and tactile.
- Objective synergy: The Horde Box’s extra goals often reward you for controlling specific zones or collecting resources. Plan routes that allow you to move through critical areas while still protecting your team from a sudden surge.
- No Rest timing: The No Rest rules push you toward aggressive early waves. Plan for a mid‑game lull followed by a late‑game push. Don’t rely on a single hero carrying you to victory; you’ll need at least two to handle the more difficult encounters that come with the expansion.

The best part is that you can mix and match with other Zombicide sets without losing the core experience. The Green Horde’s fantasy-forest vibe blends surprisingly well with other themes—if you’ve got a penchant for cosmic horror or medieval fantasy, you’ll find the tonal blend surprisingly satisfying.

If you’re artistically inclined, the partially painted minis offer you a canvas to complete a color scheme you’re proud of. ## Practicalities: Setup, Play Time, and Game Pace

- Setup: The inclusion of 3D obstacles means you’ll spend a little more time arranging terrain, but the payoff is immediate. It creates a sense of anticipation and a better visual narrative for the fight to come. A quick pre-game checklist becomes your friend: inventory the miniatures, check the rulebook, preview the new spawn rules, and place the 3D terrain pieces in a way that fosters dynamic movement without creating unsolvable bottlenecks.
- Play Time: A typical run of the core game with a couple of expansions will take longer than a base Zombicide match. Expect closer to 90–180 minutes per session depending on player count and how many times the dice tumble in your favor. If you’re playing with new players, be ready for a longer first session as people acclimate to the terrain, enemy variety, and strategic synergy. If you’re playing with veterans, you’ll shave time by leaning on common patterns—two healers, one brute, a sharpshooter, and a misbehaving scout who keeps using the terrain to hide from danger.
- Game Pace: The No Rest addition ensures you’ll never feel a quick lull. Enemies spawn with more intention, and the board feels alive with movement. This pacing is a double-edged sword: it keeps tension high, but it can become punishing if you’re not managing risk appropriately. The Horde Box adds more ways to fail forward, which is glorious for dramatic comebacks and equally painful when your last dual-wielding hero is overwhelmed by a door‑opening miscalculation.

## The Verdict: Is It Worth It?

Short answer: yes, it is worth it if you love modular terrain, a slightly more brutal zombie cadence, and the ability to blame your painted mini blues on a hobby rather than your gaming prowess. Long answer: there are a few caveats. The No Rest expansion can feel punishing if your table doesn’t like hard mode, and the Horde Box’s additional components mean more setup time. The 3D obstacles, while spectacular, can distract or slow down new players until you all learn the rhythm of moving around them. The partially painted minis give you character or a reminder of unfinished projects, depending on your mood. If you’re patient and enjoy the process of building a world on your dining room table with friends, then this collection is a gold‑foil badge of your hobbyist status.

If you’re starting fresh, you might want to introduce No Rest and the Horde Box gradually, letting players acclimate to the core mechanics first. If you’re a grizzled Zombicide veteran who wants to inflate the challenge and the aesthetic, then go all in—3D obstacles, paint dab nearby, and a playlist that makes you feel like you’re storming a forest fortress with a handful of heroic misfits and a couple of dice that are determined to roll snake eyes at the most inconvenient moment.
