### How the Cat Burglar Twist Affects Core Gameplay

The base Exploding Kittens formula remains the same: draw a card, do a thing, avoid exploding and dying of laughter. The Cat Burglar Edition adds a new layer of social deduction and opportunistic thievery. Cat Burglar cards let you steal a card from another player usually a card that someone desperately wants to defend, like a Defuse or a powerful Attack card. Thematic flavor is strong here; imagine a world where cats with tiny burglar masks try to outwit each other to maximize chaos without triggering the inevitable global catastrophe of coin-free snack deprivation.

From a design standpoint, this edition is clever about balancing risk and reward. Cat Burglar cards require you to align with the table’s mood: if everyone is giggling and goofing off, the Cat Burglar moves feel fun and fair. If you’re playing with a hyper-competent group that’s grumpier than a cat about baths, Cat Burglar cards can become a little too punishing, turning the table into a petty land of you stole from me so I steal from you grudges. The key is to emphasize humor over vengeance, which is what this edition nails when the group vibe is right.

### How to Play: Setup and Turn Flow

1) Setup: Shuffle the deck with any included Cat Burglar cards. Each player draws a starting hand that includes a few Defuse cards and a random mix of Attack/Skip cards. The Exploding Kitten cards are shuffled separately and placed into the draw pile. Place the rest of the tokens and counters where everyone can reach them.

2) Objective: Avoid drawing an Exploding Kitten without a Defuse, and accumulate fun, chaotic moments by using Cat Burglar steals strategically.

3) Turn order: On your turn, you draw a card, then play any number of cards from your hand to modify the state of play. You can use Attack to skip your next turn, Defuse if you’re holding one (which is a lifesaver in this edition as the explosions are more unpredictable than a cat’s mood), and Cat Burglar cards to steal from other players. The official phrasing in the rulebook is silly but accessible, which is exactly what party games need.

4) Cat Burglar interactions: If you play a Cat Burglar card targeting someone else, you steal one card at random from their hand (unless they counter with a card like Nope, if that is included in your edition). The steal is more comedic than brutal most of the time, which aligns with the party-game vibe. The table’s reactions—shouts, mock outrage, and requests to call an emergency snack break—are often the best part of the experience.

When you get the flow right, the game becomes a rhythm: draw, play some playful interference, cat-burglarize someone’s favorable card, dodge an inevitable Exploding Kitten with a Defuse or a cleverly timed Skip. If you’re teaching a group new to EK, emphasize the comedic principle: it’s better to laugh together than to play harshly. The Cat Burglar mechanic is a great way to ease beginners into the idea that you can influence others without dominating the table.
