## Rulebook: Korean+English bilingual edition

### Layout and accessibility
The bilingual rulebook occupies the same physical space as a standard English-only manual, but with a bilingual layout that alternates sections or pairs translations side-by-side. If you’re the kind of player who flips between languages to test your cognitive flexibility, you’ll enjoy the way the text flows: English on one line, Korean on the next, then a quick cross-check where the two languages diverge in nuance. ### Key rules distilled
- Gameplay loop: In Modern Art, players participate in a series of auctions for artworks. Each round features a new painting with a price track; players bid using money tokens. After each round, the artworks are sold, and players receive points based on their holdings and the popularity of each artist group.
- Pricing dynamics: The price of an artwork is determined by the sequence of selling prices from previous rounds and the track’s position. The more someone pays for a style early on, the more valuable it becomes later—unless you’re the player who sniffs out a trend and pivots to a different artist / style mid-game.
- Scoring: End-game scoring blends artwork value, artist variety, and the distribution of owned art. You want a balanced portfolio rather than a tunnel-vision collection of one style that turns out to be briefly hot and then forgotten by the crowd. Sound familiar, art fans? The market is fickle.

### Bilingual nuance and translation joys

## How to play (quick refresher + tips)

### Setup
- Shuffle art tiles and lay out the price track. The number of artists and artworks corresponds to player count. In this DiceTree edition, the box maintains its structure well, making setup a matter of minutes rather than a ritualistic scavenger hunt for missing components.
- Each player starts with a fixed amount of money. You know the drill: you’ll bid against players who want a style you barely tolerate—yet somehow the market rewards the brave.

### The bid and sell flow
- A round begins with a reveal of an artwork’s style. Players secretly choose a bid amount and reveal simultaneously. The highest bidder wins the right to own that artwork and places a price marker on the track.
- As rounds progress, artworks are sold and the market shifts. Price multipliers and the distribution of art styles across players drive strategic choices: do you chase a popular style early or wait for a more undervalued opportunity?
- Scoring occurs at the end of the game, with points granted for each artist style owned, adjusted by the artwork’s position on the track and its popularity. It’s a compact scoring system, but with enough levers to generate meaningful decisions each turn.

### Strategy nibs and tips
- Trend spotting is your friend: early bids on the right style can pay off if you predict a market swing. But beware the herd: if you buy too aggressively on a popular wave, you’ll end up overpaying for future pieces as the track advances.
- Diversify, but don’t overdiversify: a mixed portfolio of styles is good, but you don’t want to own every single piece from four different movements and nothing from two others. Art markets reward balanced exposure with occasional bold bets.
- Read the room: in a bilingual edition, language barriers can create subtle misreads of intent. If you suspect confusion at the table, defuse it with humor and clarifying questions in both languages. That’s right—you’re part comedian, part economist, all gamer.
