## The Cards in Focus

In every Zombicide run, the menace is met by a crew, and in this edition Carson, Gilly, Igor and Matt each bring a distinct flavor to the table. The cards are the quick, tactile way to gauge a survivor s personality and capabilities, and they are designed to reward planning and team synergy. The interplay between the four survivors allows you to craft strategies that lean into protection, loot runs, or siege style play where you attempt to bulldoze a choke point with careful choreography. The layout is clean, the color blocks easy to read, and the art provides enough visual shorthand to avoid page flipping mid battle. You will find yourself noting combos that simply feel satisfying, like a well-timed retreat or a clutch rescue that keeps the mission alive.

### Carson

Carson is the rugged improviser who makes do with what s at hand. His kit tends to reward quick thinking and fast movement, a classic hit in the Zombicide tradition. When you run Carson, you feel the thrill of a fast rescue, a clean getaway, and the satisfaction of snagging an essential weapon just as you realize the highway of doom might split into two lanes. The Carson card emphasizes timing, enabling players to string two or three actions together in a way that feels cinematic without breaking the game balance. For players who enjoy the feel of a well executed plan, Carson becomes your go to anchor, a reliable friend who helps anchor the party through the worst waves.

### Gilly

Gilly is the wildcard, the one who makes chaos feel almost like choreography. Her card brings a mix of zone control and rapid reaction, a blend that can turn a bad round into a birthday party for the undead if you misread the map. The art and flavor on the Gilly card invite you to lean into environment interactions, to see a doorway as a potential trap and a resource at the same time. When the table finds its rhythm with Gilly, you get this sense of a clever dance on the board where mistakes become opportunities and opportunities become legends. If you enjoy the thrill of playing a risk heavy but rewarding character, Gilly is your magnet.

### Igor

Igor brings the medic vibe with a little lab humor baked in. He is the planner who steps into danger with a plan to stabilize teammates and turn the tide on a critical turn. The Igor card rewards safe risk and careful sequencing, letting you convert a near miss into a successful extraction. The flavor text nods to classic lab notes and a dash of comedic stoicism, a signature of Zombicide art that geeks adore. The card’s readability supports players who prefer to chew through a problem rather than sprint past it, which makes Igor a good partner for new players learning to balance pace and safety.

### Matt

Matt rounds out the quartet with straightforward survival instincts and a penchant for weapon mastery. The Matt card is a steady, reliable option for players who want a strong backbone on the board. He helps the team maintain momentum when the zombie tide is climbing the stairs, and his presence anchors the run so you can coordinate a better plan for the following round. The design of Matt s abilities is practical without being boring, offering a nice blend of solid action output and a few surprising moments that remind you that Zombicide is never all doom and no play. 

Between these four, the synergy is the real payoff. You can orchestrate a chain of moves that feels almost cinematic, a small victory lap in a world where every mission can crash in a swarm of groaning undead. The pairing of the four survivors gives you a flexible toolkit for approaching rooms, clearing corridors, and staging a dramatic extraction sequence to get everyone to safety. It is a small thing, but in a game about survival, small things matter a lot.

## Game Play and Experience

The moment you set up the board you feel the cadence of a tactical co op run. The expansion is not just a cosmetic upgrade; it folds into the base rules with new card interactions, new towers of zombies, and new missions that push you to balance exploration with a sense of doom. The sealed box is deceptive in the best possible way: it looks simple on the outside, but inside you discover a microcosm of play that can escalate into a satisfying campaign. If you have played Zombicide before, expect new layers of difficulty that reframe how you think about lane control, line of sight, and objective driving. If you are new to Zombicide, the learning curve is steeper but the path to mastery is straightforward enough to let you enjoy the experience without getting completely lost in the rulebook jungle.

We tested the set with a crew of four, three friends and one new player who had never played Zombicide before. The new player was initially overwhelmed by the density of rules and the variety of tiles. However, the clear card design and the reassuring presence of the four survivors helped bring them into the fold quickly. The players who were veterans of the base game appreciated how the new content added layers without tipping the balance into chaos. The sessions stayed tense but fair, and the group walked away with a sense of accomplishment that felt earned rather than luck based.

In terms of difficulty, this edition sits comfortably in the mid tier for experienced players, offering enough new interactions to keep things fresh while preserving the core loop that fans love. For newcomers, the learning curve is a bit steeper, but not insurmountable. A solid approach is to start with a lighter mission or to pair the new content with a couple of easy safe zones to build confidence before diving into the more punishing scenarios. Patience is a virtue in Zombicide, especially when your team has to decide whether to risk a corner trap or to bunker up and weather the next wave.

## Art and Theme

John Kovalic has a long track record of infusing games with his distinctive whimsy. In this edition, that charm is present without compromising the gritty vibe of a zombie apocalypse. The survivors look like they stepped out of a cartoon, yet the board imagery and zombie silhouettes keep the tension intact. The art offers enough personality that you can enjoy a narrative arc about Carson and his crew even as you sprint for a helicopter escape. The balance between humor and horror is delicate, and in this set it leans toward fun in a tasteful way that suits casual gaming nights as well as more serious campaigns.

We found that the cards reflect the personality of each survivor with little visual callbacks to the character behind the text. For example, the icons on Carson and Igor feel intuitive at a glance—the color coding and shapes help quick decision making during a busy round. The printed materials are also glossy and sturdy, a welcome upgrade from lower end editions that feel flimsy after a few sessions. The miniatures are well sculpted with dynamic poses that capture the vibe of action rather than static stasis.

If you want to see more of the art, you can explore the rest of the collection on the official site or on our image gallery page. The aesthetic is not just there to look pretty; it functions as a visual guide to the chaos on the board, and that is always a plus in a game that demands both strategy and speed.

## Final Verdict and Recommendations

Bottom line: this sealed edition with four new survivors and John Kovalic art is a charming and solid addition to the Zombicide line. It is not a radical overhaul of the base mechanics, but it doesn t need to be. The new cards, mission variety, and especially the distinctive art flavor bring a fresh coat of color to the table without destabilizing what makes Zombicide so beloved. If you are a fan of the base game and you enjoy expansions that enhance narrative options, this release might become a staple of your rotation.
