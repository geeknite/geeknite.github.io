## What Phase 10 is and isn’t

Phase 10 is a card game from Mattel that sits happily in the family-friendly, quick-to-learn camp of card games. The core idea is not to rack up points, but to complete the ten phases. Each phase is a specific combination of sets and runs that you must assemble with your cards, and you must complete the phases in order. The kit includes a deck with numbers, wilds, and skips, which adds layers of strategy, luck, and occasional soups of chaos. The game is widely known for its addictive tempo: you think you’re in control, then suddenly the discard pile looks suspiciously like your future misplay.

Two players can make Phase 10 feel surprisingly intense. With a smaller opponent pool, you see more of their decisions, which means you’ll get to react quickly. But you’ll also be forced to endure longer stretches of “do I risk the wild here or save it for Phase 8?” The heart of the game is not the deck itself; it’s the dance between risk and memory. Can you remember which phase your rival is on and what cards they might need next? Or will you simply bluff your way to victory with a confidently discarded misfit?

Note: The standard Phase 10 deck usually includes 108 cards per edition, with a handful of wilds and skip cards. The exact distribution varies by edition, but the core mechanic stays the same: complete phases, deal with the chaos, and keep your sense of humor intact.

## How to play with two players

Phase 10 is famously flexible, and the two-player variant is a strong test of your repeated patience and your ability to tolerate each other’s bad puns. Here is a quick, practical run-down to get you started:

- Setup: Each player draws ten cards to form their starting hand; the remaining cards form a draw pile, with the top card turned over to start the discard pile.

- Turn flow: On a turn, you draw one card from either the draw pile or the discard pile, then you decide whether you can lay down your current phase. If you do pack down a phase, you move on to the next phase on your next turn. If you can’t or don’t want to lay a phase yet, you discard one card.

- Phases: Phase 1 through Phase 10 define the required combinations. The first six phases typically involve sets and runs that become progressively more complex, with longer runs and more intricate combos as you approach Phase 10. You’re allowed to use wild cards to substitute for any card in a phase; skip cards affect the opponent by denying them a draw on their next turn.

- Scoring and ending: The round ends when a player completes Phase 10 or when the draw pile is exhausted. In most card-keeping variants, the goal is to minimize points from the cards still in your hand when your opponent finishes; this is the catch that makes it feel like both a mental workout and a social event.

Two-player play increases the tension because you are one person against one opponent. You see what they are discarding; you deduce what they might be collecting for Phase 7 or 8; and you have to decide whether to block with a skip or to protect your own progress by discarding something your rival can’t use yet.

A quick optional rule you may enjoy: if you find two heavy players in the same living space, you can drop the 2-player minimum rule and play with a “hot potato” variant where you pass a Phase check to your opponent after completing a phase. It’s silly, but it can increase the laughs and the learning.

## Strategy tips for two players

- Card counting and memory: Track what your opponent has not yet used or discarded. If you know they need a particular card to finish a run, you can choose to ditch something they might want—especially if you hold a wild.

- Wilds and skips timing: Wisely use wild cards to complete phases that would otherwise require multiple turns. Reserve skip cards for moments when your opponent is about to complete a phase and needs a few lucky draws.

- Phase management: Don’t rush to complete Phase 1, 2 or 3 if you can safely work toward later phases that give you fewer cards to hold on to. In other words, plan ahead so that you are not stuck with a heavy hand when your opponent gets lucky.

- Discard discipline: You CANNOT control what your opponent draws, but you can influence what they discard by the choices you make. A strategic discard can cut into their potential, but you need to balance this with your own hand management.

- Endgame awareness: The moment you suspect that your opponent is about to finish a phase, you may want to hurry to finish your own by using a running plan that includes a wildcard substitution and a newly formed phase. The pressure can cause mistakes on the other side, which is what you want.
