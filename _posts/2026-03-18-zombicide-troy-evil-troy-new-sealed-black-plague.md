## Unboxing and first impressions

Opening a sealed box is a ritual: the soft crackle of shrink wrap, the slightly damp cardboard aroma, and the hopeful whisper of “maybe they finally included extra tiles.” In the case of Troy & Evil Troy, the packaging sits neatly within the Black Plague bundle, occupying a modest but satisfying chunk of real estate. The card stock feels sturdy, the plastic components are cleanly molded, and the minis – despite coming from a production run designed to flood hobby stores with undead friends – still carry a sense of personal intent. The toppled crown of “new and sealed” is a confident statement: these are not hand-me-downs; these are precise, factory-preserved miniatures for your tabletop theater.

The first thing you’ll notice is the two survivor miniatures themselves: Troy and Evil Troy. Spoiler-lite: they’re visually complementary rather than identical, with distinct gear silhouettes and personality-driven sculpts. The Evil Troy miniature leans into a darker palette and a more defiant stance, a nice nod to the “mirror universe” vibe that many Zombicide fans secretly crave. The Troy figure reads as the classic brave survivor with a practical kit, whereas Evil Troy looks like someone who has borrowed the hero’s knife and then decided the hero wouldn’t need it twice in one dungeon crawl—an archetype that’s oddly relatable when you’re rolling four zombie knives and a handful of bad dice.

As a sealed product, you also get the usual cadre of tokens, cards, and a handful of 3D cardboard chits. The plastic miniatures are clean and free of flash, which isn’t always a given with budget-friendly releases. The cards have that pleasantly tactile feel that makes shuffling feel like a real card game moment rather than a panic-driven sprint for the discard pile. If you’re a gamer who cares about the small things, you’ll appreciate the way the card backs are crisp and the typography is legible even under the bright kitchen-lamp glare.

## What’s in the box

### Miniatures: Troy and Evil Troy

The two named survivors come with base stats, survivor cards, and unique ability lines that differentiate them from the baseline roster. In practice, that means your team might lean more heavily into protection for Troy or a riskier, damage-heavy approach with Evil Troy. The miniature poses are keyboard-clicking adorable: Troy with a sturdy two-handed blade and a shield-ready stance, Evil Troy with a more aggressive, almost anarchic pose that suggests “we’re not waiting for the next rotation” energy. If you like your survivors with strong silhouettes for painting or display, these two will scratch that itch.

From a gameplay perspective, named survivors often bring power spikes or specialized equipment interactions that create dynamic plays—think situational synergy rather than “one-size-fits-all” survivorship. Obviously, your mileage will vary based on your table’s appetite for risk; if you enjoy weaving bold gambits into your round plan, Evil Troy is your go-to muse.

### Cards and tokens

The pack includes a set of survivor cards for Troy and Evil Troy, along with any relevant equipment cards that apply to their kits. The art is punchy and legible, with quick-glance icons that are incredibly helpful when you’re mid-turn and trying to recall a special move without thumbing through reference sheets. The tokens are the familiar Zombicide assortment: clear plastic markers for actions, wound counters, and a handful of objective tokens that influence where you’ll stride and where you’ll park your team to survive the night.

### Base game tiles and components

Since this is a Black Plague crossover, you’ll be integrating with the classic tile system: hex-grid dungeon rooms, outdoor streets, sewers, and burning braziers that may or may not be important depending on the scenario you’re running. The box doesn’t reinvent the wheel; it compounds the experience by adding two more flexible survivors to the armor of your party. The quality of the tile depictions is consistent with the Black Plague line: grim-dusty aesthetic, high-contrast linework, and enough path options to let you improvise a fresh map layout every time you play.

### Packaging and presentation

If you’re a collector, the “new and sealed” angle isn’t just a feature—it’s a value proposition. The set presents well on a shelf next to your Black Plague core box, with a cohesive color language that ties into the existing line. If you’re more of a gamer who opens everything immediately to maximize playtime, you’ll still appreciate how clean the seal is, how easy it is to validate that you’ve got all the components, and how little time it takes to assemble the two minis, assuming you’re not building a diorama with 15 other kits at the same moment.

## Design, quality, and theming

### Sculpts and presentation

The Troy and Evil Troy sculpts sit nicely at the intersection of “heroic stand” and “ready to swing a blade into a swarm.” The detailing on armor plates, fabric folds, and weapon edges is crisp enough that a careful paint job will yield a striking pair of models. If you’re into converting or customizing minis, these two present good canvases without requiring heroic-length prep. The Evil Troy figure, with its darker palette and more aggressive silhouette, offers a satisfying contrast that makes table talk a little more entertaining—especially when you’re debating who’s got the guts to pull off the clutch-turn in a high-stakes scenario.

### Card art and flavor text

The survivor cards lean into narrative flavor with short, punchy lines you can memorize and shout during the heat of play. Flavor text isn’t overly verbose; it’s enough to give you a sense of “these two have a history” without bogging you down in lore. It’s a fine line to walk for a game that’s all about fast-paced, cooperative action, and Guillotine Games generally manages to tread that line with a steady hand. If you’re a fan of story hooks in your board games—without sacrificing play speed—these two do a good job of providing that little nudge toward role-play moments without devolving into a novel-length trap.

### Mechanics and synergy with Black Plague

In Black Plague, you’re fighting zombies with a heroic-but-limited pool of actions each turn. Troy & Evil Troy are designed to slot into this system without requiring you to rework your entire strategy. They bring a handful of bonus actions or special effects that interact with weapons, armor, or the general pool of resources you’re managing. The exact mechanics are, of course, fully compatible with base rules and with most common expansions, meaning you can slip them into an ongoing campaign or a new game night without cognitive whiplash.

If you like to study the math behind your favorite kits, you’ll appreciate that named survivors often have clear, readable stat blocks and a handful of keywords that can drive interesting decisions. The net effect is more “oomph” when you need to save a run, and a nice strategic fallback when your board state looks grim and your options feel limited.

## Gameplay implications on the table

### How Troy & Evil Troy shift the balance

The biggest impact of including Troy & Evil Troy is the opportunity for tactical diversification. If your group tends toward the same approach every time—tirelessly sending the strongest guy into the horde for the fastest kill—these two offer a chance to experiment with different roles and timing. Evil Troy, in particular, can encourage you to push harder earlier in the round, gambling that you’ll weather the later waves with your other survivors intact.

### Pairing with other survivors and expansions

Troy pairs well with reliable front-line troops, while Evil Troy shines when you’re prepared to lean into a high-risk, high-reward moment. In a blended party with other expansions, you’ll likely see some interesting synergies: special items that trigger on critical hits, or cards that reward aggressive positioning. If you’re running a long campaign, these two survivors can become a thematic throughline—one representing the noble heroism of fighting the undead, and the other representing the more chaotic, unpredictable side of surviving a plague-ridden fantasy world.

### Difficulty curve and learning curve

For new players, Troy & Evil Troy provide approachable entry points. They aren’t overwhelming in terms of their gadgets, but they do introduce some new decision points that keep veteran players on their toes. For seasoned players, these two can be a refreshing addition, a chance to re-skin your strategy without altering the core ruleset too dramatically. If you’re running a one-shot or a campaign crossover with other Zombicide lines, the units can slot into a few different teams with minimal confusion.

## Practical considerations for collectors and players

### Sealed condition and value

New and sealed copies are a little time-sensitive in the modern market. Sealed sets can be a must-have for collectors, but if you’re a player on a budget, you may want to weigh the premium against your immediate need for play. The value often scales with demand, which in turn fluctuates with new print runs and reprints. If you’re hunting for Troy & Evil Troy specifically, keep an eye on retailer stock and surprise restocks; you’ll likely see a few price fluctuations as supply tightens or loosens.

### Painting and display potential

Miniatures in this line offer decent value for hobby painters. The sculpts are not hyper-detailed beyond a certain threshold, which makes them forgiving for beginners while still giving room for more advanced techniques like dry brushing and contrast painting. If you’re a tabletop painter who enjoys the satisfaction of a completed model, you’ll likely enjoy taking them to a glossy finish—though you might want to calibrate your expectations to avoid over-committing time if your schedule is busy with other campaigns.

### Value and resale dynamics

As with many collectable board game add-ons, resale value can fluctuate. If the set is still sealed and in demand, you may see modest upside over time; if the box shows signs of wear or you’re looking at a bulky edition with a dozen other expansions, the value proposition shifts. The practical takeaway: if you’re grabbing this as a playable add-on, ensure your budget aligns with your desire for immediate play vs. long-term investment.

## Final thoughts and recommendation

If you’re a fan of Zombicide Black Plague and you’re looking for a tasteful, themed addition that won’t derail your campaign, Troy & Evil Troy deliver a satisfying blend of personality and practical utility. They’re not the kind of add-on that will single-handedly change every plan you’ve got, but they do inject moments of memorable play, especially in scenarios where risk management and positioning matter. The quality of the minis and the clarity of the survivor cards make them accessible to new players while still offering something to discuss for seasoned veterans.

From a purely practical standpoint, if you’re collecting, the sealed copy is a nice pickup that complements the existing Black Plague lineup. If you’re playing, the added layer of choice and variety helps keep sessions fresh, particularly on nights when the undead rotation seems a touch repetitive. And if you’re after a little whimsy in your tabletop fantasy, you’ll enjoy the “two sides of the same coin” narrative that Troy and Evil Troy bring to the table—every time you glance at the board, you’ll smile, roll some dice, and decide whether you’ll be the hero or the anti-hero with a blade.

In short: recommended for collectors who want a cohesive inline addition and players who want a little extra spark in their Black Plague cycles.
