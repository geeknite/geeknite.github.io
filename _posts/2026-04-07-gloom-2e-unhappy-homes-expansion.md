## H2 What the set actually contains

First things first, the box opens with that signature micro-horror vibe: a glossy interior, a slight fear of mold, and a lot of cardboard that promises more heartbreak per square inch than a rom-com’s final act. The Unhappy Homes expansion adds a handful of new components that feel purposefully integrated rather than tacked on like a last-minute patch.

- New room cards: kitchen, parlor, attic, cellar, and a few alternate spaces. Each room has a distinct flavor of penalties and bonuses, often hinging on the inherent risk of being in that room during your family’s misery montage.
- Additional family cards: more legacy-ish families with their own quirks, curses, and a tendency to blame the weather for every bad decision made while dinner was being prepared.
- Household tokens and gloom trackers: the same glassy tension, but now with more opportunities to misplace doom tokens in a way that feels satisfying and excruciating at the same time.
- Scenario sheets: short, punchy narratives that use the new rooms to weave mini-threads of doom through the table—think a haunted lease agreement where every clause optimizes sadness.
- Event and objective cards: new twisty goals that encourage players to orchestrate tragedy in clever ways, which is exactly how your group will justify playing a game about miserable families.

The components keep the core Gloom aesthetic: the translucent cards that layer over each other, letting the gloom glow through like sad neon from a 90s arcade. The art remains evocative, slightly camp, and enough to elicit a genuine “oof” from seasoned players and a delighted, slightly horrified grin from newcomers.

You’ll notice a few design decisions that nod to the 2E refresh while leaning into the thematic pitch of unhappy homes. The room cards emphasize environment as a character, which is an intelligent alignment with the base game’s emphasis on family misfortune as a story engine. The new families don’t just stat-stick; they come with conditional text that triggers if certain rooms are occupied or if specific events happen in a given order. If the base game asked you to orchestrate misfortune, Unhappy Homes asks you to choreograph it with a house tour.

The art direction stays cohesive with the 2E line; there is a crispness to the illustrations that makes the gloom feel tactile rather than purely atmospheric. The house in the box is almost a character in its own right—a setting that breathes and creaks with every turn. We found the translucent tokens a touch brighter than usual, which makes tracking doom across a chaotic round a little easier on the eyes, but also a reminder that this house wants you to notice its tragedy in vivid color.

For those who track production quality, the insert layout remains excellent. The rulebook is a concise upgrade rather than a full rewrite, with a few sidebars for clarifications and a generous heap of examples. The clarity helps prevent the familiar Found-Family-Falls-Flat pitfalls that sometimes show up in expansions: you aren’t spending half the session trying to decipher a rule interaction that feels more like legalese than storytelling.

In the pack, you’ll also find a few little easter eggs for long-time players. A nod to iconic Gloom moments, a couple of back-pocket callbacks, and a room that seems to mock your attempts at calm. It’s playful but not insulting, which is a sweet spot for a game about being haunted by your own choices.

## H2 How it changes the flow of the game

Gloom 2E thrives on simultaneous misery. Players contribute to the gloom of a storyline by attaching family and room cards, creating a web of sorrow that slowly lowers a family’s longevity. Unhappy Homes intensifies that flow by introducing the architectural variables—the rooms themselves—that interact with the families in more nuanced ways.

One of the most notable changes is room-centric synergy. Rooms aren’t just passive locations; they provide active modifiers to the gloom track and to the condition of the families in a way that encourages players to plan not just for the next turn, but for the next few turns. This adds a light strategic layer without breaking the cooperative pacing. You’ll find yourself saying, a bit theatrically, “If we nudge this family into the attic before the cellar triggers, we can end this chain with a dramatic reveal in the parlor.” Yes, it’s as melodramatic as it sounds, and that’s exactly the temperature you want for a Gloom evening.

The new scenario sheets are a welcome addition here. They present micro-narratives that thread through the rooms, forcing players to think about sequence and timing. The temptation to chase a single dramatic reveal is still there, but you’ll also encounter smaller, cleaner goals that keep the game moving even when a round feels like a slow tragedy. If you have a group that enjoys the meta-narrative aspect of Gloom, the Unhappy Homes scenarios offer a satisfying layer of structure without rigidly dictating the story path.

Rules-wise, expect a modest learning curve. The base mechanics remain intact: you pull gloom tokens, attach cards, and watch the family’s fate warp under the weight of your choices. The new cards add a handful of edge cases, but none of them feel forced. The designers have done a good job of weaving these additions into the fabric of the game rather than creating an entirely separate subsystem. In practice, you’ll notice that the game flows just a tad differently from a bare base game or from a few other expansions: there’s more attention paid to the spatial layout and to the emotional states triggered by specific rooms.

For players who prefer storytelling angles, the expansion provides richer texture. The rooms and families interact in ways that invite players to craft small micro-arcs within the larger tragedy. The attic might become a stage for a family’s hidden secret, the kitchen a hotspot for a betrayal that compounds the gloom. It’s not a replacement for narrative play, but it is a generosity of framework that helps players co-create a more layered tableau.

There are a few potential caveats. The added complexity can feel a touch overwhelming on a first run, especially if your group is still acclimating to the two-level card system of Gloom 2E. We recommend an initial four-player or fewer session with the expansion to gently introduce the new room text and the new objective cards. If you’re bringing in a mixed group of newbies and veterans, start with a house tour scenario and ease into the more intricate combinations. As with any Gloom product, there is a balance to be struck between narrative ambition and the pace of the game. Unhappy Homes tilts toward the former—your mileage will vary depending on how story-first your table likes to be.

To give you a practical sense of how a session unfolds, here is a condensed play flow with the expansion in play:

- Setup: players lay out the new room cards, reveal starter families, and shuffle the new scenario sheets.
- Phase one: players draft or place rooms to set up the initial gloom ranges. The rooms begin to influence gloom in a subtle, almost flirtatious way.
- Phase two: players attach families to rooms, triggering room-based modifiers and the first wave of emotional consequences.
- Phase three: players execute their turns, adding cards, flipping over event cards, and watching the house columns tilt toward a somber crescendo.
- Endgame: the final round comes quickly if players have executed their plan with ruthless charm; if not, the house eases into a long, creeping decline that still manages to deliver a satisfying ending.

## H2 Design and components quality

The production value remains consistent with the 2E line. The cards are of the familiar translucency, which means you’ll partially see the glooming eyes of the families beneath the text—an aesthetic both delightful and tormenting. The room cards sport decorative borders and moody color schemes that capture the haunting ambiance of a home that has clearly seen better days. The new family cards carry a similar level of character-driven flavor, with little textual prompts that encourage players to lean into the melodrama rather than shy away from it.

One of the nicest touches is how the game preserves accessibility. The new rules are not a wall of text. They are cleanly written, with simple examples that prevent the dreaded RTFM (Read The Fine Manual) moment. The quick-reference sheets are well designed, which means you can stay in the mood rather than flipping between pages to confirm whether a certain room triggers in the late phase or after a specific card is revealed.

In terms of component quality, the Unhappy Homes box is a strong value proposition. You’re getting a handful of cards that add depth without inflating the game’s footprint. The box is not absurdly heavy; it’s portable enough to bring to a casual game night or a slightly long brunch where everyone pretends they are not being emotionally abused by their in-laws. If you are compiling a Gloom library for your club, this expansion slots in neatly without requiring a full reorganization of your storage setup.

## H2 Thematic fit and tone

Gloom has always excelled at balancing a high concept premise with a low-key humor that doesn’t break the mood. Unhappy Homes leans into the “haunted house as character” idea with gusto. It’s not just about making families sadder; it’s about injecting environment-driven drama into the storytelling engine. The humor remains intact, but it’s a bolder, more wry humor this time, like a black velvet cloak draped over a chandelier that loudly sighs when someone trips on a rug.

If your group tends to treat Gloom as a theatrical performance rather than a strategy game, Unhappy Homes will be a natural extension. The rooms offer mini-scenes to stage and improv-worthy prompts to react to. The expansion rewards groups who lean into roleplay without turning the table into a stage for cliches. You will still feel the tug of those tragic stories, but now you can steer the drama through the house’s architecture rather than just the raw emotional consequences of each family’s fate.

From a theme perspective, the expansion is well calibrated. It respects the core premise of Gloom while offering a fresh substrate to mine for dramatic reveals. It’s not a radical departure; it’s a creative augmentation. If you are someone who likes their gloom with a side of character-driven architecture, Unhappy Homes will feel like a natural evolution rather than an awkward detour.

## H2 How to play with Unhappy Homes in your rotation

If you are upgrading mid-shelf, you can integrate Unhappy Homes with minimal disruption. Here are practical tips for bringing the expansion into your sessions:

- Start simple: begin with a baseline setup using two new room cards and one scenario sheet. This way you can gauge how the rooms influence early rounds before layering in all the additional complexities.
- Dial in the room order: place rooms with cool synergy first. Some rooms amplify gloom when paired with specific families; others create tension when placed near certain events. A few thoughtful placements can yield dramatic sequences without overwhelming players.
- Tempo management: because rooms drive new interactions, you’ll want to pace introductions across a couple of rounds. Don’t dump the entire expansion on your players in one go unless your group thrives on heavy crunch.
- Story emphasis: encourage players to narrate their plans. A quick round of mini-impromptu dialog around why the attic needs to be the stage for the next reveal can heighten the mood and reduce analysis paralysis.
- Use post-game reflections: after a session, have players share their favorite room moment or the one-liner that captured a family’s misery. It’s not just fluff; it helps anchor the expansions’ narrative threads and builds a library of memorable moments for future plays.

## H2 Solo and cooperative play viability

Gloom has historically been a social, drafting-driven puzzle. Unhappy Homes doesn’t change the core co-op/competitive tension, but it does influence solo viability in interesting ways. Playing solo with the expansion is perfectly doable, though it leans into the “build a haunted house vignette” mode rather than strict challenge modes. If you enjoy running through a compact tragedy arc, you’ll find the rooms a good foil for your own storytelling impulses.

What works well in solo is the tight feedback loop. You can watch a single character’s gloom unravel in real time as you adjust rooms and events. The removal of some complexity that type of session would otherwise require lets you stay in the mood while not sacrificing the narrative payoff. If you prefer cooperative sessions, the expansion offers a fresh canvas for teamwork as players brainstorm room placements, focus on different families, and chase synchronized reveals that hit near the end of a round. It’s less about competition and more about orchestrating a shared catharsis, which is Gloom’s sweet spot.

## H2 Word on replayability and value

Replayability is where expansions earn or burn their keep. Unhappy Homes adds enough variety through its array of rooms and scenarios to justify multiple play sessions. The scenarios aren’t so long that you’ll feel stuck in a repetitive loop; they’re bite-sized, designed to slot into a single session rather than demand a marathon campaign. If your group tends to finish a base game in about 60 to 90 minutes, you’ll still be comfortably within that window with the expansion; it simply won’t end as quickly if you chase a multi-room, multi-family crescendo.

From a value perspective, the expansion is reasonably priced given the incremental depth it offers. It doesn’t feel like a cash grab nor a half-hearted add-on. It respects the time you’ve already invested in Gloom 2E and returns a thoughtful layer of design that adds meaningful choice rather than cosmetic fluff. If you’re a compulsive collector who wants every variation of gloom you can get, this is a natural addition to your shelf. If you prefer lean experiences, you can still enjoy Unhappy Homes as a supplementary kit that elevates the mood without forcing a re-rotation of your game night schedule.

How does Unhappy Homes stack up against the base game and some other expansions in the Gloom 2E line? Here are a few quick takeaways:

- With the base game, you rely heavily on drawn cards and the interplay of families. Unhappy Homes adds a layer of spatial logic—the rooms act as catalysts and plot devices—without replacing the core speed or the baseline chaos. It feels like a natural extension rather than a replacement for your usual table routine.

You’ll enjoy the added texture without feeling like you need an encyclopedia to manage it. It’s accessible without being trivial, which is a sweet spot for most gaming groups.
- The tone difference matters. Some expansions go for outright grim or tongue-in-cheek camp. Unhappy Homes sits squarely in the clever, slightly glamorized horror zone that Gloom has always inhabited. It’s not nauseatingly bleak; it’s more like a stylishly melancholic stage play where the props occasionally bite back.

## H2 Final verdict and rating snapshot

What works well in Unhappy Homes is precisely what makes Gloom 2E a joy to revisit: the ability to tilt a narrative session toward a new emotional resonance without losing what makes the base game engaging. The room interactions give you a tangible set of levers to pull, which enriches strategy and storytelling in equal measure. The art remains evocative, the components are solid, and the balance between narrative weight and game pace is thoughtfully tuned.

That said, the expansion is not a universal upgrade. If your table dislikes heavy narrative control, or if you prefer a tighter, faster misery motif, you may find the extra rooms slightly slows the tempo. It’s a trade-off: more depth and drama, or more speed and simplicity. The best fit is a group that embraces storytelling, enjoys puzzle-like planning, and wants a fresh setting for their Gloom experiments.

In terms of a rating, if the base game sits at a sturdy 8.5 out of 10 for a good night of gloom, Unhappy Homes lands at about 9.0 for players who value narrative architecture and room-driven tactics. It’s not a revolution, but it’s the kind of expansion that makes you reconsider how many tragedies you want to unpack in a single evening. For groups that love the theatrical horror of a haunted household, this is a strong addition that keeps the mood, adds depth, and leaves you smiling in a slightly exhausted, genuinely entertained way.
