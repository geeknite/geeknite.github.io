## Overview
City of Mist returns with its signature blend of modern noir and myth, this time sprinting toward a collision between a city that never sleeps and the gods that keep overreaching their leases. The Fall of the Gods is a campaign described by the authors as a keystone arc that tests the boundaries between mortal cunning and divine tantrums. In this outing, players track down the fractures in the city’s mythic layers as deities go wandering off the rails, leaving a trail of prophecies, graffiti, and probably a bus route that never ends.

![Fall of the Gods cover](assets/images/city-of-mist-fall-of-the-gods.jpg)

The campaign promises a tight mix of investigation, identity drama, and large scale stakes. It is not just about smashing the gods with a better plan; it is about listening to the city itself, which sometimes talks through street artists, streetlights, and a barista who claims to have brewed the first cup of coffee that could wake a god.

In practice, players embody Mist folk, iconic archetypes drawn from myth and legend, who must balance their own personal claims with the city’s needs. The Fall of the Gods envisions a structure where each act unspools a different facet of power, from sexual politics among deities to the ethical questions of using mythic force in a city that thrives on anonymity. It is equal parts epic and intimate, a feature that City of Mist carries well when the GM can balance scale without losing character focus.

## Campaign structure and arc layout
The Fall of the Gods uses a three act arc with optional epilogue and a handful of micro-plots that pop up as the city breathes. Act One sets up the mythic tension, giving players a taste of the divine unrest without drowning them in cosmic scale. Act Two intensifies the conflict as factions align or fracture, revealing hidden pacts between gangs, gangs of angels, and a council of merchants who have learned to speak in neon. Act Three is where the gods finally collide with the mortal world, and the players decide what morality means when the gods start to forget their lines.

The chapters are modular, with keyed scenes that can be swapped in or out depending on your table’s appetite for drama. If your group loves a heist, you can double down on the mid-campaign caper; if you prefer a conspiracy thriller, lean into the undercity politics and the ritualistic hints that thread through the clues. The design philosophy here is to give the GM sliding scale options: quick one-shot chapters for a shorter run, or longer sessions that let the city breathe and the gods sulk in their penthouse suites.

The campaign uses a mix of investigation, social conflict, and empowered action scenes. Expect a balance between talking to suspects in a bar that smells like burnt espresso and chasing a god through a subway tunnel that hums with ritual energy. The book provides ready-made scenes that feel like mini games: a social combat in a parliament of sprites, a chase through a carnival that never closes, and a ritual that requires players to gather three ingredients that are suspiciously close to three of their own secrets.

## Playstyle and tone
If you enjoy the mood of a late night alleyway where things are too quiet to feel safe, this campaign is for you. The Fall of the Gods leans into the old city myth, where gods are not heroic and certainly not reliable, and mortals become the true agents of change by choosing what to praise and what to weaponize. The tone can swing from wry humor to grave prophecy in a single scene; your players will pepper the city with myths of their own making, and the GM should be ready to treat every claim as a potential trigger for a new clue, a new alliance, or a new debt that someone will expect you to pay later.

The book instructs the GM to lean into the audience’s favorite genres: urban fantasy, caper noir, mythic tragedy, and political thrillers. It is in that blend that the campaign shines: you get to feel like you are running a godly soap opera, but the soap is made of coffee, neon, and a lot of questionable moral compromises.

## Mechanic notes and how it plays in this campaign
City of Mist uses tags to determine what your Mist folk can do in any given scene. You roll 2d6 for action and doctor the results with your character's power tags, which represent a mix of mythic identity and modern expertise. The Fall of the Gods is good at giving you a wide spread of tags to lean on, from the heroic to the suspicious to the utterly mundane. The players will often reveal a goddess-tier ability in the same sentence where they reveal that they own a food truck and the truck runs on enchanted lattes.

This campaign benefits from a GM who knows when to lean into the mythic elements without letting reality break. The best sessions occur when players treat the city as a living, breathing thing that can be coaxed into behaving with the right combination of threat, humor, and respect. The book provides a strong set of scenes and prompts that help you build that sense of a living city whose undercurrents carry the weight of a pantheon.

To help you plan, we provide a few quick notes on how to handle the different modes of play:

- Investigation mode: Use red herrings that tie to mythic imagery. Make clues feel like they are part of the city furniture rather than isolated plot points.
- Social combat: Expect rival claims to come with a price. Debates feel like duels when people wield memory, rumor, and the weight of an old oath.
- Action scenes: Use godlike scale sparingly. The fall of a pantheon should be felt in the city’s mood first, then in the streets second.

## GM prep and session flow
Prep for The Fall of the Gods benefits from a two-layer approach: macro planning and micro scene prep. Macro planning means mapping the campaign arcs, the status of the gods, and the factions that pull strings in the city. Micro scene prep means creating a few anchor moments that your group will revisit. The campaign is forgiving in that it invites improvisation, but it shines when you give players a few nonnegotiables: the mythic theme of the act, a moral choice with consequences, and a clue that nudges them toward a major logical decision.

We recommend a simple session flow:

1) Open with a scene that reveals a city fault line—where the gods used to be and where they are trying to hide.
2) Scatter clues that reference the gods’ behavior in human terms: a ritual performed in a gallery, a statue that leaks memory, a song that triggers a specific memory.
3) Introduce a tension point with a non-player character who embodies a conflicting claim about the gods and their role in the city.
4) End with a decision point that rewrites the players' relationship to power, and plant a clue that hints at the act’s next milestone.

### Macro planning
Macro planning is where you sketch the three act scaffold, identify the major divine players, and map the city’s power lines. Keep a visible big-picture diagram so you can flip a page and say to the table ah yes the river gods are up to something and the street poet knows the password. This is the backbone that keeps the campaign cohesive when the players go wandering into side quests.

### Micro scene prep
Micro prep is where you draft a handful of anchor moments for each act. These are the scenes that anchor players and give you clear calls to action. Prepare a couple of backup scenes that align with the players’ evolving Claims so you can pivot without losing momentum.

### Session flow quick checklist
- Start strong with a mythic echo in a familiar locale
- Drop a clue that pushes a personal Claim into the foreground
- Introduce a conflicting ally or rival with a stake in the divine drama
- End with a choice that will ripple into the next session

## Player experience and pacing
This campaign is best run with a group that enjoys both wit and weight. The players will have moments where they lean into their character’s mythic identity and times where they step back, choose not to reveal everything, and let the city itself become a character with striking screen presence. Pacing wise, plan for three-to-four hours per session for a good chunk of a nine to twelve session arc. If you want longer arcs, the acts can bleed together in a single long day, which can be a weirdly satisfying experience.

A typical session might look like this:

- Opening scene in a location that has a mythic echo: a cafe that sells more than coffee; a park bench that stores memories of gods.
- Investigation beats: talk to a courier who stores clues in a microfilm scroll; read a graffiti tag that reveals a past oath.
- Social clash: a negotiation with a pantheon surrogate who wants to claim the city for a price both sides would rather not name.
- Climax: a ritual that could either awaken a god or bury the pantheon in debt to the city.

The Fall of the Gods does a good job of balancing drama and humor. It recognizes that players are going to laugh at some of the more over-the-top claims and that the tension will rise when the gods start to reveal their fragility.

## Artwork, handouts, and table ambiance
The campaign includes a set of visual references that help players picture the city and its mythic layers. The art is atmospheric rather than photoreal, and the designers lean into the sense that myth exists in the cracks of everyday life. There are ready-made props and 'mythic agenda cards' that players can use to claim aspects of the city in the mist. If your table enjoys a little tabletop theatre, these cards will play especially well.

We strongly recommend a little tabletop theatre: dim lighting, coin-sized tokens, a small soundscape that hints at neon rain, and a good playlist with tracks that blend synth and choir. The mood matters as much as the mechanics, and this campaign rewards players who lean into it.

## Potential pitfalls and how to avoid them
No campaign is perfect. The Fall of the Gods can drift into two common traps if you let it:

- Too grand, not enough grounding: The gods can become abstract and the city can seem to vanish. Ground the campaign with concrete street-level stakes that matter to the players on a personal level, not just the pantheon’s reputation.
- Too moralizing: Gods falling should not automatically equal a shoehorned morality lecture. Keep the player characters actively choosing, with moral gray areas that force them to confront consequences that touch their own Claims.

A few tricks to avoid these issues:

- Use recurring motifs: a symbol or symbol set that reappears across acts to show how the city reacts to shifts in power.
- Introduce a ticking clock that relates to a god’s something—an oath, a debt, a ritual—so players feel a sense of urgency.
- Give players a personal stake: a Mage of Mist who has a family or a business intimately tied to the gods’ rules.

If you are worried about balance, lean on our experience from other City of Mist campaigns. We found that the best sessions emerge when players feel like they can influence the city rather than watching a divine soap opera unfold.

## Final thoughts and recommendation
The Fall of the Gods is a standout entry in the City of Mist line for a number of reasons. It nails the tone of noir myth, offers modular play that scales up or down, and gives the GM enough to run a full arc without needing to invent a ton of props from scratch. The campaign rewards players who lean into mythic identity and allows GMs to craft a city that feels simultaneously alive and morally ambiguous.

That said, it is not a light weekend one-shot. If you’re not ready to lean into a three to six session arc with multiple NPCs, you may want to start with a shorter run or a single run that centers on a single god’s arc rather than the entire pantheon. For veteran City of Mist tables, it is an excellent way to push the bounds of what the system can do and to explore how power, lore, and moral choice intertwine.

For players who enjoy roleplay and the long term build of a city’s relationship to its gods, this campaign should be right in your wheelhouse. If you prefer straightforward dungeon crawls with a clear wins condition, this is not your lane. This is a campaign about what power means in a city where myths walk in the open and people have learned to see through the fog that the city uses to hide its true face.
