## What is UNO Flip, and Why Does It Need a Hero Makeover?
UNO Flip is the flavor-punch variant of the beloved UNO formula. It introduces two sides to the deck: a Light (bright) side and a Dark (flip) side. The Dark side is where the humor happens: colors swap, action cards switch roles, and strategic flips create chaos that would make even All Might raise an eyebrow. The core idea remains simple: discard cards by color or number, but when someone says “UNO,” you might just flip the entire deck to a new world where everything you know about the color wheel is suspiciously wrong.

The Canada-exclusive MHA edition builds on that premise by overlaying anime-nerd vibes onto each card. Hero names, quirks-inspired text, and action-cards that feel like quirk activations make the deck feel less like a standard knockoff and more like a mini-episode where your friends become side characters in Deku’s class. The result is a package that keeps UNO’s accessibility while injecting a strong layup for fans of the series.

## How to Play: UNO Flip Meets UA Academy
If you already know UNO Flip, you’re halfway there. If you don’t, here’s the quick refresher: the game uses a two-sided deck. On the Light side, you match colors and numbers like classic UNO. When someone calls “Flip,” the entire pile is flipped, and players must navigate through the Dark side, where special action cards and reversed color logic kick in. The Canada-exclusive MHA edition keeps this core intact while weaving in hero-themed twists.

Key details in the MHA edition:
- Hero-Quirk Action Cards: Instead of generic action text, certain cards reference hero abilities—think one-for-all power surges that let you reverse the turn order or force opponents to draw extra cards in a fun, thematic way.

These moments create opportunities for dramatic comebacks, especially when you’re down to your last few cards and Deku looks you in the eyes via a small art panel.
- Limitations and Rules: The deck retains the standard UNO rules, with the added layer of the two-sided flip and the hero-vs-villain flavor text. The quick-start guide is surprisingly readable, which is essential for a product aimed at families and fan groups who want to jump in without a six-page glossary.

A couple of gameplay notes:
