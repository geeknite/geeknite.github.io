## Maintenance and Cleaning: Keeping the Bloom Bright
Cleaning a rug this size is not something you want to treat as an annual event. Daily life means daily maintenance, albeit light. Start with a good routine: vacuum weekly, in multiple directions to lift the pile and prevent compaction. If you have pets, you’ll want to be prepared for the occasional “I spilled something that looks suspiciously like cocoa” moment. For spills, blot—do not rub—with a clean cloth, use a mild cleaner appropriate for synthetic fibers, and test in an inconspicuous area first. The goal is to lift the stain without saturating the backing. If you discover a stubborn stain, you may need to repeat the blotting process or consult a professional cleaner who handles large-area rugs. 

Rotation is also a surprisingly effective technique. Rotating your rug every few months helps even the wear across the field, ensuring the floral pattern doesn’t develop a “high-traffic wear line” that makes your guests think you’ve got a favorite spot on the floor. When vacuuming, avoid the edge near the rug’s perimeter where fraying can begin if you’re not careful. A gentler setting near the edges helps preserve the finish and keep the floral edges crisp for longer. A good rug pad underneath matters here: it reduces slipping, provides extra cushioning, and protects the rug’s underside from dirt and moisture. If you’re unsure which pad to pick, our guide on selecting rug pads has you covered: {% post_url 'rug-pads-101' %}.

For deep cleaning, mild soap and water can be used on many synthetic fibers, but always check the manufacturer’s care instructions first. If the rug has experienced heavy staining or noticeable odor, you might want to consider professional cleaning services. Large-area rugs can be tricky, but with patience and a little elbow grease, you can keep the bloom looking fresh rather than wilting under the sun like a plot twist in a soap opera.
