## Gameplay and Mechanics
UNO is a deceptively simple game: match color or number, play an action card to disrupt your friends' plans, and be the first to shed all your cards. The 2004 edition keeps that DNA intact while letting you play with a few legacy quirks that make it feel both familiar and slightly novel.

### Basic Rules Recap
- Each player starts with seven cards. The rest sit in a draw pile. The top card flips up to begin the discard pile.
- On your turn, you must play a card that matches either color or number. If you can’t, draw one card. If you draw and can play, you may do so in the same turn in most house rules—though some players stick to strict turn-based flow.
- Special cards:
  - Skip makes the next player lose a turn.
  - Reverse flips play direction.
  - Draw Two forces the next player to draw two cards and lose their turn.
  - Wild lets you choose the next color.
  - Wild Draw Four adds to the chaos by allowing the next player to draw four, with the pesky caveat about when you can legally play it (the I have no other card that matches loophole).
- You must shout Uno when you have one card left. Failure to declare Uno leads to a penalty in most contexts: you draw two cards or you suffer a humorous social penalty, depending on your group’s level of melodrama.

### House Rules and Variants
That 04 edition plays nicely with a variety of house rules. Some groups add Uno Safe Mode, where you must say Uno before your last card is playable rather than after you reduce to one. Others prefer a strict no drawing to pass window, making the Draw Two/Draw Four cards more punishing and strategic. A few folks lean into a starter flip rule to ensure the first card sets the pace—nothing like a Wild Draw Four appearing on the first flip to establish your table’s mood for the night.

That era's UNO feels a bit more core to the social experience: you’re not playing a digital version; you’re playing with actual people, an actual table, and the actual possibility of someone knocking a glass of soda into a stack of 108 colored debts.

## How to Play Quickstart (For the Impatient)
- Step 1: Open the sealed box and confirm you actually have 108 cards. Count quickly, your guests will admire your math skills while you pretend to be a calm ringmaster.
- Step 2: Shuffle, deal seven cards to each player, and flip the top card to start the discard pile.
- Step 3: On your turn, play a card that matches color or number, or draw one if you can’t. If you draw and can play, follow your impulse to do so—judgment calls exist in the wild beyond the rules.
- Step 4: When you have one card left, shout Uno. If you forget, your chosen penalty will likely be mealtime adventures or a request to perform an improvised song about color matching.
- Step 5: If you’re forced to draw four or two, remember your audience: they’ll likely revel in your misfortune and claim victory in a way that makes you question your life choices.
- Step 6: The winner is the first player to discard all their cards. The applause from the table will be present if you’ve found a way to time your Wild Draw Four just before someone else’s last move.
