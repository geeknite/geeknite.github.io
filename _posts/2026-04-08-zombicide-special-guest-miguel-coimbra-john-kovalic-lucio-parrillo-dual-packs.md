## Meet the guest artists and what they bring to the table
### Miguel Coimbra
Miguel Coimbra is known for bold contrasts, heavy shading, and a flair for undead characters that feel both menacing and stylish. In these packs, Coimbra’s influence usually surfaces in two character archetypes: survivors who look like they stepped straight out of a street mural and zombies with exaggerated sinew and theatrical poses. The color choices often skew deep red, cobalt, and rusty metal tones, giving a cinematic edge that reads well on the tabletop from across the room.

### John Kovalic
John Kovalic brings a different energy entirely. Think playful chaos, visual gags, and a cartoonish sensitivity that softens horror with humor. In the dual packs, Kovalic’s impact is most obvious in survivor cards that lean into witty lines and poses that suggest the survivors are comic book heroes more than grim mercenaries. The zombies carry a sneaky wink too, with cartoonish features that still register as dangerous threats when the lights go out. If you love the idea of a zombie apocalypse with a smile, Kovalic’s touch is the reassuring punchline you didn’t know you needed.

### Lucio Parrillo
Lucio Parrillo brings the dark fantasy vibe that makes you feel the dungeon creeping under your feet. Parrillo’s lines are dense, with metallic highlights, leathery textures, and a Gothic atmosphere that makes every piece feel like a character in a pulp novel. For Zombicide, Parrillo packs in survivors that look like they’ve walked off a fantasy cover and zombies that resemble cursed knights and corrupted beasts. The palette tends toward deep purples, blackened golds, and bone-white details that pop against the green, brown, and grey plains of the board.

## The art meets the table: practical tips for painting and display
For hobbyists, these six dual packs are a feast. Here are practical tips to get the most out of them:

- Start with a color plan: choose a palette inspired by the art pair for each pack. This helps unify the survivors and zombies across the two figures in a squad and makes the display pop once you clamp the miniatures to their bases.
- Use contrast layers: the heavy shading of Coimbra works beautifully with the accent colors from Parrillo. A few thin layers of complementary tones can elevate textures such as leather, metal, and bone onto the tabletop.
- Baseline basing: keep the bases consistent, but add small thematic touches that nod to the pack’s mood. For example, Pack B can lean into Gothic stone textures, while Pack A can use urban grit with graffiti textures.
- Display goals: plan a dedicated shelf or display board for each pack. The goal here is not to hide the miniatures after a game night; it is to create a visual story you can show off at meetups or conventions.
- Playability first, artistry second: you want your game to run smoothly. When you assemble the dual packs, ensure the new components don’t obstruct board setup or cause confusion in the turn order. A quick stapled cheat sheet can help you remember the new abilities without rifling through the rulebook mid-session.

If you are new to painting or just want a quick win, these packs offer an approachable entry point. The sculpting is detailed enough to be satisfying but not so delicate that you’ll dread touching the brush. Even if your color theory is questionable, a bit of shading here and a dry brush there will yield results that look impressive in photos and even more so in person.
