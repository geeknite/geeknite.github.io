## Painting and assembly tips
If you are into painting, this set is a goldmine. The sculpt variety gives you the chance to experiment with textures: bone, leather, cloth, metal, and gore—each requires slightly different techniques. Here are a few practical tips that can turn your table into a museum of decayed art:

- Start with a zen base coat. A neutral gray for the walkers ensures your later washes pop without becoming muddy. 
- Use a wash to bring out the crevices. A dark brown or black wash can define bones and tears without making the piece look muddy. 
- Dry brushing is your friend for texture contrast. A light gray or off-white on raised surfaces will highlight the details and make the sculpt pop in dim game nights. 
- For the Abomination, start with a darker base and gradually build up using red and green washes to create the sense of rot and corruption. It matters because this model is the focal point of the final act. 
- Display is optional but recommended. If you have a dedicated display shelf, a few walkers in perfect condition can be used as a miniature gallery of your hobby. If not, the dining room table doubles nicely as a center stage for your next board game session.

If you want a quick paint-by-numbers style guide, we have a straightforward approach that works for beginners and veterans alike: a simple base coat, a mid-tone wash to bring texture, and a couple of dry-brush highlights. For a cleaner look on the Abomination, consider a splotchy green wash to simulate rot, then a red wash in the creases to evoke raw wounds. You will be surprised at how quickly a good set of paints can transform your table presence, turning what could be a messy battlefield into a moody, cinematic moment.
