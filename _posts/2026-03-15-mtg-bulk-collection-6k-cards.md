## Condition, sorting, and storage
The cards come in varying states. You’ll see sleeves that show wear, border prints that have seen better days, and foil edges that still glimmer despite minor nicks. The sorting challenge is real: sort by foil vs non-foil, then by rarity, then by color and set to create a manageable library. Start by separating foils from non-foils, to give you a sense of your foil portfolio at a glance. Then divide rares/mythics from the rest. For the rest, you can split into uncommons, commons, and basic lands. If you’re not sure about a card, put it aside to revisit after the initial pass. The goal is to create a stable, repeatable workflow that you can repeat once you’ve got the rhythm.

Storage is the other half of the equation. Sleeves are your best friend; a two-tiered approach works well: premium sleeves for the mains (mythics/rares/foil bangers) and standard sleeves for the rest. Bind a complete collection in a ring-bound binder or use a set-based binder to make retrieval simpler. If you’re building a cube, create a dedicated section for cube-friendly commons and uncommons, sorted by color and mana curve. If you’re planning to sell or trade, you’ll want a separate listing of cards by set, foil status, and condition. It’s a lot, but the payoffs in time saved later are enormous.

## Tools, setup, and workflow
To maximize your time, you’ll want a small toolkit, including:
- A stack of boxes or bins labeled by category (foil, rare, common, land, etc.)
- A color-coded organizer for quick color sorting
- Protective sleeves and deck boxes
- A robust but portable workspace for sorting
- A good playlist or podcast to keep you entertained while the bulk hums in the background

For sleeves and binders, consider a tiered approach: premium sleeves for your best cards, standard sleeves for everything else, and a binder dedicated to your most nostalgic sets. The workflow is simple: separate foils; separate rares; separate mythics; put everything else into a general pool; go back later to river-weed the rest into purgatory or storage.

## Sorting strategy: how to get through 6,000 cards without dying
- Step 1: Create a staging area. Clear a workspace and lay down a mat; this helps to avoid misplacing cards when you’re moving quickly.
- Step 2: Initial pass. Sort by foil vs non-foil, then identify any misprints or special edges. These can fetch attention later.
- Step 3: Rarity pass. Separate rares, mythics, and foil-rare variants; place them into their own labeled bins.
- Step 4: Color pass. Separate cards into color groups. You’ll be surprised by how many duplicates you have in certain colors.
- Step 5: Set pass. If you know your sets, you can group by set to organize for trade or storage. If you don’t, you can still build a general catalog in your phone or a binder.
- Step 6: Quality check. Look for miscuts, edge wear, binder-friendly corners. Decide how to handle borderline items.
- Step 7: Sleeving and labeling. Sleeve the top cards from each category; label each binder or box with color and set info.

This sorting approach is designed to be repeatable. The goal is consistency; if you can sort and store the first batch, you can do it again with the remaining mountain.
