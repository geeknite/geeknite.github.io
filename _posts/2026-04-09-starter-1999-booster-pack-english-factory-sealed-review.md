Opening a 1999 Starter pack is less about the most valuable card you might pull and more about the sensory memory it taps into. You’ll hear the crisp crack of the shrink wrap if you ever manage to open it without tearing it; you’ll smell the faint printer ink that time forgot; and you’ll feel the card stock—slightly glossy, perhaps with a whisper of chalk on the edges, a reminder that printing standards have evolved. If you’re lucky enough to get a rare or two, that buzz is real—the same buzz that made opening 1990s boosters feel like an archaeological dig in your own living room. ## Practical guidance for buyers and collectors
### Authentication checklist
- Inspect the outer shrink and seam for uniformity. Any fraying, inconsistent thickness, or excessive bowing could indicate resealing.
- Compare photos against known authentic references from credible sellers. Look for consistent color tones and packaging labels that match the late-1990s design language.
- Ask for a provenance story: where did the pack come from, how long has it been stored, and can the seller provide high-resolution photos of the wrap under light.
- If possible, request a short video showing the shrink wrap’s edges and how the pack sits on a flat surface. A pre-owned display piece in mint condition is often worth more with video confirmation.

### Preservation practices
- Store away from direct sunlight; UV light fades card faces and wrapper colors.
- Maintain a stable temperature and humidity (roughly 50-60% RH is a comfortable baseline for many paper goods).
- Use acid-free storage boxes or bags. A Mylar sleeve for the pack’s exterior can help guard against minor abrasions during display.
- Keep a log of when you acquired the item and any notable changes in its packaging state. It’s a nerdy but useful habit for collectors who obsess over provenance.

### Display ideas
If you’ve got a sealed Starter 1999 pack, consider a display case that protects while you celebrate the memory. A shallow, glass-front case lets you admire the wrapper’s aesthetic without snapping the seal. If you want to be a bit cheekier, you can line the inside of the case with a retro background (think neon geometric patterns or a scanner-scan of the old MTG logo) to evoke the era while keeping the pack safe.
