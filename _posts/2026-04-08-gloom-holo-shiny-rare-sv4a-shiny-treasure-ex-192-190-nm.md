This particular listing notes the print as ex 192/190 NM. The numbers 192 and 190 usually belong to a subset that has taken a few detours around the printer, which in collector land translates to a potential misprint or a small deck of secrets that might please the oddball completists among us. NM, or Near Mint, suggests a card that has seen light play but not much wear. The condition matters more than you might expect in a holo card, because the holo surface can reveal micro-scratches that simply do not show up on matte backgrounds. If you are buying with a keen eye toward grading, a NM label for a shiny non misprint aura can still fetch a premium, while a few faint scratches may drop the value by a few dollars or by a larger margin if the market is hot for SV4a variants.

The practical takeaway here is this: for a Gloom holo from SV4a with a fancy Shiny Treasure ex label, you want the holo surface to be clean, the card to lie flat, and the edges to show minimal whitening. A well preserved 192/190 NM card can be a centerpiece for a Gloom themed deck or a stand alone curiosity that begs to be shown off during casual play or online showcases. In short, condition matters, but the charm of SV4a holo shines through even with minor imperfections. If you are new to this game, think of NM as a trustworthy friend who kept the card safe, not a pristine deity that has never seen sunlight.

## Practical considerations for storage and display
A card like Gloom SV4a Shiny Treasure ex deserves more than a shoebox. Storage considerations vary by collector, but the common sense approach is to keep it away from direct sunlight to preserve the holo shine and to avoid extreme humidity that can cause warping in the sleeved pages. If you use top loaded protectors for presentation or display, ensure your sleeves are archival-friendly and acid-free to minimize any long term wear. It is also wise to put a magnetic or clamped binding in your binder to prevent the card from shifting during transport. In other words, treat it as a delicate artifact rather than a disposable trading commodity, because the glow has a memory and the memory likes to be kept pristine.

When you plan trades or purchases, consider the condition label of NM and the potential presence of micro-scratches on the holo surface. There is a subtle difference between cards that are truly NM and those that just look NM at first glance under a poor light. A quick check under a bright lamp with a consistent angle can reveal faint imperfections that would otherwise escape the casual eye. A buyer may want to request photos from different angles or even request a small sample video to confirm the gloss and the edge wear. This is especially important for SV4a variants that have a coveted Shiny pattern; a small misalignment of the holo can be a deal breaker or turn a potential upgrade into a patchwork of disappointment. The bottom line: invest a little extra time to verify condition; the long term payoff is worth the effort.
