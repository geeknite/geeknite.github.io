### The Pick, Pass mechanic, but with less baggage
The core mechanic remains delightfully simple: you look at your hand, pick one card, then pass the rest to your neighbor. The next person does the same, and so on until all cards are claimed and scored. The real magic happens when you realize how many little points swing from a single choice: a pair of nori rolls here, a mis-matched maki there, a secret sauce that turns your crispy tempura into a genuine win. In the Travel Edition, you’ll still get the same witty, fast-paced drafting as the original, just in a compact format that doesn’t demand a throne for your luggage.

The “pick, pass” rhythm creates micro-dramas: will you take the miso soup to deny another player a key set, or risk letting them have a peek at your plan and throw a curveball with a clever card swing? The tension is zero-sum, which is perfect for travel sessions where everyone wants to feel like a benevolent chef with a knife skills that aren’t quite dangerous enough to land you on a “Do Not Fly” list.

## How to play: a quick refresher with travel-friendly flavor

### Setup
- Shuffle the Sushi Go! Travel deck and deal a hand of cards to each player. The exact count may vary depending on player count, but the standard rules keep it snappy.
- Place the scoring pad within easy reach. On a cramped table, you’ll want to be able to mark down results without needing a magnifying glass.
- Decide the direction of passing. In the original game you pass left; in some variants, players rotate. The Travel Edition usually keeps it simple—left to maintain the social dynamic while you’re stuck in a line at the border.

### A sample round: the micro-decision economy
1) You peek at your hand and notice a set of nigiri that pairs beautifully with a future card you hope to snag. Your brain evaluates: is this the turn to snap up a dumpling that your tablemates will regret? Or do you pick the dumpling anyway to block a rival from completing their plate?
2) You choose a card and place it face down in front of you, then pass the remaining cards to your neighbor. The player to your left looks at their new hand and does the same. You repeat until everyone has played all their cards.
3) Scoring: you tally points from your collected cards. In Sushi Go!, different cards yield different points, with combos and sets giving you the big wins and singling out the most coveted dishes on the table. The Travel Edition doesn’t alter the scoring; it just makes the table easier to manage when you’re on the move.

### Scoring basics (for those who want the punchline before the joke)
- Nigiri cards earn a base score, with some varieties doubling down on the point value.
- Maki rolls reward multi-card combos; the longer the roll, the more points you brag about, especially to that one friend who always claims to hate sushi but somehow loves victory.
- Puddings add tension in the closing rounds, with final tallies often deciding who gets to pretend they’ve already booked a sushi cruise before the trip actually exists.
- The travel edition doesn’t change the math; it just changes where you place your bets and how often you can knock elbows without dislodging your passport.

## Strategy essentials: how to think when you’re on a tiny table
### General approach
- Don’t overcommit early: the beauty (and danger) of Sushi Go! lies in drafting. You want to avoid over-investing in a single path that your neighbors can easily spoil by stealing their own cards.
- Balance immediate gain vs. long-term planning: sometimes taking a modest card now sets you up for a bigger payoff on a future turn, especially if your neighbors are likely to take the big scoring cards before you get a chance again.
- Watch opponents’ bodies language (or, you know, their card signals): in a tiny travel venue, you’ll notice patterns. If someone keeps grabbing sushi rolls for points, you may want to block their next move with a different card.

### 2-5 player strategy notes
- With two players, you’ll see more direct competition for sets. This makes it a little more “duel-y,” and you’ll want to plan around the other person’s likely picks.
- In the 3-5 player range, there’s a social dynamic that becomes as important as the card math: you’ll be juggling multiple potential devantures for points, and sometimes the best move is the quiet, unassuming pick that denies a critical set that your neighbors haven’t even realized would ruin their day.

## How to build your personal strategy, step-by-step
1) Start with observation: in the early rounds, pay attention to what others are chasing. Are they collecting nigiri, or are they chasing a set that requires specific pieces that are scarce in this round? Your first goal is to map the board and anticipate the board-state changes.
2) Early aggression vs. late-phase control: sometimes you’ll want to grab a card that denies a key set from your neighbor, even if it’s not the highest immediate points. Other times you’ll want to quietly accumulate a few cards that align toward a complete set by the end.
3) The “tell” of the table: if someone is always taking the dumplings, you can use their greed against them by passing them a different card to keep them from a complete set, then capitalizing on the next turn.
4) Endgame awareness: the last few rounds often determine the winner. Don’t underestimate the value of a single card that completes a set and pushes you over the top, even if it’s not the most dramatic choice.
