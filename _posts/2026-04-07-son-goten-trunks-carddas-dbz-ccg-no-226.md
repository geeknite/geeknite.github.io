## Collecting Tips and Care
Collecting is part art, part archaeology. When you hold a very rare No 226 card, you might feel like you are holding a tiny dragon that can grant your binder the glow of a hidden dragon cave. Here are practical tips to keep your treasure in fighting form for years to come:

- Storage matters: use sleeves designed for standard Carddas to prevent surface scratches. Consider soft, archival sleeves that preserve gloss without sticking to the card surface. A secondary soft top loader can guard against edge curl during transit or casual esthetic exhibitions on your desk.
- Temperature and humidity control: cards prefer stable environments. Avoid direct sunlight, damp corners, or the kind of climate that makes a rare card look like it survived a sauna with a dragon as a personal trainer.
- Handling etiquette: wash hands, avoid eating near the cards, and try not to drop them into the seat cushions of your car. You will thank yourself later for treating No 226 with the respect it deserves. A dab of magic eraser for minor scuffs can work wonders without harming the print if used gently.
- Grading and assessment: if you plan to have the card graded, consign it to professionals who understand Carddas and the Japanese market. Grading can add value, but it also introduces risk. The key is to know what you are willing to lose or gain in the grading process and to approach it with patience rather than impulse.
- Display options: binder pages that show the card at an angle to catch light can help, but avoid overexposure. A simple upright display stand with a soft backing can let your card shine while keeping it protected from the world’s unending enthusiasm.

## What to Look For in a Great No 226 Card
If you are evaluating a No 226 card for purchase or trade, here are some practical checkpoints that help separate the fabulous from the forgettable:

- Corners: square and sharp corners indicate minimal handling wear. If you see rounding or nicks, factor in depreciation.
- Surface: look for scratches, scuffs, or fingerprints that might mar the gloss. The better the surface, the more it catches light and the cooler your display looks.
- Foil or print variety: if the card features foil elements, check for uniformity across the card. Inconsistent foil can be a sign of a lower grade or a misprint variant that some collectors might actually seek.
- Color depth: authentic prints tend to have rich color fidelity with the characters well separated from background art. If the image looks washed out or muddy, you might be dealing with a reproduction or a poorly stored copy.
- Set authenticity: verify that the No 226 card belongs to the Japanese edition set you intend to collect. There are multi-language or reprint variants that may be mislabeled as the Japanese edition in some markets.
