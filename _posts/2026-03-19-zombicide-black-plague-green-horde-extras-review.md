## How It Plays: Mechanics and Tactical Implications
### Base Game Synergy
Green Horde is designed to slot into Black Plague with minimal fuss. If you’ve played Black Plague, you’ll know the general cadence: you activate a few survivors, roll dice, combine actions, and either save the day or cry in the corner while a dozen greens advance on your position. The extras lot introduces more greens, which means the zombie density spike is real. Expect tighter lanes, more crowd control decisions, and the occasional moment where you realize you should have moved to the left instead of the right because the tile layout teased you with a ghostly whisper: “you’ll regret not exploring the corridor.”

### New Monsters and Behaviors
The Green Horde adds a few variants with distinctive movement patterns and infection rules. Some greens may be more aggressive if unthreatened, some may form flanking squeezes that tempt you into overconfident bungee plays. Strategy wise, you’ll want to:
- Use terrain to funnel greens into chokepoints rather than chasing them into open spaces.
- Concentrate fire on threats that threaten your most valuable survivors early on.
- Balance risk by trading a limb or two for a chance to trigger a powerful endgame surge when you truly need it.

### Solo and Cooperative Play
If you own Zombicide: Black Plague as a standalone, you can still enjoy the Green Horde extras: the additional boards and zombies scale fine for a single player with a couple of AI helpers. Solo players will find the added tile variety invigorating; the greens tend to punish tunnel vision and reward careful planning. The added variants can increase the game length, so pack a lunch and a spare coffee mug for the inevitable “one more round” moment.

## Practical Tips for Getting the Most from Your Extras Lot
- Sort by type and color first. A quick triage helps you see at a glance how many greens you’ll be dealing with per turn.
- Create a “graveyard” tray for discarded cards so you don’t accidentally reuse a trap card as a healing item in the same round.
- If you’re painting, consider a two-step approach: base coat for the greens, then a glaze to give depth to armor, which makes the units feel more imposing on screen or at table height.
- Use a simple lift-and-release approach for your tables: a small card holder for terrain cards makes it easy to reference while you’re plotting a tactical gambit.
- For solo play, assign a “shadow AI” for the non-player characters to keep decision trees interesting without turning the game into a solitaire slog.
