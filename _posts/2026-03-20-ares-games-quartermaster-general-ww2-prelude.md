## The Quick Pitch
If you like your history with a side of adrenaline and a dash of panic, Quartermaster General: WW2 Prelude from Ares Games might just be the beverage that keeps your tabletop empire from collapsing into a glorious, glorious crater of burnt cookies and spilled tea. This is the kind of game that reminds you that friendship is a fragile thing—will you sacrifice a snack token to manipulate an ally, or will you grit your teeth and watch as Axis and Allies shuffle through the deck while you mutter about supply lines in a thick European accent?

For the uninitiated, Quartermaster General is a card-driven, world-spanning wargame about moving pieces, playing cards, and chasing victory points while your table erupts in debates about air superiority and the superiority of your favorite supply chain. WW2 Prelude refines that formula: a tighter footprint, a sharper focus on early-war choices, and a deck that teases out the tension of decision-making before the drumbeat of a full campaign starts. It’s a prelude, not a footnote, and it behaves accordingly: fast, chaotic, and politely brutal.

If you’re chasing vibes, think Twilight Struggle with earlier tempo and more logistics. Think Diplomacy, but with dice-flavored chaos and fewer parliamentary apologies. The Prelude module leans into a pre-war mood that feels like the opening bars of a symphony—you know the melody, you sense the tempo, and you realize you’ll probably misread the conductor and end up with a cadenza of misfortune.

## The Rules and the Flow: How It Plays
WW2 Prelude is not a “teach yourself while you’re mid-battle” kind of game; it rewards a gentle reading, a careful setup, and a willingness to let the rules sink in like a warm nap after a long day of virtual escapism. The core loop is familiar to people who’ve tasted card-driven strategies before: you draw cards, you play cards to perform orders, you resolve battles, you manage supply, and you chase victory points that appear and vanish like a magician’s card trick.

Turn structure remains crisp but not merciless. You’ll typically do these steps:
- Draw up to a hand limit, replenishing your strategic options.
- Play event and order cards to influence your units and the theater of operations.
- Resolve conflicts on the map using a mix of card effects and dice-like resolution rituals that are straightforward on the surface but offer surprising depth if you want to drill into the math.
- Manage supplies and reinforcement pools so your economy remains buoyant rather than sinking into a paper boat in a rainstorm.
- Score end-of-round points and watch as momentum swirls around the table like a gale of sausages—and strategy.

One of the most gratifying aspects of Prelude is how the prelude phase reshapes the early game into a delicate dance of preparation. Players jockey for production windows, secure crucial frontlines, and calibrate influence on political tracks that unlock better cards or more favorable event chains. It’s a design that rewards patient planning, but also punishes overlong planning that ignores the possibility of early disruption—a rogue card draw that collapses even the most well-laid plan.

For new players, the learning curve can feel steep, but the prelude framing helps shorten some of the longer adoption curves you might see in other heavyweight wargames. The rulebook includes a section that acts as a patient teacher, with bite-sized examples and color-coded flowcharts that anchor new concepts without drowning you in nuance. The trick is to approach with humor, patience, and a willingness to accept that you’ll misread a card at least once as you settle into the rhythm of the deck.

The phase creates a palpable texture of anticipation: will you commit to a bold but risky front, or hedge and hope to pounce when the map is ripe for a sudden, devastating push?

If you’ve ever played a card-driven war game where the cards are the engine, you’ll recognize the heartbeat here. ## Strategy Notes: Getting the Most From Prelude
Here are a few hard-won tips that won’t ruin your love for the game but will help you avoid rookie mistakes:
- Prioritize supply management early. It’s the oxygen in the bloodstream between great plans and glorious collapse. Without it, your best cards are just pretty weighs.
- Observe the tempo of your opponents. The moment someone commits to a heavy offensive plan, you’ll often find windows to counterplay that preserve your momentum while deflating theirs.
- Use prelude events to telegraph intent. If you can lure a rival into overextending on a minor front, you’ll create leverage for a bigger swing later.
- Balance aggression with consolidation. The longer you grow, the harder it is to hold lines facing counter-punches. Sometimes a small, well-timed retreat is the bravest move you’ll make.
- Learn the synergy between cards. There are combos that, when lined up correctly, shine as bright as victory points on the score sheet. Don’t be afraid to practice in a few rounds to find your rhythm.

## Accessibility and Learning Curve
Prelude isn’t trying to be a casual party game, but it does aim for a spin that newcomers can approach without fear. The learning curve is real, but the presentation—the prelude phase, color-coded flows, and bite-sized examples—acts as a patient mentor rather than a stern drill sergeant. If you’re a newcomer to card-driven war games, plan for a couple of introductory sessions where you explain just enough to keep from tripping over edge cases. The payoff is a deep, satisfying system that rewards careful planning and flexible responses.

Ares Games has crafted a thoughtful, engaging take on the world-spanning card-driven war game with Quartermaster General: WW2 Prelude.
