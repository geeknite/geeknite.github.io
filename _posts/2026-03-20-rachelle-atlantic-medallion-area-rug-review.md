## Materials and Construction
### Fiber Composition
Expect a synthetic fiber backbone designed for stain resistance and ease of cleaning. The most common recipe here is polypropylene with a dash of polyester for sheen. This isn’t the “crystal loom of the Caspian Sea” lux we see in museum-grade rugs, but it’s a pragmatic, stylish choice for real-world living. The backing is often a woven or non-slip synthetic layer, sometimes paired with a cotton blend, which helps keep the rug in place on hardwood or laminate floors. If you’ve ever watched your dog slide a rug across a tile floor like a tiny hockey player, you’ll appreciate the practical approach to grip these days.

### Pile Height and Density
Pile height tends to hover in the mid-range, around 0.35 to 0.5 inches. That’s the sweet spot for comfort without turning every chair leg into an obstacle course. A denser surface means durability in high-traffic zones, while too-tall piles can look fluffy but collapse under heavy furniture. The Atlantic Medallion aims for that balance where the rug feels substantial underfoot yet remains easy to vacuum and maintain. If you have a high-traffic family zone, the density will make a real difference in how long it keeps its shape before you notice flattening in the center.

### Backing and Stability
Backings matter—especially on smooth floors. A good backing reduces slippage and keeps the rug from creeping away as you stroll across the doorway in the morning. Expect a non-slip layer or a tightly woven backing that grips the floor with the quiet confidence of a caffeine-fueled spreadsheet. ## Cleaning, Care, and Maintenance
### Vacuuming Tips
Make friends with your vacuum. Use a setting of low-to-moderate suction and steady passes rather than brute force. The goal is to pull dirt out of the pile without pulling fibers loose. If you’ve got a high-shedding pet, consider a weekly vacuum schedule and rotate the rug to promote even wear. In a pinch, a soft-bristle brush can help lift fuzz without roughing up the surface.

### Spot Cleaning Guide
Accidents happen. For most stains, blot gently with a clean cloth and a mild cleaning solution. Use cold or lukewarm water rather than hot—heat can set some stains. Always test a hidden corner first to ensure colorfastness. For more persistent stains, consider a gentle pre-treatment and a second pass with a clean water rinse. Do not soak the rug; saturating the backing can lead to mildew and slow-drying disasters.

### Stain Removal Do’s and Don’ts
Do: act quickly, use blotting rather than rubbing, test solutions on a hidden area, air-dry completely. Don’t: apply bleach directly, rub aggressively, or use hot water on protein-based stains (like dairy or egg). If a stubborn problem persists, call in a professional cleaner who specializes in synthetic fiber rugs.
