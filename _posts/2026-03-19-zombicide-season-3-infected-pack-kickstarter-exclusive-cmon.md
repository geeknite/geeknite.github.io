## What you get in the Infected Pack
The Kickstarter exclusive Infected Pack is not just a box of extra plastic; it’s meant to inject a little wild card energy into your scenarios. You can expect a handful of new zombie types that feel both familiar and novel. Rather than one more generic zombie sculpt, you’ll see variants designed to look extra infected—more sinewy, more feral, more likely to remind you of your last allergic reaction to a hot pizza. Thematically, these minis fit Season 3’s tone like a cast member who actually showed up to the shoot.

- A set of new zombie sculpts: The Infected pack leans heavily into the “bitey, gnarly, and sneaky” vibe. These aren’t the slow-moving stock zombies you see in many games. They feel agile, hungry, and a little less predictable, which shakes up how you plan routes and rescue missions.
- New survivor miniatures: You’re not just fighting; you’re playing as stubborn humans who occasionally forget they’re carrying a crossbow and not an emergency snack. The new survivors come with fresh loadouts that encourage varied play styles—sniper lines, tanky brawn, and cunning medic paths all get a little more sparkle.
- Tokens and cards: The pack adds infection counters, event or mission modifiers, and a few new mission cards that tilt the balance toward riskier plays. These aren’t wallpaper; they actively influence how you map your approaches and what you consider “safe” on a map.
- A couple of generic, but nicely produced, plastic upgrades: These might be replaceable parts from the base set, but they look and feel sturdier in the same way a premium factory floor paint job feels different from something a kid would use to decorate a cardboard box.

The combination is not merely about adding more minis to the tray; it’s about injecting a particular flavor into the mid-to-late campaigns when you’re tired of the safe, “defend the exit” routines and want a little chaos to remind you that you’re playing a zombie apocalypse game and not a board-game museum exhibit.

## Build quality and design language
CMON tends to strike a balance between detail and practicality. The Infected Pack doesn’t break that tradition. The sculpts are bold enough to read on a 30mm base, which helps when you’ve got a table full of critters snarling and stumbling. The infection texture on the zombies looks appropriately gnarly, which adds to the thematic vibe without appearing gloppy in a way that would bother a gamer who pays attention to paint schemes.

- Plastic quality: The minis are solid, with a weight that says “we meant business.” They require a light amount of trimming around some mold lines, but nothing so savage that you’ll end up hissing at a tiny piece of plastic like a cranky goblin. If you’re planning to paint, you’ll find that the texture is forgiving enough for beginners while still giving you surface detail to challenge your inner speed-painter.
- Assembly: Not a lot of fiddly bits, which is good news if your friend group’s best glue job is “hot glue, but it’s not guaranteed.” A quick wash, a little green stuff to fill gaps (optional), and you’re ready to go. The survivors sit on their bases with a confident stance that won’t wobble during dramatic tabletop moments, which is always appreciated when your dice decide to perform a one-man interpretive dance around your board.
- Aesthetics: The color palette leans into decomposed flesh tones and dark, saturated clothing—classic CMON style. It reads well on the table and photographs nicely for those Instagram battle reports that your gaming group pretends not to care about but secretly loves.

If you’ve played with Season 3 before, you’ll notice the new sculpts fit the world without looking out of place. They don’t scream “new toy” in a way that breaks immersion; they blend the chaotic energy of the infected with the gritty realism of a zombie survival scenario.

## How it changes the game: mechanics and balance
The core question with any Kickstarter exclusive is this: does it alter the core gameplay in meaningful ways, or is it just a pretty, expensive decoration? The Infected Pack leans toward meaningful. The new zombie variants interact with existing infection mechanics in ways that require recalibrated planning. It’s not a massive rules overhaul; it doesn’t derail the learning curve for newcomers. Instead, it adds a layer of strategic depth that can be played conservatively or aggressively depending on your crew’s play style.

- Infection dynamics: The infected variants typically have modified abilities that can ramp up the pressure. They might have special rules that make them more dangerous when triggered by certain events or map conditions. This makes positioning and zone control more interesting, especially in tight corridors where one wrong move can cascade into a chain of bites.
- Survivor interactions: The new survivors bring different skill trees and starting equipment. This adds variety to your party composition and allows for more thematic runs (e.g., a medic who’s actually wound-up enough to be reckless, or a sharpshooter who can really puncture a choke point). It’s worth experimenting with different combos to discover what works best against the pack’s infection angles.
- Campaign pacing: With new mission cards and infection modifiers, you often get that “one more turn” feeling without tipping into “we’re stuck in a puzzle of doom.” The pacing remains brisk, but there’s enough variance to keep your table from muttering the same tired lines every time you set up a map.

In terms of balance, the pack shines when you’re playing with a mixed group (veterans and newcomers). The new content gives seasoned players opportunities to exploit clever synergies, while still providing approachable hooks for new players to dive into the zombie-slaying action. If you’re worried about breaking the baseline difficulty, you can run a few trial rounds with and without the pack to gauge how your group handles the shift. The general consensus among playgroups I’ve watched is that the pack introduces excitement without making the core game feel unfair or “boss-ed” by the new content.

## Practical notes: setup, storage, and shelf presence
- Setup time: The pack doesn’t dramatically increase setup time. You’ll be able to integrate the new components into a standard Season 3 map in a few minutes. The most time-consuming part is often aligning custom map overlays or mission modifiers, but that’s a one-time investment for future plays.
- Storage: The minis are compact enough to slot into a standard Zombicide storage tray, but you’ll want a dedicated section for the infected variants to prevent them from getting mixed with base zombies. A simple foam insert or the stock insert from your edition will handle it, just label the sections so you don’t accidentally mix up your “feral” from your “regular” zombies during a heated moment.
- Table presence: With the added infected variants, your board will look more dynamic. The new colorways and sculpting details pop on a camera and hold up well in photos—great for those battle reports you pretend are for “research” but are really for bragging rights.
- Painting considerations: If you enjoy painting, these minis are a good test bed for flesh tones and weathered fabrics. If you’re not a painter, fear not: these are still legible on the table and read well at gaming distances, which is the sweet-spot for most co-op sessions.

## How to run a session with the Infected Pack: tips and tricks
- Start with a quick “what’s new” summary for your players. It makes the moment you roll the dice feel earned instead of confusing.
- Use the new mission cards to introduce a theme. Try a “time pressure” scenario where you’re racing to retrieve a critical resource while the infection surge escalates.
- Pair the survivors with kit builds that compress risk into reward. A medic who can heal a critical player, combined with a ranged specialist who can thin the herd from a safe distance, makes for a satisfying dynamic.
- Keep a running tally of infection pressure on a separate tracker. This helps players visualize how quickly the map is turning against them and fosters better communication about retreat and regroup strategies.
- Play a quick “drop-in” game with new players using the Infected Pack to demonstrate how synergy works. If they survive the first map, they’re hooked for the next session.
