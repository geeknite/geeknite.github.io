## How Monopoly Deal Works (in case you forgot)
Monopoly Deal isn’t about rolling dice and moving a little plastic man around a board with a mortgage-shaped existential crisis. It’s a card game where you collect property cards to complete full color sets, then rapidly collect rent to drain your opponents’ wallets (figuratively—don’t expect an actual bank to appear with a currency crisis and a dramatic gasp).

### Card types explained
- Property cards: The heart of your collection. Build color sets by stacking these in front of you. Each color set is a potential victory condition; collect enough and you’re cooking with gas.
- Rent cards: Exchange these to demand payment from opponents. You can play a Rent card based on the color(s) you control, or you can mix it up with a Wild Rent card that stretches across colors. Timing matters: rent works best when opponents have multiple properties because you want to maximize your payout without giving away too much information about your holdings.
- Action cards: The spicy ones. These let you steal cards, demand money, force trades, or swap entire sets with an unholy glee that would make a pirate blush. They’re the reason this game has replay value: each hand feels different, and every tantrum has a tactical counter-tantrum.
- Wild cards: The “everything and nothing” of the deck. They can stand in and fill a missing color, but you’ll often hold onto them for a late-game power move or a sneaky block to keep your rivals honest (or at least distracted).
- Debt/Deal cards: If someone interferes with your property empire, you can cleanly respond with a debt claim to extract coins or property, turning the game into an negotiation circus where you pretend your kid’s lemonade stand is a multinational corporation.

### Property sets and rent cards
The core strategy revolves around assembling color-coordinated property sets. A complete set yells “I am unstoppable!” at the table, while an almost-complete set acts as a tempting target for your friends, family, or colleagues who have nothing better to do on a Sunday afternoon. Rent cards expand the financial leverage you wield. They’re not just payments; they’re mood-altering chips that let you feel like a cunning mastermind when your opponent looks at their hand in despair and mutters, “I should have kept that red set.”

### Action cards and their timing
Timing is everything. You’ll want to play action cards to steal or swap at moments that maximize disruption to your rival’s plans. The best moment is often when someone just achieved near-stability by assembling a small property crew, at which point you swoop in with a well-timed Deal Breaker or Sly Trick to flip the entire board, or at least the entire strategy on its head. It’s a phenomenon of social engineering masquerading as a card game—a phenomenon that makes family gatherings feel like a heist movie but with more laughter and fewer explosions.

## Strategy and Shred of Luck
Like all good card games, Monopoly Deal: Complete Cards is a dance between strategy and chaos. You control what you can see, but you can’t control what’s in other players’ hands. Here’s how to tilt the odds in your favor without consulting a wizard.

### When to hold, when to discard
- Early game: You want a mix of property and rent cards to set up your future buys, but you should avoid hoarding too many of one color. You’ll hand your opponents a clear target and maybe even stoke envy about your color-blocked empire.
- Mid-game: When people start closing in on their own complete sets, debt cards and action cards become your best friends. This is the moment to push back, sabotage, and remind everyone that you read the instruction manual (even if you pretend you didn’t).
- Late game: If you’ve almost completed a set, you’ll need to guard it with some stealthy plays. Use wild cards wisely; they are flexible and can be a saving grace when you’re one card away from victory and someone steals your last blue.

### House rules for family nights
Every family has its own quirks, so here are a few light house rules that preserve the drama without breaking the bank (or your nerves):
- Rule of Mutual Disdain: If a hand contains no more than four cards after a round, everyone draws one extra card at the end of the turn. This keeps the pace snappy and gives the underdog a chance to rebound.
- Dramatic Pause: Allow a 10-second “stare-down” after a big move. It gives everyone a chance to savor the moment and pretend they’re not sweating through their cardigan.
- Friendly Swap Amnesty: If someone offers a trade that makes everyone groan with laughter, you’re allowed to veto once per game. It’s your chance to remind people you still have a soul underneath the glow of their phone screens.
