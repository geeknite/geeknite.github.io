## Gameplay and rules

### The core UNO rules, with a Canvas twist
UNO’s bedrock is simple: match color or number, play a Wild or Wild Draw Four when you need a lifeline, shout UNO when you’re down to one card, and hope your friends don’t gang up to punish you for misplays. The Billie Eilish Canvas edition respects that baseline and introduces a few additions that align with its art-forward persona.

Key differences you’ll notice:
- Canvas Wilds: In place of ordinary Wild cards, you’ll find variants that allow a color change plus a small art-card interaction. The exact effect can vary by print run, but the core idea is that you can pick a color and then trigger a small effect from the art deck.
- Mood cards: A tiny subset of cards introduces mood-based effects (think a quick swing in momentum). They might skip the next player, force a discard-run, or demand drawing from the art card pile under a simple rule. These are not crippling changes; they are tempo shifts designed to keep the game moving with a party vibe.
- The art deck interaction: Some art cards, when drawn, instruct players to perform a light, non-disruptive action (like revealing the art card and choosing a color to honor visually). It’s about adding flavor rather than turning the game into a sandbox of extra rules.

### The Canvas twist: a lightweight, optional mode
If you’re playing with purists in mind, you can opt out of Canvas-specific effects and play straight UNO using the standard 108-card deck. If you want a more social, photo-friendly experience, you can run a lightweight “Canvas mode” that includes the art cards as wilds and the mood cards as optional modifiers. This keeps the session short and snappy while giving players something to marvel at during the downtime between turns.

### A sample playthrough idea
Imagine you’re in a four-player game. You’re holding a handful of mid-range cards, and the table is illuminated by a lamp that makes the neon on the deck glow in a retro-futuristic way. Your opponent plays a Canvas Wild, changing the color to blue. You decide to slam down an art card from the art deck (yes, there is a card draw here) which reveals a small lyric fragment and instructs the next player to draw one card if they can’t immediately match the new color. The mood card from the art pile nudges you to switch to an alternate rule for this round, which includes a one-card draw penalty for the player who fails to respond in time. The moment passes, the tempo quickens, and you’re left with a satisfying mix of strategy-lite and aesthetic punch. If this sounds like your jam, this edition has your back.
