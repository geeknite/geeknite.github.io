### What’s inside a 1979 International Games UNO deck?
Standard UNO decks contain four colors (red, yellow, green, blue) with numbers 0-9, plus action cards: Skip, Reverse, Draw Two, plus Wild cards. The 1979 International Games print is one of the early editions that didn’t have the modern flourishes of later runs. In this era, you’d expect a four-color stack of numbers, each color card showing the big bold digits and the necessary two-ofs for 1-9. The Wild cards exist, but the era’s designs differ from later years; you’ll see a slightly different font, a more matte finish, and a back that looks like it’s keeping a secret from the future. There’s also a subtle printing id on the lower corner of the cards that can help you date the set more accurately than your aunt’s birthday.

This particular deck is described as Mostly Complete, which is a polite way of saying: We found a box full of cards, but it’s missing some of the lines that would let you marginalize your siblings with absolute certainty. In practice, what you get is all the colored number cards, almost all the colored action cards, and a handful of Wilds. The two most common things you’ll encounter as missing are a couple of the less dramatic action cards (Skip or Draw Two) and maybe a stray Wild card or two that never learned to stay in line with the rest of the set. If you’re hunting a complete vintage UNO for display or a period-perfect gameplay experience, this deck will require some hunting and a little luck, because a few missing pieces quietly slide away like socks in the dryer of time.

If you want a true 108-card experience, you’ll need to verify the count carefully. Count the colored numbers: four colors, 0 to 9; then count action cards: two per color for Skip, Reverse, Draw Two; add four Wilds and, in later years, Wild Draw Four; this 1979 variant would typically include four Wilds but no Wild Draw Four. Some sellers mix early 80s decks with later prints, which can complicate valuation. The safe approach: check for a small 1979 copyright mark within the card, a blocky UNO logo, and a back that’s a little less glossy. Authenticity matters if you’re paying a premium.

## Authenticity cues and authentication tips
If you’re serious about buying or valuing a vintage UNO deck, you should consider a few authentication cues:
- Look for the copyright stamp on the bottom of the cards: © 1979 International Games is a good sign.
- Check the inner packaging for the original box insert; older inserts usually feature the International Games brand and may show price disclaimers from the era.
- The card stock is thicker, heavier, and less glossy than modern iterations. It feels more matte and robust.
- The back design should be consistent across cards, with the same geometric pattern and color balance.
- The Wild cards in this edition will be present as four in number but without the modern multi-color rings around the Wild emblem.
- The font on the front of the cards is chunkier; look for the early UNO logo design rather than the later sleek version.

## Care and restoration tips
A vintage deck deserves gentle care. Here are a few practical tips to keep your UNO deck surviving for future generations of gamers:
- Storage: Keep the deck in a cool, dry place. Avoid high humidity, which can cause the card stock to warp and the inks to bleed.
- Sleeves: For display and practice games, use acid-free card sleeves to prevent further wear while preserving the look.
- Handling: When shuffling, broaden the fan out slowly so you don’t crease the cards; the age of the ink means you’ll want to handle with care.
- Cleaning: If you think a card is slightly dirty, a soft microfiber cloth; avoid water or chemical cleaners that could degrade the ink or card stock.
- Display: If you choose to display the deck, consider a glass case to protect from environmental factors while showcasing the vintage art.

## How this deck compares to modern UNO editions
Modern UNO is designed with accessibility and mass production in mind: glossy cards, a standardized set, and a user-friendly approach to printing numbers and symbols. The 1979 International Games deck represents the hardware of a time when a deck of cards was a personal treasure and a gift for a family to enjoy—hard edges, bold colors, and a sense that every card needed to prove it belonged in the pack. There are subtle differences in typography and card face finish that affect readability, which adds to the charm, but may require players to adjust their approach when playing with more modern sets. The presence of Wild cards in this edition adds a strategic layer that modern players may already know, but you’ll notice the absence of Wild Draw Four from this era, which makes the deck simpler and perhaps a touch less chaotic than 1990s and 2000s variants.
