## What is FDM, and why should a zombie-slaying board game care?
FDM stands for Fused Deposition Modeling, a friendly term for a process where a melted thermoplastic filament is laid down layer by layer to forge objects. For board games, FDM prints are popular because they are cost-effective, easy to modify, and accessible to hobbyists with consumer printers. The rough edges and visible layer lines are the trade-offs that often come with the territory. The upside is customization at a price point that lets you print once, cry once, and still have enough left over to print a second dashboard when you realize you want one for every hero sheet in your party.

In Zombicide Black Plague, a well designed dashboard and tray not only speed up setup but also help keep track of the chaotic state of play: wounds, poison, armor, status effects, and the occasional token that decides the fate of your survivor. It is not magic, but it does feel a little magical when you are not scrambling across the table mid-turn for a misread token or an empty slot on a hero card. The ergonomic win is real: the dashboard sits neatly beside the board without obstructing dice rolls, line of sight, or the cat-nap zone where your stray scythe token tends to wander.

## Print settings and material talk: practical guidelines
Print choices will impact both the experience and the final product. Here are practical guidelines for typical dashboards and trays:
- Material: PLA is forgiving and stiff; PETG offers better durability and impact resistance. If your play table sees rough handling, PETG may be worth the extra effort.
- Layer height: 0.15–0.2 mm. Finer layers give crisper edges on slots and features but cost time. If you are printing several pieces, 0.2 mm is a good modern default.
- Infill: 15–20% is usually enough for dashboards and trays with light stress. If you travel with your prints or expect heavier use, 25% adds rigidity without exploding print times.
- Shells: 2–3 perimeters to maintain edge integrity. A little extra perimeter goes a long way on a small piece.
- Orientation: Print dashboards with the token slots facing up to minimize support needs; print trays flat for stability.
- Supports: Only where necessary. You can usually print a dashboard with minimal supports if you orient carefully.
- Post-processing: A light sand on contact surfaces, followed by warm soapy wash, keeps things clean and minimizes wear on cards and tokens. A light clear coat can help with wipe-downs and add a hint of professional polish.
- Testing: Print a small test piece first to verify fit with your exact hero sheets and tokens. This reduces heartbreak when you realize your print is off by a fraction of a millimeter.

The exact settings depend on your printer and filament. The process is iterative and fun, like leveling up your craft while you level up your zombie slaying prowess.

## Setup and assembly: steps that won’t ruin your evening
1) Print both components in your chosen material and color. If you want a multi-color look, you can switch filaments mid-run or print separate pieces and assemble later.
2) Do a quick dry-fit to verify slot alignment with hero sheets and token pockets. This is your warm-up jog before the sprint to victory.
3) Lightly sand rough edges around contact surfaces to prevent wear on cards or tokens. A smooth interface means longer life for both cards and markers.
4) If you use magnets or double-sided tape, add a non-slip base to hold tokens in place during chaotic rounds.
5) Clean and cure any residue. The last thing you want is Gunk Gate becoming a feature of your next match.

## A quick tour of practical tips
- Test fit before you commit: Print a small proxy piece to verify slot sizes, then adjust nozzle width or tolerance as needed. Tiny misfits can become giant annoyances when you are mid campaign.
- Fit, don’t force: If something does not slide in with a gentle push, do not force it. It is a sign you need to adjust your tolerances or check cure times. The universe has a sense of humor about tight fits.
- Tame the surface: Light sanding on rims that contact tokens reduces wear over long campaigns. If you want to go the extra mile, a tiny amount of plastic polish can yield a smoother surface for easier wipe-downs.
- Keep it clean: A microfiber cloth and a dab of isopropyl alcohol keep the dashboard and tray looking pristine after snack breaks and dice-fueled mayhem.
- Consider a faction rotation: If your table rotates between Black Plague factions, consider printing multiple dashboards so you can switch layouts quickly without reconfiguring slots.
