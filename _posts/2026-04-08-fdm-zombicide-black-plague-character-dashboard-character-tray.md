## What is FDM, and why should a zombie-slaying board game care?
FDM stands for Fused Deposition Modeling, a 3D printing method that melts thermoplastic filament and lays it down layer by layer to create objects. For board games, FDM prints are popular because they’re relatively inexpensive, easy to tweak, and can be printed on consumer-level machines. The trade-off is surface finish. Your dashboard might have slightly visible layer lines; your tray might whisper—okay, practically shout—about the print orientation you chose and the amount of post-processing you’re willing to do. The upside is customization at a price that won’t make your wallet scream louder than a dragon’s roar.

In the case of Zombicide Black Plague, a well-designed dashboard and tray can speed up setup, reduce misplacement of tokens, and give players a quick reference lane to the important numbers on the hero sheets. It’s not magical, but it does feel a little magical when you don’t have to rummage through a pile of plastic to find the poison counter while your partner announces a fresh swarm of walkers. And yes, the dashboard typically fits alongside the operating area without obstructing dice rolls or miniatures—an ergonomic feat that earns bonus nerd cred.

## Print settings and material talk: practical guidelines
This is where the difference between a showpiece and a nightly workhorse becomes obvious. Here are practical guidelines based on typical FDM workflows for this kind of accessory:

- Material: PLA is user-friendly and stiff, but PETG offers a little more durability and impact resistance. If you’re rough with your board games (and who isn’t), PETG is worth considering for longevity.
- Layer height: 0.15–0.2 mm. A finer layer height yields nicer edges on slots and features but increases print time. If you’re printing multiple dashboards, consider 0.2 mm for a good balance.
- Infill: 15–20% is usually enough for a dashboard or tray with light stress. If you’ll stack heavy hero sheets or carry them around a lot, you can go to 25% for more rigidity.
- Shells: 2–3 perimeters to keep the edges clean and avoid fragile outer walls.
- Orientation: For dashboards, print with the face that contains cuts for tokens facing up to minimize support needs. For trays, print flat to maximize stability unless you’re chasing an overhang dream and you’ve got the support-material budget to spare.
- Supports: Use them only where necessary. A lot of dashboard surfaces can print cleanly without supports if you orient correctly, but you might need a little support for the inner slots.
- Post-processing: A light sanding of rims that contact tokens, followed by a rinse in warm soapy water, helps remove printing residue. If you’re feeling fancy, a thin coat of clear spray can improve wipe-downs and give the piece a sense of “polished professional.”

Remember: the exact settings depend on your printer, filament brand, and how brave you are about risk. The best practice is to print a small test piece first to check fit with your actual hero sheets and tokens before committing to a full print run.

## Setup and assembly: steps that won’t ruin your evening
1) Print both components (dashboard and tray) in your chosen material and color. If you want to do a multi-color print, you can attempt color changes mid-run or print separate pieces and assemble later. 2) Do a quick dry-fit to verify the slots align with your hero sheet and the token pockets accommodate your status markers. 3) Lightly sand any rough layer lines around edges that will contact cards or tokens to prevent wear on card corners or token edges over time. 4) If you have magnets or double-sided tape, you can add a non-slip base or small magnetic catches to hold the tokens in place during quick-table shenanigans. 5) Clean and cure any excess residue; you don’t want Gunk Gate to become a feature in your next match.

## A quick tour of practical tips
- Test fit before you commit: Print a small proxy piece to verify the slot sizes, then adjust your nozzle width if you see fit issues.
- Fit, don’t force: If something doesn’t fit with a gentle push, don’t battle it with brute force. It’s a sign you need to adjust tolerances or that the print isn’t fully cured.
- Tame the surface: Light sanding on the edges helps prevent wear on tokens and card corners. If you’re feeling fancy, a tiny amount of plastic polish can produce a smoother surface for easier wipe-downs.
- Keep it clean: A microfiber cloth and a little isopropyl alcohol keep the dashboard and tray looking good after long nights of gaming and inevitable snack breaks.
- Consider a test run with a single faction: If your table frequently rotates between Zombicide Black Plague factions, you may want to print multiple dashboards to keep things quick and personal.
