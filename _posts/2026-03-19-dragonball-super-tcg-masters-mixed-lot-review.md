## What is a mixed lot in the Masters set?
The Dragon Ball Super Card Game Masters set is legendary for its breadth and nuance. A mixed lot labeled as Common, Uncommons, Rares Non Holo is exactly what it sounds like: a pile that contains mostly commons, some uncommons, and a subset of rares that are not holo (foiled). Non holo rares can still be valuable, especially if they’re staples in competitive builds or rare printings that are harder to find in the wild. The mix could include reprints, promo variants, or simply the natural distribution of a printing run. If you’re shopping with a budget, a lot like this gives you the chance to batch-learn about card quality, flavor text, and card text accuracy without paying top-dollar for fancy foils.

Why non holo matters: foils are shiny and flashy, but non holo cards are plentiful and cheap, which makes it much easier to fill out your binder or build a budget deck. The risk is duplicates and bulk that doesn’t actually bolster your collection. The upside: you’ll often find a few useful uncommons and rares that can slot into deck builds or trades.

## Why someone would sell a Masters mixed lot
There are several practical reasons a seller would bundle cards this way:
- They want to clear space and avoid listing hundreds of individual cards.

As a buyer, that’s a green flag if the price per card is enticing and you’re comfortable with a high probability of duplicates and non-valuable bulk. The trick is to estimate what you’ll actually take away in value and time spent sorting the pile. We’ll get to that, don’t worry.

## Reading the listing: what to look for
A good Masters mixed lot listing will be honest about the contents, but reality rarely aligns perfectly with the description. Here are the critical things to check before you press that “Buy” button:

### Card counts and variety
- Look for a rough breakdown in the listing: how many commons, how many uncommons, and how many rares non holo? A fair expectation for a larger lot might be anywhere from 60-70% commons, 20-30% uncommons, and 5-10% rares non holo. If the listing says “mixed” with no numbers, treat it as a red flag and ask for a detailed photo set or a spreadsheet.
- Check for duplicates: a lot with hundreds of dupes is not inherently bad, but you want to know whether you’re getting value beyond bulk. If you’re chasing a specific card or a specific subset, duplicates can be a headache rather than a boon.

### Condition and quality cues
- In a non holo lot, condition matters less for aesthetics but more for playability and price: straighter corners, no creases, and no obvious print defects matter if you hope to swap or sell singles later.
- Photos are your best friend. If there aren’t clear, well-lit pictures of the cards, ask the seller for a complete photo set. You’d be surprised how often a seller’s “mint” label conflicts with a photo that reveals shading, edge wear, or misprints.

### Flair vs reality: promo or reprint traps
- You’ll occasionally see non holo rares that are actually reprints or promos. They look the same but can carry different values. If the listing is vague, you might be looking at something that’s less valuable than you expect. A quick reverse pull on price databases later is not the end of the world, but it’s better to know up front.

### Pricing sanity check
- The price per card in a mixed lot is your most important metric. If you see a lot offered at a fraction of typical card values, you may be trading risk for reward. Do the math: if you’re paying $40 for a lot that contains roughly 200 cards, you’re in for about $0.20 per card. If the average value of those cards in perfect condition is $0.60, you’re in the black—if you’re prepared to sell or trade singles you don’t want.
- Don’t forget shipping. Mixed lots can be heavy; shipping can eat into your savings. Ask about combined shipping or local pickup to maximize savings.

## The anatomy of common, uncommon, rare non holo
Let’s unpack what you’ll encounter in a Masters mixed lot when you’re sorting by rarity and holo status. This helps you calibrate expectations without needing a degree in card taxonomy.

### Commons
Commons are the backbone of any card pool. In a Masters mix, you’ll find many copies of these filler workhorses: staples for deck builds, synergy stackers, and the kind of card you might use in a budget deck because it’s cheap and reliable. Common cards can provide consistent value across multiple decks; sometimes they’re your “starter cards” for a new player, or the spare copies you stash for trades.

What to look for in commons:
- Playability: Some commons see light tournament play, especially in older printings or specific archetypes. If you see a common that’s a known engine piece or draw power, you’re not just building a binder. You’re building potential.
- City-grade condition: If you’re sorting a pile containing a few near-mint examples of a popular common, those can hold value as singles much more than the rest of the bulk.

### Uncommons
Uncommons start to separate the explorers from the pack. They’re the glue that holds deck strategies together but aren’t the shiny baubles of the holo rares. In a Masters mix, uncommons can vary widely in utility and price.

What to look for in uncommons:
- Utility and synergy: Cards that enable loops, combos, or strong removal often have more lasting value than random uncommons with limited applicability.
- Printing quirks: Some uncommons exist in multiple printings with different art or minor text changes. These can affect collectability and price. If you’re chasing a particular iteration, verify the exact printing before counting on it.

### Rares non holo
Non holo rares are the tricky middle ground: they have more shelf-stable value than most commons and can be essential in cost-effective builds, but they won’t turn heads in a display case like foils. The trick is to identify which rares non holo are modern staples, and which are “meh” printers that got drawn into a mixed lot due to the vetting process.

What to look for in rares non holo:
- Deck-building relevance: A rare that supports a widely used deck archetype or a niche but popular synergy is more valuable than a rare that’s rarely used. If you’re buying for play value, this matters more than rarity alone.
- Print run and insert dates: Some rares are part of older or more limited print runs. They can fetch better prices on the resale market if the supply is tighter.

## Practical evaluation steps when you receive a lot
If you snag a Masters mixed lot, here’s a practical workflow to turn bulk into clear data and better decisions.

### Step 1: Quick triage with a buddy system
Lay out the lot on a flat surface. If you have a friend who’s into the DBS card game, recruit them. A second pair of eyes helps catch misprints, mislisted rarities, or subtle holo variations that your first pass missed.

### Step 2: Create a rough inventory
With a notepad or a simple spreadsheet, record counts by rarity, and note any standout cards (potential reprints, promos, or staples). Even a rough tally helps you estimate value and time investment required to sort later.

### Step 3: Check for condition markers
Rotate the pile under light and look at corners and edges. This is especially important if you plan to sell singles. You’ll save yourself future headache by separating near-mint from damaged copies before you post listings.

### Step 4: Price benchmarking
Use a mix of your own knowledge and a few quick web checks to determine a plausible price range for the included cards. If you’re unsure, you can always run a quick poll with trading buddies or use price-checking communities without exposing yourself to guesswork.

### Step 5: Sorting plan
Decide how you’ll sort the lot for sale or personal use. Some builders prefer to extract all rares and uncommons first, then fill the deck with a precise number of commons. Others like to bust the entire pile into a binder-friendly bag, sorting by card name for easy searching. Choose a method that matches how you like to operate, because you’ll be living with it for a while.

### Step 6: Risk management
Expect duplicates, misprints, and non-standard art. If you’re buying to profit, be conservative in your pricing. If you’re buying to build a personal collection, think of the duplicates as “free trade bait” that can help you expand your circle of card trading acquaintances.

## Typical price math for a Masters mixed lot
A 200-card non holo Masters lot might fetch you somewhere around a fraction of a dollar to a couple of dollars per card, depending on the distribution and condition. A rough rule of thumb:
- Commons: $0.05–$0.25 each depending on reprints and deck relevance.
- Uncommons: $0.20–$0.75 each if they’re popular in evolving meta lists or part of cycles that remain in use.
- Rares Non holo: $0.50–$3.00 each depending on the exact card and printing history.

Your actual revenue will hinge on your ability to trade or sell singles, the speed with which you move copies, and the cosmetic quality of the cards. If your goal is to learn the meta, you’re already ahead of the curve: you’re learning how to parse stacks of cardboard into a future deck or wallet-friendly resales.

## Practical tips to maximize value
- Prioritize trading: If you don’t need a large number of the same common card, trade them away in bundles for a couple of higher-value uncommons or rares. It’s like trading in your spare kitchen utensils for a fancy coffee maker; you’ll use that coffee maker a lot more than the extra spatula.
- Bundle for discounts: Offer to sell or trade the lot in smaller bundles (e.g., 50-card bundles) to attract buyers who want to build specific archetypes. You’ll often find that the right mix of common and uncommon cards is worth more in a targeted trade than in bulk.
- Check for value outliers: If you find a few high-value rares non holo lurking among the pile, consider pulling them out early and pricing them aggressively. Those “islands” of value can subsidize the rest of the lot.
- Keep track of shipping costs: If you’re shipping lots to customers, factor in packaging materials and tracking. You don’t want to eat your profits because you underestimated the cost of a padded mailer.
- Learn market demand: The value of non holo rares can shift with new decks and meta changes. Stay aware of which archetypes are currently popular. A rare that was dormant for years can suddenly climb in value if a new deck uses it as a core component.
