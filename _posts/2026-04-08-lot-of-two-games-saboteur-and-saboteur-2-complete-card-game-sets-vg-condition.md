## Quick orientation: what are Saboteur and Saboteur 2?
Saboteur is a classic among party-ish, quick-to-learn card games that nonetheless rewards cunning enough to make your friends blink. You play as dwarves digging for gold (or gold-adjacent points). The catch? Some players are miners who will happily misdirect the team, planting red herrings, traps, and, most importantly, a pinch of chaos in a perfectly reasonable gold-for-all scheme. The cards blend role mechanics, path-building, and a dash of deception. It’s the kind of game where a well-timed “I’m not the saboteur” can be as powerful as a perfectly plotted tunnel sequence.

Saboteur 2 expands the sandbox: more roles, more twists, new paths, and fresh ways to mess with your colleagues’ best-laid mining schemes. ## What’s in the “VG Condition” lot, practically
When a seller notes VG (Very Good) condition for a complete card game set, you should picture a deck of cards that has been used, enjoyed, and perhaps spirited away for a brief life as a finger-puzzle, but remains fully intact with most or all required components. In practical terms, VG cards show signs of wear, but they remain perfectly playable. Corners may have small dings, surfaces might have mild scratches or minor whitening along the edges, and the box could show light shelf wear. In the Saboteur duo, you’re looking for:
- All Saboteur base cards, including the treasure routes and the secret roles cards.
- All Saboteur 2 base cards, including its extended roles and path variants.
- The mining-path cards, gold tokens, and any action or ruin cards that typically end up as “every-hand-gets-quietly-angry” triggers in a good game night.
- The box: enough heft to not squish the cards when you take it to the coffee shop, a stable insert to hold it all in place, and no catastrophic box damage that would hinder storage or transport.

VG condition does not imply pristine mint; it implies used with care. If you’re a purist who wants the exact printing of 2009 with no crease, you may be disappointed. If you’re a pragmatic player who cares more about the experience than the aesthetic perfection, you’ll likely be pleased. The real question is whether the set is complete and readable after years of double-checking the rulebook to remember which card defeats which curse word you didn’t say aloud in front of your in-laws.

## A closer look at what you’re getting
### Saboteur (base game) overview
Saboteur is built around social deduction and path-building. Players are dwarves who lay down tunnel cards to connect from a starting point to a treasure card. Hidden among the players are saboteurs who try to derail the path, sabotaging the team with blocked routes, misleading paths, or the classic “I’m not the saboteur” moment with dramatic flourish. The main tension is cooperation meets betrayal, and the win condition can swing on a single card flip or a well-timed announcement about who is lying and who is not. The game thrives on player interaction, a little chaos, and the occasional pivot when the path you thought was secure suddenly becomes a dead end with a terrible pun attached.

The art style is accessible, not overly elaborate; the cards are friendly to new players, which is essential for a party-like game to take off. The deck is logically organized: path cards, action cards, and the coveted Goal cards that determine whether you end with treasure or a cautionary chorus of groans when the tunnel collapses around you.

### Saboteur 2 overview
Saboteur 2 introduces extra layers of complexity without turning Saboteur into a time sink. You’ll find more role cards to keep track of, more possible routes, and new actions that can change the entire tempo of a round. It’s a little more chaotic, a little more strategic, and a lot more opportunities for bluffing your way to glory. In a long-running game night, Saboteur 2 can elegantly slot into the middle slot: you’re not re-learning the wheel, just swapping in a few new spokes to make the wheel feel a touch more dynamic.

### How the two games feel when played back-to-back
If you’re lucky enough to host an extended Saboteur night, you’ll want to compare them side by side. ## How to play: a practical starter guide
If you’re not already familiar with Saboteur, a quick starter guide helps. The core loop in Saboteur is simple: establish a route from your starting point to the treasure while defending against saboteurs who are trying to derail or block. The base mechanics revolve around card placing, path-drawing, and role-matching. The secret roles encourage accusation, defense, and surprising switches in momentum as the tunnel grows or collapses.

### Quick-start outline for Saboteur
1) Set up the table with the starting point and treasure cards. 2) Deal a role card to each player (some become saboteurs, some become miners). 3) On your turn, you draw a card and place a path or an action card. 4) Your objective is to see a continuous path from the start to the treasure, or to be the saboteur who cleverly sabotages that path without being immediately exposed. 5) When a path reaches a treasure or when a saboteur’s plan comes to a dramatic, well-timed end, tally gold tokens and declare who wins.

### Quick-start outline for Saboteur 2
Saboteur 2 introduces more roles and circumstances. The core mechanic remains similar—build the path and misdirect—but you now have additional options, alternate victory conditions, and more opportunities for dramatic reveals. The key shift is that you’ll sometimes need to align (or at least tolerate) a different subset of players to achieve a shared goal before pivoting to a more selfish endgame. The result is a more dynamic experience that rewards adaptation and social reading as much as card management.

## Care tips: keeping Saboteur fresh for years to come
- Store the set in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight to prevent color fade and cardboard warping. 
- If you’ve got a box insert, use it; if not, consider a small fabric organizer to avoid the cards jingling around like a pocketful of coins during transport. 
- Sleeve the cards, especially if you play with kids or snack-happy groups. Sleeves protect the faces from stains and edge wear that can chip away at long-term readability. 
- Keep the rulebook intact; the Saboteur rules are not monstrous, but a quick refresher before hosting a new group saves a lot of “who is allowed to do what again?” confusions.
- Clean your cards occasionally with a dry cloth if there’s dust accumulation; avoid water or liquids that can warp cards or smear symbols.
