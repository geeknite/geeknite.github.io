## How to Play: A Quick-Look Overview
### Core mechanics in play
Throw Throw Burrito is a push-your-luck, dodge-and-dodge-some-more kind of game. The Extreme Outdoor Edition keeps that DNA intact but scales up the chaos. The general flow:
- Each player has a hand of action cards and a few “defense” options (think “protect your burrito,” “steal a burrito from a pal,” or “swap the burritos”).
- A number of “target zones” are set up around the playing area. The object is to avoid getting hit by someone’s burrito while trying to hit other players’ burritos in those zones.
- If a burrito hits a card (or a player’s body, depending on how risky you want to be), you trigger a reaction from the deck: draw cards, perform actions, or face the dreaded penalty.
- The round ends when a player hits their own burrito into the space of an opponent or when a specific event card dictates a meltdown. The winner is the one who survives longer than the others while still keeping their dignity intact.

In all this, a strong emphasis is placed on movement and spatial awareness. You’ll sprint, dodge, and dive into the kind of improvisational comedy that only safely executed chaos can deliver. Don’t worry if your cardio isn’t up to par; part of the fun is the spectacle of people trying to outrun a plush burrito while simultaneously trying to act cool about it.

### Differences from the indoor version and the classic Exploding Kittens crossover
Outdoor versus indoor changes are more about the margins than the core rules. Here are a few notable shifts:
- Radius and space: you’ll need more room. The box says “outdoor edition,” but your living room can still accommodate a scaled-down version with care.
- Weather readiness: cards are slightly glossy, burritos are plush, and you’re more likely to encounter a breeze that tries to steal your burritos away. Adjust play by wind conditions—if you’re in a windy park, anchor mats or pick a sheltered corner.
- Rule variance: some outdoor editions add “zone control” cards that encourage players to occupy safe spots while others attempt daring throws. These elements ramp up the playful competition without turning the game into a sport you actually practice.

## Outdoor Edition: Safety, Setup, and Space
### Safety first, always
The Extreme Outdoor Edition doesn’t pretend you’re going to operate like a pro athlete. It’s a party game with a cardio side quest, so safety remains the top priority:
- Clear the play area of sharp objects and trip hazards. Grass is forgiving; gravel is not.
- Use soft, plush burritos that won’t scratch or harm anyone. If you’re worried about a high-speed ricochet to the nose, slow the toss or swap to a safer target.
- Keep beverages away from the play zone. We know you want a real cold one after sprinting, but nobody wants to dirty their cards with sticky summer diplomacy.

### Weather and wind: don’t let nature turn your game into a slapstick tragedy
Wind is the silent competitor here. A light breeze can turn a dramatic throw into a wayward arc that ends up in a neighbor’s yard or in someone’s soda. Plan ahead: choose a windbreak or set up near a sturdy fence or tree line. If the wind becomes a factor too often, we recommend a quick pause, a reset, and a strategic decision: do you relocate, or do you commit to the “weatherproof” variant where every throw is a dramatic arc that lands in your own beverage cup?

### Space matters, but not in the way you think
The rule of thumb here is simple: more space equals more chaos. If you’re playing in a small backyard, you’ll want to mark two to three risk zones and rotate turns quickly to avoid static chaos. In a park or open lawn, you can widen the field and let the burritos cruise through the air with the grace of a confused helicopter. And yes, this is exactly what your neighbors imagine you’re doing with your weekend.

The best players rotate their resource usage with the tempo of the game: save your “dodge” and “swap” cards for big moments, and keep a couple of low-stakes cards handy for early rounds to establish control without over-exposing your fleet of burritos.

## Tips, House Rules, and Variations
### Quick-start variant for the impatient crowd
- Pick two safe zones far apart from each other.
- Each player must perform one “dodge” or “guard” action per round before throwing.
- Limit throws to a 3-throw-per-turn maximum to prevent chaos from becoming a comedy of errors.
- Use a timer (60 seconds per round) to inject rhythm and prevent marathon sessions that turn into sun-soaked epics.

### Cheating the heat with the “shaded refuge” rule
If the sun is punishing, designate a shaded play area where players must stay within a certain boundary. It doesn’t reduce the chaos; it preserves your sunscreen budget and keeps the game moving when sweat starts to blur the cards.

### Championship mode for seasoned chaos engineers
Create a tournament bracket: the winner of each mini-round advances to a final face-off, the loser cheers with wildly caffeinated enthusiasm. It’s basically a sports league for people who still respect the burrito as a noble weapon.

## Maintenance and Storage
When the sun goes down, the fun doesn’t have to vanish. Store the burritos and cards in the weather-proof tote. Keep the boundary markers clean and dry, and consider a quick wipe-down after sun exposure to maintain plush integrity. If wind loses interest in the game, a gentle nudge from a player in a hoodie is a perfectly acceptable “maintenance” ritual.
