## What is the World's Smallest Skip-Bo Card Game?

## How to Play: Tiny Rules, Big Possibilities

The core loop remains: build piles from 1 to 12 using your hand cards and the stock piles, aiming to be the first to clear your stock. The tiny version preserves the alternating rhythm of draw, play, and discard but condenses the table space so you’ll be playing with elbows tucked in like a crab on a sunbeam.

### Setup
1. Each player receives a small number of stock cards. The rest form a draw pile.
2. Build piles are created in the center, and each player starts with a handful of hand cards. The number of cards in hand scales down with the micro format, but the pace of decision-making remains delightfully snappy.
3. Determine play order as you would in the full-size game—toss a tiny die, or simply decide with a show of hands and a chorus of groans.

### Turn Structure
On your turn, you look at your hand and (if you’re lucky) place one or more cards onto the building piles in ascending order. If you can’t make a legal move, you draw from the stock or the draw pile until you can. It’s classic Skip-Bo pacing: fast enough to stay exciting but slow enough to let your inner strategic genius whisper, “Yes, you, the master of micro-mulligans.”

### Winning the Game
The winner is whoever empties their stock pile first. In the micro-version, you’ll experience that same triumphant rush when you slide the last tiny card into the final build pile, triggering a confetti cannon of your own imagination. The joy of victory comes with a tiny flourish—maybe a victory jig that is half proud, half suspicious of how much you’ve enjoyed a card game that’s the size of your wallet’s change slot.

## Practical Tips and House Rules for Tiny-Tabletop Play
- Add a “micro-mulligan” rule: each player may toss one card back into the hand if the initial draw is catastrophically unhelpful.
- Use a tiny timer (30 seconds) for each turn to keep the pace zippy and the suspense high.
- Create “tiny-talent” challenges: if a player can complete a build sequence in exactly three moves, everyone must clap like a simulated crowd. It’s silly, it’s fun, and it keeps the vibe light.
- For a longer session, rotate players and keep score in a compact notebook—this ensures your little battlefield has real stakes.
