## What’s in the Box?

The real treasure of any Zombicide expansion is not just the miniatures but the story that the miniatures tell before you pop them onto the board. The Lost Zombivors Box Set 7 that we’re looking at today is sold as a subset of the larger Lost Zombivors line, and this particular set sells itself with the phrase unused as though it’s a badge of honor rather than a failed attempt to move inventory from the warehouse to the living room. Inside, you’ll typically find a handful of zombie tokens, survivors, and perhaps a couple of specialized miniatures designed to stand out from the standard sculpts. In this Set 7, the “unused” moniker implies that the pieces may be in mint condition, never punched, never painted, and possibly still in their plastic sleeves like a snooty museum piece.

In practice, what you get is a mixture of zombie figures and survivors that can be plopped straight onto the table with minimal prep. The miniatures are not mass-produced in a way that makes you squint and say, “Did the cast forget to finish the eyes?” Instead, the sculpts typically show crisp lines, well-defined gear, and enough personality to differentiate survivors from the walkers without needing a degree in cross-referencing piece numbers. The paint jobs in factory sets can vary, but with “unused,” you’re more likely than not to find pieces that are clean, sharp-edged, and not smeared with a mysterious brown goop that some people mistake for farmer’s market salsa.

One thing we love about boxes like these is the little sense of mystery. ## Minis and Material Quality

### The Survivors

Survivors in Zombicide sets are the heart of the game’s narrative. They’re the plucky folks who have to decide whether to stay and fight or sprint to a helicopter that never actually exists in your basement. In Set 7, the Lost Zombivors survivors are sculpted with enough personality to tell you, “I am a person who has seen some stuff” without requiring a novella about their life choices. The sculpts tend to be a little more dynamic than the vanilla survivors you get with base sets, which is a win for people who want their table to look as lively as their fantasies.

The details on the armor, the weapons, and the little accessories are well-defined. The guns, melee weapons, and gadgets look distinct enough to tell apart on the table, which saves you from the inevitable moment of confusion where two similarly-named survivors both want to do the same action and you have to squint at their bases to figure it out. Yes, you can magnetize these for storage and swap their weapons, but we’ll get to that in the next section. If you’re a painter, you’ll appreciate the opportunity to give each character a distinct color scheme that matches your table’s theme instead of painting every survivor in a single uniform shade of “tomato soup brown.”

### The Zombies

The zombie sculpts in Lost Zombivors Set 7 are the real bread and brains. They’re not the fully grown, hulking super-zombies you see in some expansions, but they’re not runty either. They walk that fine line between “standard stock walker” and “scary enough to make you reconsider your strategy.” The poses are varied: some walkers shuffle, some lunge, and a few appear to be attempting a tragic interpretive dance with their own weapons. This variety is perfect for gameplay as you can create a more cinematic feel on the board when the bad guys have different gait patterns—one walker might appear to have learned to moonwalk, which is simultaneously hilarious and terrifying.

If you plan to paint, the details hold up under base coats and washes. The skin tones, the ripped clothing, and the clothing textures all come alive with a little airbrush work or a careful brush approach. The “unused” label gives you a clean palette, so you’re not dealing with paint corrosion or factory excess paint in the crevices. In short, these minis are a solid starting point for a painting session, or they’re a convenient, put-them-down-and-play option if you’re more into actual gaming than the hobby’s more elaborate aspects.

## Storage, Handling, and Modding Potential

### How to Store Them

The “unused” label pairs nicely with the typical scenario of “you haven’t punched the punch boards yet, so everything is pristine.” If you’re a careful organizer, you can preserve the mint condition by keeping the box closed, slipping the pieces into small bins with little separators, and labeling the trays according to unit type. This is particularly handy if you’re someone who rotates expansions in and out with frequency and likes to keep a tidy war room rather than a chaotic gaming lair. If you don’t want to keep them sealed, you can also magnetize the bases to drop them into a foam tray that slides into a rickety carrying case—this is a favorite technique for players who want to stage battles on the go without the risk of chipping a spear or bending a lance on the way to the table.

### Painting and Customization

The clean, unused status is a dream for painters who want to apply their signature style without fighting the box’s previous owner’s brushwork. Prepped minis with clean seams are a joy; you’ll save time on cleanup and can dive straight into prime, base coat, wash, and highlights. There’s something satisfying about painting characters that are not already battle-worn; it gives you the creative freedom to establish a color palette that matches your table and your mood. If you’re into conversion work, these sets offer a blank canvas for reposing zombies into dynamic poses or trimming off extra bits to enhance their silhouettes. For conversion wizards, the box becomes an atelier where your imagination can sprint and then rest a moment to catch its breath.

Let’s talk about the box itself as a prop. It’s not strictly a gaming component, but it functions as aesthetic furniture in your hobby space.
