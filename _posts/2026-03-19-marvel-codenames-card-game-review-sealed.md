## How to play Marvel Codenames Card Game: a quick overview

If you’ve played standard Codenames, the structure will feel familiar, though with a Marvel spin. The game is designed for two teams, with a spymaster for each side—the one who gives clues. The other players are guessers. The board in play (the table) contains a matrix of hero cards laid out for guessing. The spymaster’s job is to encode enough information to guide their team to several of their own agents on the table without leading them to the opponents’ agents, the neutral agents, or the dreaded assassin card. Here’s the skeleton of a turn, in typical Geeknite style:

- The spymaster gives a clue: a single word that ties together several heroes on the table, plus a number indicating how many cards on the table relate to that clue.
- The guessers confer and decide which hero cards on the table fit the clue. They pick cards one by one, trying to identify all and only their team’s agents.
- The moment a guess hits the table, the spymaster indicates whether it’s a correct agent (your team) or a wrong one (opponents’ agent, neutral, or the assassin). If it’s the assassin, the turn ends immediately and the team loses a point or loses the round, depending on house rules.
- The game continues, with turns alternating or continuing until a team has found all of its agents, or the table runs out of candidates.

What’s distinct in Marvel Codenames is that the “agents” on the table are Marvel characters. The clue options are still word-based, but the cultural shorthand around those characters becomes a big part of the experience. The synergy between clue-giving and hero recognition can create delicious moments of near-missilarity: a clue that perfectly ties together a handful of heroes, a misreading that triggers an enthusiastic but misplaced guess, or a spontaneous team joke that makes the room crack up and forget to calibrate the strategy. If you’re a Marvel fan, the flavor is a big plus; if you’re not, you’ll still get the mechanical satisfaction of a well-phrased clue that nudges your team toward victory.

## Strategy and tips: getting the most out of your clues

- Clue economy: The best clues are tight and precise. A word that unlocks multiple heroes without venturing into neighboring domains is worth gold. Remember that the bigger the number, the more cards your team is expected to guess; a high number is a risk-reward scenario. If your team is confident, a high-number clue can speed things along; if you’re uncertain, pick safer, smaller targets and let your teammates whittle down the choices.
- Thematic awareness: When clueing Marvel heroes, being mindful of recurring wordplay around the franchise helps. Some heroes are strongly associated with certain adjectival phrases or powers. If you can play to that mental model, you’ll unlock groups of heroes that your teammates are more likely to recognize.
- Reading opponents: In two-player or small-group versions, reading the other team’s fear is crucial. If you see your opponents leaning into a certain hero, you may be able to bluff with a broader or narrower clue that helps your team while steering the guessing away from the threats.
- The assassin factor: The game punishes blind guessing with the assassin card. A cautious approach to bridging clues to multiple heroes while not tipping off the enemy danger cards is a delicate dance. The more you play, the more you’ll get a feel for how much risk to take depending on the state of the board.
- Card stock and handling: Since you’re dealing with many hero cards, keeping them in good shape matters. The sealed box is appealing because it preserves condition; once opened, consider sleeves if your group tends to be a little aggressive with shuffling or heroic zoom-ins on the art.

- If you’re buying sealed: Inspect the outer box for any crushing or moisture, as that can compromise the cards inside. A dented corner isn’t the end of the world, but you want to avoid bent cards or sticky adhesives from moisture damage.
