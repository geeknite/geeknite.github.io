How to play: a practical, not-porny guide (with new flavor)
The rules are intentionally approachable, and you can run a standard session in roughly 45 to 90 minutes depending on play speed and how deep conversations go. Here’s the updated blueprint for a modern Geeknite table:
- Setup: Choose a starting month or have a player shuffle and reveal the first month. Establish explicit content boundaries, time limits, who answers, and who prompts. Create a clearly stated safe space before you begin.
- The turn structure: On a turn, a player draws a prompt card from the current month deck and reads it aloud. Other players respond according to the prompt. Some prompts call for a personal story, others require a tag-team mini-game, and a few simply ask for a consent-forward truth or dare.
- Scoring: The goal isn’t to win money or glory; it’s to earn communication kudos by being honest, respectful, and funny. You can assign points for consent-forward responses or use a simple laughter meter. In a casual night, you might skip points entirely and just let the zingers flow.
- Endgame: After exploring all 12 months, tally points (if you’re playing with scoring) or crown the night’s winners as the most curious and supportive team. The game suggests a Wrap-Up Month-style debrief to leave players with tangible takeaways—better communication skills, clearer boundaries, and a handful of new jokes to carry into tomorrow.

Two playstyles you can lean into: quick-start brisk mode and slow-burn narrative mode. Brisk mode trims prompts, uses shorter stories, and keeps the room buzzing; narrative mode invites longer anecdotes, deeper dives, and a few more teambuilding prompts. The facilitator’s job remains the same: read the room, nudge gently, and keep things moving when the vibe flatlines.

You can game the system a bit to suit your group. Want fewer awkward silences? Start with lighter months and ease into deeper topics. Hosting strangers or a mixed party? Use the skip-and-swap option and keep a few “opt-out” prompts ready for those moments when someone needs a breath or a boundary check.
