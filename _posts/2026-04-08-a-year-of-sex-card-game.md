It leans into humor and playful prompts rather than shock value, though you should be prepared for some cheeky prompts that could produce eye-rolls, blushes, and surprisingly insightful conversations. The core premise: 12 months, each with a deck of prompts, challenges, or mini-games designed to spark conversation about intimacy, boundaries, desires, and humor. It can be played as a couple, with a small group, or with a larger party in a “cruise-control” style where players rotate prompts and reactions.

The packaging promises sexy but not pornographic content, consent-forward design, and a dash of nerdy flavor that fits the Geeknite vibe: witty, slightly irreverent, and relentlessly practical about how people actually play games in real life (and at conventions, naturally).

## How to play: a practical, not-porny guide
The rules are intentionally approachable. You can run a standard game in about 45 to 90 minutes depending on play speed and how deep conversations go. Here’s the quick blueprint:
- Setup: Pick a starting month or let a player shuffle and reveal the first month. Decide on house rules—explicit content boundaries, time limits, who answers, and who prompts. You’ll want a safe space clearly established before you begin.
- The turn structure: On a turn, a player draws a prompt card from the current month deck and reads it aloud. The other players respond according to the prompt. Some prompts require a personal story, others require a tag-team mini-game, and a few simply ask for a truth or dare with a consent-based twist.
- Scoring: The goal isn’t to win money or glory; it’s to earn “communication kudos” by being honest, respectful, and funny. You can assign points per consent-forward response or use a simple laughter meter; in a casual night, you might not track points at all and just let the zingers roll.
- Endgame: After all 12 months have been explored, you tally up the points (if you’re playing with scoring) or simply crown the winners of the night in the most supportive, most curious way. The game suggests ending with a “Wrap-Up Month” style discussion that helps players debrief and leave the table with something tangible—better communication skills, a better sense of boundaries, or at least a bunch of good jokes.

You can game the system a bit if you want to speed things up or slow things down; that’s the beauty of party games—the rules are flexible enough to bend without breaking. If you’re playing with a group that hasn’t seen you in your most honest, unfiltered form, expect some awkward moments. This is part of the charm, and the game does a solid job of providing a safety net to keep things respectful while still letting the group giggle at the boundaries being tested.
