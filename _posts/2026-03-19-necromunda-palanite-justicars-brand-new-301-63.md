## H2: Painting and color schemes that pop without shouting at the referee

Painting Palanite Justicars is a delight because their aesthetic invites a disciplined color scheme without requiring you to paint 18 layers of edge highlights. Here are a few tried-and-true routes you can adapt:

- Classic enforcement blues and whites: a deep navy or military blue over neutrally toned armor plates, with white accents on belts and insignia. This creates a crisp, law-and-order vibe that looks professional on the table and in your gallery pictures.
- Sepia Underhive vibe: more brown-toned armor with warmer highlights that evoke old-school law enforcement photos taken during a power outage. It hides grime well and looks gritty in a way that suits the Necromunda mood.
- High-contrast authority: black armor with electric blue or vivid red trim. This is the “I mean business” look: dramatic, bold, and gorgeous under properly positioned lighting.
- The cameo paint job: a nod to classic sci-fi cinema with metallics and a little patina. If you want the minis to look like they’ve survived decades of huffs and gas leaks, this is the palette for you.

What helps here is that the Palanites’ armor panels often have clean lines and defined edges, which is a boon for speed-paint sessions. You can build up the base coats, midtones, and a couple of careful edge highlights without needing to carve out a full day’s worth of time. Use a subdued wash to bring out the depth in the armor panels, then a final dry brush to catch the edges for that crisp, magazine-cover look.

If you’re unsure about your scheme, a great trick is to pick a primary color and a secondary color that contrast nicely. For example, navy blue armor with pale amber trim or helmet accents. The contrast not only looks good on the table but also makes it much easier to identify which Palanite is which during a game—this is handy when you’re trying to run a disciplined squad in a city that seems to forget what order looks like.

For fans who like to push the “lore first” approach, consider integrating symbolics like a small palanite badge on a cape or a banner with a heraldic motif. You don’t need a full house-banner setup to pull this off; a single badge can carry the mood across your entire squad and tie your models into the broader Necromunda narrative with a subtle, grown-up touch.

## H2: Practical tips: assembly, basing, and storage

- Assemble in a clean workspace with a good lamp. The under-surface details can catch on your skin if you’re not careful, and you’ll want the full effect of any scuff marks or wear that tells your tale on the tabletop.
- Use a hobby knife to remove mold lines, then a small file or sanding pad to smooth the edges. The Palanites tend to have crisp lines, so you don’t want to sand away the very sharpness that gives them their crisp look on the table.
- For basing, a simple grit-sand base with a few grav-lift pebbles looks terrific and keeps the focus on the figures. If you prefer a more narrative base, you can add tiny bits of broken pipe, cracked signage, or a small piece of ruined barricade to anchor the “Underhive enforcement” vibe.
- Storage-wise, fit your Palanites into a magnetic tray or a dedicated mini-diorama box. The last thing you want is bent capes and misaligned poses after a carrier ride to your friendly local tournament.
