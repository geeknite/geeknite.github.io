## The Full Set: 10 Tiny Heroes
In this section we meet the lineup. Each figure stands about 2 inches tall, comes with a tiny display base, and is designed to pair with UNO cards as if the character themselves were somehow the wildcard suited for the game. Here are the ten members of the squad:

### 1) Izuku Midoriya (Deku)
Deku leads the charge with a bright green jumpsuit and a confident-but-still-student grin. The sculpt captures his scrappy energy, from the belt buckle to the scrunched knee pads. In the UNO world, Deku’s figure feels like you’re playing a red card with a burst of wind behind it—pure, hopeful chaos. The paint job is clean, with Deku’s iconic green and white hues, and a surprisingly expressive face given the tiny scale.

### 2) Katsuki Bakugo
Bakugo explodes onto the shelf with explosive shoulders and a scowl that practically screams, “I would win this game before you even draw.” The figure’s sculpt emphasizes his grenade-like gauntlets and spiky hair. In terms of UNO compatibility, Bakugo’s character energy translates to a “draw 4” attitude: you feel the urge to punish your opponents for their mistakes while admiring his confidence. The color palette punches hard—yellows and blacks with a hint of red that reads as danger, in the most cute-regret way possible.

### 3) Shoto Todoroki
A cool-headed literal ice-cold icon. Todoroki brings a dual-tone aesthetic—frosty white and molten red—perfect for a “color match” moment when you slap down a blue card and pretend you control both fire and ice, which you do not. The sculpt captures the duality vibe nicely: half of his hair is white, half is red, and the face expression sits between calm and quietly dramatic. Todoroki is the perfect centerpiece for those UNO matches that escalate into “let’s pretend we’re in a sports anime montage.”

### 4) Ochaco Uraraka
Uraraka’s figure radiates buoyant optimism, with a pose that looks like she just realized she can float your morale as well as your cards. The greens and pinks pop nicely, and the sculpt includes her gravity-themed hero gear in a compact form. Her presence on the table lightens the mood, which is good because UNO can get intense when a player is suspiciously calm about a countdown to zero.

### 5) Tenya Iida
Iida marches in with a disciplined stance and a look that says: “I am here to win with speed and strategic card-laying.” The miniature armor plating on his legs, the glint of his goggles, and the sleek blue-white color scheme communicate “team captain” energy. On the UNO table, Iida is the reminder that sometimes you want to play with a plan, not just reckless color-changes.

### 6) Tsuyu Asui
Frog-themed hero Tsuyu brings a playful, no-nonsense vibe. Her figure sports a dynamic pose that suggests she’s leaping into action—perfect for the moment you dramatically slam down a wild card and declare an epic victory. The paintwork is clean, and the character’s wide eyes are captured with simple, effective sculpting. If you’re trying to make your UNO table look more like a fun anime diorama, Tsuyu is your amphibian ambassador.

### 7) Eijiro Kirishima
Kirishima stands tough, with blocky shoulders and the rock-hard vibe of his hardening ability. The figure’s red tones and angular lines give him a “brick wall against bad hands” appeal. In UNO terms, Kirishima is your defense—he reminds you that sometimes you need to brace for a “draw 2” storm and still keep your cool.

### 8) Momo Yaoyorozu
Momo’s figure brings a poised, bottle-of-confidence energy. Her designer bag and elegant pose speak to intelligence and resourcefulness—traits you want when you’re calculating the odds of which color will come up next. Yaoyorozu’s color scheme leans purple and cream, which makes her stand out on a rainbow-stacked table and adds a touch of “boardroom strategist turns casual gamer.”

### 9) Fumikage Tokoyami
Tokoyami’s raven-themed look makes him one of the more striking miniatures. The figurine captures the birdlike silhouette and the dramatic cape, giving you a mysterious, “I know a secret about the UNO deck” vibe. His presence adds a dash of nocturnal cool to the table—perfect for late-night game sessions where your brain is a little foggy but your will to win is loud.

### 10) All Might
The old-school icon of heroism, All Might, is the capstone of the set. He’s bright, bold, and impossibly cheerful—the kind of card that tells your opponents: “I might be smiling, but I am absolutely going to win this round.” The figure channels a classic pose, with a big grin and a cape-like aura that makes the entire shelf look like a victory parade. If Deku is the heart, All Might is the hype train you strap to your UNO strategy.

The ten-figure lineup is designed to be displayed together, forming a mini-arc that echoes MHA’s cast evolution. If you’re into display aesthetics, you’ll want to line them up with the UNO deck in front, letting the cards’ colors echo across the heroes like an anime montage cue.

## Design, Build, and Display: Are They Worth It?
Here’s the practical breakdown you care about: build quality, display potential, and whether the fun pays for itself. The figures are small, which means tiny hands can beach them across the table with minimal risk of major hazard. The plastic feels durable enough to survive occasional shelf drops, though a well-aimed “oops” from a toddler may still create a dented moral victory. Paint apps are clean for the most part; you’ll see a few minor specks on rare models, but nothing that ruins the overall aesthetic or the recognizable character silhouettes.

Display wise, the set shines. The ten figures look cohesive on a shelf, with a shared art direction that keeps the anime vibe in line with UNO’s bright color palette. If you’re a desk collector or a display fiend, this is a set you’ll happily rotate in and out, arranging them by color, by hero arc, or by “which character would absolutely trounce me at UNO right now.”

Playability-wise, the UNO deck is standard-issue with a My Hero Academia twist: the color scheme and card illustrations lean into the anime visuals. The cards themselves feel sturdy enough for regular play; they aren’t premium casino-grade, but they hold up to weekly family game nights and the occasional rage-quit when someone misreads a +4 as a prank card and forgets that the deck hates them. If you want to mix trivia or “what hero would you choose for a given color,” you’ll add spice to the match outcomes. The synergy between the figures and UNO cards is mostly thematic, but it’s enough to make the set a cozier partner for your gaming table rather than a mere display piece.

Value-wise, it’s a toss-up depending on your expectations. If you want a complete, camera-ready display for your shelf and you also enjoy UNO, then you’re getting two products in one purchase: the mini-figures and a usable UNO deck. If your main goal is to collect rare or exclusive McDonald’s items, this set is a mainstream tie-in that lands in that sweet spot of fun and accessibility. It’s not a museum-grade rarity, but it’s something you’ll reach for during family game night and not regret. The packaging, by the way, is compact and colorful, designed to catch the eye of a kid—but it also photographs well for adult collectors who still post their finds on social media with that “look what I scored” glaze.
