## Design and Build

The core concept is deceptively simple: two robust work lines, a shared channel for cables, and enough legroom to pretend you are a grownup who respects space and privacy. The oak veneer top is both timeless and practical—dings and scuffs vanish more gracefully than your last manager's motivational poster. The black metal frames give the whole thing a sense of gravity, as if it could anchor a small town.

The dimensions tell a story in themselves: 9000 x 1530 x 730 mm translates to roughly 9 meters of continuous working surface, 1.53 meters of depth, and a desk height of 730 mm which is within typical ergonomic range. At that length, you could host a company stand-up meeting without leaving your seat, or you could stage a tiny conga line as a team-building exercise. In practice, that length is best measured not just in millimeters but in the number of power strips and network drops you’ll need to keep everything humming.

Aesthetic notes could fill chapters, but I’ll summarize: oak veneer that reads warm under artificial light, with black powder-coated steel legs that resist the temptation to vanish in the background. The contrast is not merely decorative; it's functional: the darker legs hide scuffs from chair wheels, and the lighter top reflects a mood of productivity rather than pure industrial efficiency.

Ok, now to the nitty-gritty: materials and durability. The top is typically engineered wood with a real-wood veneer; edges are sealed to resist moisture and coffee, because you will spill coffee on something expensive sooner or later. The frames use a combination of steel and perhaps a forged connector plate that helps you align your boards with surgical precision. The overall build is heavy—not surprising for something designed to host ten people, a system of monitors, and a mirror or two for those heart-to-heart moments in the break room.

### Layout options and modularity

One of the selling points here is modularity. The arrangement allows you to set two distinct work zones while still sharing a common cable spine in the middle. It's a thoughtful compromise between collaboration and privacy: your five-person cluster can share references, swap sticky notes, and maintain a friendly sense of personal space. If you want to reconfigure the space for a different team size, you can adjust the partitions, slide the bays closer or apart, and realign the cable trays to minimize spaghetti volcanoes behind your screens.

Cable management is crucial. The product includes channels or troughs along the spine where power, data, and audio cables can be tucked away. The goal is to reduce tripping hazards and the occasional 'why is my mouse 13 inches away?' moment. A well-executed cable plan not only looks tidy but also reduces the load on your IT department when someone wants to hang a monitor from a questionable mount at the last minute.

### Assembly and setup

Let's be honest: if you hired ten people to assemble this, you'd likely get eleven opinions and a heated debate about walkways. The manufacturer claims straightforward assembly with standard tools. In reality, you'll want at least two capable teammates who understand the difference between a washer and a spacer, and who can manage a level with the precision of a NASA rocket operator. Expect a couple of hours of assembly for the core units, plus extra time for seating alignment, monitor arm mounting choices, and the inevitable repositioning after you realize you placed the personal protective equipment cabinet too far from the power strip.

### Ergonomics and user experience

Ergonomics matter more in a 10-person bench than in a single-desk setup. Each workstation assumes a chair with adjustable height, depth, and back support. The 730 mm height is in the ballpark for most adults; you'll want to confirm the seat height and monitor at each station to keep eyes from drifting toward the ceiling. A shared central spine can be a blessing or a curse depending on how you plan to route cables and how strict your IT policy is about power usage.

### Aesthetics and environmental impact

The oak blk finish is a statement. It speaks to a work environment that wants to be serious but not clinical. It can anchor an open-plan office while maintaining an executive vibe. From a sustainability standpoint, you'll want to verify what 'oak' veneer means in terms of source, finish, and lifecycle. Geeknite readers appreciate transparency, so you should check with the vendor about the environmental certifications and the ability to recirculate or recycle the components at end of life.

### Comparison with alternatives

If you're not sold on the Infinite, there are alternatives: a pair of five-person benches, or a single massive 10-person bench with integrated partitions. The two-workstation configuration could be ideal for a design studio that wants to keep collaboration going while ensuring a clear path to individual work. The main drawback is space: at 9 meters long, you'll need a large, well-lit room. If your floor plan resembles a shoebox, you may want to consider two smaller modules to avoid the awkward 'I'm screaming across the table to hear you' syndrome.

### Maintenance and care

The oak veneer finish is durable but not impervious. Wipe with a damp microfiber cloth and avoid harsh cleaners that can strip the finish. For cable channels, use cable ties and/or Velcro strips to keep things tidy; you'll thank yourself when you're unpacking a server upgrade and don't stumble into a tangle of HDMI cables. The metal frames will benefit from occasional wipe-downs to remove fingerprints and dust, especially if you have large windows that bring in daylight and reveal all your desk-grime sins.
