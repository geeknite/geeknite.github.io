### How to Play: Quick Start Guide
The beauty of these games is in their simplicity. The quick-start guide is a blessing if you’ve ever worried about meta games or overly complicated rule sets. The basics:

- Go Fish: Ask for cards to form sets; if you’re told to go fish, you take a card from the random draw pile. The goal is to collect sets of four (or more, depending on the variant you like).
- Crazy Eights: Players discard cards by matching rank or suit; eights are wild and open new possibilities.
- Old Maid: Players try to form pairs and avoid ending up with the single Old Maid card at the end.
- Slapjack: When a Jack appears on the table, players race to slap the pile; the winner takes all the cards.
- Memory: Flip over cards to find pairs; memory matters, but luck plays a strong role too.

The rules are consistent across editions, though minor tweaks appear in some batch variants. You’ll probably end up inventing a house rule within the first five minutes anyway—this is where the social joy of card games shines.

### Play Sessions: Getting the Most Out of It
- Quick turnover rounds: Set a timer; each player gets 60 seconds for their turn. The chaos is half the fun.
- The host rotation: A different host can call out “Uno rules” or “new round” to keep energy high.
- Snack synergy: Pair rounds with snacks; you’ll be less likely to cheat at memory if you’re hands are full of popcorn.
- House rules that work: If Go Fish becomes too long, reduce the number of cards per hand. If Slapjack gets too loud, designate a “slap zone” away from the TV.

The result is a lively social dynamic that keeps people engaged without turning the night into a full marathon of chess-level planning.
