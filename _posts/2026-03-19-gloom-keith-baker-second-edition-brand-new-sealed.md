## What is Gloom? A brisk primer for the curious and the courageous
Gloom is a storytelling card game designed by Keith Baker and published by Atlas Games (later reissued in the second edition). The premise is delightfully morbid: each player takes control of a family and tries to make their characters as miserable as possible, all while trying to keep the rival families cheerful. The core twist is that your gloom cards (which carry negative numbers) stack on characters, and you want your family to accumulate as much gloom as possible while the other families accumulate less. The catch: you weave micro-stories on each card to influence how gloom is assigned, making each turn a mini-satire of your own life choices. It’s not just a game about numbers; it’s a game about crafting a saga of unfortunate events with your friends as your co-authors and victims.

The second edition streamlines the rules, updates some art, and—most importantly—keeps that signature translucent card aesthetic that makes Gloom so distinctive. The original Gloom was built around transparent plastic cards that you could layer atop one another like the most morbidly clever stained glass. The second edition keeps that vibe while polishing the print quality and design so even a casual player can tell the gloom from the gloomier gloom. The result is a package that feels both retro and modern, like a steampunk laptop that still runs Windows 95 with a charmingly stubborn persistence.

## How to play in a hurry: a quick guide to the chaos
Gloom’s turn structure is elegant in its simplicity. No heavy apps, no complicated app-based rule checks, just you and the story you’re about to ruin. Here’s a quick overview to whet your appetite (and perhaps whet your glass of dark ale while you pretend to be responsible for your own tragedy).

### Setup
- Each player chooses a family and takes the corresponding family cards. You’ll place gloom cards and story cards within easy reach. The rest of the deck is shuffled and placed face-down as the draw pile.
- Each player receives a set of modifiers: little positive and negative cards that can be played to alter the gloom of a character. The artful fusions of doom begin with a whispered promise of misfortune and a roll of fate that is not there because the game uses cards, not dice, to tell its tale.
- You can opt to use the “storytelling” variant from the rulebook to add a layer of narrative spice. If you’re new to the game, start with the standard variant and only gradually introduce the fancy storytelling constraints as your group becomes more comfortable with the gloom-sandwich.

### Turn structure
- Draw phase: players draw a card. This card could be a story card or a gloom card; your mileage may vary depending on the mood of the room.
- Play phase: you can attach gloom modifiers to your own characters or to others (with a fair amount of taunting allowed). The trick is to craft tales that justify the gloom in the most dramatic way possible.
- Story phase: you create a short micro-story for the card you played, sometimes linking it to other cards. Here is where the game becomes a storytelling exercise masquerading as a grim card game.
- End phase: you discard down to your hand limit and pass the turn to the next player.

### Scoring and end game
The end goal in Gloom is to minimize your characters’ happiness—no wait, to maximize gloom on your own characters and minimize gloom on your opponents’ characters. The exact numeric rules can be fiddly if you overthink it, which is perfectly in line with how some of us live our lives. In practical terms, you want to end the game with your family’s total gloom as low as possible once all other players’ families have met their doom. The person with the most gloom at the end can call the last round and hope their own family gets a mercy kill by the closing bell. The exact math is less important than the storytelling and the laughs you generate along the way.

#### A note on two-player modes
Gloom shines best with a table of players, but there are two-player variants that let you enjoy the misery with a partner or with a coach yelling at you from the other side of the table. The key is to keep the other players engaged in creating entertaining stories or you’ll just end up with quiet guilt and a lot of sighs.

## Strategy tips for maximum gloom and giggles
- Lean into narrative hooks: The best gloom stories are those that people remember because they’re outrageous, ridiculous, or painfully relatable. If you set up a character with a memorable personality trait and then attach a gloom card that aligns with a ridiculous scene, you’ll have your table laughing while your score climbs (or sinks) in the background.
- The layering trick: With translucent cards, you can create forced narratives by layering gloom and story cards. Use the stacking mechanic to reveal different aspects of a poor soul’s fate. The more dramatic and surprising, the better.
- Managing the endgame: Don’t reveal all of your gloom too early. Save a few painful plot twists for the final rounds to keep the table guessing and reduce the risk that someone else steals your thunder.
- Interaction with other players: Gloom thrives on social dynamics. Teasing, storytelling, and a dash of friendly sabotage can turn a quiet table into a theater of gloom. If you’re worried about ruining friendships, consider a short, one-liner per turn to maintain a lightweight vibe.
