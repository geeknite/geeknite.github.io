## Overview
Sushi Go is a light drafting game where players pass a hand of cards around the table while selecting one card before passing the rest to the neighbor. The hands slide around the table until each player has drafted a full set of cards from the original dish lineup. The scoring is determined by a handful of classic sushi themes: nigiri combos, maki rolls, sashimi, and a few surprise surprise cards that interrupt the flow with little surprises. The 2 to 5 player version is the portable dream that fits on any coffee table, dorm desk, or the tiny tray of a spaceship’s captain. The Gamewright edition keeps the color palette punchy and the iconography crisp so even a distracted can glance up and know exactly what they are trying to aim for on a given turn.

## How to Play: Quick Start Guide
If you want a truly compact version of this guide, here it goes. Each player starts with a hand of cards. Everyone secretly chooses one card from their hand and places it face down. On the count of three, all players reveal their chosen cards and simultaneously swap the remainder to the next player in a cyclic order. The chosen card is set aside in your personal display and will contribute to your final scoring at the end. Then you pass the rest to the left (or right if you prefer a mirrored play style) and repeat until all cards are consumed. The scoring stage calculates points from the various target patterns the cards create when combined in your collection. The person with the most points wins. Simple, fast, and delightfully addictive, as far as addictive goes without dealing with actual addiction. And no, you cannot steal the next person’s sushi through psionic mind-meld—yet.

### The Pick and Pass Mechanic
The core mechanic of Sushi Go is the pick and pass. It is the elegant simplification of drafting that makes the game approachable for newcomers while still offering delightful decisions for veterans. The reason the mechanic works is that every round you have to decide: do I collect a card that is great in isolation but less synergy with future cards, or do I gamble on something that will combo nicely with what I think other players will be building? The pass action means the table becomes a rotating matrix of possibilities, and your brain becomes a tiny office of risk assessment and improvisation. This is where the jokes about buffet math and sushi greediness arise; the fun is less about complex calculations and more about reading social cues and table talk. The lighthearted tension is a feature, not a bug. You will catch yourself exclaiming that a single card could net you a perfect sashimi line or a corner case of miso soup points that only pays off after you have also collected three dumplings. And yes, you will sometimes regret the one card you passed away just a moment too soon.

### Scoring and Card Types
Scoring in Sushi Go is a friendly mix of cardio and strategy. The typical card types include nigiri, maki rolls, sashimi, mochi, and a few special gain cards that can tilt the balance in key moments. Nigiri cards are the quick burst of points you chase early in the round, while maki rolls create a more long-run objective that rewards the collector with the largest stack. Sashimi rewards are a neat three of a kind that are easy to monitor and hard to misplace. Gamewright has kept the scoring approachable, with a compact reference that sits in the corner of the table to remind players that hey, you will get a few points for this pick, a few more for that combo, and a last burst at the end for good measure. The 2 to 5 player setup means you will see the distribution of charms shift with every round, which adds a delightful dynamic to what could have been a very simple scoring scheme.

## Complexity, Accessibility, and Replayability
Sushi Go is not a heavy game. It is designed to be accessible to new players, including families with children who want something a tad more strategic than a memory game. The learning curve is almost non existent, and the core mechanic is so elegant that you will find yourself explaining the concept in less than a minute. The replayability comes from the variability of the cards that appear in each round and how players choose to optimize their picks under time pressure. In a sense, Sushi Go is a microcosm of strategic thinking: every choice matters, but no single decision is existential. You can play a dozen rounds and still feel a spark of newness because the outcomes are influenced by the behavior of other players as much as by the cards themselves.

For players who care about deeper strategy, there are subtle elements worth exploring. For instance, your decision on a given turn may be influenced by what you think your neighbor will do with their next pass. This creates a dynamic where you can bluff by passing a card you randomly suspect your neighbor desires, or you can tilt the board by choosing a card that denies others a critical piece for their planned combos. The fun here is that the depth emerges from social interaction and the natural rhythm of quick rounds rather than from an overly complex rule set.
