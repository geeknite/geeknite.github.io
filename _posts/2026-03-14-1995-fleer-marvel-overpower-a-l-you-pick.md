## What is OverPower, in a sentence and in a broom closet full of glitter
OverPower is a collectible card game where you assemble a team of Marvel heroes and villains and battle another player using a combination of numerical values and special abilities. Each card has a name, a class (e.g., Hero, Villain, Mastermind), a team affiliation, and a set of powers that often require you to roll the big two six-sided dice (the dice were a big deal back then; dice-owned by kids and their parents alike, probably).

The core idea: you attempt to reduce your opponent’s “Endurance” or “Stamina” to zero while managing your own team’s health, powers, and resource tokens (which, in the Great 90s Spirit, were often called “Power” or “Energy”). The art style felt like someone took the Marvel Universe, sprinkled in a dash of neon, and then asked artists to capture the essence of a character in one frame while the game’s rules did their best to justify why that frame mattered in a skirmish between two people wearing glasses with tape on the bridge.

## Why the A-L singles matter (and what the heck A-L even means here)
A-L singles refer to characters whose first names (or common aliases) fall between A and L in the alphabet. It’s not a strict law of the universe, but it’s a neat lens to look at a subset of cards that often balanced iconic heroes with the occasional wild-card villain. In practice, this means you had to weigh choices like: do you want a character with pure martial chops backed by a solid “Energy/Flight” toolkit, or do you prefer someone with a flashy single-use ability that can swing a game if timed perfectly? The A-L pool tends to feature the bread-and-butter heroes that casual players recognized, plus a few surprise picks that showed up in the occasional “how did this card survive the design phase?” moments.

Here are some archetypal picks you could land from A-L (and what they typically brought to the table in the era’s design language):

- Ant-Man (Hank Pym / Scott Lang) — Masters of shrinking, growing, and flurries of micro-skirmish tricks. In OverPower, Ant-Man usually offered a balance of utility and goofily satisfying combat turns that rewarded players who appreciated timing more than brute force.
- Black Panther — A blend of tactical prowess, tech-assisted attacks, and a knack for “getting out of tight spots with style.” Panther cards often functioned as reliable all-rounders who could keep pace with more flashy bruisers.
- Black Widow — Stealth and precision. Widow-type heroes in card form often carried the ability to circumvent straightforward block-kill sequences and force misdirections that bugged beginners but delighted veterans.
- Captain America — The classic beatstick with leadership vibes. Cap cards frequently carried a “team up” flavor, enabling your squad to work in concert in ways that felt almost chess-like in practice.
- Daredevil — Acute street-level prowess, often with special rules that rewarded clever misdirection and agility rather than raw power. Daredevil cards could be punishing to over-commit chloride-level players who forgot to respect the stealth tag.
- Elektra — A killer for the tempo game; Elektra cards could swing a match by pressuring a slower foe with relentless strikes and risky gambles that paid off in the right hands.
- Fantastic Four roster entries (like Mister Fantastic) often offered puzzle-like combos with the right allies, turning battles into logic games you could win with patience.
- Incredible Hulk — Messy, loud, and absolutely satisfying when it lands. Hulk cards were your “feel the power” moment—big numbers, big impact, and a risk-reward dynamic that could turn games on a single over-the-top strike.
- Iron Man — Tech-heavy build-arounds; Tony Stark’s armor provided consistent value through a blend of ranged power and gadget-driven tricks.
- Loki, Luke Cage, and other “L” picks — A mix of trickster vibes and street-level grit that could complicate an opponent’s plans in the best 90s way possible.

It’s important to note: each card in this universe came with its own flavor text, art, and power distribution that could dramatically alter how you played your team. The power of the A-L pool wasn’t just about raw numbers; it was about synergy, tempo, and whether you were playing for a big swing or a steady drumbeat of advantages.

## How to build from A-L: strategies you could actually use (in a time before blogs explained it all)
If you’re going to pick from A-L singles, you’re seldom just picking your favorites. You’re picking a deck’s spine. Here are practical strategies you could deploy, even if you were a kid trading with friends in a school cafeteria:

- Tempo via the two-dice engine: OverPower‑style battles leaned on dice, so you didn’t just want a string of high-attack cards; you wanted a few that could reliably enable your bigger plays on subsequent turns. Look for heroes whose abilities scale with the number of dice you roll or the number of allies you have on the board.
- The captain’s call: Teams built around Captain America often benefited from leadership-type abilities that let you pull more of your library into the field or protect weaker teammates. If your local meta loved a slow-burn approach, Cap cards could be your glue.
- Stealth and misdirection: If you found yourself facing big hitters, a Daredevil or Black Widow pick could help you dodge direct peeks and push the match into a more tactical phase. It’s all about forcing your opponent to react rather than letting them dictate the tempo.
- The tech edge: Iron Man and similar tech-forward picks gave you options to out-think your opponent with gadgets and armor-based buffs. It was less about pure punch and more about cleaning up the battlefield with gadget synergy.
- The big swing: Hulk and a few other powerhouses could swing the game in one or two turns if you aligned your power cards and your timing, which often meant you learned to count the number of turns left in your opponent’s lead and strike just as they misstep.

Sample deck archetypes from A-L could look like this:
- Tempo bruiser + stealth support: Daredevil + Black Widow + Ant-Man in a shell where stealth cards enable big hits on the opponent’s vulnerable blockers.
- Tech-mech control: Iron Man + Captain America + Black Panther to weave team-based boosts with gadget-driven disposals, forcing your opponent into missteps.
- The overpowering Hulk shell: Hulk paired with a few mid-range brawlers to pace the game while the big green threat looms as your late-game finisher.

The key here is to think about the matchups you’re likely to face. If your playgroup leaned toward “keep swinging until something sticks,” you’d want sturdier frontline types and a couple of safe enablers. If your playgroup loved control and tempo games, you’d lean into stealth, misdirection, and quick patchwork combos that let you threaten multiple win conditions on a single turn.

## Collectibility, rarity, and the value of A-L singles
For collectors, the A-L subset often holds a sweet spot: it includes beloved icons people wanted on their teams and a handful of less common variants that make for exciting pulls. In the mid-90s, the printing runs weren’t as massive as modern collectible sets, which could make even base cards somewhat scarce, depending on the subset. Foil options, misprints, and chase version cards add a layer of value that isn’t purely tied to power level. The joy of opening packs and discovering a rare A-L hero or a foil variant was a joy that transcended power calculations; it was about the thrill of the chase, the possibility of a trade, and the memory of staying up late with friends to plan the perfect deck strategy.

If you’re cataloging or pricing now, consider: condition matters more for the rare and foil cards; the popularity of certain characters in the broader Marvel canon can also nudge value upward; and the condition of the original packaging can impact long-term desirability. The rule of thumb then remains: collect what makes you smile, not just what makes your binder heavier.

## The rules refresher: a quick tour for anyone who’s hopped back in the time machine
If you’re picking this up again after decades, a quick refresher on what makes OverPower tick is helpful. The game is typically played with two players. Each player builds a team of heroes or villains from their chosen pool, taking into account the card’s powers, endurance values, and any special conditions. The objective is to deplete your opponent’s endurance to zero. You accomplish this by a mix of direct attacks, special abilities, and sometimes team-based effects that trigger when certain allies are on the field together. Many cards feature two primary modes of use: a standard combat mode and a special power mode that requires you to spend or roll tokens or dice to unlock a more powerful effect.

The A-L set often relies on: solid stat lines to ensure bulk; a handful of high-impact abilities that require timing; and defensive options that help you weather early aggression while you set up your later turns. A player who appreciates resource management—judging when to hold a powerful card for a turn when it matters most—could outplay someone with bigger numbers but fewer timing tricks.

## A closer look at a few representative A-L cards and how they tended to perform
To give you a sense of the practicalities of selecting A-L singles, here are a few character archetypes and hypothetical power interactions you might have seen on a typical table:

- Ant-Man: Agile, quick to reposition, with small but reliable damage that could set up bigger combos. In practice, Ant-Man players learned to leverage position and timing rather than relying solely on brute force. A great pick for players who enjoy micro-control and outmaneuvering the opponent.
- Black Panther: A well-rounded option that didn’t scream “overkill” but could contribute both on offense and defense. Panthers often served as the deck’s backbone, providing synergy with teammates and maintaining board presence.
- Captain America: The team leader archetype—cards that provide buffs to allies, help you stabilize early game, and then convert board presence into a late-game advantage. Captain America players learned to sustain pressure without burning their resources too early.
- Daredevil: The tempo disruptor. Daredevil’s card could force the opponent to react, sometimes stifling their attempts to execute a bigger plan. In the hands of the patient player, Daredevil could outtime stronger opponents and tilt the match in your favor.
- Elektra: High-risk, high-reward. Elektra cards could punch above their weight class when you timed their most dangerous abilities perfectly. If you were the kind of player who enjoyed “gotcha” moments, Elektra was your ally.
- Loki and Loki-adjacent picks: Trickster-style cards that thrived on forcing strategic misreads. In A-L you could lean into deception and tactical deception, winning games more on misdirection than pure numbers.
- Luke Cage: A durable frontline option. Luke Cage players could soak hits while you built a plan around multi-turn strategies and avoid giving the opponent a clean path to finish you off in a single turn.

The takeaway here is not just about the character’s power value, but about how their toolkit matches your preferred tempo and your deck’s overall plan. A well-rounded A-L deck would balance direct attackers with control and support to keep the lines moving even as the opponent tried to disrupt you.
