## How to Set Up: A Quick 5-Minute Crash Course
1. Unshuffle the 25-card deck and lay it face-up in a 5x5 grid. Yes, 25 cards—like the famous “we’re all in this together” moment when the Sorting Hat’s memory charms reveal the first row of suspects.
2. Decide which team goes first. The usual suspects apply: the team who was last sorted into a house, or, you know, the team that wins a quick game of rock-paper-scissors with a wand-gesture that vaguely resembles a thunderbolt.
3. Place House tokens within easy reach of the Spymaster and the field team. Tokens will mark which cards belong to which House orbit, as well as which cards are safe vs. dangerous (the Assassin equivalent is still lurking in the deck, grinning like Draco Malfoy at a Slytherin Quidditch practice).
4. Shuffle the spell cards into a separate draw pile. These cards will act as modifiers for clues, letting you spice up basic clues with a little magical flair—for example, “Charm 2” or “Alchemy 3,” which can either help your team dramatically or lead them into a magical trap.
5. Choose a starting team and get ready to argue with style, because in Hogwarts, even the rules have a wearing-a-wizard-hat moment of drama.

Pro tip: keep a small notebook handy for tracking clue associations you think of in the moment. You’ll thank yourself later when you realize your “Potions 3” clue also covers a stack of cards you didn’t initially realize needed a second glance.

## Gameplay Deep Dive: How a Round Unfolds
Note: While the exact wording may vary slightly by print run, the core loop remains consistent with the spirit of the base game.

### Overview: The Spymaster’s Turn
- The Spymaster looks at the grid of 25 cards and thinks of a clue that can tie together multiple cards their team needs to identify. In Hogwarts Edition, clues must adhere to a magical or Hogwarts-related theme, making a single word sometimes insufficient or hilariously misleading. You’ll occasionally want to get clever: “Potions 3,” “Plot 2,” or “Weasleys 2.” The Spymaster speaks the clue aloud, then adds a number that indicates how many cards the clue applies to. The goal is to point your team in the right direction without tipping off the other side too hard.
- The team then discusses, debates, and votes on which cards match the clue. They guess one card at a time, as long as their guesses stay within the limit of the clue’s number plus one extra optional guess (some variants allow an extra stab at glory if the Stars Align). If a guess is correct, the card is flipped to show the team’s color. If a guess is wrong, a card is turned over to indicate it belongs to the opposing team or is an assassin. The round ends when all of a team’s cards are found or someone hits the assassin.

### Clue Crafting: The Fine (or Fateful) Art
Clue-giving in Hogwarts Edition benefits from the lore-obsessed mind. You’ll want to draw on memories of class schedules, long libraries, and those obscure details from the Potions Master’s syllabus. A good clue should strike a balance between broad enough coverage to pick multiple relevant cards and specific enough to avoid the opposing team’s cards or the deadly Assassin. If your clue is too broad, you risk handing the other team a few free cards, which is basically a wizard-level betrayal without the proper wand-on-wand credit. If it’s too narrow, you might only reveal one card and the round becomes a patience contest rather than a clever puzzle.

### House Mechanics: The Theme That Binds the Team
The Hogwarts Edition introduces a house-affinity mechanic that nudges players toward a house-specific playstyle. Here’s a typical breakdown:
- Gryffindor: Courage and teamwork are rewarded. When Gryffindor is the leading house, you’ll get a small point bonus for every correct card that is a character card rather than an item or spell. This encourages builds that center around identifying key characters (Harry, Hermione, Dumbledore, etc.).
- Slytherin: Planning and cunning. Slytherin-flavored rounds may allow one extra hint to be used per game, provided it’s followed by a more challenging penalty for misfires. In practice, this means longer clues with riskier targets—great for players who enjoy high-stakes mind games.
- Ravenclaw: Clever deduction. Ravenclaw tends to reward the team that earns a bonus for making a guess that leads to two correctly identified cards in a single turn. It’s a fun incentive for expansive clue strategies, but it requires strong memory and pattern recognition.
- Hufflepuff: Diligence and fair play. Hufflepuff turns often grant a “second chance” mechanic, allowing a penalty-free second guess if the first guess misses. You’ll get a little grace on misreads, which is perfect for players who enjoy a more forgiving experience.

These house twists aren’t game-breakers; they’re flavor that can steer your strategy toward a particular vibe in a given session. They also provide a built-in reason to rotate the “Spymaster” role to share the limelight and avoid run-away advantage for any single person.

### Endgame and Scoring: How Do We Decide the Winner?
Like many party games, the win condition is simple on the surface: be the first team to correctly identify all of your cards. The Hogwarts Edition adds some light modifiers:
- Points for character cards: Certain characters are worth extra points if revealed under Gryffindor or Ravenclaw guidance.
- House bonuses: If your team matches the house of the Spymaster or the current round’s theme, you might receive a tiny house-point bonus.
- The Assassin: Hitting the assassin ends the round immediately in a spectacularly dramatic fashion. It’s a reminder that in this magical world, power comes with consequences. You’ll want to avoid giving your opponent a direct path to victory by tapping that familiar black card too early.

All of these rules can be adjusted for casual play or a more intense tournament vibe. The important note is to set expectations before the first clue is uttered, or you’ll spend half the game arguing about how many house points should be awarded for a single clever reference to Professor McGonagall.

## Words on Strategy: How to Be the Hogwarts Whisperer
If you’re seeking to improve beyond “I think this is a Snitch” and more into “I can chain together three cards with an elegant clue,” here are actionable strategies:
- Build cluster clues: Look for concept clusters that tie several cards together. For instance, a clue like “Quidditch 3” could point to the Quidditch match, the Golden Snitch, and the Nimbus 2000—three distinct cards that share a single theme.
- Use spell cards strategically: The Spell Card mechanic is a wonderful way to save a rough round. Don’t waste it on a single obvious card. Save it for a moment when you’re about to reveal three cards with a tricky misdirection, and you need one of them to be more easily guessable.
- Mind the Assassin: In Hogwarts, the Assassin still lurks, but the deck might reward you for thinking beyond obvious connections. If you’re too eager to chase a clever link, you might accidentally steer someone toward the Dark Mark. Remember: sometimes the simplest clue is the most effective.
- Balance risk and reward: Ravenclaw players might favor clues that yield multiple correct cards, while a Gryffindor-led team might prefer more straightforward, safer clues. The balancing act is part of the fun—and the friction—so savor the tension without letting it fracture friendships.
- Practice the art of misdirection: The best clues aren’t always about direct connections. They weave a small story that helps your team see the link without tipping off the opponents. That storytelling skill is what makes a Spymaster truly legendary in Hogwarts terms.

## Variants and House Rules: Keeping the Night Fresh
If you want to wander beyond the standard rules, there are several fun variants you can try:

These variants simply amplify the energy of the game night.
