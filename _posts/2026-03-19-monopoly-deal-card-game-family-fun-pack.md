## Setup and Rules: Quickstart Guide
Timesaving tip: you can start playing in less than five minutes if your group agrees on the quick setup. The general flow remains simple:

- Each player draws five cards to start.
- The rest forms a draw pile. On your turn you draw two cards (some variants allow only one if you go first, to keep the early pace snappy).
- You may perform up to three actions per turn. Actions include playing money to your bank, laying down property cards, and using action cards to steal, charge rent, or block an opponents move.
- The objective is to accumulate three complete property sets. Yes, it sounds easy. It is not. The interplay of action cards and the wildly unpredictable Wild cards adds a layer of strategy and chaos that makes every game feel surprisingly dynamic.

The Family Fun Pack introduces a couple of kid friendly tweaks aimed at smoothing the learning curve for younger players. The tweaks speed up the endgame by slightly reducing the number of steps required to assemble a full set, and there are gentle prompts to help less experienced players stay in the game without feeling overwhelmed by a sudden power swing from an opponent who seems to wield a deck of uncanny masters of rent manipulation.

### Turn structure in plain English
- At the start of your turn, draw two cards (or one if you already have two or more). This keeps your hand fresh and ensures you have options without drowning in a sea of options.
- You can play up to three actions. You can play money, you can place property, or you can play an action card that can steal, charge rent, or alter your opponents hand or bank.
- Your goal is to lay down property cards to create color sets while keeping enough money to defend and to pay if someone charges rent.
- End your turn by checking if you have any legal moves left; if not, pass and hope the next turn brings better fortune.

This flow keeps the game fast and friendly and ensures even late arrivals can jump into the action without wading through a rulebook.

- The rent chessboard: Rent cards are powerful, but they require you to have the cash in your bank to collect. A well-timed rent can swing a lead into a dramatic near finish. - Wild cards as flexibility: Use wild cards to fill gaps or to shield critical sets when opponents are hot on your tail. But remember, wilds are precious and should be saved for your strongest targets or the turn that could break the game in your favor.
- Bluff with intention: The visuals of the Family Fun Pack make bluffing and misdirection feel natural; you can pretend to be less dangerous than you are, and that can coax opponents into making mistakes.
