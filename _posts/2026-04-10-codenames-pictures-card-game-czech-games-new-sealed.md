How to play (quick primer)
Codenames Pictures follows the same team-based guessing vibe as the word-based classic, but with a twist: you’re guessing based on single images rather than words. Two teams compete to identify their agents on the grid by giving clues that connect multiple pictures at once.

- Setup: lay out a grid of 5x4 or 4x5 image cards (depending on player count). One team has the red agents; the other blue; one assassin card sits in the grid; neutral cards are sprinkled around.
- Clue-giving: the clue-giver offers a one-word clue followed by a number (e.g., “Animals, 3”), indicating how many images on the table connect to the clue.
- Guessing: players on the clue team discuss and guess images one by one, trying to avoid the assassin and the other team’s agents.
- Endgame: first team to uncover all of their agents wins. If someone accidentally flips the assassin, the other team claims victory instantly.

Mechanics deep dive
The core mechanic of Codenames Pictures sits at the intersection of pattern recognition and shared mental models. The clues are one word plus a number, but the word you choose is an abstract pointer to a cluster of images distributed across the table. The team must decide which images the clue applies to — and in 2v2 or larger group formats, coordination becomes a study in social deduction and consensus-building.

- How clues connect images: Because each card is a picture rather than a word, players rely on broader visual categories like “animals in motion,” “objects on a desk,” or more abstract associations like “things that begin with the letter B.” The risk here is that the same picture can be interpreted to fit several clues, so the tension comes from negotiating between a clue’s helpfulness and the risk of giving away the other team’s strategy.
- The assassin: The black card is a merciless moment that forces players to balance speed with caution. If you hit the assassin, you’ve almost certainly handed the victory to the other team. In a sealed copy, the assassin’s presence remains unchanged: a tiny black badge on a card that can ruin your entire night.
- Turn structure: Turns rotate between teams, with one clue-giver per team. The chosen clue must correspond to at least one image and ideally more. Each guess increases the metaphorical tension in the room: the more you guess, the closer you are to victory or catastrophe.

Strategy and tips
- Start with broad connections: When you’re the clue-giver, begin with a broad yet defensible category that you’re confident the team can map to. Avoid over-narrow clues that only one person can understand.
- Use collaborative interpretation: In Codenames Pictures, the interpretive process is communal. When teammates explain why they think a card fits your clue, you’re not just teaching them; you’re calibrating everyone’s mental maps to the same endpoint.
- Manage risk: The assassin is a constant presence. If you’re unsure about two or three cards, it’s usually better to stop guessing and let the opposing team take the turn rather than forcing a risky chain that could end the game in a single misstep.

Variations and house rules
- Team size and grid: With more players, you might adjust the grid or the number of agents per team to speed up or slow down gameplay.

Comparisons: Codenames Pictures vs. the original
- Word versus image: The original Codenames relies on vocabulary and cross-connections, which can become a test of lexical associative power. Pictures trading words for images lowers some barrier to entry but raises a different kind of interpretive effort, especially for players who’re more comfortable with literal than abstract connections.
- Clue economy: The word-based version allows for more granular clues; the pictures edition rewards broader conceptual leaps. This can be refreshing or frustrating depending on your group’s playing style.
- Replayability: Both games shine when you have a group that enjoys laughter, friendly debates, and the occasional strategic misdirection. Codenames Pictures tends to shine with a group that loves visual humor and fast banter.
