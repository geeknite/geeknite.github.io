## How to spot fakes and avoid buyer’s remorse
This is the part where your inner skeptic steps in with a trusty flashlight and a magnifying glass. Fake cards exist, and shiny rares are particularly appealing to counterfeiters who want to exploit the aura of value without the accompanying sense of sticker shock. Here are a few practical tips to stay safe:
- Check the foil pattern. Legit SV4a shinies tend to show consistent holo lines under even lighting. If the foil looks too uniform or displays odd patchy reflections, take a closer look.
- Inspect the card stock and edges. Fakes often use subpar stock or slightly wrong edge finishes. In some cases, the print quality on bootleg cards is noticeably off—color bleed, misaligned borders, or overly glossy finishes.
- Compare to reference images. When in doubt, pull up high-resolution reference images from reputable sources and look for subtle differences.
- Look at the numbering and font. Print anomalies, such as slightly off fonts or spacing in the ex designation, can be giveaways, though some high-quality fakes try to mimic these details. If the numbers “192” and “190 LP” strike you as odd or out of pattern, exercise caution.
- Verify the source. Buying from established shops or verified sellers with return policies reduces risk. If you’re buying on secondary channels, insist on a return window and payment protection.

If you want a deeper dive into counterfeit detection, there are plenty of nerdy guides online that walk through the tactile cues you can rely on in person. The key takeaway: trust your eyes, and don’t let the shimmer blind you to the possibility of a well-made counterfeit disguised as a treasure.
