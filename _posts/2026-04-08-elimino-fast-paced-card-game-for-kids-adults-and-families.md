## Rules quick reference and pacing notes
A typical round has three acts:

1. Draw phase: On your turn you draw a card from the deck. This card could swing the round in unexpected ways, so brace yourself for a momentary surge of drama.
2. Play phase: You may play one or more cards depending on the effects available in your hand. The timing of these plays determines the pace of the round and, frankly, how much stage presence you display.
3. Resolve phase: After playing, effects resolve and any eliminations are confirmed. If you have eliminated enough rivals, you might clinch the round victory or push someone closer to the exit of the round table.

The end game occurs when a player achieves the victory condition the group agrees on before play begins. That condition is set by the rule sheet so you can tailor the length of your session. In practice, you will often find yourself playing three or four rounds before someone yells for snacks, which is exactly the pace most families crave after a long day.

While the core rules are straightforward, there is room for clever play. ## Host tips for a great Elimino night
- Set a timer: A simple sand timer or phone timer helps keep rounds snappy and prevents the game from stalling. The vibe should be timer-charged rather than endless.
- Establish a rotation: Decide on the dealer rotation to ensure that everyone feels part of the action and nobody hogs the table for too long.
- Encourage playful trash talk: A little friendly banter goes a long way. Keep it light and kind so younger players learn how to win gracefully and losing gracefully without tears and pizza stains.
- Snack strategy: Have a snack break after every two or three rounds. Keep the table clear for the next sprint and avoid sticky fingers on the cards.
- Accessibility window: If someone struggles with reading small text, offer larger print or a quick verbal recap of tough card effects. The goal is to keep the game inclusive and fun for all participants.

## House rules and variants to try
- Quick draw variant: Allow players to draw two cards at once in the first round to get the engine running. This adds immediate momentum and reduces the risk of early stalemates.
- Mercy round: In one round per game, allow all players a zero penalty elimination to keep everyone engaged and smiling, especially with younger players at the table.
- Team play: For larger gatherings, pair players into dynamic duos where one player handles hand management while the partner agitates with strategic plays. Team play can maintain energy while reducing the intensity clash for some players.
