## Assembly and Installation: It Takes a Village
If you enjoy assembly projects that resemble large-scale Lego builds, this is your moment to shine. The 9-meter expanse is likely delivered in several boxes, each full of half-curtains of hardware, fasteners, and possibly an Allen key that somehow always ends up as a lost relic behind the bookshelf. The recommended approach is simple: recruit a small crew, assign roles (construction foreman, alignment guru, cable wizard, and the “we’ll never find the spare screw” intern), and roll up your sleeves.

Key steps include:
- Unpacking and identity verification: ensure you’ve got all modules, legs, shelves, and cable management bits. You’ll need a floor layout that doesn’t end in “oops we broke the ceiling tile.”
- Floor anchoring: depending on your space, you may want to anchor the long bench to the floor to avoid the wind from someone’s coffee machine blowing over the worksurfaces. This is not a rolling-based workstation; it’s a stationary, ceremony-laden behemoth.
- Module alignment: two 4500 mm blocks must align perfectly to avoid that “disco floor” effect where the seam catches the knee when you walk by. Laser levels, string lines, and a willingness to swear softly help here.
- Cable integration: once the surface sits level and the legs attached, it’s time to route cables. If your kit includes cable troughs or trays, now is the moment to create a vein system that would make any cardiologist proud. Label everything, otherwise you’ll be burying a monitor arm in the vacuum of post-implementation chaos.
- Finishing touches: install any partitions, add monitor arms, and apply safety checks. You’re now in the zone where your open-plan dream turns into a functional work environment—hopefully with enough space for ten humans to share one keyboard during a hackathon and still not hate each other.

Assembly time will vary by team size and floor type, but plan for a day of camaraderie and questionable snack choices. If you’ve assembled a game table with similar length before, you’re halfway there; if not, bring extra mindfulness and the willingness to negotiate with the hex keys as though they’re tiny corporate co-founders.

## Ergonomics, Productivity, and the Human Element
Let's talk about human factors. A desk this long has both blessing and curse potential when it comes to ergonomics. On the pro side:
- Shared focus zones: large benches encourage collaborative work, pair programming, and rapid whiteboard sessions. You can line up ten minds to attack a problem and still have room to park a coworker’s chair at the end of the table for a strategic offshoot discussion.
- Flexible IMAX-like collaboration: the length makes it easy to organize sub-teams into clusters, with one group at the “front” and others at the back, while everyone quietly monitors the same central screen (your screens, of course).
- Personal space within a team: despite the shared surface, you can assign a few feet of real estate to each person, allowing personal devices, notepads, and cups of coffee to exist without a daily tug-of-war over elbow space.

On the con side:
- Fixed height: if you have a mix of short and tall teammates, you’ll either have a few elbow-bump moments or need to add adjustable height solutions elsewhere. If you’re serious about ergonomics, you’ll likely want to pair this with height-adjustable chairs and monitor arms to strike a balance between collaboration and personal comfort.
- Visual noise: a 9-meter bench can become a sea of screens, papers, and post-its. Without careful management of aesthetics and storage, it’ll feel less like a workspace and more like a bulletin board that’s grown veins.
- Privacy trade-offs: in an open-plan environment, privacy becomes a currency you spend carefully. The absence of screens might push you toward privacy screens, partitions, or sound management to keep distractions in check.

That said, the real productivity magic happens when you treat this like a platform rather than a fixed piece of furniture. Add modular monitor arms, task lighting, and a structured cable system. Then invite your team to co-create a workflow that uses the bench as an extension of the people seated around it. If you’ve ever coded a project with a dozen collaborators, you know that the right desk can feel like an extra teammate—one who doesn’t sleep and doesn’t demand equity, just a little bit of respect for the shared space.

## Alternatives and Comparisons
If you’re shopping around, you’ll want to weigh this bench against other open-plan solutions. Some teams prefer shorter, modular desks that can be rearranged daily; others opt for fixed long tables in a single room to minimize spatial fragmentation. Here are a few quick considerations:
- Shorter modular desks: easier to reconfigure, typically cheaper, and less intimidating in small offices. Great for teams that frequently re-shuffle or require quiet zones.
- Divisible long tables: allow more flexible layouts but require careful cable management and possibly more partitions to prevent openness from turning into noise.
- Dedicated screen-equipped stations: provide both shared space and personal display real estate, reducing the need to bring your own screens, at the cost of increased price and potential space constraints.
