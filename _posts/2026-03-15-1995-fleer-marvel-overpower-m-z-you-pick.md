## A quick primer on Overpower and why the M Z slice matters

In the vast pantheon of collectible card games from the 90s, Fleer Marvel Overpower stands tall as a giddy, chaotic blend of hero worship and elbows out combat. The premise was simple enough for a kid with a stack of allowance and a stubborn sense of fairness: build a duel deck featuring Marvel heroes and villains, roll some dice, and fight your opponent in a race to see who has the bigger capes and better catchphrases. The M to Z singles are a particularly delicious slice of the set because they include a cast that is equal parts iconic powerhouse and delightfully obscure oddball. This is where the game lets you dream about the big names while also shouting into your ear about underappreciated goons who still had a shot at glory when you paired them with the right scheme cards.

Overpower was never shy about flavor. The cards looked like they were drawn from a glossy hotel lobby of 1995 midlife crisis, each one flaunting bold art, metallic foils, and a few dramatic little quotes that made you feel like you were stepping into a comic panel. The M Z range is where you see two things in balance: the legend and the curveball. Magneto and Moon Knight sit next to Mole Man and Zzzax, and the contrast is part of the charm. You get to imagine the world deciding not just who is stronger, but who is cooler, which in a game built on cartoonish power swings is basically the whole point.

To set the stage, here is a quick mental map of what the M Z singles tend to do in play: big hitters with dramatic art, a few manipulation or control abilities, and a handful of quirky support cards that make weird combos sing. If you love the moment when a villain outsmarts a hero through pure theatrical menace, you will find your people among these pages. If you prefer straightforward beatdowns, the top tier of Magneto and Punisher adjacent cards will still scratch that itch. And if you suddenly realize you enjoy the theme of electrical mayhem in a fantasy battle card game, Zzzax is about to ruin your afternoon in the most sparkly way possible.

Before we dive into the roster, a quick note on deck building and the vibe of these cards. Overpower did not hinge on hyper precise numbers alone. It felt like a board game first and a numeric puzzle second. The art and flavor text carried a lot of the experience, and the M Z batch is a microcosm of that ethos: you pick a line that feels thematic, you hope the mechanics align with the narrative you want, and then you roll the dice like you are tossing a live electrical charge across a dinner table. The singles market for M Z is a playful echo chamber of this vibe. Some cards are common enough to find at card shops or online auctions, while others exist as late night dreams for collectors who have the patience to chase one rare foil that shows up once in a blue moon.

## The M to Z roster in detail

The following sections spotlight a handful of representative cards from letters M through Z. I am not claiming this is an exhaustive catalog of every card in the range, but it should give you a sense of the flavor, the balance problems, and the tiny sparks of brilliance that made this subset so memorable. Wherever possible, I reference iconic cards and the kinds of moments they could create when paired with others in a well rounded deck.

### M cards

#### Magneto
Magneto is the classic punchfirst villain who turns the concept of metal into a personality trait. In Overpower, his card is all about gravity and force channels that feel both controlled and dangerous. When you pair Magneto with other metal loving or metal manipulating cards, you get a control tunnel where your opponent spends the entire match worrying about the next metal trigger. The art on Magneto cards is dramatic, with his helmet catching the light and his cape waving like he just solved a riddle about sulfur content in steel. If you enjoy narrative tension in combat as much as raw numbers, Magneto is the poster child.

#### Moon Knight
Moon Knight is the enigmatic contrast to the hard edge of Magneto. His kit leans into stealth, misdirection, and sudden ambush style effects. On a table loaded with flashy heroes, Moon Knight can sneak into the shadows and pop up with an unexpected advantage. The tactile thrill of using Moon Knight is less about pure power and more about tempo: you stage your board, tease your opponent with a false sense of security, and then reveal the strike that decides the round. The card art captures that mid century noir vibe that feels like a mini comic panel in your palm.

#### Mister Fantastic
Mr. Fantastic brings the idea of reach and versatility. In a game built around mixing and matching powers, a flexible card like Mister Fantastic can be a life raft. The trick here is to lean into synergy with adjacent tech or science themed cards, to show that genius is not just about brute force but about making the math of your deck sing. The design invites you to imagine a world where you can extend your strategy beyond the board edges, and that is a vibe worth celebrating.

#### Mole Man
Mole Man flips the script by leaning into the underground, the underground army, and the unexpected ally. His card tends to offer a different route to victory — a path that rewards nontraditional play and thoughtful timing. If you ever wanted your board state to feel like a subterranean tunneling expedition, Mole Man is your spirit animal. It is a reminder that Overpower is as much about story as it is about the numbers, and this card embodies that perfectly.

#### MODOK
MODOK might be the single most meme friendly card in this subset, a mastermind with an oversized ego and an even larger rulebook. In play, MODOK tends to excel at disruption, steering the fight away from brute force and toward a chess game of who can outthink whom. The balance between his intellect and vulnerability on the card art is delightful, and it makes him a fun companion to the more straightforward brawlers listed here.

### N cards

#### Namor
Namor is the king of the underwater throne room, and in Overpower his stat line and effects tend to reflect that dual life — coastal warfare meets surface world politics. Namor is perfect for players who like to control multiple layers of the board and punish overconfidence. The Namor card is a lesson in how you can project athletic leadership onto a single character and still have him feel like a real threat when the match heats up.

#### Nova
Nova is the cosmic face of speed and renegade force. In M Z, Nova cards can offer fast tempo—hitting hard and moving fast—and you can imagine this as a spacefaring strike team spirit within your deck. The aesthetic often leans into starfield backdrops and bright energy blasts, which makes Nova one of the more visually satisfying cards in the set. Nova is a reminder that speed can be weaponized in a game that sometimes leans toward heavier hitters.

### O cards

#### Onslaught
Onslaught is the kind of forces of nature card that makes you feel like a cosmic storm is about to erupt in a kitchen table. His presence on a board can shift momentum with a few well-timed plays, particularly if you manage to chain a couple of effects that reward multi card interactions. Onslaught cards invite you to think in layers: how do I set up a turn that pays off multiple times? The visuals often convey a sense of unstoppable momentum, which is exactly the mood you want when you look across the table and realize your opponent also brought a big swing card.

#### Omega Red
Omega Red brings a classic, ruthless villain energy to the M Z slice. His card usually plays into the theme of relentless pursuit and risk versus reward. When Omega Red shows up, you can feel the tension in the room rising as both players sense that a single misstep could swing the fight. The art emphasizes a skeletal menace with a red glow that feels almost cinematic, which makes him a fun center piece for a deck built around fearsome presence rather than flashy combos.

#### Odin
Odin appears as a mythic grandmaster, a figure who tips the balance with heroic authority. If you enjoy the mythic side of Marvel and want your deck to echo that energy, Odin cards fit nicely. They tend to offer a stabilization effect — something that can keep you in the game when the clock starts to run out. The moral of the Odin card is clear: sometimes you win by patience and a little cosmic luck, not by pure brawn.

### P cards

#### Phoenix
Phoenix is the drama queen of the Marvel cosmos and a perfect fit for Overpower drama. The Phoenix cards often carry high risk, high reward mechanics that feel like a heartbeat accelerator. They demand careful planning and a willingness to gamble with your board state for the big payoff. If you like cards that tell a story of rise and rebirth, Phoenix in M Z is your narrative lane.

#### Punisher
Punisher is the straight shooter in the M Z lineup. He cuts through the bravado with a no nonsense style, delivering practical bite to the match. In deck design terms, Punisher cards can anchor a more grounded strategy, providing a counterpoint to the more naive showmanship of other characters. The art tends to lean into gritty realism, a refreshing contrast to the more flamboyant bits elsewhere in the set.

#### Quasar
Quasar brings the cosmos into your hand with a bevy of energy tricks. If you enjoy controlling the tempo of the game through resource management and timing, Quasar gives you a toolkit to do that with flair. The card art is all about starbursts and constellations, which makes Quasar a fun focal point for a deck that wants to feel like a small solar system of its own creation.

### R cards

#### Red Skull
Red Skull is a master of moral hazard, a villain with a grin and a plan. In the M Z chunk, Red Skull cards tend to emphasize disruption and betrayal within a controlled framework. The thrill comes from watching your opponent misinterpret the threat level and respond conservatively, only to realize that Red Skull has his own way of turning the board into a chessboard you control with a single clever move.

#### Spider-Man
Spider-Man is one of the iconic faces of Marvel and a surprisingly versatile card in Overpower. He often has a flexible toolkit that can adapt to multiple playstyles, from direct aggression to sneaky support. If you want a hero you can rely on to stay relevant as the game evolves, Spider-Man is your guy. You can imagine the web slinging energy of these cards as you plan a sequence that pins the opponent down just long enough for a dramatic finish.

#### Super-Skrull
Super-Skrull brings a wild, adaptable toolkit that presses you to stay on your toes. He is a reminder that in Overpower, sometimes the best approach is to diversify your threats and force your opponent to guess which card you will unleash next. The art plays with a sense of brutal energy, which helps you feel the pressure even when you are not physically rolling dice.

#### Scarlet Witch
Scarlet Witch is the mind bender that makes opponents rethink their plan mid match. In the M Z subset, she often operates as a strategic disruptor, turning the tide by altering how cards interact rather than delivering one big hit. If you enjoy a puzzle game vibe wrapped in a superhero skin, Scarlet Witch cards bring that satisfying sense of misdirection that makes players grin through gritted teeth.

### S cards

#### Spider-Man (a second note)
If you want to see how strong Spider-Man can be with the right support, the S cards give you that chance. A familiar face with a twist, Spider-Man in this subset often acts as a reliable anchor while the bigger guns do their dramatic entrances.

#### She-Hulk
She-Hulk adds a different flavor, balancing raw power with a more practical, legalistic swagger. In matches where you need a capable tank to absorb hits and still land heavy blows, She-Hulk can be your best friend. The card art often emphasizes her confident stance and the energy around her punches, which is exactly the vibe you want when you imagine a superhero legal drama that translates into tabletop combat.

#### Sub-Mariner and The Wasp analogues
This portion of the roster leans into characters who carry both presence and a sense of tactical niche. They may be less flashy than Spider-Man or Phoenix, but their contribution to a well rounded deck is real. They teach you the lesson that a balanced team needs both heavy hitters and precise, strategic pieces that can pull the moment out of a draw.

### T cards

#### The Thing
The Thing is the quintessential blunt instrument of the M Z block. He arrives with a simple philosophy — hit hard, take a hit, and keep going. The card design reflects that physicality, delivering a satisfying crunch on attack and a sturdy base for defense. If you enjoy crunchy, straightforward fights with a touch of vintage pulp energy, The Thing is your go to for a reliable bully card.

#### Thor
Thor sits at the crossroads of tradition and awe. His cards tend to carry the mythic weight of Asgard with a practical frame that lets you feel like you are wielding a godly hammer without needing to file taxes for it afterward. Thor is about tempo and spectacle; a well built deck featuring him can feel like a cinematic climax every time he lands a hit.

#### Taskmaster
Taskmaster is the cunning foil, the character who makes you respect the way control and mimicry can turn a game around. In M Z, Taskmaster cards often create value by copying or tutoring certain effects from your side of the board. The trick is to time the impersonation so that your copies land when they matter most, like a stage magician pulling the right card out of an empty hat just as the villain declares victory.

### U cards

#### Ultron
Ultron is the archetype of cold efficiency. He wants to create a machine like rhythm on the board, a predictable performance that still hits hard. In practice, Ultron cards reward players who plan several moves ahead and who are excellent at reading the opponent at the moment the next piece lands on the table. The art leans into the metallic menace with a cold gaze, a reminder that sometimes the best strategy is a perfect algorithm wearing a supervillain cape.

### V cards

#### Vision
Vision acts as the elegant hybrid, a character who can blend human intuition with machine precision. In M Z, Vision cards tend to excel in hybrid strategies that strike a balance between aggression and resilience. The art and flavor text support the idea of a being designed to be the future of battle, which makes him an exciting addition to any deck that wants to feel futuristic even as it fights with old school bravado.

#### Venom
Venom is the chaos engine. He pushes your deck into more aggressive territory and rewards you for if you can keep a threat alive long enough to explode into a powerful counterplay. If you want a card that pushes your opponent to react to multiple threats, Venom could be your catalyst for a wild, high tempo game where the table feels like a chaotic carnivale of powers.

### W cards

#### Wolverine
Wolverine is a staple for any big hero roster, and in the M Z slice he demonstrates why the character remains so popular. His cards usually lean into resilience, surprising bursts, and the possibility of a long, grinding duel where the character you love becomes the backbone of a favorable late game. Wolverine is a perfect example of how a character can feel iconic in a card game while still delivering practical, repeatable value across multiple matches.

#### War Machine
War Machine offers a practical armored approach that plays well with a heavy weapon theme. The deck building story with War Machine tends to emphasize synergy with tech and defense oriented cards, creating a dependable backbone that can hold the line while more flamboyant allies go in for the kill.

#### Wasp and Wonder Man vibes
These cards round out the section with a lighter, more kinetic energy. They show a different side of the spectrum — speed, improvisation, and the possibility to finish a game faster than your opponent expects. The W set is a good reminder that not every win has to be a grind; sometimes you sweep in with a well timed surprise and ride that wave to victory.

### X Y Z cards

#### X Men and Xavier lineups
The X family in this slice leans on the classic mutant core. If you love a board where you feel like you are managing a small squad, the X cards give you that flavor while remaining accessible to new players. They invite you to think about team composition and the value of synergy across a few different classes of heroes.

#### Yellowjacket
Yellowjacket is the cheeky underdog with a sting in the tail. In the M Z range, Yellowjacket’s role is to provide utility that catches opponents off guard and makes them recalibrate their plans mid fight. He is the kind of card you want on a night when you feel your opponent is overcommitting on big hitters and you want to pivot to a more nimble plan.

#### Zzzax
Zzzax is the electricity obsessed villain that you bring into a game with a grin because you know things are about to get shocking. His cards often revolve around energy, disruption, and the kind of multi card plays that feel like a mini engineering problem you get to solve on the fly. Zzzax is the spark that can turn a dull round into a quick, memorable knockout if the stars align.

#### Baron Zemo vibes
Baron Zemo gives you that classic caper flavor mixed with menace. Not every game needs to be about raw power; sometimes it is about the psychological battlefield and the art of pressing the opponent into making a mistake. In M Z, Zemo and his kin serve as the reminder that a clever villain can make any fight feel like a tactical puzzle you enjoy solving.

## Rounding out the experience and a note on collectibility

The swell of art that looks like it belongs in a glossy weekly comic, the way the cards smell faintly of plastic and old cardboard, and the social joy of sitting across a table with friends who know all the lines of your favorite heroes.
