## How CODENAMES Is Played (A Quick Refresher)
CODENAMES is a team-based word game that hinges on communication, miscommunication, and the fine edge of a clue that is clever enough to guide your teammates without steering them into catastrophe. In the CGE Czech Games Edition, the core rules remain faithful to the original formula: two teams (red and blue), a Spymaster for each team, and a grid of Code Cards laid out for everyone to see.

- The Spymaster gives one-word clues accompanied by a number. The number tells your teammates how many Code Cards on the board relate to the clue. Example: “Ocean, 2” could point to two cards that hint at the sea, boats, or something broadly nautical.
- Teammates may touch the Code Cards to reveal them. If a team touches a red agent, that card gets marked as found for that team. If it’s blue, the opposing team benefits. If a neutral card is revealed, your team loses a bit of momentum. If the Assassin card is revealed, the game ends immediately with a loss for the team that chose it.
- The objective is simple in concept but deliciously chaotic in execution: find all your team’s agents before the other team does.

The Czech print retains the same cadence as the original: snappy clues, rapid back-and-forth, and a learning curve that rewards clever associations more than grand vocabulary. If you’re new to CODENAMES, expect a few rounds of “Wait, we misunderstood that clue,” followed by a few rounds of “Okay, I’ve got this—let’s try something unorthodox.” And if your friends are propensity for puns is off the charts, you’ll be in for a glorious night of wordplay that spirals into the ridiculous and somehow ends with everyone yelling about a turtle named Guillermo because your Spymaster accidentally referenced a Simpsons character in a clue.

For those who crave a deeper dive into strategy, the general guidance remains the same:
- The best clues tie together several target cards with a concept that is widely recognizable to your team.
- Avoid overloading a clue with too many target cards, which can invite chaos and accidental hits on the other team’s cards.
- The Assassin is your ultimate trap: a reminder that cleverness without caution can end a run in an instant.

If you want a tangible example of a typical round, imagine your Spymaster whispering: “Space, 3.” Your team sees three cards that could be related to stars, planets, or flights to the moon, and suddenly you’re debating whether “astronaut” counts as fair play, or whether saying “rocket” is a two-letter code for “red card.” The tension is real, and the laughter is usually louder than the actual gameplay length.
