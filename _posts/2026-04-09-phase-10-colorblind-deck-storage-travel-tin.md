## Color-Blind Accessibility: What It Means and How It Works

Color accessibility in a card game usually means a few things: more robust symbols, redundant cues, and ideally, a design that doesn’t force you into a debate about the exact shade of red you thought you saw on a card. The Phase 10 color-blind deck leans into these goals with a thoughtful approach:

- Symbols over color: Each color on the face of the Phase cards is accompanied by a distinct symbol. If you’re color-blind or simply terrible at color-matching under time pressure, these symbols are your lifeboats. The same symbols appear on the corresponding color chips and on the phase cards’ headers so you have multiple cues to rely on.
- Redundancy in iconography: The color indicators aren’t just decorative. They’re mapped to a second cue on each card—an icon, a number, and sometimes a little embossment along the edge. This redundancy is the kind of robust design that makes it easier to play in dim lighting, while wearing sunglasses at the same time (okay, maybe not sunglasses, but you catch my drift).
- Back-of-card design: The backs employ a geometric motif that is intentionally non-color-associative. This means you can pivot to a color-blind strategy and still know which deck is yours without accidentally snatching your neighbor’s deck because you mistook a purple-back for a blue-back during a particularly dramatic bluff.
- Quick-start adaptation: The rule sheet includes a tiny section on color-blind play with tips and a quick summary. It’s not a NASA-level manual, but it’s enough to prevent the one-hour “we’re all winners” debate that sometimes accompanies the first game night of a new variant.

Pragmatic note: if you’re one of the players who sees colors normally but still appreciates the symbols, you’ll enjoy the extra layer of clarity. If you’re strictly color-blind, the deck is designed to be a bridge—no more squinting, no more “Is that red or orange?” conversations that derail the game’s momentum. In practice, the deck delivers a lot of value for a modest price premium compared to the standard Phase 10 set. It’s not a superhero cape for your eyesight, but it’s a reliable utility belt with a compass and a flashlight attached.
