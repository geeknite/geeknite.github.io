The design typically uses a tiered approach, with easy entries to teach mechanics, followed by progressively harder challenges that demand more careful deduction and cross-referencing clues. - Clear starting conditions: A simple setup helps you learn the puzzle language without getting overwhelmed.
- Progressive clues: The cards or pages provide more nuanced hints as you advance, allowing players to calibrate their confidence and pace.
- Pattern recognition: Common motifs include grid-based arrangements, compatible resource placements, and consistent rule sets that encourage developing a personal heuristic.
- Edge cases: The best puzzles throw in tricky scenarios that require you to re-check assumptions and question the obvious path.

From a practical standpoint, expect a broad spread of difficulty.
