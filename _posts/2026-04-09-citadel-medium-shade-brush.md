## What is the Citadel Medium Shade Brush?
The Citadel Medium Shade Brush is, ostensibly, a brush made for shading washes on miniatures. The word "Medium" suggests a size that isn't the tiny precision brush nor the big room-cleaner, but something in between. In practice, it's a brush you reach for when you want to apply washes or shading strokes across mid-sized flat surfaces, like leather belts on a marine or the drape of a cape on a hero. The brush's design is intentionally forgiving: a slightly longer ferrule that lets bristles bend with your strokes; a robust synthetic fiber that holds a decent amount of wash and releases it with predictability; and a handle that's not too long to become a weapon in the middle of a tabletop whodunit.

### Design and Materials
- Bristles: Synthetic, with a taper toward the tip to help push product into recesses without flooding raised edges.
- Ferrule: Metal, snug, and not prone to the infamous "ferrule flop" after a few sessions.
- Handle: Comfortable grip, though not so fancy you forget you're painting the common orc.
- Size: Medium is relative; it's not a micro-detail brush, but it's not a mop either. It balances nicely on a 28mm model, allowing for smooth shading without getting lost in the cloak or armor.
- Capabilities: Great for applying shaded washes, glazing, and mid-tone shading across medium areas. Also useful for feathering around edges or gently sweeping across repeated textures.

### How to use the Medium Shade Brush effectively
For many hobbyists, shading is where you either get elegant transitions or end up with a muddy swamp on the model. The Medium Shade Brush is intended to minimize the latter while giving you a controlled, deliberate shading path. Here are practical tips:
- Load management: Dip the brush lightly into your wash and wipe on a paper towel. The goal is to have a brush that is damp, not drenched. Too much moisture means the coat will pool in awkward places.
- Strokes and technique: Use short, confident strokes to apply shade into recesses or along natural creases. For larger surfaces, you can use longer sweeps that cover the area evenly.
- Control: Start with a conservative shade in a dry-linish approach; you can always build up later, but you can't easily erase a heavy shade without repainting the base layer.
- Handling: Hold the brush like a wand; you want some distance from the miniature to control flow. If you feel your hand tremble, lean back, drink more coffee, and pretend you are the art director of the galaxy.

## Performance and Use Cases
### Shading large surfaces
The Medium Shade Brush excels on mid-sized cloth and leather surfaces where you want a smooth gradient without pooling. A well-loaded brush can deposit shade into folds with a single pass and leave behind a natural recession that reads as depth rather than a dirty puddle.

### Edges and transitions
Because the tip is tapered, you can nudge shade into seam lines and edge recesses with a light touch. The trick is balancing wash intensity with the surface texture; a glossy armor plate will resist the same exact amount of shading you’d apply to a matte cloak, so adjust pressure and angle accordingly.

### Layering and glazing
If you’re into glazing, the Medium Shade Brush provides enough bristle surface to spread a glaze evenly across a flat area. It also lends itself to feathering from a darker shade into a mid-tone. The key is thin washes and patient layering—you’ll thank yourself when you see the subtle gradient on a cape rather than a single, muddy color.

### Dry-time and cleanup
Like all shading brushes, this one benefits from prompt cleanup after painting. A quick rinse, a mild soap, and reshaping the bristles go a long way toward preserving tip integrity. The synthetic fibers handle routine cleanup well; you won’t be chasing stray dog-hair-like bristles after a single session, which is a win for those who treat their hobby time like a space mission.

## Real-world Testing: A Miniature Test Run
To truly judge a tool, you need a tangible test. We grabbed a 28mm model, primed in matte black for maximal shadow potential, and ran a few shading scenarios:

### Test 1: Cloak shading
We loaded a mid-tone wash onto the bristles, then applied quick, even strokes across the cloak. The shade settled into the deepest folds first, then faded toward the mid-tones as the brush heated with more air and less paint. The result: a cloak with readable folds but without harsh demarcations.

### Test 2: Leather belt and armor details
Switching to a slightly darker shade, we traced the belt edges and the riveted sections of armor. The medium head made it possible to hit the lines without smudging adjacent highlights. It wasn’t a miracle, but it was a confident step toward better shading with less manual precision.

### Test 3: Large flat surfaces
We attempted a broader sweep across a cape. The brush carried a comfortable amount of wash, and the stroke behaved predictably. There was no flood along the edge, and the flow remained controlled as we moved from top-right to bottom-left. This is where a medium shade brush earns its keep: when you need broad, even shading without babysitting every little crease.

### How it handled after multiple uses
After several sessions, the brush retained its shape and continued to perform consistently. The bristles did not splay excessively, and the core remained stiff enough to resist accidental splayed edges. It’s not indestructible, but it’s not a fragile magician either.

## Care and Maintenance
A brush is only as good as its maintenance routine. Here’s how we keep the Medium Shade Brush singing:
- Cleaning after every session: rinse under lukewarm water, then lather with a gentle brush soap to remove pigment. 
- Shape and dry: pat dry with a microfiber cloth, reshape the tip, and lay flat to dry to avoid bristle warping.
- Storage: keep bristle tips pointing upwards or flat—avoids bending the head.
- Periodic conditioning: if bristles feel stiff after weeks of use, a light brush conditioner can help restore suppleness. Avoid over-conditioning; you want to preserve the brush’s original feel.

## Comparisons: How does it stack up against other shade brushes?
### Against other shade brushes
In the pantheon of shade brushes, the Medium Shade sits near the center in terms of size and control. It’s smaller than large mop-style brushes used for heavy washes, yet larger than micro-detail shade brushes. The balance is its strength: you can shade mid-sized surfaces efficiently, then pivot to finer work without swapping tools mid-scene.

### Synthetic vs sable for shading
Citadel’s brush lineup uses high-quality synthetic fibers designed to emulate sable performance without the price tag. For shading, this means a resilient brush that holds a modest amount of pigment and releases it smoothly. If you’re someone who swears by sable for its feel, you might notice a difference in paint glide, but the synthetic Medium Shade remains a reliable, budget-conscious workhorse with predictable results.
