## The Five-Game Used Collection

Here are the five core Zombicide experiences you might find in a used collection touted as five-game bundle. They cover a broad spectrum of themes and rules complexities, so you can pick accordingly or just play the set as a mega campaign.

### Zombicide (Original)

The grandfather of the zombie-cooperative puzzle. The original Zombicide introduced the basic mechanic: you and your friends coordinate survivors to clear rooms, rescue civilians, and survive wave after wave of bitey undeads. The used copy you'll encounter typically includes the base rulebook, a tile stack, the early miniatures, dice, and the first wave of survival figures. What to watch for in a used copy: worn tiles with edge fray, missing standees for optional survivors, and heavy use on the player boards that might dull readability. However, if you get a clean base with the scenario book and the standard zombies, you can expect a solid morning or evening of chaos.

Key components to check:
- The board tiles: look for creases or bent edges that might make setups unstable.
- Survivor miniatures: verify that the plastic is not deformed and that the base pegs fit into the stands.
- Rulebook: ensure the pages aren’t missing or heavily creased; a scuffed rulebook can ruin the first game night.
- Dice and cards: confirm you have a full deck and a spare pair of dice for the safety dance of rerolls.

Pros and cons of used Original Zombicide:
- Pros: cheap entry point, perfect for a starter group, plenty of nostalgia.
- Cons: older components mean more risk of missing bits and broken boards.

### Zombicide: Black Plague

Black Plague is essentially Zombicide in a medieval fantasy setting with bigger boards and more minis per scenario. Used copies of this box often come with a robust pile of undead and a handful of hero characters that look ready to charge into the Black Forest... or at least into your living room floor lamp. The main thing to check here is board warping because larger boards can flex and warp if the plastic wrap has aged. Also verify the piles of enemies; the Black Plague set tends to come with more dragon-scale types and knights, which translates into bigger tables and bigger chaos.

Important checks:
- Board integrity: ensure the larger tiles aren’t bent; if they are, you might still salvage with a large play area on a flat table.
- Card decks: verify you have the correct number of unique cards; missing or duplicated cards will break the game's balancing act.
- Miniatures: these are the centerpieces; check for mold lines, tool marks, and paint wear; used copies often have some paint wear which can add charm or be a headache—depends on your tolerance for a wargaming vibe on your dining table.

Why this edition often hits the used market: it's a beloved favorite, but many players upgrade to newer fantasy settings in the line, so sellers rehome the core set to free up shelf space for dragons and goblins with better sculpts. It remains a powerhouse for cooperative play with friends who want a larger tactical challenge.

### Zombicide: Green Horde

Green Horde brings woodland boogeymen and forest vibes into the mix. The sculpts lean on trolls, orcs, and a luscious green color palette that makes the minions look like they drank a lot of leafy juice. A used copy is often a safe bet if you like a more grim, natural look rather than urban decay. The board edges and the wrap can be more delicate because of the nature-themed artwork. The core mechanics remain the same but you might find a few printed cards with quirky art and some slightly frayed corners.

What to inspect:
- Environment cards: ensure you have a complete card deck with no stuck or bent cards.
- Terrain pieces: these can break easily if the box had a rough ride during transit.
- Hero minis: Green Horde uses some unique sculpts; check for paint chips but remember that a patina of tabletop paint can be a badge of honor.

The Green Horde box is generally the most forgiving when you consider price and availability. It tends to be a good starter for players who want a strong theme but still want relatively straightforward rules. ### Zombicide: Invader

Space aliens plus zombies—a mashup that somehow works as a co-op game night. Invader introduces a sci-fi setting, with space suits, laser weapons, and an airlock that might double as a storage container for soda cans. Used copies can feature some sci-fi heavy minis and more tech-y cards. The risk here is mechanical: boards with wrap and modular tiles that may show wear, as well as standees that might have lost their bases. The upside is a different flavor of chaos with faster closing-in horde waves and the occasional end-of-round tech check.

What to look for:
- Modular boards: ensure tiles align well and haven’t warped; misaligned tiles kill the flow.
- Alien zombie types: some editions include special zombies; make sure you have the right number of special figures or you’ll lose the thematic balance.
- Rule book and tech cards: verify the pieces that govern the new tech actions are present.

Why buy used Invader? If you or your group likes sci‑fi settings and you want to swap out medieval aesthetics for something more starship-y, Invader is your go-to. ### Zombicide: Black Death

Black Death is a plague-themed twist with a darker, industrial aesthetic and a new mechanic that influences how zombies infect survivors. A used copy in good condition can be a cult classic purchase because it often includes a strong number of tiles and a large pile of zombie minis. The box can be heavy and the boards wide, which means you need space but you get a more intense run of a campaign with a heavier feel.

Check for:
- Insert and packing: the insert can wear out with age and impact how you store the cards; consider a replacement insert if it looks bad.
- Plague-infected minis: some paint wear is common; check that there are no cracks or detachments.
- Card stacks: verify the deck remains in good condition; mis-sorted cards can be a nightmare at setup.

Where Black Death shines: the difficulty curve and the war-torn urban vibe; the heavier pace can be a great next step after you have played the base game a few times. ## What to check when buying used Zombicide sets

- Completeness: confirm you have all of the 5 game set's core components plus any extra expansions if included. A missing box insert is a minor annoyance; a missing rulebook is a showstopper.
- Board integrity: tiles that are warped or peeling edges impact setup and play comfort.
- Miniatures: look for broken limbs or bent bases; depending on the edition, the minis might be snap-fit or fixed to pegs.
- Dice and tokens: blank dice are a drag, tokens missing or bent can break the pacing and add extra work to the first playthrough.
- Safety and cleanliness: if the box smells like old gym socks or the minis smell of smoke, you may want to pass or negotiate a discount accordingly. A quick wipe-down and air out can fix some issues but not all.

## Value, storage, and playability of a used Zombicide five-game collection

The value proposition here hinges on two things: price and playability. If you can land a well‑kept five-game set for under the price of a single brand-new box, it is hard to argue with the entertainment potential. The key is to be honest about your gaming group size and your willingness to house a behemoth of a collection on a coffee table that doubles as a battlefield and a snack station. The boards can be large and the miniatures numerous, so plan your setup and storage with care.

Storage tips:
- Use a dedicated gaming tote or stackable bins with labeled sections for each edition. The more modular your setup, the easier it is to rotate games in and out of rotation.
- Consider replacing broken inserts with a simple drawer system; a few organizers and zip bags can keep cards sorted and protected.
- Protect the minis: you can bag the tiny pieces in resealable bags to prevent misplacement during transit or storage.

Playing tips:
- Start with a common map for quick onboarding; then escalate to the more complex campaigns as your group gains experience.
- Use a shared house rule to deal with hero deaths or misplays; a little humor goes a long way when you realize your survivor just sacrificed themselves for a sandwich.
- When playing with five games in one night, plan a rotation so each game ends with a satisfying payoff rather than a sudden, brutal wipe.

For readers curious about cross-edition compatibility, a used collection can be a perfect testing ground for mixing components. The core mechanics stay consistent across editions, so you can mix tiles and cards to suit your taste, which especially helps when you want to host a Friday night that spans multiple Zombicide vibes.
