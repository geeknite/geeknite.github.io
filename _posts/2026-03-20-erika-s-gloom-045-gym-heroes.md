## Condition, Grading, and What NM Means Here
### Understanding 132 NM in Gym Heroes Context
In a Gym Heroes context, NM (Near Mint) is typically used to describe a card that shows minimal evidence of wear: sharp corners, clean surface, no creases, and a clean back with crisp borders. For a card like Erika Gloom 045/132, NM might mean a near-perfect specimen that has survived two decades with grace, perhaps a tiny edge ding you wouldn’t notice unless you’re inspecting under a magnifying glass. The nuance here is that the NM grade can carry different values depending on the presence of whitening, corner joins, or surface glossificance.

### Why Collectors Want NM Condition
- Aesthetics: People want to admire the art with minimal distraction.
- Value: In many sets, NM copies command better resale values and higher interest from collectors who liquidate their collection in a organized fashion.
- Grading Stability: For older WOTC-era cards, reliable grading can make or break how you present a collection.

### Grading Nuances: What to Look For
- Surface: Are there scratches or scuffs that aren’t part of the image? In Gym Heroes’ glossy finish, a lot of the surface is fragile but also forgiving if the card has been stored properly.
- Corners: The hallmark of NM is tight corner geometry with minimal whitening.
- Edges: The card should look sharp with no brownish tinges along the border from humidity or aging.
- Centering: The typical acceptable centering is a few percentage points off, but highly-coveted NM pieces are centered well enough to give a pleasing symmetrical look.

If you’re new to grading, consider sending in a few decisive cards like Erika Gloom 045 to get a sense of how the grading ecosystem treats gym-era cards. It’s not just about price; it’s about the ritual of preserving a piece of your childhood in a way that can be passed down to future collectors who will mutter, “the packaging was different back then,” and you’ll nod, because you know exactly what they mean.
