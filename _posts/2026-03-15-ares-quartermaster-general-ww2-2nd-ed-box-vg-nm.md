# Quartermaster General WW2 (2nd Ed) Box VG+ NM: A Geeknite Review

Welcome, fellow finger-plickers and cardboard connoisseurs. Today on Geeknite, we dive into the thick, coffee-stained world of supply lines, air superiority debates, and the kind of strategic chaos that only a 2nd Edition box can provide: Ares Games' Quartermaster General WW2 (2nd Ed). The box in question is described here as VG+ NM, which in board-game shorthand means “shelved with the care of a veteran general, yet the war never really leaves the carpet.” If you’ve ever thought, “I want to feel like Eisenhower with a pencil and a handful of 1-inch counters,” this box might just be your new best weekend friend.

In this review, we’ll unbox expectations, unpack the mechanics, and dissect the tactical fiber of a game that claims the speed of a blitz with the depth of a long-range bomber. And yes, we’ll throw a few jokes at the supply chain gods—because nothing says ‘war is hell’ like a deck of cards that can ruin your day faster than a German panzerschreck at a game night.

If you’re here for quick recommendations: Quartermaster General WW2 (2nd Ed) is best for players who want a brisk, theater-level strategic experience with lots of chaos and minimal downtimes. It scales from 2 to 6 players, which means you can drag your stubborn friends into the same room to argue about supply routes for roughly an hour per player. The 2nd Edition improves components, sharpens art, and fixes some of the little gremlins from the first run—gremlins like “this counter doesn’t fit in the tray” and “that rulebook diagram is a cryptogram.”

Plus, as a box labeled VG+ NM, you’ll get a shelf-buzz of nostalgia: the glossy card stock, the faint but noble smell of cardboard, and the silent expense of a hobby that somehow multiplies your to-read list even as you play it. If you want a game that feels chaotic but fair, this is your theater.

## Unboxing and First Impressions

### Box and Components

The VG+ NM descriptor is a perfectly polite way of saying the box shows signs of having existed and fought bravely, but has not yet surrendered its contents to the Dust of Ages. The box lid has a couple of scuffs that tell you it endured a move or two, but the inner tray—oh, the inner tray—remains a sanctuary of neatness. The rulebook is sturdy, with a glossy finish that suggests it’s seen a few “reads” and perhaps a single gaming-session-tear from a dramatically furious player. The counters are clean, punchy, and surprisingly legible for the cardinal sins of non-3D printing: tiny font and heavy rule obscurity. The map boards? Crisp enough that you won’t mistake your supply routes for constellations in the night sky.

### Card Stock and Art Direction

The card deck in 2nd Ed does what card decks should do when you’re trying to simulate WWII logistics without turning the table into a spreadsheet: it’s punchy, with readable icons and a color palette that doesn’t feel like the inside of a broken WWII tank. The art style nods to historical flavor without forcing you to attend a boring slide deck about supply convoys. It’s not going to win a major art prize, but it doesn’t offend your mid-evening aesthetic either. If you’re a “capsule of WW2 vibes in a 60-minute sprint” kind of player, you’ll appreciate the design language here.

### Components Quality in a Word: Reliable

The quality is reliable enough to survive a few accidental shuffles and a sip of coffee that somehow escaped its mug. There are no sharp edges to slice your dice bag; no cards announced a mid-game retirement; no components committed to a black hole of doom in your storage bin. In short: this is the kind of quality that invites you to crack it open again for a quick round—the sign of a well-made 2nd Edition box.

## Rulebook and Setup: It’s Not a Long Lecture, It’s a Short War

Quartermaster General WW2 (2nd Ed) leans into the “quick-to-teach, quick-to-play” ethos. The setup is fast: lay out maps, shuffle decks, hand out roles, and establish supply tokens. The game shines when you realize that you can toss players into the board like a surprise blitz and still have time to complain about the weather while the logistics engine starts whirring. The rulebook uses a clean structure: core rules, then a few optional twists that you can enable if your group wants more complexity without drifting into “board game appendix purgatory.”

### The Flow of a Turn

A typical turn flows like a well-practiced drill: draw a hand, plan a sequence of moves using the action cards, play cards to move armies, secure supply lines, and trigger battles with a mix of luck and correct timing. The deck-building-lite approach means you’ll rely on your card draws to determine your options, but you’ll still feel the brunt of decision fatigue when a critical supply shortage card slides to the top of the deck at the worst possible moment. It’s a little like chess, if the chessboard was a rumor, and the pieces were your logistical ambitions rather than your pawns.

## Gameplay: The Satisfying Chaos of a 60-Minute War

### The Theaters and The Thematic Flavor

The game splits the action into theaters of war, letting players coordinate across different fronts with a shared goal: push the Axis back, reestablish supply airframes, and keep your own troops fed with something other than air-dropped rations (which, in game terms, is a deck of supply cards). The thematic flavor lands nicely: you feel the pressure of limited resources, the urgency of a timetable, and the occasional luck of a favorable card draw that makes you question how you ever doubted the wisdom of your co-op team.

### Deck and Cards: Hand Size, Luck, and Strategic Sacrifice

The card deck is where the game breathes. You’ll encounter cards that push your units forward, others that retrench, and some that force you to decide if you want to invest in air superiority or go heavy on naval logistics. The luck factor is present but not tyrannical. You’ll have moments where the dice of fate roll your way, followed by others where your plan crumbles in a particularly dramatic fashion—perfect for a game night that loves drama as much as strategy.

### The Map: A Theater That Teaches, Not Teeters

The map boards present a snapshot of WWII that’s clean enough to read at a glance but dense enough to reward careful planning. You’ll mark supply routes, identify chokepoints, and delight in the moment your well-timed supply line brings a “here-and-now” burst of momentum. It’s everything a wargame should be: approachable for newcomers, but with a surprising depth if you decide to press your luck with riskier maneuvers.

## Strategy, Balance, and Thematic Flavor

### A Balanced Yet Player-Driven Experience

Balance in a game like this is a matter of perspective: the deck rewards bold plays, but the board punishes imprudent advances with supply penalties that sting. You won’t feel handed a “win” every time you pull the right card, but you’ll feel a thrill when you execute a long-term plan and watch the momentum swing in your favor. The 2nd Edition’s refined components help with that sense of fairness; the improved iconography reduces “rulebook gymnastics” and lets you focus on delicious planning instead.

### Allies, Axis, and The Dynamic Tension

The narrative tension comes not from a single battle, but from the choreography of multiple theaters. You’ll coordinate with your co-op teammates to assign roles: logistics, offensive operations, defense, and perhaps some “let’s check the weather” contingency planning. Your choices ripple across the board, creating a satisfying cascade of near-misses and timely breakthroughs. In practice, that means you might plan to reprint a supply line in one theater while praying that your ally’s airborne card lands just in time to salvage a critical assault elsewhere.

### Thematic Flavor vs. Mechanical Depth

The game nails the feel of WWII logistics without devolving into a bureaucratic expenditure report. You’ll hear the whispers of supply convoys, see the faint glow of air superiority superiority tokens, and feel the ephemeral weight of a strategy that nearly falls apart due to one miscalculated comms card. The flavor is strong enough to satisfy your “what if” fantasies, but pragmatic enough that you won’t drown in minutiae. It’s a sweet spot: thematic enough to matter, mechanical enough to play in a single evening.

## Box Condition and Content Quality: VG+ NM in a World of Shelf-Well-Worn Beauties

### Is Box Wear a Problem or a Badge of Honour?

With a VG+ NM box, you’re looking at a display piece that can easily be the centerpiece of your game shelf. If you’re one of those folks who treats a box like a loved pet—dusting it, talking to it, and occasionally pretending to be a market stall dealer for the counters—this is your kind of wear. The “VG+ NM” label says: you won’t find coffee rings on the map, but you’ll probably spot a couple of mild scuffs that tell the story of a storied shelf life. In the grand tradition of board-game collecting, it’s perfectly acceptable to celebrate lived-in gear when the content remains pristine enough to boast a satisfying playthrough.

### Are the Contents Worth It?

Absolutely. The cards are clean, the tokens are intact, and the rulebook presents a sane path from “what the heck is this” to “we can play this now.” If anything, the revival of the box box is a reminder that this edition was built with a modern sense of component reliability, while not sacrificing the feel of a fast, shareable war-game night. The 2nd Edition’s polish pays off: you won’t be re-reading the rules mid-round, which means more time for banter and the occasional good-natured trash talk about who’s “responsible” for the latest failed supply run.

## How It Compares to First Edition (Spoiler: It’s Better in Small Ways)

If you’ve played the original Quartermaster General WW2, you’ll notice that the 2nd Ed sharpens some pain points. Setup is a touch quicker, the card art is more legible, and the rulebook clarifications reduce those “did we misread this?” moments. The components are improved, though not drastically game-altering; the real win is the smoother experience: fewer ambiguous rules, faster setup, and better flash when you pull off a well-timed supply run that saves a theater. If you loved the first edition but dreaded the odd confusion, you’ll feel right at home with this updated version.

### A Note on Player Count and Session Length

Two players is surprisingly tense, as you pair up roles and attempt to coordinate your limited actions without stepping on each other’s toes. Three to four players offer the sweet spot for a dynamic, chaotic coalition, while five to six can be a spectacularly messy, laughter-filled symposium about logistics and the art of not stepping on the supply line. Session length varies with your pace, but plan on roughly 60–90 minutes for a complete battle-to-battle arc, and maybe an extra 15–20 minutes if you insist on debating the weather cards mid-turn for flavor.

## Who Should Buy This? Targeted Recommendations

- Fans of fast, theatre-scale WWII games who want a shared-victory feel rather than a solo, “I conquered the map” vibe.
- Groups that enjoy a little chaos with their strategy: the deck-driven element tilts events in unexpected directions, creating stories you’ll repeat at future game nights.
- Collectors who want a box that looks good on a shelf and plays well at the table without requiring a three-hour safety briefing.
- Players who want a gateway into more complex war-games without diving into the deep end of rulebooks that resemble treaties.

Not ideal for players who dislike reliance on card draws, or groups that treat tabletop decisions as purely solo surgery rather than collaborative warfare. If you crave a heavy rules monolith, this might feel too breezy. If you want “rules-light, big feelings,” this one earns a place on your table.

## Final Thoughts: Is It Worth It?

Yes, with caveats. If you crave a compact, high-energy WWII experience with a cooperative tilt and enough variability to keep returning players on their toes, the 2nd Edition of Quartermaster General WW2 gives you a solid yes. The box’s VG+ NM condition means you won’t be embarrassed to pull this out on a fancy shelf, and the play experience is robust enough to justify a recurring night. It isn’t a mind-bending monolith; it’s the kind of title that brings friends together, argues about supply routes, and somehow ends with cheers for a successful air mission that felt like a miracle against the odds.

If you’re ready to roll the dice, shuffle the deck, and pretend you’re the chief of logistics in a war room that smells faintly of coffee and old paper maps, this box is your ticket. The 2nd Ed corrections, better art, and sturdier components are the cherry on top of a very workable, very fun little war-game confection.
