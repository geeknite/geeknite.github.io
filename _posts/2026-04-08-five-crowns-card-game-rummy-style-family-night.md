## What is Five Crowns?
Five Crowns is a rummy-inspired card game built around a five-suit deck and multiple rounds of set-and-run building. The core idea is simple: form combinations of cards in your hand and minimize the points left in your hand when the round ends. The deck adds a twist with jokers that act as wild cards, opening up wild combinations and glorious moments of optimistic overreach.

### The deck and rounds
- Five suits means a little more chaos compared to four-suit classics. This isn’t your dad’s standard deck of cards; this is a five-suit party that looks at gravity and says, you shall bend.
- The game is structured into a series of rounds. Across the session, you gradually push your brain toward faster recognition of sets and runs. It’s like training wheels for your strategic brain, only the wheels are made of cardboard and the training is basically snack breaks.
- There are jokers, and jokers are wild. They can stand in for any card, which makes late-round acrobatics possible and early-round despair manageable. Jokers also add a little drama: do you use them early to secure a safe run, or save them for a potential miracle on a future turn? The choice is often brutally beautiful.

### How the play unfolds
- The dealer shuffles, hands are dealt, and a round begins with a fresh draw and a discard. You aim to lay down sets (three or more cards of the same rank) and runs (three or more consecutive cards of the same suit). The more you can lay down, the better your score—though you may still get dinged if you can’t shed your cards fast enough.
- The number of cards you hold in each round generally increases as you progress, creating a gentle escalation from casual fun to, well, still casual but with more stress about not letting a joker slip away.
- The round ends when a player can go out, i.e., deplete their hand by laying down all required sets and runs. Then points are tallied for any cards left in others' hands. The goal is to accumulate the fewest points over all rounds.

## The mechanics: how it feels to play
Five Crowns is all about hand management and pattern recognition in a social, slightly competitive setting. Here’s what the core experience looks like when you’re elbow-deep in the snack zone.

### Sets and runs: the bread and butter
- Sets: three or more cards of the same rank but different suits. The presence of five suits means there are more ranks to combine, which can be both liberating and confusing if you’re trying to track what everyone else has.
- Runs: three or more cards in consecutive order within the same suit. This is where the game shows its elegance, letting players chain together a melody of cards rather than relying on blunt force volleys of high cards.
- Jokers as wilds: they can bend to any need. Do you use them to complete a risky run, or save them to rescue a stubborn hand? Either choice can swing momentum and morale.

### Strategy tips for every player type
- The conservative players: prioritize safe sets and avoid overreaching. Use jokers to shore up uncertain runs rather than force a hero move that might backfire later.
- The risk-takers: chase big, flashy runs late in a round. The joker becomes a tool to unlock dramatic endgame scenarios, but the downside is a higher potential penalty if you misread the table.
- The casual players: focus on getting cards down, even smaller sets or short runs, to minimize your standing until you feel the momentum swinging your way.
- The social players: read expressions, notice table chatter, and use the occasional misdirection to keep your opponents guessing. This is where a family game night becomes a comedic improv show with card counting as the plot device.

### House rules and optional twists
- Speed rounds: reduce the number of rounds but increase the starting cards per hand. This compresses all the tension into a shorter time frame—great for a busy evening.
- Jokers as one-time healers: allow jokers to revert to their wild state at a cost, creating a mini possibility window that players can chase.
- Family handicap: younger players receive a few extra cards early on to keep the game inclusive, ensuring laughter rather than tears when someone yells, I had a run and you stole it with a joker.

- Start with a quick practice round: explain sets, runs, and jokers, and let players experiment with a few cards before formal scoring kicks in.
