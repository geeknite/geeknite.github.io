## The new mechanics

UNO is a simple game: match color or number, yell UNO, and hope the person across the table doesn’t have a better plan. The expansion adds:

- Mercy Cards: when drawn, you can compel a player to skip, draw, or swap hands—but not before you taunt them with your best line.
- Showdown Mode: a set of challenge cards you can play to escalate late-game drama. Think of it as a friendly roast with numbers attached.
- Mercy Tokens: a resource that must be spent to activate certain Mercy effects.

The effect on gameplay is heavy, but not overwhelming. It introduces a social layer: when you play a Mercy Card, the room learns your inner monologue and the table becomes a stage for improvised shading.

## How it changes UNO

The core loop is still UNO: play a card that matches color or number, draw when you can't, yell UNO, flop your plan when someone interrupts. But the new mechanics add:

- A mobile-like feel of 'cool it, I’m not the villain,' while showing off a couple of dramatic 'outplay' moments
- A structured way to taunt: some Mercy lines are witty, some are savage, all within the friendly framework of a party game

## Setup and playthrough

Setting up is simple: lay out the deck, separate the Mercy cards, give players a starting hand, and decide who is 'the mercy host' (the player who tracks showdowns). The rulebook is short but clear. A quick read will have you starting in under five minutes.

During play, look out for Mercy Cards that can flip the table (in a good way). The Showdown Mode introduces mini-challenges that can break a stalemate, like 'Who can sing the chorus from a cartoon intro in under 10 seconds' or 'Who can swap hands without looking at them.' The latter is both dangerous and delightful.

The new mechanics also offer the opportunity to adapt to your own group. If you have players who love shouting, this expansion gives them more opportunities to talk. If you have players who avoid confrontation like a cat avoids water, this expansion can test their resolve to stay silent—and still win.

UNO is easy to pickup, even for players with cognitive or physical limitations. The expansion maintains that core approach, though the added components can be a bit more fiddly for younger players. If your group includes novices, consider a one-card-at-a-time pace while they learn the new mechanics. Visual cues on Mercy Cards are large and bold to help those with sight challenges — though we always recommend a good spread and an accessible table height for everyone involved.
