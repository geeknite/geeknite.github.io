## How to Play: Rules, Pacing, and The Great British Etiquette
### Base Rules (A Quick refresher)
- Each round starts with one player drawing a black card (the prompt).
- The remaining players select a white card from their hand that they believe best matches the prompt.
- The black-card czar, chosen each round by rotation or preference, reviews the submitted white cards and selects the winner.
- The winner keeps the black card as a point; the game continues until a target score is achieved (usually 5, 7, or 10, depending on time and tolerance).

The core loop remains intact: participants attempt to craft the funniest or most shocking response, and the czar’s subjective taste determines the outcome. The Gift Game Card UK version adds a mild territorial twist by ensuring a portion of prompts naturally lean toward British life, making it easier for the UK audience to lend insider knowledge to the humor.

### Pacing and Flow
Pacing is essential in CAH, and Version 2.0’s design assists in maintaining momentum. The prompts are varied enough that rounds don’t stagnate into the same punchlines, while the UK references create moments of recognition that generate a quick laugh and a quick passing of the turn to the next player.

If you’re playing with a larger group, consider a “British Parliament” rotation where the czar role moves clockwise after every round; this keeps everyone engaged and prevents the same person from hogging the spotlight.

### The Gift Night Playbook (Special Rules)
The Gift Night variant is designed for occasions where you’re presenting this as a present or a party-set. Here are some optional rules to enhance the gift-night vibe:
- Gift Mode: Wrapped gift cards are added to the white deck. The player who wins the round keeps a wrapped card as a “gift” for the next round’s exchange.
- Teatime Rule: If a round center punchline references tea or cake, the narrator must take a sip before handing in their card (responsibly, of course).
- Queue Rule: If a prompt features a queue scenario, players must state their “queue strategy” before submitting their card. Best strategy wins a bonus point.

These rules are optional, but they help the room feel more cohesive and playful, especially if you’re hosting a casual gathering rather than a high-stakes tournament.
