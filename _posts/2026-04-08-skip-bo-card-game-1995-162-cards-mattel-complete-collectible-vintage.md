## The 162-card universe explained

### The numbers-first principle
Skip-Bo uses a simple, elegant mechanic: build piles on the table in ascending order from 1 to 12. You win by shedding all your stock pile cards. In the classic 1995 Mattel edition, the deck comprises 144 numbered cards (fourteen? nope—12 values x 12 copies each) plus 18 wild Skip-Bo cards that can stand in for any number, which creates a delicious tension between luck and strategy. The 18 wilds aren’t random; they’re a resource you must manage like a tiny, caffeinated army.

### Wilds and strategy
The wilds are not filler; they are the heartbeat of the engine. The right moment to drop a Skip-Bo when your build piles are staring you down can turn a potential stalemate into a sprint. ## How Skip-Bo actually works: a quick primer

### Setup
1) Each player takes a stock pile face-down on their designated spot. 2) Five cards form each player’s hand from their own draw pile. 3) The table gets four empty build piles to be filled with ascending sequences. 4) The rest becomes a central draw pile for the players to harvest from on their turns. This setup is a ritual—the instant you place the first card on a build pile, a quiet tension fills the room because you know the chaos is coming.

### Turn structure
On your turn you can:
- Play a card from your hand or the top card of your stock pile onto any build pile in ascending order.
- If you can’t immediately play, draw from your stock pile to refill your hand and consider your options for the next turn.
- You may also use a wild Skip-Bo card to bridge a gap to the next number in line.

### Building to 12 and aiming to win
The objective is to empty your stock pile. Build piles must advance in exact sequences from 1 to 12. Once you complete a pile (i.e., you place a 12 on top of a 11, etc.), that pile is out of the way and frees up space for new builds. The first player to shed all their stock cards wins the round. Simple, glorious, and mercilessly fast once the group hits a rhythm.

### Common misplays and how to avoid them
- Overlooking a top card: Always scan the top cards of all players’ hands and piles before making a move.
- Misusing wilds: Don’t burn a wild when you could instead place a straight, sheathing yourself in a better position for the next turn.
- Hawking the deck too long: The longer you wait, the more your opponents sprint—don’t become the chess player who forgot to move.
