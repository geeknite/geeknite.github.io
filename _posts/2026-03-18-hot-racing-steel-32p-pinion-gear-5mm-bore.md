The 32P gear standard and 5mm bore
For those new to the 32P lingo, it’s a tooth profile standard that has persisted because it works. A 32P pinion is a good compromise: enough teeth to mesh with typical spur gears, while offering a reasonable contact ratio to avoid slipping at high RPM. The 5mm bore is the most common motocross for many small-to-mid sized brushless motors. It’s not universal, but it’s widely supported, which means upgrades and replacements are easier to source. If you’re upgrading from a plastic or aluminum pinion, steel adds a reliability layer that many drivers appreciate for long sessions.

Weight and inertia
Steel is heavier than aluminum or plastic options. In a rock-solid chassis, that extra weight translates to inertia that helps resist sudden torque spikes from rough tracks. The downside? You’ll feel a slight delay when you switch from micro-acceleration to hard acceleration, especially on 2S setups or smaller 1/10 scale platforms. The trade-off is typically worth it for those running high-power motors on dusty or wet tracks where gear wear would otherwise bite back.

The bore is precisely sized for a 5mm shaft, which reduces slippage and misalignment. If you’re curious about the steel’s resilience, it’s the kind of gear that takes a beating on heavy throttle and returns for more. This is not the gear you want if you’re aiming for the lightest possible weight; this is a reliability play.

Fitment and compatibility
The 5mm bore is the star; it fits most 5mm shafts on brushless motors, including sensored and unsensored varieties. If you run a pump of a motor in a custom config, you may need to adjust the motor mount or spacing to maintain correct mesh with your spur gear. The pinion’s depth (the distance from the face of the spur to the back face of the pinion) should be considered. For many setups, you’ll want a shallow depth to avoid mesh too deep in the bore that could cause binding or wobble.

Installation notes
- Clean shafts and bores to remove burrs or residues that could compromise fit.
- Install the pinion with the set screw in the correct orientation; some gears have a slightly bevelled face that should align with the spur to reduce noise.
- Use a small threadlocker on the set screw if vibration is heavy or you operate at high RPM. Be careful not to contaminate the gear teeth.
- After installation, rotate the drivetrain by hand to confirm smooth engagement with the spur; a grinding or rubbing sound often indicates misalignment or burrs.

This is where your setup becomes a ritual rather than a chore. The alignment is the moment of truth: if you can get the mesh right, your car will shout with joy (or at least bark a satisfying click).

Performance and testing
In controlled bench tests, steel pinions like the 32P 5mm bore show their value in torque-rich conditions. The steel’s resistance to wear translates into consistent mesh, especially after extended runs where aluminum would gradually flatten or wear. In real-world driving, you’ll notice a couple of things:
- Durable engagement: The gear teeth hold alignment over longer sessions, reducing the chance of slip.
- Stable torque delivery: You’ll see consistent acceleration, with fewer mesh dips as the temperature climbs.
- Slight drag: The steel pinion’s friction characteristics are slightly higher than aluminum. This isn’t a deal-breaker, but it’s worth acknowledging if you’re chasing the last tenth-of-a-second.

Heat and longevity
Under heavy throttle and higher voltage, heat management is critical. Steel dissipates heat differently from aluminum; you may see your motor run at slightly different temps depending on mesh and gear choice. If you’re pushing a hot 6S setup, ensure your motor heatsinks, cooling, and battery management are up to the task. The steel pinion can reduce the chance of the teeth bending under heavy load, but you should keep an eye on the spur teeth and bearings; heat can accelerate wear, especially if your mesh is too tight or too loose.

Mesh and noise
The mesh should be a goldilocks zone: not too tight, not too loose. With a steel pinion, you may notice a louder whirr if you have a very tight mesh, but with proper tuning, you should achieve a satisfying mechanical purr. If you’re building a show-off truck that stands still for photos, the steel grind sound might become part of the ambience; if you’re a racer chasing suppressor noise, you’ll want to tune accordingly.

Maintenance tips
- Periodic inspection: Check for chips or wear on the pinion teeth after a few sessions, particularly on heavy sand or dust environments.
- Cleaning: Remove debris that can accumulate in the teeth; use a soft brush or compressed air, but avoid blowing into the bore.
- Lubrication: Use light gear oil or a dedicated RC gear lube; avoid heavy greases that attract dust.
- Set screw checks: Verify torque on the set screw after a few runs to avoid the chance of the pinion slipping.

Alternatives and comparisons
If steel isn’t your vibe, consider these other options:
- Aluminum pinions: Lightweight with fast acceleration but wear quickly under heavy loads.
- Brass or titanium variants: Higher cost and unique density that can affect inertia and heat.
- Plastic pinions: Cheap and quiet, but wear out quickly under high power, often leading to mesh issues.
- OEM or specific brand alternatives: Some brands offer proprietary alloy or surface coatings that claim longer life.

Value for money
Steel pinions, especially from reputable brands like Hot Racing, usually command a premium. You’re paying for durability, predictability, and the ability to sustain performance in high-stress conditions. If your gear train sees extended sessions or you frequently push your rig with high-power motors, you’ll likely find steel pinions to be a cost of doing business. If your usage is casual or you’re just experimenting, you might prefer aluminum to cut weight and price.
