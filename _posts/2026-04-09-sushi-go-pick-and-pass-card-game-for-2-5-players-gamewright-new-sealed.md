## Overview

Sushi Go! is a pick and pass card drafting game published by Gamewright. The concept is simple: each player starts with a hand of cards, chooses one card to keep, then passes the rest to the next player. The process repeats for several rounds, with players trying to collect specific sets of cards to score points. The sealed copy for two to five players is the core, unadorned experience that has teacher-friendly simplicity and depth for veteran players who enjoy optimizing tiny decisions.

In Geeknite style, the premise is a literal sushi conveyor belt in card form. You glimpse the cards for a fleeting moment, pick a plate that fits your strategy, and pass the rest to your left. ## How to Play: The Fast, Fun Drafting Mechanic

The core mechanism is deceptively simple: draft and pass. Each player starts with a hand of cards. On your turn, you pick one card to keep for scoring and pass the rest to the player on your left. The round ends when all cards have been taken, then you tally points for the round and begin the next round with a fresh hand. In practice, you feel like you are playing a quick mini puzzle for every turn, with a new clue every few seconds.

### Rounds and Scoring Foundations

Sushi Go! is typically played over a series of rounds (the number of rounds varies by player count). Scoring centers on assembling specific combinations:

- Nigiri cards: each has a different point value, and some require you to collect multiple copies to maximize points.
- Sashimi cards: usually a set that pays out when you collect three of them; two might be useless, making the risk-reward feel tactile.
- Tempura and Dumplings: set collections that reward you as you amass more of them.
- Maki rolls: these contribute points for having the most in a round, creating a light heads-up dynamic.
- Pudding: a longer-term scoring payoff that sticks around after the final tally of the game, sometimes swinging the outcome in surprising ways.

The two to five player variant tends to create a nice balance between competition and collaboration without crossing into chaos. In two players, you get a quicker tempo and tighter decision windows; with five players, you get more diverse card pools, more blocking opportunities, and more opportunities for opponents to steal your good combos by selecting a card you might have coveted next round.

### The Decision Matrix on Your Turn

On each turn, the decision is typically framed as: which card can you safely keep to advance your best scoring line, while also denying your neighbor the opportunity to grab something that looks valuable? The answer is rarely straightforward because some cards excel in specific sets, while others are merely filler that round out your hand. This tension is the heartbeat of Sushi Go!: a game of micro-optimizations that rarely leaves you bored.

### End-of-Round and End-of-Game Timing

Rounds repeat until the deck is exhausted. After every round, players tally their points for the cards that have resolved in that round. Pudding cards count only at the very end of the game, which means you might be blithely accumulating pudding while chasing a different primary objective. End-game timing matters; don’t sleep on pudding because it can swing final tallies when everything else is tied up in a cashmere-thin point margin.

## Strategy Across Player Counts

Strategy in Sushi Go! scales nicely with player count, but the core ideas stay consistent. Here are some pragmatic tips to navigate both 2-player and 5-player games:

### Two Players: Focus and Finesse
- Target a clear primary objective for the round, such as completing a specific set of nigiri or salad cards, and commit to it for the entire round.
- Monitor card flow. In a smaller player count, the same valuable card might circulate more quickly. If you pass on a card you want, hope your opponent deprives someone else instead of you of the same card.
- Be mindful of patterns. In two-player games, you control more of the deck’s rhythm, so your choices can be more deterministic. Use that to set traps and force your partner into suboptimal paths.

### Five Players: Diversify and Disrupt
- Prioritize flexible sets. With more players, the card pool becomes broader; you can chase multiple score options without getting locked into a single strategy.
- Pay attention to what others chase. Denial becomes an effective weapon because a single wrong pick can derail someone else’s plan and alter the round’s dynamics.
- Plan for end-game pudding late. Pudding is less about immediate points and more about a long-term swing factor. If you can accumulate pudding cards without sacrificing your primary objectives, you set yourself up for a late-game victory push.
