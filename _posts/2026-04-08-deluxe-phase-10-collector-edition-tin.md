## The Phase 10 Core: How It Works (In Case You Need a refresher)

Phase 10 is a raucous, mildly chaotic race to complete ten phases. Each phase has a specific combination, like two sets of three, plus a run of four, etc. The catch is that you can only advance to the next phase after you’ve completed the current one—hence the name “Phase 10.” It’s a non-cooperative, hand-management game with a heavy emphasis on pattern-building and turn-to-turn planning with a dash of luck from the card draw.

Key mechanics to remember:
- There are four colors and number cards 1-12, with some wild cards and skip cards mixed in.
- Each player starts at Phase 1 and must complete it before moving to Phase 2, and so on, until someone completes Phase 10.
- If you can finish your phase before others, you’ll do a little victory dance in your seat while the rest of the table mutters “Next round.”

The base rules haven’t changed much since 1990s card-gami. It’s a game that can spiral into hilarity when someone miscounts or when you draw the exact card you need—and then promptly discard it on your own turn. That’s classic Phase 10 in a nutshell: a blend of math, memory, and mischief—seasoned with a little luck and a lot of friendly banter.

For the sake of completeness and to jog memory: a typical round involves players drawing a card, attempting to arrange their hand into the current phase, and discarding. The first person to finish all ten phases wins the round; the game continues until someone has won a predetermined number of rounds.

If you want a quick refresher on the phases themselves, you can skim through an abbreviated guide here: [Phase 10 Phases Overview]{% post_url 2023-11-02-phase-10-phases-overview %}.

 
## The Phases, in Brief (For the Curious, Not For The Rule Lawyer)

- Phase 1: Two sets of two cards (two pairs)
- Phase 2: One set of three + One run of four
- Phase 3: One four-of-a-kind + One run of four
- Phase 4: Five consecutive cards of the same color (a long run, basically a mini sprint)
- Phase 5: A three-of-a-kind + A four-of-a-kind
- Phase 6: A full house (three of one kind + a pair)
- Phase 7: A run of seven
- Phase 8: A run of 8
- Phase 9: A run of nine
- Phase 10: A run of 10 (or a cocktail of patterns depending on the edition)

Which sounds spicy until you realize that your opponents are also plotting their own phase combos. Welcome to the thrilling world of “I hope this is the card I want, not the one I don’t.”
