## Setup, installation, and tips
Installation is mostly straightforward if you have a level surface to mount on and a few common hand tools. The mounting holes line up with standard patterns on many bumpers, but you should measure and confirm before drilling. The instruction sheet provides a typical wiring diagram and a few notes on wiring safety. If you are new to winching, take a moment to assess the load path and the anchor point. Do not winch under a vehicle if you cannot clearly see the situation. A good anchor point is something sturdy, such as a welded frame member or a solid tree that will be kind enough not to snap under pressure.

When you configure the rope, keep it neat on the drum and avoid over winding or under winding. The synthetic rope dislikes kinks and will degrade if the drum is overloaded with twists. A little maintenance goes a long way here. After a heavy pull, re-spool the rope slowly to ensure it sits flat and does not develop a bird nest that would resemble a modern art sculpture more than a functional line.

The two remotes give you flexibility. The wireless link is robust enough for a few meters of distance, which is usually enough for you to stand on the bumper and pretend you know exactly what you are doing. The wired remote offers a fail safe if the wireless link is down, something you will appreciate when the elements conspire to disrupt your signal near the mud pits. A quick tip: keep extra fuses and a set of tools in the glove box, because you never know when you will need to perform a roadside maintenance opera to the sound of a dropping playlist.

If you are a neat freak, you will appreciate the synthetic rope color and texture. If you are a rebel, you might test it by dragging a stubborn obstacle with a bit more swagger than recommended. Either way, you should respect the forces at play and avoid overloading the line since the purpose of the winch is to assist, not to become a catapult.

## Safety first, always
Winching is simple until it stops being simple. The key is to maintain a safe working zone, keep spectators at a distance, and never stand in the rope path.
