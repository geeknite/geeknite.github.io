## What is Skip-Bo
Skip-Bo is a shedding type card game where players try to get rid of all their stock cards by playing them in sequential piles from 1 to 12. The deck includes a handful of wild cards that can stand in for any number, and players draw cards to replenish their hand as the action goes around the table. The 1986 International Games edition, while very much a product of its era, adheres to the same bones with a few 80s flourishes. It is a game of tempo and misdirection, where misplay can be as entertaining as a perfectly timed hand reveal.

### How to play the 1986 edition
Skip-Bo plays fast and loose, because the rules in 1986 were designed to get you into action quickly, not to spawn a post match debrief on game theory. Here is the basic flow that aligns with the classic experience:
- The aim is to be the first player to shed all the cards from your stock pile by placing them in sequence on building piles starting at 1 and working up to 12.
- Each turn you draw to a certain hand size and play as many cards as you can from your hand and from your stock into the building piles.
- Skip-Bo wild cards can stand in for any number, which gives you a little tactical flexibility and also the chance to needle your siblings with a parting shot when you drop a wild card to complete a race to the finish.
- The first player to empty their stock pile wins the round, and a new round begins.

### House rules and variations
Retro games shine when players toss in their own house rules. For this edition you can try:
- Quick-fire mode: remove the stock pile entirely after you run out of cards in hand, speeding up rounds for a chaotic but joyful vibe.
- Family alliance mode: allow players to team up in pairs for a few rounds to emphasize cooperation rather than cutthroat competition.
- Photo finish rule: require players to display their last card to the table before playing to add last second drama.

### How to care for this edition
Storage matters when dealing with older card games. A cool, dry place, away from direct sunlight, will help preserve the colors and the card stock. If the edges show a bit of fray, you can sleeve the cards to prevent further wear and to keep your friends from complaining about sticky fingers. I recommend placing the box inside a larger storage container to avoid crushing the lid, and adding a silica gel pack to maintain humidity in the storage area. This is not a high maintenance game, but a little care will ensure it keeps delivering the same nostalgic vibe for years to come.
