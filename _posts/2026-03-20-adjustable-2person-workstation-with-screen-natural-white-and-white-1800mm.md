## Setup and Assembly: The Real-World Test
### What You’ll Need
- A bit of time (roughly 1–2 hours depending on your alignment with “read the manual once, then improvise”).
- A standard screwdriver set and a rubber mallet (for stubborn joints—but only if you’re into satisfying mechanical sounds when you tighten a bolt).
- A friend with a decent sense of spatial awareness. Two minds are better than one when you’re aligning two monitor arms and a central screen within 1800mm.

### Step-by-Step (High Level)
1. Unbox and sort hardware into labeled bags. This is the part where you’ll discover you own more washers than an antique clock store.
2. Assemble the frame as per the schematic. Expect some minor alignments adjustments—this is normal for wide desks. Don’t force a bolt if it doesn’t feel right; back out and try another hole.
3. Attach the central screen mount or dual monitor arms. The arms should glide smoothly, with micro-adjustments possible for height and tilt.
4. Route cables through the tray and up into the grommets. The goal is quiet, clean lines and no tripping hazards if you stand up in a hurry.
5. Tighten everything to spec. Give the frame a little shake—if it wobbles, revisit balance adjustments.
6. Place the workstations and set each user’s preferred height. A quick test for comfort: do you get a relaxed shoulder angle and a neutral wrist position while typing and mousing?

The assembly is generally straightforward for a two-person setup. If you’re solo, it’s still doable, but you’ll benefit from a helper for the initial alignment and for mounting the arms on opposite sides without turning the task into a physics exam.

## Screen Integration and Tech Considerations
### Screen Ergonomics
The central screen (or the pair of arms) is the showpiece. It’s designed to be easily reachable and adjustable for both players in a two-person setup. If you’re using a single central monitor, aim for a screen size between 32 and 34 inches; if you’re pairing two 27-inch monitors, ensure the arms can handle the angular range without hitting each other. Pro-tip: set your primary monitor on the side you use most to minimize neck strain from repetitive glances.

### Cable Management as a Feature
Cable trays are not glamorous, but they are essential. A well-managed cable system reduces the risk of snagging arms or yanking a monitor during a tense debugging session. The included routing channels are usually enough for the typical home office configuration, though if you’re running a bevy of USB-C hubs or external GPUs, you may want to add a small cable loom to keep things tidy and future-proof.

### Compatibility Notes
- Monitor arms typically support VESA mounting patterns up to 100x100 mm or 75x75 mm, depending on the model. Confirm your monitor’s VESA specs before ordering and ensure the arms’ weight rating covers your monitors.
- If you plan to upgrade to a larger display in the future, check the maximum load on the arms and frame. Some setups may require a beefier frame or additional reinforcement for long-term durability.
