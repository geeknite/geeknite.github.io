## Unboxing and first play

Set up is intentionally simple. Shuffle, deal 10 cards to each player, the rest forming a draw pile. The top card of the draw pile is flipped to form the discard pile. The rules are presented in a compact format that is easy to skim during a tense moment. The first player can begin by either drawing from the draw pile or taking the top discard. The phase you are working on must be completed by the end of your turn, after which you can move to the next phase if you are ready or continue building toward the current one. The 25th Anniversary edition does not alter the fundamental timing, but the improved readability of the rulebook does help with a quick recall of the baseline rules when you come back to the table after a few months away.

### Phase by phase strategy tips

- Phase 1 and 2 are bread and butter. Early on, focus on forming at least one set and one run that share cards. Try to funnel a hand toward a phase you can complete within two turns. You want a couple of solid discard options and not end with a hand full of high cards that you cannot use.
- Phase 3 and 4 become more about planning ahead. If you can, keep track of what your opponents appear to be collecting and adapt your plans. Reading the table is an underrated skill that pays off big time here.
- Phases 5 to 7 introduce bigger demands. You will want to pick up cards that help you cover more than one phase or that prevent your opponents from easily stealing the momentum by finishing quickly.
- Phases 8 and 9 demand careful risk management. You might hold onto a few wildcard options to adapt to what the table is building. Do not be afraid to take a few extra turns to optimize a phase, but be mindful of the table pace if you aim to prevent someone from finishing early.
- Phase 10 is the finish line. The final phase is often a test of whether you have kept enough flexibility to bridge any gaps. A nimble hand can salvage a late rush, but a static hand can ruin a good run. The Anniversary edition helps you feel these decisions by giving you more weighty visual cues in the cards themselves.
