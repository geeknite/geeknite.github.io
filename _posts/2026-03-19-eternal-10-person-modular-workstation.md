## What is the Eternal 10 Person Modular Workstation?

This isn’t a single desk. It’s a system: ten individual workstations that snap into a cohesive, scalable network. The core idea is simple: give you a central spine for routing power, data, and ergonomic adaptors, plus modular bays that each team can customize for monitors, docking stations, or 3D-printed cup holders shaped like tiny dragons. The result is a long, collaborative workspace that can be rearranged into clusters for pair programming, triads for design reviews, or a straight line for the marathon build-a-thon where everyone blames the forklift that moved the coffee machine.

## Design philosophy and modularity

The Eternal’s core claim is modularity without chaos. It’s not a kit you assemble and disassemble weekly; it’s a long-term layout designed to adapt as teams grow, shrink, or pivot from “We code” to “We design.” The spine acts as the central nervous system: power, data, and ergonomic routing ping out to modular arms that slot into place with a satisfying click. The system favors ergonomic variety: users can swap from a two-monitor setup to a three-monitor grid or, for the dreamers among us, a curved ultrawide with a companion display.

### Cable management: the secret sauce
If you’ve ever spent an afternoon wrestling a nest of cables that your cat could navigate with confidence, you’ll appreciate the Eternal’s cable channels. A trunk-like core runs beneath the surface, with modular drop-ins that let you route power, USB-C, HDMI, ethernet, and the occasional stray audio jack. There are cutouts for grommets in each bay that seal when not in use and a “shoreline” management system that keeps long cables from wild loops and aerodynamic embarrassment during a sprint to the deadline.

### Ergonomics by design
Ergonomics aren’t an afterthought here; they’re a design constraint. The height-adjustment motors are quiet enough to not wake a sleeping designer, and the desk’s surface separation prevents the “my elbow is trying to high-five your monitor” syndrome. The bays can hold keyboard trays, mouse surfaces, or even a snack shelf—because firmware updates aren’t the only thing that deserves a high-altitude snack break.

## Assembly, setup, and getting started

You’ll likely need a small crew of two to four people for a comfortable assembly window, depending on your state of caffeination and how many times someone says “anchor point” and actually means “this is where gravity gets excited.” The company provides a modular toolkit with standardized fasteners, spare washers, and a fancy Allen key that’s basically a Swiss Army knife for desks. The upfront investment in time pays off in the long run as you realize you can reorganize the entire layout in under an hour—the “mom-dad-robot” version of a team retrospective where you actually get a table that doesn’t require a forklift to move.

### Step-by-step (spoiler-free)
1. Unpack the spine modules and verify all connectors are present. The moment you see the first “click,” you’ll understand why your childhood LEGO habit was a training period for this moment.
2. Install the central trunk and route initial cables to the spine. It’s a bit like wiring a small, friendly dragon—if the dragon wore a cardigan and smelled faintly of coffee.
3. Attach bays according to your team’s layout. The design uses color-coded connectors and modular footprints so you don’t end up with a row of “why does this one fit here?” moments.
4. Secure surface panels, attach the monitor arms, and calibrate the height settings. A good calibration session saves you from physics-related humiliation during a keynote.
5. Connect power and data. If you’re in a shared space, coordinate with your IT team to avoid a synchronized blackout that becomes a team cry for ‘more outlets.’
