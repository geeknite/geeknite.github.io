## What is All Out War Anyway? A Quick Overview

Zombicide: Walking Dead - All Out War is a standalone box that bundles the core gameplay of Zombicide with a Walking Dead license—think of it as a curated, flavor-packed experience with more character-centric storytelling and fewer generic NPCs. Themed around the survivalist tension of a post-apocalyptic world, the ruleset stays true to the “easy to learn, hard to master” ethos that fans of the series adore. You won’t find a grand novel-length rulebook—the text is concise, with plenty of examples and diagrams to get you to the table without needing a PhD in zombie studies. 

The big selling point? It’s co-op. You and your friends are the good guys… sort of. The survivors you control will have efficient turns, bad luck will happen, and if you’re unlucky, you’ll end up yelling at the sky as a line of walkers overrun your position. It’s part tactical combat, part dice-rolling fantasy, and all chaos—delicious, table-side chaos. 

In terms of aesthetics, All Out War leans into the gritty, muddy palette of the TV show—rusty weapons, stained clothing, and a neon-lit desperation that somehow makes the miniatures feel like they’re about to go on a coffee run before collapsing into a pile of undead. The box art is sharp, the component quality is typical CMON—solid plastic, clean sculpting, and a few sculpted details that make your tabletop look cooler than your life choices, which is a nice trick. 

If you’re a fan of the show and you crave a board game that captures the “every decision could be your last” vibe, All Out War aims to satisfy that itch while giving you a mechanism that scales from cozy co-op to something that feels like a small-scale skirmish wargame. And yes, the game also includes some light campaign elements where survivors level up, gain new gear, and unlock abilities. It’s not a full RPG campaign by any means, but it scratches the itch for long, story-rich sessions. 

## Box Contents and Setup: What You Actually Get on the Table

The All Out War box is not the lightest in the Zombicide family, but it’s nicely organized. Here’s a quick tour:

- A central game board with modular tiles that let you customize the cityscape. It’s not a megacampaign map, but the tile system is flexible enough to keep setups feeling fresh.
- A cadre of Survivor miniatures—each with distinct silhouettes and weapon options. The production value of the sculpts is consistently good, with enough personality to tell the survivors apart at a glance.
- A swarm of Walker miniatures—varied poses, some with tattered clothes, some with screaming faces, all with the same “you’ll regret this” look on their faces.
- Combat tokens, item cards, and mission cards. The item/cards provide your gear options, from reliable firearms to improvised melee weapons, which usually means you’ll either feel like a tactical genius or a chaotic danger to your own teammates. 
- A small deck of mission goals and event cards that push the players into decisions with real consequences. The event cards add the dreaded random factor that makes every session feel alive, which is a fancy way of saying “prepare for chaos.”
- A well-annotated rulebook with quick-reference sheets. The reference material is clear enough that you won’t need to pause the game mid-battle to figure out whether you can shoot through your own teammate’s line of sight by accident. 

Setup time is reasonable. You won’t be building an entire city skyline to play a skirmish, but you will spend a few minutes arranging fences, setting up the initial zombie wave, and distributing starting gear. If you’re a rule-nerd who loves efficiency, you’ll map out your first couple of turns in your head while your friends bicker about who gets to be Daryl (or Rick, if you insist on leadership drama). The good news: the game’s pacing tends to keep everyone engaged, even if you’re losing Survivors to a single, unassuming roll of the dice.

## How All Out War Plays: Core Rules Without the Blindfold

The core loop is recognizable if you’ve played Zombicide before: players coordinate to remove zombies, rescue survivors, and accomplish mission goals while the board becomes more dangerous with every cadence. Here’s a breakdown of the essential mechanics, with a geek-friendly twist.

### Turn Structure: Plan, Act, Grieve
Every round comprises an initiative phase, an action phase, and the inevitable cleanup. Your Survivors take turns in order, chosen from a pool that suggests the group’s leadership dynamics—some players run the calm tactician, others the reckless risk-taker, and a third person who only shows up to carry the loot. The zombie side “activates” after players, following a simple drawing-rule: you reveal the top card of the zombie deck, which dictates which walkers advance, which claws swing, and how many new threats spawn. It’s combat chaos with a little bit of predictable pattern, which is perfect for a game that wants you to second-guess your plan every couple of rounds. 

Combat uses a standard hit-miss mechanic with dice pools—one of those things you either love or tolerate because it feels fair. The best/worst part: if you roll a perfect storm of bad luck, you’ll see a cluster of walkers gobbling up crucial positions; if you roll well, you’ll feel like a tactical genius who somehow managed to shoot a zombie in the head, and possibly also catch your breath from the adrenaline of the moment. There’s a built-in tension between long-range planning and short-hop improvisation, which is exactly the sweet spot that makes cooperative games exciting rather than merely cooperative. 

### Survivors and Gear: A Cast of Characters with Distinct Voices
Each Survivor has a card with moves, an equipment ladder, and sometimes a unique ability. The abilities give you moments of “oh, that’s surprisingly clever” and “why did we choose the trait that makes me susceptible to fear tests right now?” It’s a reflection of the genre—your heroes aren’t invincible; they’re people who are good at surviving because they’re stubborn and occasionally lucky. Gear cards offer the usual suspects: pistols, shotguns, shotguns with better aim, improvised weapons, and sometimes crossbow bolts that you swear you’ll use someday after you stop tripping over a chair in your apartment. The joy is that gear often changes how you handle a given scenario, forcing you to re-evaluate your target priority and your risk tolerance. 

### The Walking Dead Tax: Pressure and Scarcity
Only a fool would claim the walkers are optional; the walkers are a slow, relentless tax on your planning. Every card flip, every spawn, reminds you: you either clear space or you risk being overwhelmed. The All Out War box does a good job of escalating tension gradually, with each round bringing new threats into play and forcing you to adapt. It’s not about who has the best aim in a vacuum; it’s about who can coordinate the strongest defensive line, with a fallback plan for when your wall breaks and you’re forced into the “every survivor for themselves” scenario that often feels more authentic to the theme. 

### Campaign vs. One-Shot Scenarios: To Campaign or Not to Campaign
All Out War offers a gentle-yet-substantive campaign lane alongside individual mission cards. The campaign allows survivals to level up and choose new gear, which adds a long-term incentive to keep playing. It’s not the deepest strategic campaign you’ll find in a genre that prides itself on “serious game design,” but it’s satisfying enough to justify multiple sessions. The alternative—one-and-done missions—are perfect for the quick game night, especially when your group is busy with life, a cat, or the grand redirection of a streaming service you forgot to cancel. 

If you’re into narrative resonance, the mission cards offer short, thematic prompts that nudge the table toward a story. One moment you’ll be saving a child in a rooftop sequence; the next, you’ll be debating whether to split the party to cover more ground or stay together and risk a big zombie tide. The storytelling potential is surprisingly decent for a box that also demands precise dice rolls and resource management. 

## The Thematic Glow and Aesthetics: Does It Feel Like The Show? 

The Walking Dead license shines through in the character designs, the zombie aesthetics, and the general mood of the art. The miniatures are robust enough to stand on a tabletop without the need for a magnifying glass, and the terrain bits support a believable street-to-suburban environment that never breaks immersion. There’s a certain grimy charm to the set that’s appealing whether you’re a die-hard fan of the show or simply someone who enjoys a post-apocalyptic mood lighting in the form of a table full of plastic figurines. 

If you’re chasing the “cinematic moment” at the table, All Out War does deliver. The moments where a Survivor narrowly escapes a horde or when you fumble your way into a critical hit feel like something straight out of a tense two-episode arc. The only caveat: the thematic weight sometimes clashes with the practical realities of gaming time. There are nights when you’ll want to immerse yourself in the drama; there are nights when you’ll be grateful for the quick missions that don’t require a campaign-level commitment. The game handles both directions with competence. 

## Tabletop Experience: Table Presence, Components, and Longevity

From a distance, Zombicide: Walking Dead - All Out War looks impressive on the table. The miniatures carry the cinematic vibe you’d hope for, and the color palette is accessible enough to keep confusion at bay even during a moment of pure adrenaline. The components hold up well after repeated play; you’ll find enough variety in gear and zombie types to keep your group from falling into a repetitive groove too quickly. 

Durability is a practical concern for any boxed game with a cast of wobbly characters. The plastic magnets that hold pledge boards to the tray? For the tech-heads and table-regulators: the game scales surprisingly well. You can adjust the number of walkers for lighter sessions, or dial up the difficulty for a serious challenge. It’s not a one-note experience; it invites you to calibrate the chaos to your table’s temperament.

## Co-Op Nature: Teamwork, Tension, and Turbulent Trust

All Out War is best enjoyed when the players embrace the co-op. You’ll discuss your plan in the planning phase, adapt during the zombie surge, and celebrate the small victories that come with a well-executed turn. The group dynamic is part of the game’s charm; you’ll see players fall into roles—one becomes the vox of reason, another the risk-taker who’s willing to gamble a little to push through a doorway, and a third who acts as the moral compass, reminding everyone that this is just a game and yes, you’ll probably need therapy later. It’s goofy and occasionally poignant, which is a delightful combination for a zombie-splashed session. 

The flip side: the game can become punishing when you roll a few fat dice and watch your carefully laid plans collapse in a cascade of bad outcomes. That tension is by design, but it’s worth noting that some sessions can drift toward frustration if players are chasing an unrealistic perfect run. The cure is simple: set expectations, adjust the mission difficulty, and don’t be afraid to laugh at the ridiculousness of your own mistakes. The “All Out War” in the title is not just theater; it’s a reminder that you’re signing up for a chaotic, messy, glorious ride together. 

## Replayability, Longevity, and How It Feels After You’ve Played It a While

Replayability in All Out War comes from two places: mission variety and the civically unpredictable walker deck. The missions offer enough variety to keep a campaign interesting across several sessions, and the walker deck ensures that no two rounds unfold in exactly the same way. You’ll discover new strategies, test out different Survivor builds, and realize that sometimes the best plan is no plan at all—just move forward and react. 

From a longevity standpoint, All Out War has legs, but not infinite legs. It’s a game you’ll reach for when you want a robust cooperative experience that respects both group dynamics and your own desire for a little tactical flavor. Don’t expect a 400-hour campaign or a deep character progression tree; the balance is more toward quick-to-learn, satisfying to master, and easy to revisit on a weeknight. If you crave a deeper, more narrative-driven experience, you’ll probably want to pair All Out War with a separate RPG-like system or a dedicated campaign box.
