If you own the physical 2nd Edition, the digital version won’t replace the tactile joy of shuffling real cards and watching a friend try to read the table like it’s a weather report. However, the digital edition does something the real-world version can sometimes struggle with: it removes friction. You don’t have to pass a handful of cards around the table or remind everyone of the exact discard restrictions; the game does that work for you and lets you focus on communicating, cooperating, and occasionally laughing at the degree of panic in your partner’s voice when a critical move slips away.

The trade-off is that you lose some of the tactile rituals—the feel of card stock, the clack of the table, the goofy shuffle shanties that card collectors instinctively perform while waiting for a decisive round. If that is non-trivial to you, you may still reach for the physical copy for those special nights. If you value convenience, replayability, and the joy of playing with people who might live far away, the digital version is a big win.
