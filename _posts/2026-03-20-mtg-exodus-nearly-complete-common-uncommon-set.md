## The color-by-color breakdown
We will go color by color, calling out typical two mana to four mana bodies, removal, and utility spells within Exodus commons/uncommons. We will also discuss synergy with artifact cards in the set and how your draft environment shapes the value of these cards.

### White
White in Exodus is often about efficient early blockers and life gain or board control with minimal mana. White commons anchor your aggressive and defensive builds, serving as the glue that keeps a midrange battlefield stabilized.

Possible archetypes to consider:
- Aggro tempo with early creatures that trade evenly and push damage while your opponent stumbles on blockers.
- Widespread anthem effects that buff your small army across multiple turns.

Uncommons in white offer slightly more efficient bodies or protection spells, offering your casual decks more resilience.

### Blue
Blue is often the trickiest color to pin down in Exodus: a combination of card draw, counterspells, and tempo. As with other sets from the era, blue commons tend to push tempo or provide options for control at a modest price. The near complete set in blue can reward builders who enjoy stacking incremental card advantage or preventing an opponent’s big plays.

Common blue cards might occasionally include early decision counterspells or bounce effects; uncommons could offer better draw engines or more reliable counter options. If you are aiming for a balanced binder, blue is your go to for a few surprising play sequences that catch opponents off guard.

### Black
Black in Exodus often features removal and discard themes, possibly with a few reanimated or swamp-hinged effects. Common removal spells and cheap discard can help you keep a dominated board state, while uncommons usually present more efficient creatures or slightly more painful effects for your opponent.

Key considerations for black:
- Early removal that trades with your opponent’s early threats.
- Card-draw or graveyard-synergy options in the uncommons that enable grindy games.

### Red
Red is typically your go-to for face-smashing aggression and direct damage. In the common/uncommon pool, red cards often emphasize speed and tempo. You will likely find early two two for s or three one s that press the assault, a few burn spells, and perhaps the occasional direct damage effect to push through those last points.

For the common set, red can be a surprising source of value if you are building a right-now deck, while uncommons add a little more staying power and some more efficient removal or utility.

### Green
Green in Exodus usually emphasizes big creatures, mana acceleration, or synergy with a handful of green removal spells. In a near complete set, green should present a comfortable mix of efficient early drops and some midrange starts, with uncommons offering bigger bodies or more impactful ramp and synergy options.

Drafting Exodus with a nearly complete commons/uncommons set is a different beast than chasing rare cards. The draft environment rewards players who can identify the minor edge cases and pivot quickly when a color is open or when the seat next to you is full of signals.

- When a color is under draft pressure, you will rely on uncommons with better efficiency to maintain options. The trick is to identify the most sticky pieces—creatures that trade well and remain on the board with minimal support.

## Collectability, value, and how to price a near-complete set
One of the challenges with any evergreen project is determining value. A near-complete commons/uncommons Exodus binder has different value drivers than a complete set of rares or mythics. The fundamental principle remains: commons often balance the draft environment in mono-color or two-color decks; uncommons provide the tiebreakers that can shape your deck's approach and your playgroup's opinion of the set.

- Condition matters: Since Exodus is a collector’s vintage, the card condition is crucial. If you are chasing near-complete status, you will want well-protected stock, sleeves, and a binder to preserve the color identity.
- Market demand: The current market sometimes overvalues the odd, hidden gem, commons or uncommons that enable a well-known combo or synergy. Be mindful of your spending and keep a list of the value pieces versus collector interest.
- Playability vs. collectability: For a near-complete common/uncommon set, arrange the collection by playability first and collectability second. The former becomes the binder’s core, the latter adds the lure for potential buyers at a later date.
