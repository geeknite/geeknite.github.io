## Overview

## How to Play
### Setup
Deal out a fixed number of cards to each player, place the remainder in a draw pile, and decide the target score. The person who last saw a rainbow is the first player, naturally, because who else would volunteer for the chaos?

### Turn structure
On your turn you draw a card, then discard one, and if you time it right you can lay down a run or a set. The rainbow color system means you might be chasing a red 7, a blue 9, or a green trio of Minnie icons. The rules keep you moving, and occasionally they throw a curveball that makes you question all your life choices.

### Special cards
There are a handful of special cards that flip the game on its head. Maybe a wild card that can substitute for any color, or a swap hand card that forces another player to exchange cards with you. The moment you realize you used the swap hand card on your best friend and then immediately regret it is the moment you know you are alive.

## Strategy and Variants
### Core strategies
The rainbow color system favors flexible planning over rigid color chasing. You learn to balance short term gains against potential long term setups. A good player will watch opponents and notice when someone is collecting a particular color and then pivot to a different target, possibly even using a wild card to block a run they suspect someone else is building.

### House rules and expansions
Like many family games, you can tweak the difficulty by shortening or lengthening the score target, or by adding a cap on the number of times a guarded wild card can be used. An optional variant is to allow players to draw two cards on their turn for a longer, more dramatic arc to the finale. If you want to go full chaos mode, add a timer and pretend you are a game show host with a microphone that amplifies your exasperation.

## Practical tips for playing with different groups
- For families with younger players, set a lower target score and add a simple rule such as you must lay down at least one set before a run. This gives kids the chance to win without long drawn out finales.
- For couples game night, introduce a small wager like the winner picks the next snack flavor. The stakes add humor without ruining friendships.
- For larger groups, split into two teams and use the swap hand card to steal turns between teammates. This can either ignite alliance shenanigans or general chaos, depending on the mood.
