H2: Painting and assembly tips (pro-level or novice-friendly)

Whether you’re a veteran painter or someone who still uses primer as a decorative varnish, Orc Zombie 4 has something to offer. Here are tips to help you maximize your minis gaming time without turning your hobby into a full-time job.

H3: Primer and base coat strategy

- Begin with a neutral gray primer to give the greens and dark browns something to cling to. A mid-gray works particularly well because it makes the green tones pop without making the skin look radioactive.
- For the skin, pick a base that’s greenish-gray rather than pure green. Think olive, sage, or a muted forest green, depending on how rotten you want your undead orc to appear.
- Armor and weapon bits should be treated with a slate or charcoal base to give them a battered metallic feel. If there are studs or rivets, a lighter metallic shade helps them catch the light in a dim dungeon.

H3: Layering and shading quick-guide

- Apply a mid-tone green (or your chosen primary color) and let it settle into the recesses. This creates a natural contrast between raised areas and crevices.
- Use a wash: a dark brown or black wash to tint the crevices. The idea is to bring depth to the skin and armor without turning the model into a muddy blotch.
- Dry brush with a lighter green or bone color to highlight edges. Do not overdo this; one light pass is often enough to create scale-like texture without looking chalky.

H3: Details, eyes, and menace

- The eyes are a tiny but important detail. A small dot of bright red or yellow can lend a terrifying glint. If you’re feeling generous, you can paint a little sclera white with a dark pupil to give the zombie an unsettling stare.
- Teeth can be highlighted with bone-white, then touched with a touch of green wash to blend with the rotted look.
- Blood spatter or gore effects, if you enjoy the dramatic, can be added with a deep red dabbed sparingly on edges or on the armor plates as if the zombie dragged itself through a fight.
