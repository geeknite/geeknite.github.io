## What is Exploding Kittens, and why should you care?

Exploding Kittens is a fast-paced, chaotic card game designed by Elan Lee, Matthew Inman (yes, the internet’s favorite feline cartoonist), and Shane Small. It shot from a quirky Kickstarter to a mainstream pop-culture moment, delivering a compact, face-smackingly funny card game that can be learned in minutes and played in under an hour. The core premise is simple: you don’t want to draw the Exploding Kitten card, because that means you’re out—unless you can Defuse it with a Defuse card. The rest of the deck is a carnival of action cards that help you dodge explosions, steal turns, force your opponent to draw, or just mess with the gameplay in delightfully petty ways.

This “Original Game with Bonus Cards” edition is typically the one people cite when they say, “I want the cardboard box with extra surprises.” The bonus cards, which arrive either as a special insert or an add-on pack, are designed to spice up the baseline experience by adding more tactical choices and more WTF moments. Whether the bonus cards land as a decent upgrade or a gimmick depends on your group’s appetite for risk, sass, and mid-game chaos. We’ll unpack that in a bit.

## The box and the contents: what you actually get

In the standard Exploding Kittens box, you’ll find a dozen or so different kinds of cards. The core pillars are:

- Exploding Kitten cards (the big nope button for your luck and nerves)
- Defuse cards (your one-time get-out-of-trouble card)
- Various action cards (Shuffle, See the Future, Attack, Favor, Skip, etc.)
- A few thematic jokey cards to keep the mood buoyant

The “Bonus Cards” add-on (whether it comes packed with the Original Game or offered as a separate expansion) introduces extra action cards, additional twists, and sometimes alternate win conditions. Some bundles include a mini-rulebook update to reflect how the bonuses change the flow of turns. The practical upshot is simple: more variety, faster decision points, and a slightly higher ceiling for players who want to optimize their cheating-with-hriendliness (we’ll come back to the ethics of “cheating” in a moment).

If you’re a visual person, here’s a quick cardboard to meme ratio: you’ll still feel the same slapstick groove, but the extra cards give you more chances to pull off the “I don’t even know what I’m doing” play that somehow works. You’ll also appreciate the quality of the card stock and the vibrant, meme-friendly illustrations that, frankly, look ready to be printed on a t-shirt you’ll regret wearing to a formal dinner.

## How to play in under 10 minutes (and why that matters)

Exploding Kittens thrives on its quick setup and even quicker learning curve. Here’s the gist, with a Geeknite twist:

- Setup: Remove all Exploding Kitten cards except the number equal to players minus one. Shuffle the rest of the deck and deal 7 cards to each player. The remaining deck sits facedown in the center, like a silent threat. Everyone gets a Defuse card (if you’re lucky) as part of your starting hand. The vibe is low-stakes chaos—until someone draws the fated bomb.
- Turn structure: On your turn, you can play one or more action cards from your hand, go through the deck by drawing a card, and hope you didn’t humbly misread your luck. Action cards range from “See the Future” (peek at the top three cards) to “Shuffle” (shuffle the deck to reset everyone’s expectations). The central mechanic is simple: you want to avoid drawing the Exploding Kitten, but sometimes you’ll be forced to take a turn you didn’t deserve. It’s a balance of risk management and petty mind games.
- Defusing the danger: If you draw an Exploding Kitten, you’re out unless you can play a Defuse card. Defuse cards can be saved for later or used immediately when the kitten hits your hand. They’ll often come at a premium, so you’ll want to keep a couple around for the “just in case we abandon empathy” moment near the endgame.
- Endgame momentum: The last player standing, after all others have exploded (figuratively, or maybe literally, depending on your table’s noise level), wins. It’s a quick victory, a loud celebration, and a reminder that the real enemy is your own pride as you watch someone blow up their last chance with a failed gamble.

In short: Exploding Kittens is a party game that rewards bluffing, timing, and watching your friends do something incredibly stupid in the most entertaining way possible. The bonus cards, if used judiciously, can tilt the balance toward a more strategic game rather than pure luck. If your group enjoys light strategy, social deception, and a shared love of ridiculous humor, you’ll feel right at home here.

### Turn-by-turn nuance and player interaction

The beauty of Exploding Kittens lies in how quickly you can tilt a table from friendly banter to competitive chaos. See the Future lets you peek, giving you an idea of what’s coming and letting you plan a clever bluff. Attack cards force the next player to take multiple turns, an excellent way to shift pressure onto someone who’s been playing the long-term game. But beware: if you overplay your hand, you’ll find yourself in a position where you’re on the receiving end of a mass draw, and suddenly your “I’m fine” face is a costume you cannot unmask.

There’s a legitimate tension here: you want to make other players draw when it benefits you, but you are one unlucky draw away from an indefinite hiatus from this specific game-night vibe. The bonus cards can increase these moments of high-stakes risk by injecting new mechanics—commonly new risk-reward decisions and additional ways to dodge or trigger explosions. Whether that’s a blessing or a curse depends on how much you enjoy the unpredictability of a well-timed card that says, “Yep, you’re about to regret your life choices.”

## Bonus Cards: value beyond the novelty

So, do the bonus cards actually add value? Short answer: yes, but with caveats. Here’s a breakdown.

- Variety and pace: The bonus cards give you additional decision points per round. This can speed up or slow down the game, depending on how aggressive your group is. If you like fast rounds, you’ll appreciate the faster tempo; if you prefer a longer, more thoughtful session, you’ll enjoy the extra layers of choice.
- Strategic depth: The extra cards invite micro-strategies. Although Exploding Kittens remains heavily luck-driven, the bonuses let you stack your odds a little more, or at least disrupt your opponent’s plan in a satisfying way. If you enjoy “planning your next two moves while pretending not to be planning,” you’ll probably love the bonuses.
- The social factor: Bonus cards often bring a few new memes into circulation. If your table lives for inside jokes and pop-culture references, you’ll get more mileage out of the humor. If you’re there for a strictly “game as competition” vibe, you might feel that some bonus cards skew the balance toward comedy rather than pure skill.
- Rule clarity: The downside is potential confusion. New cards require new interpretations and sometimes new house rules. If you’re playing with people who memorize every rule nuance or who are too eager to bend the rules, you’ll want a quick pre-game briefing to avoid the “but the card says” debates that derail the vibe.

Bottom line: Bonus cards are a nice upgrade for players who want more variety and more opportunities to force chaos on their friends. For casual players who just want a quick laugh, the base set often suffices. If you’re buying the edition labeled as “Original Game with Bonus Cards,” expect a humidity of jokes, a few clever new mechanics, and a slightly longer playtime—because more cards means more moments that will cause you to question your life choices as a gamer and as a human being.

## Variants and house rules: making it fit your crew

If you want to tailor Exploding Kittens to a particular night, you can adopt a few light house rules:

- Quick-play variant: Remove two to four Exploding Kitten cards from the deck to shorten round times. This is great for shorter sessions or a wide audience with varying attention spans.
- End-game turbo: Play the game to three eliminations instead of last-man-standing for a more cooperative tension that still ends in a dramatic last round.
- Bonus shuffle: Allow players to swap a bonus card with one from the deck once per round, introducing more strategic risk and reward.
- The meme veto: If a player instantly jokes about the art that lands a hit with the entire table, they get a “Save Card” discount—this is less about mechanics and more about social flavor.

House rules like these keep Exploding Kittens fresh and accessible for your group, but they also run the risk of diluting the core tension if overused. The best approach is to pilot a simple variant on one night, see how it lands, and then adjust accordingly for future sessions.

## In-game tips and recommendations

- Keep Defuse cards handy. They feel like insurance—precious and easy to underestimate until you need one most.
- Don’t be afraid to bluff. You don’t need to reveal intent; you can use a well-timed action card to nudge an opponent into a risky draw while you save your major move for a defensive position.
- Read the room. If your table is the kind that erupts in laughter over a bad pun, lean into it with the bonus cards. If everyone’s in “serious business mode,” keep a few quick play rules handy to avoid derailment.
- For families and mixed-age groups, consider a gentler variant. Reduce the number of Exploding Kitten cards or start with a couple of extra Defuse cards. The key is inclusivity: you want everyone to participate, not just the kids who memorize every rule and the adults who want to win at all costs.
