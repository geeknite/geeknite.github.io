## What is Phase 10 and why should you care?

Phase 10 is a card game about progression, not necessarily speed. The objective is to complete ten specific phases in order, moving from the simple to the elaborate as the deck wears you down with cunningly placed skip cards and wilds that feel like a magician’s last-minute reveal. The game supports a surprising range of player counts, typically from 2 to 6 players, and it scales in a way that makes sense without needing a PhD in mathematics to determine who’s winning. It’s the sort of game that can be both a friendly family activity and a high-stakes after-dinner face-off depending on the mood and the number of caffeinated beverages involved.

You’ll hear phrases like phase one: two sets of three, phase two: a set of three plus a run of four, and so on, with each successive phase requiring more planning and a little more risk. The set-and-run combo design means you’ll often be prioritizing which cards to lay down now and which to hold for a later phase. It’s a mix of light puzzle-solving and push-your-luck experimentation that keeps your brain warm and your competitive spirit led astray in equal measure.

## How Phase 10 is played: rules at a glance

Phase 10 can be summarized with a few core ideas:

- The game is played in rounds. Each round corresponds to a phase. You can advance to the next phase only after you’ve completed the current one.
- You’re trying to lay down your own phase on the table (so everyone can see your plan). Once you’ve laid down the phase, you can add cards to your own phase or to others’ phases on the table as long as you’re legal.
- The goal of each turn is to draw, assemble your hand to meet your current phase, optionally extend your phase with new cards, and discard one card to end your turn.
- A wild card can substitute for any card in a phase, while a skip card can force a neighbor to miss a turn, giving you a little breathing room or a chance to pull ahead.
- If you can’t or don’t want to complete your phase on your turn, you draw and pass. The round continues until someone has discarded all their cards after meeting their current phase.

If you’re a seasoned gamer, you’ll recognize the bones of Phase 10: the game rewards you for planning ahead, but not so much that you can’t salvage a round when luck tilts the deck in an unexpected direction. If you’re new to the game, you’ll discover that the learning curve is pleasantly shallow at first and then slides into a mild, engaging challenge as you begin to juggle phase requirements and card availability.

The point is that the phases progressively demand more coordination and longer card sequences, which can be a relief for players who live for pattern recognition and a mild sense of triumph when a difficult run finally lands on the table.

### Strategy notes for players who want to level up
- Prioritize the early phases that require small sets or short runs, but be mindful of what cards you’re discarding; a card you keep could enable a rival’s run.
- Use wilds to salvage a tricky phase or to complete a longer run, but don’t waste them on a phase that’s already close to completion by someone else.
- Pay attention to the discard pile. If a particular color or rank is drying up, consider adjusting your approach to less common cards that might still complete your phase.
- When playing defensively, consider bringing your own phase to fruition early in a round and then using your turn to disrupt others with skips or by throwing off their runs with strategic discards.
