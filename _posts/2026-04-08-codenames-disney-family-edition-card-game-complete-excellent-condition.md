## What this edition is all about

Codenames Disney Family Edition takes the classic word guessing premise and gives it a Disney twist. Think of two teams, each with a spymaster who has a grid of universal word grid tokens on a board. The goal remains the same: give clues that connect as many of your agents as possible without tipping off the opposing team or hitting the assassin. The Disney spin adds familiar character names and items to the word cards so that players can lean into the theme without having to pretend they are reading ancient runes to decipher a clue.

This edition is designed for lighter, family friendly play. It aims to be accessible to younger players while still giving adults something to giggle about. The cards include a mix of characters, places, objects, and iconic phrases all intertwined with the Disney universe. If you have a kid who loves princesses, superheroes, or pirates, this box will feel like a treasure chest rather than a learning curve. If you are a grown up who has a soft spot for Disney trivia, this game will spark those delightful memories while you try to be sneaky with your clues.

## How to play this Disney flavored edition

### Setup and overview

Set up is straightforward. Lay out the 25 word cards in a 5 by 5 grid. Each team is assigned a color, and a spymaster sits with a set of clue cards ready to issue hints. The twist here is that your clues should help your team guess as many of your color cards as possible while avoiding the other team cards and the assassin. The assassin is a built in hazard that ends the game if you stumble into it, so the tension remains high even when the theme keeps things wholesome.

### Basic flow of a turn

On your turn you give a clue that consists of a single word and a number. The number indicates how many cards on the board relate to that clue. For example, a spymaster might say a clue like magic 3 if there are three of their color cards that are connected to magic. The other players then discuss, interpret the clue, and guess which cards on the grid match the clue. The aim is to reveal your color cards while avoiding the other team cards and the assassin. If you click a card that belongs to your opponent, their turn begins; if you click an assassin card, the game ends immediately and the opposite team wins. It is a tense dance of deduction and risk assessment that works beautifully when players are engaged and not distracted by snacks.

### Clues and risk management

The art of clue giving is the heart of Codenames. You want to supply enough information to guide your team toward several cards without saying the exact cards themselves. Disney themed cards give you a wealth of potential connections to draw from. You might use a clue like hero 2 to nudge your team toward a couple of hero related words such as hero and princess, or you might lean into a composite idea like magic 3 to link magic wand, fairy, and spell in one neat mental bundle. The best clues feel obvious in retrospect but require a moment of creative leap when you first hear them. The challenge is not only in finding the right link but in weighing the risk of hitting a card that belongs to the other team or the assassin.

### Endgame and pacing

As with all Codenames, momentum is key. Disney flavor cards help keep the energy high; the pacing tends to be brisk in a family setting, but it does require some patience as younger players formulate their guesses and sometimes misfire with hilariously wrong interpretations. The deadlock that occurs when the grid becomes a sea of potential clues can feel frustrating, but the humor in watching someone guess a princess card and a pirate card in the same turn tends to lighten any friction quickly.
