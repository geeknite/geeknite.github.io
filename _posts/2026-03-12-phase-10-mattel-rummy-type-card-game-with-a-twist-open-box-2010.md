## How to Play: A Quick Refresher
If you have never played Phase 10 before, fear not. The rules are not a riddle wrapped in an enigma; they are a relatively friendly set of constraints that become second nature once you see a couple of rounds happen in front of you. The basic flow is simple:

- Each player starts with a hand of ten cards. Your goal is to complete the current phase by collecting certain combinations of cards. A phase might require a set of four of a kind, then a run of six, and so on. You can always use the cards in your hand to satisfy the current phase while you try to block others from finishing theirs.
- On your turn, you draw two cards or one card if your table rules permit. Then you may discard one card to end your turn. The discard pile is where your opponents may glean your strategy, which is a reminder that in Phase 10, information is power and sometimes power is more valuable than a perfect hand.
- When you meet the requirements of the phase you are on, you can lay down your hand to indicate that you have completed the phase. After doing so, you move on to the next phase in the sequence. The catch is that you must start filling the next phase with new combinations, and you will usually be forced to rearrange your already-assembled cards to fit the new objective.
- If you can complete all your phases, you win. If you find yourself stuck in a cycle of near-misses, you can also try a house rule to extend the race, but in the spirit of the open box edition we recommend first learning the official route before you conjure up your own twists.

The twist rule can modify the moment you declare a completed phase, offering a small window of influence that can swing the table. It is not a mind-bending alteration, but it is enough to make players rethink their approach to each hand. It also creates moments where the same set of cards can produce two very different outcomes depending on the order of play and the decisions you make under pressure.
