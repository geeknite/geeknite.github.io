## Painting and Assembly Tips
### Getting started with priming and color choices
If you’re painting these, start with a good primer to pull out the texture on each model. Black primer works nicely if you’re going for a grim, shadowy look; white primer offers more brightness for gore and exposed musculature. A common technique is layering with desaturated greens and browns, then adding a splash of sickly yellow or purplish tones in the folds to simulate decay. The goal is to achieve a believable undead hue without turning every figure into a lime-green poster child for amateur painters.

### Speed painting vs. detailed work
If you’re pressed for time, you can use a wash over the base colors to bring out depth quickly, followed by a dry brush for highlights on raised areas. If you’re more patient, you can build up glazing layers that add realism without heavy brushwork. The key is to keep the color transitions clean enough to stay legible in game terms while still delivering the “ick factor” on your tabletop.

### Assembly tips
Glue can be your friend here; use a tiny amount just to secure joints. If a limb sits awkwardly, a quick pinning with a hobby pin can help alignment. If you’re new to assembly, don’t worry—the poses are designed for readability at standard tabletop distances, so you don’t have to chase perfect symmetry to have them look great on the board.

For painters who want a quick-start guide, here’s a common workflow: assemble, base coat, apply a mid-tone, wash, highlight, and then a final edge highlight if you’re feeling dramatic. You’ll be surprised how a single box can upgrade the “feel” of your zombie table without requiring you to reinvent your entire painting process.
