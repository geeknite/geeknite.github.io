## Sorting strategy: how to approach a 3K-card behemoth
Sorting is where a lot of the magic (and the headaches) happens. Here’s a practical, no-nonsense approach you can implement in a weekend, or at least while the first season of a new anime drops.

- Create three piles: Keep/NM for playable cards with clean edges, Trade/Sell for duplicates or promos with decent condition, and Inspect/Decide for borderline cards that need closer look.
- Separate by rarity and set: Create labeled binders or boxes for C, UC, L, EX, PR, and then sub-sort within those by set TB01–BT16. This helps you spot gaps in your collection and identify the best targets for trades.
- Foil and holo management: Foils look terrific but are more prone to surface wear. Separate foils early, so you don’t accidentally sleeve a slightly damaged card into a prized slot.
- Condition notes: Keep a simple inventory with a rough condition rating for each batch. It’ll save you time later when you price or trade; buyers love transparency, and you’ll love fewer headaches.
- Counting accuracy: It’s easy to miscount 3,000 cards, especially when there are duplicates. Use a reliable counting method—sum by batch, not by memory—and cross-check with a quick sample (e.g., count a small, repeatable subset to verify the density). You’ll thank yourself later when you don’t misrepresent the lot.

If you want to go pro fast, pair this with a small binder system (notebooks, labels, and sleeves) and a basic price guide that you put on your wall. The psychology of seeing a plan visually helps you keep your sanity as the stack grows taller than your understanding of Goku’s punch escalation.

## Practical storage and handling tips
Storage quality matters when you’re dealing with 3,000+ cards. It isn’t just about fan-out; it’s about preserving the cards so you can actually enjoy the tall stack years from now.

- Sleeves, then top-loaders: Use standard card sleeves for playability, and consider top-loaders for premium or rare cards if you’re stacking them for an exhibit or display. The extra protection is cheap insurance for your future self.
- Binders with marked dividers: Color-coded dividers by rarity and set help you quickly locate cards during practice sessions or when negotiating trades.
- Climate control: Avoid hot attic spaces or extreme humidity. DBS cards hate heat; they pretend to be extremely dramatic about temperature while quietly warping edges if the environment is off.
- Desk-friendly workflow: A stable workspace with a roll-out mat makes counting and sorting tolerable. If you’re forced to sort on the couch or in bed, you’ll eventually get that feeling of “this is a good idea” that ends with your knee explaining the meaning of regret.
- Digital backup: If you’re so inclined, you can keep a rough paper inventory and complement it with a simple spreadsheet. It sounds nerdy but it saves you time and money when you’re negotiating trades or setting up a tournament roster.

The practical magic of storage is that it converts chaos into opportunity. With a well-organized system, you can turn a stack that once felt infinite into a series of actionable moves—deck-building wins, and a few valuable trades you’ll feel good about.
