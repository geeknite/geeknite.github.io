Fortune Row adds a glittering twist to that mix by turning tokens into a design playground for players and a nimbly lethal tool for GMs. If you like a noir city where every choice has a consequence and every consequence can be reinterpreted as a clever mythic moment, Fortune Row is the kind of supplement that makes your table lean in with interest and a sly grin.

Fortune Row is not a rival faction guide, a combat pack, or a strict mechanical overhaul. Tokens operate as narrative levers — you claim one, and suddenly a scene tilts in a new direction. They can become tools for dramatic reveals, minor bonuses, or plot accelerants. The balance comes from codifying a few clear triggers and limits so tokens enhance play without turning sessions into a power fantasy parade.

If you’re new to City of Mist, Fortune Row is a friendly entry point that doesn’t demand memory of every rule from the core book. If you’re a veteran storyteller, think of Fortune Row as a way to pepper sessions with bespoke prompts that feel both thematic and mechanical rather than simply decorative.

## The Custom Token system explained

The heart of Fortune Row is the Custom Token system. Tokens have a simple anatomy and a few rules that keep play balanced.

### Token anatomy

- Name: A short evocative label that hints at the mythic or noir flavor.
- Theme: The vibe the token aligns with (Fate, Betrayal, Fortune, Reckoning, etc.).
- Effect: The primary mechanical or narrative advantage the token provides.
- Trigger: The situations in which the token can be used or activated.
- Consequence/Backlash: What happens if the token is used too recklessly, or if it fails to deliver as promised.
- Tie to the character: How the token reflects or enhances the character’s Masks, backstory, and personal arc.

Tokens are not generic +1s. They are story prompts that map onto the city’s myths and onto a character’s persona. A token might be a symbol from a deceased parent that suddenly appears in a scene to guide a decision, or a cursed coin that changes the odds of a moment to reveal a hidden truth.

### Creating tokens (a quick-start checklist)

1. Start with the Mask and the Case: Pick a core conflict or weakness in the character’s Mask or a recent case they’ve chased.
2. Name and Theme: Craft a short, punchy name and a theme that aligns with Fortune Row motifs (luck, consequence, deception, miracle, fate, etc.).
3. Define the core effect: Decide what the token does in a scene — a boon, a twist, or a clue. Keep it specific and situational so it feels earned.
4. Set the trigger: When can a player or GM invoke the token? Is it a once-per-session thing, or does it require a narrative cue? 
5. Determine the consequences: What is the price of using the token? Is there a risk, a complication, or a moral cost?
6. Balance with scope: A token should be powerful enough to feel meaningful, but not so broad that it unbalances scenes. If a token can solve a problem outright, pair it with a constraint or a cost in its Trigger or Consequence.
7. Tie the token to Fortune Row: Every token should echo the Fortune Row atmosphere — a mix of danger, glamour, and folklore. If it doesn’t feel like Fortune Row, tweak the theme until it does.

### In-play use: how these tokens actually change the flow

Fortune Row tokens shine when used to pivot a scene toward a mythic beat without derailing the momentum. They are designed to be called on by players or the GM at pivotal moments, thus creating narrative payoffs and, crucially, consequences that ripple forward. A token should feel like a small but meaningful investment: you spend a moment chasing a lead, you gain a flash of truth, or you slip into a different perspective for a beat. The real trick is to keep tokens flexible enough to handle both investigative and action-oriented scenes.

The GM’s toolkit in Fortune Row emphasizes three practices:

- Prompting and responding: Tokens should invite replies from multiple players, not just the token bearer. That keeps everyone engaged and makes room for group storytelling.
- Weaving with the Masks: A token should interact with a character’s Mask, not fight it. If a token undercuts a Mask in a way that harms character agency, you’ve likely misaligned it.
- Containing the scope: If a token’s effect tilts an arc too hard in a single moment, constrain it with the Trigger or Consequence so it remains a spark rather than a blaze.

## Running Fortune Row: GM tips and tricks

### Scene design and token pacing

- Use tokens to mark a turning point in a session. Drop a token early to set a mood and then reveal how it reshapes the stakes later.
- Pair tokens with locations on Fortune Row. For example, a token tied to the Fortune Row Market can affect vendors, factions, or the distribution of goods and information.
- Mint a few universal tokens that any character can claim, and a handful that are tied to specific Masks or backstories. The mix fosters both shared and personal stories.

### Balancing and player agency

- If a token becomes a must-take option, introduce a cost elsewhere in the session so players still feel challenged to choose between power and narrative risk.
- Encourage players to negotiate token usage as part of the scene. The fun in City of Mist often comes from who can craft a better narrative bargain, not who can roll the best die.
- Remember that tokens are not just mechanical bonuses; they are catalysts for character evolution. Use them to reveal a truth about a Mask, a hidden allegiance, or a past mistake that matters to the current plot.

### Integration with existing City of Mist rules

Fortune Row sits nicely atop the core rules and does not require a full rework of your playstyle. If your table has a favorite errant mechanic from the core book, ask how tokens could extend it. Perhaps a token grants a temporary aspect of Mist to the scene, or perhaps it creates a narrative truth that the GM must respect in later scenes. The key is to treat tokens as a complement, not a replacement for the established structure.

### Session flow example

1) The detective checks Fortune Row for a forgotten memory clue. A token activation reveals a hidden memory linked to an NPC.
2) The GM responds with a twist: the memory points to a different suspect or ally, shifting the investigation angle.
3) The player uses a second token to test the consequences of sharing this memory with the team, balancing risk with potential advancement.
4) The scene closes with a new lead and a moral question that will echo into future sessions.

## Accessibility, inclusivity, and playpace considerations

Fortune Row is designed to be accessible to new players while offering layers of complexity for veterans. The token system is presented with clear guidelines to prevent token hoarding or over-complication. If you’re running a group with mixed experience levels, start with three to five tokens in play and gradually introduce more as players get comfortable. Keep tokens thematically anchored to Fortune Row, so new players can recognize the tone and participate without feeling overwhelmed by a flood of mechanics.

In terms of inclusivity, the content remains mindful of sensitive topics and prioritizes consent in scene framing. The GM’s guide emphasizes clear communication about content boundaries and the capacity for players to opt into scenes that involve tricky moral questions and risky decisions. The approach here is to invite discussion rather than pressure players into uncomfortable territory.

## Practical next steps

- Pick three tokens to begin a Fortune Row arc and weave them into the first session two or three acts in. This gives your players a taste of how tokens influence both personal arcs and the city-wide mystery.
