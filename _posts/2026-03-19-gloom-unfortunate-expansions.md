Gloom: Unfortunate Expansions is the kind of product that makes you want to narrate a tiny eulogy for your perfectly happy weekend plans. It is the sort of add-on that arrives with the solemnity of a funeral coach and the punchline timing of a sarcastic mermaid — all wrapped up in a shiny package that promises to make your gloom even gloomer. If you thought the base game Gloom was delightfully macabre, then the Unfortunate Expansions is the bittersweet cherry on top of a very morbid cupcake. And yes, you should read that sentence aloud with a dramatic sigh, preferably while rolling dice that would make a dungeon master blush.

In this review, we dive into the heart of the expansion — what it adds, how it changes the vibe, whether it justifies the price tag, and if it can survive a brutal sunlight test without dissolving into tears at the edges of your table. Spoiler: it can, but only if you approach with the right balance of humor, skepticism, and a tiny bag of sour gummy worms to keep the mood buoyant.

Overview

Gloom is a game about making your family of eternally miserable souls as tragically unhappy as possible while trying not to cry yourself to sleep. The Unfortunate Expansions pack aims to deepen the gloom by sprinkling in new families, new artifacts, and new ways to twist the fates of the already doomed. The tonal target remains the same: everything should be humorous in a dark, theatrical way, like a goth novelist who discovered slapstick by accident. If the base game is a candlelit vigil, this expansion lights a bonfire and then slaps you with a handful of confetti made from fortune-telling cards.

What’s in the box

The box feels like a treasure chest that belongs to a very dramatic pirate captain who collects cursed heirlooms. Inside, you’ll find:

- A set of new family cards with even more unfortunate surnames and even more dramatic backstories.
- A new fate deck that tilts probabilities toward catastrophe in ways both clever and cruel.
- Several artifact cards that can flip the table in spectacular ways, including one artifact that makes everyone sigh in unison and another that compels your own gloomy character to quote poetry about rain.
- A handful of new game boards for special scenarios that let you zoom from intimate domestic tragedy to full-blown existential farce in a single turn.
- A small booklet of optional rules that you can ignore for a year and then use as a bookmark while you pretend to read the rules again.

If you’re thinking, wow, that sounds like a lot of doom, you’re right — but doom with a punchline is still doom, and doom with a punchline is basically comedy in a cloaked cape.

As with all Gloom expansions, the real signature is how it reshapes interaction. The Unfortunate Expansions do not simply add more cards to the deck; they tilt the social dynamic of the table. Some cards encourage collusion, some foster petty rivalries, and others force you to reveal secrets about your own characters that you didn’t know you had — like a family weather report that nobody asked for.

Thematic tone and humor

Gloom has always succeeded when it treads the fine line between sympathy and schadenfreude. The Unfortunate Expansions lean into that line with a few extra steps. The new families come with destinies that feel delightfully petty and a little cruel — not cruel in a cruel way, but cruel in a way that makes you want to narrate the misfortune aloud to your cat. The humor lands best when you let it breathe: a quiet sigh, a pun that lands with the theatrical timing of a soap opera cliffhanger, and a small, almost wink-like reminder that you are all in on the joke together — a joke about the inevitability of life’s little tragedies.

What this expansion changes about the mechanics

The base Gloom is a card-drawing engine married to a countdown clock of doom. The Unfortunate Expansions add new layers without turning the system into a bureaucratic nightmare. Key mechanical shifts include:

- Additional family cards that increase the potential for overlap in gloom triggers. This can lead to more dramatic chain reactions where one character’s misfortune triggers another’s, which triggers a cascade of doom that looks suspiciously like a tabletop Rube Goldberg device for despair.
- New artifact cards that modify the effect of fate cards. Some artifacts help you engineer a doom sequence that feels like a haunting movie moment; others are mischievous, nudging players toward suboptimal choices that still somehow lead to hilarious outcomes.
- Expanded fate deck with cards that impose new timing constraints. You’ll occasionally have to rush a turn or spend extra actions just to keep the gags coherent. The pacing benefits from these twists but can be brutal if you are table-turn unlucky.
- New scenario boards that alter victory conditions or doom thresholds. These scenarios breathe fresh life into the game by providing fresh setups that create novelty without stripping away the core tonal aim.

A quick note on table dynamics

If your table has people who love to roast and improvise, the Unfortunate Expansions will be a gift. If you have players who prefer steady, predictable outcomes, they will claim you have cursed your dice to moodiness and demand a safety net. The reality is somewhere in between: the expansion adds hooks for storytelling and improvisation without forcing a new, heavy-handed strategy. It is possible to overdo this, leading to a situation where every turn becomes a dramatic monologue about your character’s sad life. The trick is to lean into it without letting the gloom swallow the entire game.

The element of docile doom

One of the better qualities of this expansion is its restraint. You do not need to run out and gather every piece in a single sitting; you can sprinkle in a couple of new families or artifacts per session and still retain the signature feel. That restraint matters. In a world where many expansions pounce with a 70-card expansion pack, Unfortunate Expansions invites you to dip your toes in and decide if you want a longer soak in the gloom or a short, sharp drizzle of despair.

Featuring artifacts and families

The new cycles of artifacts and families feel like a curated anthology of misfortune. Some artifacts are fun to discover, and some seem to exist to punish your own character for harboring too much self-pity. The best combination typically involves pairing a new family with a compatible artifact that can push, pull, and twist the doom plan you’ve been quietly plotting. If you enjoy table talk and a bit of roleplay, the artifacts become invitations to mini-narratives rather than mere game effects.

Play experience and pacing

The pacing with Unfortunate Expansions shifts depending on how many players you have and how comfortable your group is with dramatic tension. In a four-player setting, you can reach dramatic beats quickly, but you may also find yourselves pausing for long, theatrical silences while others frame their gloom in cinematic terms. In smaller groups, the narrative bones show more clearly, and you can chase smaller micro-arcs without losing track of the overarching doom. The addition of new scenarios helps keep things fresh, and the different doom thresholds mean you aren’t simply following one formula over and over again. Variety is the spice of gloom, and this expansion provides a minecart full of it.

Artwork and presentation

The art direction remains a high watermark for gloom — evocative, a little whimsical, and never overly macabre to the point of intimidation. The new families bring distinctive visual themes that complement the base game’s atmosphere while offering enough novelty to justify a revisit. Color palettes tend to be complementary rather than jarring, so the table feels cohesive rather than cluttered when all the new cards are out. If you enjoy the aesthetic of gloom, you will be satisfied by the tone and the little visual jokes tucked into the cards, which reward careful inspection and slow reading aloud.

Quality and components

The physical quality follows the standard Atlas Games practice: sturdy cards with a nice card stock, crisp artwork, and components that survive a few boisterous table flips. Don’t expect premium foils or fancy oversized cards; this is more about mood and storytelling than pure tactile luxury. But the cards are readable at a glance, and the new boards lay flat, which is critical for a game that relies on quick turn flips and dramatic reveals. If you like cool packaging, the Unfortunate Expansions pack won’t disappoint, though you may need to find a dedicated spot on your shelf for the extra gloom rather than tucking it between two other expansions.

Role of expansions in a gloom game night

Expansions for gloom tend to act as spice rather than main course. They are best used when you want a change of pace, a chance to tell new mini-stories, or when you want to introduce new table dynamics with relatively low risk. Unfortunate Expansions gives you a handful of reliable tools to refresh your sessions without forcing a heavy re-learning curve. They are not a complete reimagining; they are more like mood music that you can drop into the background while you continue to narrative-craft your way through a cascade of misfortune.

Table talk and storytelling

If your group loves improvisation, there are delightful opportunities in the expansion. Some card interactions prompt you to narrate, to improvise a few lines about a doomed character, or to stage a faux eulogy for your own doom-laden heritage. If you enjoy collaborative storytelling with a comedic slant, the Unfortunate Expansions provide a reliable toolkit for making every session feel unique. It is not mandatory to role-play every turn, but if you do, you’ll likely walk away with a richer memory of the gloom you crafted together.

Final recommendations

Final thoughts and a verdict

Gloom: Unfortunate Expansions hits the sweet spot of being a sensible, well-integrated addition to the base game. It respects the core mechanics, preserves the sense of theatrical doom, and offers enough new material to keep repeat plays fresh. It does not feel like a half-hearted cash grab or a gimmick; it feels like a well-done extension crafted by people who understand why gloom works on a table: it brings out storytelling, it invites playful cruelty, and it gives you a toolkit to narrate your character’s downfall with flair. The expansions shine brightest when used in moderation — a handful of new families here, a couple of artifacts there, and a scenario or two in a single session. When used judiciously, it becomes a memory-making engine that lets your group push the envelope of what it means to be delightfully miserable.

If your goal is to create a game night that you and your friends will still reference in jokes months later, this expansion is a sure-fire bet. It is a celebration of mood, humor, and the joy of collaboratively telling a story that ends with a bittersweet, cathartic sigh. You’ll hear the collective, well-timed heh as doom cards reveal themselves, a chorus of dramatic gasps at a particularly cruel artifact, and perhaps the odd toast to the melancholy — a ritual as old as tabletop gaming itself.

Conclusion

Gloom: Unfortunate Expansions is a thoughtful, well-executed addition to the gloom universe.
