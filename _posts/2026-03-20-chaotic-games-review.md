## Throw Throw Burrito: A Nerf-Napkin Rollercoaster in Card Form

### What it is and how it works

Throw Throw Burrito (from the Exploding Kittens universe) is a mixed-media party game that blends card matching with physical throwing of soft burritos. The core goal is straight-forward: collect matching burrito cards to score points, while dodging or retaliating against other players who fling foam burritos in your direction. The table becomes a three-ring circus of bright colors, soft projectiles, and shouted admonitions about where not to throw—aka, everywhere near grandma’s glass of lemonade.

Mechanically, the game leans on: 
- A deck of “throw” and “dodge” action cards that trigger immediate burrito-flinging chaos.
- A separate burrito card collection mechanic that rewards players for pairing burrito cards of the same color or type—think “memory meets arena dodgeball with a rubbery prop gunner’s belt.”
- A set of neutral relief cards that let you swap, steal, or simply late-join a chaotic moment when you least expect it.

Dice are conspicuously absent here; the randomizer is the action on the table and your willingness to jump into the foam-flinging fray. The theme is loud, the storylines are ridiculous, and the whole production is bright enough to double as a portable sunshade. It’s a group activity that makes you work for it—work your arms, your reflexes, and your sense of decorum.

### How it feels to play

If you’ve ever watched a dodgeball scene in a movie and thought, “That would be fun if it didn’t involve actual referees and injuries,” Throw Throw Burrito translates that whim into a game-night-friendly format. The burritos themselves are plush, squishy, and harmless to furniture (for the most part). You’ll slice through a few plastic-score counters, dodge a handful of burrito throws, and groan theatrically when you realize someone has matched your burrito card and you’re about to be burrito-prosecuted. The humor lands hard and fast, with goofy sound effects and the kind of chaos that makes you question your life choices—then immediately asks for a rematch.

Pros:
- Immediate engagement; game starts fast and never slows to a boring moment.
- Great for 4–6 players (though it scales up with bigger groups, more on that later).
- High replay value due to randomized throwing moments and card draws.
- Clear, goofy theme that new players instantly “get.”

Cons:
- Can feel chaotic or loud for a quiet-night crowd; not ideal for intimate, low-energy gatherings.
- The foam burritos can become slippery or misbehave if you’re not careful with the throw, which can lead to slips and snorts.
- Rule clarifications can be necessary; you’ll end up arguing about who actually “owns” a matched burrito pair after a dramatic flip.

### Strategy and tips

- Positioning matters: stand in a comfortable arc around the center, not directly in line with a burrito launcher. You want to be able to dodge with minimal risk of tripping over someone else’s beverage.
- Watch the card flow. If you notice a few “throw” cards coming up, brace yourself for imminent chaos. It’s almost always wise to hold a burrito-card pair until you can secure a clean hit or a clean steal.
- Communication helps. In a game like this, a quick “I’ve got the pair!” can save you from unwanted target practice.
- Don’t chase too hard. It’s easy to blow through your turns trying to complete a burrito pairing, but the game rewards timing. Sometimes you’re better off holding your cards and waiting for a more generous opportunity.

### The big picture

Throw Throw Burrito nails the “party game energy” requirement: it’s loud, silly, and deeply social. It’s not a game to play if you’re aiming for deep strategy or a long, quiet evening; it’s a high-tempo party activity that thrives on interaction, mischief, and quick-witted taunts. If you’re planning a birthday gathering, a gamer meetup with friends who aren’t die-hard strategists, or a family game night (with older kids who won’t tangle with the burritos), this is a top-tier pick.



## Dodgeball Card Game: The Speed-Run of Card Chaos

### What it is and how it works

Dodgeball Card Game is the fast-paced sibling to the more narrative-heavy dexterity games we’ve reviewed. It’s a card-slinging, quick-thinking title where you’re rarely safe. The goal is to outplay your opponents through a series of dodgeball-inspired challenges—think dodge, duck, dip, dive, and, yes, dodge—without sacrificing your dignity or your snacks. The deck contains action cards that turn the game into a flurry of near-misses, tactical discards, and last-second gambits that could swing the entire round.

The core loop is simple: draw a card, resolve its effect, and try to set up a dodge that ends in a favorable discard or attack on an opponent. You’ll learn to gauge risk on the fly, because every draw could flip your status from “calm strategist” to “human slip-n-slide.” The art and color palette lean into sports-parlor humor, making the game feel energetic even when you’re down to your last couple of cards.

### Game experience and vibes

Dodgeball Card Game is about pace. It rewards attentive players who can read the table and exploit the moment when someone is about to falter. The mechanics often create a cascade of near-misses and clever plays that feel less like “proper strategy” and more like a chaotic improv scene—except you’re the one trying to avoid getting hit by a score card. The learning curve is gentle; it’s easy to teach to new players, which makes it a great candidate for multi-generational game nights.

Pros:
- Lightning-fast rounds; you’ll be through many games in an evening.
- Great for players who enjoy interaction and quick counters.
- Simple rules with satisfying payoff moments—especially when you pull off a sneaky discard to trap an opponent.

Cons:
- The chaos can be a bit much for players who prefer a quieter game or a more methodical pace.
- Some players may feel overwhelmed by the number of action effects and “what does this do again?” calls; a quick recap helps.
- It’s easy to tilt if you’re not careful with turn order and timing; a few missed plays can snowball.

### Tips for success

- Learn the best moments to deploy your top action cards. You don’t want to waste a reversal on a weak moment; hold your big plays for when they truly swing the table in your favor.
- Keep track of who’s low on cards. In a dodge-centric game, the winning move often comes from forcing a weary opponent to draw one more card than they can handle.
- Position your attention on rhythm: if the group is in a lull, accelerate the tempo back up with a high-impact card so you don’t lose momentum.

### The big picture

IfThrow Throw Burrito is the carnival ride, Dodgeball Card Game is the straight-to-the-chase sprint. It fits well in a night where you want a dozen short, punchy rounds rather than a single long strategy session. It’s particularly suitable for folks who love quick thinking and playful deception. It’s less about building a long-term plan and more about outplaying your friends in the moment—think micro-dettors of dominance rather than grand strategy.



## Exploding Kittens: The Meme Dream That Isn’t Slowing Down

### What it is and how it works

Exploding Kittens is the archetype of modern meme-driven card games. It blends a silly art style with a surprisingly tactile risk-management loop. The deck features a mix of draw cards, action cards, and the titular Exploding Kitten cards. The catch: you don’t want to draw an Exploding Kitten, unless you’ve got a defuse card to keep yourself alive. The game plays out in rounds where players draw cards one by one, trying to dodge the dreaded kitten while using other cards to disrupt, misdirect, or shield themselves from catastrophe.

The humor is front-and-center here, and the design does a masterful job of turning tension into laughter. The tension isn’t mechanical only; it’s the comedic timing of players deciding when to push their luck and when to risk the chain of “Nope” and “Steal” cards that will land you in the hot seat.

### Gameplay notes and balance

Exploding Kittens shines in its chaos management. It’s accessible enough for casual players but with enough occasional complexity to keep seasoned gamers engaged. The defuse cards introduce a light strategy layer: do you save it for a late-game moment or play it early to deter others from pressuring you? There’s also a strong “misdirection” element, where you pass the buck and hope others shoot themselves in the foot with a bad draw.

Pros:
- Quick rounds with lots of laughter; low barrier to entry for new players.
- Balanced humor with surprising strategic depth when you start planning your defuses and hand-blocks.
- The art and theme age well, proving meme-based games can have staying power if well designed.

Cons:
- Repeated play can feel a little repetitive; you’ll want to mix in new titles after a few sessions.
- Some players may not love the grim tolerance for “risky” discard play; it can feel unforgiving if you’re unlucky with draws.
- The box feel and components are sturdy, but some pieces can go missing if you’re not neat about storing them (we’ve all had the corner-of-the-living-room nightmare with a loose card).

### Tips and tricks

- Save defuse cards for the late game, but don’t hoard them to the point you’re in danger the moment you reach for them. A calculated risk often pays off.
- Use your “Nope” cards judiciously. It’s not always the best moment to block a hit; sometimes a late-block is more valuable than an early one.
- Pay attention to color cues and card prompts. A careful memory of what has already been drawn helps you predict who might draw the next Exploding Kitten.

### The big picture

Exploding Kittens remains one of the most influential meme-driven party games in the modern era. It’s bright, silly, and highly approachable, with enough quirks to made experienced players chuckle while rookies feel like they’ve joined a particularly outrageous club. It’s a trophy title for the “we all deserve a ridiculous night” brigade and a reliable fallback when you want a quick, robust narrative to fuel laughter and a few dramatic survivals.


## Comparative snapshot: Which one for your next game night?

- Complexity: Exploding Kittens > Throw Throw Burrito > Dodgeball Card Game. All are accessible, but Exploding Kittens introduces more planning around defuses and timing.
- Player count: Throw Throw Burrito and Exploding Kittens scale nicely with 4–6 players; Dodgeball Card Game shines in larger groups and tight, fast rounds.
- Duration: All three are designed for short bursts, but Burrito can stretch into longer play sessions if you’re chasing a lot of matches.
- Humor: All three lean into humor, with Exploding Kittens delivering meme-driven jokes, Burrito offering physical slapstick, and Dodgeball Card Game serving fast, witty banter.
- Theme: Burrito is riotously physical, Dodgeball is kinetic and rapid-fire, Kittens is meme-laden and cheeky. Pick your vibe: physical ridiculousness, brainy sprint-craft, or meme-powered misdirection.

## Final verdict and recommendations

All three games deliver something special for the right crowd. If you crave physical comedy and aren’t shy about a little chaos, Throw Throw Burrito is the showstopper. It’s not just a game; it’s an experience—the sort of thing you’ll remember as “the night we hurled plush burritos at each other and laughed until we forgot to eat.” If you want a brisk, high-velocity card game that thrives on timing and social play, Dodgeball Card Game is your best bet. It keeps the adrenaline high and the group involved, all in a compact footprint. For a meme-forward, extremely approachable party staple with broad appeal and a lot of laughter, Exploding Kittens remains a cornerstone—easy to teach, fast to play, and always ready to co-host a funky energy surge.

If you’re choosing a single title to start your chaotic collection, pick Exploding Kittens. It’s the most flexible in terms of player count and reusability, and it helps new players feel included without a long ruleset. If you already own Kittens and want something with more physical interaction, add Throw Throw Burrito to your cart next. Finally, keep Dodgeball Card Game handy for evenings where you want a speedy torrent of plays, quick rounds, and a constant stream of “did you see that?” moments.
