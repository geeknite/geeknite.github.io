## How to Play in 2-5 Minutes (or 15, depending on your confidence)
Sushi Go! is famously accessible, and the Travel Edition leans into that accessibility without sacrificing the strategic flavor that makes it a playground for crunchy decision-making. Here’s the quick-and-dirty version, seasoned with travel-friendly flavor for our culture-loving geeks who adore culinary analogies as much as the game itself.

### Setup
- Shuffle the deck and deal a hand to each player.
- In a 2-player game, you’ll notice a slightly different dynamic that feels more like a sparring dojo for quick reads rather than a full banquet. In 3-5 players, rounds light up with more competition and more “I need that wasabi card” adrenaline.
- Each player glances at their hand and you’re ready to start drafting. The pace is brisk; there’s no time for overthinking unless you enjoy dramatic monologues about eel nigiri emotionally essential to your point harvest.

### The Draft (Pick and Pass)
- On your turn, pick one card from your hand and place the rest facedown in a discard pile (or, if you’re feeling mischievous, in a friend’s beverage—note: we do not endorse stealing drinks, but we do endorse stealing glances at your neighbor’s maki totals).
- After you pick, pass your remaining cards to the left (or right, depending on the round’s rotation). The drafting continues until all cards are claimed.
- The trick is balancing short-term gain with long-term synergy. Do you chase a single, juicy set (a sashimi trio) or spread bets across several small, safe points (rice, egg, and a couple of maki)? The Travel Edition’s compact footprint nudges players toward bolder, faster gambits.

### Scoring (The Important Bit)
- Each sushi type has its own scoring rule. Maki counts scale with how many you collected, nigiri grant flat points, and sashimi is the rare dessert that pays off big when you chase the three-card set.
- Bonus cards can swing the rounding scales. One card can turn a minor lead into a dramatic victory or doom your plan by a single point—the friend who wants their cucumber roll to be relevant will understand the drama.
- In travel mode, scoring stays straightforward. If you crave crisp math on a plane, you’ll appreciate the reduced mental heft and the moment when you realize you just outscored your seatmate with a napkin as a scorepad.

### End of Round and Reset
- After all cards are drafted, tally scores for that round and reset for the next. The Travel Edition often uses logbook-style sheets to minimize head counting—perfect for deciding which airport snack earns you bragging rights after your triumph.
- The game ends when a pre-determined point threshold is reached (or when the flight attendant announces that everyone must power down their devices and pretend to enjoy the in-flight meal). The winner is the one with the highest score after the final tally.

## Strategy: Pick, Pass, and Provoke Your Opponents
Let’s cut to the chase: what actually matters in a two-to-five-player environment where you want to win without overthinking and becoming that guy who mutters about efficient scoring heuristics while shoveling sushi into your mouth.

### Opening Gambits: Reading the Table
- In smaller counts (2-3), patterns form fast. The first rounds often privilege quick pursuit of a single objective (sashimi or a strong nigiri line). The risk is overcommitting early and becoming predictable, inviting counter-drafts that steal your thunder.
- In larger groups (4-5), diversify early. Spreading bets across several types creates a safety net because someone is always aiming for your points. The real skill is deciding when to pivot toward a single heavy hitter mid-game and risk tipping your plan into someone else’s hands.

### Mid-Game Shocks: When to Pivot
- The swing tends to come from a well-timed sashimi or a rare maki card. If you can accumulate three sashimi cards, you unlock a big chunk of points; blow that opportunity and others will chase, and you’ll watch your lead evaporate as your rivals mirror your play.
- Draft order becomes a chessboard. If you notice a neighbor hoarding greens and oranges (in sushi terms, not literal colors), you might jump to block a coveted card before they secure it for themselves.

### Endgame Frenzy: The Final Pivots
- The last rounds are where drama erupts. Endgame is often about maximizing a secondary objective and blocking others from their best-laid plans. A timely two-card discard can swing the final tally, especially in travel mode where the score sheet is crisp and easy to reference.
- Don’t neglect defense. If you’re ahead by a slim margin, a well-timed blocking draft can protect your advantage. The beauty of travel edition is you can execute this with a cheeky grin and a spare napkin as your scorepad.

### Card-Level Decisions: What to Chase and What to Pass
- Maki: In doubt, draft a few maki cards. Endgame rewards scale with maki control, but be mindful of your rivals catching up. If everyone wants maki, you may end up with a bigger chunk of points than expected—or points that get shared in a way that doesn’t favor you.
- Nigiri: The reliable baseline. They’re straightforward and less chaotic than chasing multi-card combos. If your hand is heavy with nigiri and a few high-risk, high-reward cards, consider whether you can stabilize your lead or risk a small buffer for a big swing later.
- Sashimi: High-risk, high-reward. Sashimi can be game-breaking when you land the trio, but chasing it too early can distract you from building a robust, versatile portfolio. The Travel Edition makes sashimi approachable and less punitive when you’re fighting to keep your other lines alive.
- Dessert: Desserts are rare but can deliver a hefty payoff. In travel, you might not rely on desserts every game, but they can become the hero when you need that extra push to crest the finish line.
