## How to Play in 2-5 Minutes (or 15, depending on your confidence)
Sushi Go! is famously accessible, and the Travel Edition leans into that accessibility without sacrificing the strategic flavor that makes it a playground for crunchy decision-making. Here’s the quick-and-dirty version, with a touch of flavor for our culture-loving geeks who love the culinary analogies almost as much as the game itself.

### Setup
- Shuffle the deck and deal a hand to each player. In a 2-player game, you’ll notice you get a slightly different dynamic that makes the table feel more like a sparring dojo for making quick reads rather than a full banquet. In 3-5 players, you’ll find the rounds light up with more competition and more “I need that wasabi card” adrenaline.
- Each player takes a look at their cards, and you’re ready to start drafting. The pace is brisk; there’s no time for overthinking, unless you’re one of those players who enjoys a dramatic monologue about why the eel nigiri is emotionally essential to your harvest of points.

### The Draft (Pick and Pass)
- On your turn, pick one card from your hand and place the rest facedown in a discard pile (or in your friend’s beverage, if they’re into that sort of thing; we do not endorse stealing drinks, but we do endorse stealing glances at your neighbor’s maki totals).
- After you pick, pass your remaining cards to the left (or right, depending on the round’s rotation). You’ll continue this until all cards are claimed.
- The trick is to balance short-term gain with long-term synergy. Do you chase a single, juicy set (like a full sashimi trio) or spread your bets across multiple small but safe points (rice, egg, and a couple of maki)? The Travel Edition’s smaller footprint naturally encourages bolder, faster gambits—your opponent’s eyes say, I’m here to duel, not to ponder life’s meaning.

### Scoring (The Important Bit)
- Each type of sushi has its own scoring rule. Maki cards give you points depending on how many you collected, nigiri cards grant flat points, and sashimi is the rare dessert that demands you collect three for a hefty payoff.
- There are also “bonus” cards that can swing the rounding scales. The beauty here is how one card can turn a minor lead into a dramatic victory, or doom your aspirations by a single point—the friend who wants his cucumber roll to be relevant will understand.
- In travel mode, scoring remains straightforward. If you dream of keeping your math crisp on a plane, you’ll appreciate the reduced mental heft and the satisfaction of watching someone calculate three-digit sushi combos on a napkin.

### End of Round and Reset
- After all cards are drafted, tally scores for that round and reset for the next. In the Travel Edition you’ll often have logbook-style sheets to minimize head counting—perfect for those moments when you’re trying to decide which airport snack you want to reward yourself with after you win.
- The game ends when a pre-determined point threshold is reached (or when the flight attendant announces that everyone must power down their devices and pretend to enjoy the in-flight meal). The winner is the one with the highest score after the final tally.

## Strategy: Pick, Pass, and Provoke Your Opponents
Let’s talk about what actually matters in a two-to-five-player environment where you’re trying to win without overthinking and becoming that guy who quietly mutters about “efficient scoring heuristics” while shoveling sushi into your mouth.

### Opening Gambits: Reading the Table
- In smaller player counts (two to three), you’ll notice patterns form faster. The first couple of rounds may privilege a quick, blunt pursuit of a single objective (often a sashimi or nigiri line). The trick is not to overcommit early; if you chase a single goal too hard, you’ll become predictable, and your opponents will bully your plans with a cunning counter-draft move.
- In larger groups (four to five), you’ll need to diversify your approach. Spreading your bets across several sushi types creates a safety net, because someone is always aiming at your points. The real skill is deciding when to pivot toward a single heavy hitter mid-game and risk giving your rivals the chance to mirror your plan.

### Mid-Game Shocks: When to Pivot
- The mid-game swing often comes from a well-timed sashimi or a rare maki card. If you can accumulate three sashimi cards, you’ve unlocked a big chunk of points; if you blow that opportunity, your lead can erode quickly as others snap up similar combos.
- The pick order can become a chessboard. If you see a neighbor hoarding a few greens and oranges (in the sushi universe, not literal colors), you might jump on a rival block by pulling a card they really want before they do the same to you.

### Endgame Frenzy: The Final Pivots
- The last couple of rounds are where the drama happens. Your endgame is often about maximizing a secondary objective and blocking your opponents from their own best-laid plans. A timely two-card discard can swing the final tally, especially in travel mode where the scoring sheet is quick to reference and quick to weigh.
- Don’t neglect defense. If you’re ahead by a slim margin, a well-timed pick toward a blocking card (or forcing your neighbor to take a less valuable card) can protect your advantage. The benefit of a travel edition is that you can do all this with a cheeky grin and a spare napkin as your scorepad.

### Card-Level Decisions: What to Chase and What to Pass
- Maki: When in doubt, draft a few maki cards, because the endgame reward scales with the number of maki you control. However, be mindful of your opponents’ potential to catch up. If everyone wants maki, you may end up with a larger chunk of points than expected—or those points could be shared in a way that doesn’t favor you.
- Nigiri: Nigiri cards are the simple, reliable baseline. They give you straightforward points and are less susceptible to the chaos of early-round picks. If your hand is heavy with nigiri and a few risky, high-reward cards, consider whether you can stabilize your lead or risk a small buffer of extra points for a big swing later.
- Sashimi: The high-risk, high-reward option. Sashimi can be game-breaking when you land the three-card set, but chasing it too early can distract you from building a robust, versatile portfolio of smaller, dependable cards. The Travel Edition’s crisp scoring makes sashimi feel like a pack of dynamite that’s surprisingly user-friendly when you’ve got a few other cards stacking up.
- Dessert: Desserts are the rare, but if you can snag them, they add a high payoff. In travel, you might not rely on desserts every game, but when you need that extra push to cross the finish line, dessert cards can become the hero of the night.
