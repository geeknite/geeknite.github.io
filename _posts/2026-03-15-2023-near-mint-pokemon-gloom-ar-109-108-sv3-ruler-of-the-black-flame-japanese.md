## SV3 Set and Japanese edition
### What is SV3 and why Japan-only vibes matter
SV3 stands for a Japanese release line that often emphasizes superior print quality, unique foil patterns, and a curated set of art variants that don’t always appear in Western markets. The SV3 run includes a handful of AR cards that pop up with that extra layer of sheen and a slightly rarer print run. For a Gloom from SV3, you’re looking at a collector-friendly variant that’s meant to be both talked about at lunch tables and kept under a glass dome with a cedar-scented humidity control device (the small, expensive one, obviously).

Language and packaging differences matter too. The Japanese edition often uses slightly different card text or a different holo distribution than its English counterparts. If you’re chasing a pristine Near Mint condition, you’ll want to scrutinize the borders, corners, and surface for any micro-whites that betray a rushed batch. The Japanese SV3 ARs tend to have strong corner integrity when well-preserved, but there’s always a possibility of subtle print lines or minor scuffing that can be more noticeable under direct light. The key is to evaluate the card under consistent lighting and to ask for a close-up of the holo pattern to ensure it’s not disharmonic—i.e., that the pattern aligns across the entire surface rather than appearing jarringly broken in a single corner.

### Practical notes for buyers and graders
- Always request multiple photos showing the card edge and surface under different lighting angles. Micro-whitening around the edges can kill a Near Mint label quick.
- Verify the holo’s consistency: some ARs have more pronounced holo arcs than others. The more uniform, the better for a high grade bracket.
- Confirm that the numbering 109/108 aligns with your interpretation of AR variants; in some printings, AR cards are intentionally misnumbered for collectible mystique. Your grading report should clearly state confirmed set and variant.
- For Japanese cards, examine the back for any discoloration or gloss differences that might occur due to storage conditions.

## Condition and grading: Is it Near Mint as advertised?
Near Mint is a tricky label that can be as much about lighting and perception as it is about actual card integrity. For a 2023 print in SV3, there are a few rough guardrails to consider:
- Corners: Look for crisp corners with no dings or flattening. In ARs where the holo is heavy, a touch of edge whitening can creep in—especially around the card’s lower left if it was bumped during sleeving.
- Edges: Tiny scratches or micro-abrasions along the border can knock a Near Mint to a soft Mint 9 or even 8, depending on the grader’s standards. If the gloss shows any waviness under direct light, the grade can drop.
- Surface: The holo surface should be evenly distributed without clouding or fingerprint smudges. ARs can be more sensitive to micro-scratches or micro-swirls that appear under a strong light.
- Centering: While many collectors are forgiving of slight centering deviations in AR prints, a truly pristine Near Mint expects proper alignment, especially when the card is part of a limited-run set like SV3. A 60/40 to 55/45 margin is typical acceptable depending on the grader, but anything more off-kilter should be noted.

If your copy checks all these boxes with discipline, you’re looking at a solid Near Mint 9 with a whisper of a potential 9.5 if the grader is feeling generous and the holo pops with impeccable dispersion. The bottom line: a 2023 Gloom AR in Japanese SV3 is a premium example of “I’m here for the art, not the meta” and should be treated as a display piece more than a tournament staple.

### How to verify condition before purchase
- Request high-resolution scans for corners and the holo pattern.
- Seek confirmation of no surface stains or fingerprint marks that often appear as tiny specks under light.
- Check the card back for uniform coloring, consistent font clarity, and no misprint hints.
- If possible, have a reputable grader examine the card, especially if you plan to invest significantly.

## Practical care and storage tips
If you want your Gloom AR to look as pristine as possible for decades, here are some straightforward habits:
- Use high-quality sleeves with a transparent outer layer for display (avoid rough edges that can catch on the holo surface).
- Place the card into a protective top-loader or a magnetic-closure binder page with a sturdy, acid-free backing.
- Store away from direct sunlight and extreme heat—UV can fade holo patterns and alter the card’s appearance over time.
- Consider a controlled humidity environment; a stable RH around 45-55% is generally safe for cardboard collectibles, though you should tailor this to your local climate.
- Handle with clean hands or gloves to minimize oils that can smudge the holo or gloss.
- Maintain an organized catalog with gradients from Near Mint to higher grades if you’re chasing PSA 10 potential in the future. A little organization goes a long way when you’ve got a shelf full of dreams.
