Getting into the details: setup, rules, and pacing tips

Setup

1) Unfold the board and place it in the center. 2) Shuffle the scenario deck and flip the top card to reveal the mission. 3) Distribute player boards and give each player a starting hand. 4) Place tokens on their . . . well, whatever your shared scoring track requires. 5) Draw the first couple of cards to populate the initial hand. 6) Decide the first roles and read the flavor text that explains the mission’s stakes. The rest is pure player-driven momentum.

Turn structure

Each round consists of a set of actions where players can play cards, move on a shared map, refresh resources, or trigger a team ability. The exact actions vary by scenario, but the logic remains: use cards to influence the environment, coordinate with your teammates to optimize moves, and keep an eye on the ticking clock. Some rounds provide a neat window to push a risky operation, others demand you retreat and regroup. The drama comes from the tension between pushing forward and preserving the team’s viability.

Victory and failure conditions

Victory usually hinges on achieving the shared objective before the clock runs out or before the scenario deck unleashes a cascade of consequences. Failure is not a personal defeat but a gentle nudge toward learning and retrying with a slightly different approach. The deck reshuffles, the roles re-align, and the crew returns to the table with a story to tell about the near-miss that felt like a tiny heroic moment.

Strategic depth without the brain burn

While the game is accessible, there is measurable depth for players who want to optimize. The core strategic question is always: when do we commit resources now versus saving them for a later, possibly better, window? Some players will be great at coordinating, others at spotting opportunities to chain actions. The beauty is that these roles are fluid, and the best teams let players experiment with roles, learning by doing rather than by theorycrafting in a chat group.

Recommended strategy starter tips

- Communicate early and clearly about your intended moves. A quick plan beats a great plan that never happens.
- Prioritize essential actions. If you can prevent a worst-case scenario on this turn, you may prevent a cascade of failures later.
- Balance risk and reward. It is tempting to go for a high-reward move, but a safe progression can also be the path to victory.
- Use a little bluffing, but keep it light. Let your teammates think you are about to do something big, but avoid spoiling the surprise that makes the victory feel earned.
- Rotate roles to prevent fatigue. A little variety keeps the table engaged and prevents one person from carrying the whole load.
