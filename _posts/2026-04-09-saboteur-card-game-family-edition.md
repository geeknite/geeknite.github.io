The Family Edition emphasizes shorter sessions with a few added variants so you can tune the challenge level to younger players or to a rowdy group of adults who enjoy a lighter puzzle with their jokes.

Bottom line: if you loved Saboteur but found the original a tad spicy for family nights, the Family Edition is designed to preserve the DNA (treasure, tunnels, and trust issues) while turning the knobs toward accessibility, usability, and family-friendly fun. ## How to Play (Setup, Turn Structure, and Endgame)

In true Geeknite fashion, we’ll break this down into actionable chunks you can read aloud during a pre-game briefing. The rules are purposefully readable at a casual pace; you should be able to teach a new player in less than five minutes and start playing within ten (assuming a well-stocked pantry and a kid who’s keen on dramatic reveals).

### Setup

1) Shuffle the role cards and deal one to each player face-down. If you’re playing with younger kids, you can reveal roles after the first round to keep excitement manageable. The number of saboteurs changes with player count, and the booklet provides a simple chart so you don’t have to google “how many saboteurs for 5 dwarves.”

2) Shuffle the path cards. In the Family Edition, the path cards are color-coded and the artwork is friendlier to younger eyes. The goal is to connect the tunnel from the dwarves’ starting position to the treasure, which is represented by a special card.

3) Each player receives a set of action cards that let them block, rotate, or bend the path in helpful or villainous ways. Some cards are quick to deploy, others require a moment of strategic thought (an important lesson for growing minds). 

4) Set up the board with the dwarves on one side and the treasure on the opposite. Place the treasure cards near the end of the path area. You can start with a few initial cards laid on the table to establish a hint of “the tunnel” without giving away too much. It’s a balancing act—like assembling a sandwich with too much mayo.

5) Decide on any optional house rules. If you’re playing with a mixed-age group, you might agree on a “no bluffing until round two” policy to give younger players a fair shot at contributing ideas aloud.

### Turn Structure
A typical turn goes like this:

- Draw phase: players draw a card from the deck. You can earn a quick point of bragging rights if you manage to draw a card that looks suspiciously helpful to your own plan.
- Action phase: you may place a path card to extend the tunnel, play an action card to slow down a rival, or try to safeguard your own route to the treasure. Some action cards require you to sacrifice a card from your hand—this is where the family-friendly nature shines: you’ll often be making tactical moves that are clever rather than brutal.
- Sabotage phase: if you’re a saboteur, you’ll have opportunities to derail others’ paths with a stealthy flourish or a bold misdirection. If you’re a builder, you’ll focus on connecting the path and avoiding the most costly dead ends.
- End-of-round check: once the path reaches the treasure, the round ends and you tally the gold. If the treasure remains unreachable but all players have participated, you may switch to a new round with a fresh slate. The Family Edition encourages shorter rounds, so you aren’t stuck in a stalemate while the adults debate corner cases.

### Endgame and Scoring

At the end of a round, you’ll count the gold nuggets secured by the builders who reached the treasure. Saboteurs score points when their misdirections lead to the loss of treasure or a misaligned path. The exact scoring details are in the rulebook, but the gist is simple: you want the most gold, and you want as few dead ends as possible. The Family Edition often includes a compact scorepad or a quick-reference sheet that makes tallying effortless. You’ll want to celebrate wins with a chorus of dwarven “ho-ho-ho!” or a groan-worthy pun about gold ore.

If you’re playing multiple rounds, you’ll swap roles and keep a light track of who’s who. The best feature here is that the kids tend to remember who was a saboteur in the last round, which leads to delightful discussions like, “Is it really you, or are you being sneaky again?” Those moments are gold for family bonding, even if your table looks like a group of caffeinated squirrels negotiating a cave-in.

## Strategy for Small Groups: Team Play and Social Dynamics

Saboteur is often about head games and misdirection; the Family Edition preserves that flavor while making the social dynamic gentler and more inclusive. Here are a few strategy notes you can practically apply at your kitchen table:

- Read the room, not just the cards: Since secrecy is toned down, you’ll want to observe how players react when new path cards are revealed. Subtle tells can be a lot of fun in mixed-age groups.
- Build with intent: In the Family Edition, you’ll benefit from planning a couple of steps ahead, especially when you’re a builder aiming to connect to the treasure without creating obvious dead ends.
- Sabotage smartly, not aggressively: The family rule-set typically rewards clever misdirection rather than chaotic disruption. Consider timing your sabotage to block a pathway an opponent is likely to rely on in the next turn or two, not just to cause sudden chaos.
- Pair up when helpful: In some groups, you’ll see a “two-heads-are-better-than-one” vibe developing. If your group enjoys collaboration, you can incorporate a “co-op round” variant where two players work together against the others. ## Variants and House Rules: Customizing the Night

The Family Edition is designed to be flexible. Here are a few variants that work well in practice:

- Quick Play Variant: Reduce the total number of rounds to two or three, and use a fixed treasure card. This keeps the session tight and lets you end on a satisfying note rather than a cliffhanger.
- Co-op Round: For a change of pace, pair players into teams for a single round where both teams are trying to reach the treasure together. Add a shared objective card to encourage communication and strategy.
- Age-Scaled Sabotage: If you have a wider age range, allow younger players to skip sabotage cards until the second round. This gives older players some headroom for more complex moves while younger players still feel essential to the game’s momentum.
- Drawer’s Choice: In a round, the person who draws first gets to select one special event card that remains in effect for the rest of the round. It’s a fun way to introduce a random twist without breaking the game’s balance.
