## Gameplay and mechanics

### How Jenga Xtreme differs from classic Jenga
The Xtreme subtitle is not just a marketing flourish. It implies that the gameplay is tuned for slightly more risk, speed, and drama. In practice, the tower remains the same basic structure as classic Jenga. You still remove a block from any level and place it on the top to build the tower higher and higher. The difference lies in how players approach the game: you will be more aggressive with the lower levels, you will attempt to create a more dramatic wobble near the top, and you will be tempted to just one more block before calling it a night.

In the Parker Brothers 2003 edition, the blocks feel a touch more densely milled, with a smoother glide when you extract them. ## Strategy and tips

### Basic strategy without spoilers
The core strategy of Jenga Xtreme is simple on the surface: remove a block that yields the least rotation and place it on top. But the devil is in the details. You want to identify the blocks that are the most inert, those that will shift weight most gracefully when removed, and those that will cause the least tangling of adjacent blocks. The more you can optimize your pull, the longer you can delay the inevitable collapse while your opponents squirm.

A practical approach is to start by scanning the lower levels for blocks with visible gaps or wear. The wear marks tell a story of previous attempts; if a block is darker along the long edge, it has been pulled before and may be looser now. When you pull, avoid twisting your wrist into a weird angle; a smooth, almost clinical resistance is what you want. The goal is to move a block with minimal friction so you can preserve the stability of the top layers. When placing the block on top, do so with a steady hand and a sense of ceremony, as if you are crowning a tiny tower of triumph.

## Practical care and maintenance

### How to care for the blocks
The Jenga blocks in this edition respond well to standard care. Keep them dry and relatively clean, since grime will impact how smoothly they slide between layers. If you notice a slight stiffness in certain blocks, a gentle wipe with a dry microfiber cloth should do the trick. Avoid soaking the wood or exposing it to high humidity, which can cause swelling and a noticeable change in how the blocks balance.

If you want to store the set for a long period, you can wrap the blocks in a soft cloth and place them back into the box. This is not essential for weekly use, but it helps when you want to keep the pieces from collecting dust or curiosity from cats that think the blocks are chew toys. A small silica gel pack in the box helps keep humidity down and the wood from taking on a life of its own over time.
