## What is Fortune Row Map Pack Expansion?
Fortune Row is a map-centric expansion for City of Mist that introduces a curated neighborhood of locations, block-by-block maps, and a handful of new encounter hooks designed to slot into any ongoing campaign. The expansion leans into the idea that the Mist overlays a city with legends, ambitions, and unspoken deals, and Fortune Row gives you a physical canvas for those stories to unfold. The heart of the pack is a modular map grid that can be configured into different districts, alleys, and rooftop runways for chases and confrontations. Think of it as a chessboard for mythic crime with a noir filter.

The box includes multi-panel map sheets, marker tokens, street signage cards, and encounter tokens that glow with the same neon aura you expect from a City of Mist night. It also ships with a small booklet of micro-scenarios that can thread into your existing city narrative or spark a one-shot that punches above its 3-hour runtime. The intention is not to replace your current city map but to provide a dense, walkable section of Fortune Row that feels alive, tactile, and a little bit dangerous to stake your claims on.

If you want a quick visual intro, the box art is unapologetically pulpy: steam and rain, a glistening cobblestone street, and silhouettes that suggest you should watch your back and watch your choices. It is the kind of art direction that telegraphs mood before you roll a single die. And that mood matters because City of Mist thrives on the vibe, not just the mechanics. The Fortune Row map pack leans into that vibe and gives you a set of tools to stage crime scenes, chases, and whispered bargains under the city’s metaphorical neon sky.

## How Fortune Row Changes the Gameplay
The core change is spatial: you now have a dedicated, bounded neighborhood to stage investigations, set-piece confrontations, and reveal conversations that hinge on location. The map encourages players and GMs to think in terms of local networks and micro-arcs rather than large city-wide campaigns. It invites you to sketch a crime scene, then drop the players into it with a sense that every alley and doorway might reveal a new mythic truth.

From a mechanics perspective, Fortune Row complements City of Mist’s tag-based system rather than overriding it. The new tokens and signs provide physical cues that can trigger the narrative tags you want to trigger without relying solely on verbal description. This is a boon for groups that enjoy a tactile layer to the story. It also helps new players ground themselves in the setting; if you describe a scene and point to a lamppost with a fortune-sign, the players instantly have a visual anchor to anchor their choices.

If you are curious about how this integrates with the dice and tag management, the feed is straightforward: you add the map as a local hub, assign scene-defining tags to areas that correlate with the mythic themes, and let the players explore the space. It is not a rules overhaul; it is a toolset that expands how you stage and manage scenes. The system still rewards the same patterns of risk, leverage, and mythic ambition, only with a more tangible urban stage.

A practical example: a chase sequence along Fortune Row’s riverfront necessitates vertical movement on rooftops and peril on street-level alleys. The map gives you physical platforms and line-of-sight constraints that you can describe with cinematic flair. The encounter tokens can represent witnesses, informants, or rival mythics lurking in the shadows. The GM can scaffold a scene by narrating a couple of moves on the map, while players decide where to position their assets and where to push their luck. The result is a compact, intense sequence that feels orchestrated, yet remains entirely emergent from the players’ choices and the district’s myths.

For GMs who worry about balance, Fortune Row does not tilt the table toward any single playstyle. It respects the game’s core structure and adds flavor and functional space. If your table loves urban exploration, you will likely adore Fortune Row. If your playgroup gravitates toward high-concept mythic clashes that skip most street-level details, you’ll still find value in the micro-locations and the mood they generate, but you may not use every piece to the fullest extent.

Additionally, the micro-scenarios in the booklet are designed for quick integration. They aren’t lengthy campaigns that demand commitment; they are modular seeds you can plant in a single session or seed across a two-to-three session arc. That gives you flexibility whether you run a weekly game or an occasional one-shot binge.

## Design Details and Practicalities
The design direction of Fortune Row leans into a tactile experience. The map tiles are not printed on flimsy stock; they’re thick enough to survive repeated setup, breakdown, and the occasional accidental napkin misplacement during an intense moment. The color palette is intentionally moody rather than garish, preserving the neon-soaked atmosphere that City of Mist is known for. The tokens are sized for easy handling; you won’t fumble for a tiny piece in the heat of a tense confrontation.

One welcome touch is the inclusion of street signs and city features that feel authentic enough to be useful but not so specific that they lock you into a particular city’s lore. You can slot Fortune Row into any metropolitan setting with a similar mythic overlay, which keeps the expansion valuable for long-term campaigns rather than becoming a one-off novelty

The map’s layout supports both grid-based and more free-form maps. If your GM style skews to precise positioning, you’ll appreciate the clear grid lines and alignment markers. If you enjoy looser spatial storytelling, the modular panels allow you to rearrange on the fly without losing the sense of place. It is a design that respects both rigidity and improvisation, which is crucial in a system built on narrative agency.

From an accessibility standpoint, the symbols on the tiles are clear, and the color-coded tokens help players quickly identify threats, allies, and points of interest. For groups with color vision diversity, the team has included a secondary set of symbols through the encounter cards and the signs to make key functions distinguishable without relying solely on color. This is the sort of practical attention that makes a map pack genuinely useful rather than just pretty to look at.
