### The Missing Dice Conundrum
Now, let’s talk about the dramatic twist: missing dice. It happens. You open the box, you spread the tiles, you arrange the survivors, and… where did the dice go? Are they in a parallel dimension where dice roll themselves? Were they stolen by mischievous dice goblins who ride the rails of a particularly intense dungeon crawl? No one knows. What we do know is that a missing dice situation changes the entire tempo of a demo: it slows the pace, forces house rules, and makes the board feel a little more like a scavenger hunt than an epic battle.

Here’s a practical approach: keep your sense of humor, check the packing list, and consider ordering replacement dice from CMON or your local retailer if the supplier is kind enough to ship them to you. For readers in the store demo orbit, you may need to brace for the possibility that the dice are shinier in the store’s display than in your own basement.

## Gameplay Experience

### Cooperative Play and Tactical Depth
Zombicide Black Plague is a cooperative battle against a zombie tide, and the tactical depth comes from selecting survivors, positioning on the board, and managing the color-coded actions on your cards. The more players you have, the more chaotic and brilliant it becomes. The demo unit won’t necessarily demonstrate every corner of strategy—after all, you’re trying to coax players into spending money, not watching a chess match between undead overlords. Still, the core vibe remains: you’ll coordinate with your team, pick which doors to bust, which dudes to shoot, and which survivors to risk for the sake of the mission.

### Card and Zombie Mechanics
The game’s card mechanics drive the pace: event cards, survivor actions, and the ever-present threat tracker. The zombies have predictable patterns (at least in the sense that they move toward living flesh in a zombified herd). The interplay between card draws and player decisions creates those dramatic “one more turn” moments that turn casual game nights into story-driven marathons.

## Production Quality and Components

### Miniatures and Paint Jobs
The minis are the showpiece. They’re chunky, highly detailed, and designed to be used on a large table where you can actually appreciate the sculpts. In a demo unit, you’ll notice some variety in paint quality and assembly—some display minis look like they came off a professional painter’s bench, while others might look a little more “adventure in a kitchen table workshop.” Either way, the promise of a living, breathing medieval zombie apocalypse is clear.

### Board Tiles and Terrain
The board tiles are sturdy and large enough to accommodate a decent number of players without feeling claustrophobic. The tiles interlock reasonably well, and you’ll often find yourself rearranging the setup mid-mission as you realize where the groaning undead are likely to sprout next. The art on the tiles captures a moody, grim vibe that suits the setting—gothic arches, blood-stained stone, and enough corpses to make you question your life choices.

## The “Missing Dice” Incident: What Happened and How It Affects You
If you’re hoping to avoid any dice-related drama, the demo unit is going to challenge you. Missing dice are unsatisfying, yes, but they aren’t the end of the world. Here are a few practical takeaways:
- It’s a reminder to have a Plan B: keep spare dice handy, or at least a couple of d6 proxies that won’t ruin the experience.
- It gives you a chance to explain die-hard strategy in a way that only a dice shortage can illustrate.
- It becomes a memory anchor: future games will always have that one demo night when the dice refused to show up, making your final boss victory a little sweeter.

### Troubleshooting in a Demo
If you’re a store staffer, here are some quick tips to avoid the scariest thing since a misprinted card: lost inventory turning a demo into a nuisance:
- Verify the packing list in the manual before customers arrive. If the dice aren’t there, you’ve got time to kit your shelves with replacements.
- Keep a small box of generic dice as placeholders. They don’t look as pretty, but they save many a headache.
- Communicate with the community: post about the issue, and offer replacement dice or a discount on the full set to mitigate the sting.

### Customer Service and Replacement Policy
CMON typically has a pretty friendly support channel for replacement parts. If you contact them with a photo of the damaged or missing item, they’ll likely set you up with the missing dice or appropriate substitutes. It’s not the same as having your box complete on day one, but it’s better than a sour note fading into a memory of “that one demo where the dice disappeared.” If you’re reviewing a demo, mention the support experience: it’s part of the product’s life story and helps future players decide whether the game is a keeper or a good story to tell at parties.

## Solo and Cooperative Play Tips
Whether you’re riding solo or assembling a team of brave survivors, the game rewards careful planning and flexible tactics:
- Start by identifying your strongest survivor and plan two moves ahead. The other players will appreciate you for avoiding the classic “I forgot to move” tragedy.
- Use doors and choke points to funnel zombies, but don’t overcommit your survivors to a single corridor. The best strategy is something like keep one hero near the loot and one hero near the exit.
- Remember the objective cards: sometimes you win by completing a mission goal rather than annihilating the undead. In the absence of the full dice, improvise: a two-step plan beats “roll the dice and cross your fingers” any day.
