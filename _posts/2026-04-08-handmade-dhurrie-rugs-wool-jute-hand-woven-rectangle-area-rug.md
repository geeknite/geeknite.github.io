## What is a dhurrie rug?
A dhurrie is a traditional flatweave rug. No long pile here; the surface is compact and dense. The weaving technique uses a loom to pass weft threads over warp and under, creating a tight, smooth finish. The flat weave yields a rug that lies flush with the floor, reducing trip hazards and making cleaning easier. The rectangle shape makes it versatile for almost any room.

Wool adds softness and resilience. The wool portion provides warmth and bounce, and jute adds strength and a natural, earthy texture. The wool plus jute mix yields a rug that balances warmth with rugged practicality.

## Wool and jute: The dynamic duo
Wool is widely known for its bounce and resilience. It repels moisture better than some natural fibers and holds dye well, giving you color longevity. Jute is a strong fiber that holds up under stress without clumping. The combination yields a rug that is comfortable to walk on yet tough enough to handle daily life.

Design considerations: The fiber pairing influences how the rug ages. Wool tends to acquire a soft patina with use; jute improves with a little sun, turning more golden with age. The combination often results in a warm, versatile palette that fits many interior design schemes.

## The craft: Hand weaving process
The weave is performed on a loom, with a weaver guiding the process by hand. Each rug requires careful alignment and tension management. The weaver selects yarn colors, weaves according to a pattern, and finishes the edges with a careful binding to prevent unraveling. Because the rug is handmade, you might notice slight irregularities that add character rather than defect.

The process is a reminder that artisanal craft is alive and well in the age of mass production. Each rug becomes a small narrative of the workshop day, the loom, and the hands that worked it.

## Practical considerations: sizes, shapes, and rectangles
Rectangular rugs are versatile; measure your space before purchase. In living rooms, the rug should accommodate all main furniture pieces or at least the front legs of sofas. For dining rooms, ensure the chairs remain on the rug when pulled out. The rug should extend beyond the chairs to prevent edges from catching clothing.

Contracting hallways or passageways in narrow spaces can benefit from a long, slender dhurrie that runs the length of the corridor. A rug with a low profile is ideal for such applications because it won’t trip door thresholds or hamper rug-to-floor transitions.

Thoroughly consider maintenance: a hand woven rug benefits from a stable base like a rug pad. The pad helps with noise insulation and reduces wear along the edges.

## Room pairing and styling ideas
Living rooms: Use a larger dhurrie under a sofa group to anchor the space. A muted palette is ideal for a minimalist look, while a bold pattern can become the focal point in a more eclectic room. ## Care and maintenance
Wool and jute require different care approaches. Vacuum frequently to remove dust and prevent soil from embedding in the weave. Avoid rough scrubbing that can damage the fiber. For spills, blot immediately with a clean cloth. For stains, use a mild cleaning solution suitable for natural fibers and test in an inconspicuous area first.

Sun exposure can affect color over time; consider rotating the rug so it ages evenly. Avoid using bleach or strong solvents that can degrade the fibers. If a deep cleanse is needed, hire professionals who handle natural fiber rugs.

## Comparisons with other rug types
- Compared to machine made flatweave: The handmade dhurrie has slight irregularities that enhance character. The machine-made version can be more uniform but lacks the subtle personality of a hand woven piece.
- Compared to pile rugs: Dhurries are flatter and easier to clean; they also lay flatter on hard floors and align with contemporary aesthetics.
- Compared to kilims: Kilims can be more colorful or geometric; dhurries may lean toward earthy neutrals that pair well with wood tones and natural textures.
