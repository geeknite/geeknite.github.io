## What makes Fortune Row custom sheets different (at a glance)
Fortune Row sheets are designed to be readable at a glance, stress-test narrative choices, and keep track of two things that City of Mist players care about most: mythic identity and street-level constraints. Here’s what these sheets typically emphasize:

- Mythos and Logos balance: A clean, visual split that immediately shows which lane your character is leaning into when a scene kicks off.
- Tag management: A compact system for managing the numerous tags you’ll accumulate as clues, powers, and relationships emerge.
- Clue-to-action mapping: A focused area to connect plot hooks to in-world actions, helping GMs weave scenes without constant page-flipping.
- Narrative carryover: Space for “Coincidence counters” or “Fortune points” that remind players and GMs of the ongoing momentum in the mystery.
- Visual rhythm: A design language that uses iconography and color blocks to reflect allegiance (city, myth, and personal arc) without bogging down the mechanical side.

If you’re used to a more traditional RPG sheet, Fortune Row’s design can feel like stepping into a jazz club after a night at a corporate workshop: familiar chords, but with pizzazz. The sheets make room for both the storytelling beats and the procedural needs of a game that moves between the mythic and the mundane at the speed of a storyline twist.

## Design philosophy: form follows function (with style)
The heart of these sheets lies in a simple principle: players should be able to answer, in a glance, who they are, what they can do, and what kind of trouble they’re courting. To achieve this, the sheets push together several design strategies:

- Minimalism with meaning: Less clutter, more storytelling cues. The layout prioritizes the most used fields during a session: identity, mythos tagline, noir triggers, and key relationships.
- System transparency: The sheet should reveal how many “Narrative Points” or “Fortune Clues” you’re carrying and how your icon powers scale with milestones. Information density is good, but it should be legible density.
- Visual encoding: Color blocks, icons, and tab-style sections encode the character’s three axes (Mythos, Logos, and personal stakes). This reduces cognitive load during tense moments when the scene demands quick references.
- Print-and-play friendly: The sheets are designed to be printed on standard letter or A4 stock with clear margins, so you can hand them to players mid-session if a new character steps in or a new mystery unfolds.

From a GM’s perspective, the most valuable payoff is speed. If a sheet lets you drop a clue, recall a trait, or flip onto a tag in under five seconds, you’ve got a winner. For players, the payoff is confidence: knowing that the sheet visually supports their character’s story arc rather than concealing it behind a labyrinth of micro-details.

## A closer look at the sheet layout
The Fortune Row sheets I reviewed typically organize information into distinct, color-coded zones. Here’s a practical tour of what you’ll encounter:

- Header zone: Character name, concept, and a compact identity portrait. This space establishes the tone of the character without burying you in backstory.
- Mythos vs Logos split: Two main columns or blocks. Mythos tracks your destiny, your god-killer, your noir myth, etc. Logos captures the rational side: occupation, personal code, and the day-to-day mechanics that keep your life from spiraling into mythic cliché.
- Power tags and abilities: A grid or stacked list for your active powers, with quick markers to show limits, number of uses, or cooldown. The sheet might use icons to denote a Power, a Gimmick, or a Special Ability.
- Clues and connections: A dedicated space to log clues you’ve gathered, people you know, and factions you’re entangled with. This area is essential for a mystery-driven game where social leverage and information is currency.
- Fortunes and Fates: A strip that keeps track of points, counters, or currency used for narrative leverage. The “Fortune” theme is not just thematic; it’s a playable resource that can swing a scene’s direction.
- Backstory hooks: A compact panel for three to five hooks or “props” that connect your character to Fortune Row’s intrigues. This is the GM’s map to weave your story into the city’s threads.

In practice, this layout supports a session’s ebb and flow. When the action heats up, you’re not rummaging through a sea of microfields; you’re sliding your thumb along a color-coded index and narrating from a place of clarity. When a new clue comes in, you can slot it into a hook or link it to a mythic theme, and you’ve still got room for a dramatic flourish on the final lines of a reveal.

## Playtesting notes: what works on the table
I ran a handful of sessions with Fortune Row sheets, focusing on a medium-length noir arc and a one-shot that leaned into a heist motif. Here are the practical takeaways from those sessions:

- Speed of reference: The sheets’ color zones let players locate the right section in under a breath. This reduces dead air while players search for a rule or a narrative cue.
- Narrative momentum: The Fortune-related counters felt natural on the table. When a player spent a counter to trigger a twist, the sheet clearly indicated what changed in the scene, which in turn accelerated storytelling and GM improvisation.
- Character orientation: The Mythos/Logos split helped players articulate both dramatic identity and grounded motivation. It reinforced the dual nature of City of Mist characters: you’re both mythic and mortal, weaving scenes that swing between grand destiny and everyday choice.
- House-rule friendly: The sheet’s zones adapt well to a few simple modifications. For example, you can temporarily convert the Clues zone into a “Doorway” log for a dungeon-like sequence or convert the Fortunes into a “Stress/Complication” track for a tense social encounter.
- Accessibility considerations: The fonts and contrasts were chosen for readability at the table. If your group uses a projector or screens, the sheet scales nicely to larger prints and still reads well from a distance.

## Printing, binding, and setup tips
If you’re considering printing your own Fortune Row sheets, here are practical tips to get you from desk to table without drama:

- Paper choice matters: Use a slightly heavier stock (80–100 lb cover) with a matte finish to reduce glare during sessions. The color blocks are sharp on matte stock, which improves legibility in low-light gaming rooms.
- Margins and bleed: Ensure your printer’s bleed settings are correct if you’re printing two-sided sheets. Fortune Row sheets often rely on two-page spreads for the full layout, so a tiny misalignment can ruin the flow.
- Binder or folio: For ongoing campaigns, a three-ring binder with page protectors keeps sheets safe and adaptable as you add new Clues and Fortunes across sessions.
- Digital version: If you’re using laptop or tablet notes, consider keeping a digital copy with editable fields. A PDF form-fillable variant can be a nice middle ground, letting players update their Clues without rewriting the sheet by hand.
- Accessibility: Provide a high-contrast printable version for players with visual impairment. A simple toggle of color blocks to grayscale can maintain the information hierarchy without sacrificing readability.

## Visual critique: what worked, what could be brighter
The sheets generally succeed in delivering a clean, readable aesthetic that doesn’t overwhelm the player with iconography. The color palette tends to favor noir blues and muted golds, with bright accents used for critical powers or urgent clues. A few notes for potential refinements:

- Iconography density: A handful of icons can become quickly overloaded if the table uses a lot of powers or relationships. Consider a tiered icon system where early levels use simple icons and advanced arcs introduce more complex glyphs.
- Cold read vs. hot read: In a tense scene, you want players to be able to scan the sheet for the exact phrase that describes the current conflict. It can help to bold or highlight the phrases that tend to be used during a scene, such as “Active Power,” “Clue,” or “Fortune Point.”
- Margin clarity: If you’re using two-sided sheets, ensure that nothing important sits too close to the binding. A small change in layout can preserve readability when printing double-sided.

If you’re into fan-made variants, the community has produced a handful of alternate colorways and typefaces. These variations aren’t required, but they can offer a quick way to customize the vibe for a particular arc—say, a neon-drenched caper in Fortune Row versus a smoky, rain-slick investigation.

## Accessibility and inclusivity considerations
No design review would be complete without acknowledging accessibility. Here are some practical tweaks you can apply to Fortune Row sheets to make them more inclusive:

- High-contrast mode: Provide grayscale and high-contrast color palettes to ensure readability for players with color vision differences.
- Font choices: Use legible fonts with clear letter shapes. Avoid overly decorative fonts in body text; reserve stylized type for headers only.
- Tactile-friendly options: For players who rely on tactile feedback or prefer heavy sheets, consider thicker stock or laminated versions with dry-erase markers for quick changes during sessions.
- Alternate input methods: Encourage players who use keyboard navigation to tap through fields if you’re running a digital version, and ensure alt text is attached to images for screen readers.

The beauty of Fortune Row is that it invites storytelling without forcing players to become spreadsheet savants. When you tailor the sheet to be accessible, you widen the circle of who can tell a great noir story in City of Mist.
