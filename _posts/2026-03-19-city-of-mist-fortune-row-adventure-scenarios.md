# City of Mist: Fortune Row — Adventure Scenarios

![Fortune Row Map](https://example.com/images/fortune-row-map.jpg)

Fortune Row is the neon gutter where luck is regulated by the clock and the clock is usually late. Welcome to a district that only surfaces in memory after a dramatic fade-to-black. In this guide we dive into Fortune Row, a pocket of the City of Mist where jazz riffs with shadows, and where every deal has a price that doesn’t always come with a receipt. If you’ve ever run a City of Mist session that felt like a bad dream you woke up from laughing, this is for you.

## Overview

Fortune Row is not a single neighborhood so much as a social experiment gone charmingly wrong. It sits at the crossroads of myth and market, where rogue gods moonlight as investment bankers and a nightclub uses a mortgaged moon as a stage prop. GMs should expect a tone that leans into pulpy noir with a wink to high fantasy, all wrapped in neon and jazz riffs.

### The District That Murmurs

Fortune Row is built on locations that echo: a casino that knows your secrets, a theater that rewrites past performances, a spa that’s more of a confession booth, and a bookshop that trades in futures rather than pages. The city’s masks—the surface personas your players wear—intersect with Fortune Row’s motifs: luck, debt, memory, and fate. The Row thrives on conversations that feel like prop bets and ends with someone either buying the world or slinging it into the rubbish chute of oblivion.

- The Fortune House: a towering complex where fortunes are brokered and lies are valued as collateral.
- The Velvet Ledger: a speakeasy that records every secret told within in exchange for a loan.
- The Neon Bazaar: a market where enchanted trinkets change hands, and literally anything can be bought if you have the right moral currency.

These locations aren’t just backdrops; they’re active engines that push the story forward. The GM will want to map out a few anchor NPCs who own the Row as if it were a shared asset in a board game: a broker who negotiates with gods, a chanteuse who sings down curses, and a debt collector who never collects but only lends you your next mistake.

### Key Players of Fortune Row

Fortune Row isn’t short on personalities. It’s drenched in charisma and consequences. The following archetypes give you a quick cast list to drop into a one-shot or weave through a longer campaign.

- The Broker: An ensemble of ancient powers wearing a slick suit and a calculator. They speak in probabilities and always carry a contract that folds itself when you blink. They’re the dealer, but they’re also the true game master.
- The Velvet Singer: A performer who can bend luck itself, though every note costs a memory.
- The Chalk Prophet: A soothsayer who reads futures in chalk dust; their prophecies rewrite themselves if not paid.
- The Debt Collector: Not the guy who collects money; the one who loans it. They offer dangerous choices with a smile that makes crime look charming.

## Adventure Hooks in Fortune Row

Fortune Row shines when players strike a deal with something they don’t fully understand. The hooks below are designed to be modular so you can slot them into a variety of your campaigns, whether you’re running a one-shot or a longer arc.

1) The Whispering Ledger: A ledger in The Velvet Ledger begins to rewrite itself, muttering the names of celebrities who didn’t do what the ledger accuses them of, and the players start seeing their own names appearing and disappearing in the margins. The twist: the ledger is a living contract that feeds on fear. To break the pattern, players must dive into a past mistake that their mask is trying to forget.

2) The Lottery of Echoes: Anyone who wins fortune at a specific machine in the Fortune House has their memory swapped with someone else in Fortune Row. The machine has a life of its own and a history of victims who attempted to undo the swap and failed spectacularly.

3) The Parade of Debt: A nightly parade through the Neon Bazaar reveals that debt is a living creature in the city; the parade’s 'float' is a massive creature made of unpaid IOUs. The players can either negotiate with the debt creature or become its new debt collectors, which could be a delicious moral trap.

4) The Masked Ball: A masquerade takes place in a palace that physically rearranges itself. Guests become stuck in an intricate puzzle that forces them to reveal a truth about their own masks or risk being trapped forever in a warren of corridors.

5) The Fortune Heist: A known artifact—the Coin of Chance—vanishes from public view. The heist is a trap that reconfigures fortune around the players themselves, turning minor success into a catastrophe and casting a harsh light on who they truly are beneath their masks.

Include hooks about the players’ relationships with Fortune Row’s NPCs to provoke roleplay that feels personal. For example, if a player’s Mask has a debt motif, put them at risk of losing something precious unless they navigate a social bargain with The Broker. The aim is to weave social conflict with supernatural stakes, letting the players feel both the weight and whimsy of the Row.

## Sample Scenarios

Here are three ready-to-run adventures you can drop into Fortune Row. They’re designed to be modular and can be scaled with more danger, complexity, or whimsy depending on your table’s appetite for chaos.

### Scenario A: The Elevator to Nowhere

Setup: A building in Fortune Row contains an elevator that only ascends when someone pays a debt—literal debt owed to a god of luck. The party must navigate the building’s tenants and the debt rules. Clues are hidden in elevator control panels; players who read the control panels learn about a past incident in which Fortune Row’s luck was broken by a single misdeed.

Act I: The party enters the building and meets residents who insist the elevator is a test. Each floor reveals a different facet of luck—some floors grant luck with costs, others deny it with consequences.

Act II: The party discovers that the elevator is a trap that traps a god of luck in the building. The players can choose to release the god (freeing fortune for all in Fortune Row but at a price) or close the elevator for good (but risk a backdraft of misfortune that will haunt them elsewhere).

Twist: The elevator starts moving of its own accord, regardless of passengers, hinting that Fortune Row is trying to tell them something about their own masks.

Climax: The party must weigh whether to sacrifice a memory or a relationship to restore fortune to the city. The choice will shape the city’s mythic balance.

### Scenario B: The Masquerade That Feels Real

Setup: The Velvet Singer hosts a masked ball where attendees’ masks become their true selves for a limited time. The twist: the masks begin to reveal the audience’s hidden guilts and wrongdoings through dreams and memory flashbacks.

Act I: The party infiltrates the ball and meets NPCs who are not who they seem. Motifs are actively playing across scenes, more pronounced than usual.

Act II: A subplot emerges: someone at the ball has been slipping reverse bargains into the contracts of Fortune Row; the party must identify who and why before the Mask breaks down completely.

Twist: The party’s own masks begin to falter, forcing them to confront the fact that their own secrets could ruin the city’s fragile balance.

Climax: A social showdown in which the players must overturn the reverse bargain or let a catastrophic truth slip into daylight.

### Scenario C: Fortune’s Debt Returns

Setup: A familiar NPC from a previous adventure returns with a debt they cannot repay. They offer a dangerous bargain: a single choice that could save the NPC but doom the city’s already wobbly balance of luck. The players must decide whether to accept, refuse, or craft an alternative that benefits all.

Act I: The group discovers that not paying a debt is not always the worst fate; sometimes paying the wrong debt creates a new problem. The party will need to gather old favors and connect with other Districts.

Act II: The NPC’s past and present converge, revealing a plot to collapse Fortune Row into a single, monoculture version of luck—a complete monolith that will punish those who aren’t blessed by fortune.

Twist: The debt isn’t owed by the NPC but by the mask they wear. Taking responsibility for that debt means the NPC may have to relinquish something priceless—perhaps a memory or a relationship.

Climax: The players must decide whether to honor the debt, forge a new repayment plan, or gamble with a far more dangerous bet: tearing down Fortune Row’s mythic structure entirely and starting anew.

### GM Tips for Fortune Row

- Tone management: Fortune Row thrives on a balance between noir mood and playful whimsy. Encourage players to lean into their masks and the city’s pulsating rhythm of risk and reward. If the table feels too grim, nudge with a humorous NPC or a goofy magical item that still carries a consequence.
- Thematic anchors: Use debt, luck, memory, and fate as your anchors. Riff off these motifs in both social scenes and action sequences. The players’ choices should matter beyond a single scene; fortune should feel like a chain of consequences that extends beyond the one night.
- Social mechanics: City of Mist leans into masks and social mechanics the moment you step into Fortune Row. Encourage players to harness their social masks when negotiating with The Broker, or the Velvet Singer, or the Chalk Prophet. Each NPC has a “mask-strength” stat that can influence how a negotiation unfolds.
- Pacing: Fortune Row stories tend to run episodically. Use short scenes to build momentum, then pivot to a larger mystery that demands collaboration. The bag of tricks includes live music cues, contract negotiations, and sudden memory recaps that reveal a crucial clue.
- Combat vs. style: You don’t want a fight to be the only resolution. Use a mix of social drama, investigative clues, and stylized action. A well-timed dramatic reveal can be far more satisfying than a standard encounter check.

## Motifs, Clues, and Clocks

Fortune Row’s power sources—motifs—are keys that unlock the city’s deeper secrets. Use a handful of recurring clues to tie sessions together, but hide them behind quirky, memorable moments. A few ideas:

- A coin that always lands on the edge and refuses to tell you which side is “really” up.
- A clock in the Fortune House that moves backwards when a lie is spoken aloud.
- A song that rewrites the memory of a scene when played at a certain tempo.
- A debt ledger that reorders itself when a new debt is added.

When players interact with these clues, embed a choice: perform a social bargain, reveal a truth, or accept a temporary boon that will demand repayment later. These choices are what keep Fortune Row feeling alive and dangerous rather than a static stage.

## Using Fortune Row: A Scene-by-Scene Approach

To help you run Fortune Row in a heartbeat rather than a slog, here’s a scene-by-scene template that you can clone for almost any scenario. It’s lightweight, but it’s enough to keep players moving and thinking in the same rhythm as the city.

- Scene 1: Welcome to Fortune Row. The players arrive and meet their anchor NPCs. The mood is established: neon lights, suspicious smiles, the faint smell of power and something else—like cinnamon and regrets.
- Scene 2: The choice. A deal is offered that may seem too good to be true. The players must decide whether to take the risk, or walk away with a smaller reward but fewer consequences.
- Scene 3: The reveal. The truth behind the deal is exposed via a matter-of-fact, almost clinical explanation by the GM. This scene should shift the party’s perspective on what they’re willing to sacrifice.
- Scene 4: The chase. Fortune Row has its own tempo. A chase through The Neon Bazaar or a shuffle down a back-alley corridor can feel like a dream sequence with sudden realization punctures.
- Scene 5: The choice re-uttered. The players choose again, with new information painting their options in a more dramatic light.
- Scene 6: Epilogue. A short closing beat that leaves a thread for future sessions or a final image that will linger in players’ minds long after the dice are packed away.

## Practical GMing Tips

- Pre-Session Prep: Create a master document that includes NPC motivations, known debts, and the Row’s current balance of fortune. The goal is to have a few lines of dialogue for each major NPC and at least two potential misdirections per scene.
- Table Experience: Consider using a live “fortune dial” that tracks players’ luck. Every time someone makes a seemingly reckless choice, tilt the dial toward risk. When a character acts with selflessness, tilt toward reward.
- Custom Items: Fortune Row isn’t short on weird artifacts. You can design a few items that touch on motifs without becoming power creep: a pocket watch that runs backwards while telling the truth, a coin that refuses to flip if a lie is told, or gloves that grant a successful social encounter but erase a memory.
- Handling Time: The Row invites a quick rhythm: talk, bargain, reveal, resolve. If a scene bogs down, cut to a quick “fortune check” with a few consequences rather than dragging a scene into a slow melt.

## A Quick Reader’s Guide to the City’s How-To

- Masks: The masks your players wear are both their surface persona and a source of power. Learn to use their mask triggers in social scenes to push the plot forward.
- Motifs: Let the Row echo motifs in every scene—debt, luck, memory, fate. Tie clues to motifs so players learn to recognize patterns rather than collecting random items.
- Clues: Clues aren’t just items. They’re social leverage: a name whispered in a signature accent, a receipt that hints at a larger scheme, a rumor that opens up a new path.
- Scenes: Fortune Row runs on mini-scenes that can pivot your plot. Use one or two strong dramatic moments per hour to keep energy high.
