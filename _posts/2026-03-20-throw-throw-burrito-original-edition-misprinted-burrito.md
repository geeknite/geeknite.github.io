## Overview: what is Throw Throw Burrito?

Throw Throw Burrito is a party game designed by Matthew Inman (the man behind the infamous Exploding Kittens universe) and published for players ages 7 and up. The core idea is simple and brilliantly ridiculous: players collect or discard cards to score points, all while two oversized plush burritos fly around the table. The burritos function as both projectiles and comedic devices that create immediate, chaotic tension. The original edition in a complete-in-box state promises all tokens, cards, rules, and two burritos intact. You’ll need space, a sense of humor, and a willingness to dodge a fuzzy, bean-sack meteor if you want to truly embrace the theme.

The misprinted burrito twist in this edition adds an extra layer of story to your gaming night. ## Setup and gameplay flow: getting into the chaos

### Setup
Setting up Throw Throw Burrito is quick. Clear a nice, large surface area—your coffee table is probably insufficient unless you’ve got a dedicated game shelf. Shuffle the cards, deal a hand to each player, place the burritos within arm’s reach, and set the score tracker in the center. The rulebook warns you to build a “no-mourners” zone: a little buffer space around the players where the burritos can safely soar without taking out your plant shelves or your TV remote. It’s advice you’ll appreciate when someone tosses a burrito with the enthusiasm of a caffeinated penguin.

### How a round plays out
Each round consists of players drawing cards and playing attacks, defenses, or special actions. The objective is to score points by completing card sets or by triggering misdirection moves that cause opponents to misplay. When a card requires you to “Throw” or “Hit” someone with a burrito, you do so with a flourish—don’t aim for the face, aim for the top of the headrest, the shoulder, or the captain’s chair if you’re playing in a living room. The moment a burrito makes contact with a player’s zone (arms up, head dodged, etc.), the action resolves, and points are scored or lost accordingly. The misprinted burrito adds a bit of whimsy to this process; occasionally you’ll throw and miss because the burrito’s whimsy was too powerful, not because you aimed poorly.

### Turn structure and decision-making
Decisions in Throw Throw Burrito are often a blend of risk assessment and pure chaos management. Do you dump a hefty Attack card hoping your opponent is distracted by the misprinted burrito mascot? Do you defend, knowing that a misfire could land you a penalty instead of a point? The card deck includes various effects: draw extra cards, steal points, or force an opponent to discard. The pace moves quickly; a misprinted burrito can turn a joke into a panic as someone fumbles to dodge or is unluckily hit by a tail-end toss. The quick rounds mean you’ll cycle through hands fast, which keeps the energy high and the commentary sharper than a burrito’s hot sauce joke.

## Tactics, strategies, and how to win the chaos

Winning in Throw Throw Burrito isn’t about complex math or perfect memory. It’s about reading the room, managing risk, and learning when to sprint for the card draw or duck under a flying foam projectile. Here are a few thoughtful tactics to help you win more rounds without losing your dignity:

- Dodge with purpose: Dodging is half the game. Train your reflexes, but don’t forget the moral of burrito night: the table’s laughter is a bigger prize than a few extra points.
- Card synergy: Some cards reward you for completing specific sets or triggering chain reactions. Pay attention to what your opponents are collecting and time your throws to deny them a critical piece.
- Protect your eyes, not your ego: The misprinted burrito is iconic, but safety matters more. Wear glasses if you’re worried about stray hits, and ensure there’s enough space to swing without hitting furniture or pets.
- Quick calculations: In a fast game, you’ll rarely have time for complex math. Instead, trust your gut about whether to defend or attack based on what you see on the table and how chaotic the room already feels.
