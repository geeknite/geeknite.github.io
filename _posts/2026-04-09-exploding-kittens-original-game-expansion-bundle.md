## What’s in the Original Game
The base game is a lean, fast experience designed to be taught in under five minutes and played in under 20 if you’re among decent competitors. The deck contains a mix of Exploding Kittens, Defuse cards, and a cadre of action cards that tilt risk versus reward in small, delicious bursts. The action cards do the heavy lifting on the board-room-noise side: See the Future (look at the top three cards), Attack (pass the turn with a stack of extra turns for your target), Skip (avoid drawing on your own turn), Favor (make someone give you a card), Shuffle (mix the deck anew), and so on. The balance is deceptively delicate: too many Defuse cards and the game drags; too few and the tension snaps to brittle speedrun mode.

The core gambit is simple: avoid Exploding Kitten cards, keep your Defuse reservoir stocked, and try not to make yourself the primary target by overreacting to a single card draw. The humor comes from the art, the silly card names, and the way players riff on the imminent possibility of literal kitten kaboom. The result is a game that is easy to teach to newcomers but quick to become a running gag among seasoned players who learn to savor those tiny, moment-to-moment decisions.

### How a typical turn plays out
On your turn you must either play an action card or draw a card from the deck. If you draw a non-exploding card, you’re free to continue to the next player's turn. If you draw an Exploding Kitten, you’re eliminated unless you can play a Defuse card to avoid an untimely doom. But even with a Defuse, the kitten isn’t out of the woods for good—the card is placed back into the deck in a random location, potentially threatening future players with a deliciously cruel twist of fate. The “defuse” mechanic adds a tactical nudge to the game’s tempo. You want to save Defuses for high-stakes moments, but you’ll often find yourself using one to stay in the game, even when your gut says you should hold out for a bigger moment.

The pacing skews toward the quick. In a standard six-player round, you’ll be bouncing from moment of intense attention to lull of banter much more often than you might expect. That rhythm is exactly what the original design aims for: high-energy turns with plenty of social pressure, but not so much downtime that people forget what they were laughing about in the first place.

## Setup and Playthrough with the Bundle
If you’re introducing this to a new group, you can follow a few practical steps to minimize onboarding headaches and maximize laughter:

1) Decide on the mode: base game or bundle. If the table has six or more players, the expansion rules tend to feel natural; with fewer players, you might want to scale back the additions to preserve clarity. 
2) Establish a simple rotation. With more cards and more players, you’ll want to keep a clean turn order. A quick reminder player can be useful: draw a card if you can; use an action card if it benefits your immediate objective; and remember that you can Defuse to survive a bad draw—just don’t overinvest early unless you’re chasing a long game.
3) Prepare a quick reference. The rulebook is short by design, but a one-page cheat sheet helps non-native firefighters (i.e., people who panic when they see a bomb and a kitten on the same card) acclimate quickly to the new set.
4) House rules for chaos. If your group loves speed and chaos, you can start with a house rule: every time a player draws the Exploding Kitten, they must declare a random reason for the cat’s misadventure. It sounds silly, but the social microdrama can be the real highlight of the night.

The important thing is to keep the energy high and the table conversational. Exploding Kittens and its expansions thrive on banter, misdirection, and the kind of playful cruelty that makes a party game memorable rather than forgettable.
