## How to play (rules overview)
The game is designed to be approachable, with a turnout-friendly flow that makes sense to players who have never seen a Life board in their life, let alone the lifetime of decisions that the original game prescribes. The core loop can be summarized in a few sentences, but as with many life-themed games, the joy comes from the little detours and the storytelling you build around those turns.

### Objective
Accumulate the most life points by completing events and milestones drawn from the card deck. Points flow in from multiple sources: successful events, well-timed plays, and a dash of luck that ensures no two rounds feel identical. The winner is the person who ends the game with the highest score, or in some variants, the first to cross a pre-determined threshold.

### Turn structure
- Draw phase: At the start of your turn, draw a card. The card might spark a new event, a risk, or a goofy bonus that has you question your personal life choices—purely in a game context, of course.
- Play phase: You may play one card from your hand if it aligns with your strategy for that turn. The card effects usually center around scoring, altering routes, or adding mini-challenges for your opponents.
- Resolve phase: Resolve any ongoing effects and tally points for cards that have immediate scoring value.
- Pass the turn: The next player takes their turn. The pace is brisk; there’s rarely a moment to stare anxiously at the ceiling contemplating your life choices in real-time.

### Card types
- Event cards: These are the star of the show. They present mini-scenarios like “You landed your dream job” or “You adopted a pet dragon” and grant immediate or conditional points.
- Milestone cards: These are evergreen targets you’ll aim for across several turns; completing a milestone can unlock big point multipliers or special actions that impact the end-game scoring window.
- Power-up cards: Temporary boosts that can tilt a single turn or a sequence of turns in your favor. These are the kind of cards that make players grin like a cartoon villain—just for a moment.
- Risk/Opportunity cards: The spicy bread-and-butter of the game. They come with positive and negative effects that force players to weigh short-term gains against potential long-term consequences.

It’s not a heavy rulebook, but it’s not a “shuffle and pray” situation either. The rules are accessible, and the synergy between card types creates a compact web of decisions that rewards players who pay attention to both what’s in their hand and what their opponents might be chasing.

## Strategy and tips for winning
- Read the table. A quick scan of opponents’ hands (where permissible by house rules) can reveal which milestones are most contested and which paths are becoming too crowded. If a couple of players are chasing the same milestone, you might pivot to a less popular route and clinch a quiet victory.
- Balance speed with caution. Because rounds are quick, there’s a tendency to chase big points aggressively. Don’t forget to reserve a few reactions to sudden event cards that can derail a lead in a single turn. The best players keep a small buffer of safe plays for the late game while occasionally pulling off a risky double-dip for a big payoff.
- Leverage event cards. Look for events that synergize with your milestones. A well-timed “double-dip” event can multiply your points if you align it with your current card inventory.
- Deny points when needed. In some situations, it’s worth taking a smaller, safer path to reduce an opponent’s opportunity to score on a future turn. The game’s compact ruleset rewards cognitive flexibility more than brute-force greed.
- End-game awareness. The closer you are to the final milestone threshold, the more each draw matters. As we roll toward the end, prioritize cards that can nudge you over the line and try to block opponents from reaching their goal on the same turn—if your play style allows this kind of strategic interaction.

If you want a concrete example of a winning approach, consider building a mixed strategy around two core milestones that aren’t as hotly contested as the marquee events. This way, you exploit the gaps in others’ plans while preserving a robust route to victory. It’s not a guaranteed blueprint, but it’s a satisfying way to approach the table with both humor and noticeability.
