## How to Play: Rules, Flow, and the Retro Vibe
### Quick-start rules refresher
UNO’s rules are famously simple, which is why you can teach a roomful of relatives in under a minute and still have time to **accidentally** change the table stakes. The Retro edition sticks to the core mechanics, with a couple of mild, tasteful twists to honor the era:

- Players try to discard all their cards by matching either color or number with the top card on the discard pile.
- Action cards do what they always did, but with a wink to retro design:
  - Skip: skip the next player.
  - Reverse: change the direction of play.
  - Draw Two: the next player draws two cards and loses a turn.
  - Wild: you pick the color to continue play.
  - Wild Draw Four: same as standard, but with extra drama.
- When you’re down to one card, you must say UNO. If you forget, other players can call you out and force you to draw a single card—classic social pressure as a game mechanic.

### Strategy meets nostalgia
If you’re a numbers person, the Retro deck rewards you for scanning color blocks quickly and planning a few steps ahead. The color palette’s legibility makes “color-based memory” a surprisingly practical skill, which is not only satisfying for brainy folks but also a gentle reminder that your memory was never going to be as reliable as the actual card arrangement. For social players, UNO remains a social lubricant; the retro case elevates the experience by providing a sturdy, portable centerpiece for conversations ranging from “who had the last moon landing” to “which color palette belongs on a time capsule.”

### House rules and the joy of customization
This edition blends well with light house rules. For example, you can add a quick “five-second UNO” bonus where anyone can play a Wild or Wild Draw Four card if they can justify it with a pithy pop-culture reference. Just be prepared for chaos and a few dramatic gasps when someone uses a Wild Draw Four on a player who’s already declared UNO and somehow still wins the round. It’s silly, but that’s the point of family game night with a retro flavor: nostalgia plus a dash of chaos equals memories that outlive the old console you still refuse to retire.

## Quick-start Rules for New Players (Cheat Sheet)
1) Shuffle thoroughly. The retro case makes a satisfying thump when you slam it closed, which is a ritual cue for new players to calm down and remember they’re about to have fun.
2) Each player draws 7 cards when starting. No snooping on other players’ hands—though let’s be honest, you’re going to glance at someone’s cards the moment you’re about to draw two.
3) Match color or number. Esteemed players can whisper hints about color preferences to the table to keep the chaos manageable, but not too many hints—this is UNO, not a TED Talk.
4) Use Wild cards strategically. Say the wild color aloud with theatrical flair and appoint your color captain for the next round if you’re feeling dramatic.
5) Say UNO when you have one card left. If you forget, a gentle reminder from the table is part of the tradition—risks minimal, bragging rights maximal.
