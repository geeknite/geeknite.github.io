### Condition and quality

The quality of a card lot is the difference between a handful of glossy pictures and a binder that looks like a chaotic treasure map. Here are the criteria I used to gauge condition:

- Edges: Are there noticeable nicks or whitening? A tiny corner dent is not a fatal flaw, but a huge crease is an axe to your deck history.
- Surface: Are there scratches or print lines that distract from the art? A slight gloss variance is normal for older sets; deep scratches are not.
- Centering: Are the borders roughly equal on all sides? This matters more on rarer cards that you plan to grade, but you should consider it for the overall presentation.
- Playability: Do the cards shuffle well? Will they respond to a potential top decking situation? If they are sticky or warped, you might need to rectify them with a proper storage method.

If you are buying used or from a third party, I recommend a simple routine: inspect under good light, feel the surface, and test a few flips to ensure you do not have a hidden ping.

### Practical tips for dealing with a card lot

- Organize immediately: Sort by form then by rarity. This helps you see what you actually own versus what you wish you owned.
- Protect with sleeves and a binder: Card sleeves are underrated. A good set of sleeves protects against coffee spills and accidental binder chaos.
- Photograph for your records: A quick photo session helps you keep track of what you have and what you might trade or sell.
- Consider graded options if you are serious about value: Grading can increase value, but it is an expensive and time consuming process that you should plan for.
- Avoid impulse buys by setting a budget: If you must, you can always do a swap with your favorite trading partner.
