## A Blip from the Factory: Who is Edouard Guiton?

If you follow miniature sculpting as a subculture, you’ve probably heard the name Edouard Guiton before. He’s known for a flair that blends dramatic anatomy with cinematic storytelling—think no-nonsense survivors, zombie antagonists who look like they’ve just decided to unionize, and details so crisp you could use them as a museum display. The Special Guest line in Zombicide is a chance to take a concrete leap from the standard boxy heroes and rebels and into the realm where sculpting becomes a co-star rather than a footnote.

From a design standpoint, Edouard’s style brings a distinct silhouette language to the table. You’ll notice elongated poses, sweeping fabric, and a few micro-details—rivulets of rain on a cloak, a dent in a helmet that tells a story, or a pistol that looks as though it could misfire with a dramatic arc. If you’re painting along with this line, you’re not just slapping paint on a model; you’re completing a narrative.

For those who prefer the dry stats, the minis are typically measured against the Zombicide standard: scalable to base game actions, compatible with standard boards, and built to survive a few rounds of table-wobbles and enthusiastic backpack-slams. The Edouard line ups the ante by offering sculpts with dynamic lines, characters who feel like they belong on a comic page, and a strong sense of personality in each face sculpt. It’s art with utility—art you can actually fling across a board without feeling guilty about your dice tray’s integrity.

### Box contents and presentation

Inside, you’ll find several miniatures featuring Edouard’s sculpting voice. The set focuses on key survivor archetypes and one or two standout zombie denizens designed to be the emotional anchors of a scenario. The materials are standard Zombicide quality—sturdy plastic that holds fine details and resists de-molding mishaps, with bases that fit snugly into the existing game’s footprint.

There’s a glossed insert card with a short blurb about Edouard’s approach, plus a tip card that offers practical painting suggestions tailored to these sculpts. The whole thing feels like a bridge between a gallery show and a board game insert, which is exactly the vibe the line aims for.

In terms of reusability, these minis aren’t just a one-off spectacle. Their scale and design are compatible with the base game’s encounters, but the silhouettes invite new tactics and different leveling trajectories—more on that in the gameplay section.

### The sculpting language: what Edouard brings to the table

Edouard’s signature is the cinematic still-frame moment: a figure mid-action, catching light in just the right way, telling you a story with a single pose. You’ll see that in the swash of a cloak, the tension in a muscled leg, or the tilt of a rifle that suggests the figure is already reacting to a threat. This is not generic heroism; it’s a set of characters with a clear mood and motive.

The lines avoid the hyper-sculpted caricature that sometimes plagues high-drama minis. Instead, you get a believable, grounded look that can still punch above its weight on the table. It’s the difference between a standard-issue survivor and a protagonist who looks like they could lead a march through a horde and still have the composure to light a cigarette afterward—metaphorically speaking, of course.

## Sculpt Quality: Detail, Proportion, and Practicality

A lot of the Edouard minis hinge on their readability at gaming distance. The faces are crafted to be expressive up close, but still legible from four feet away when you’re busy arguing with a gob to prevent a zombie from sprinting into your lane. The proportions stay believable, with careful attention paid to hands gripping weapons, stances that convey momentum, and a level of fabric texture that looks right for a zombie apocalypse wardrobe.

Texture work varies per model but generally maintains a crisp scale. Small accessories—like pouches, belts, and holsters—are present without crossing into “you’ll never paint that.” And yes, the hair sculpting on one of the survivors shows strands that will take contrast paints gloriously, if you’re into that kind of thing. The pricing of this line is commensurate with the level of detail; you’re paying for a product that clearly aspires to be gallery-worthy rather than a quick stand-in for a zombie encounter.

From a practical standpoint, these minis clean up well during miniature-assembly sessions. The mold lines are minimal and placed where they’ll be least obtrusive in the final piece. This means more time painting and less time cursing the recalcitrant line that refuses to disappear, which is the hobbyist’s equivalent of a holiday miracle.

## The Paint Challenge and Display Potential

If you’re a painter who loves a technical challenge, Edouard’s line provides ample opportunities to showcase technique: layered skin tones under grim lighting, subtle fabric gradients, and weathering that doesn’t look like someone dumped a can of mud on the model. The sculpt detail invites a careful primer and a deliberate color plan. The result, if you commit to it, can be truly display-worthy.

For those who want a simpler path, the line also accommodates more straightforward schemes. The silhouettes are strong enough to read at tabletop distance with a minimal base coat, a wash, and a midtone highlight—perfect for players who want to enjoy the game and still have a respectable showpiece on the shelf.

One helpful tip: given Edouard’s dramatic lines, you’ll want to prepare a small color palette that emphasizes the contrast between the character and the zombie threats. A slightly desaturated base with a few pop colors for accessories can help the figures stand out in a game night lineup.

## Tabletop Realities: Does a Designer-Driven Line Change the Game?

From a pure gameplay perspective, the minis don’t re-invent the wheel. Zombicide’s rules universe remains intact and balanced, which is precisely the design choice that makes guest miniatures both a joy and a responsibility. The new sculpts do not come with bespoke rules that would derail the system; instead, they impact feel and theme. You’ll notice more cinematic vibe when you look at the figures on the table and in cards, which can inspire more dramatic play. The addition of these new figures can shift how your group approaches scenarios—more emphasis on positioning, more dynamic hero-vs-zombie exchanges, and a tendency to stage dramatic outcomes rather than simply “kill the next 10 walkers.”

If you’re the kind of player who loves to narrate your campaigns, Edouard’s minis are an excellent prompt for storytelling. The survivor poses push you toward tactical lines of sight and risk-taking, while the more expressive zombie designs remind everyone that the undead aren’t a static obstacle but a storytelling partner in your game night.
