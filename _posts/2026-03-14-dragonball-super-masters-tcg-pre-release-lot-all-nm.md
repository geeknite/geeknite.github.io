## H3: All NM — what does it really mean in practice?

All NM (Near Mint) is a phrase you’ll see a lot in card listings. It’s not a guarantee that every single card is flawless, but it’s a signal that the cards have minimal signs of wear—the corners aren’t dinged, the packaging superb, and the surface clean enough to pass for showroom. In reality, NM can range across a spectrum: tiny whitening on edges, a couple of minor scratches on holo surfaces, or slight centering shifts that don’t win awards but don’t automatically disqualify the card either. For a Masters pre-release lot, the NM label is more meaningful when you’re evaluating value: a handful of NM cards plus foils can still deliver significant ROI (Return on Investment) if you’re stacking the deck for long-term investment or serious deck-building.

To maximize your NM experience, you’ll want to check:

- Corners: Are there tiny nicks that might be hidden by the foil’s shine? Small corner dings are often considered acceptable in NM condition.
- Edges: Any whitening or chipping along the edges? Very common in heavily foiled cards or cards that have seen a rough ride in transit. 
- Surface: Any scratches, scuffs, or print defects on holo foils? Foil cards can disguise minor surface imperfections; a good light check is essential.
- Centering: Cards at or near 60/40 or 65/35 are usually acceptable; anything far off-center could affect grader’s opinions later on.

Pro tip: take photos under bright, even lighting, and preferably in a neutral color background. If you’re stacking NM cards, you’ll want to document them in both a wide shot and macro shots of tricky edges so you don’t end up babysitting a return claim that looks like a dramatic interpretation of a “ding” compensation request.

## H2: How to authenticate, inspect, and protect your NM treasure

If you’re eyeing an All NM pre-release lot, you’ll want to implement a tiny fortress of sanity around your cards:

## H3: Authenticity and red flags

Unfortunately, not every “pre-release lot” is a mint dream. Here are red flags to watch for:

- Inconsistent set symbols or misaligned borders on the foil art. If the border looks oddly clipped, it could be a non-standard print or, worse, a counterfeit mix.
- Suspect packaging: If the lot includes unsealed packs or promo cards that seem too pristine to be real, pause. Real-world value often relies on legitimate packaging and verified promos from official channels.
- Odd distribution: If the lot promises an overly high number of “rare chase” cards and too few mid-tier commons, you should question the odds. A well-balanced lot has a reasonable spread, not a lottery-dense mountain of foils.

If you’re new to authentication, start with the obvious: compare your cards against official image galleries, read community-submitted guides, and consider a quick second opinion from a trusted local shop or a known collector. It may save you from buying a counterfeit umbrella during a rainstorm.
