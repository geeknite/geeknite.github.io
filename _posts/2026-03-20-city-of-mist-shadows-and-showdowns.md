If you’ve played City of Mist, you know the drill: you’re a person who’s also a myth, navigating a city that’s half neon crime drama, half fever dream. The rules encourage you to pick a Mask—your public-facing identity—and a Mythos—your hidden power source. The rest is improvisational jazz played on a rent-controlled bohemian stage. Shadows and Showdowns slides into that stage like a velvet curtain being tugged aside at 2 a.m.—suddenly the stakes are higher, the duels more theatrical, and the city’s shadows have personalities of their own.

## What is City of Mist, and what is this expansion trying to do?

City of Mist isn’t just a role-playing game; it’s a mood board with spandex. The core system leans into storytelling over crunchy simulations, encouraging players to lean hard into character concepts and thematic beats. Masks are the visible faces—your public identities—while Mythos and Logos become the secret engines behind your actions and the city’s reactions. Shadows and Showdowns, the newest companion piece, doubles down on two things: character-driven conflicts and the cinematic, stage-like feel of a hero’s ascent.

This expansion isn’t about adding more rules for rules’ sake. It’s about sharpening the knife you already have: more memorable Showdowns, deeper villain psychology, and a set of new Masks that feel like plot twists you can strap to your chest and wear into the night. The result is a more theatrical, more character-centric experience that still respects the game’s core ethos: you aren’t just fighting bad guys; you’re becoming the myth that your city believes you are—or fears you might become.

### A note on tone

If the base game is a velvet-draped alleyway, Shadows and Showdowns is the moment the neon finally leaks into the room and chooses a color. It’s bold, a little over-the-top, and somehow also intimate. The book doesn’t shy away from moral ambiguity or the messy repercussions of power. It invites you to choreograph a Showdown as if it were a one-act play—and invites your GM to pace that show like a conductor who loves misdirection almost as much as the audience does.

## The new masks and how they feel

One of the most exciting parts of any City of Mist expansion is the introduction of new Masks. These are not just cosmetic add-ons; they tilt the axis of what your character can be and do. Shadows and Showdowns delivers a handful of new Masks that feel both iconic and surprising. They’re designed to slot into existing archetypes, but with enough flavor to feel fresh. Here are a few highlights (without spoiling the surprise of every page):

- The Velvet Inquisitor: A Mask that thrives on interrogation as performance art. Your questions aren’t just questions; they’re scenes in which truth pretends to be a character and then sells you a better version of reality.
- The Neon Archivist: A keeper of public secrets who can pull threads of memory and data into the present tense. Expect moments when the city’s past literally glows on a wall, illuminating both plot and your own backstory.
- The Rusted Oracle: A Mask that sees prophecies in rust, smoke, and the way a streetlight flickers. It’s less about destiny and more about dangling a possibility that forces everyone else to improvise.

Each new Mask comes with a suite of custom moves, themed Flares (the game’s emotion-based prompts), and a set of two or three Showdown angles that pair especially well with the Mask’s predispositions. If you enjoy playing masks that feel intended to clash with or complement the city’s mood, you’ll find Shadows and Showdowns worth your time for these additions alone.

Feel free to mix and match with your existing Masks, because this expansion embraces the improvisational heart of City of Mist. The rules don’t punish you for trying a combo that’s a little too on-the-nose; they reward you for telling a bigger, bolder story.

## Showdowns: the real spotlight

Showdowns aren’t new to City of Mist, but this book gives them a substantial glow. A Showdown is a structured, cinematic encounter designed to be the moment where the crowd gasps, the streetlight flickers, and a character’s true power is tested on a stage built of neon and consequence. Shadows and Showdowns refines this concept into a few key ingredients:

- A clear objective with thematic stakes: what does your Mask want, what are you willing to sacrifice, and what will the city’s shadows do to push you toward that sacrifice?
- A dynamic environment: the Showdown location is more than a battlefield; it’s a stage that can bend—by design—under the pressure of the story and the players’ choices.
- Mechanically distinct options: new moves, tag interactions, and narrative prompts that encourage fast-paced, high-drama turns.

If you enjoy the sense of performance that City of Mist is meant to evoke, these Showdowns will feel like home: a place where each round is a beat in a song, and the chorus is the city reminding you of who you’re supposed to be.

### How Showdowns feed dramatic tension

The book presents Showdowns as a series of escalating acts. You begin with a setup that reveals a fault line in your relationship to the city; you step through a middle section where consequences compound; finally, you reach a resolution that reshapes your Mask and perhaps the entire mythos of your crew. The tension comes not from raw power, but from narrative pressure: the clock runs, a crowd forms, and every choice you make has a ripple that could boomerang back on you in a subsequent scene.

This is the element that helps new players into the City of Mist experience: you’re not sprinting toward a numeric victory; you’re choreographing a scene that you and the table will remember. If your group loves dramatic tesla-coils of emotion, this expansion is likely to electrify every session.

## How the mechanics feel in actual play

City of Mist uses a modular dice pool system where you combine your Masks’ aspects with narrative tags to roll D6s. You’re trying to achieve the right combination of success and spectacle, not to math your way through a chart. Shadows and Showdowns leans into this by presenting Showdown prompts that become deterministic storytelling engines rather than arbitrary obstacles. You’ll find it easier to describe a scene with platforms and walls: a rooftop windstorm becomes a character, a flickering streetlamp becomes a witness, and a cracked mirror becomes a portal for a memory.

The new Masks interact with this dynamic in predictable-yet-satisfying ways. Their special moves are designed to tilt the dice pool in meaningful directions without making the game feel out-of-control. The GM tools provided in the book are particularly handy: you’ll get quick-narrative prompts that help you stage scenes that feel both cinematic and personal. In short, the mechanics align with the book’s tone: you want to create moments that feel like a movie poster has leapt off the wall and walked into your living room.

## Practical tips for players and GMs

If you’re thinking about running Shadows and Showdowns tonight, here are some concrete tips to help you capture the vibe without drowning in a sea of autographs and neon signs:

- Embrace the stage: Treat each Showdown as a mini-scene with a beginning, middle, and end. Encourage players to describe actions in cinematic terms, not just “I roll and hope.”
- Lean into masks’ conflicts: The best Showdowns come from internal or moral tensions, not just external threats. Use the Masks’ core struggles as fuel for the scene, not as background flavor.
- Prepare a murky city map: A few hand-drawn doodles of districts, with sticky notes for flashpoints, can go a long way. The city’s texture matters as much as its villains.
- Use the environment as a character: A rain-soaked alley, a haunted subway car, a billboard that forgets to blink—these aren’t set pieces; they’re participants in the fight for narrative control.
- Don’t forget consequences: When Showdowns resolve, the city should feel changed. The mythos grows, the Logos shifts, and your Mask’s relationship to power should reflect those changes.

If you want a quick reference, this is the book that makes Showdowns feel inevitable, not optional. It’s like adding a new chorus to a dish you’re already cooking: it doesn’t replace the flavor; it intensifies it.

## How Shadows and Showdowns compares to the base game and other expansions

- Strengths over base game: sharper Showdown sequences, more clearly defined pacing for cinematic moments, and a handful of Masks that spark new character arcs. The expansion doesn’t just throw new toys in the sandbox; it redesigns some of the sandbox’s lighting to make dramatic spots stand out more.
- Strengths over other expansions: if you love theatrical storytelling, this one leans into stagecraft in a way that feels organic rather than gimmicky. It provides a practical toolkit for GMs to shape memorable scenes without turning sessions into a rulebook cosplay parade.
- Potential drawbacks: the emphasis on showmanship can marginalize players who prefer more grounded or less theatrical storytelling. If your table isn’t into the “perform and reveal” vibe, you might find some sections a bit skewed toward a specific flavor of play. That said, the core mechanics remain robust, and with a light touch, you can mine the expansion for great character moments regardless of your preferred style.

## Running the expansion at your table: an actionable plan

- Start with one Showdown per session: don’t overload the table with multi-scene Showdowns right away. Introduce the mechanic gradually, letting players discover the rhythm of staging and payoff.
- Use the new Masks to seed conflicts: pick a Mask and align the Showdown’s objective with its central tension. This guarantees that the climactic moment feels personal and relevant to the character’s journey.
- Prepare two “memory moments” per session: these are events tied to the city’s mythos that players can trigger with a single narrative cue. Memory moments are excellent for deepening themes without derailing the plot.
- Keep the city a character: sprinkle small, tactile details—interacting elements that reflect the city’s mood. A single image can bring a show to life more than a paragraph of exposition.
