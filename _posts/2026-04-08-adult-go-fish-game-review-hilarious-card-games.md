## How to Play: A Quick Start Guide

### Setup and Roles

1) Shuffle the deck and deal each player a hand of cards (typical counts are 5–7, depending on player count). 2) Place the remaining deck in the center as a draw pile. 3) Decide on any house rules you want to weave into the night (optional penalties, extra dares, or a “soft fade” rule if things get too spicy). 4) Choose a starting player. The rest of the round will proceed in clockwise fashion.

In this edition, each time you fail to make a match, you’re also likely to draw one of the “prompt” cards. Those prompts can incentivize players to perform dares, tell a quick story, or share something embarrassing—always with the shared goal of getting a roar from the table rather than a partisan argument about scoring. The house rules help tailor the experience to your group, so don’t be afraid to adjust the balance if you’re playing with newbies or with veterans who’ve seen every joke in the deck.

### Core Rules (A Quick Refresher)

- The goal: collect as many complete sets of four of a kind as you can. The person with the most complete sets at the end wins. 
- On your turn, you ask a player for cards of a specific rank. If they have any, they must give you all of that rank. If not, you draw from the pile and you must answer a prompt card, if prompted by the game’s variant rules. 
- When you collect a set, you lay it down and shout your celebratory line (which, in this edition, is usually something witty and a hair more explicit than “Got ’em!”).

The prompts are where the flavor lives. They’re not just filler; they push players to contribute stories, impressions, or silly dares. Some prompts invite you to tell a short anecdote about an embarrassing moment at work; others prompt you to perform a goofy voice or imitate a famous fictional character. The interpretive challenge adds improvisational spice that can push your night from “fun” to “legendary.”

### Variants and House Rules

No two game nights are the same, and the adult Go Fish edition is no exception. Some groups like to add an “all-in” round where players must complete a set within a stricter time limit, which intensifies the tension and the giggles. Others opt for a “no-dare” mode for more serious players who want to lean into strategy rather than improv. A few home teams prefer to swap prompts with “roast cards,” which force players to roast the last person who drew, ideally in good humor and without crossing lines. The key to a successful variant is consent and audience awareness; if someone isn’t comfortable with a prompt, they should be allowed to swap it for a tamer alternative.
