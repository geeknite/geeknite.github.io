## Introduction
Brace yourself, fellow geeks, for a raid into Wulfsburg, the fortress that supposedly hosts more wolves than a Shakespearean tragedy. Zombicide Black Plague: Wulfsburg brings a new map, new encounters, and a hero named Theo who looks like he borrowed the toque from a Viking chef. This review covers what Wulfsburg actually adds, how Theo changes the tempo of a typical round, and whether you should pick up the expansion for your next game night or just use the box as a very expensive cookie jar. Spoilers: you’ll want both.

## Box contents and first impressions
The Wulfsburg box arrives like a well-behaved child: compact, well organized, and shouting “I am here to ruin your Tuesday for a good cause.” Inside you’ll find:

- A brand-new double-sided map tile pack featuring the Wulfsburg fortress, caverns, and a wolf-filled courtyard that makes you reconsider your life choices.
- A set of wolf-themed zombie spawns and special wolves that move like greased lightning when you’re not looking.
- A new survivor: Theo. A human-shaped bundle of optimism and questionable judgement who somehow carries both a sword and a bad pun.
- New tokens, cards, and a couple of special traps (watch out for the tripwires; they do not enjoy your jokes).

Set up is straightforward if you’ve memorized the base game, which, if you haven’t, is your cue to get a coffee, inhale deeply, and pretend you’re participating in a low-stake ritual to appease the dice gods.

## Theo — The Wulfsburg Survivor
Theo is the star of this expansion, the hero who seems to have wandered off a mapping app into a medieval nightmare and decided to stay. He’s the kind of survivor who says, “I’ll be the tank for the team,” and then promptly forgets to wear armor and gets mauled by a pack of wolves (in game terms: terrible at remembering to keep his distance). But his heart is in the right place, which in Zombicide is usually enough to keep your party from guzzling each other’s brains.

### Theo’s abilities
Theo has a few tricks up his tunic:

- Howl of the Pack: Once per round, Theo can re-roll one attack die, representing the wolves howling a second wind and distracting the undead with jazz hands.
- Wolf’s Guard: If Theo is adjacent to another survivor, he can reduce the damage taken by that survivor by dipping into a hero shield token (mental note: bribe Theo with extra rations; he tends to forget his own safety).
- Feral Charge: On a successful move, Theo may close the gap by one extra space, trading safety for closer proximity to the zombies' chomping range. If he survives, you’ll hear the other players sigh in relief or chuckle at the risk.

Playing Theo requires a mix of aggression and babysitting. He shines when you create a distraction chain: the wolves rush in, Theo charges, and your other survivors manage to skeleton-walk their way to the loot tokens in the same turn.

In terms of balance, Theo is not overpowered. He’s a glass cannon with a big heart. He can deal damage, but he also has a habit of rushing into rooms full of wolves because he saw a “wolf-shaped loot token” and thought: “loot first, laugh later.” The design encourages you to think in terms of risk-versus-reward, which is exactly what you want from a Zombicide hero.

### Theo in the campaign
If you’re playing through a Wulfsburg campaign, Theo’s arc is delightful. He starts as a reckless wanderer who learns to trust his team and—spoiler alert—a few times, he learns to wield his howl to call for backup instead of charging headlong into the maw of doom. The narrative engine of the campaign uses Theo to highlight teamwork, planning, and the occasional heroic sacrifice (or at least heroic attempts to block a doorway with a laundry basket, which is what the real world calls “dressing room improvisation”).

## The Wulfsburg map and environment
Wulfsburg’s map pack is where the expansion really shines. The fortress walls are sturdy in a way that makes you believe you’re playing a historical diorama that just happened to be soaked in zombie blood. The alignment of rooms, corridors, and artillery towers is designed to encourage multi-stage planning: you lure zombies into choke points, protect your load-bearing survivors, and then kick them into the next puzzle like a bored game designer who finally got creative.

Themes are strong here: the Wulfsburg design leans into forest ambushes, wolf dens behind crumbling archways, and a courtyard that becomes a deathtrap if you don’t coordinate detours. The art direction nails the “grim, glitzy, slightly cartoonish fantasy” vibe, which is exactly what you want when you’re trying not to spill your drink while rolling hex dice.

The tiles themselves are sturdy and easy to assemble; you won’t spend half your night trying to align miscut edges. The tokens, cards, and dice are all well-labeled, making it easier for newcomers to find their bearings without resorting to a manual for every move.

If you’re a nuts-and-bolts player who wants to customize your experience, you’ll appreciate the variety of tokens: traps, loot tokens, hero tiles, and zombie spawn cards. It’s not exactly a rave of color-coded energy drinks, but it’s a well-executed design that allows you to focus on strategy rather than deciphering icons.

## Mechanics and balance — how it changes the base game
Adding Theo to your roster changes the dynamic of a standard Black Plague run in several ways:

- Aggression pacing: With a hero who can re-roll attacks and a wolf-charged map layout, you’ll likely accelerate the tempo of rounds. That means more engagement, but also more risk. It’s a heart-pounding run that tests your reflexes and your ability to say, “We’re fine, we’ve got this,” while your halved health pool breathes its last.
- Team synergy: Theo’s proximity-based defenses encourage you to cluster your survivors—careful, this also means the wolves will bite more of you at once. The expansion nudges you to coordinate: one person distracts, one runs loot, one holds the line, and Theo charges ahead with a feral grin. The result is that players who enjoy cooperative planning will feel the expansion rewarding.
- Map incentives: The Wulfsburg map includes choke points and trap opportunities. That means you’ll need to decide which doors to open, which routes to take, and where to deploy the important loot tokens. The tension is delicious; it’s the sort of design that makes you curse at the dice but also clap when you finally win a close combat.
- Difficulty curve: If you’re playing with the Wulfsburg expansion on “Normal” base game settings, expect a moderate bump in challenge. The wolves provide bite-sized threats that appear unexpectedly, similar to that roommate who shows up again unannounced with snacks that you supposedly don’t want. The key is to control the tempo; slow it down with careful planning and flush out the corridor ambushes with Theo at the right times.

## A sample solo playthrough
If you’re someone who loves to play solo to decompress from life’s chaos, Wulfsburg can still be an entertaining sandbox. Here’s a quick imagined run that captures the flavor.

Setup:
- Place the Wulfsburg map and shuffle the wolf spawn deck. Place Theo in a starting zone with two health. Add three extra zombie walkers to nearby tiles to emulate a “warm-up.”

Round 1:
- Theo uses Howl of the Pack to re-roll an attack against a zombie. The re-roll yields a critical hit, and the zombie is eliminated. The other survivors spread to cover the next corridor.

Round 2:
- A wolf pack breaks into the courtyard. Theo uses Feral Charge to close the gap and chip away at the strongest zombie on the board. The team’s archer throws a molotov-like improvised weapon to disrupt the pack; a spark of brilliant luck happens, and the survivors are still standing.

Round 3:
- The team secures a loot token behind a trap, triggering the courtyard mechanism. A misstep nearly collapses a tower, but careful planning prevents disaster. Theo stands tall, delivering a heroic line in a faux-dramatic voice: “We have wolves on the doorstep, but we have a map, and I have a plan.”

If you’re playing solo, remember: your goal is to navigate the wolves’ lair while not burning through your life pool. It’s a balancing act that requires careful decision-making. And if you fail, well, you still get to narrate how the wolves carried you off to a wolf-only post-credits scene, which is something your inner nerd will appreciate.

## Coop synergy and team composition
In a six-player cooperative format, Theo often acts as the primary front-line hero while other players provide ranged support and healing capabilities (in-game survival tokens or equivalent). Karl, the mage of the party, might stay behind to shoot and give you enough cover to push forward. The point is that Theo alone won’t carry you; he is part of a team machine. The expansion rewards players who communicate, coordinate, and decide as a group which corridor to clear next. It’s less about “I roll a big die and win” and more about “We roll a lot of medium dice and still win.” Since Zombicide is a dice game, it’s refreshing to see a hero who embodies the “slow and steady wins the massive zombie crowd” philosophy.

## Thematic notes and humor
The Wulfsburg box is a little silly in the best possible way. The wolves are ferocious yet comical when they miss their bite by inches. The fortress is a character in itself, with architectural flourishes that are just fancy enough to look expensive but not so fancy that you worry about breaking a rib if you lean on a table. The Theo character art evokes that “he’s seen some stuff” look that nerds love to adopt after a long night of table-turning decisions.

Humor in the game comes from the interplay of hero quotes and the inevitable pun-laden moment when a survivor mutters, “That’s a wolves-ome thing to happen.” The Wulfsburg expansion doesn’t push jokes into the front seat; it just lets them ride shotgun while you’re busy trying not to get eaten by a Viking werewolf. The tone remains light-hearted enough to keep the session fun, but gritty enough to feel survivalist in a fantastical world.

## Replayability and campaign potential
Wulfsburg adds a lot of value for players who want to experience more hours of Zombicide Black Plague. The map’s layout provides multiple routes, and Theo’s abilities offer a few different playstyles. The campaign mode gives you a sense of progression and a moral to the story (even if the moral is often “don’t trust the wolves with your loot tokens”). The campaign structure encourages replays to explore alternative routes, different orders of operations, and the joy of discovering new wolf-related tragedy each time you return to the fortress.

## Pros and cons
Pros:
- Theo is a compelling and thematic survivor with useful abilities that reward cooperative play.
- The Wulfsburg map design encourages tactical planning and multi-room engagement.
- High-quality minis and durable map tiles that withstand countless re-entries into the zombie apocalypse.
- Strong thematic cohesion with a nice balance of danger, humor, and strategic choice.

Cons:
- The wolves can feel punishing if you don’t manage the pace, potentially leading to a few frustrating rounds.
- For players who dislike heavy setup, the expansion adds a bunch of components that require some time to get used to.
- Theo’s always-on push mode can get repetitive if you don’t vary your strategies.

## Final verdict and rating
Wulfsburg with Theo is a standout addition to Zombicide Black Plague, providing a cohesive theme, challenging encounters, and a charismatic hero who makes you laugh even as your heart rate climbs. If you’re a fan of cooperative board games, you’ll find the expansion to be a welcome challenge that pushes you to think tactically and work as a team. It’s not flawless—no game is—but the momentum and atmosphere more than compensate for occasional rough patches.

Rating: 9/10. The expansion is worth it for the new tactical playground, the Wolf Pack presence, and the delight of playing as a hero who uses a howl as a battle plan.
