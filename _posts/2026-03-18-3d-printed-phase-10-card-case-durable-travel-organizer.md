## Design and specs: what you actually get
The typical Phase 10 card case design uses a couple of core principles that matter on the road: fit, access, and durability. Here’s what you’ll likely encounter in a well-rounded model.

### Interior layout and capacity
Most designs feature:
- A main card cavity or a tray that holds a full Phase 10 deck in sleeve-free or sleeve-heavy form depending on your preference.
- Optional dividers to separate phases, score sheets, and tokens.
- A lid that either snaps or friction-fits closed, depending on how you slice the tolerances in your slicer file.
- A small index or notch to help you grab the top card without pulling out three other cards in the process.

If you’re into modularity, some designs offer a stackable approach: lower tray for the deck, upper tray for tokens, a separate slot for the instruction manual. The advantage here is clear: you can remove modules when playing with a different game or when space is at a premium.

### Dimensions and fit
A typical deck with sleeves measures about 2.6 x 3.6 inches; with sleeves, you’ll want a little extra room. A common safe approach is a case around 3.0 x 4.0 inches with a depth of about 0.8 to 0.9 inches for the deck, leaving a margin for the lid. If you’re printing in PETG for extra durability, remember that PETG tolerances can be slightly looser than PLA. If you’re using PLA, a 0.2 mm layer height and 20-25% infill tends to give you a sturdy shell without turning your part into a brittle popsicle when dropped on hotel carpet.

### Materials and finish: PLA, PETG, or ABS
- PLA: Easy to print, cheap, but a bit brittle under direct sunlight and heat. Great for indoor travel, not ideal for hot car trunks or picnic tables in the sun.
- PETG: A better balance of toughness and heat resistance; it resists yellowing and is more forgiving about minor print defects.
- ABS: Strong and durable but fumes are not kid-friendly; also more challenging to print cleanly. If you go ABS, ensure proper ventilation and a heated enclosure.

In Geeknite fashion, we endorse PETG for most travelers who want a robust, flexible case that lasts beyond a couple of trips. If you are a PLA-only printer wizard, you can still pull it off with careful print settings and a protective travel bag.

### Wall thickness and tolerances
Plan for at least 1.6 to 2.0 mm walls on all sides for a durable shell. A common approach is a 0.8–1.0 mm single-wall thickness on the lid if you go for a cap-style closure, but most designers double that for a sturdy feel and to reduce the risk of warping during cooling. The interior dividers can be as thin as 0.8–1.0 mm depending on the tracer path; the trick is to avoid overly aggressive tolerances that trap cards while still allowing a smooth slide in and out.

### The lid mechanism: friction vs catch
Two main paths: friction-fit lids or integrated latch systems. Friction-fit lids rely on slightly oversized lids or snug walls that press down on the edges of the deck. Latch designs may incorporate tiny features like a micro-ridge or a small tab that you press to release the lid. Friction-fit is easier for beginners, while latch designs give you a more secure hold—great for travel through bumpy airports. If you print a latch, consider adding a small flexible tab to avoid card-drag.

### Aesthetics and finish
We all love a flashy print, but travel cases aren’t fashion runways; they are practical tools. You can add a light smoothing with sanding or a simple acetone vapor bath for PETG/ABS if you’re feeling extra. Aesthetics-wise, you can color-code the deck slots by color family (red, blue, green, yellow) for quick recognition during a game, or go with a monochrome look to blend in with your luggage. Either way, a little texture can hide minor printing flaws and make the case feel less like a toy.


### Material and print settings: a practical starter guide
- Printer: Any FDM printer with a 150–200 mm build area will cover a standard case with decent margins.
- Layer height: 0.2 mm for a good balance of speed and detail.
- Infill: 20–25% is plenty for a deck box; bump to 40% if you want extra rigidity for long-term travel.
- Bed adhesion: brims or skirts to reduce warping, especially with ABS or PETG.
- Supports: not usually needed for simple lid designs; if you have internal cavities for dividers, consider removable supports.

### Printing tips for a quick, reliable build
- Start with a 0.2 mm layer height; it’s fast and keeps the fit tight without pulling too many strings on the edges.
- Print orientation matters. If you print with the lid on the same piece as the base, ensure your slicer handles bridging or supports as needed. If you can print the lid separately and snap it on later, you’ll reduce risk of print failure due to overhangs.
- Use a brim for adhesion if you’ve got a small footprint on the bed. Warping around the edges can ruin a perfect fit.
- Post-processing can make a big difference. A quick light sanding on the lid edge improves sliding action, while a touch of acetone vapor can smooth PETG for a slick feel. Be mindful of fumes and safety when you do any chemical smoothing.

### Maintenance: keeping your case cute and functional
- Regular wipe-down with a soft cloth to remove dust and oils; a damp microfiber cloth usually does the trick.
- If you have PETG or PLA with slight surface wear, a light sanding and re-spray of a clear protective sealant can rejuvenate the finish and improve glide for the lid.
- Store the case in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight when not in use. This keeps color from fading and reduces the risk of your lid getting brittle with age.

### Quick start checklist
- Measure your deck and ensure sleeve compatibility; plan for extra tolerance. 
- Choose your material: PETG for durability, PLA for quick prints and experimentation, ABS for heat resistance if you have the setup. 
- Decide on lid mechanism: friction-fit for easier printing, magnet or latch for a more secure closure. 
- Print a small test module first: a tiny corner piece and a recessed lip so you can evaluate fit before committing to the whole deck tray. 
- Test fit with your actual deck: ensure you can insert and remove cards without bending or snagging. 
- Finish and assemble: lightly sand the lid edge if needed, wipe with a light alcohol solution to remove dust, and snap the pieces together to confirm a smooth action.

## Final recommendation and where to start
- If you’re new to 3D printing, start with a PLA-based model and a simple lid to learn tolerances. Once you’re comfortable, switch to PETG for a longer-lasting build.
- If you’re a seasoned printer, grab a robust PETG design, tweak the interior dividers to your liking, and consider a magnetic latch for a premium feel.
- If you’re low on time and still want a credible travel organizer, consider a pre-printed version from a reputable maker and use it as a baseline to iteratively improve your own design.
- If you’re not sure about your slicer’s tolerance, print a calibration cube and a few test slots before diving into the full deck case. You’ll thank yourself later when you don’t waste filament printing something that doesn’t fit your cards.
