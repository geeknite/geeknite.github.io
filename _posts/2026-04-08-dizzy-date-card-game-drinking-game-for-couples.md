### How to Play: The Quick & Dirty (But Delightfully Silly) Guide
Dizzy Date is designed to be accessible. The core loop is simple: draw a card, complete the instruction, drink (if you’re choosing to), and pass the turn. That’s it in training-wheel form; the real fun emerges when you start layering the goofy constraints on top. Here’s a compact outline to get you through Round 1 without needing a referee:

- Setup: Each player sits opposite, ideally with enough space to perform tasks without stepping on a cat or a stray LEGO. Place the card deck in the center, within easy reach of both players.
- Turn sequence: The active player draws the top card and reads it out loud. The card’s instruction falls into one of three broad buckets: Task, Question, or Dare.
- Resolutions: Depending on the card, you either answer honestly, perform a goofy action, or answer with a creative spin (which may involve an accent, a props, or a dramatic reading of a grocery list).
- Drinking rules: If you’re playing with drinks, the card often triggers a sip, a gulp, or a small chug. If you’re choosing to skip the drinking component, simply assign a “Dizzy Points” penalty and continue. The game is forgiving—your relationship’s baseline tolerance for chaos is the real factor here.
- End condition: The deck runs through a fixed amount of cards, or you can set a time window (e.g., 30 minutes). The winner is whoever racks up the most Dizzy Points, or more romantically, the person who can still tell a coherent bedtime story after Card #83.

For a brand-new couple, Dizzy Date offers gentle introductions to vulnerability through humor. For veterans of the relationship-wonk-and-wobble, it provides a shared vocabulary of jokes and awkward moments that suddenly explain why you’ve been hiding the microwave for years. The game does a surprisingly good job of staying light while nudging you into real conversation—just enough pressure to be interesting without becoming a therapy session you didn’t sign up for.

### Drinking Rules: Safety, Moderation, and Maximum Laughs
If you want to make this a drinking game, set some clear rules before flipping the first card. Here are some practical guidelines we’ve found helpful in Geeknite-land:

- Pace yourself: It’s a game, not a marathon. If either player wants to slow down, allow a non-alcoholic option. The point is the laughter, not a hangover with character development.
- Hydration is cute and sensible: Have water handy and a quick “hydrate break” every three rounds.
- Penalties exist, but always with consent: Sips can be minimized or replaced with a mini-forfeit—like telling a five-second compliment to your partner or performing a silly pose for a photo.
- Safety overrides everything: If a prompt makes you uncomfortable, flip a card face-down and opt for a neutral prompt or a swap to a different type of card. The goal is connection, not drama club.

The game’s structure encourages lighthearted risk-taking, but the rules you set at the start will define the line between charming chaos and wreck-your-night territory. We recommend a mutual “one and done” cap on the number of alcohol-based actions per game to prevent the kind of moments you’ll be telling your grandkids about with a slightly worried smile.

### House Rules to Elevate Your Dizzy Date Night
Two or three little tweaks can transform a standard run into a cracking-good date-night experience. Here are some of our favorite house rules that don’t require a lawyer or a parachute:

- The Compliment Cascade: Whenever a card asks for vulnerability or honesty, both players must compliment the other’s choice on the spot. Extra points for sincerity and a wink that doesn’t feel rehearsed.
- Props Optional: Allow one prop per round (a hat, a sock, a spatula). Props add a visual element that amplifies the silliness without breaking the bank.
- Time-boxed stories: If a card asks for a memory or story, set a two-minute timer. The discipline creates energy and keeps the chaos from spiraling into a monologue about the time you burned toast in college.
- Theme Weekends: Play with a theme (80s Night, Film Character Night, or Sci-Fi Date Day). The prompts take on new character voices and you’ll be surprised how much you LOL when you’re trying to be Lando Calrissian while answering a question about your first date.

House rules keep things fresh and ensure every session feels like a new mini-adventure rather than a stale rerun. The best part? These aren’t “rules” you memorize; they’re social swag you sprinkle on the experience.
