## Gloom The Card Game Lot of 2 Unhappy Homes and Unwelcome Guests

If you come to Gloom for sunshine, you might want to grab a raincoat. Because this card game is all about stacking misfortune on fictional families until the mood of the table becomes thick with melodrama and bad puns. When you open this Lot of 2 Unhappy Homes and Unwelcome Guests you are basically boarding a tiny gloom scented circus where the acts include misfortune, murder, and the occasional polite nap. The two expansions that come bundled here do two very different things: Unhappy Homes adds more domestic drama to the gloom, while Unwelcome Guests introduces a merry band of interlopers whose arrival makes every round feel like a surprise party where the cake is spiritually poisoned. In short, if you want your players to voice their regrets about life choices while making up excuses for their cookie recipes, this is the set for you.

![Gloom Unhappy Homes](/assets/images/gloom-unhappy-homes.jpg)
![Gloom Unwelcome Guests](/assets/images/gloom-unwelcome-guests.jpg)

Gloom The Card Game is built to support storytelling and light card manipulation rather than heavy strategic planning. The base game is already a theatre of family misfortune, and these expansions crank the drama dial up to eleven while keeping the humor intact. The mood is in the vibe more than in dice or heavy rules. So if your tabletop crew enjoys storytelling, theatrical misfortune, and puns that reference etiquette and social graces, you will feel right at home.

### Unhappy Homes

Unhappy Homes focuses on the tension inside a family unit that is not quite meeting expectations. It introduces new families and house cards that you place under or over other cards to indicate that the family is being subjected to a cascade of unfortunate events. The art style remains playful but with a slightly darker tilt that suits the theme. The new cards are more about domestic disasters than grand epics, which keeps the gameplay accessible but still loaded with character. This expansion blends nicely with the base game because it keeps the inside jokes in play while expanding the gloom you can dish out to opponents.

The new cards include families with names that evoke everyday disappointment such as stalled renovations, missed bus routes, or the neighbor with an opinion on your lawn. The interactions are designed to be slightly more forgiving for casual players, so you can enjoy the humor even if your memory for rules is slightly lacking. In addition to new families, Unhappy Homes adds more story hooks and event cards that seed the table with mini plots that take a couple of turns to resolve. Thematic depth is the name of the game here; the more you play, the more you discover quirky narratives about the trials of everyday life that you can weave into the round.

### Unwelcome Guests

If Unhappy Homes is the domestic tragedy family therapy session, Unwelcome Guest is the wacky house party you did not RSVP to but somehow ended up hosting. This expansion adds guest cards that crash the gloom party and change who is drawing cards, who is forced to endure misfortune, and how quickly the table can shift from friendly competition to cutthroat pity. The guests are a carnival of personality: loud neighbors, silent aesthetes, cosmic pranksters, and that aunt who reveals questionable family secrets during the dessert course. They cause temporary effects that can swing the balance in surprising ways, which keeps the rounds fresh and unpredictable.

The structural addition here is that guests interact with gloom in more dynamic ways than typical negative events. Some guest effects force players to discard or redraw hands, creating moments when you realize that your best plan will be undone by a cunning party crasher. The guests encourage more interaction and more narrative possibilities; your table will likely invent a rapid series of micro plots as you race to accumulate gloom for your own family while preventing others from achieving their own greedy ends.

### Components and quality

The two expansions share the same quality of the base game: durable cards with charming art that sits nicely on the table and with a matte finish that resists glare under typical dining room lighting. The card stock is sturdy enough to survive multiple play sessions, but you may want to sleeve them if your group is rough with cards or if you want to keep the art pristine for longer. The artwork remains a highlight of Gloom; it gives each character a distinct personality that you quickly recognize which is essential for the storytelling vibe. The new art style matches the tone of the base game and enhances the humor rather than pushing it too far into cartoonish territory.

In the end the physical components are what you expect from a quality indie card game: good readable typography, a color palette that emphasizes gloom without injuring your eyes, and icons that are easy to parse after a couple of rounds of discipline and despair.

### Setup and learning curve

One of the biggest perks of Gloom is how quickly you can get a session going. The Lot of 2 does not require you to tear apart your existing base game; you simply add the new cards to the appropriate decks and shuffle. If you want to keep things simple, you can introduce Unhappy Homes as an optional module for your first session, then add Unwelcome Guests as a later upgrade once your group has internalized the core mechanics. The recommended player count for standard Gloom is two to four players, and the expansions scale well to that; more players means more opportunities for dramatic misfortune and more chances to accuse the neighbor of petty crimes.

The queue for the round is straightforward: each player draws a hand, you play events and family cards that create gloom on that player’s families, you resolve interactions, and then you pass the turn to the next player. The real fun comes from the timing of gloom events and the way you weave story lines around the cards. The expansions give you more ways to string together those little stories, which is the bread and butter of a memorable Gloom session.

### Mechanics and synergy

Gloom is a game about stacking misery on your own families while ensuring your opponents suffer less. The base mechanism involves playing cards that affect gloom values and that can be attached to family cards to adjust their cycles of misfortune. With Unhappy Homes and Unwelcome Guests in the mix, you gain new families, new events, and new modifiers that can shape the way you calibrate your gloom to achieve the best worst outcome.

Unhappy Homes adds more domestic complications, which means more opportunities to narrate a mini tragedy about a living room that never quite functions as it should. You might shuffle a new event that makes a couple of cards more fragile or a family that starts with a pre compromised gloom value. It creates opportunities for dramatic misfortune without adding complexity that overwhelms new players. The interplay with base cards remains intuitive; the new cards function as new voice actors in the same theatre, delivering lines that feel familiar yet fresh.

Unwelcome Guests introduces a dynamic that often catches players by surprise: guests who do not simply add gloom but change the rhythm of a round. Some guest effects force players to discard or redraw hands, creating moments when you realize that your best plan will be undone by a cunning party crasher. The guests encourage more interaction and more narrative possibilities; your table will likely invent a rapid series of micro plots as you race to accumulate gloom for your own family while preventing others from achieving their own greedy ends.

### Storytelling weight and humor

A core strength of Gloom is the way it encourages players to weave mini stories around the cards. The two expansions amplify that storytelling feel without sacrificing readability. The humor is in the collisions of gloom, the puns on family life, and the dramatic irony that comes from watching other players sabotage their own characters while praising yours for their strategic cunning. You will find yourselves cracking up at the most melodramatic line of a card or lamenting the fate of a family that was almost in reach of the final doom only to be snatched away by a guest with impeccable timing.

### Artwork and theming

The art direction in both expansions continues the Gloom tradition of combining vintage postcard charm with a touch of gothic whimsy. The visuals strike a nice balance between humor and gloom which is essential for a game that promises both melodrama and fun. The designs make the cards easy to identify at a glance even when you are juggling multiple families and events at the table. The contrast between light and shadow in the art mirrors the emotional tone of the game; you are always a step away from a bright moment of happiness and several steps away from a truly catastrophic tragedy.

### Player experience and flow

In terms of flow the Lot of 2 keeps the pace moving by providing additional card types and interactions that do not require a heavy rethinking of the base mechanics. For players who enjoy long multi act evenings with heavy strategy there are deeper combos to explore. For players who prefer shorter warm up sessions with lots of storytelling and laughter there are plenty of short punchy moments that are easy to slot into a game night.

If your group uses a timer to keep things moving you will be glad that the expansions do not introduce a significant time sink. The rounds remain snappy and the chances to derail someone else with a well timed event card are still high. The social energy at the table is a big part of the experience; the expansions give you more opportunities to talk banter bluff and groan about the gloomy outcomes.

### Value and replayability

The bundled set of two expansions offers a strong value proposition, particularly if you are already a fan of the base game. If you have not yet dipped your toes into the gloom deck you can still air on the side of caution and try the base game first, then bring in the two expansions as a double feature. The replayability factor is high because the new cards introduce enough variation to keep sessions fresh, while the core mechanics remain stable and reliable. The sheer number of new cards means there are more combinations to explore, which translates to more stories and more opportunities to craft your own little tragedy.

### Strategy tips and tricks

- Start with a plan to spread misfortune across your own families while keeping an eye on the reactions of your opponents. The best moment is when you deliver a devastating combo for your own family but make sure that the other players have already sacrificed their own.
- Use Unhappy Homes to seed family dynamics that will yield better gloom later on. The houses you place can create a chain of events that will pay dividends in future turns.
- When you see a guest card that can disrupt someone who seemed to be nearing the end of a round, do not be shy about triggering it. Sometimes the best defense is a clever offense that disrupts the path to victory.
- Pairing with Unwelcome Guests means you can focus your attention on your own gloom while still reacting to the unpredictable moves of guests. The guests can create opportunities to steal the late game from players who are already rolling.
- Manage your hand discipline you want to avoid over committing to a single strategy too early. The best rounds come from balancing a few small careful misfortunes rather than a single flashy effect that could backfire.

### Comparisons and alternatives

If you are deciding whether to buy the Lot of 2 or to pick up a single expansion at a time consider your group dynamics and the level of chaos you enjoy. Unhappy Homes tends to lean into domestic storytelling and is a great entry point that does not overwhelm new players with too many moving parts. Unwelcome Guests on the other hand adds new social dynamics that intensify interaction and unpredictability. If you have played the base game many times and crave new twists that feel like a fresh scene rather than a clone of existing mechanics you will likely prefer to go with the Lot of 2 since it gives you the best of both worlds.

If you want to explore similar games you might also enjoy other light storytelling card games like Dixit or Mysterium but be aware that those games focus more on narrative cues than on the gloom engine for misfortune. ### Conclusion and final verdict

In the end Gloom The Card Game Lot of 2 Unhappy Homes and Unwelcome Guests is a strong addition to any Gloom fans collection. It preserves the core darkly witty vibe of the base game while expanding the narrative possibilities and the structural options you can use to modulate the evening. If you want to take your gloom to the next level without turning your table into a math nightmare, these expansions deliver the goods: more cards, more opportunities, more dramatic stories, and more reasons to talk over each other in a conspiratorial hush as you cheer for the misfortune of your rivals.

#### Pros
