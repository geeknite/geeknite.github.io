# What’s in the Box and What You’re Getting

Zombie Kittens is a compact, sealed package that feels like a portal to a very silly after-hours world. The contents are designed to be approachable for casual players while still offering enough quirky twists to justify repeat plays. Here’s what you’ll typically find inside a new-and-sealed copy:

- A deck of zombie-kitten themed cards with bold, cartoonish art. The art direction leans into neon colors, oversized fangs, and a surprising amount of glitter for a zombie apocalypse aesthetic. If you’ve bought any Exploding Kittens product, you’ll feel right at home with the visual language.
- A handful of special action cards that shuffle the tempo of the game. These range from “Nibble Attack” style moves that force other players to draw, to “Brain Freeze” style defenses that let you shrug off a bad draw for a turn.
- Score markers or tokens to track who’s still in the graveyard-steeple of luck—or lack thereof. The tokens are nice enough to look at; they won’t win any design awards, but they’re sturdy and do the job.
- A rules pamphlet that boils down the basics to a few quick pages. The beauty of this package is that you can teach a group of strangers or a party full of deranged relatives in under five minutes. The longer you stall to tell your best “zombie cat” joke, the more the game glides by.

If you’re a completionist who loves box insert tricks, you’ll notice there’s a pleasing little sense of order to the card types, and the color-coding helps you skim the board at a glance. This is not a game where you’ll spend 20 minutes sorting through a sea of tiny cardboard components. It’s a game that wants you to start playing, and fast.

# Rules Overview: How the Cataclysm Unfolds

If you’ve played Exploding Kittens, Zombie Kittens feels like the more mischievous cousin that never forgives you for petting the wrong cat while you were told not to.

- Objective: Survive until you’re the last kitten standing—or, more thematically, survive the zombie apocalypse long enough to avoid being turned into a snack for a brain-eating publisher of terrible puns.
- Setup: Shuffle the deck, deal a hand to each player (the number varies slightly depending on the exact edition, but it’s designed to be quick), place a few zombie-kitten cards face-up to indicate the “undead threat” level, and prepare to stare down the metaphorical abyss.
- Turn flow: On your turn, you draw one card and may play one or more action cards (depending on the card text). Action cards alter who draws next, who gets to peek at the future, or who’s forced to reveal their hand. Think of it as a mischief deck where every card is a tiny, gleeful weapon against the person you’re currently sitting next to.
- Core tension: Avoid drawing the wrong card at the wrong time. The deck tends to accelerate as players use more action cards, which creates a delightful push-pull between “I’ve got a trick up my sleeve” and “I really don’t want to deal with the next bad draw.” The mood can swing from ridiculous to tense to ridiculous again in the space of a single turn.
- Endgame feel: As the zombie threat escalates, the table becomes a chorus of curses, groans, and squeals of delight. The last few rounds are where the chaos truly shines—players scrambling to misdirect their opponents while a few “one more card” effects delay the inevitable doom of someone who really miscalculated their life choices.

The rules are clean and approachable, but there’s a nice depth that reveals itself after a couple of plays. You’ll catch subtle hints in the card text that reward careful memory, reading the room, and exploiting a momentary lull. It’s light strategy with heavy helping of luck, which makes it perfect for casual gamers who still want a shred of control and for those who enjoy dramatic table talk—the kind of thing that turns a simple night into a stories-and-mweasels kind of evening.

# The Gameplay Experience: Tone, Humor, and Social Chemistry

Zombie Kittens leans into the comedic horror vibe with gusto. The jokes land not with a loud slam but with a satisfying, know-your-audience wink. It’s the sort of game where the art and the text do most of the heavy lifting, letting players riff off each other’s reactions. The humor lands best when the room is warmed up—early game jokes about “undead purrs” or “brains in your snack drawer” tend to land best after a few cards have been played and the laughter level is just under the boil.

That said, the humor isn’t for everyone. Some players prefer a more serious or thematic experience, and Zombie Kittens swings toward the goofy side of the spectrum. If your game group includes folks who have a strict rule about “no undead animals” jokes, you might want to skip this one. For everyone else, you’re likely to hear a chorus of groans and snorts as someone draws a card that changes the entire table’s fate in a matter of seconds.

The pace is micro-shuffled into snappy rounds. On average, you’ll blitz through a 15- to 25-minute game session with 2-5 players, depending on how often players hijack turns with chain-reaction effects. The session length is short enough to be an easy “one more round” proposition, but long enough to feel like you’ve accomplished something if you’ve managed to bluff, bait, and survive long enough to hold on to your hand without giving into a wild card eruption.

# Strategy Tips: How to Outsmart the Zombie Herd

While luck plays a big role in any card game, there are a few practical tips that can help you survive the long night:

- Remember the tempo: If you find yourself in a stretch of bad draws, hold off on playing extra action cards unless you’re certain they’ll disrupt the lead player’s momentum. In other words, don’t blow your big trick in the first round unless you’re chasing a dramatic swing.
- Pay attention to opponents’ expressions: A classic party-game trick is reading the table. If someone looks suspiciously calm after drawing a card, that’s often the signal you should watch out for their counter-moves in the next round.
- Balance risk and reward: Some action cards help you steal advantage but at the cost of tipping off the entire table that you’re a threat. Use these to shift the game’s odds, but don’t be predictable. Predictability is the enemy of chaos.
- Concentrate on the endgame: As the zombie threat grows, the game becomes a game of “who can resist the temptation to draw that last card.” If you’re ahead, you may want to adopt a conservative approach; if you’re behind, try a bold play that forces your opponents into a mistake.
- Social play matters: The social layer is often more important than the card mechanics. The people you’re playing with will influence how much you laugh, how long you can stay engrossed, and how aggressively you lean into “the bit.” If you’re playing with strangers, consider setting some ground rules for banter to keep things friendly.

A few practical in-game tactics—like reading the room, timing your big plays, and not sweating the occasional bad hand—will come with repetition. The real thrill comes from using the deck’s chaos to create moments that become stories you’ll tell at future game nights.

# How to Play It: Quick Teaching Notes for Hosts

If you’re a host who wants to maximize fun with the least pre-game friction, here are a few tips to get everyone in the mood fast:

- Emphasize the social banter: Encourage players to comment on each other’s choices. The social energy matters more than strict accuracy.
