## How Phase 10 Works (A Quick Refresher)

### The Basic Flow

Phase 10 is a rummy-inspired card game where players try to complete ten phases in order. Each phase tasks you with collecting specific combinations of cards — typically a mix of sets (same number, different colors) and runs (consecutive numbers in any color). The twist is that you must complete a different phase on each of your hands. If you complete Phase 1, you move to Phase 2, and so on. The first person to complete all ten phases wins, but the true victory is surviving the onslaught of phase chaos that your friends unleash with a smug grin and a well-timed Wild card.

### Phases 1 Through 10

- Phase 1: 2 sets of 3 (three of a kind) or a different combination depending on the version you own
- Phase 2: 1 set of 3 + 1 run of 4
- Phase 3: 1 set of 4 + 1 run of 4
- Phase 4: 1 run of 7
- Phase 5: 1 run of 8
- Phase 6: 1 run of 9
- Phase 7: 5 sets of 1 (a colorful buffet of pairs, triples, and more)
- Phase 8: 1 run of 4 + 1 run of 4
- Phase 9: 2 sets of 5
- Phase 10: 1 set of 5 + 1 set of 2

The rules about Wild and Skip cards vary slightly by edition, so make sure your group is aligned before you shuffle. The core idea remains simple: you race through the ten phases as best you can while making life miserable for your opponents by stealing their phase opportunities with Skip cards and using Wild cards opportunistically.

### Turn Structure

On a typical turn, a player draws one card from the deck or the discard pile, then discards one card. If a player can lay down a valid phase on their turn, they do so and advance to the next phase. If not, they wait, hoping for a miracle in the next draw. The pressure builds as more players drop phases, and the table becomes a scoreboard of near-misses and triumphant returns. You will find yourself calculating probabilities as if you are studying for a math finals you forgot to study for in the first place. Welcome to Phase 10 therapy, where the deck becomes your therapist and your friends are your chaotic life coaches.

### Scoring (In Theory vs In Practice)

The scoring rules of Phase 10 are straightforward on paper: players score points for each phase and for the cards left in their hand at the end of a round. The Score Pad makes it easier to track phase completion and total scores across rounds. In practice, you might still find yourself debating whether a wild card should count toward a run or a set, and whether a particular set qualifies as a valid Phase in your house rules. The score pad helps you avoid the kind of algebraic anxiety that comes from scribbling numbers on a napkin while Chaos Theory unfolds on the other side of the table.

## How to Play With the Score Pad: Step-by-Step Quick Start

### Step 1: Set Up the Table

- Shuffle the Phase 10 deck and deal 10 cards to each player (or as your rules specify).
- Place the rest of the deck face down in the center and flip the top card to start the discard pile.
- Hand out the Score Pad and a pencil to each player. Decide on the order of play and who keeps score first. If you have a guest who loves scribbling wildly, consider giving them extra space on the pad to avoid chaos.

### Step 2: Understand the Ten Phases

- Each phase requires players to primarily collect sets and runs in order.
- You must complete the current phase before moving to the next one. If you fail on your turn, you can draw and try again on the next turn, while others progress through their own phases.
- Wild and Skip cards exist to spice things up. Plan how to deploy them when the table is most likely to lean into the dramatic chaos that Phase 10 is famous for.

### Step 3: Track with the Score Pad

- Use the Score Pad to record who completed which phase on each round, and total scores after each round.
- If you are using a house rule for end-game triggers or bonuses for completing a phase early, jot those notes on the pad for clarity.

### Step 4: End Game and Celebrate

- The game ends when a player completes Phase 10 and has the lowest total score after the final round. If there are ties, you can break them with a sudden death round or a friendly tiebreaker like rock-paper-scissors with a card draw twist.
- Celebrate with high fives, snacks, and the quiet satisfaction of being the only person who can claim Phase 10 glory on a Tuesday night.
