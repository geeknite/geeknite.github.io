## What is Sushi Go Card Game?
A quick primer: Sushi Go is a fast, light-hearted card drafting game by Gamewright based on the concept of building a menu of sushi combinations to score points. Each round, players simultaneously pick one card from their hand and pass the rest to the neighbor. After a handful of rounds, points are tallied from nigiri, sashimi, dumplings, maki rolls, and other sushi-themed combos.

While the original Sushi Go! is already well known, the card game version is even more portable and quick, perfect for airports, hotel rooms, and the I-just-woke-up-and-I-need-coffee gaming sessions.

## How to play in under 10 minutes
Setup is breezy: shuffle, deal, and reveal the first card to the center for the board's reference. On your turn, you pick one card from your hand and pass the rest to the left. The next player does the same, and so on, until all cards are exhausted. The rounds wrap up and you tally points for the combos you assembled.

The scoring rules are simple enough to learn on the first pass: keep the dumplings, aim for the sashimi if it shows, collect the maki with the most in the round, etc. The rules emphasize memory and risk assessment: do you chase a risky sashimi line or pivot to a safe dumpling soup?

## Strategy and tips
Greed vs. value: The game rewards a mix of greedy improvisation and careful calculation. Here are some Geeknite tips to level up your play:
- Watch the table: See what your neighbors are chasing and pivot your picks to block them if possible, but be mindful of the cost.
- Build one or two reliable combos: For example, aiming for sashimi sets or maki stacks yields stable point floors.
- Diversify your hand: Don’t chase just one combo; variance reduces the risk of being left with junk cards at the end.
- Timing matters: In travel games, you sometimes have to rely on tempo rather than brute force. A well-timed pick can deny someone a perfect run and swing the entire table.
- Know when to bail: If you see the end of a round approaching and your plan is failing, shift to a lower-variance strategy to minimize negative points.

## 2-5 players dynamics
With 2 players, you’ll find faster hands and quicker cycles; in 4 or 5 players, the passing becomes a little more chaotic and the board more crowded. The pick-and-pass mechanic shines with more players because the board becomes a rhythm: you pick, you pass, you read minds, you pass again. The social dynamic shifts: players talk less or more, depending on whether they’re winning or losing and how hungry the sushi looks on the table.
