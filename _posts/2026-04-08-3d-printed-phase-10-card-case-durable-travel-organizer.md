## Design goals
The design goals for a Phase 10 card case are simple, yet deceptively complicated:
- Capacity: handle a full Phase 10 deck (108 cards) plus the extra wilds, plus space for a few spare tokens.
- Organization: a way to separate phases and store discard piles, result trackers, and rule sheets without turning the case into a Jenga tower.
- Portability: lock it closed for travel, but easy to open during a chaotic airport gate chaos moment.
- Durability: the print should survive a few drops, a curious child, and the occasional pet, without turning into a pile of cardboard confetti.

### Capacity and organization
The baseline approach is to split the case into three main compartments: a card tray for the deck, a second tray for phase-chips, and a lid that doubles as a tiny writing surface for notetaking while you pretend to be a cool gamer. The card tray typically features 9 slots for 12 cards each; that’s a snug fit for 108 cards when printed at a moderate layer height and a robust infill. The key is tolerance: you want a snug hold—no rattling—and enough clearance that cards slide in without requiring Tony Stark-level precision.

For Phase 10, where you rarely need to separate black from red, a simple quad-compartment approach can also work: four little trays, one for each set of 25 cards plus a bit, with a center divider for the wilds and the 'Penalty' cards. If you want to be fancy, add a tiny ‘phase of doom’ indicator on the lid that slides into place when you’re about to hit the last three phases in a race to 500 points.

### Materials and printing
PLA is the easy start: cheap, printable, and decent for light travel duty. PETG offers better durability and a little more heat resistance if you’re going to leave the case in a hot car seat while you search for a spare D20. ABS is sturdy, but prints are trickier and more prone to warping, which yells at you from the print bed every morning.

- Typical print settings: 0.2 mm layer height for a balance of speed and strength, 20%–30% infill for the wall panels, and 2–3 perimeters for rigidity.
- Tolerances matter: aim for a tolerance of around 0.2–0.3 mm between moving parts (like the lid and the base) so the lid snaps cleanly without becoming a model of a broken jaw.
- Infill pattern: gyroid or cubic for strength; hexagonal for a quirky, stable feel.

If you’re printing multiple units for friends, consider printing a “family” of shells with slightly different internal dividers, so each person can label their own tray and stop fighting over the blue one.

## Features of the case
- Snap-fit lid: A lid that stays on when you want it on and pops off when you need to rummage for that last Phase 3 card. The snap-fit design reduces the need for screws and makes the case lighter and quicker to service.
- Card trays: Interchangeable trays with uniform slots; you can swap them in and out if you change your Phase 10 strategy and want to rearrange by color or by deck order.
- Card thickness tolerance: The case accommodates standard Phase 10 cards (approximately 2.5 inches by 3.5 inches) with a little wiggle room for sleeves.

### Durability features
- Corner reinforcement: Slight bevels at the corners to reduce stress concentrations if you drop the case on a tile floor.
- Filament compatibility: The design can print cleanly with a variety of filaments; if you’re using CFPLA or PETG with added stiffness, you can increase the wall thickness to 0.24–0.28 mm for even more durability.
- Surface finish: Sand lightly and optionally apply a light coat of clear coat for smoother sliding of cards.

## Printing guide and practical tips
- First print: test a single tray to verify the slot size. If the cards don’t want to slide in, you need to increase clearance slightly.
- Print orientation: keep the slots vertical to minimize supports; the base should be flat to avoid a wonky lid.
- Post-processing: a light sanding of the inner walls reduces friction for a smoother card glide.
- Assembly: snap the lid into place and test the closure with a few practice runs before you commit to printing multiple copies.
- Finishing touches: label the sections with small decals or printable labels to remind your crew which Phase goes where.

## How to get started
1) Design or download a Phase 10 card case model. If you’re drawing from existing designs, consider a modular approach so you can swap trays without disassembling the entire case.
