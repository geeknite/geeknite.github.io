## Great Condition: What It Really Means in a 1997 Lot

“Great Condition” is a phrase that requires a bit of interpretation, especially for a 1997 batch. Here’s how to translate it into practical terms so you can assess the lot without turning into a card-bargaining Kratos in the middle of a flea market:

- Corners: Look for sharp corners with minimal whitening along the tips. A tiny fleck here and there is often acceptable, but you don’t want actual rounded corners that soften the edges.
- Edges: Check for edge wear, chipping, or discoloration. Panoramic scenes in some older cards can look cooler than they actually are if the edges are dull.
- Centering: Some 1990s MTG cards aren’t perfectly centered. A small tilt may be acceptable if the card remains visually appealing when sleeved.
- Surface: Light scratches aren’t the end of the world, but heavy scratches, stains, or warping can seriously impact grade and playability.
- Print defects: Don’t confuse cosmetic quirks (like a misprint or a border inconsistency) for damage. Some collectors actually pay more for certain printing oddities.

Grading standards vary by organization, but for a private collection, a lot described as “Great Condition” should feel solid, presentable, and suitable for display or light play. If you’re chasing a higher grade for resale, you’ll want a reputable third-party grading service to confirm exact numbers. However, for display purposes and personal nostalgia, great condition usually means you can confidently sleeve the cards and show them to friends who claim they saved their lunch money for a pack of Rebels one sunny afternoon in the 1990s.

## Grading Basics and How to Verify Authenticity

If you’re serious about turning this 1997 lot into a long-term investment or a centerpiece in your personal collection, you’ll want to understand grading basics. Here’s a quick primer:

### Common grading scales
- CGC/CCG style grading: A formal appendix that assigns a numerical grade and a quality description. Great for long-term value, but you’ll pay for it.
- PSA-style grading: The public face of modern grading, often used for modern collectibles; older MTG cards can be graded too, though it’s less common.
- Ungraded/Raw: Perfect for budget collecting, quick trades, and enjoying the card without a professional certificate. If you’re going this route, protect the card well and keep it in a high-quality sleeve and top loader.

### What graders look for
- Centering accuracy relative to the era’s printing standards
- Edge wear and whitening (especially for white borders)
- Surface integrity: no deep scratches, no stains, no creases
- Corners: sharp versus worn
- Print defects that aren’t actual damage (for example, slight misprints that are considered “character” by some collectors)

If you’re new to grading, a good rule of thumb is: a “Great Condition” lot should be ready for protective sleeves, a binder, and occasional display without requiring urgent professional intervention. If you want to go pro, shop around for local grading services and ask for examples of 1990s MTG cards in similar condition to calibrate your expectations.

## Storage, Handling, and Care: Keeping the Magic Fresh

This is where the real work happens. Cards from the late 1990s are sturdy, but they’re not immune to the forces of time, humidity, and careless binder pomp. Here are practical tips for preserving your 1997 Smoke/Plains/Swamp lot:

- Sleeving: Start with soft inner sleeves, then move to rigid top-loaders for the most valuable pieces. If you’re pairing with a full deck, consider a two-drawer system for quick access.
- Storage: Use airtight cases or binders with acid-free pages. Avoid cardboard boxes that trap humidity and give your cards a coffee-stained patina that’s not chic in the sentimental sense.
- Humidity and temperature: Keep cards in a climate-controlled space. Ideal humidity is generally around 50-60%, with stable temperatures to avoid warping.
- Handling: Handle with clean hands or wear cotton gloves if you’re really serious about preserving the pristine look. Avoid touching the surface with fingers; the oils can degrade the finish over time.
- Display: If you want to show off, consider a non-glare display case. A well-lit shelf is fine, but sunlight is a thief that steals color and stability.

## A Quick Field Guide: What to Look For in a Great Condition Lot

When you’re evaluating a 1997 lot that promises great condition, here are practical checks you can perform quickly:

- Card edges and corners: Inspect corners for whitening and edge wear. A lot with clean edges and minimal whitening is a plus.
- Surface and center: Look for surface scratches and scuff marks. A well-preserved surface with crisp text is ideal.
- Print consistency: Some 1997 cards show minor printing variations. If you’re into the micro-details, you’ll savor those quirks.
- Color integrity: The colors should be vibrant (as much as the printing style allowed). Faded colors or darkened tones can indicate aging or improper storage.
- Back and margins: The back should be uniform, with no obvious damage. Margins should be consistent with other cards from the same batch.
- Authenticity cues: Check for typical telltales of authenticity for the era—font consistency, border design, and the texture of the card stock.

If you’re serious about a buy, consider a quick third-party check for a subset of cards and cross-verify with online databases to ensure you’re not accidentally purchasing a counterfeit or mislabeled item. While counterfeiters aren’t as ubiquitous as foil tokens, the older the card, the more you should be discerning.
