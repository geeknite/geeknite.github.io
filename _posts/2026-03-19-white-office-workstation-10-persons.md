### What is it?

A 10-person white workstation is usually a benching setup or a cluster of linked desks designed for ten users. It’s built around a clean, minimalist aesthetic, with white surfaces, discreet privacy panels, integrated power and data hubs, and an emphasis on easy cleaning. The concept is simple: maximize collaboration and light without turning your space into a chaotic data center. The real trick is modularity—so you can reconfigure for new teams, new workflows, or the occasional company-wide hackathon.

### Why white?

White is not just a color; it's a performance metric: it reflects light, reduces glare on screens, and helps create a perception of space, which is essential in smaller offices. White also acts as a neutral background that makes color-coded sticky notes, boards, and monitors pop. The downside? Maintenance. Coffee spills, marker scribbles, and accidental-glue mishaps show up faster. But with the right coatings, textiles, and a good cleaning routine, white can be luxurious rather than tiring. A white workspace signals clarity and focus—a perfect stage for your team to perform, even when the tasks include a lot of brainstorming, planning, and caffeination.

## The Setup: Components and Specs

### Desks

Ten desks at 1200 x 600 mm are a common starting point; 1400 x 700 mm desks give more shoulder room for keyboard and mouse. There’s a trade-off: larger desks take more space but reduce crowding and allow for bigger monitors and docking stations. In a benching configuration, the desks share back-to-back panels, with under-desk cable trays and a central power hub. This centralization can save time in the morning, when you’re frantically plugging devices into every available socket before stand-up.

### Chairs

Ergonomics matter more than fashion. The chairs should have adjustable seat height, lumbar support that actually supports, adjustable armrests, and a backrest that breathes. If your team includes people who occasionally stand or move around to stretch, consider chairs with dynamic support and tilt tension that isn’t so stiff you feel like you’re sitting in a car seat for eight hours.

### Monitor arms and screens

A typical setup includes two monitors per person or a shared 34" ultrawide for teams who want to see the same screen at the same time. Monitor arms help you optimize desk real estate and reduce neck strain by letting users fine-tune height and distance.

### Cable management and power

A robust cable management system is the backbone of an orderly workspace. Under-desk trays, vertical cable channels, and central power hubs reduce clutter and simplify reconfigurations. A good plan includes:

- A central power unit with surge protection and enough outlets for all devices
- Data distribution for network reliability
- Cable guards to prevent tangling and tripping hazards

Cable management is not glamorous, but it is the unsung hero of a tidy space. It also reduces the risk of tripping over a phantom charger that seems to have 12 feet of USB-C.

## Layout Configurations for 10

### Configuration A: Long bench row

A single long bench with ten seats encourages collaboration, with privacy panels to dampen side-to-side chatter.

### Configuration B: Double-row with central aisle

Two rows facing each other allow for easy teamwork without the inconvenience of leaning over a colleague’s shoulder. It’s a nice balance between space efficiency and social interaction.

### Configuration C: Island cluster

Three to four desks form an island, with a central meeting area or whiteboard. This layout supports spontaneous standups, whiteboard sessions, and impromptu design sprints.

### Configuration D: U-shape with open center

Perimeter desks leaving a central open zone can accommodate a stand-up desk area, a mini lounge, and a coffee station. This arrangement is perfect for teams that crave collaboration but also want a small zone for deep work.

## Acoustics and lighting

Silent spaces make for productive work. Acoustic panels, ceiling baffles, and carpet tiles can absorb noise. Lighting matters too: a mix of overhead LEDs and task lighting reduces glare and creates even illumination. A well-lit space reduces eye strain and, as a bonus, makes your cube farm photograph nicely for your marketing videos.

## Modularity and Future-proofing

The beauty of a white 10-person workstation is the ability to reconfigure when teams scale. You can add or remove desks, re-route power and data, and swap panels without reworking the entire floor plan. If your company's growth forecast is a rollercoaster, modularity is your safety belt.

### Cable management and power, again, cannot be ignored

The central cable tray and under-desk channels become the hero of this story. When properly designed, you can swap modules, expand to additional rows, or convert to a standing desk layout without tearing up the floor. Don’t underestimate the importance of a well-labeled, color-coded cable system: it saves hours at month-end when someone asks, “Where does this cable go again?”

## A Day in the Life: Real-world usage

The prototype team is a mix of software developers, product managers, designers, and a QA engineer who insists on a separate monitor to watch cat videos. The space must evolve with their routine. It should support deep work sessions as well as quick standups. The day might look like this:

- Morning warm-up: a quick standup around the whiteboard and the center hub, with everyone contributing their top priority for the day.
- Mid-morning sprint review: teams cluster around the central screen for demos while sipping coffee.
- Afternoon design collaboration: two pods pair up for a design sprint, switching desks to maximize sunlight exposure.
- End-of-day wrap-up: a quick tidy of desks, cables, and monitor arms.

A note on culture: The space can influence team dynamics. White desks can convey openness and openness can trigger transparency. But if the team always works with headphones on, the whiteness will reveal the silence more clearly, which is not necessarily a bad thing.

## Maintenance and cleaning

White surfaces require routine care: daily wipe-downs for dust and fingerprints, weekly deeper cleans for monitors and chair fabrics, and monthly maintenance checks for the cable network. Keep microfiber cloths, screen spray, and spare chair tips handy. Your future self will thank you for it when you don’t have to play “Where did that coffee ring come from?” every morning.

## Sustainability and longevity

Durable materials, robust chairs, modular components—these three things extend the life of your workspace project. The more you can reuse and repurpose, the more you save. Look for furniture with recycled materials, water-based finishes, and reconfigurable components that can adapt to new workflows. Your budget will thank you, your IT team will thank you, and your team will thank you when you don’t have to deal with the replacement of broken desk surfaces.

## Value and budget

The 10-person white workstation can be expensive, but the long-term value is substantial if you plan carefully. A thorough cost-benefit analysis should include the furniture expense, installation, ergonomic considerations, maintenance, and potential future expansions. The best approach is to pick a system with a modular upgrade path: you pay more upfront but you’ll be able to reconfigure with more desks, more power hubs, and more privacy panels as the team grows or shifts.

## Pros and Cons

Pro: The space appears bigger, the mood is calm and modern, and the layout tends to promote collaboration.

Con: The ongoing maintenance is real; white surfaces show marks; you will have to commit to cleaning.
