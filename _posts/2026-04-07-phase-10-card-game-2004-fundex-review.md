## How to play Phase 10 in 60 seconds
The objective is simple: complete all ten phases before your opponents, or at least not finish last in the scoring.

Phases:
1. 2 sets of 3
2. 1 set of 3 + 1 run of 4
3. 1 set of 4 + 1 run of 4
4. 1 run of 7
5. 1 run of 8
6. 1 run of 9
7. 2 sets of 4
8. 7 cards of one color
9. 1 set of 5 + 1 set of 2
10. 1 set of 5 + 1 set of 3

The catch: players can lay down a phase only when they have the exact combination; otherwise, they draw and pass, trying not to whiff on the final turn. Wild Cards can substitute for any card in a phase, but there is usually a limit on how many Wild Cards you can use per phase depending on the edition. The score works in reverse; the lower your total at the end of a round, the better.

Pro tip: keep track of your opponents' revealed phases to gauge when to push for a double-run or a bold set of five. But remember, Phase 10 is as much about social dynamics as it is about mathematics, so a bit of bluffing can spice things up without crossing grandma's line of acceptable behavior.

## Variant notes and house rules
House rules can adjust the pace and style of the game. A common tweak is to allow certain wild card substitutions or to modify scoring to emphasize some phases over others. Another popular tweak is to shorten the game by removing phases 9 and 10 after a few rounds to keep things moving for younger players. For families with younger players, you can implement a simplified scoring system that keeps track of rounds with sticky notes or a whiteboard, which adds a nice tactile feel to the experience.

As with any game, you need to set expectations; Phase 10 rewards planning, but it also rewards social lubrication in the form of jokes and playful heckling. The right group will turn a near disaster into a legendary memory.
