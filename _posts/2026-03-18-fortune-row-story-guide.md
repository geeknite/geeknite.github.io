## Fortune Row: The Setup
### The Hook and the Spark
Fortune Row begins with a choice. A character who has lived under the shadow of a debt, an old promise, or a lie told to keep a name clean is suddenly confronted by the past made literal. The GM drops a neon clue that hints at a debt collector who is more seller than collector, a vendor who trades in fate rather than goods, and a person who speaks in proverbs and reloads a gun with memories. The hook should feel both intimate and ominous; it should promise a story about identity, about what it means to own a lie, and about the thin line between luck and consequence.

The opening scene usually unfolds in a place that embodies Fortune Row — a bar with a jukebox that refuses to play the same song twice, a pawnshop where clocks tick counterproductively, or a street market saturated with fortune tellers who see futures that have already happened to someone else. The players discover a common thread: a name that appears in a ledger, a symbol on a token, and a rumor that someone has been playing the Row like a fiddle—pulling strings that bend fate itself.

### The Row and Its Denizens
Fortune Row is stacked with archetypes that feel intimate to the players while staying ominous to the locals. You have:

- The Velvet Dealer: a crime lord who peddles debts, not drugs. They know who owes what, and they also know where the bodies are buried in velvet pockets. They speak in velvet phrases, and every bargain has a price tag that ends up in someone else ledger.
- The Paper Oracle: a fortune teller who prints prophecies on cheap newsprint and then sells them at the bus stop. They see patterns and sometimes reveal them with a flourish that feels more dangerous than helpful.
- The Glasswright: a fixer who can alter surfaces, making windows and streets look different than they actually are. They know that perception is a currency more valuable than cash and that a single pane can reveal a truth that nothing else can.
- The Row Kid: a street kid who knows the back alleys, the side doors, and the shortcuts through the Fate Maze. They carry a crucial clue but they may also be bait in a larger trap.

Each denizen carries a mask that ties into the Row and into the broader myth cycle. The GM should tailor these masks to the player characters so that every encounter feels personal, not merely procedural. Remember that in City of Mist you can always turn a villain into a reluctant ally, a reluctant ally into a suspect, and a suspect into a partner on who wears the same kind of mask you do.

The Row thrives on rumors. The GM should seed the city with rumors about who runs Fortune Row in the shadows, about why the debts are so heavy this season, and about a rumored event that will tilt the row and force each player to decide what they owe to themselves versus what they owe to the city.

## Narrative Structure: How Fortune Row Unfolds
### Act I: The Spark
In the first act, you want to spark a personal investment. Your players should realize that someone they care about — a friend, a mentor, a partner who turned crypto in the wrong market — has been touched by the Row. The spark can be a micro event: a debt note found on a coat hook; a call from a number that should be long dead; a mural that transforms from a city block into a map of the Mist that points toward the next lead. The key is to make the Row feel inevitable, as if stepping into it is not a choice but a necessity. The players discover a piece of the Row that ties to their character backstory and a thread that connects to an existing GM run or to a new villain who will be the hinge of the arc.

### Act II: The Pit
The second act drops the players into the deeper gears of Fortune Row. They must confront the debt that is binding the city, the totem that has become a habit, and the stakes that threaten to erase a character from the story. Common obstacles include:
- A time window that collapses changes into the location of a critical clue.
- A betrayal that reveals a long hidden past connection between two players.
- A public ritual that hums with mystic energy and reveals a layer of the Fortune Row myth.

In the pit, the GM should escalate tension through three threads: a place thread (the bar that never serves the same drink twice), a person thread (the Velvet Dealer who knows your every fear), and a clue thread (an object that reveals where the Row is heading next). This triad helps maintain momentum while you weave the players through the core mystery.

### Act III: The Beat and Revelation
The last act in Fortune Row is less about a cataclysmic showdown and more about a revelation that reshapes who each character is. The revelation can be dramatic — a character learns they have been playing a role in someone else mythic plan — or it can be intimate — a long overdue confession that changes how a character views luck, debt, and duty. The end should feel earned rather than forced: a choice made under pressure that reveals not only the truth but the cost of that truth. A strong closing beat should leave a few unresolved threads that promise future stories while giving the players a sense of closure on the Row itself for this arc.

## GM Tools: Pacing, Clues, and Atmosphere
### The City Map and Flow
Fortune Row benefits from a promiscuous map — places that reveal themselves only under the right Mist, places that blink in and out of reality, and locations that feel more like a mood than a place. Build a walkable path of linked locations around a few anchors. The anchor sites could be the Velvet Dealer’s club, the Paper Oracle’s printing press, and the Glasswright’s workshop. Each anchor should offer one or two investigative windows, a single emotional beat, and a danger mechanism that can escalate tension if players linger too long.

As a GM, you should avoid letting the Row become a single dungeon or a single set piece. Make Fortune Row feel like a living street that changes its mood depending on who you talk to and what the players are wearing that night. A person’s mask and a place’s vibe should influence what clues the players see and how those clues are interpreted by the Mist.

### Masks and Mist Tokens
Fortune Row thrives on masks and tokens. The masks are not simply character aesthetics; they unlock access to certain information, certain reactions from NPCs, and sometimes special actions in scenes. Mist tokens function like a currency that can be spent to reveal probabilities, reveal hidden connections, or force a small twist when the players step into a scene with the urge to bargain. When you run this arc, prepare a handful of Mist tokens (conceptual, not physical), and make sure players know the tokens exist even if they don’t immediately use them. The sense of scarcity increases the stakes and the drama of every choice.

### Clues, Evidence, and Red Herrings
Fortune Row thrives on a misdirected yet satisfying clue flow. Your clues should be layered and interlocked, because a single thread can be a breadcrumb that leads to something real, while another thread is a decoy that leads to a dead end. Build a list of three to five core clues and two or three decoys per scene. Each clue should tie into at least one character mask or a row’s secret. The players should feel clever when using the clues correctly and simultaneously feel the weight of a wrong assumption when a decoy misleads them into a folly. This approach keeps the middle acts lively and prevents stalling in corridors of dialogue.

## NPCs: Cast, Motives, and How to Use Them
### The Velvet Dealer
The Velvet Dealer embodies the high stakes and velvet violence of Fortune Row. They offer deals that look generous on the surface and reveal the cost only later. When you bring the Velvet Dealer into a scene, make the environment reflect their power: dim lighting, soft fabrics, and the murmur of a crowd that knows better than to ask too many questions. The Dealer should push the players toward a decision that feels like a moral shortcut, only for the decision to turn out to be an emotional long cut that leaves a mark on the characters’ identities.

### The Paper Oracle
A mobile print shop roams Fortune Row, blasting a rumor mill with a scent of ink and prophecy. The Paper Oracle can provide a prophecies that foreshadow events in surprising ways. The challenge is to avoid making their predictions feel like cheat sheets. The Oracle should be a mirror and a mirror only: a reflection of what the players fear or desire most and a reminder that fate in the Mist is never simple or fair.

### The Glasswright
The Glasswright can alter perception, creating windows into truths that would otherwise stay hidden. They should pose as a fixer who can open doors — but each door you open reveals a corridor that leads to more questions, not fewer. They are a crucial contact for the players who want to navigate the Row with style and speed, not merely brute force.

### The Row Kid
A counterpoint to the powerful adults in Fortune Row, the Row Kid knows the real heartbeat of the block. They can point players toward the themes that matter most: who gets to decide what counts as a fortune, who pays the price of luck, and how much of your life you are willing to gamble for a moment of advantage. The Row Kid is a potential ally but also a catalyst for moral choices that will define the tone of the ending.

## Scenes and Beats: A Walkthrough
The following is a flexible scaffold you can adapt to your table. The times are suggestions; the mood determines the pacing. Each scene includes a setup, a primary objective, a key clue, and a twist that can adjust the players’ understanding of Fortune Row.

- Scene A: The Beginning — A cryptic sign on the wall of a bar announces a night when fortunes flip. Objective: find the first clue among the crowd, which includes a debtor, a rumor mill, and a door that looks ordinary but rings when closed. Clue: a printed page that lists debts with dates that match a ritual schedule. Twist: a misdirection that points toward a dead end unless a Mist token is spent on a connection reveal.
- Scene B: The Market of Echoes — The players interrogate sellers who claim to know the Row's rhythm. Objective: locate a vendor who has a map to the next location. Clue: a token that can be traded for a single secret from any buyer. Twist: a decoy map that splits the party physically and emotionally, testing trust.
- Scene C: The Velvet Club — The Dealer makes an appearance in the velvet glow, offering a bargain that would free a debt but cost a memory. Objective: decide whether to bargain or to refuse. Clue: an encoded note hidden in a velvet glove. Twist: taking the bargain triggers a personal consequence for one PC.
- Scene D: The Glasswright Workshop — An explosive opportunity to glimpse an alternate version of Fortune Row. Objective: obtain a key window that reveals the true scale of the debt  ledger. Clue: shards of glass that show parallel versions of a scene. Twist: a door that can only be opened by confessing a truth to another player.
- Scene E: The Revelation — The Row culminates in a moment where a character must decide if they accept a myth or reject it. Objective: end the arc with a choice that reshapes the character and the Row. Clue: a final token that marks the new cycle; it binds the group to the Row with a ritual. Twist: the Row is revealed to be a reflection of the group own decisions rather than a separate antagonist, forcing players to confront collective responsibility.
- Scene F: Aftermath and Reflection — The city moves on, but Fortune Row leaves a mark on the characters. Objective: debrief and tie up emotional threads; offer a lead for future adventures. Clue: a worn badge or a memory that returns when the lights dim. Twist: a memory returns with a new meaning that reframes what counts as fortune.

For each scene, adapt the clues to fit your players masks. If a PC wears The Velvet mask, for example, they might decode a clue faster but face a higher risk of entangling in debt that follows them into the next arc.

## Player Tips: Hooks and Personal Stakes
Fortune Row excels when it ties directly to the player characters. Here are ways to weave personal stakes into the arc:

- Tie a debt to a character backstory: a past relationship with a mentor or rival who appears as a debt collector in neon ink. The debt should feel personal and costly, not merely professional.
- Make the Mist expose a hidden desire: a player may want recognition, control, or secrecy. Fortune Row should push them to choose between the masks they wear and the truth they refuse to acknowledge.
- Offer a path to redemption or ruin via a single choice: the players should feel the weight of their decisions in a way that reshapes their characters and the city around them.

Character alignment with the Row is not about good or evil; it is about how each character negotiates the price of fortune and what they are willing to sacrifice to hold onto it. The Paper Oracle, for instance, could be a friend whose prophecies reveal their own vulnerability, prompting a character to choose between helping a stranger or safeguarding a secret that could ruin their life.

## Optional Mechanics and Variants
Fortune Row can be run with a few optional rules that add texture without complicating the core system.

- Mist currency: in addition to standard in game currency, the DM may award Mist tokens for clever use of masks or for resisting a tempting bargain that would break a character bond. These tokens can be spent to reveal a hidden facet of a clue or to momentarily alter a given scene’s mood.
- Fate dice: replace or supplement standard dice with a Fate dice mechanic to emphasize the luck and fate aspect. Rolling a Fate die might trigger a short cutscene, a flash of memory, or a new clue that appears in unexpected ways.
- Fortune Row heists: for players who crave action, introduce a non lethal but high tension heist sequence that takes place within the Row. The plan should go wrong in at least one meaningful way, forcing players to improvise and leverage their masks in new ways.

## Visual Aids and Props
Visualizing Fortune Row helps the table lean into the mood. Consider these simple props:

- A map that shows several key locations with neon glow and weathered edges. You can place this in a handout that the players can study during sessions. The image can be as simple as a rough city plan with a few highlighted blocks representing the anchor sites.
- A set of token cards that represent Mist tokens, debt obligations, and rumor threads. Each card can have a short description and a color that matches a mood or mask.
- A printed prop page with quotes from the Velvet Dealer, the Paper Oracle, and the Glasswright. These quotes can be used to create atmosphere or to set up misdirection during crucial scenes.

## Final Thoughts and Recommendation
Fortune Row is not a one trick pony. It offers a balance of character focus, mythic resonance, and investigative play that can accommodate a short run in a single evening or a longer arc across several sessions. The key to success is to treat the Row as a character in its own right — a living block that listens and responds to the masks your players wear. The NPCs are flavorful and the scenes are modular enough to fit into a larger campaign or stand alone. The mood stays moody, the stakes stay personal, and the mysteries stay satisfying rather than punishing.
