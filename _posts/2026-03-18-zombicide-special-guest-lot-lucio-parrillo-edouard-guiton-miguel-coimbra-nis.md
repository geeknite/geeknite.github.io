## What is the Zombicide Special Guest Lot?

The Special Guest Lot is a celebration box set that CMON (the folks who brought you Zombicide) sometimes releases to spotlight top-tier artists who have contributed concept art, full-page cards, or character line work across multiple editions. This particular trio—Lucio Parrillo, Edouard Guiton, and Miguel Coimbra—brings a symphony of fantasy-horror vibes to your board: saturated colors, dramatic shadows, and enough gore to make a medieval painter blush.

The box often includes:

- A curated set of survivor miniatures with sculpts inspired by the artists’ signature styles.
- A number of zombie variants that reflect the artists’ aesthetics (think more gothic, more demon-drenched, more nightmare fuel).
- Art cards and maybe a backdrop or two that can be used as a display piece while you’re waiting for the next wave.
- A glossy manual or “in-world art bible” that explains the story behind the designs. If you like lore almost as much as you like decapitating zombies with a power knife, you’ll eat this up.

What would a Geeknite post be without a little tongue-in-cheek commentary about price-to-sanity ratios? If you collect these, you basically own a mini art gallery that occasionally moans for snacks. ## Artists Spotlight: Parrillo, Guiton, Coimbra

### Lucio Parrillo: Gothic Fervor meets Zombie Apocalypse
Lucio Parrillo is known for gothic fantasy art that belongs on the cover of a dimly lit tavern’s menu for monsters. When you see his name attached to a Zombicide character, you expect dramatic capes, bone-white highlights, and eyes that seem to follow you as you roll your dice. In this Special Guest Lot, Parrillo’s influence shines through in the survivors’ cloaks, the menacing posture of the zombies, and the overall moody atmosphere. The color palette tends toward deep purples, cursed reds, and bone-whites—a combination that makes the table feel like a haunted cathedral. The mini designs might feature flowing coats, sinister grins, and a sense that the knight in dented armor has seen some truly disturbing things in the afterlife’s discount section.

Parrillo’s influence also sneaks into the card art. Expect titles that look like they belong on a grim fantasy card game rather than a standard survivor card: dramatic typography, bold shadows, and a sense of epic doom that makes you want to strap on a helmet and go dungeon crawling into the kitchen to fetch snacks during a break between waves.

### Edouard Guiton: Whimsy, Color, and Urban Monster Couture
Edouard Guiton injects a more vibrant, almost comic-book freshness into the mix. His work tends to lean into cinematic lighting, with neon glows and high-contrast color blocks that pop under the glow of your gaming lamp. The survivors in his design often look like they’ve just stepped out of a late-night city shoot, complete with streetwear that somehow defies apocalypse fashion laws. The zombies echo that same urban vibe—gritty, expressive, and a little bit cheeky in their grotesque charm. Guiton’s contribution helps the set feel modern and alive, a visual reminder that in Zombicide, the dead aren’t just threats—they’re a runway of decayed style.

The cards carry his kinetic energy too: bold, punchy captions, and dynamic action icons that feel like they were sketched in a war-room-turned-art-studio. If you like your zombie apocalypse to look like a neon-drenched street scene, this is the artist you want steering the visuals.

### Miguel Coimbra: Nightmarish Detail and Textured Doom
Miguel Coimbra is the heavy artillery of this trio. His art often exudes a meticulous, almost tactile texture—the kind you want to run your fingers over (carefully, with a gloves-on approach) to feel the grit and bone fused into the armor of the survivors. Coimbra’s monsters aren’t just creatures; they’re architectural horrors, with nooks and crannies of shadow that seem to invite you to lean in and discover the miniature’s secrets. The lending of Coimbra to Zombicide uses a lot of intricate patterns, scaly surfaces, and a sense of ancient, ruined architecture as a backdrop to the action.

If you’re a painter who loves fine linework or you want your minis to look like they’ve stepped out of a museum exhibit with a killer hangover, Coimbra’s presence there is a gift. The survivors under his design feel sturdy, weathered, and plausibly capable of withstanding more bullets, traps, and zombie boss fights than you’ll realistically ever need. It’s the kind of artistry that justifies keeping the set on a display shelf when your table is empty—because the miniatures still tell a story even when you’re not playing.

## The Artwork Across Cards, Tiles, and Tokens

Artwork in Zombicide isn’t just flavor; it’s a gameplay signal. The Special Guest Lot uses Parrillo’s gothic silhouettes to signal high-threat encounters, Guiton’s neon highlights to make special items stand out from the chaos, and Coimbra’s texture to add depth to monster tokens that would otherwise blend into a monochrome sea of red.

- Survivor cards: Expect bold typography with dramatic drop shadows and art that makes you squint at the small print in a way that seems heroic rather than clunky. The survivors feel distinct, each with a little backstory nugget in the flavor text. It’s enough to make you want to paint the backstory into the miniature’s base so your tabletop narrative has a little facing depth.
- Zombie tokens: The zombies aren’t just generic horde fare here; they’ve got personality. Some look like they crawled out of a long-forgotten stained-glass window, others resemble the resident of a chained dojo of doom. The three artists provide a spectrum—from the grim to the flamboyantly grotesque—that increases the vibes per square inch.
- Tiles and terrain: The background art and tile borders pick up the mood from the cover art. Expect dramatic shading on ruins and minimal interference with line-of-sight rules. It’s not just pretty to look at; it’s functional and atmospheric, which is exactly what you want when you’re trying to pretend you’re a battlefield general while stacking dice.

If you’re a painter, you’ll notice little design languages you can borrow for your own projects: high-contrast light sources (Guiton), heavy cloth textures and cape drapery (Parrillo), and meticulous bone/scale textures (Coimbra). It’s like a mini-masterclass in fantasy-horror painting—without the actual burden of paying for a semester in art school. You’re welcome.

## Gameplay and Table Feel

Artistic excellence is great, but does this Special Guest Lot actually play well? Yes, with one caveat: it’s designed to be visually striking first, mechanically robust second. If your goal is a tournament-grade optimization, you may want to pair this set with a more aggressive balance patch or house-rules. If, however, you want a narrative-driven campaign where the table aesthetics wrap around your decisions with a velvet-gloved zombie hand, this box is an excellent choice.

- Initiative and morale: The new survivors bring personality, which translates to morale choices and tactical synergies. You’ll find yourself describing “the painter’s hero” as they stride across the board, and it changes how you plan turns. It’s less about “maximize DPS” and more about “how do we tell this story without sacrificing the next wave.”
- Boss and special encounters: Expect more cinematic boss moments with sculpts that imply larger threats than their card stats might suggest. This is where the artists’ work shines: the models look threatening enough to justify the dice you’ll roll to survive them.
- Replay value: With three distinct art directions, you’ll notice different feelings from the same base set depending on which mini you pick to lead the charge. It’s not just a paint job; it’s a gameplay variant, a way to bias your first impressions of a mission. You can rotate the hero lineup to align with Parrillo’s moody vibe one night and Coimbra’s textured nightmare the next.

From a gamer’s perspective, the Special Guest Lot fits well in a casual to mid-core campaign. It isn’t a budget sprint; it’s a slow-burn immersion into a world where the art matters almost as much as the dice. For players who value narrative cohesion, this box is a gold mine.

## Painting Tips for a Great Look

To maximize the impact of your Parrillo-Guiton-Coimbra trio, here are some practical tips you can apply right after you finish reading this post:

- Prime and undercoat with care: A solid base coat helps the shadows pop and lets the textured surfaces read well under game lighting. A gray or black primer is a reliable starting point for that moody, atmospheric look.
- Layering for depth: Use a quick three-layer gradient on capes and coats to simulate the dramatic lighting that Parrillo and Guiton favor. Start with a mid-tone, then add shadows, and finish with a selective highlight. The result? A cape that sways with every in-game motion.
- Texture the monsters: Coimbra’s textures benefit from a dry brush technique. Use a light, almost-white dry brush on scales and bone armor to catch the light. It will not only look tactile but also help the monster tokens stand out on the board.
- Non-metallic metal (NMM) for swords: If you want to try a more advanced technique, Parrillo’s pieces often look great with NMM on armor. It’s a fun challenge and makes your swords sparkle under the gaming lamp without requiring metallic paints.
- Sealing and protection: A matte varnish will reduce glossy reflections that can wash out the dramatic shadows you’ve built. If you want a dramatic, almost cinematic glint on certain elements, use a satin or soft gloss selectively.

These tips will help you achieve the painting vibe that the set’s art direction promises: bold, cinematic, and a little bit terrifying—in the best possible way.
