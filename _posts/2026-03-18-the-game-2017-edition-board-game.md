## The Game: The 2017 Edition — A Co-op Card-Stack With Attitude

If you thought watching a suspenseful three-hour documentary about taxes was edge-of-your-seat, wait until you try The Game: The 2017 Edition. It’s not a board game about playing a game; it’s a board game about not playing a game—at least not in the way your brain expects. The 2017 Edition is the latest re-release, re-skinned with slightly shinier art, a few rule tweaks, and a sense of swagger that says, You thought The Game was hard before? Hold my card sleeve.

This review aims to answer one important question: is the 2017 Edition more than just a clever marketing refresh, or is it a legitimately improved way to test the limits of your group’s patience and friendship? Spoiler: it’s both, and that’s exactly what makes it Geeknite-approved material.

If you’re new here, welcome to the party. If you’re returning after reading our previous whispers about card ethics and the dangers of coordinated discards, you’ll notice that The Game remains a strange beast. It’s a cooperative puzzle where players try to discard cards from their hands onto two piles in ascending order. The catch? You can only see your own cards, not what your teammates have, and you’re legally prohibited from shouting, “Play that red six already!” (though we’ve seen some folks try to slip that in under the radar).

The 2017 Edition amps up the tension with new art, slightly revised rules, and a handful of “twist cards” that nudge the difficulty without turning the deck into a monster. In other words, it’s still the same core vibe—frantic, clever, and suspiciously satisfying when you finally stack the last card—but with enough polish to feel like a proper update rather than a cynical reprint with a fresh coat of varnish.

### Components and Setup

The 2017 Edition is a lean package, which is a relief if you’ve lugged around the original’s card pile and found yourself considering a cardio workout just to carry the rulebook. The box contents are clean, compact, and (most importantly) transportable. You’ll find:

- A deck of numbered cards, typically 2 through 99, in four colors. In practice, you’ll be dealing with a lot of numbers, which is a reminder that life is a linear ascent, and so is this game’s discard process.
- Two shared piles, left and right, where you’ll place cards in ascending order. It’s the classic The Game mechanic: keep the piles moving, but don’t derail the entire train.
- A handful of new “twist” and event cards introduced in 2017 that spice up the play without turning it into a calculus seminar.
- Minimal components otherwise; the art is new but the gameplay remains elegantly minimal. The real artwork victory here is not splashy illustrations but a cleaner layout that makes it easier to scope the table without needing an engineering degree.

### How the Rules Feel in 2017 Edition

In The Game, you don’t win by destroying monsters or racking up points. You win by avoiding catastrophe—namely, the two piles getting clogged with a mis-ordered cascade. The 2017 Edition tucks in a handful of minor rule-adjacent changes that streamline play and reduce the occasional “disco inferno” moment when the table erupts in rapidly flipping cards.

- Core mechanic remains the same: players have a hand of cards and must discard them to either the left or right pile, in ascending order, while trying not to go over the top of the pile or break the continuous flow. The result is a delicate dance of risk assessment and whispered consensus.
- The new twist cards introduce optional rules that can be toggled for longer sessions. They add tiny bits of chaos that reflect “life happens” moments—like miscommunications, a sudden urge to check your phone, or that moment when everyone realizes you’ve been discarding the wrong color for five turns.
- The 2017 edition also includes a handful of updated edge-case rules to avoid stalemates and boring stretches. If you’ve played the original, you’ll notice these changes are not radical; they’re like upgrading from dial-up to fast enough to stream cat videos during the downtime between rounds.

Rulebook quality is a highlight here. The writing is crisp, with a friendly, slightly exasperated tone that matches the mood of a table trying to outwit itself. The diagrams are clearer than a fogged windshield, and the three-column layout for each example round makes it easier to see where you typically go wrong and why your brain insists you can actually trust a four-color logic puzzle more than your best friend’s casual optimism.

### Gameplay: A Tale of Two Piles and a Team of Five Clue Givers

The game shines in group play. You’ll likely want three to five players for the best balance of information and miscommunication. Two players can still have a fantastic time if they’re prepared for a little more cognitive gymnastics and a lot more groaning when a critical discard goes wrong late in the round.

- Turn flow is straightforward: you draw (or you start with a hand, depending on the variant you’re playing), you decide which card to play, and you place it on the correct pile. If you’re wrong, you kick off a cascade, which in The Game means you’re in danger of the entire stack going over the limit. The feeling is part strategy, part social experiment, and entirely addictive in a way that makes you want to blame the person who suggested a second round.
- Pressure builds as you approach the end of a round. The deck doesn’t care about your narrative arc; it’s a metronome counting down to a potential meltdown. The 2017 Edition does a better job of balancing this pressure with moments of relief—especially when a well-timed safe discard clears the way for a few easy moves.
- The asynchronous nature of information (you know your hand, you don’t know theirs) triggers a lot of great “aha” moments. You’ll find yourself mid-round whispering, “I think the red cards go left now,” only to realize that your teammate’s hand is flush with blue cards and your prediction was off by an inch.

For players who are worried that cooperative games require a therapist on retainer, The Game’s 2017 Edition is a gentle reminder that teamwork can be fun without needing to negotiate budgets and vacation days. It’s a microcosm of group dynamic: trust, bluff, and a shared sense of ridiculousness when the plan collapses with the elegance of a poorly made soufflé.

### Difficulty, Depth, and Replayability

Is it hard? Yes. Is it unfair? Only if you pile on too much blame after a streak of poor discards. The Game is not about hand optimization or perfect memory; it’s about how quickly your group can adapt to changing information and whether you’ll gamble on a risky discard when victory is within a single card’s reach.

The 2017 Edition adds a handful of new cards and optional rules that can stretch replayability without tipping the balance into “impossibly hard.” If you’re playing with newcomers, keep the twists off until they’re comfortable with the baseline. If you’re playing with vets who want maximum chaos, flip on a couple of the event cards and watch as the table discovers new strategies you didn’t know existed for placing a single card on the correct pile.

Replayability is high, because you can mix and match twists, scale the number of players, and experiment with different starting hands. The core satisfaction remains intact: the moment when the last card falls into place, and everyone breathes a tiny, triumphant sigh together. It’s perfectly emblematic of why humans enjoy puzzles with friends—sharing the small victories while pretending you had a flawless plan all along.

### Artwork and Theme

The Game doesn’t pretend to be a thematic adventure. It’s a puzzle wrapped in a clean, modern aesthetic. The 2017 Edition nudges the art direction toward clearer symbols and a more cohesive feel across color palettes. It’s not going to win any “best storytelling” awards, but it doesn’t need to. The theme lives in the interaction: the “two-pile” system, the escalating tension as the piles approach the ceiling, and the sly humor when someone misreads a color cue and triggers a domino effect of nearly-sane decisions.

If you’re a fan of thematic overlap, you can still use this game as a study in group psychology. The minimalistic design leaves room for your own riffs—your table can invent a whole sub-narrative about “the Great Card Heist at Table 7,” and you’ll be the mastermind who pulled it off (or burned it down, depending on the roll of the dice and the mood of the room).

### Accessibility and Audience

The Game is surprisingly accessible. It’s not a time sink in the way many modern co-ops are, and it scales nicely for players who are new to tabletop gaming. People who enjoy quick, clever puzzles will adore it; folks who want a long, heavy, narrative-driven experience might prefer something with a longer arc. The 2017 Edition is a great entry point for casual players who want something less intimidating than a heavy Euro or a sprawling legacy game.

If you’re shopping for a gift for a friend who’s new to board games but loves mind-bending puzzles, The Game: The 2017 Edition is a thoughtful choice. It’s compact, relatively affordable, and endlessly re-playable in the best possible sense: you’ll want to try again because you know you can do it better next time.

### Comparisons: 2017 Edition vs. Earlier Iterations

Compared to the original edition, the 2017 Edition is a subtle but meaningful upgrade. The rule clarifications reduce common points of friction that can stall a round, and the art refresh makes the physical experience more satisfying without changing the core rules that define The Game’s identity.

If you’ve played the 2016 or earlier editions, you’ll notice: fewer moments of “wait, what color was that again?” and more moments of “we’ve got this—just trust the process.” For players who loved the original, the 2017 Edition offers a reason to revisit with the promise of smoother sessions and a slightly sharper challenge curve.

If you’re coming fresh, the 2017 Edition is a forgiving entry point with enough bite to keep you engaged. It’s a good middle ground between casual party games and more intense strategy puzzles—enough to feel like you’ve earned a geeky badge without requiring a cheat sheet the size of a small novel.

The Game: The 2017 Edition is a worthy update to a beloved minimal masterpiece.
