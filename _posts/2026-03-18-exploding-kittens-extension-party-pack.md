## Setup and Play Flow with the Extension
Setting up the game with the extension is not rocket science, but it is a little more involved than the base version. The good news is that the rule sheet is fairly clear about mixing the new cards with the old deck, and it includes examples that look like tiny cartoon diagrams drawn by a cat that just learned how to sit up on its hind legs. Quick steps to get you playing:
1) Shuffle the base deck with the extension deck in a recommended ratio for the number of players.
2) Deal cards as usual, but keep in mind that the new cards may require you to rethink defuse distribution.
3) Set up any party-mode modifiers according to your table size and vibe.
4) Draw your starting hands and prepare to yell defuse just in case a bomb cat sneaks into the pile.

The turn sequence remains the familiar weave of draw, play, pass, and defuse, but with the extension you’ll notice some actions that encourage team-style misadventure or sneaky techie plays. The key to mastering this kit is recognizing the edges of chaos and learning to ride it rather than letting it ride you.

### Card Types You’ll Encounter
- Cat cards: The meme engines of the game. Collecting pairs and mimicking rhyming exchanges can yield playful advantages or trigger mischief outside the standard rules.
- New action cards: These swap, steal, or manipulate cards in ways that keep players guessing and the groans audible.
- Trap and defuse grown-ups: The defuse cards now come in variants that spice up the risk management portion of the game.

### Strategy and Social Dynamics
The Extension Party Pack thrives on the social energy of a full table. In a six to nine-player game, the room tends to be loud, the table becomes a negotiation center, and the moral calculus of who is allowed to live (and who gets exploded) gets prodded with witty banter. Here are practical tips:
- Don’t fear the new defuse options; use them to set up future turns and pass catastrophic risk to the next player.
- In groups, the presence of additional card types reduces the likelihood of quick game endings, which is a feature for social hours but a challenge for players who hate a cliffhanger ending.
- The cat cards are not just cute puns; they encourage players to slow down and interact more, which adds to the laughter even if your strategy is hilariously terrible.

## Component Quality and Durability
A good extension lives and dies by its components. If your cards chip and your tokens fold easily, you’ll be less forgiving of tiny design decisions. On the other hand, if the stock feels solid and the art remains legible, you’ll be inclined to pull this out longer than your usual rotation of games. In this pack:
- Card stock is middle-of-the-road robust, similar to base set levels, which is fine for party nights but not ideal for heavy-shuffle champions.
- The tokens are tactile enough to pass around without turning into confetti.
- The rule sheet is concise and readable; this matters during a chaotic night when someone asks how does this work again and you respond with a confident shrug.
