## How to play the game and what to expect on the table
Skip-Bo is a shedding game at heart. The goal is to be the first to play all of your stock cards by building up into piles of 1 through 12, using a hand of face-down draw cards, and employing wild cards to bridge gaps. It feels like a brisk math exercise that rewards memory, sequencing, and a dash of luck.

### Setup and learning curve
Setting up is simple: shuffle the stock piles, deal them face down to each player, and give everyone a hand of five or so cards depending on house rules. The rest of the deck forms the draw pile. The game rotates quickly; you will be counting up to 12 with every turn, and that’s the kind of cognitive workout almost anyone can enjoy after a long day at work or school. The learning curve is gentle. If you can count to 12 and recognize a wild card when you see it, you’re halfway there.

### Turn structure and strategy tips
On your turn you draw cards, play from your hand to the discard piles or the central build piles, and attempt to play as many cards as possible. The strategic twist is deciding when to unleash a sequence, when to hold back, and how to time your wild cards to block opponents at the most critical moment. A savvy player will watch the table for signs of which numbers are likely to come next and whether someone is about to reach a critical milestone. The joy of Skip-Bo comes from small, satisfying decisions that accumulate into a victory that feels earned, not handed to you by luck alone.

A few practical tips:
- Always keep at least one card in hand when possible; running dry in a crucial moment is the fastest route to trouble.
- Use wild cards to fill in gaps that would otherwise stall a big run, but don’t waste a wild card on a minor improvement if a normal card will do.
- Observe opponents’ stock piles and infer what numbers they’re likely to draw next; this helps you time your plays to block or pressure them effectively.
