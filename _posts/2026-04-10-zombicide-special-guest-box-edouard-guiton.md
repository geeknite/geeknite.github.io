## Unboxing and first impressions

Opening this expansion is a little like opening a mystery gift from a friend who knows your taste and also loves gruesome puns. The packaging is decorated with Edouard Guiton’s stylized grin—part cartoon villain, part Renaissance painting. If you’re into designer mini vibes, you’ll appreciate the craftsmanship here. The plastic miniatures are crisp, with clean lines and a touch of that CMON attention to base detail that makes the rest of your terrain look almost underdressed by comparison.

Inside you’ll find a handful of miniatures (Edouard included, of course), a couple of new tiles, a handful of mission cards, and a handful more tokens than you probably want to admit you own at 2 a.m. on a Tuesday. The instruction sheet is concise, though not short on personality. It tells you exactly how this guest box slots into the core game, which is crucial because the last thing you want is to discover you’ve mixed a rule from a non-existent edition into your solo game while your dog watches in judgment.

The high-level takeaway: this isn’t a “game changer” in the sense of doubling your play area or flipping the entire meta upside down. It’s more of a spice rack: a few extra ingredients that can make certain sessions sing, or at least moo in a fun, awkward way.

### The physical components
- Edouard Guiton miniature: a gleefully grim little fellow with a fondness for chaos and a respectable beard game. The sculpt is expressive—perfect for painting or leaving as-is for that “heroic zombie disruption” vibe.
- A set of zombie figures and faction tokens that fit the normal Zombicide vibe: grim, goofy, and capable of tipping a game into hilarious disaster if you roll the wrong numbers.

If you’re curious whether Edouard’s presence adds to the “wow” factor, the answer is a definite yes, with a wink. It’s not merely “a new figure” but a little character-driven addition that nudges the tone toward wilder, more comedic misadventure as you battle the undead for the day’s coffee sachet and survival.


## How it fits within the Zombicide universe

Zombicide is a living, coughing, occasionally glamorous system. It’s designed for cooperative play where players work together to survive zombie onslaughts, scavenging, fighting, and planning. The Special Guest Box series is CMON’s way of introducing a quirky, memorable figure into the mix—someone who adds a distinct personality to your table, a character that can influence player behavior and decision-making beyond just the stat line.

Edouard Guiton, as a guest, brings a few thematic hooks:
- A chef of chaos: his background implies improvisation and surprising reactions under pressure.
- A style-forward antagonist of the undead: the swagger of his poses and sculpts nudges players toward a more cinematic, less sterile play experience.
- Narrative potential: you can weave little micro-stories around his actions that enhance the co-op experience without derailing the core mechanics.

For players who enjoy the “guest star” concept in games, this box delivers that in spades—without turning Zombicide into a game of cosplay. It’s a flavorful add-on that respects the base game’s rhythm while offering a few fresh beats to rhythmically disrupt your party’s plan.


## The minis and figure design: a closer look

If you’re the kind of gamer who paints minis for a habit rather than a hobby, Edouard’s sculpt is forgiving. The details hold up under a standard primer coat and won’t demand heroic layers of patience to look good on the table. The pose captures a balance between villainy and charm—an expression that makes you wonder whether he’s about to heal a zombie with a fond memory or set up a trap with a smile.

The other figures included in the box are less showy than Edouard but still solid additions to your hoard. They complement familiar factions, expanding your roster without overcrowding your space or requiring a nerdy strategy spreadsheet to remember who belongs to which side.

If you’re an alt-paint enthusiast, you’ll find plenty of piecework for shading, dry brushing, and edge highlighting. If you’re more of a “spray-and-pray” painter, you’ll still end up with a tabletop-level result that looks good on camera and in the group chat when you post your latest chaos montage.


## Rules, balance, and gameplay impact

In terms of mechanics, this expansion slots into the standard Zombicide flow: you have your survivor team, you have zombies, you have board tiles, you roll dice, you collect gear, you reach objectives, you scream at the night, and you hope your coffee is strong enough to power through it all. The Special Guest Box modifies this through a few new mission cards and possibly a special rule or two associated with Edouard’s presence. Those rules aren’t game-breaking; they’re designed to be a little bump to keep sessions fresh rather than an overhaul that would ruin beginners’ days.

From a balance perspective, the box leans toward “fun first, power spike second.” It’s rare that something so clearly adds chaos ends up feeling unfair; most of the time, it feels like a clever way to reward risk-taking during mid-game. That said, if your group is extremely optimization-focused or if you’re playing a highly competitive variant, you’ll want to agree on how this guest interacts with existing modifiers and weapon caches. It’s not a trap, but it can influence how aggressively you push into a spotted spawn area.

For solo players, the box remains a good spice: you’ll get a more characterful playthrough with Edouard’s presence—more atmosphere and more dramatic missteps to narrate in your head as you flip a tile and reveal a zombie horde you didn’t fairly anticipate.

## Accessibility and suitability for different players

If you’re new to Zombicide, this box can feel a bit intimidating at first glance because it adds more components and a few new rules. Take it slow: start with the base game’s standard setup, then drop in Edouard’s mission cards as a gentle extension. The more you play, the more you’ll discover how fluid these boxes can be. The guest box is particularly friendly to groups who enjoy scenario variety but don’t want to dive into heavy rulebooks every session.

For veteran players, Edouard Guiton offers an enjoyable thematic break from the usual cadence. It’s a good way to juice mid-game tension and to introduce a few “aha” moments that aren’t about brute force or dice luck but narrative momentum and character interplay.


## A look at replayability and long-term value

Zombicide as a system is designed for replayability, and this expansion compounds that effect. The new mission cards provide alternative routes to victory, so you’re not forced into the same sequence of moves every night. The variety of maps and the presence of a guest character keeps the play experience fresh across multiple sessions. If you’re the kind of player who limited edition releases to “collectors’ items,” you’ll likely appreciate Edouard’s unique personality as a living, breathing part of your table’s story arc.

That said, if your gaming group only plays once in a blue moon, this box might feel like a shiny distraction rather than a practical upgrade. If you’re a weekly group, you’ll likely enjoy every session more, thanks to Edouard’s presence and the new scenarios that pepper your table with fresh misdirection.


## Troubleshooting and tips for a smoother session

- Start with one new mission card per session to avoid overwhelming new players. Ease into Edouard’s rules and see how the table responds.
- Use the included tokens to track special effects. They’re designed for readability and quick reference, but you still want to keep a loose note on which effects are active during the round so you don’t lose track in the heat of the moment.
- Consider a pre-game painting session if you want the Edouard miniature to shine. A little color here and there goes a long way to boosting table presence and player enthusiasm.
- If you’re streaming your game night, the Edouard figure makes for a great centerpiece and a talking point that can help maintain viewer engagement during zombie-fuelled chaos.
