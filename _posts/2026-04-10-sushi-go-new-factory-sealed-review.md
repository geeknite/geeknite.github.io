## How to play in a sprint: the quick start guide

Sushi Go! is a 2-5 player card drafting game with quick rounds and easy teachability. You pass cards around the table, pick one, and pass the rest. The aim is to assemble sets of sushi that score points in quirky ways. The mechanics are tiny but elegant, like a perfectly rolled tuna maki with the subtle complexity of a Randy Newman guitar riff in the background.

### Setup and turn structure

- Shuffle the deck. Deal a hand to each player according to the player count.
- Everyone picks a card from their hand and places it face down in front of them.
- All players reveal their chosen card.
- The remaining cards are passed to the left (or right, depending on the round). This continues until all cards are taken.
- Players tally points from the collected cards. The next round begins with a fresh set of cards from the main deck, and the passing direction flips for variety.

The cadence of a round is short and satisfying. You get the rush of decision making, the relief when you spot a plump maki set that could break the jam, and the comedic timing of when someone discovers that a pile of wasabi cards will doom their curry-colored dreams. The base game typically plays in about 15 minutes, which makes it perfect for a casual night or a quick lunch break where you pretend to be a responsible adult who actually eats with chopsticks.

### Card types and scoring vibes

- Nigiri: Simple point cards you want to collect, often paired with wasabi for a spicy interaction.
- Maki: Sets that score for having the most; a classic risk-reward mechanic that makes you sweat in the most adorable way.
- Tempura and Sashimi: Larger point boons that reward multi-card collection. They require patience and a little planning, which may be at odds with the snack table’s siren call.
- Wasabi: A multiplier that rewards careful timing and a dash of luck when you manage to pair it with a high-value nigiri.

The scoring system is straightforward enough for newcomers, but it still offers opportunities for bluffing, misdirection, and strategic misreads that can make repeat play feel fresh. The novelty of the scoring variants, especially when you attempt to optimize tiny combos over multiple rounds, can lead to a surprisingly satisfying sense of mastery without devolving into hyper-competitive math nerdery.

## Strategy thoughts: optimizing your tiny sushi empire

Sushi Go! is not a heavy strategy game, but there are subtle plays you can master to maximize your points.

- Read the room: Quick pattern recognition goes a long way. If you notice that the table keeps chasing maki or sashimi, you can pivot to different card families to maximize your own gains.
- Timing is everything: The trick with wasabi is that its power is realized when you combine it with a high-value nigiri. Don’t squander it on a low-scoring card unless it blocks a competitor’s plan.
- Don’t overcommit to a single set: It is tempting to chase a big combo, but sometimes a balanced spread across multiple categories yields more predictable scores.
- Pay attention to opponents’ nose for sushi crates: If you can infer what someone values, you can strategically pass early cards that help them and hurt them at the same time. It’s like chess with chopsticks and a slightly more fun atmosphere.

These ideas become more meaningful as you move from a fresh factory sealed experience into more seasoned gameplay. The beauty of Sushi Go! is that you can implement sophisticated tactics without sacrificing the light, fast tempo that makes it so addictive in the first place.
