Installation and fit
---------------------

If you’re upgrading from a blunt, budget-friendly gear to something like the Axon DTS 64P, expect a more precise fit as you complete your first test. The teeth establish themselves in a way that feels deliberate rather than amorphous—like a well-choreographed heist scene where everyone knows their exact role. You’ll want to verify center-to-center distance with a caliper, check for axial alignment, and ensure the pinion geometry matches the 64P tooth count. The more exact your setup, the crisper the drive becomes. The 64P’s finer teeth can be more sensitive to misalignment than coarse-pitch gears, but when everything is lined up, they reward you with smooth engagement and a quiet operation that feels almost ceremonial.

One practical tip: if you’re using a smaller RC chassis with limited clearance, consider a shim kit to optimize the spacing between spur and pinion. It’s not glamorous, but it’s the kind of thing that saves you from a late-night mystery rattle that only appears when you’re two meters away from a habeas of solder smoke. A small amount of assembly grease or a light synthetic lubricant can help with initial break-in, but avoid over-lubricating to the point where the teeth start to do a wet-wok dance in your gearbox. A little goes a long way, especially with 64P teeth that might trap extra grease in the root fillets if you’re not careful.

Maintenance and care
---------------------

Maintenance is not glamorous, but it is essential. The Axon DTS 64P spur gear benefits from periodic inspection for tooth wear, burrs, and signs of misalignment. Use a magnifying glass if necessary; you’d be surprised how much you can catch at a tiny scale when you squint enough. Clean any surface dust that has managed to accumulate in the root creases. If there’s any sign of pitting or micro-cracking on the tooth profile, it’s worth investigating whether the assembly tolerances are within spec or if there’s a more fundamental issue with how the motor and gearbox are configured.

Lubrication matters, but not in the way most hobbyists initially think. A light film on the gear teeth reduces heat, reduces wear on contact surfaces, and helps keep the noise profile predictable. Periodically re-check the lubrication level because dried lubricant can cause more friction than a well-coated surface. If you’re running at higher speeds or in a hotter environment, you may consider a more robust lubricant with higher viscosity; just remember to re-check for any changes in backlash—over-lubrication can create hydraulic effects that push the backlash into uncomfortable territory.
