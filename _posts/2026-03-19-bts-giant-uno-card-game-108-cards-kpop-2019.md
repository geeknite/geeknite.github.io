## How to Play With Giant Cards

UNO play is simple: match color or number, play a wild card to switch it up, and draw cards when you hit a penalty. The giant format adds a few practical twists. First, visibility is improved for everyone at the table, which is a rare win in the world of family game nights where someone might have a vision problem but refuses to sit closer. Second, the sheer size of a few cards means you might accidentally slam the table with a triumphant shout when you declare UNO; this is exactly the vibe the BTS edition seems designed to encourage. 

Setup is straightforward: shuffle the oversized deck, deal seven cards to each player, place the remaining deck face down to form the draw pile, and flip the first card to start the discard pile. The standard UNO rules apply, with the potential for unique house rules that emerge whenever a large group of fans are involved. The extra surface area of the cards adds a theatrical element to chivalrous card laying, as players can do dramatic passes and pretend to conduct a concert within their own living room. 

For the younger players, the game can be quickly explained, though you may need to physically demonstrate how to hold multiple giant cards. Adults may enjoy the added theatrics and the fact that the giant cards are often easier to swat away from cat tails and stray elbows during a chaotic round. If your space is at risk of becoming a stage set, consider a soft surface or a sturdy table with a leg that won t mind a few card battles between songs.
