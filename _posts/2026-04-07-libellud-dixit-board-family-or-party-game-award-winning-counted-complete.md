## What is Dixit?
Dixit is a storytelling card game designed around evocative artwork and social deduction. Each round pits a narrator against a jury of players who attempt to guess which card best matches the clue given. The draw of the game lies in balancing clue craft: you want a hint that is specific enough to evoke your card but not so obvious that everyone will pick it. When all players vote, the number of points earned comes down to who guesses what card was the storyteller using, and who enjoyed the humor and imagination the most.

The core loop is simple, but the results can be wildly uneven in the best possible way. One round you may spin a clue that sails over every head like a kite in a windstorm; the next round you’ll nail exactly what someone had in mind and all players cheer with recognition. The art on the 84 large imagery cards is the star here, a kaleidoscope of styles from painterly to surreal to a dash of comic-book whimsy. The board and tokens are functional rather than flashy, which is exactly the vibe Dixit benefits from: let the pictures do the talking.

For families and groups that appreciate a party game that rewards imagination rather than reaction time, Dixit shines. It’s not a game of who is fastest or who is loudest; it is a game where you win by being merely a little bit poetic and a lot of a good guesser.

## Components and setup: what you actually get
Dixit base comes with a sturdy, art-rich deck of 84 cards, a set of voting tokens, an adorable little scoreboard, and a handful of wooden components to track players and scores. A typical setup looks like this:

- Each player receives a hand of six cards.
- A storyteller is chosen to begin the round. They select one card from their hand and give a clue in any form that relates to that card. The clue can be a phrase, a sound, a snippet of a memory, or even a riddle – the sky is the limit as long as you can justify it to the table.
- The storyteller places their selected card face down, while the rest of the players pick one card from their own hands that best matches the clue they heard, then pass their chosen card face down.
- All chosen cards are revealed simultaneously. The storyteller shuffles the cards and places them in a row, each associated with a vote token. Players vote on which card they think is the storyteller’s.
- Scoring happens after votes are tallied. The storyteller and the guessers earn points based on whether the card was guessed by some but not all players; other players earn points for correctly guessing the storyteller’s card.
- A new round begins with a new storyteller and reshuffled hands, until someone hits the target score or the deck runs out.

Visuals matter in Dixit, and the icons are cleanly designed to be legible even in a noisy room. The simplicity of setup means you can drop into a game night with little friction: you don’t need a math degree to play, you don’t need a curated playlist to feel festive, you just need some friends who aren’t afraid to pretend they are the narrator of a dream they just lived.

## The storytelling mechanic that counts
One of the most interesting facets of Dixit is its scoring logic, which we can loosely describe as a social counting system. The storyteller earns points when at least one person guesses their card but not everyone. If the clue is too obvious and everyone guesses the card, the storyteller gets nothing. If no one guesses the card, the storyteller also gets nothing. Meanwhile, players who pick the storyteller’s card and those who guess correctly earn points. The balancing act between making a clue that is evocative enough to resonate but obscure enough to exclude some players is the heart of the game’s design and its replays.

This balancing act also means Dixit challenges players to think in abstract, associative terms. You aren’t telling a story in the conventional sense with a beginning, middle, and end. You’re crafting a key that will unlock a shared mental image among a group of people who each bring their own experiences. The result is a social lubricant that can help friends feel more connected or in some cases, reveal how differently people perceive the same artwork. Either way, it is a fascinating exercise in communication and imagination.

## How to play: a quick refresher
If you haven’t played Dixit before, here is the quick version in a few steps:

1) The storyteller looks at their hand of six cards and selects one. They give a clue that relates to that card. The clue can be a word, a phrase, a sentence, a sound, or even a riddle. It should evoke the card but not give it away completely.
2) Each other player selects a card from their hand that best matches the clue and places it face down. All chosen cards are shuffled with the storyteller’s card and revealed.
3) Players secretly vote on which card they think is the storyteller’s card, using their voting tokens. No one can vote for their own card.
4) After votes are revealed, scoring happens. The storyteller earns points if some but not all players guess their card. Voters who picked the storyteller’s card gain points, and players who guessed a card that wasn’t the storyteller’s may gain points as well if their chosen card was guessed by others.
5) The next round designates a new storyteller (usually clockwise), and everyone draws back up to six cards. Play continues until someone reaches the target score or the deck is exhausted.

The rules are intentionally light, and the flow is designed to keep moving. The real magic happens in the conversations that follow each round and the lingering sense of shared creativity as the table mutters about why that particular card felt right for that clue.

## Strategy, luck, and the art of storytelling
Like most party games that rely on social interpretation, Dixit balances luck and skill. The luck comes from the draw: you don’t control which cards come to you, which can force you to improvise. The skill comes from reading the table, calibrating your clues, and nudging the group toward a card that feels just right but not obvious. Over time, you’ll start to notice patterns: players who gravitate toward poetic clues, players who lean into obvious cues, players who love dark humor, players who are terrible at art-based metaphors, and so on.

If you’re a storyteller, your success often hinges on how well you know your table. A clue that stuns your best friend who loves abstract art can be a winner, while the same clue might crash and burn with a family member who prefers literal descriptions. The balance is part of the charm: it’s a social experiment in a box, where the experiment is really about how you talk to each other through pictures.

If you’re a voter, your job is to interpret the storyteller’s intent through the lens of your own association with the card. It’s less about naming the exact card and more about aligning your intuition with the storyteller’s vibe. The satisfaction comes when you guess correctly and realize your table’s sense of humor or memory alignment was shared, even if you didn’t choose the exact card the storyteller had in mind.

## Tips for hosts and players
- Start with a few simple clues to warm up the table. You’ll learn the group’s humor and references quickly.
- Consider a time limit for clues. A short timer keeps rounds moving and reduces awkward pauses.
- Use expansions strategically. Start with the base game, then grow as your group enjoys the vibe. If you add too many cards too fast, you may lose the feeling of intimacy that makes Dixit special.
- When you’re the storyteller, mirror your clue to the table’s shared experiences. A good clue should feel personal yet not overly specific to a single player.
- Encourage players to discuss their reasoning after rounds. The talking is part of the fun and helps everyone feel included even if they didn’t win.

## The value proposition: is Dixit worth the space on your shelf
Dixit is not just a game; it is a social experiment disguised as a party game. If your goal is to foster conversation and laughter, if you want a shared visual language that grows with your group, if you want to invite players who say they do not like games but enjoy storytelling, Dixit is a strong candidate.
