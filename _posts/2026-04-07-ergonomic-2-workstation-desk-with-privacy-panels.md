## Assembly and setup: two humans, a handful of screws, infinite possibilities
The unboxing experience was pleasant enough: the packaging is designed to minimize fear, with clear labeling and the always-satisfying “easy-start” screws. During assembly, we found the following tips helpful:
- Start with the central spine or main frame and build outward. This reduces the number of times you have to re-align sections.
- Keep a small tray handy for screws and nuts. It’s easier than chasing tiny metal pieces across the floor like an amateur archaeologist.
- Have a buddy assist with alignment; two hands are better than one when you’re lining up desktop panels with the frame.
- Don’t overtighten the first pass; you may need to re-adjust after you attach the panels and realize the desk wants to be a tad taller or shorter.

Even with two people assembling, the process took roughly 60–90 minutes, depending on your level of ritualistic pre-assembly breathing and your ability to interpret the tool labels without decoding them into a new language. ## Ergonomics in action: two stations, one healthy posture
### Monitor positioning and seating posture
Each workstation supports the standard modern layout: monitor at eye level, roughly 20–28 inches from your face (depending on screen size and your eyesight). The two-user design allows you to tailor monitor heights individually, which is especially helpful if one person prefers a slightly higher monitor due to their chair height or personal preference. A monitor arm on each side will maximize adjustability and reduce neck strain; if you’re a minimalist, you can rely on the desk’s own surface and base-level stand options.

A quick ergonomic reminder: keep wrists straight while typing, elbows at roughly 90 degrees, and feet flat on the floor or on a footrest. The two-workstation layout makes it easy to toggle between different seating heights if one person likes to stand occasionally during a long coding sprint. If you’re into standing desks, you’ll be delighted to know the height range accommodates most standard standing configurations.

### Chair compatibility and sit-stand synergy
We tested with two typical office chairs, neither of which were named after a famous celebrity. The desk’s two-sides mean you can pair two different chairs if your office policy requires one tall and one short, or if you’re sharing with a coworker who insists on the exact opposite lumbar support. The key is to verify your chair’s base clearance and the desk’s leg extents so you don’t collide with the panels while moving in and out. At minimum, you’ll want 2–3 inches of clearance between chair arms and the privacy panels on each side for smooth swiveling.

### Keyboard tray and input devices
There’s space for a keyboard tray on each side if you want to keep the main desk surface uncluttered. We found the tray option handy for ergonomics testing because it allowed us to drop the wrists to an optimal angle without crowding the monitor area. If you’re not into trays, you can still place a keyboard directly on the desk, but you might want to consider a shallow desk mat to aid wrist comfort during long sessions.

### Privacy panels: more than looks
The visual barrier helps with focus but also reduces temptation to peek at your neighbor’s screen (not that we’d encourage such behavior in a professional setting). Privacy panels can double as a subtle sound dampener if you’re in a noisy environment. If you need deeper acoustic privacy, consider a set of small acoustic panels or a privacy screen that attaches to the outer edge of the desk. A little extra sound dampening can turn a chatty coworking room into a zen cave of productivity.

## Setup tips and best practices
- Measure your space before ordering. A too-wide desk can feel imposing in a compact room, and a too-narrow desk can push the privacy panels into your elbows.
- Decide early who sits where to optimize cable routing and screen alignment.
- Invest in a good monitor arm per user for height and tilt adjustments; this reduces neck strain and helps you find the “just-right” angle.
- Use a consistent cable management plan (label, route, secure). Your future self will thank you when you’re doing a late-night debugging sprint without tripping over power cords.
- Consider adding a couple of desk mats or wrist rests to improve overall comfort and wrist health during long sessions.
