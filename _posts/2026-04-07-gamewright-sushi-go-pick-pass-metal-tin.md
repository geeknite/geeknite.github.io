## How to play: the pick & pass rhythm in a compact tin

### Setup

- Shuffle the deck and deal five cards to each player (for typical 3-5 players; the variant supports more with a shared center pile). Place the remainder in the center as a draw pile and flip the top card to begin the discard or scoring track depending on your house rules.
- Decide who will be the first picker. The first player picks one card from their hand, places it face-up in front of them, and passes the rest of their hand to the left. Play continues around the table until all cards are drafted.
- The round ends after all cards are played and scored; you move into the next round with a fresh hand, continuing for three rounds in most standard configurations. The final pudding scoring kicks in at game end and can swing a surprising number of points if you’ve been careful with your pudding collection.

### Turn structure

- Simultaneous selection: In each phase, all players secretly pick one card from their hand in preparation for the reveal, then all players reveal together. The chosen card goes into your score pile. The rest of your hand passes to the left, and you’re left with a new set of options for your next pick.
- Draft cycle: After each reveal, you pass again. The tempo is brisk, which is part of the game’s charm. You’re constantly evaluating: what will my neighbor take next round? Do I want to disrupt their plan or try to complete a chain of cards from my own deck? The answers arrive at a delightful pace that makes you feel clever but not suffocated by complexity.
- Scoring moments: Some cards pay off immediately when earned, others grant points at the end of a round or end of the game. Nigiri cards, for example, provide points that depend on the type; Sashimi requires a trio to unlock a big payoff; Maki tracks provide a round-based bonus; pudding tracks own the endgame, lurking quietly until their moment arrives.

### Scoring overview: what actually counts

- Nigiri: egg (1 point), salmon (2 points), squid (3 points) each round you hold those cards. Wasabi doubles the value of your next Nigiri played, which makes planning a little chess match with your own hand.
- Sashimi: three Sashimi cards score 10 points—an excellent payoff if you can align a little trio across the rounds.
- Dumplings: a delightful staircase of points (1, 3, 6, 10 for 1-4 dumplings), with bigger stacks delivering bigger smiles (and sometimes bigger jaw clench moments).
- Maki rolls: the player with the most Maki rolls (per round) receives a bonus. It’s a nice way to steal a few points from someone who is chasing a single high-value card.
- Pudding: there are pudding cards that score at the end of the game. The longer you hold onto them, the more value they might provide, turning early drafting into a late-game strategic chess match.

- Sushi Go! Party: This version expands the number of players and introduces a few new role cards and scoring options. It’s a more bustling experience that can require a bigger table. Sushi Go! ## House rules, tweaks, and house personality

- Timer rounds: If you want a more brisk session, set a 2-minute drafting timer and force a fast pass. You’ll be surprised how much you can accomplish in a couple of minutes of frantic tapping on the table and pointing at cards.
- Solo-ish play: If you’re waiting for someone to arrive and you want a quick test run, you can play a round or two against a ghost player with a simple scoring rule. It’s not the intended mode, but it’s a fun way to warm up.
- Alternate endgame: Some players like to include an extra pudding round at the very end of the game, which can swing the final score dramatically and provides a satisfying narrative for those last-minute heroes who chase pudding like treasure.
