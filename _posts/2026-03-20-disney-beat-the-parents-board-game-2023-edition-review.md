## Setup and Rules (No MBA Required)
The setup is the first kind of magic that makes this game sing: you unpack the box, lay out the boards, shuffle the cards, and assign teams. If you’re playing with multiple generations, you’ll want to split players into “Kids” and “Parents” teams, or alternatively go with “Disney Buffs” vs “Non-Buffs” if you want to avoid any sighing about age gaps.

The basic flow goes like this:

1) Draw a card and spin to determine the category. Categories might include: Movies, Theme Parks, Songs, Characters, and Behind the Scenes. Sometimes you’ll hit a “Wildcard” that changes the environment—maybe you must answer with a line from a famous villain’s song, or you must describe a ride without naming it.
2) Team answers within a time window. The time window is intentionally generous for younger players and tight for older ones; it scales with the difficulty of the card.
3) Points are awarded for correctness, with multipliers and penalties via Wild Cards. If you land on a penalty, your team may lose a turn or forfeit a potential bonus.
4) After a set number of rounds, the team with the most points wins. Simple, right? Almost. The fun comes from the way the game nudges families to argue in a playful, friendly way about who knows which character from a movie that came out in the last decade.

One little tip we discovered during testing: keep the pace brisk. If you spend too long hashing out a single question, the room’s energy dips. The best rounds are those where the kids volley questions and the parents volley sass in response, all while the toddlers refuse to nap and demand a snack break every twenty minutes.

### Strategy and Play Tips
- Pair high-velocity memory with savvy clue-giving. The teams that succeed often have a player who excels at rapid-fire recall and another who can give helpful nudges without giving away the answer.
- Don’t be afraid to skip a question with a Wild Card if you have a safer option on the board. It’s better to cash in a sure thing than risk a turn that opens a door for a comeback.
- Leverage the categories that suit your teammates best. If your group has a Disney encyclopedic friend who loves Theme Parks, let them anchor those rounds while the rest contribute quick guesses in Movies and Songs.
- Use the age difference to your advantage. If parents have spent a lot of time in the pre-digital era, leaning on older films and classic characters can create amusing misdirection that helps you stay ahead.
