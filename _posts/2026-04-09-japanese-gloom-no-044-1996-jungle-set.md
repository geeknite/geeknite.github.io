## Card Overview and Quick Facts

### Artwork, Flavor, and Theme

Gloom (No. 44) is one of those cards that makes you go, “That face should be on a poster in a garden center.” The artwork typically features Gloom’s perpetually bored, yet endearing umbrella of despair—the plant that smells so bad it might be a defensive mechanism against inquisitive kids. ### Stats and Typing

In the Jungle era, Gloom was a Grass-type with typical HP values for its time, decent for the era, and a few attacks that felt like a learning exercise in resource management. Mechanically, the card’s power was not the top of the line by today’s standards, yet it offered a nostalgic charm: a reminder that the game’s starting days were less about sticker-sheets and more about learning the rhythm of energy costs and basic strategy.

### Print Variations: Japanese vs English

The Japanese Jungle Set contains subtle differences in printing quality, color saturation, and cardstock that make some copies pop more in a binder than others. Collectors often discuss edge bevels, holo patterns, and subtle gloss. For Gloom No. 044, those differences can influence value, especially when the card is in pristine condition. If you’re hunting for a player card, you might not stress the gloss; if you’re hunting for a display-worthy piece, you’d better check centering, corner wear, and surface scratches with a magnifying glass and a coffee cup nearby.

## Authenticity, Reproductions, and How to Avoid Counterfeits

There are fakes in every corner of the internet, and Pokémon cards from the ’90s are no exception. Here are practical checks you can perform without needing a lab and a magnifying glass the size of your hopes:

- Paper stock and gloss: Japanese Jungle cards often used a different stock and gloss than English variants. If a copy looks too glossy or too matte for its era, be suspicious—but not judgmental; some printers skewed differently.
- Print quality: Look for crisp edges, sharp font, and consistent holographic or ink patterns if applicable. The No. 044 Gloom line, in particular, should have consistent coloring and a clean back.
- Weight and feel: Compare to a known authentic card. Fakes can feel off in hand. If you’re unsure, use compare-and-balance tests you’d use for vintage comics: stack two slabs and see if your sense of “this is off” wants a second opinion.
- Seals and packaging: If you’re buying sealed or boxed items, verify the wrapper and seal integrity. The Jungle era packaging can reveal signs of tampering.

If you’re unsure, consult with a trusted hobby shop or a community that specializes in early Pokémon card authentication. And for the record, never rely on a meme caption for authentication. No matter how many times it’s posted, memes don’t count as certificates of authenticity.

## Authenticity Checklists and Quick Reference

Here’s a compact, practical checklist you can use in the field (or at your favorite online listing):

1) Compare image quality to known authentic scans of Gloom No. 044 from Japanese Jungle Set print runs. Look for accurate color tones in the green palette.
2) Check card stock texture against other Jungle era cards in your possession. Subtle differences in gloss and feel can indicate a non-original stock.
3) Inspect the back for uniform color and font. Misalignment, smears, or a back with inconsistent color can be a red flag.
4) Confirm the card’s number and set symbol are stamped correctly. Inaccurate numbering or misaligned symbols are common counterfeit signals.
5) Verify the seller’s return policy and certificate of authenticity offered by the seller, especially for higher-priced cards.

If you’re unsure, consider sending clear photos to a trusted online community or consult a professional appraiser who specializes in vintage Pokémon cards. The investment in time is often worth the peace of mind—and the card, once authenticated, can sing on your shelf for decades.
