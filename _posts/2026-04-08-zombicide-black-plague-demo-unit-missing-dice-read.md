## The Demo Unit Dilemma: Missing Dice

Let’s get to the heart of the issue: the demo unit arrived with missing dice. In a game like Zombicide, dice are not a mere accessory; they are the lifeblood of combat. Without them, you’ve got a scenario where heroes clap their hands and declare “we’re turning this into a narrative battle, not a dice battle,” which is fine in a story meeting but less ideal once you’re in the heat of a zombie attack.

The missing dice create a cascade of small, annoying problems:

- You can’t resolve attacks in the standard fashion. The game demands dice results to determine hits, saves, and sometimes special effects.
- Scavenging and loot decisions become guesswork rather than calculated risk. Do you push for a high-reward loot with uncertain odds, or do you hunker in place hoping for a safer outcome? In a sense, the dice are your moral compass.
- The game can stall as players wait for replacements or improvise. And improvisation is wonderful in an art project, not when you’re streaming a demo session and your viewers are counting on you to show off the full experience.

I contacted CMON to report the missing dice, and I was prepared for either a dry “we’ll ship them out” response or a finger-tipped grinding of the gears like an archaeological dig through a stony bureaucracy. What I got was the human version of a zombie: responsive, friendly, and a little exasperated by the QA process. The replacement dice arrived faster than a zombie sprint, and the whole process turned into a mini-quest of its own: a diplomatic negotiation with a tiny pile of replacement bits. It wasn’t dramatic, but it felt like the product team was listening, which is a big win in my book.

For readers who might encounter the same issue, here’s a practical mini-guide:

- Document everything. Take photos of the missing pieces and the packaging to show the issue clearly.
- Contact the vendor or CMON through their official channels. Polite, precise messages tend to get faster results than “hey, where are my dice?” with all the melodrama of a late-night gaming tournament.
- Keep the rest of the components organized. It’s easy to misplace a card or a tile when you’re dealing with a partial setup.
- If you’re in a pinch, use a neutral dice substitute for a session. It will be rough around the edges, but it keeps the game moving and your show on the road.

The good news: even with missing dice, you can still glean a lot about the system. The design intent remains intact, and you can riff on the missing piece during the narrative. It’s a reminder that the joy of a board game lies not merely in pristine components but in how you adapt when things go a little sideways.

## Solving the Missing Dice Problem: Workarounds and Workflows

The best part about playing board games in a modern era is that a missing component is not the end of the world. It’s a problem that demands improvisation, collaboration, and a little bit of nerdy ingenuity. Here are practical ways to keep your game night moving forward when the dice vanish into the ether.

- Use alternative dice: If you have a standard D6 set, you can translate outcomes using a simple mapping (e.g., 1–2 miss, 3–4 hit, 5–6 critical). It’s not a perfect substitution, but it keeps the flow intact for the purpose of demonstration.
- Create a paper-die substitute: A quick, in-session “die” can be a 6-sided die with different face icons that you draw on the spot. The ritual is silly, but it creates a moment of shared laughter and keeps things moving.
- Reroll rules-on the-fly: For demonstration sessions, you can simplify combat by allowing players to roll and narrate results for the first few turns. It’s a storytelling approach that can still capture the feel of the game while you await replacement parts.
- Use prompts for weapons effects: If a weapon has special effects that require dice, you can create a list of pre-determined outcomes that the group agrees on. It’s not the canonical experience, but it’s a fair compromise for demo nights.
- Contacting CMON for replacements: The best long-term solution is, of course, to reach out to the publisher. In my experience, the response was reasonable and prompt. They ask for the standard information: order number, batch code, and the list of missing items. If you’re a reviewer or content creator, you can request a direct replacement kit that includes dice and perhaps a “demo pack” with extra tokens to ensure smooth livestreams.

If you’re buying this game for a store, an event space, or a gaming club, I’d recommend stocking a few spare dice sets (both standard and themed variants) to prevent future demo-night calamities. The peace of mind that comes with reliable components is measurable in the number of smiles per hour at your table.
