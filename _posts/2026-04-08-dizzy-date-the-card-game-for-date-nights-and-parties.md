## How to play: the bite-sized rules that actually work
Dizzy Date is meant to be approachable, so you’ll see three core stages in most rounds: draw, perform, and resolve. The game is designed so you can seamlessly switch gears from a chill couch vibe to a chaotic party moment without requiring a full boardroom briefing.

### Setup and player order
- Gather players around a table or a comfy living room floor. The game accommodates couples nights and larger groups, but you should keep it manageable—ideally 4 to 8 players for a balanced dynamic. If your group contains a lot of competitive folks, consider designating a neutral moderator who won’t hijack every challenge for the glory of their own ego.
- Decide on a starting player. This is the role that, in a future sequel, will inevitably be played by a cat who dares to walk across the table mid-round.
- Shuffle the card decks (Action, Dare, Twist). Place them in a central spot. If you want to feel extra fancy, you can set up a little “rug-pull” area where Twist Cards fade into the audience with dramatic flair.

### The turn flow
1) Draw phase: The active player draws an Action Card (or perhaps a Dare Card, depending on your house rules) and reveals it.
2) Perform phase: The active player must complete the card prompt. Depending on the card, this could involve singing a snippet of a favorite song, answering a goofy “would you rather” question, or performing a short improv scene with another player.
3) Resolution phase: Other players vote on whether the action was completed satisfactorily or spectacularly. In Dizzy Date fashion, you’ll often hear playful judgments like, It was bold, It was chaotic, It was oddly romantic, or It occurred in a suspiciously cookie-crumbed manner.

The scoring is intentionally light: you earn points based on creativity, courage, and audience appeal. The bar for “success” on many prompts is effectively set by the players themselves, which is where the real magic lives. If you’re not worried about being judged by your friends (and your relationship), you’ll find a lot of room to riff and improvise without needing a syllabus in social dynamics.

### Card types: what prompts actually look like
- Action Cards push you toward quick, physical, or silly tasks. Think “do a ridiculous dance for 20 seconds” or “invent a new handshake with your neighbor.”
- Dare Cards are a bit more nerve-wracking. They might require you to reveal an embarrassing moment from your past, or to propose a goofy date idea that risks immediate disaster but results in fantastic stories.
- Twist Cards flip the table in some way—maybe you’re forced to swap seats or everyone must speak in a pirate accent for a round. It’s the spice that prevents the game from becoming predictable.

Pro tip for new players: embrace the prompts you dread. The beauty of Dizzy Date is that the fear you feel is almost always funny when you’re at the table with friends who are equally invested in avoiding serious life decisions and instead choosing to tell a ludicrous story about their last vacation that involved an unplanned goat mascot.

### How rounds stack up across groups
In small couples’ settings, rounds tend to lean into affection, goofy flirting, and shared storytelling. In larger groups, you’ll get a festival of personalities colliding in a beautiful mess of punchlines, accidental improvisations, and the occasional philosophical debate about whether a given task constitutes a fulfilling connection or a perfectly reasonable excuse for someone to dodge the dishes.

If you want more adult-oriented content, you can adopt a house rule or two to tailor the prompts toward your group’s comfort level. The important part is to keep the vibe fun, not terrifying.

## How to host a Dizzy Date night that works for everyone
If you want to maximize the odds of an unforgettable night, here are some practical tips:

- Set expectations: tell participants it’s okay to be silly, to fail, and to celebrate other people’s successes in the silliest possible way.
- Create a comfortable space: a well-lit, breathable room with enough seating and surfaces to place cards and snacks without cramping anyone’s style.
- Have a loose structure: assign a moderator, set a rough time limit for rounds, and allow for a couple of “light rounds” to keep the energy up.
- Prepare backups: a few backup prompts, a couple of icebreakers, and a playlist that evolves with the night.
- Respect boundaries: if a prompt feels too personal or makes someone visibly uncomfortable, empower players to swap in a gentler prompt or opt out with a laugh and a high-five.

When you run Dizzy Date well, it feels less like “a game” and more like a shared improv session where everyone has a moment in the spotlight and the group cheers rather than judges.
