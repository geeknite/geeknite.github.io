### What exactly is Codenames?
Codenames is a two-team word game where one player from each team acts as the Spymaster. The Spymasters know which words on the board belong to their team and which word belongs to the rival team, to the mysterious assassin, or to nothing at all. The rest of the team acts as operatives who try to guess the words based on one-word clues given by the Spymaster. The catch? The clue can point to multiple words, but you must avoid the assassin word at all costs. It’s like playing a high-stakes game of “telephone” where the only thing you’re allowed to say is one word reveals a lot, and the consequences are potentially deadly (metaphorically, of course—your dinner party won’t actually explode if someone guesses the wrong card, but tension might resemble a cinematic thriller).

In the English edition, the word cards are a bilingual-friendly treat: crisp English words with vibes ranging from the whimsical to the ominously generic. If you’re playing with folks who speak fluent puns, this box becomes a playground of associative gymnastics. The game’s core principle remains delightfully simple: one clue, one word, and a number that tells your team how many cards to chase. The simplicity is why it works so well in casual settings and why it scales up to the rambunctious chaos of a full party night.

## How to play (quick refresher)
Codenames is played in rounds of around 15–25 minutes, depending on how much you enjoy negotiating with your own brain while pretending that your friends aren’t about to ruin your day with a potentially catastrophic guess. The rules, in a nutshell:
- The Spymaster draws the grid and picks a color (red or blue) to indicate which team’s agents they’ll guide. The other team will follow the same process on their own green-screened stage.
- The Spymaster gives a one-word clue and a number. The clue links as many of their words on the board as possible, up to that number. For example, if the clue is “Ocean 3,” and three of your team’s words are related to water, you point to them in sequence. The number tells your teammates how many cards to target; you’re trying to maximize hits while avoiding the assassin or the other team’s words.
- Teammates discuss—but do not reveal—noises of agreement, and then make selections. If they hit a word of their own color, they continue; if they hit the opponent’s word, that team’s turn ends. If they hit a civilian, the turn ends without a win. If they hit the assassin, the game ends immediately in the worst possible dramatic fashion. No pressure, though.
- The round continues until all of one team’s agents are found or the assassin is accidentally summoned by a misfit clue. The table either celebrates victory or laments a near-miss that will be remembered in memes for years.

### Setup and play rhythm (in practice)
The setup is mercifully fast. Shuffle the word cards, lay out the 25 cards in a 5x5 grid, reveal the key card, and assign Spymasters for each team. The first clue usually sets the tone for a night that oscillates between “how did you even think of that?” and “okay, I can work with this.” A great match is where every clue sparks a chain of connections that makes even your most linguistically stubborn friend chuckle in resignation. The pace is dependent on your group’s size and how aggressively you want to juice the guessing. You’ll often see a rhythm emerge: a few safe hits, a couple of hilariously wrong takes, then suddenly a perfect metaphor that ties three words together with a witty, time-stopping aha moment.

### Variants and extensions worth knowing
- Codenames: Pictures — swaps words for a grid of pictures. The same core idea, but the visual vocabulary changes the entire dynamic. It’s analogus to moving from riddles to visual metaphors; your brain works differently when the clues guide you through a gallery instead of a dictionary.
- Duet — a cooperative twist where two players work together to find agents across a shared grid. It’s perfect for long-distance nights or couples who want a puzzle they can solve without a noisy crowd.
- Deep Undercover and Disney variants — add spicier content or IP-based cards for mature audiences or family-friendly setups, respectively. If you’re playing with kids, choose the appropriate edition and skip the assassin anxiety.

### Strategy, but make it conversational
Codenames rewards a few core strategic ideas without requiring a degree in cognitive science:
- Think like your teammates, not like a cryptanalyst: Your clues should align with how your team perceives the world, not how a crossword constructor would approach semantic links.
- Aim for high-yield clues, but keep it safe: A great clue might connect 3–4 words, but a risky clue that points to 6 words or more could explode in your face. Find that sweet spot where you maximize potential hits with minimal danger.
- Use modifiers to widen the net: You can choose a clue that teases multiple layers of meaning—e.g., “Transport 3” might pull in train-related words, vehicle names, and perhaps a city’s nickname if you’re feeling bold. The trick is to balance breadth with accuracy.
- Don’t overspecify: Sometimes the best clue is a broad, elegant connection that your team can interpret, rather than a precise cross-reference that leaves little room for interpretation.

Some groups treat Codenames as a social experiment on teamwork and language. Others treat it as a mini-competition where bragging rights define eras. Either way, you’ll end up with stories like: “Remember that time we clutched victory from the jaws of a terrible clue?” or “We might need a thesaurus and a therapist.” The emotional arc is real, and that’s what keeps players coming back for more, even after a brutal defeat.
