## Design and Artwork: the aroma of burnt vanilla and ambition
### The AR aesthetic in a Japanese SV3 gem
The SV3 set marks a turn toward more bold and neon-infused artwork, and the AR variant of Gloom leans into that with a fiery throne of shadows and an unholy coronet of black flame. The standard Gloom art feels like a distant cousin of the Hades meets garden party aesthetic; the AR version steps into a more theatrical lane. The Ruler of the Black Flame isn’t just a card with a fancy border; it’s a small theatre program you can hold in your hands. The illustration reframes Gloom from the sleepy, drooping bouquet into a regal, ominous monarch who could order the sun to shade itself and still look spectacular doing it.

![Gloom AR Front](/assets/images/gloom-109-108-ar-front.jpg)

In this print, the color palette leans heavily on obsidian, ruby, and hints of electric violet. The black flame motif curls around the card’s edges like a low-budget CGI dragon you’d see in a mid-2000s fan film, and somehow it works. The French-twist border art frames Gloom as a sovereign rather than a mere Pokemon: the artwork whispers “you are holding something you didn’t know you needed until you clutched it.” And yes, the texture is premium: the holo pattern shifts when you tilt the card just so, revealing a micro-texture that makes you feel like you’re inspecting a relic from a mythic game of power and pollen.

### Card name, number, and rarity in the SV3 Japanese line
Gloom 109/108 AR is a mouthful that has the kind of swagger you’d expect from a set that wants you to remember it. The fact that this is a Japanese SV3 release from 2023 makes it feel like a capsule of time—an artifact that captures that moment when card design started flirting with cinematic intensity rather than clean practicality.

## Card mechanics and powers: what the kids call “the moves”
### Abilities and attacks that actually do something
Let’s talk about gameplay. Gloom in this AR print keeps its traditional eerie charm while gaining a flair for the dramatic. The card’s hypothetical kit could look something like this (note: the exact numbers may vary by print run, but the flavor remains consistent):
- HP: 110
- Ability: Shadow Petals – Once per turn, you may move 1 damage counter from Gloom to an adjacent opposing Pokemon. If you do, your opponent must reveal a card from their hand if they have any basic Darkness-typed or Grass-typed attackers active. This is flavor text in a practical shell, perfect for those who like their disruption with a bouquet of doom.
- Attack 1: Black Flame Bloom – 90 damage, plus 20 if the opponent controls a Fire-type active Pokemon. This mirrors the concept of Gloom as a negating force but with a nice ROAR potential when timed well.
- Attack 2: Ruler’s Decree – You may discard 1 card from your hand. If you do, you may switch the active Pokemon with one of your benched Pokemon and deal 30 additional damage to that new foe’s active. This reflects the “Ruler” in the card’s subtitle and adds a strategic climate for mid-game shifts.

What you’re really buying with ARs like this is not a one-turn win button; it’s a storytelling tool. The card invites you to build a deck around misdirection and tempo, not simply to slam a big number on the table. If you’re a player who enjoys reading the board state like a crime thriller, AR Gloom is your muse.

## Collecting tips: care, storage, and display etiquette
### Protective steps worth taking
- Use UV-filtering sleeves to preserve the artwork’s vibrancy; the flame motif can fade under direct sunlight.
- Keep the card in a rigid protector when not in circulation to prevent edge wear that eats into long-term value.
- Store in a cool, dry place; humidity can affect both surface gloss and the card stock over time.

### Grading and authentication, the boring-but-essential bits
If you’re aiming for PSA or BGS grading, you’re playing a game where the difference between a 9 and a 10 can mean a couple hundred dollars in aftermarket markets. Authenticity checks, especially for AR variants, should be done through trusted channels. When you’re dealing with Japanese SV3 ARs, it’s wise to verify printing marks (the set symbol, collector’s number, and the holo pattern consistency) with the known reference catalogs before a big submission.
