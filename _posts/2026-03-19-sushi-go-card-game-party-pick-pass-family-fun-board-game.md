### The quick pitch

In this pick and pass masterclass, each round you start with a hand of, say, 7 cards. You select one card to keep and simultaneously pass the rest to the next player. The challenge is that you must decide what you want now and predict what everyone else wants later. It is a little like predicting a weather forecast for a dragon, but with fewer flames and more seaweed.

## How to play (Quick Start Guide)

### Setup

- Shuffle the deck and deal 7 cards to each player (gently remind everyone that this is a game and not a sibling monopoly)
- Place a starting card as a reference on the table to remind everyone how scoring works
- Decide how many rounds you will play or simply go until the joy of cardboard becomes too much to resist

### Turn structure

On your turn you pick one card from your current hand and place it in front of you. Then you pass the remaining cards to your left. The next player picks a card from their hand and passes on again. This continues until all cards are taken. The round ends when all cards have been claimed, and scoring happens based on the cards you collected. Expect a chorus of groans, cheers, and the occasional triumphant high five when someone nails a perfect combo.

### Scoring

Sushi Go uses a simple, digestible scoring method. Some cards score small points when collected in pairs, others score big when you collect multiples of the same type, while some rare cards offer a big payoff if you can fit them into your set. The pudding cards are a wildcard in some versions, introducing a flavorful twist at the end of a round. The scoring rules are straightforward enough for kids to understand but nuanced enough for adults to enjoy the strategic tension.

### End of game

After the predetermined number of rounds, tally the scores. The player with the highest total wins. In practice this usually turns into a friendly sprint to the finish with plenty of jokes about who stole the maki roll and who demanded revenge by pastry instead of rice.

### The quick strategy bits

- Start by prioritizing low risk cards that give you options for later rounds
- Observe other players tastes; if you notice someone chasing the same card you want, pivot to a different path or steal a different card from the same sushi family
- Use the pudding and dessert cards strategically to seal a late lead or recover from a bad round
