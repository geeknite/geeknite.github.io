## Miniature quality and design language

The 9-piece set adheres to Zombicide’s characteristic design language: bold, readable silhouettes that translate well on a crowded board. Abominations appear as hulking bodies with grotesque features—big enough to command attention and small enough to keep them from monopolizing the entire table. Runners have slender torsos and elongated limbs, which not only looks dynamic but helps differentiate movement during the game’s narrative moments. Walkers are the signature zombies: a consistent, readable silhouette that players instantly read at a glance.

Detail-wise, there are some micro-scratches on the plastic that you expect from mass-produced miniatures. It’s not a deal-breaker, though; with a wash or two, those lines can pop and give the models that “dungeon-tashed” vibe that players crave. If you’re aiming for a museum-quality display, you’ll want to apply thin coats of color with careful dry brushing to emphasize the sculpted textures. If you’re more of a gamer than a painter, a quick wash and some contrast paints can do wonders without turning the minis into a two-week hobby project.

Size-wise, these minis fit comfortably with Zombicide’s standard 28mm scale. They won’t tower over your survivors; instead, they stride into the play area with a presence that says, Hey, you don’t want to mess with this one. The Abominations’ base footprint is larger than Walkers, which is thematically appropriate and practically useful for you to block line of sight or wedge them into a hallway with a sense of inevitability.

## How they affect gameplay on the table

This set shines when you deploy it as a mid-game escalation. Runners add urgency: their speed creates gaps in your line that you have to cover with careful positioning and timely card draws. Walkers keep the pressure consistent: a handful of these on the board ensures players don’t get complacent, forcing the survivors to manage space, line of sight, and the threat of multiple zombie directions.

From a balance perspective, the combined 9-piece set gives you flexible play options. If you’re running a smaller mission, you can pull in one Abomination and a couple of Walkers to create a boss-lite encounter that still scratches the zombie itch. If you’re staging a full-blown siege, you can deploy all three Abominations with supporting Runners for dramatic cinematic moments. You can also mix and match these minis with other factions or expansions to tailor the difficulty curve to your group’s appetite for pain—and drama.

For new players, a cautionary note: Abominations are not something you can safely ignore. They demand planning, perhaps a strategic retreat, and a plan B that doesn’t rely on luck alone. Runners, while interesting, don’t allow you to lazily hide in a corner—their speed punishes passive play. The Walkers, in their traditional role, remind you that sometimes the simplest threat is the most persistent. The synergy between these three types creates a rhythm: a surge of Walkers around a corner, a Runner cutting a line of fire, and finally an Abomination crashing into your plan with a damp, smelly, and very loud exit.

## Painting and hobby tips

If you’re into painting, the nine minis are a reasonable weekend project, not a full-time commitment. Here are some practical tips to get excellent results without turning your hobby into a full-time job:

- Prime with a neutral color (gray or white) to optimize color uptake and to keep the temperature of your spray sessions calm and friendly. 
- Block base colors first, then apply washes to bring out textures. A dark brown wash on cloth parts and a muted blue or gray wash on armor yields a grim, atmospheric look.
- Dry-brush to highlight edges. A pale gray or bone color on the edges makes the models pop on the table, especially under game lighting.
- For Abominations, use a mix of dark greens and purples to give them a grotesque, almost otherworldly feel. Runners benefit from accenting with reds and dark skin tones for an agile, blood-sport vibe. Walkers are best left with desaturated tones and a subtle red wash to indicate blood and age.
- Keep the bases consistent with your table’s terrain. If you’re playing in a dungeon-like setup, you can add small stones and basing materials for extra ambiance. If your table is a ruined city, a thin layer of sand and grit works nicely.

If you’re not into painting, don’t worry—these minis look fine even with minimal treatment. A quick varnish and a spray coat will protect the paint job and keep them looking respectable through several campaigns. The goal is not perfect museum pieces but durable, readable miniatures that contribute to the fun rather than overshadow the gameplay.
