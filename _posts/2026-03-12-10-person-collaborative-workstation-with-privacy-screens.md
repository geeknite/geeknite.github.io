## Design and Build: Form Meets Function
### Core concept
The core idea behind a 10-person collaborative workstation is simple: give each person a dedicated space with enough surface area for laptops, prop toys (sticky notes, your favorite snack stash), and documents, while maintaining a sense of shared purpose. The privacy screens act as visual boundaries without isolating team members to the point of mutiny. The result is a layout that can host 8–12 people depending on the table length and screen height, but that feels intimate enough for meaningful interactions.

### Layout options
The standard configuration places ten seats along a generous elongated table with privacy screens positioned between every two seats. The design supports several alternative arrangements:
- A single long bench with alternating privacy screens for a café-style vibe.
- A U-shape or horseshoe for teams that do daily stand-ups but still want a conversational edge.
- A modular strip that can be split into two five-person islands for cross-functional pods.

Our tested setup leaned into the elongated table with ten seats, which works well for design sprints, data-heavy reviews, and code-focused sessions where you need per-seat space but want the room to stay collaborative. You can tweak the positions to maximize whiteboard reach or to ensure everyone has an easy line of sight to the central display.

### Materials and finish
- Desk surfaces: durable laminate with a subtle textured grain that resists fingerprints and coffee rings. The feel is somewhere between practical and premium, which is exactly what busy teams need when the project deadline is breathing down your neck.
- Privacy screens: frosted acrylic panels held by slim aluminum supports. The screens are tall enough to obscure legible details from casual passersby but not so tall that they block light or create a cave-like feel.
- Frame and supports: powder-coated steel legs with adjustable glides to accommodate uneven floors. The overall build reads as sturdy but not bulky, a design choice that helps rooms feel more spacious rather than dungeon-like.
- Cable management: a built-in trough under the desk row that can accommodate power modules, USB-C hubs, and a handful of data cords. The goal is to keep cables out of sight but easy to access from the seated position.

In our test, the materials held up well under daily use. The privacy screens showed minimal scratching after several weeks of desk-chair shuffles and a few cat-in-the-office moments (cats: they always want to snoop at the screens, it’s their thing).

The screens are the star of this setup. They do a few things well:

## Setup Guide: Getting It Right the First Time
### Step 1: Measure and map the room
Before bringing in ten workstations, map the room for clear pathways, fire exits, and power availability. Ensure the central display can be viewed from all ten seats without distortion. If you need to reposition for a town hall or offsite training, you should be able to reconfigure within a few hours without breaking the space into chaos.

### Step 2: Install and align privacy screens
Place screens between each seat and at the ends to avoid visual noise from neighboring rows. The screens should be perpendicular to the line of sight so people can glance toward the center display without turning away from their own monitor. Ensure the frames are level and stable to prevent wobble during intense brainstorming moments.

### Step 3: Route power and data
Install the central power module and route data cables through the trough. Use cable clips to guide cords toward the nearest floor outlets. Label ports to prevent confusion during quick reconfigurations. If your IT policy requires data separation or particular security standards, configure the cable routes accordingly in the network switch room.

### Step 4: Calibrate seating ergonomics
Adjust the chair heights to an ergonomic target: feet flat, thighs horizontal, wrists neutral on the keyboard. Confirm that screen height is comfortable at eye level, not forcing neck extensions or downward gazes. A quick ergonomics checklist for ten people can save you days of back pain later.

### Step 5: Test with a full team session
Invite a mock team to run through a sprint demo. Observe how easy it is for participants to focus on their own device while collaborating with neighbors through the central display. Note any bottlenecks or glare spots and adjust screen height or lighting as necessary.

## Maintenance, Durability, and Long-Term Value
### Cleaning and upkeep
- Desk surfaces: wipe with a microfiber cloth and a mild cleaner. Avoid harsh solvents that can degrade laminate. A weekly wipe-down keeps the space looking fresh for longer.
- Privacy screens: gentle dusting or a damp cloth for smudges. Avoid aggressive scrubbing that can scratch the acrylic. A light cleaning routine is enough and keeps screens readable rather than opaque.
- Cable troughs: periodically check for loose cables and secure them with zip ties. The goal is “stable enough to survive a sprint review” rather than “labyrinth of tangled spaghetti.”

### Durability and warranty
Most vendors offer 3–5 year warranties on the frame and screen components, with surface finishes rated for high-traffic environments. If you’re in a heavy-use environment (think software capitalists who never take weekends off), consider extended warranty and routine maintenance checks as part of your annual budget.

### Maintenance tips
- Schedule quarterly checks to tighten hardware and inspect screens for micro-scratches.
- Use protective mats under the desk edges to minimize floor wear when chairs slide in and out.
- Keep cleaning supplies in a discreet cabinet to prevent apprentice-level experiments from turning the space into a science fair.
