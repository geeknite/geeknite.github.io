## Authenticity and grading: a quick primer

Authenticity is king in any collectible market, and Carddas is no exception. Because Carddas cards were produced in a variety of print runs and packaging variations, it’s not unusual to encounter promotional versions or regional print differences. A practical approach to authenticity is to verify the card’s condition and packaging evidence that matches the era of release. For example, original packaging, the presence of holographic foil or particular gloss finishes, and the back printing can be telltale signs of a genuine No 262 card from a specific print run.

For grading, a conservative approach works best. You’ll often see Graded 8, 9, and 10 cards in excellent condition, with 10 representing a near-perfect condition with minimal signs of wear. If you’re serious about value, consider professional grading services that specialize in Japanese plastic-based card products. They can provide a documented assessment of corners, surface, centering, and edges, which are the typical levers that influence the grade. If you plan to hold the card long-term, grading can be worth it, but for casual display and light trading, a careful measurement with a good light and a magnifier often suffices to determine whether you’re happy or you’d prefer a re-purchase in mint shape.

The best way to preserve Carddas No 262 is to keep it away from direct sun and humidity. The Japanese printing era used inks that can fade or smear with moisture, so a stable environment matters.
