## Ergonomics and practicality

### Space planning for six workers
A six-person workstation is not a furniture item; it is a social experiment. The layout must ensure comfortable reach for each user, adequate elbow room, and a line of sight that doesn’t feel like you are performing at a dental appointment. Seven key considerations come into play:
- Personal space: Each user should have a personal swath of desk to avoid the dreaded elbow-to-elbow tango.
- Shared screen position: The central barrier should be at a height and distance that scales comfortably for everyone, not just the tallest person in the room.
- Power and data access: Grommets and channels must align with power strips or kiosks; you do not want six users wrestling to reach a single outlet.
- Keyboard trays and monitor arms: These should be adjustable to accommodate different postures and tasks.
- Lighting: White surfaces reflect light. Ensure overhead lighting and task lamps help reduce eye strain across all six stations.
- Acoustics: While the barrier helps with privacy, you may still want acoustic panels or soft furnishings to dampen the chorus of team chatter during deep work.
- Accessibility: Route access and clearance need to be clear for chairs rolling in and out without bumping into a person at the end of the row.

### Posture, posture, posture
Let us be honest: a long desk is a romance with good posture. The 1200 mm height is decent for sitting. If you have a mix of standing desks or height-adjustable chairs, you will want to map zones where standing sessions can happen without cutting off the circulation for others. In six-person arrays, a handful of people will enjoy a slight elevation to keep eyes at a similar level to the central screen. The magic word here is adjustability: invest in a couple of ergonomic chairs with lumbar support and some monitor arms that allow tilt and swivel without turning your workspace into a 3D puzzle.

### Cable management and cleanliness
Cable chaos is the silent killer of productivity. The integrated channels are a solid start, but you should plan for additional cable ties, trunking, and a few cable sleeves to keep the floor litter-free. A clean cable strategy reduces the risk of accidental unplugging during the end-of-quarter sprint and makes it easier to reconfigure the space if your team shifts roles or if someone discovers a new way to optimize their workflow by rearranging the furniture like a tiny interior designer.

## Assembly and maintenance: the real world test

Building this monster is not a weekend DIY project for the faint of heart. Expect a multi-hour affair with at least two people who can read a schematic without converting it to interpretive dance. The steps usually involve laying the base frame, aligning the long ledger panels, bolting the modular sections together, and then threading the data and power channels through the grommets in a way that would impress a master tailor. Here are some practical tips:
- Inventory check: Before you begin, lay out all parts and hardware. It’s amazing how often you find an extra screw that seems to have a purpose and a memory in the hardware world.
- Tools: Have a basic toolkit on hand, including a torque wrench of appropriate range, a drill with a driver bit, and perhaps a helper who can hold panels in place while you secure them.
- Step-by-step: Do not skip the alignment steps. A misaligned panel can throw off cable management and trigger a cascade of minor, but irritating, issues.
- Finish care: A soft, non-abrasive cleaner and a microfiber cloth will keep the white surface pristine. Do not use harsh solvents that might degrade the laminate or give you a questionable milky sheen.

Maintenance is a three-beer ritual: seal the joints, wipe down the surfaces, and remind yourself that this thing is designed to outlive many of your current team members. In other words, it is built for the long run, with the occasional need for re-tightening as the space settles into its new rhythm.
