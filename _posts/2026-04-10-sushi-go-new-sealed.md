## How to Play: A Quick Overview

Sushi Go is a drafting game with a deceptively simple premise: you pass cards around the table, selecting one each round, and try to assemble the best combination of sushi cards to maximize points. The game lasts three rounds in the base version, with the number of rounds increasing if you’re playing with more players. Here’s the gist:

1. Each player starts with a hand of 7 cards (for a standard 2-4 player game).
2. Everyone picks one card from their hand and places it face-down on the table.
3. All players reveal their chosen card at once, and then pass the remaining cards to the left (or right, depending on the round). This is where the tension begins: do you grab that tempting maki roll now or wait for the later combine that could net you more points?
4. The chosen cards flow until all cards are drafted. Then you tally points for each player according to the scoring rules on the back of the cards.

The result is a game that feels brisk, friendly, and cunning—the perfect combination of “Oh, I forgot about that card” and “I want to pretend I know exactly what I’m doing.” The base game plays in about 15 minutes, which makes it a reliable opener or closer in a game night, or a quick warm-up before you dive into something heavier like a legacy epic that requires a thousand tears and a better roll for the RNG gods.

## Strategy, Depth, and Replayability

Sushi Go’s beauty lies in the balance: you want to collect combos that maximize points while denying other players from achieving their high-score dreams. The key strategies boil down to a few concepts you’ll grab within a couple of plays, but you’ll still second-guess yourself during the next session:

- Napkin math: The scoring for different sushi types is straightforward, but the real joy comes from recognizing when to chase a particular combo. For example, getting three maki cards yields points equal to the largest number of maki icons you possess, multiplied by an outcome factor. This means that high diversity can beat singular power; your strategy often toggles between “collect all the maki” to “go for nigiri combos that explode” depending on what cards you see in the current round.
- Timing is everything: The drafting mechanic rewards you for predicting what others will pass to you. You’ll learn to read the table the same way you learn to read the last page of a suspense novel—the clues are there if you squint. If someone keeps grabbing nigiri early, you have to adapt and pivot toward a different scoring objective for the rest of the round.
- The pudding factor: Pudding is a special scoring deck that becomes relevant at the end of the game. It can swing the final results in unexpected ways. If you ignore pudding entirely, you’re not playing Sushi Go to its full potential. If you treat pudding like a hot potato, you’ll either gain extra points or lose friends around the table while arguing about its meaning in the cosmic dessert metaphysics.

Replayability is high because even though the core rules are simple, there are numerous shipping variants, player counts, and optional rules that you can add. Some players enjoy adding a table talk rule where you must explain your sushi strategy in a pirate voice; others just want to watch someone attempt to explain “three of a kind” while juggling a forkful of wasabi crackers. It’s flexible enough that you can adapt it to a family game night or a late-night party session with friends who love anime finales and food puns.

## Practical Tips for New Owners

- Learn the scoring basics quickly: nigiri cards focus on eating quality points, maki stacks reward the largest stack of printed icons, sashimi rewards a pair with big payoff for the long-run memory game, and pudding is the hidden wild card that may swing the final score. Remember to glance at the back of the card for the scoring rule; it saves you from miscounting your own points and from accidentally arguing about theoretical math while a tablemate amuses themselves with a pun involving wasabi.
- Pay attention to the round’s flow: you don’t always want to chase the biggest point card; sometimes the best move is to deny a rival their desired card. Sushi Go rewards this level of table awareness more than many people initially expect.
- Manage your run of rounds: with three rounds, you’ll want to diversify your early choices to avoid leaving a pile of worthless cards at the end. It’s a small game, but it rewards thoughtful planning and a willingness to adapt to what’s left in the deck.
- Consider using a post-game reflection: after a session, discuss what you would do differently next time. It’s not necessary to overanalyze, but a quick recap helps players remember important card combos for future sessions.
