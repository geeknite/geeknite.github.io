### Quick-start installation guidance: obvious steps, not a forest-quest

Wiring a winch is basically three steps: mount, connect, test. But since we’re geeks, we add the formule: bolt geometry first, then wiring, then test. Here’s a no-hurt, no-drama checklist:

1) Confirm bumper compatibility. Most 3.5-inch mounting bolts will work, but you might need a winch plate if your bumper isn’t cut for a winch spine.
2) Mount the winch on the plate. Tighten bolt patterns to spec. Do not over-torque; you don’t want a bolt to become a dramatic prop shooter in your engine bay.
3) Wire the winch to the battery with proper gauge cable. A larger gauge reduces voltage drop, which means faster pulling and fewer swears when the garage light flickers.
4) Route the remote control receiver and the winch controller in a place where rain isn’t invited to the party.
5) Attach the synthetic rope, feed it through the hawse or fairlead, and spool a couple of turns by hand to avoid a bird’s-nest moment. 
6) Do a dry test at no-load first, then a gentle load test on a pinned-down object to verify the brake engagement and line speed.
7) Finally, hook to a recovery point and take a breath. It’s time to make your vehicle feel like a tiny mechanical dragon—safely and responsibly.

### Maintenance, upkeep, and pro tips

- Inspect the rope regularly for kinks, wear, and UV degradation. Replace if you see any sign of a core break.
- Clean the winch after use, especially after muddy or sandy adventures. Dirt can trap heat and shorten the life of internal components.
- Lubricate the gears per the manual—this is not a part of the “set it and forget it” category; it’s more like oiling your bike chain, but for a mechanical beast.
- Test the winch under load at least a couple of times per year to ensure the brake is engaging as intended.
- If you’re not comfortable with wiring or mount selection, seek a professional install to prevent wiring mistakes that could cause a battery drain or fire risk.
