Components
----------
- Boss miniatures: The Jiangshi sculpts are characterful with bent limbs that scream the vibe of stiff-legged elegance. They are not the cutesy zombies you see in other games; these are guests at a banquet who forgot to floss and are definitely going to confiscate your soul if you sneeze incorrectly.
- Tokens and tiles: The bite-sized tokens feel substantial, with a finish that resists chipping and a color palette that reads clearly on every light level. The tile art evokes lantern-lit corridors and old porcelain patterns that would fit into a museum’s Chinese porcelain wing, if the museum also allowed you to punch them into a grid and chase you with rules lawyering.
- Cards: Action, Event, and Rune cards all come with art that nods to traditional Chinese folklore while still looking fresh enough to be on a modern card sleeve. The Runic subtitles are not just there for vibes; they spawn interesting effects that can chain into dramatic late-game swings.
- Dice: A small set of polyhedral dice is included, because nothing says ancient undead tribunal like rolling a D6 with a dragon watermark on it. The dice add a satisfying crunch to the decision points without becoming a central mechanic, which is exactly how you want dice to behave in a thematic game.
- Deluxe bits: The cardboard components are thicker, the insert is well-designed, and the miniatures snap into their bases with a satisfying click. This is the kind of upgrade that makes you feel comfortable leaving the core rules on the table rather than tucking everything back into a baggie like it is a spoiler for a season finale.

Setup and Rule Overview
-----------------------
Setting up Jiangshi Blood in the Banquet Hall is less like assembling a board game and more like preparing for a rehearsal of an ancient play. The rules lean on a short setup but a satisfyingly chunky rulebook that rewards careful reading. If you are the sort who skims, you will likely stumble into a few questions that will require a quick consult of the FAQ cards included in the Deluxe Version. The core loop is simple enough to teach in a side-by-side with a snack break included, but it hides layers of strategy that reveal themselves as you play more rounds.

Core Loop
---------
- Each player manages a delegation of guests and Jiangshi guardians who have competing desires: eat, sleep, perform, and avoid becoming someone else’s appetizer. You will move through rooms of the banquet hall to collect what you need and to deny it to your rivals.
- The action cards drive the engine: you will standardly perform a movement, claim a room, or trigger a ritual that shifts the balance of power on the table. The timing windows are important; you are not just shuffling resources but also managing fear, honor, and the honor-bound fear of being eaten by a pale librarian from the afterlife who forgot to apply sunscreen.
- The victory conditions mix area control with a little deck manipulation. It is never purely about who has the most corners, but who has the best story arc in the banquet and the most convincing excuse for bending the rules after someone sneaks a peek at your hand.

Rules Learning Curve
--------------------
The Deluxe Version keeps the core rules accessible but layered. There are a few new terms, and the ritual system adds a social deduction tilt that is surprisingly minimal in chaos but maximal in flavor. If you have played other medium-weight Euro-style games, you will likely feel right at home with the timing of turns and the way combat or conflict resolves with dice, tokens, and a little bit of misdirection. If you are more into heavy Ameritrash experiences, you will still find the stakes surprisingly personal thanks to the banquet’s interpersonal politics and the tension of a clock counting down to a reveal.

- Don’t underestimate the power of a well-placed “block” or “redirect” to keep a rival from locking down a key room. The board is a social sandbox and the right move is often a clever misdirection rather than brute force.
