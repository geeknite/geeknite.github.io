## The value proposition of flat shipping: $4 for everything that fits in a mailer
Shipping costs are the hidden cost of card hunting. A flat $4 ship can feel like a bargain, especially when you’re stocking up with NM Commons and Uncommons that otherwise ship for a few dollars each. But consider these trade-offs:

- Packaging quality: If it’s a bulk NM common/uncommon haul, expect to see card sleeves or top-loaders, but not always thick card protection. A $4 shipping policy often pairs with simple padded mailers and a quick handoff; that’s fine if you’re patient, but you want to confirm that the bulk hasn’t turned into a bulk of surface-chipped corners due to rough handling.
- Card protection: Are the cards in semi-rigid sleeves? Are they cut the wrong way? If a bulk listing mentions NM condition, you’ll want to confirm stacking protection – typically top-loaders for single cards or smaller bulk shipments – and the seller’s policy on damaged cards.
- Lead time: Some sellers ship within 24–72 hours; others, especially those who do weekly batches, can take longer. If you’re building a deck for a weekend tournament, the timeline matters as much as the price.

In short: flat shipping reduces the sting of cost, but you should still verify the train schedule. The last thing you want is a “complete” NM list delivered in a box that clearly took a few detours through the Bermuda Triangle of cardboard bends.

## How to audit a NM Common/Uncommon complete set listing: a buyer’s checklist
Here’s a practical list you can use before you commit:

- Ask for the included sets: request a list or spreadsheet enumerating each set and the exact card counts per rarity. If a seller can present an audit, you’ll know they’re serious.
- Confirm condition notes: look for explicit NM condition, centered edges, and no whitening on corners. If possible, request that photos cover the most common wear areas: corners, edges, and surface.
- Check the bulk composition: are there any rares mixed in? If so, what’s the plan for those cards? Some listings include a separate set of rares, but a complete NM Common/Uncommon haul should not rely on unsanctioned substitutions.
- Review packaging: ensure cards are protected (top-loaders or semi-rigid sleeves) for transit. For bulk NM, robust packaging helps; you’re paying for protection in addition to content.
- Talk about returns: a simple policy that supports buyers if the listing misrepresents condition or misses items is a strong sign of a buyer-friendly seller.
- Request a few sample cards: ask for four or five images of random cards to verify NM condition across various sets. This helps set expectations.
- Compare shipping terms: some sellers offer flat-rate shipping but with restrictions on location or service method. If you live in a country with extra taxes or long transit times, make sure that $4 shipping truly applies to your destination.

If all of the above checks out, you’re probably in for a decent NM Commons/Uncommons haul that meets your needs without turning your wallet into a spectral goblin. If not, you can always walk away with your dignity (and possibly your patience) intact.
