## Overview
Zombicide Black Plague Huntsmen Box drops into a world where knights and zombies aren’t on a medieval scavenger hunt so much as a chaotic paintball game played with axes, swords, and just enough curses to guilt you into buying more dice. If you thought the core Black Plague experience was already a love letter to cooperative chaos, the Huntsmen Box shows up with a wink, a propulsive new hero, and a handful of nuisance zombies that feel suspiciously like they had a few pints too many at the Fealty Inn. The core question this review seeks to answer is simple: does Paul the Huntsman add enough flavor, balance, and bite to justify a box on your shelf, or is this another collectible that ends up in the back of your closet alongside three different versions of the same plastic skeleton arm? Spoiler alert: it mostly lands in the former camp, with a few caveats.

If you want the short version: the Huntsmen Box provides a memorable new hero, a tricky tilt to the normal zombie spawn patterns, and a set of sculpts that feel like they belong in a high fantasy epic rather than a dungeon crawl. If you want the long version, read on as we dive into contents, mechanics, painting potential, and whether this add-on is worth your placeholder goblets of coffee and the extra space in your board game shelf.

### Paul the Huntsman: the mini and the person behind the plastic
The Paul the Huntsman miniature is the star here, and not just because he’s tall enough to need his own moonlighting job as a siren for the board’s attention. The sculpt captures a feeling of someone who has walked through a few villages with the patient confidence of a hunter who knows the terrain as well as his own shadow. The pose is active, almost cinematic, with a bow slung across a shoulder that looks like it could survive a thunderstorm and a second axe that looks perfectly ready for close quarters stabbings if the moment demands.

In the rules, Paul isn’t merely a stronger fighter; he brings a different flow to turn economy and resource management. His abilities encourage you to treat a corner of the board as a hunting ground where you can set up ambushes, cut off zombie reinforcements, and orchestrate a few well-timed splits in the party’s focus. It’s a reminder that Zombicide is not a simple “kill the zombies” game; it’s a puzzle in which you must balance risk, movement, and the occasional distraction by a particularly stubborn ghoul.

The miniature’s paint-friendly details deserve a nod as well. The Huntsman’s leather garb has enough texture to be satisfying under a wash or dry brush, and the weaponry’s metallic accents pop when you’re painting with a bit of bravado. If you’re into display painting, Paul is a candidate for a shelf piece that says The Neat Squad Did This Box Without Flaming 40k-Style Choreography. If you’re a gamer who dislikes painting, well, Paul still looks pretty cool on the table during play and will not demand you turn your hobby into a small business venture.

### Abilities and rules: how Paul changes the rhythm
Paul the Huntsman introduces a mix of ranged and melee capabilities that work best when you’re plotting turns a couple of steps ahead. Here are the core ideas you’ll want to wrap your head around before you pick up the dice:

- Ambush potential: Paul’s kit rewards setting up lines of attack that lure zombies into controlled zones. You’ll want to consider terrain features that act like funnels, forcing walkers to crowd into narrow corridors where your team’s combined fire can thin the herd safely.
- Resource economy: the Huntsman box favors a measured approach to ammo and knife use. You’ll find that certain scenarios reward saving ranged shots for the right moment and relying on Paul’s melee prowess to finish off wounded targets when feasible.
- Terrain synergy: there are terrain tiles that interact with Paul’s kit in meaningful ways. Some tiles may grant a line of sight bonus, others can block or trap zombies in place, which helps teams coordinate a trap-based takedown rather than a rush into a swarm.
- Risk vs reward: like any good Zombicide expansion, Paul doesn’t turn the game into a walk in the park. The box pushes you to plan multi-turn sequences to maximize Paul’s advantages, and in return you’ll feel a greater sense of satisfaction when a difficult round goes exactly as you predicted.

If you have played through a few base game campaigns and found the early rounds a bit repetitive, Paul’s introduction acts like a fresh air system. The box nudges you to think about positioning, timing, and resource management in a more deliberate way, which is a nice counterbalance to the sometimes chaotic early rounds where the zombies come at you in a wave of numbers.

### Gameplay experience: how it actually feels on the table
Putting Paul into a Black Plague game is a little like swapping a stock weapon for a custom build in a classic RPG. The box doesn’t reinvent the wheel, but it adds a new axle that makes the wheels turn in a slightly different rhythm. The first few games with Huntsmen content often feel like you are playing a familiar game but with a friend who has brought along a strategy guide he swears is written in runes and coffee stains.

The box’s new spawn cards and zombie types tend to tilt the balance toward more tactical decisions early on. You’ll encounter moments where a swift withdrawal from a dual-front battle becomes the correct decision, not because you are playing conservatively but because the box rewards you for anticipating the zombies’ movement and sacrificing a turn for better positioning on the next one. Those moments feel very satisfying because the big enemy hates being tricked, and the Huntsmen box gives you a way to do it without resorting to brainy puzzles that require a matematical degree to solve.

The visual elements on the table — Paul’s model, the axis and the bow, the new terrain tokens — contribute to an experience that feels cinematic without breaking the game’s rhythm. You won’t need a cinematic speaker system to hear a dramatic moment when a zombie is finally corralled into a choke point and eliminated with a satisfying “thunk” of a heavy blade meeting undead flesh. The art direction stays consistent with Black Plague, but with the Huntsmen’s kit, there’s a heightened sense of heroism that suits our party moments of high fives and questionable decision making.

### Strategy and tactics: maximizing Paul’s potential
If you plan to run with Paul the Huntsman, here are a few practical tips that tend to pay off in most campaigns:

- Map control is king: Look for terrain features that allow you to corral zombies into narrow lanes. Paul’s ranged option can then soften targets before the frontline teams close in.
- Chin-up on resources: Don’t waste the Huntsman’s kit on trivial targets. Weigh the cost of a ranged shot against a potential in-turn kill that gives you a more secure future round.
- Coordinate with your squad: The Huntsman content shines when your group communicates a plan that uses Paul as a pivot. A well-timed sacrifice on a valuable objective proves that you can balance aggression with safety and win conditions.
- Don’t forget the board state: The new spawn types are designed to punish noise and chaos. If you go in with a loud plan, you’ll probably pay in a pile of activation cards. A quieter approach that focuses on positioning can be far more effective.
- Hero synergy: Paul can work well in teams that have a mix of ranged and melee capabilities. If your squad already leans heavily into melee, Paul gives you a reliable alternative to stretch your tactical options. If your squad already has a strong ranged component, his terrain and ambush options let you create stronger crossfire opportunities.

The beauty of Zombicide is that you can iterate quickly. The Huntsmen Box offers enough variation to run several different strategies within a single campaign, which keeps even veteran players engaged without feeling like they are re-reading the same chapter of a rulebook.

### Box balance and scenario fit: does this make Black Plague better or just different?
Balance is always a moving target in co op board games, especially with expansions that introduce a new hero and a few extra chess pieces into a already crowded board. The Huntsmen Box does a decent job of preserving the core feel of Black Plague while injecting a few new levers to pull. If you are chasing a more aggressive, positioning-heavy style of play, Paul delivers. If you prefer pure brute force, you might feel his added options are nice but not essential. In practice, the box sits in a sweet middle ground: it nudges the meta toward smarter play without breaking the baseline difficulty curve.

For players who have already devoured the base game and its earlier expansions, Huntsmen feels like a natural extension rather than a weird detour. It’s the kind of box you can slot into a longer campaign without needing to rearrange your entire play cadence. If you have a regular group that enjoys the occasional puzzle element in combat, Huntsmen doubles down on that without turning the game into a rules-heavy mecha fight.
