## How to set up and begin a round

1) Shuffle the word cards and lay them in a neat 5x5 grid. If your table is small or you’re sitting on couches with questionable stability, the board can also be approximated with cushions and a coffee table—but don’t blame the game when the clue hits the carpet and you spend 10 minutes searching for the assassin.
2) Decide which team goes first and hand the Spymaster role to a willing candidate. The Spymaster looks at the key card without showing it to the team and then begins the clue-giving ritual that will either cement lifelong respect or result in a group therapy session.
3) The Spymaster gives a one-word clue with a number. The number tells how many words on the grid relate to the clue. For example: “Car 3” means there are three cards connected to the word “car.” The catch? You might hit your own team’s words, the other team’s words, or the dreaded assassin. The game ends when one team reveals all its words or someone reveals the assassin and ends the round in spectacular fashion.

## Playstyle and strategy: how the New Edition plays with your brain

Codenames is the kind of game where your brain does Shakespearean soliloquies to justify a one-off clue. The New Edition doesn’t rewrite the fundamental logic, but it does invite you to escalate with smarter clues and sharper word pairing. A few practical strategies worth knowing:

- Balance is your friend: start with safe, high-probability words that give your teammates a solid lead, then thread the needle with riskier connections to steal the round. The trick is to avoid giving your team a clue that points too obviously to the assassin—or to a word that the other team is itching to guess.
- Context matters: because the card deck is language-rich, you’ll want to consider the likely cultural frames of your players. If your group is heavy on sci-fi fans, you’ll get more mileage out of space- and gadget-related clues; if you’re playing with kids, you’ll lean on everyday objects and familiar terms.
- Mend the gaps with synonyms: sometimes a word seems off but has a strong synonym that your team can latch onto. The Spymaster needs a mental thesaurus and a ability to pivot when your teammate misreads your clue. The New Edition’s updated word list helps avoid dead-ends, but you still need to nudge your team along with precise language.

Note: there’s a natural tension between “too obvious” and “too obscure.” If you’re playing with non-native English speakers, opt for clues rooted in everyday, common knowledge. Your group will thank you when the assassin-free momentum continues beyond the third round.
