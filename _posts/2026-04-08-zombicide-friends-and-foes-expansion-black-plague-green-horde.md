## Overview: what is this expansion actually doing?

Zombicide: Friends and Foes is a compact expansion that slides into your existing Black Plague or Green Horde campaigns with the grace of a hungry zombie sliding into a kitchen window—unexpected, loud, and a little unsettling. The core idea is delightful chaos: introduce a set of new Survivors (the Friends) who offer supportive or morally ambiguous options, and pair them with a set of new Enemies (the Foes) that complicate the standard zombie-slaying ballet. It’s a thematic mismatch you didn’t know you needed: social dynamics in a coop game where your teammates might be more of a liability than a help—at least until the adrenaline kicks in and someone pulls off a heroic clutch move.

In practice, the expansion is designed to be drop-in compatible with both the medieval-flavored Black Plague and the evergreen fantasy-slasher Green Horde. The Friends can be allies who can turn the tide on a tough turn, while the Foes introduce new tactical challenges that force you to rethink route planning, tile control, and which graveyard you’re willing to defend with your last stand.

## Box contents and components: what you actually get

The Friends and Foes expansion is deliberately lean and modular. It doesn’t try to reinvent the wheel; instead, it adds a few key pieces that shift the meta just enough to justify a second shelf space between the core games and the expansions you’ve already forgotten while cleaning your desk at 2 a.m.

What’s inside:

- A handful of new Survivors (the Friends) with unique starting kits and quirky special abilities. Some are mercifully straightforward; others feel like you’re playing a political negotiation with a tiny human-shaped die-roller attached to your arm.
- A handful of new Enemies (the Foes) that introduce fresh triggers, flanking risks, and sudden changes to the “clear the room” calculus you’ve come to rely on.
- Token sheets, mission cards, and maybe a couple of new rule clarifications to keep all this friend-and-foe business tidy rather than a chaotic, dice-driven free-for-all. 
- A handful of fresh artwork and flavor text that makes you go, “Of course that character talks like a pompous goblin; why wouldn’t they?”

If you’ve got a group that loves to experiment with new alliances and betrayals, this box is right up your alley. If your group is still arguing about whether the Horde should have a strong leader or if the Black Plague needs more diversity of cheese in its cheese wheel (spoiler: it does), you’ll still find plenty of entertainment value here. The balance between “friendly” and “foe” is the trick: you’re not signing up for pure cooperation or pure chaos—you’re signing up for a delicious, dissonant hybrid that can make your games either glow with laughter or squeal with tension.

## How it feels in play: Black Plague versus Green Horde

### Black Plague: a dark mess with a dash of snark
In Black Plague, the world is a grim, plague-ridden medieval nightmare. The Friends and Foes expansion leans into the chaos by injecting allies who can offer pivotal assists in tight corridors and on every hero’s worst turn. The Friends aren’t universally benevolent—some will offer powerful one-time benefits at the cost of a later consequence, mirroring the uneasy moral math you always wanted in a zombie apocalypse comic book.

The Foes in Black Plague are a mix of cunning undead and ritualistic horrors that force you to rethink your route and your threat assessment. You’ll find yourself considering whether you should spare a tile for a possible rescue or sprint straight to the next objective, even if a few extra zombies are hot on your heels. The net effect is a more dynamic, less predictable session that rewards careful planning and a willingness to expose your survivors to risk for a potential payoff.

### Green Horde: more tactical sprinting through goblin-town
Green Horde leans into more fantasy-friendly chaos: orcish marauders, goblin mischief, and shambling greenskins with bigger personalities than their stat blocks would suggest. Here, Friends and Foes gives you a way to leverage temporary alliances with the environment and with the new foes who block your path in fun, sometimes ridiculous ways. You’ll experience a different flavor of decision-making when you must decide between helping a “Friend” to grab a crucial loot or activating a “Foe” that blunts the next hero surge—without dragging your team into an extended moral debate.

The design balance across both settings is the clever part. The expansion doesn’t force you into one playstyle: you can still run a tight, cooperative game where everyone pulls for the group, or you can tilt a little anti-hero without turning the board into a literal bloodbath. The rules remain familiar enough to not require a full reread, but there’s enough new behavior to spark lively discussion and multi-episode campaigns if your group is into long-form Zombicide storytelling.

## Mechanics and balance: what to expect on the table

- The introduction of Friends adds a layer of negotiation and optional risk-taking. Some Friends reward cooperation with reliable power boosts; others offer leverage to manipulate AI or to change the order of play in a pinch. The tension is delicious: do you trust the Friend who promises a big payoff if you chase a risky objective?
- The Foes add new threats that sometimes act with their own agendas. They’re not merely tougher zombies; some Foes require you to pivot, plan a trap, or split up to avoid losing the whole squad to a single bad roll.
- The token system and mission cards are designed to interlock with existing mechanics. You might need to reach certain positions to unlock a Friend’s benefit or avoid triggering a deadly Foe-enabled ambush. This changes the pace: you’ll often have a moment where everyone quotes a favorite movie hero while trying to calculate the probability of success on a 12-die combat pool.

In terms of balance, the expansion lands in a comfortable middle ground. It introduces new lines of play without making core strategy irrelevant. It’s not a slam-dunk “more is better” scenario; rather, it’s a thoughtful nudge toward smarter decisions and a dash of luck-based drama that keeps players honest about their risk tolerance.

## Box to table: setup and playthrough tips

- Setup is quick if you’ve already got Black Plague or Green Horde set up. The Friends and Foes components slot into your standard weapon/gear system and can be introduced mid-campaign without breaking ongoing storylines.
- Start by selecting a few Friends and a couple of Foes to test the waters. You don’t need to go full crazy on the first run; let your group learn the new cadence at a comfortable pace.
- Use a few house rules to keep things tight: consider limiting the number of Friends you can activate per round, or give the team a reliable turn once every three rounds to simulate a “cooperative plan” moment. This helps balance the chaos and prevents the table from dissolving into an all-out chaos-fest.

Pro tip: take screenshots of your favorite moments. They’ll be gold when you’re bemoaning RNG for three months straight and need a reminder that you once rolled three critical hits in a row on a crucial turn.

## Thematic notes: humor, flavor, and the vibe

Geeknite has always loved a game that doesn’t take itself too seriously while still delivering a robust strategic experience. Friends and Foes hits that sweet spot with humorous flavor text on the cards and a design that invites you to role-play your choices, not just roll dice. The Friends can be a little cheeky, offering oaths, testimonies, and witty one-liners that give your table character beyond the usual heroic slog. The Foes, meanwhile, come with their own dramatic flair, sometimes playing the “villain reveal” beat in a way that lands as both cinematic and ridiculous in the best possible way.

If you enjoy the little in-game moments where a character’s trait aligns perfectly with a turn of events, you’ll be rewarded with more of those moments here. If you want a stern, grim challenge with no jokes, you’ll still get a tough session—but you’ll have to search for the humor on your own, which could be a fun challenge in its own right.

## Design, components, and production quality

The production quality remains solid. The art style aligns with the existing Zombicide feel—grim yet whimsical—so you won’t feel like you’ve entered a completely different universe. The Friends’ character designs pop with personality, and the Foes have distinct silhouettes and token traces that make them easy to identify on a crowded board. The rulebook is clear enough to be the voice of reason in a fan-fueled debate over mechanic interactions, though you’ll still need a couple of careful plays to lock down edge cases.

The expansion does a nice job of staying within the established color codes and iconography of both base games, which makes it easy to combine with your current table setup. If you’ve already invested in acrylics or fancy sleeves for your Zombicide experience, you’ll find this expansion compatible with your nerdy ecosystem without forcing a downgrade in aesthetic.

## Replayability and long-term value

If you’re a Zombicide veteran who’s played through every mission in both Black Plague and Green Horde, this expansion offers enough bite to stay relevant across a handful of sessions. It’s not a colossal add-on that doubles your play length; it’s a punchy, flavorful injection that changes the rhythm of a typical round and invites you to try new strategies. For groups who enjoy campaign-style play, the Friends and Foes can sustain a few arcs of storytelling and dramatic “who can you trust?” moments before you’ll want to rotate them out for a while and bring them back later.

The expansion also plays well in one-shot sessions, thanks to its modular nature. You can drop in a Friend and a Foe or two for a single session and still have a satisfying experience without committing to a multi-game arc. It’s flexible enough to suit both the “short night at the table” crowd and the “let’s marathon this until we hear the sound of the dragon they forgot to put away” crew.

## Pros and cons (TL;DR)

- Pros:
  - Adds meaningful choice without exploding complexity
  - Works with both Black Plague and Green Horde seamlessly
  - Thematically flavorful; great for roleplay moments and memorable quotes
  - Modular; easy to drop in or out of a session
- Cons:
  - Not a full-game overhaul; some players may crave a bigger mechanical shake-up

If you love Zombicide but crave more twists in your cooperative chaos, this expansion is a solid addition that respects the core gameplay while giving you fresh choices to explore.

## Final verdict and rating

Geeksnite verdict: A solid, flavorful add-on that leans into what Zombicide does well—cooperative puzzle-solving tangled with enough chaos to keep everyone laughing and panicking in equal measure. If you’re curious about spicing up your Black Plague or Green Horde campaigns with a dose of “nice-to-meet-you, we might be betraying you in the next turn,” Friends and Foes earns a thumbs-up (and possibly a raised eyebrow).

## Quick strategy tips for maximizing Friends and Foes

- Start with a plan, not a wish. Map out a route that secures two objectives rather than sprinting to one distant goal. The new Foes will chase you, so you’ll want an efficient path.
- Use Friends as force multipliers, but don’t overcommit. Some Friends require protection or exploitation of a turn-based synergy to unlock their power.
- Watch the morale. The emotional stakes are part of the fun; keep a fun vibe at the table, even when the dice gods are laughing at you.
- Practice makes permanent. Run a few practice rounds with different Friends and Foes to understand risk/benefit trade-offs before committing in a campaign.
