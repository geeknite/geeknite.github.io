# Sushi Go! - Engaging Family Card Game, Perfect for Quick Game Nights

Sushi Go! is not just a card game; it’s a micro-culinary adventure that fits on a coffee table and in the brain of your average pun-loving gamer. In Geeknite style, think of it as a speed dating session for sushi: you snap up the cutest pieces, pass the rest, and hope your plate (and your scoring sheet) looks delicious when the whistle blows. It’s breezy to learn, fast to play, and surprisingly strategic for something that fits in your pocket-sized foodie fantasies.

## How to play in 15 minutes: a quick primer

The core loop is elegant in its simplicity: you’re drafting a hand of cards, one per turn, then passing the remainder to the left. When all cards of a round are exhausted, you tally points from the sets you collected. The game plays in three rounds for the standard 2–5 player count, with the end-game scoring (pudding cards) coming into play after the last round.

### Setup

- Shuffle the deck and deal seven cards to each player (the exact number can adjust slightly for player counts; in many families, seven is a comfortable standard).
- Place the draw pile in the center. The first player is chosen randomly or by initiative, and play proceeds clockwise.
- If you’re playing with the optional Chopsticks rule, each Chopsticks card goes into the scoring pool as another tactical lever.

### Draft and pass

- On your turn, choose one card from your hand to keep, then place the rest face-down and pass them to the left. Simultaneously, players reveal their chosen cards.
- The drafting continues until all cards are played. Then you move to scoring for the round.

### Scoring and round-end nuance

- Tempura: Collect two Tempura cards to earn 5 points. If you only have one Tempura, you get nothing—precision matters.
- Sashimi: Three Sashimi cards grant 10 points. It’s the classic “go big in one go” gamble.
- Nigiri: Egg (1), Salmon (2), Squid (3). Wasabi doesn’t alter the base values by itself; the trick is to pair Wasabi with a high-value Nigiri to triple the point payout. Yes, it’s a tiny, delicious risk-reward mechanic.
- Maki rolls: The player with the most Maki Roll icons gets 6 points, second-most gets 3; ties break by looking at how many Maki you hold compared to others.
- Pudding: End-game scoring lives on pudding cards. The most pudding cards earns 6 points; second-most earns 3; everyone else stays neutral. Pudding is the quiet hero that makes the final tally feel fair even if the rounds were chaotic.

### End of game

After three rounds (or more, if you’ve customized for your group), tally pudding points and total up the round scores. The winner is the chef with the highest total—though in practice, you’ll see a lot of competitive dining chairs around the table as players debate their final counts.

### The twist of Wasabi

Wasabi is the tiny “chef’s kiss” of the game. In the right moment, Wasabi triples your next Nigiri’s value. It creates a discipline: you want to time Wasabi to hit those big Nigiri moments, but you don’t want to lock yourself into bad adjacency where you’m pass over strong cards that could block your opponent’s scoring lines.

## Strategies and tips: get more points without turning it into a math problem

### Read the table, not just your hand

A core skill in Sushi Go! is anticipating what your next neighbor will pass back. If you can sense that your left-hand player is already chasing Nigiri, you can deny them access to specific cards by passing them earlier or focusing on complementary sets that require their cards to be less valuable to them.

### Wasabi timing is everything

If you can coordinate a Wasabi with a high-value Nigiri, it’s a small but mighty payoff. The risk is passing Wasabi away too early and losing that triple potential. If you’re not confident about which Nigiri will appear at the right moment, you can prioritize a safe card in the near term and commit to Wasabi later.

### The Tempura-Sashimi dance

Tempura rewards pairs; Sashimi rewards triples. The meta decision here is whether you’re going for reliable small wins or risking your fortune on a big two-card Tempura or three-card Sashimi payoff. It’s a dance: sometimes you collect two Tempura early hoping to hit the 5 points; sometimes you skip a high-value card to leave an opponent guessing.

### Maki as micro-control

Going for Maki gives you a “sure-ish” path to points, but it’s also a social negotiation: if you’re certain someone else will clinch the top Maki, you may choose to block or pivot to other scoring baskets. It’s not about pure math; it’s about table psychology in a tiny, delicious package.
