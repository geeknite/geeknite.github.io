Introduction

When you first open a Gloom box, you expect sadness to be contained in order to be unleashed. The Gloom family has a long tradition of burying its own within a blanket of transparent cards and ominous art, and the Cthulhu Expansion continues that grand tradition with more tentacles, more sorrow, and a suspiciously large number of eldritch beards. If the base game is a recipe for tragic family gatherings, the Cthulhu Expansion is where the pot boils over and the neighbors hear mysterious laughter from the attic. In this review I will walk you through what this expansion brings to the table, how it changes the tone, how it affects balance, and whether you should invite it to your next family game night or leave it chained in the attic with the other cursed dice.

Overview

Gloom has always stood out among card games for turning doom into a social sport. The Cthulhu Expansion leans into cosmic horror while keeping the core gimmick intact: your goal is to ink the most miserable lineage in the family, while ensuring your rivals end up with the worst fates imaginable. The expansion introduces a Mythos deck, more artifact and condition cards, and a handful of Lovecraftian twists that make eldritch rituals look suspiciously like a late night infomercial for misfortune.

What’s in the Box

- A set of new holo-like cards with a slightly more eldritch color palette that makes your eyes say no but your heart says yes to more gloom.
- Mythos deck that influences events, often in ways that reward players for causing chaos, if chaos comes in a fancy suit and a monocle.
- A handful of new character cards with Lovecraftian families and cursed traditions. Expect more haunted aunts, more ritualistic hobbies, and a surprising amount of debt to shadowy cosmic entities.
- New artifact cards, some of which are genuinely useful, some of which are delightful mood lighting for tabletop horror.
- A ritual token set and a few extra status markers to track the slow ascension of the unspeakable.
- A small mythos guide for new players who want to feel like they understand the cosmic order after the first couple of rounds.

Thematic Fit and Flavor

Lovecraft is not known for mercy, and this expansion leans into that vibe without turning the game into an exercise in existential dread that would require a therapist instead of a deck of cards. The artwork pairs antique elegance with an undercurrent of tentacled dread, giving you the sense that every card could be a family photo that suddenly morphs into a portal to a seaside nightmare. The expansion does a clever job of integrating with the base game. It does not rewrite the core rules; it enhances the mood and adds new decision points that feel meaningful rather than random. You will still be murdering your own characters and trying to drag everyone else down with you, but now you can do it through a ritual or a forbidden text discovered in an ancient library that smells suspiciously like a damp basement.

New Mechanics and Balance

The Mythos deck creates interlocking narrative threads that run through multiple rounds. As you draw mythos cards, you’ll encounter events that alter the timing of curses, accelerate or delay family misfortune, and occasionally reveal a cosmic truth that makes you realize your aunt’s secret is not just that she never leaves the house, it is that her fate is tied to a distant star and a cosmic contract with a floating, sentient teapot. The core mechanic of doom and gloom remains intact, but the expansion introduces new layers of strategy.

- Mythos events: These cards trigger at specific points or in response to certain actions, creating a shared sense of looming dread that can tilt the table from bitter rivalry to outright paranoia.
- Rituals and forbidden texts: Some cards enable players to perform rituals that accelerate the downfall of a rival, often at the cost of their own misery later on. They can be powerful tools for manipulation, but they require careful resource management and precise timing.
- Artifacts and curses: The new artifacts offer strong, but sometimes risky, benefits. They might grant a short term boost to your gloom score or alter how doom tokens accumulate. The curses, on the other hand, can be wonderfully petty or devastating, depending on how much you revel in the misfortune you’re dishing out.
- Interaction and pacing: Expect more table talk, more swift diplomatic sabotage, and a few moments where everyone silently nods in agreement that yes, this is exactly what the universe would throw at you if your family existed in a Lovecraftian setting.

Components Quality

The physical quality remains high. The card stock is sturdy enough for the repeated shuffles that a group of players will demand, and the art direction merges pastels with abyssal black in a way that reads well at the table. The transparent cards that Gloom fans know and love get an extra layer of polish here, and the new mythos deck sits neatly alongside the base game without feeling tacked on. The box insert is serviceable, and there is a little less risk of misplacing a card in the chaos of play because the expansion deck has clearly labeled sections.

Setup and Teardown

Putting the expansion into your game night adds a few steps, but nothing nightmarishly complicated. You will set out the mythos deck and the new family cards, shuffle in the artifact tokens, and decide whether the group wants to start with any particular mythos effects in play. The first few rounds require a little more attention to the mythos rules, but once you get the hang of them, the flow returns to familiar Gloom territory: a push and pull of fellow players tugging at your fate while your own doom track snakes toward a final, ridiculous misfortune.

Playthrough Sketch: A Thematic Round

To give you a sense of flow, imagine a six-handed table where you are managing a family called the Eldritch-Smyth clan. The opening hand gives you two mild curses on your matriarch, a new artifact that increases your gloom by a modest amount, and a mythos event that hints at a coming cosmic misalignment. By turn three, you realize the mythos deck is not just a flavor boost; it actively nudges the table toward a dramatic twist—perhaps exposing a hidden relationship between two families or forcing a ritual to delay a doom outcome that would otherwise end your lineage. The ritual card is tempting, but it requires sacrificing a tally of your own resources—resources you had earmarked for a different, more personal tragedy. You decide to gamble, and the table appreciates the audacity even as a rival finishes your matriarch with a particularly elegant, tentacled flourish. That moment captures the expansion: it rewards bold choices, punishes lazy play, and adds a playful sense of cosmic absurdity that players will either embrace or curse in equal measure.

Strategy and Tips

- Embrace tempo shifts: The mythos events often reward players who read the table and anticipate the long game rather than the next turn. If you can smell a looming tragic arc, you will be better prepared to either accelerate or delay your own personal doom accordingly.
- Balance risk and reward: Artifacts can swing the balance, but they often come with a price. If you can time your artifact use for the moment when an opponent is about to leap ahead, you can reclaim a surprising amount of ground.
- Use rituals judiciously: Rituals are powerful accelerators of doom, but they require precise conditions. A well-timed ritual can derail a rival’s plan, but a poorly timed one can leave you overextended and vulnerable.
- Play with the theme: The expansion thrives when players lean into the Lovecraft vibe. Embrace the eerie mood and let it inform your decisions, rather than just treating the cards as mechanics.
- Team play vs free-for-all: The mythos deck can feel more enjoyable in a group that enjoys social interaction and misdirection. If your group loves heavy negotiation and bluffing, this expansion shines; if you prefer tight mechanical play, it can still deliver but with a bit more downtime.
- Track the doom clock: Some cycles accelerate doom tokens in waves. A conscious plan to push or slow a few cycles can be the difference between a spectacular finish and a ruinous collapse.
- Mind the table chatter: The mythos layer rewards players who narrate their own misfortune with flair. A quick aside about a cosmic bargain can shift sympathy or suspicion in your favor.

Comparison to Base Gloom and Other Expansions

Base Gloom relies on a clean, elegant cycle: draw a card, apply your misery, watch the gloom score accumulate, and scheme to outlive the others with the most spectacularly unfortunate results. The Cthulhu Expansion respects that lineage but adds a thematic spine that can be treated as optional or central depending on your mood. It does not overshadow the core loop; rather, it expands the storytelling possibilities. If you have played other Gloom expansions, you will recognize a familiar pattern: more cards, more interactions, and more opportunities to craft a theme of tragic family misfortune.

One potential pitfall is density. The mythos effects add complexity, and in groups that prefer quick rounds, the additional setup and rule-checking can feel a touch heavy. On the flip side, if you have a gamer circle that loves storytelling and chaotic fun, the Cthulhu Expansion can become the star of the show. It becomes a way to orchestrate a Moody Lovecraftian evening without needing a full roleplaying game—just a table, a dozen sleepless nights, and a lot of pop culture references.

Art and Production Value

The art direction continues to strike a balance between whimsy and dread. The imagery is not meant to scare your grandmother into losing sleep; it is designed to evoke a sense of strange wonder and the queasy delight of a family portrait that fought back. The color choices swing toward moody blues, deep purples, and a touch of sickly green, which sells the Lovecraftian atmosphere without sacrificing readability. The typography remains crisp, and card backs are consistent with the base game so your deck stack still looks like a cohesive, terrifying family album rather than a mismatched pile of spooky cards.

Replayability and Longevity

Gloom rewards theme and iteration. The Cthulhu Expansion adds a larger pool of character types and mythos scenarios, which translates to a higher ceiling for replayability. You can mix and match family cards with different mythos seeds across multiple sessions to produce a wide variety of narratives. The number of unique combinations grows rapidly as you introduce artifact and ritual variations. If you love chasing the perfect grim arc, you will find yourself returning to this expansion for many nights of theater and laughs that sound like coughs from a long hallway.

Solo and Co-op Variants

While Gloom is naturally social, a handful of groups enjoy solo or small party variants. The expansion supports such play by offering mythos situational cards that can scale with a single player or two players. The solo mode tends to emphasize self-sabotage and personal tragedy with a focus on managing resources and timing. For two players, the expansion can increase interaction by introducing dual curses racing toward a climactic finish, which can be extremely entertaining when both players lean into the roleplay aspect of Lovecraftian doom.

Playtime and Learning Curve

Expect slightly longer sessions than base Gloom due to the mythos layer, but not by a prohibitive margin. The first game or two might feel like a puzzle you are not yet invited to solve, but the rules click quickly once you see a couple of rounds in motion. For your players, that means a small bump in cognitive load, but a significant increase in the joy of orchestrated misery. If your group likes a good long narrative arc with foreshadowing and a final, crisp punchline, this expansion will feel like a natural extension rather than a weird detour.

Accessibility and Learning Curve

The expansion does not bury newcomers in legend books. The mythos deck is the main extra burden, but the core mechanics remain intact and approachable. A patient group can walk through a first game with a quick teach and a shared grin at the unfolding calamities. For players who enjoy a storytelling layer, the mythos events provide a reliable hook to keep everyone talking and negotiating rather than waiting for a single decisive draw. If you value a lean, fast push your luck experience, you may want to merge mythos effects gradually until you feel comfortable weaving them into the rhythm of your table.

Design Details and Play Tips

The best praise for this expansion is that it does not feel tacked on. The mythos deck exists with a purpose, not as a gimmick. Its events carry weight because they are anchored in the recurring themes of cosmic dread and family melodrama that Gloom fans know and love. The rituals require you to plan multiple turns ahead, which is a rarity in the base game where most players are trying to cheat fate in the moment. The tension created by a rival’s cunning play or a sudden mythos event is genuine, which a large part of what keeps Gloom’s community coming back for more.

If you are new to Gloom, approach the Cthulhu Expansion as a high class upgrade to the existing experience rather than a replacement. It preserves the central mechanics—ink your misery, stack gloom, unveil the final tragedy—while layering in size and theming. As you grow more comfortable with the mythos layer, you will discover new strategies: which family pairs with which artifact, which ritual combinations yield the best dramatic reveals, and how to time your curses so the finale arrives exactly as you need it to cement your victory in a most spectacular way.

Cultural Moments and Humor

Gloom as a game thrives on the shared joke that a family gathering is the perfect place to reveal long buried secrets. The Cthulhu Expansion leans into this idea with a wink that the universe itself is a family caretaker who forgot to pay the cosmic electricity bill. The humor remains gentle enough to avoid descending into snarky cruelty, but sharp enough to prompt genuine laughs when a player reveals a curse that doubles as a perfect punchline for a dinner table fantasy. It is satire dressed as a card game, and that balance is what makes the expansion so much fun to bring into the rotation.

Final Verdict and Recommendation

If you already own the base Gloom and enjoy the atmosphere of misfortune, the Cthulhu Expansion delivers a robust and thematically evocative extension that rewards thoughtful planning and bold social maneuvering. It adds enough complexity to keep experienced players engaged without alienating newcomers. It is not a mandatory upgrade, but it is a highly recommended one for groups who adore the Lovecraft vibe, dramatic reveals, and a table full of friends who relish the chance to narrate a family tragedy with tentacles in attendance. If you crave more story, more tension, and more reasons to stare at the ceiling while the last card is laid down, this expansion will be your friend for many sessions to come.

Short Take: A solid, thematically rich expansion that respects the Gloom DNA while giving you extra lore, extra laughs, and extra gloom. It is not a casual add on; it is a genuine enhancement that invites you to lean into the madness with your crew, and to revel in the shared experience of misfortune made wonderfully theatrical.
