## Introduction

If you've ever wrangled six heroes, a handful of undead, and a handful of dice in Zombicide Black Plague without losing your mind, you're either a saint or you own a character tray that doesn't squeal under the weight of cardboard. ## What is this thing?

At its core, the FDM ZBP Character Dashboard is a modular tray system designed to hold the various character cards, action tokens, wound counters, and equipment tiles that come with Zombicide Black Plague. It is marketed as a “character dashboard” because, you know, dashboards are where you put your car’s speed and—okay, I’ll stop with the car metaphors. The point is: it's a tray that organizes your heroes so you can locate stats, items, and abilities faster than a goblin can roll five fake dice and pretend to be helpful.

## Design and materials

The design ethos of a board game accessory is often "function over form," but there’s nothing wrong with a dash of flair when you’re doing something as mundane as sorting swords and shields. The FDM dashboard is typically a 3D-printed piece (as the name implies), with multiple slots and wells designed to cradle Zombicide tokens and cards. The build quality can vary depending on the printer, filament, and your tolerance for minor warps around the edges. If you’re a perfectionist, you’ll probably do a quick post-processing pass: light sanding, a coat of primer, and perhaps a spray of matte sealant to keep the glossy goblin grease from smudging your hero sheets.

In terms of materials, you’ll often find the dashboard printed in PLA or PETG. PLA is the friend of the beginner: forgiving, cheap, and easy to print, but it’s a little brittle under sustained pressure. PETG is tougher, with better heat resistance and a more flexible edge, though it can be a pain to calibrate (and by “calibrate,” I mean “don’t warp when you print at higher speeds”). If you’re into printed accessories, you may also encounter resin prints, which can achieve finer detail but demand a post-cure to be usable on the table and maybe a tiny sacrifice of your wrist strength from snapping off supports.

## Fit and finish

One of the trickiest parts of any tabletop tray is whether the slots align with the official components. Zombicide Black Plague tokens and hero boards aren’t perfectly consistent in every release, so a dashboard that’s generous in its tolerances tends to be more forgiving. The FDM dashboard typically features:

- Card slots sized for standard Zombicide character sheets
- Clear wells for wounds, action tokens, and equipment tiles
- A modular layout that can be rearranged to suit the player count and scenario
- A few color accents to differentiate hero types or factions

Now, if you’re picky about alignment, you’ll want to test-fit with your own tokens before a tournament night. The worst feeling in the world is to realize that your ghoul’s ghastly blade token doesn’t fit in its slot, forcing you to tilt it, swear softly, and then pretend it’s all part of the ambiance.

## Setup and usage

Setting up the dashboard is a two-step affair: print and assemble. If you’re lucky, your printer ran a clean job and you can just pop the pieces out and start placing cards and tokens. If you’re less fortunate, you’ll be sanding edges and aligning tiny magnets, which is a vibe regardless of whether you’re into metal detectors or malevolent cardboard.

To use the dashboard effectively, you’ll want to organize hero sheets by class or by the power level you’re playing. The card slots help reduce the frantic flipping that often occurs when you have to check a special ability mid-battle. The wells keep tokens from rolling off the board during a “vore” attack (okay, a normal zombie attack, but I like to add drama). In short, the dashboard can transform your mental chaos into a sort of hacky, organized cool.

## Features in detail

### Card slots

The heart of the dashboard is the card slots. They need to hold hero sheets and item cards without bending them into a pretzel. In testing, most standard hero sheets fit with a little room to spare. If your cards run a touch wider due to a slightly different printer profile, you can gently widen the slot by sanding the inner rail with a fine-grit sandpaper. It’s a small tweak, but it prevents you from having to resort to aggressive card gymnastics mid-mission.

### Token wells and overlays

Wells for wounds and action tokens matter when the undead start to pour in. A good dashboard keeps these tokens in clearly labeled wells, reducing table noise and miscounts. Some builds incorporate acrylic overlays or silicone mats that rest on top of the wells to provide a silent floor beneath the tokens. If you like to customize, you can add your own overlays with a label print to indicate what each well holds.

### Iconography and readability

The design often includes raised icons or etched marks to indicate what each slot is for. This is one of those small details that matters in the heat of battle: you glance, you know. It’s the difference between “I’m out of bolts” and “I’m just replacing a bolt with a sword, obviously.”

## Durability and maintenance

Durability depends on your printing settings. PLA prints are acceptable but brittle under stress. PETG prints fare better under heat and stress, at the cost of a longer, more stubborn print job. If you want a compromise, you can print at moderate temperatures with a slightly higher infill to increase rigidity. The maintenance is simple: wipe down with a damp cloth after a session, avoid soaking in water if you’re using PLA, and apply a light protective finish if you’re painting. The real trick is to avoid letting the surface accumulate grime from sticky hands; a simple wipe is often enough to keep it looking good after many sessions.
