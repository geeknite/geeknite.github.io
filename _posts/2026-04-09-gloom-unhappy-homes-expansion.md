## Overview: what this expansion actually does

Unhappy Homes adds a new chapter to the Gloom saga by injecting the house into the tragedy. The core idea remains the same: you want your family to suffer more than your opponents’ families, and you do this by playing gloom cards on their characters to increase their gloom tone while amplifying your own lives' misery to keep your score readable only by those who enjoy long-term therapy bills.

What you get in the box is a mischievous blend of new house cards, mood cards, haunt mechanics, and a handful of optional house rules that make you reconsider your life choices at least thrice during setup. The new cards are thematically tied to home life gone wrong: malfunctioning stairs, rebellious appliances, and wallpaper that seems to whisper your darkest family secrets at night. The art direction leans into the spooky yet whimsical, so if you have a soft spot for cute cottages with a creepy undertone, you are in for a visual treat. The components feel sturdy, the card stock is perfectly card-gaming acceptable, and the tokens have just enough heft to prevent accidental soul-crushing shuffles during two players who insist on arguing about the weather midgame.

## Box contents and components: a closer look

- 16 new house cards that turn every residence into a character with preferences, egos, and existential dread
- 12 mood cards that act as portable sadness NPCs you can deploy at will
- 6 haunt cards that you trigger by meeting unusual conditions, like the moment you realize the kitchen has been plotting against you
- 1 small rule booklet that is short enough to be charming and long enough to remind you that you should have read the base game rules first
- 20 gloom tokens, slightly shinier than the original tokens, to make you believe in glittery sorrow
- 4 scenario quickstart sheets for players who want to jump straight into the action without a prelude that asks for a therapist's number
- A single-page reference card that summarizes new rules in a way that makes you feel both smarter and more confused at the same time

The new house cards are the star of the show. They typically feature a house name, a derived mood or trait for the household, and a couple of special abilities that interact with gloom dice and haunt cards. Some examples might include a Victorian mansion with a wallpaper that alters gloom thresholds, or a mid century rambler with a basement that occasionally doubles as a safe haven for your opponent to dump gloom on your team. The mood cards tilt the emotional balance in a friendly way, letting you decide whether to give your opponents a little break or a lot of breakable things you can’t return once you’ve handed them the card.

The haunt cards, as usual, demand a bit of attention. They typically trigger when certain conditions are met, such as when a particular house card is facing a doom or when both players have a house card that shares a distinct theme. Haunts are the spice that makes late-game decisions feel urgent, not simply inevitable. They can swing the board by lowering the overall gloom for one hand while skyrocketing it for another, which is a delight for players who love the dramatic irony of watching other people suffer while you keep it together enough to win.

And then there is the mood pile, which is a fun little twist on the standard gloom tokens: mood cards that you can slide into play to shift the mood of the game in subtle but meaningful ways. They can reduce or increase the rate at which gloom tokens accumulate, depending on the cards you have in play and the emotional weather forecast you pretend to read aloud before each turn.

## How to set up Unhappy Homes

Setting up is similar to the base game with a few considerations. First, clear your table space of any illusions of control. Shuffle the new house cards and lay out a small neighborhood of gloom prompts. Shuffle the mood cards into a separate pile and place the haunt deck within reach. You may decide to adopt the optional house rules for a more chaotic experience, such as allowing a single haunted card to affect multiple players if you place it in a central location. Do not worry if this sounds terrifying; that is exactly the point.

During setup, you should also decide whether to play standard rules or fast track rules for experienced gloom fans who want a few extra minutes of victory pride poured into their cup of despair. In either case, the new components are designed to thread into your existing strategy without requiring a full rewrite of your play style, which is nice for people who want to feel clever without algebra homework on a Tuesday evening.

## Mechanics: how the expansion changes the game play

Unhappy Homes sticks to the crunchy core of gloom: you want to cause gloom on your opponents while keeping your own family slightly happier than a candle on a rainiest day. The addition of the house cards means you now have multiple dream houses to mismanage rather than a single fixed stage. The mood cards behave like thematic micro-sabotages, offering momentary advantages at the risk of long-term penalties. The haunt cards push you toward riskier plays that require a bit more planning and a willingness to let your plans unravel in spectacular fashion.

From a mechanics perspective, there are a few notable shifts:

- The threshold system is subtly altered. Houses now have mood thresholds that can be adjusted by the mood cards you play and by the haunt events that trigger mid-turn. This creates moments where one bad draw can domino into a cascade of misfortune, or conversely, a brilliant chain of well-timed plays can turn near disaster into a near victory.
- There is a new synergy mechanic between house cards. When two or more houses share a thematic link, they can amplify each other’s effects in ways that reward thematic thinking rather than brute-force gloom application. This encourages players to plan around themes like family dysfunction, haunted kitchens, and staircases that refuse to be properly leveled.
- Haunts introduce an emergent gameplay loop. Rather than simply increasing gloom on the target, haunts can cause ancillary effects like reshuffling cards, redistributing tokens, or forcing players to swap a card with an opponent under penalty of dramatic sighs. The sense of momentum is real, and it is deliciously devastating.

The core appeal of Unhappy Homes is how it pushes players to balance long-term strategy with short-term chaos. The best moments come from when a carefully laid out plan is suddenly upended by a haunt card or a well-timed mood effect. It feels like a dramatic sitcom where every character is a risk factor and the punchline is always just around the next corner.

## Setup tips and playing tips: getting the best out of the expansion

- Start with a practice round. Allow both players to run through a couple of turns on the house cards you think will be central to your strategy. This helps reduce confusion and the inevitable arguments about rules semantics that accompany any new expansion.
- Favor mood cards that give you temporary bonuses but plan for the long term. The expansion rewards long-term planning more than a one-turn miracle, which is a hallmark of playful strategy rather than pure chaos.
- Use haunts strategically. Do not fear the haunt cards; embrace them as a way to create dramatic arc. The best players use haunts to pivot the game from a one-note misery to a layered tragedy with escalating stakes.
- Consider the optional house rules if you want a more chaotic, high-energy session. These rules are optional for a reason: they are a little wild, a little ridiculous, and a lot of fun if you are in the mood for theatrical despair.
- Manage your table talk. Gloom has always thrived on storytelling. Use the mood cards to prompt silly or grim mini narratives about your families. The more you lean into the storytelling, the more you enjoy the experience, even if the end result is a little less competitive.

## Strategies: how to survive and thrive in the gloom

- Thematic drafting matters. Build your deck around a theme that resonates with your playgroup. It could be a family with a weaponized vacuum cleaner, a kitchen that doubles as a portal to a forgotten era, or a staircase that seems to have personal ambitions. The thematic cohesion makes your plays feel purposeful rather than random.
- Prioritize early mood control. In the first few rounds, the mood card plays will shape the trajectory of the game. If you can gain the mood advantage early, you give yourself more options to respond to haunts and house card interactions later.
- Timing is everything. The difference between a successful haunt and a failed haunt can be a single turn. Watch the other players' boards and anticipate the moments when the haunt will trigger. You want to be ready with a counterplay that does not reveal all your cards too soon.
- Manage risk and reward. The cards in Unhappy Homes are designed to reward players who take calculated risks. Do not be afraid to push a little on your opponent, but do not overspend gloom tokens to the point where you become a liability for your own team.
- Use your opponent's misery to your advantage. The expansion is a social game as much as a mechanical one. If you can read your opponents and craft a narrative that makes them feel like they are under the weight of the world, you will gain psychological leverage that translates into better board results.

## Solo and cooperative play: does the expansion work for non-competitive sessions?

Yes, for players who enjoy gloom with a twist, Unhappy Homes provides a surprisingly satisfying solo variant. The solo mode tightens the tempo and uses a slightly different balance to ensure the player does not feel overwhelmed by runaway gloom. The coop options require a bit of trust, which is a tall order in any despair-filled party game, but it can yield some of the most entertaining misadventure of all. The narrative prompts are particularly strong in cooperative play, offering little vignettes that feel almost like a miniature haunted anthology while you shuffle gloom tokens into a big, dramatic pile of shared woe.

If you are considering solo or co-op, plan on a longer session. Unhappy Homes isn't a lightweight filler; it is a deeper dive into the misery well with a structure that rewards patience, planning, and the occasional ridiculous risk that pays off in spectacular fashion.

## Player count, duration, and replayability

Unhappy Homes scales well from two to four players, with some additional fun opportunities in larger groups if you house rule a few extra pesky haunts. Duration stays within the comfort zone for gloom games: long enough to feel substantial, but short enough that you can finish a session without needing a therapist after every third turn. Replayability is enhanced by the number of house combinations and the way haunts and moods interact. Thematic combos yield different play experiences, and the new cards ensure that no two games feel exactly the same, which is a win in a game that already thrives on variation.

## Value and final verdict

If you already adore Gloom and crave more of the same delicious misery served with a side of whimsical horror, Unhappy Homes is a natural next step. It preserves the core charm of the base game while introducing meaningful mechanical depth and a few new layers of strategy. It is not a must-have for casual players who are not into the gloom aesthetic, but for fans of the line who want more cards, more dilemmas, and more ways to stack up the house cards and watch the drama unfold, this expansion pays dividends.

For collectors and completionists, Unhappy Homes offers enough new content and quality to justify a purchase even if you already own multiple gloom expansions. The production quality is solid, the art remains charming, and the new mechanics provide fresh avenues for cunning plays and storytelling alike. If you believe in gaming as performance art and you enjoy watching the narrative arc of misfortune unfold with elegant chaos, this expansion will bring a smile to your face even as the tokens pile up on your board.
