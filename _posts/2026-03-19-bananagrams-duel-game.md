## How to play Bananagrams Duel
Bananagrams Duel is built on the same DNA as Bananagrams—the wild scramble of letters, the joy of turning scrabble-like constraints into a free-form word grid, and the thrill of discovering that your best word was hiding behind two vowels you initially discarded. The Duel variant adds two players, a timer, and a dash of direct competition that makes you second-guess the neatness of your grid under the gaze of another person who is also calculating your likely next move.

### Setup
- Each player takes a rack and starts with a handful of tiles pulled from the central bag. We recommend 15–21 tiles per player depending on desired pace; the official Duel variant often uses a shorter pool to keep rounds brisk.
- The timer is set, or at least takes a few seconds to realize it’s the boss of the table. The aim remains simple: form a valid grid with your tiles, where words cross and interlock like family drama at Thanksgiving.
- The rest of the tiles sit in the bag, gossiping about potential word formations you’ll conjure later in the game.

### Core rules recap (the two-player twist)
- Build a grid with your tiles forming valid words that read left-to-right and top-to-bottom. Your grid should resemble a constellation, not a crime scene.
- You may rearrange your grid at any time. The only limit is your sanity and how many vowels you can juggle without crying.
- The first player to use all of their tiles wins the round. The losing player must gracefully accept the banana-shaped fate of being second best in a fruit salad of words.
- When a word is rejected by the dictionary gods (or you realize it’s not actually a word), you can swap tiles from the pool or adjust your grid. In Duel, speed matters, but so does minimal chaos. 

### The duel timer and its pressure
The timer is the wild card here. When it buzzes, you draw more tiles or, in some variants, you must peel a tile onto your rack. The timer is designed to inject bite-sized pressure without making the game feel unplayable. It’s the board game equivalent of a friendly push into a pool on a hot day—start hot, stay hotter, but never drown in the chaos.

### Dictionaries and word validity
Officially, Bananagrams relies on standard English words recognized by reputable dictionaries. In Geeknite fashion, we encourage the fun of flexible spelling in the spirit of friendly competition, but unless you’re playing with a ruleset you trust, you should probably avoid attempting city names and highly-technical terms that will make your grandmother cry.

## Strategy and pacing tips
If you want to win without mutiny, you’ll need more than a bag of letters. You’ll need a plan and a willingness to rewire your brain on the fly.

### Rack management
- Build a balanced rack with a healthy mix of vowels and consonants. A rack full of consonants is a doom-spell for your ability to form words quickly.
- Reserve a few tiles that commonly lead to cross-words, such as ER, ING, and the ever-useful A, E, I, O, U. Having these on standby makes it easier to thread longer words as your grid grows.
- Don’t be afraid to reorganize your grid completely if you see a better layout emerging. The best word structures look like a cosmic map once you’ve trimmed the edges and forced a few cross connections.

### When to peel, when to stay steady
- Peel when the current set of tiles no longer yields productive moves, or when you spot a tile in the bag that can unlock a cascade of words.
- Stay steady when you’re in the middle of a clean grid and you know you can finish with a tight line of finishing words. Delays can be deadly under timer pressure.

## Variants and house rules
Two-player games thrive on house rules, and Duel is no exception. Here are a few variants you can try to tailor the experience:
- Quick Duel: drop the starting tiles to 12–15 per player for a 10–15 minute sprint. Great for lunch breaks or chaotic family game nights.
- Banana Swap: allow one free tile swap per player per game to nudge back into a playable grid when things go wrong.
- Timer Tight: shorten the timer to increase pace and raise the pressure, perfect for adrenaline-fueled gaming or for streaming purposes.
- Cooperative Duel: for players who want to test teamwork, allow two players to combine grids under the same timer competitor-style, sharing racks and working toward a joint victory.
