## How to Play: Choose Your Target Mechanics Explained
This is the heart of the Bullseye Edition: the moment when your fate (and that of your fellow players) might hinge on your ability to select a target with style. The core UNO rules still apply—match color or number to the previous card, draw cards if you can’t, pass the turn—but the Bullseye Edition spices things up with a targeted action layer.

### Setup and turn flow
1) Each player draws seven cards to begin. The remainder becomes a draw pile. The top card flips up to start the discard pile.
2) On your turn, you normally play a card that matches either color or number. If you can’t play, you draw a card and your turn ends.
3) When you play a special card that triggers a Bullseye moment (the exact card types are listed in the rule booklet, but you’ll quickly remember the ones with a bullseye icon), you pick a target. The bullseye tokens are used to signify the intensity of the target’s punishment or the protective nuance of the action you’ve chosen.
4) The targeted player must respond according to the card’s effect. This can involve drawing extra cards, swapping hands under certain conditions, or discarding cards in a manner that reshapes the table’s balance.
5) Play continues clockwise until someone shouts UNO and then wins by finishing their last card. The winner might celebrate with a tiny victory dance, or at least a small confident nod—whatever keeps the group entertained long enough to forget the snack budget.

### Targeting options: what can you actually target?
- Draw penalties: force a specific player to draw a fixed number of cards, which can dramatically shift lead changes.
- Color-block: direct a target to skip turns or reverse direction, turning a calm game into chaos in the blink of an eye.
- Swap-and-hope: a card lets you propose swapping hands with a chosen opponent, introducing strategy but also a fair amount of social risk—after all, no one wants to swap away their peace of mind along with their cards.
- Protected pass: some bullseye moments allow the targeted player to pass or reduce penalties by playing a matching card themselves, creating a moment of tactical Tetris on the table.

The variety here isn’t just a gimmick. It forces players to think beyond the “play a card that matches” instinct. It rewards memory, risk assessment, and a little bit of theatrical bluffing when you declare your chosen target out loud with dramatic flair. On the other hand, it can also invite petty chaos if everyone chooses to target the loudest voice in the room. And yes, that is a feature you will either adore or loathe depending on how much you like a board-game stage whisper battle.

### House rules and optional tweaks
A good board game always invites you to tailor its chaos. The Bullseye Edition scales nicely with some light house rules:
- Proximity rule: if you’re within three seats of a target, you can reduce the card penalty by one. It adds a little suspense without turning into a math seminar.
- Timed rounds: for family gatherings, you can cap rounds at 10 or 15 minutes to keep the energy high.
- Bullseye cooldown: once per game, a player can nullify a target’s effect if they can play two cards in a single turn. It’s dramatic and a little silly, which is exactly what we want in a party game.

## House Rules: Tweaks for Your Table
House rules are where you make a game truly your own. Here are some ideas that fit with Bullseye’s spirit:
- The Target Auction: before any targeting action, players bid with small chips (or pretend points) on the target they want to influence. The highest bid wins the right to decide the effect, but you must pay with a card from your hand.
- The Mercy Rule: a “mercy round” can be invoked once per game, allowing all players to discard down to one card if the table agrees. It creates a dramatic swing let’s you escape with style.
- The Bullseye Bust: if someone makes a play that instantly turns the round, allow a one-turn penalty bounce-back to equalize the score. This keeps the game from spiraling into three-turn chaos.
- The Quiet Round: if the table goes too loud, implement a silent round where you must communicate your target and intent with only gestures and grunts. It’s stupidly fun and surprisingly challenging.

 House rules aren’t mandatory, but they’re a friendly invitation to tailor the chaos to your group’s vibe.
