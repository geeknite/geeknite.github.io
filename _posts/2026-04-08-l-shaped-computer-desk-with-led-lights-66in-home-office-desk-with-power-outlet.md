## Power Outlet and Cable Management
Power outlets on desks can be a life saver and a hazard if not properly designed. A built-in outlet is convenient, but you want to ensure it is properly rated for the devices you plug in and that it is arranged to reduce clutter. Here is what you should expect and monitor:

- Outlet count and configuration: Most desks offer a handful of outlets, sometimes including USB ports. This is handy for charging phones, tablets, and wireless peripherals. If you rely on a workspace PC, you might need more outlets for additional devices like a camera capture card, a microcontroller, or a small NAS. Balance is key; too many outlets can tempt you into a cable spaghetti situation.
- Cable management: The desk should provide channels, clips, or a tray under the top surface to route cables away from your wrists. A well-hidden set of channels reduces snagging and makes cleaning easier. If the desk offers integrated cable management, test it by plugging in a few devices and simulating daily movement - especially the moment you stand up and you realize you just staged a tiny universe of wires onto the floor.
- Safety and heat: The power strip should handle the collective load of your gear without overheating. Look for certified surge protection and a reasonable maximum wattage rating. If in doubt, check the load calculator from your device vendor and map out how many devices you realistically plan to run at the same time.

A good rule of thumb is to do the math on your setup and allocate the outlets to the hardware that consumes the most power. The LED system itself is typically low-powered; placing it on a separate channel or using a controlled dimming function prevents it from hogging the energy budget of the moment you flip on a couple of 3.5 mm lamps on your desk with huge amounts of mood lighting.
