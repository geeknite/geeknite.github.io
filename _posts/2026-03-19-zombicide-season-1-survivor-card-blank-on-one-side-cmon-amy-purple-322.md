## How to use the blank side in play and how not to ruin it
If you are adventurous and decide to embrace the blank side as part of your campaign, here are a few practical guidelines to ensure the experience remains fun rather than frustrating:

- Treat blank as a homebrew starting option: you choose a backstory and an optional starting item or special rule. Example: Amy chooses a makeshift wrench as a melee-start tool and gains a small ability to re-roll a single zombie bite per game session. Remember to write this on your copy the first time you play so everyone agrees on the rules for your session.
- Use it as a campaign log: jot down what you decide Amy’s custom rule is after each scenario. This helps with continuity across multiple sessions and provides a fun memory for later when you flip through the campaign log at the end of the year.
- Keep it balanced: since you are adding a house rule, inform other players and ensure the effect does not trivialize the difficulty or trivialize the Zombie Horde. The point is to enhance story, not to create a one-person win button.
- Protect it with sleeves if you value it as a collectible: the blank side might be more prone to scratches or notes, depending on how aggressively you shuffle. A good card sleeve keeps the art pristine and the comfort of a well-loved game intact.
- Don’t forget the rest of the deck: a house rule for one card should harmonize with other variations you have in play. The last thing you want is a chaotic meta where every player tries to outdo the others with “creative” survivors. Harmony over chaos, unless your table is into chaos with good vibes.

If you want a quick DIY starter guide for customizing the blank side, consider these steps:

1) Pick a color for your new rule text that contrasts with the card print. A whiteboard marker works for testing, but a fine-tip permanent marker looks professional and long-lasting.
2) Write a short ability or flavor text that clearly defines what the starting advantage is and how it scales during the game.
3) Add a page marker or a small sticker patch to indicate the change so you can revert easily if your campaign ends up in a different direction.
4) Take a photo of the changed card for your campaign log so you can show it to new players who join mid-campaign. A visual reference beats a thousand words of text when introducing house rules to new friends.
