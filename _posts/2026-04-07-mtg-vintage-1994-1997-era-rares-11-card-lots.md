## Condition, NM vs LP, and what you’re actually paying for
The famous card whisperer question: what does NM or LP actually mean in practice? In the 1990s, production quality varied a lot, and grading standards drifted. Today, NM typically means the card shows no more than minor wear visible to the naked eye under standard lighting, with crisp corners, sharp edges, and no creases. LP implies more visible wear: light whitening at edges, minor corner blunting, and sometimes minor surface imperfections that don’t scream “destroyed value,” but do whisper “please check the fine print.”

Here are practical tips for evaluating NM/LP for vintage rares:
- Edges: Look for whitening, chipping, or visible color loss. The Power Nine often has pristine edges on top-condition copies, while mid-era rares may show more age-localized wear.
- Corners: Points should be sharp. A soft corner can significantly impact value, especially on high-demand prints.
- Centering: Vintage printing had looser standards on centering; but a reasonable balance matters for display and value. A miscut or heavy centering shift can hamper grading.
- Surface: Look for scratches, scuffs, or printer lines. Some surface marks are tolerable if they don’t affect playability or the card’s aesthetic aura.
- Gloss: The “gloss factor” is real—older printings can display gloss loss or cloudiness that affects photography, which matters when you’re trying to sell or trade online.

A good rule: ask for multiple high-resolution images, including angled shots to catch edge wear, and request a trusted third-party grading reference if you’re buying sight-unseen across great distances.

## The market mechanics you might encounter
Vintage markets don’t move in straight lines; they dance a jittery waltz between nostalgia and practical value. In the 1994-1997 era, a few dynamics were particularly pronounced:
- Power Nine premium: The big-ticket items can single-handedly swing a lot’s price. The exact edition (Alpha, Beta, Unlimited) and the card’s condition are paramount.
- Print runs and reprints: Some cards appeared in multiple printings with similar art but different borders, which can affect both desirability and price.
- Grading impact: Modern grading (PSA, BGS) helps normalize price across buyers, but desyncs between raw NM/LP expectations and graded outcomes exist. Be mindful of how a lot will feel after grading is applied.
- Display and storage: Vintage cards are best shown in protective sleeves, top loaders, and proper binders to avoid edge wear. A lot’s buyer gets extra value when packing materials and handling were with care.
- Shipping risk: The 1990s cards are old friends with a fragile past. Proper packaging, insurance, and tracking dramatically reduce the risk of mail-day heartbreak.

## Buying tips: what to ask and what to beware
When you’re eyeing an 11-card NM/LP lot from this era, a succinct buyer’s checklist helps:
- Documentation: Does the listing include clear photos, a grading reference, and confirmation of the card’s edition (Alpha/Beta/Unlimited/Revised, etc.)?
- Provenance: Is there a supplier story, prior consignor, or any available grading certificates? Provenance can matter, especially for ultra-rare prints.
- Condition notes: Are NM/LP ratings stated for each card, or is the lot described in aggregate? If the latter, request per-card condition details.
- Return policy: Vintage cards are a long-term investment for many buyers. A fair return policy and clear terms protect both sides.
- Shipping and insurance: Ask for protective packaging standards and whether the lot ships with full insurance. A fragile 11-card cluster is not something you want bouncing around in a bubble mailer.
- Authentication and reprints: If a card’s legitimacy is uncertain, request authentication or a trusted third-party assessment. The last thing you want is a mismatch between what you buy and what you fetch later on the market.

### The packaging and shipping realities
- Card sleeves: New, acid-free sleeves protect the edges and corners. A reputable seller uses either their own branded packaging or standard industry practice.
- Top loaders: A rigid top-loader or a semi-rigid sleeve helps protect corners during transit.
