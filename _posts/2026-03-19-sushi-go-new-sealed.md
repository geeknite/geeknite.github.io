## How to Play (brief refresher with edition-specific tweaks)

Sushi Go is a three- to five-minute-per-round drafting game. Each round, players pass a hand of cards around the table, choosing one card to keep and then passing the rest to the next player. You’re collecting combinations of sushi dishes to form scoring sets. The challenge is balancing short-term points from single cards with longer-term bonuses from collecting certain combos that show up in later rounds. The New Sealed edition doesn’t alter the core mechanics; it simply clarifies some of the scoring and streamlines some of the UI prompts so you can focus on the “eat, score, repeat” loop.

### Setup and round flow (simple, but not simplistic)
- Each player gets a scorepad and a tray (if you’re fancy) or a napkin (if you’re extra grounded).
- Shuffle the deck and deal a hand of cards to each player. The exact number depends on player count, but the flow remains consistent across variants.
- Everyone selects a card and places it face-down in front of them. When all players have chosen, the chosen cards are revealed, scored according to the dish combinations, and then the rest of the cards are passed to the left.
- The round ends when the deck is exhausted, and players tally a final score. The winner gets the glory—and perhaps the last piece of nigiri you’ll pretend to savor.

### Card types and scoring in the New Sealed edition
The New Sealed edition preserves the familiar cast of sushi cards: Nigiri cards (score points directly), Maki rolls (points for size and wave of laughter when someone rolls their eyes), Sashimi (more points for collecting three), and a few bonus elements that spice up the standard set. The tweaks in scoring are subtle but meaningful: clearer icons reduce miscounts, and the included reference card helps newcomers understand what combos matter most without needing constant rules checks.

To give you a taste, here are some representative scoring moments you’ll encounter:
- Nigiri: direct point values, typically simple to track and fiercely satisfying when you snag a perfect trio.
- Maki: players race to collect the most Maki rolls within a round; the top dog gets a bonus, while second place still feels like a victory snack.
- Sashimi: three-of-a-kind yields a hefty payoff, but there’s strategic nuance in when to go for it and when to pivot toward other combos.

### Strategy: how to draft without losing your lunch money
Sushi Go is as much about reading your table as it is about picking cards. The New Sealed edition nudges you toward a slightly more readable drafting experience, which benefits both beginners and diehard table-gazers alike. Here are some practical strategies to keep in mind:
- Early-round tempo: aim to pick cards that set up future rounds without compromising immediate scoring. You’ll often find that a safe pick in round one yields a larger payoff later if you’re willing to adjust your plan.
- Look ahead: while you can’t see the future, you can infer other players’ tastes. If someone else seems to be chasing a Sashimi combo, you might deprioritize that route to avoid letting them run away with a big chunk of points.
- Balancing act: don’t overcommit to one strategy. Sushi Go thrives on flexibility; the true skill is knowing when to pivot mid-round.
- The art of the fork: sometimes you’ll pass a card you want because it enables someone else; that’s not a mistake; it’s a chess move in a game about quick decisions and even quicker smiles.

New Sealed shines in a few key categories:
- Accessibility: The updated UI cues and clearer scoring sheets make it easier for new players to onboard without a mentor. The rules remain approachable, and the pacing is brisk enough to resolve debates without devolving into a civ-building saga.
- Family-friendliness: The game remains an excellent option for multi-generational groups. The brief rounds mean kids and adults can participate without long attention spans wearing thin.
- Social dynamics: The game thrives on social interaction—the look of mock disappointment when someone misses the trio of Sashimi, the triumphant gleam when you draft the perfect mix, and the gentle chaos of passing cards around the table. This edition preserves those vibes with a little extra polish.
