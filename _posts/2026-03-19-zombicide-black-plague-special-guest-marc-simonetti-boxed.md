Unboxing is half the fun with a CMON box, and the Zombicide Black Plague Special Guest edition does not disappoint. The lid opens with the theatrical drama of a necromancer reveal, and you’re greeted by the familiar chaos of zombie limbs, swords, and boards that feel chunky enough to survive a trebuchet attack. The Marc Simonetti influence is everywhere, from the character art to the blister packs full of plastic swirls that scream epic fantasy rather than “two dozen zombie peons.”

### The miniatures and assembly

If you’re new to Zombicide, assembly is minimal compared to some other wargames. These are pre-moblized pose minis that require basic trimming and maybe a dab of super glue if you’re particular about pose alignment. The Simonetti variants are very painting-friendly, with clear delineations between armor, flesh, and the blood-splatter textures. The challenge is not the assembly per se, but the sheer desire to repaint every single zombie with the same level of care you’d reserve for your own D&D character sheet. Pro-tip: separate the parts and plan your color scheme before you start popping limbs; you’ll thank me when you’re not chasing a spare shield halfway through a scenario.

One caveat I’d offer to new players: if you want to store these minis in a way that protects the delicate edges, consider already-case solutions or large bubble wrap. The Simonetti minis bring art-forward aesthetics that can be a little more brittle than your average cardboard-cut soldier, so treat them with the gentle respect you’d give to a prized sculpture.

## Rules and gameplay: How this edition changes the Black Plague experience

Zombicide Black Plague is a cooperative escape room with swords and necromancy in a medievalist setting. The Special Guest edition does not rewrite the rules of the baseline game; rather, it weaves a stronger thematic tapestry with the same core mechanics. Here are some of the nuanced shifts you’ll notice:

- Thematic integration: The art direction isn’t just window dressing. It nudges you to imagine a world where the Black Plague is a royal catastrophe and not just a local nuisance. The survivors look like they’ve stepped out of a high fantasy saga, and the zombies carry a bit more of that aristocratic decay you’d expect to see in a dragon-guarded fortress.
- Scenario variety: The edition introduces a handful of scenarios that leverage Simonetti’s artwork and the new minis. These scenarios often emphasize heavier combat, as the new undead units come with more dynamic attack patterns. If you enjoy a little more risk with your victory condiments, you’ll appreciate these options.
- Objective design: Some of the board overlays and objective tokens provide fresh ways to trap or outmaneuver the undead. The overlay piece is not merely cosmetic; it helps create a path for your survivors to escape or corner the zombie horde in ways you may not have considered in earlier editions.
- Difficulty tuning: If you’ve played Black Plague to death (pun intended), you’ll find that the Special Guest edition offers a slightly tweaked balance. It’s not a radical overhaul, but there are moments where the encounter density nudges up just enough to keep even veteran players alert without becoming a total evisceration nightmare.

For players who enjoy the “choose-your-own-chaos” vibe of Zombicide, the Simonetti edition is a gold mine. It invites you to lean into the theatrical, to let your inner siege engineer design barriers and kill zones that look good on camera and work well on the table.

## Artwork and thematic experience: Marc Simonetti’s influence on the table

Marc Simonetti’s involvement in this edition is not just a signature on the box. It’s a design philosophy. Simonetti’s art is known for dramatic lighting, imposing silhouettes, and a sense of motion that makes static miniatures feel like they’re mid-battle. In Zombicide Black Plague, that translates to pieces that appear to be in motion even when they’re at rest on the mini-holds. That sense of energy helps the game feel more dynamic even when nothing is actively happening.

The color palette—rich burgundies, onyx blacks, brass and steel—feeds into a storytelling mode. When you play, you’re not just killing zombies; you’re participating in a canvas where each figure is a brushstroke. The narrative weight is enough to propel a lazy Sunday afternoon into an enthusiastic campaign of “one more mission.” The power of Simonetti’s art in a board game context is that it invites your imagination to fill in the gaps—undead chancellor, knight of the decaying order, necromancer with a cape that looks suspiciously like a cape of shadows. It’s kitsch in the best way: bold, unabashed, and wonderfully over-the-top.

For painters and hobbyists, the edition offers a canvas worth painting. Even attendees who do not intend to prime or paint may appreciate the sculpted details that reward careful handling. If you’re a collector who wants to display a tabletop-evergreen, this edition does not disappoint. The box itself is a conversation piece and not just a game. It’s a gallery piece that can moonlight as a functional board game when required by quarantine-level gaming sessions.
