## How to play: a quick start guide for two players
### Setup and objective
Arrange the cards into their color-coded piles and place the spinner in the middle. Decide your goal for the evening: is it to reach a certain score, to complete a certain number of rounds, or simply to ride the wave of awkward fun until the dinner plates are cleared? The game assumes two players, but with a couple you can simply pair off into two teams, or each partner plays a solo game against the other in a friendly, low-stakes competition. The goal is not to win in a vacuum but to share an experience, so the real victory is the laughter and the connection you build along the way.

### The flow of a round
1) The active player draws a card from the top of the deck. 2) The card tells you what kind of prompt you will face. 3) You read or perform the prompt with a partner. 4) If you complete the prompt, you earn a small point or a token; if you fail, you take a sip or perform a light penalty as described on the card. 5) End of round, switch roles, and repeat. The spinner can be used to time the duration of a dare, adding a theatrical element for those who love drama.

The pacing is essential. The cards are designed to escalate gradually: expect small conversation prompts in the early rounds, moving toward higher stakes or more physical dares as you warm up to the vibe. If you find yourself stuck on a prompt, you have the option to swap or veto; the risk is that you lose an opportunity for laughter, so consider negotiation rather than ramming through a card you both dislike. It is all about consent, comfort, and shared mischief.

### A sample playthrough snapshot
Let us imagine a sample run to give you a feel for the tempo. You draw a conversation prompt about favorite shared memories, a dare to recreate a silly dance from your first date, and then a memory challenge that asks you to recall three details of a date you had way back when. If you breeze through these, you might pick a spicy truth card that pushes you to reveal something you would usually keep private. If you stumble, you can swap in a non-embarrassing alternative or trade a card with your partner. The key is to keep things light, and to be ready to laugh at yourself when you inevitably misremember a detail.

If you want a non-alcoholic variant, you can swap the drink tokens for dessert spoons or soft music prompts to maintain the energy without beverages. The game is designed to be adaptable; you can dial up or down the intensity to suit your mood, your schedule, and your current relationship status. The great thing about Dizzy Date is that it is not trying to be a life coach; it is trying to create a shared moment that you can later tell stories about at parties or on future anniversaries.
