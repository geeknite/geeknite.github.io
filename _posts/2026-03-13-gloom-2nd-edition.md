Then I tried Gloom: Second Edition, and my laughter collapsed into a foamy, melodramatic sigh as I realized the game was a gleeful caper about sadness, storytelling, and competitive pettiness. In Geeknite fashion, this is not merely a game about misery; it is a device to orchestrate a tiny, theatrical tragedy with your friends on a coffee-stained table. The second edition takes the original concept—a perfectly pitched mashup of storytelling, take-that mechanisms, and a pinch of cruelty—and refines it with sharper components, cleaner mechanics, and a more approachable learning curve.

If you’re new to Gloom, you play as a family or troupe of misfit characters. Your goal is simple in concept, vexingly tricky in execution: accumulate as much gloom as possible on your own characters while ensuring your opponents’ families become the most miserable. The winner is the one who has the lowest total gloom when all your characters have met their untimely, tragically humorous demises. Yes, it’s morbid; yes, it’s adorable in the way a goth kitten wearing a teacup sombrero can be adorable. This is the paradox Gloom thrives on: by embracing gloom, you craft memorable stories, pivot to clever plays, and somehow still feel like you’re in on the joke with your friends.

In this review, we’ll dive into the box contents, the setup, the core rules, family drama campaigns, and the ways in which Second Edition nudges players toward faster, richer, and more ridiculous sessions. Expect humor, some anatomy of a good card-play, and a few fun comparisons to previous formats and to other storytelling-ish games in the cozy corner of your game shelf.

## What’s in the box

Gloom: Second Edition keeps the same storytelling skeleton, but the internal bones have been reworked to feel sturdier and more legible. In the box you’ll find:

## Setup and rules basics

### The core concept
Gloom sticks to its “storytelling and misfortune” backbone. Each player controls a family of misfits, and your task is to ink their sorrow in ways that future gloom cards and tragedy cards will flow into a chain of sorrow. The lower your gloom tally, the better your story is for you—since you want your own characters to be dead at the end, the gloom on them should be high; meanwhile, you aim to keep your opponents’ families in the worst shapes possible. It’s a paradoxical moral fable where the moral is murderously funny, and every play will pivot on you being both the narrator and the trickster at the same time.

### Turn structure (in short)
- Draw phase: You draw a card from either the tragedy or gloom deck, depending on what you want to affect first.
- Play phase: You attach cards to your own characters or others. The cards have effects that can increase gloom on characters, trigger family-specific actions, or alter the balance of risk and reward around the table.
- Story phase: You narrate a small blurb to “justify” the actions on the table, a micro-plot that often becomes a running joke for the rest of the night. The social contract here is essential: you’re telling a story as you manipulate stats, not running a pure math exercise.
- End phase: You tally gloom on your own family. When a character dies, they pass to the graveyard, along with any gloom attached to them. Only this death can reduce your overall gloom score, and you want that to happen at the right moments so you can stay in the lead (or rather, stay behind in the intended fashion).

### The gloom track and scoring
The scoring mechanic is the clever centerpiece. The gloom track is as fierce as a villain’s diary. The more gloom you accumulate on opposing families, the more advantage you might have in future turns, because certain tragedy cards reward you for spreading sorrow. Gloom accumulation on your own family is what you want to avoid, but you’ll often be nudged into a moral gray zone, where sacrificing one family member paves the way for bigger “story arcs” later. The game ends when all characters are dead, and the player with the least gloom wins the round. It’s a strange arithmetic paradox: you win by losing first, then losing more slowly than everyone else.

### Solo play and hot-seat variants
Gloom’s minimal setup translates surprisingly well to solo play or hot-seat games with two players. The second edition does include rules tweaks and optionally a few AI helper cards for a “practice round” feeling, where you learn to manipulate gloom without the social tensions of a full crew. While the warmth of social interaction is the lifeblood of Gloom, the solo modes give you a chance to experiment with dramatic narratives in a quieter, more considered way.

## How to build a compelling table story

### The art and mood
One of the most delightful elements of Gloom Second Edition is how the cards tell a story with tiny, almost sketch-like phrases. The tragedies are a mix of the poetic and the pulpy; they invite you to imagine a Victorian ghoul begging for mercy while your friend’s character pines for a romance that never quite arrives. In Second Edition, the typography and layout are clearer, which matters when you’re trying to read a card mid-turn while your character tries to avoid an early exit from the stage. The humor lands because the melodrama isn’t overbearing; it sits inside the rules and then rises to the surface through player narration.

### The cards and the play rhythm
Because each card is designed to be readable at a glance, you don’t have to memorize a dozen quirky phrases or track obscure modifiers. You can quickly skim and say, “Okay, this card adds four gloom to the opponent’s character and gives you a one-turn advantage.” The rhythm emerges from this: you leverage a quick card play, you describe a little micro-scene, and the table responds with laughter or groans or a combination of both. The audience’s involvement is part of the charm: the more your fellow players participate, the more you create a shared memory rather than a dry series of card interactions.

### House rules that keep things spicy
House rules can underpin a lot of replay value. For example:
- Narrative buffs: allow players to insert a 1-2 sentence micro-scene each turn to justify the card effects. This converts the gameplay into a collaborative performance rather than a strict algorithm.
- Editing the gloom clock: add a rule where after every two turns, a global “twist card” is drawn that modifies gloom in a thematic way (e.g., a new tragedy card that multiplies gloom on a single family for a single turn).
- Leave-open endings: designate one player as “the playwright” who gets to nudge the conclusion with a unique end-game card when a specific combination appears.

These tweaks keep the game fresh and preserve the core design’s humor and narrative feel.

## Strategy and tactics

Gloom rewards clever targeting and careful negotiation, tempered with a willingness to let a little drama play out. Here are some tactics to keep in mind:

- Target a leading player late in the game: in many rounds, the player with the least gloom is the one who can claim victory by forcing others into more gloom as the endgame approaches. Consider timing your crisis cards to cap their lead.
- Diversify your misery: rather than pouring gloom onto a single family with all the powerful cards, spread it across multiple families. This makes it harder for opponents to predict which character will die when, creating more opportunities to unleash a well-timed tragedy later.
- Use narrative discipline: your best tool is story context. When you tell a vivid, humorous mini-scene that fits the card’s effects, you gain social leverage. People remember how you framed the moment, and this memory translates into favorable card draws or at least better banter on the table.
- Learn the long game: many cards have delayed effects. Plan your plays a few turns in advance, but stay flexible. The joy of Gloom arises when you pivot your strategy based on your friends’ storytelling choices as much as the cards themselves.

The best players in my sessions were not the ones who played the most cards in a single turn; they were the ones who wove the strongest micro-narratives, turning every tragedy into a cliffhanger and every gloom increase into a laugh-at-ourselves moment.

## Theme and tone

Gloom: Second Edition revels in melodrama and self-deprecating humor. The theme is dark, yes, but it’s performed with a wink that acknowledges the silliness of everyone involved. The game invites players to craft fictional, pie-in-the-sky tragedies for their characters, but the real draw is watching your friends improvise a little scene that can be shared and laughed about for weeks afterward. The humor isn’t mean-spirited; it’s a collective exploration of storytelling where the rules themselves encourage dramatic tension, quick improvisation, and a small sense of perverse pride in making your own family suffer the most tasteful of fates.

The “second edition” polish makes this balance easier to achieve. The new art makes violent drama feel more cute than creepy, the card text is concise enough to be read aloud in under a minute, and the audience can jump into the fun without a long trial by fire.

## Comparisons: Second Edition vs First Edition
If you’ve played the original Gloom, Second Edition feels like a thoughtfully updated, more accessible cousin. Here are a few key differentiators:
- Visual clarity: the second edition’s icons and typography are easier to parse at a glance, which speeds up play and reduces misreads during tense moments.
- Card balance: a few powers were tweaked to avoid runaway combos that could detonate the table’s mood too early. You’ll still have dramatic turnarounds, but they occur with a little more grace.
- Component quality: the deck stock and the gloom tokens feel sturdier, and the cover art is a touch more cohesive in the overall aesthetic. This matters when you’re shuffling, peeling cards, and slapping a gloom marker on a character who has just delivered a monologue about life’s disappointments.
- Rulebook polish: the new edition includes clearer examples and better flowcharts, making it simpler for new players to join in quickly without the table muttering about a “rule 4” that seems to apply only to some cards.

If you owned the first edition and found yourself juggling a couple of ambiguous card texts, you’ll likely appreciate the Second Edition’s more straightforward clarity. If you never played the first edition, the Second Edition is the better entry point with all the modern packaging and none of the complexity that some older rules can lean on when you’re trying to keep a party of six on the same page.

## Expansions and replayability
As with most beloved party games, a small but vibrant community grows around homebrew expansions and a handful of official add-ons. Gloom: Second Edition shines here because it offers a stable core with plenty of room for expansion without tipping the balance into chaos. You can add new character types, introduce special event cards, or create theme packs that tailor the gloom to your preferred vibes—Halloween spookiness, Victorian melodrama, or sci-fi weirdness if you’re feeling particularly curious about misfortune in space.

For players who crave more permanence to their campaigns, consider a short-form “arc” approach: build a mini-story across several sessions with a fixed pool of characters. Each session ends with a dramatic reveal that changes your relationships and affects the gloom calculations in the next game. The result is a tabletop soap opera that’s short, sharp, and deliciously silly.

## Accessibility, teaching, and table management
The Second Edition’s improved layout makes it easier to pick up in a single session, which is ideal if you’re introducing new players who aren’t seasoned hobbyists. The quick-start feel of the game suits casual game nights and post-work meetups. When teaching, emphasize three pillars:
- Story first: remind everyone that their primary role is to narrate a short scene that justifies the card effects.
- Play cards, not paragraphs: encourage concise prompts. You want momentum, not an epic soliloquy every turn.
- Remember the theme: it’s okay to lean into the melodrama; the humor grows from the exaggerated misfortune rather than the grim reality.

Moderation is key. There’s a thin line between entertaining gloom and moral gloom-spiral—keep the mood light, and the game remains a joyous exercise in theatrical embarrassment rather than a bummer of a session. The Second Edition sits comfortably along this line, providing a playful but sharp instrument for storytelling with friends.
