### Setup and getting started with ten players

If you have ten players, you are hosting a mini event. The trick is to reduce downtime between turns and keep everyone engaged without the table turning into a mosh pit of elbows and nachos. Here are practical tips:

- Designate a “turn chair” or a calm moderator who can remind players to take their turns, and who can splinter off to manage the pace if needed. In huge groups, the person who calls the order of play can become the de facto host for the session and keep the mood light.
- Clear a little extra space. Ten players means at least a six to eight foot circle of tabletop real estate, with snacks and drinks distributed so no one blocks the cards with a soda can. The whole point is to keep things moving, not to create a cardio workout while you reach for a card.
- Consider a quick practice round. A single round where players just play through a couple of cards helps everyone understand the flow without the pressure of a real game. It’s also a hysterical way to see who reads the card text and who just vibes with the cat art.

The pace naturally shifts with ten players. Some rounds fly by in under two minutes, while others stall as someone contemplates the best Defuse timing while a fifth person asks if the Defuse can be used on behalf of someone else. The real skill here is to manage expectations and keep the energy high. Humor, not precision, is your best weapon for a ten person game night.

### How the gameplay scales up

The classic Exploding Kittens mechanic remains: draw cards, avoid drawing the Exploding Kitten without a Defuse, and use a variety of action cards to alter outcomes. The Party Pack adds more variability, aiming to keep every round fresh. With more players, you’ll notice:

- More bluffing and misdirection. People try to read your face, your body language, and your eye movements as you pretend to search for a See the Future card while secretly planning a move that will drastically alter the next draw pile.
- Longer, more interactive turns. A single round can stretch as players draw, counter, and react, which means your cat appreciation goes from a two second chuckle to a multi-minute theatrical scene complete with dramatic gasps, whispered strategies, and the occasional cat pun that makes the table groan. This is where the humor shines and the tension can become surprisingly real.
- Substantially more chaos management. With ten players, you’ll quickly learn which cards cause the kind of chaos that’s skin-deep funny versus the kind that triggers real jealousy about who got the best hand. Expect occasional table talk that would make your mom proud and your friend’s aura of calm shatter in the best way possible.

If you’re worried about the game dragging on, you can use a time-box to cap each round. A two minute timer can work wonders to keep the mood electric and the pace snappy. The key is to keep momentum and ensure the interactions stay inclusive rather than turning into a hard to manage duel of wits between two players while everyone else watches.

### Variants and house rules for extra flavor

Large groups thrive on variety. Here are a few variants you can try to keep the Party Pack fresh over several game nights:

- The Lightning Round: set a two minute timer and see how many eliminations you can pile up in that window. The objective here is not to win a round but to survive the round without exploding. It is chaotic, silly, and surprisingly tense.
- The Peek and Preacher: players can peek at the top three cards of the draw pile, but only if they declare a joke that lands with the table. Laughter acts as a kind of currency; the better your joke, the more you can peek. This variant turns into improv plus card play and is perfect for groups who like to talk through everything aloud.
- See the Future Marathon: a longer session where players alternate using See the Future multiple times in a row, stacking anticipation, and the suspense of what comes next. This is a great way to teach new players how to read the board without turning the session into a math lecture. It also gives veteran players a chance to outplay others with long term planning that might crumble under one bad draw.
- Silent Round: no talking allowed for a single round. It sounds easy until you realize your mouth is shouting silent signals and the table is reading your body language like a spoof of a spy movie. The humor emerges from the struggle to communicate without words, which tends to produce the most memorable moments of the night.

If you want to point your future self to a couple of pre-built variants, try the See the Future Marathon on nights when people are hyped up on snacks. It’s not a marathon in the literal sense, but the mood and energy can feel marathon-level without exhausting the table’s attention span.

### Quick contrast with single deck play

If you are used to a standard Exploding Kittens game with a handful of players, the Party Pack is a different beast. You’ll notice:

- More social interaction per round. Ten players means you’re not waiting your turn alone; you’re hearing comments from multiple sources as cards flip and accusations fly.
- More unpredictable outcomes. The presence of extra players means that the draw pile can get disrupted in ways that you would not see in a smaller group. Expect more dramatic moments when a surprise Cherry Bomb, Cat, or See the Future is revealed by a player who normally keeps quiet.
- A tendency toward longer sessions. This is not a bad thing when you are hosting a party; it’s a feature, not a bug, as long as you pace things and keep energy high with short breaks.

If you crave a lean, quick party game, the base game is probably enough. But if you want to host a social event where the group vibe carries the rounds, the Party Pack shines.
