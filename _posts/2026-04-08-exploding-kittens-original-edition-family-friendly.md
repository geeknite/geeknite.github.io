## How to play: extended quick-start guide with practical setup tips
The base game supports 2-5 players. The core loop is simple, but the expanded rules give you more to chew on while keeping the pace brisk: draw a card, play a card, or dodge your draw—repeat until one player remains or the bomb is defused in a timely fashion.

Setup (expanded tips):
- Prepare the Exploding Kitten deck by removing as many Exploding Kitten cards as there are players minus one. This small culling creates the adrenaline spike that keeps late-game decisions tense without becoming a runaway train.
- Shuffle in the Defuse cards and lay them into the draw pile, with a few extra Defuse cards set aside for late-game drama. You want enough to keep players from instantly running out, but not so many that the endgame becomes trivial.
- Deal starting Defuse cards to each player (per the official rules), and set up any cat-pair requirements for your group size.

Turn flow (expanded): On your turn, you can play one or more action cards to influence the flow (for example, Attack forces the next player to take two turns, See the Future lets you peek at the top of the deck, Shuffle breaks a possible fatal order, and Skip dodges your draw for that turn). You can also deploy Cat Cards by pairing two of the same variety to steal a card from another player, which is where a lot of social maneuvering happens.

Exploding Kitten vs. Defuse (expanded): If you draw an Exploding Kitten, you’re out unless you possess a Defuse card. Defuse cards provide a critical safety valve, but they’re not infinite. The tension ramps up as players exchange defuses, bluff, and bluff-defuse, and the deck’s remaining cards shift the odds until someone squeals with delight or despair.

Endgame (expanded): The last player standing wins. If you thought you were close, you’ll discover Exploding Kittens has a knack for dragging a short game into a humorous stalemate where the table eye-bulges at the possibility of another round, and then—boom—a new sequence begins.

The pacing is influenced by how many Defuse cards are in play and how aggressively players deploy See the Future and Attack cards. The See the Future mechanic isn’t just a cute gimmick; it provides information that reshapes risk calculations just when you need it most.

Example playthrough snippets (to get your group comfy):
- Round A: A cautious start with minimal risky plays; See the Future reveals that the top two cards are safe, and a well-timed Skip avoids a draw that could trigger catastrophe. A Cat Card swap shifts a key card into the hands of a player with fewer Defuse options, which spices up negotiation.
- Round B: The table erupts in a flurry of Cat Card trades and a well-timed Attack that compounds two draw turns on a single target. It’s chaotic but strategic—people are laughing, but they’re also calculating every risk.
- Round C: A player uses a defuse to neutralize a bomb, only to realize a passed Cat Card can steal a defensive card right back. The social dimension kicks in, and the room becomes a lightly choreographed dance of bluff and restraint.

## Card types in depth: expanding the decision-making playbook
### Exploding Kitten and Defuse
The core tension engine. Exploding Kitten cards are the doom, and Defuse cards are your lifeline. The interplay between the two is what keeps everyone honest and anxious in equal measure. You’ll be calculating whether you should take a risk now or bank a Defuse for a potential late-game debacle. The updated guidance helps players read the table’s rhythm: who has already burned a Defuse? Who’s hoarding Cat Cards? Which players tend to overcommit to Attack plays early on? These aren’t merely child-friendly questions; they’re real-time strategic signals.

### Attack, Skip, See the Future, Shuffle, and Favor
- Attack: Forcibly pushes the next player to take multiple turns, creating a cascading risk chain and a dramatic moment when the next player misreads a top-deck order.
- Skip: The silent hero that buys you time. In tight late-game moments, Skip can be worth its weight in gold and giggles.
- See the Future: Peek at the top three cards of the deck to guide risk-taking. This is where math meets whimsy, and players learn to balance probability with social distraction—because sometimes the best move is to stare solemnly at the cards while you plan a cat-swap.
- Shuffle: Reset the deck’s randomness when the odds tilt unfavorably in a particularly tense moment.
- Favor: Push a card to your chosen opponent, an action that can break fragile alliances or solidify a quiet power-play. The social angle here is rich for gameschoolers and family negotiators alike.

### Cat Cards
These cute felines aren’t just filler. You collect them in matching pairs to steal a card from another player, producing a satisfying snap of “Ha ha, Tacocat, your game just got spicier.” The names—Tacocat, Hairy Potato Cat, Beardy Cat, and friends—are puns with personality. The Cat Card mechanic adds a social layer: negotiation, trading, and dramatic reveals become central to the fun, not just a sidebar.

## Strategy and tips for family-friendly minds
- Balance risk vs. reward: Early on, a small risk can pay off if you’re ahead and can survive one more draw. In late-game, minimize exposure to Exploding Kitten draws and deploy Defuse cards thoughtfully.
- Manage your hand: Don’t hoard Defuse cards to the point of paranoia. You want a few Defuses in reserve but not so many that you neutralize all tension. It’s a delicate balance between security and opportunity.
- Cat Card diplomacy: Use Cat Cards to form tiny, temporary alliances or to bait opponents into a trap. The social dynamic is a big part of the charm. Remember: the Cat Cards’ power scales with how many players you have, so a game with six or seven players becomes a bustling negotiation chamber.
- Timing is everything: Save See the Future for moments when the deck’s composition makes a draw feel like stepping into a minefield. A well-timed peek can turn a near-certain loss into a clever pivot and a smile.
- Adapt to age and energy: With kids, keep the pace brisk. Let younger players have a few safety turns while adults handle heavier decision moments. Don’t underestimate a kid’s bluff; a shrewd youngster can bluff their way into a defensive play that stuns the table.
- House rules to consider: If your group craves longer games, you can experiment with allowing multiple Defuses per player per round or adjusting the number of Exploding Kitten cards for a more dramatic endgame. If you want tighter cycles, you can implement a soft timer on turns (e.g., a minute per player) to prevent marathon evenings.
