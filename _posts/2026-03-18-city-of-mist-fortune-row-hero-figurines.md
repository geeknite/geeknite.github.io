## A noir-drenched look at Fortune Row's finest plastic citizens

In the neon-drenched alleys of City of Mist, Fortune Row is the latest batch of hero figurines designed to bring swagger to your tabletop. The line promises to capture the vibe of mythic noir—where detectives double as gods and crooks double as legends. But do these miniatures actually carry the mood, or are they just tiny capes with a personality deficit? We rolled up our trench coats and took a long, giggly look at the five-figure lineup.

![Fortune Row - The Dealer](/assets/images/fortune-row-dealer.jpg)

Fortune Row comes as a five-figure ensemble, each sculpt aiming to embody a different flavor of fate-wielding street-level myth: the gambler of chance, the clairvoyant who sees through smoke, the trickster who bakes chaos into every plan, the masked socialite who operates from the shadows, and the mechanic who literally makes luck run on rails. The scale is a familiar 28–32mm range—plenty big enough to show off a well-crafted jacket lapel, but small enough to slot into a noir-drenched board with your city-fantasy token stack. The assembly required is minimal; these are largely single-piece or two-piece builds that avoid the dreaded “glue nightmare” you might encounter with more elaborate diorama-ready kits.

## The lineup: five heroes of Fortune Row

### The Dealer

![Fortune Row - The Dealer](/assets/images/fortune-row-dealer.jpg)

The Dealer wears a midnight pinstripe suit, a glove with hidden cards, and a smile that says, I know your next move before you do. The sculpt captures that slippery elegance of a guy who makes a living off of bragging rights and bad decisions. The pose is dynamic without being over-the-top, which makes him a great centerpiece for a scene where someone is trying to bluff their way to a miracle. The card motif is implemented in subtle, tasteful ways rather than shouting casino neon, which helps with the “mythic noir” vibe rather than turning the figure into a cartoon.

### The Oracle

![Fortune Row - The Oracle](/assets/images/fortune-row-oracle.jpg)

The Oracle is all about eyes that seem to see through the smoke and into the future’s backstage. She carries a crystal orb that refracts light into tiny, questionable prophecies. The sculpt plays with drapery and a cascade of jewelry that suggests a person who channels fate as if it were a power source. The feathered cape and languid pose give her an almost cinematic quality—like she stepped out of a black-and-white film and into your RPG table. Painting her will be a delightful challenge if you enjoy gradient transitions and metallic accents.

### The Jinx

![Fortune Row - The Jinx](/assets/images/fortune-row-jinx.jpg)

The Jinx is the chaos engine of the group. A figure whose grin hints at schemes that could topple kingdoms or at least topple a couple of streetlamps. The sculpt captures mischief in the shoulders and a head tilt that telegraphs confidence with a hint of danger. Expect dramatic drapery and a couple of accessories—perhaps a joker card tucked into a belt or a tiny catapult of luck hidden under the cloak. The Jinx’s silhouette reads as a silhouette-first design: easy to paint, spectacular on the tabletop.

### The Velvet Mask

![Fortune Row - The Velvet Mask](/assets/images/fortune-row-velvet-mask.jpg)

The Velvet Mask is the social chameleon: a frontline figure who can pass through layers of society with a smile and a practiced shimmer. The sculpt is all about silhouette work—the mask itself is an anchor for your color choice, and the suit is tailored to suggest both danger and charm. Expect crisp lines and a pose that says, I’m two steps ahead of your plan, and I’m filming it for my memoirs. For painters, this figure offers a satisfying challenge: metal flakes for jewelry, satin sheen on the mask, and a blazer that demands dry brushing and subtle wash work.

### The Mechanic

![Fortune Row - The Mechanic](/assets/images/fortune-row-mechanic.jpg)

The Mechanic brings a bit of industrial grit to the collection. Think grease, bolts, and a wrench that looks like it could double as a reaction weapon in a pinch. The sculpt emphasizes tool-based accessories, which makes it a great partner for terrain-heavy scenes where you want a character that truly feels at home in a workshop or street-side repair shack. The mechanical details are crisp, with lines that should read cleanly even at tabletop distance. If you like your heroes to feel grounded in practical craft, The Mechanic is the sleeper hit of Fortune Row.

## Build quality and materials

Overall, Fortune Row leans toward plastic and resin hybrids designed for ease of assembly and durability. The resin parts (where used) show excellent surface detail, with a few mold lines that are easily resolved with a careful trim-and-sand approach. The plastic components hold detail well enough for mass-market play, with minimal flash that won’t ruin your paint job if you’re not a perfectionist on day one.

A word on bases: Fortune Row includes pleasant, round bases with understated rims that don’t fight with your scenic choices. They weren’t designed with dynamic dioramas in mind, but they lay flat and accept standard basing materials without a hitch. If you like to magnetize or swap heads for different pose options, you’ll appreciate that most parts fit cleanly and click into place without forcing a glue habit you’ll regret during a long session.

## Painting and assembly tips

- Start with a baseline prime (black or grey) to keep shadows deep and colors punchy. The noir vibe loves a strong chiaroscuro, so don’t shy away from a mid-tone dry brush on jackets and leather.
- Use a thin glaze to bring out metallic accents on The Dealer’s gloves and The Mechanic’s tools. A tiny accent of gold or copper can elevate the character without shouting “holiday ornament.”
- For The Oracle, experiment with a blue-violet gradient on the orb. A subtle glow effect can sell the “seeing through the future” vibe without requiring advanced airbrushing.
- The Velvet Mask benefits from a two-stage skin tone approach: base flesh color, a light red glaze on cheeks, and then a final protective wash to bring out facial features.
- Patience is a virtue here. Allow each layer to dry before the next—these sculpts are crisp, and you don’t want a brush stroke to become the main event on a hero’s lapel.

If you’re brand-new to miniature painting, you might want to start with one figure as a trial run. If you’re a veteran, Fortune Row gives you five distinct silhouettes to exercise your color theory and weathering techniques in one sitting. ## How Fortune Row fits into City of Mist gameplay (and why you might want it anyway)

City of Mist is famously narrative and tag-driven rather than grid-focused. Fortunate Row figurines don’t change the core mechanics, but they do enhance your game table in several pleasing ways:

- Visual storytelling: These figures give players a tangible, character-based focal point during scenes. When a GM shouts, “The Oracle steps out of the fog,” your table can point to the literal figure in the middle of the narrative.
- Scene framing: The large silhouettes let you stage quick “cutaway” moments—close-ups of a crucial clue or a sudden reveal—without text on a card doing all the heavy lifting.
- Thematic cohesion: The five personas align with classic City of Mist archetypes—mystic, rogue, enforcer, social influencer, and engineer—helping players visualize who’s who in the crime-ridden city.
