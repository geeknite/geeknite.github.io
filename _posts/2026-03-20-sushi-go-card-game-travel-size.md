## How to Play in 15 Minutes or Less
If you’ve played Sushi Go! before, you know the drill. If you haven’t, prepare for a delightfully breezy introduction to drafting and set collection. The game typically supports 2-5 players and revolves around a rapid-fire drafting loop across four rounds.

- Each round, players secretly choose one card from their hand and pass the rest to the neighbor. The passing direction alternates every round, a small mechanic that keeps you on your toes as you anticipate what your neighbor might snag next.
- When all cards have been picked for a round, hands are collected and scored according to specific combinations: set of nigiri, maki rols, tempura, sashimi, and dumplings all interact with unique scoring tokens and thresholds.
- After four rounds, total scores are tallied. The player with the highest score wins. In travel mode, you’ll likely knock this out in 15-20 minutes, depending on how many “one more round” enticements your group manages to conjure.

The core loop is blisteringly simple: draft, collect, score, repeat. But there’s nuance in the choices. Do you go for a high-value set that could become a trap when your neighbor sniffs out your plan and blocks you? Do you pivot early to chase a different pattern that’s more likely to pay off in the surrounding board state? That tension—between short-term gains and long-term targets—is where Sushi Go! Travel Size earns its keep.

### A Quick Playthrough Sketch
- Round 1: You’re likely chasing a nibble of Maki or a single Dumpling score. The pressure is to avoid catching a lousy mix that will drag you down in future rounds.
- Round 2: The board becomes a little more hostile as you and your neighbors start telegraphing your intentions. You’ll see a few early birds snag specific cards, which informs your pivot.
- Round 3: This is the make-or-break moment. The lead can flip on a single card, enabling a late surge or a defensive grab that preserves your lead.
- Round 4: The finishing sprint. You’ll finalize your sets, hope you’ve left your opponents with suboptimal leftovers, and tally the scores.

One thing travel-size does well: the pacing. Because you’re not managing dozens of unique components, you can sit in a quiet corner of a coffee shop and still feel in control. The game shines on buses, planes, and hotel lobbies where space is at a premium and your neighbors will appreciate the polite, non-commitment-required gameplay.

## Strategy 101: Quick Tips for Traveling Players
Let’s get to the heart of it: even in a tiny package, there’s room for strategic decisions. Here are quick takeaways for players who want to maximize their results without pulling their hair out on a red-eye flight.

- Know the patterns: Maki rolls, Tempura, Sashimi, and Dumplings represent different scoring mechanics. Familiarize yourself with the thresholds (two or more Maki, pairs of Tempura, etc.) so you can spot opportunities and avoid obvious misses.
- Balance speed and risk: In travel play, you’ll want to pick fast, valuable cards early but don’t neglect the possibility of a late surge. Sometimes the best move is to take a card that blocks an opponent more than it increases your own points.
- Read the room: If your neighbor is going all-in on a single combo, you can pivot to spoil their plans or switch to a complementary strategy that pays off regardless of their choices.
- Endgame awareness: In the final rounds, keep a mental tally of what you’ve collected and what remains. It’s easy to overcommit to a high-value card that turns out to be a dead end if the right matching card never shows up.

For travel enthusiasts, these tips translate into a practical mindset: keep rounds snappy, don’t overthink every pick, and let social play drive the session. You’ll find that the game rewards you for reading the room and making crisp, decisive calls—the sort of decisions you can make even in a cramped seat with a coffee spill on your sleeve.
