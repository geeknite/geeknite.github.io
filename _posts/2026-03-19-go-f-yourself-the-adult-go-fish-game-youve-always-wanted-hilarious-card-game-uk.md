## What is Go F Yourself, exactly?

Go F Yourself is a hands-on, crowd-pleasing take on the classic Go Fish mechanic that trades innocence for innuendo. The deck contains a mix of standard Go Fish prompts and new, adult-oriented prompts that encourage players to perform dares, answer wittier-than-average questions, or act out mini-scenarios. It’s designed to be played with 4–8 players in a living room, den, or any space big enough to create a makeshift stage for silly acts.

### The core deck and variant piles

The cards break down into several families:

- Regular Go Fish prompts: familiar phrases like Ask for a card, Do you have any eights? etc.
- Adult prompts: tricky questions, outrageous dares, and light-hearted provocations that keep the room buzzing.
- Challenge cards: special rounds where players can call for a mini-game, like a quick truth or dare that must be completed on the spot.
- Wild cards: shuffle these in and you’ll +1 the chaos with a twist that can dramatically shift the game’s momentum.

Two quick notes on the mechanics:

1) You still collect pairs or sets, but the prompts can be more flexible (and meddlesome), so expect a lot of banter and improv.
2) There’s a consent layer built into the rules. Players can opt out of certain dares or prompts if they feel uncomfortable, and there are safer alternatives clearly marked. The game does not push players into anything they can’t handle, which is a relief—nobody wants to be that person who ends up hosting an awkward debate with the wall color while the rest of the group giggles awkwardly.

## How to play: Rules and flow of the game

Go F Yourself remains a social deduction of sorts: you want to collect enough cards to form sets, while the prompts and dares push conversations from casual chit-chat to surprisingly deep (or delightfully silly) territory.

### Setup

- Shuffle both decks and deal a hand to each player (the recommended starting size depends on the number of players; 5–7 cards works nicely for a 6-player round).
- Place the remaining deck in a draw pile and flip the top card to start the discard pile, framing your first prompt.
- Decide on the level of boldness (PG-13 or R-rated) and set that as a group standard before you begin. This is your party mood board, not a strict rulebook.

### Turn structure

On your turn:

1) Ask another player for a rank you need to complete a set. For example: Do you have any fives? If they have a matching card, they give you the card and you take another turn. If not, your turn ends and you draw from the deck.
2) If you draw a card that completes a set, you show it and lay down the set; the player who completes a set tends to bask in minor glory and maybe a short, silly victory dance.
3) When a player runs out of cards, they are out of the round. The round ends when the draw pile is exhausted or when a pre-agreed score target is reached.
4) Triggered prompts: if you land on a Wild card or a dares card, the group negotiates the prompt. You might be asked to perform a lighthearted challenge or to answer a question with a funny stake attached.

### Variants and house rules

- Truth-and-dare mode: swap some prompts for truth questions that reveal hilarious anecdotes or hidden preferences.
- Speed round: set a timer (60 seconds) and see how many prompts you can complete before it ticks down.
- Seeded edition: include a few “in-joke” prompts tailored to your group to get guaranteed laughs.

It’s worth reiterating: consent and comfort are essential. The makers include a few safety reminders, and you should feel empowered to skip anything that doesn’t feel right for your group. This isn’t about pushing boundaries cruelly; it’s about nudging the boundary line in a way that’s inclusive and funny.

## The humour: what works, what lands, and what flops

Humour is the lifeblood of this game, and in the UK, where wit is a social currency, this edition shines when the group embraces playful banter rather than hostility. Here are a few observations:

- The prompts land best when they hinge on shared experiences or mild embarrassments. Personal stories tend to generate the loudest laughs, especially if the crowd has enough trust to dish out cheeky anecdotes without crossing personal lines.
- The dares are a clever addition, not a demolition crew. A well-timed, light-hearted dare can turn a bland hand into a legendary moment, but a dare that asks someone to reveal a deeply private habit may derail the energy. The product’s design makes it easy to skip or reframe such prompts without breaking the game rhythm.
- The card art and phrasing lean into British sarcasm, which plays well in pub-friendly spaces but can land poorly in more formal gatherings. Assess your audience before unleashing the full R-rated mode—this is a party game, but it’s still your party, so set expectations early.
