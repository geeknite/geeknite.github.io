## Overview

### What is Sushi Go!
Sushi Go! is a micro-card-drafting engine that fits in with your busiest schedule since calendar apps were invented. The goal is to collect sets of adorable sushi cards that score points in various ways. You’ll find Nigiri cards (Egg, Salmon, Shrimp), Maki rolls, Tempura, Sashimi, Wasabi, and a few other culinary oddities that make you smile as you play. The real magic is how dramatically simple the drafting mechanic scales with players and rounds.

This is a perfect gateway game for people who like the idea of “drafting” but don’t want to invest a weekend deciphering a rulebook the size of a phone book. The rules are crisp, the icons readable, and the scoring—though it can feel arcane on the first pass—unfolds in a friendly, almost spa-like rhythm once you’ve played a couple of rounds.

## How to Play (Rule Summary)

### Setup
- Shuffle the deck and deal a hand to each player. The exact number of cards per hand shifts slightly with player count, but the drafting rhythm remains the same: you pick one card and pass the rest to the neighbor (left or right depending on the round). When all cards are drafted, you reveal and score.
- Place any Wasabi cards in front of you as a separate “prep zone”; they affect the next Nigiri you play by tripling its value (this is the kind of clever timing that makes you feel like a culinary ninja).

### Turn structure
- On your turn, you select one card from your hand and place it in front of you, then pass the remaining cards to the neighbor. This continues until everyone has drafted all cards for the round.
- After all cards are drafted and revealed, you tally points for that round according to the card types. You then reshuffle or discard as per your group’s preference and move into the next round.

### Scoring basics
- Nigiri: Each Nigiri card has an intrinsic point value (Egg = 1, Salmon = 2, Shrimp = 3). If you have a Wasabi card in front of you, the next Nigiri you play will be worth triple points. This is where the “planning around Wasabi” strategy begins to glow.
- Maki: If you have the most Maki rolls, you earn a large chunk of points; if you come in second, you get a smaller chunk. It’s a fun anti-lottery mechanic: you want most, but not at the expense of other combos you could chase elsewhere.
- Tempura: A clean two-card set earns you points; a lonely Tempura is basically a dietary experiment with no points.
- Sashimi: A set of three Sashimi cards is worth a nice chunk of points; two or fewer yields zero. This one rewards you for committing to a specific trio rather than trying to juggle many separate plates.
- Pudding: At the end of the entire game (which is typically three rounds for standard player counts), pudding cards tally up. The player with the most pudding can get a bonus (and sometimes second place gets something smaller). This introduces a gentle longer-term strategy and keeps late-night players engaged even when a couple of rounds feel obvious.

### End of round and end of game
- After each round’s scoring, you clear the table (or glide into a clean reset) and start again with a fresh hand. The game wraps after three rounds for standard play (although some house rules or expansions stretch this). The end result is a compact, satisfying arc: you sprint, you dash, you chow down on the final tally, and you realize you probably should have planned for Wasabi a little more strongly.

## Strategy and Tips

### Quickfire drafting heuristics
- Go with a plan, but don’t over-commit. The best Sushi Go! players aren’t the ones who chase one perfect combo; they’re the ones who adapt to what’s drifting around the table. A rogue Sashimi might appear, or a strong Maki lead might be your best route to points—read the table.
- Wasabi is on a timer. Don’t pass up a strong Nigiri when you’ve got Wasabi set up, but don’t force it either. The timing of where you hold Wasabi and when you play Nigiri to maximize triple points can swing a round’s score by a surprising margin.
- End-game pudding awareness matters. If you’re behind in pudding, you may need to re-gauge your strategy to chase more pudding cards later rounds. Conversely, if you’re ahead, you can lean into the faster scoring tracks (Nigiri, Maki) because the pudding lead gives you some wiggle room.

### Player count considerations
- 2 players vs. 5 players changes tempo but not the core feel. In 2-player games, the drafter tends to cycle a little faster with fewer hands to pass, which can heighten the tension of early decisions. In larger groups, the belt flavor comes alive; the table becomes a lively carnival of glances and gentle glares as people pass cards with the seriousness of a sushi chef weighing the tuna.

### Common pitfalls
- Overvaluing a single card type. It’s tempting to chase one scoring path (like lots of Maki), but in a tight game, diversified scoring tends to yield more stable results.
- Neglecting Wasabi. It’s easy to forget Wasabi exists until someone triples a Nigiri you really wanted. Respect Wasabi; it’s the silent decider in some rounds.
- Forgetting to reference the end-of-game cards. Pudding can swing a finish line you didn’t expect, especially when you’ve built a lead elsewhere.

## Expansions and Variants

### Sushi Go Party!
If Sushi Go! is the quick snack, Sushi Go Party! is the full buffet. This expansion adds many more menu cards, more players, and a rotating menu element that can dramatically shift strategy per game. The core experience remains—the drafting, the passing, the quick scoring—but the variability keeps it fresh for groups that want something more than the base’s reliable rhythm.

### 2-Player variants
There are official and fan-made variants that tailor the experience for two players. They usually tweak the deck size or passing direction to keep the drafting feel lively when you’re sharing a table with a single opponent rather than a full crowd.

### Thematic tweaks and house rules
Some groups like to treat the game as a tournament mini-series: a best-of-three, a highest total after a midnight lobby, or even a “chef’s choice” round where players pick a random penalty for the other players (purely for laughs, of course). These variations can heighten tension, but they also risk dulling the elegant simplicity that makes Sushi Go! so accessible.

## Art, Theme, and Accessibility

Sushi Go! leans into a cute, approachable theme with accessible mechanics. It’s not trying to be the deepest economic simulator; it’s a bright, breezy, family-friendly card game. The art, the quick rounds, and the simple scoring means you can pick it up mid-game with someone who’s never touched a board game, and within five minutes be drafting and laughing at the goofy fish-shaped cards. The design intentionally avoids heavy mental load, making it a prime candidate for intro nights and casual game days.

If you’re hunting for a game that plays well with kids but doesn’t insult the adults’ intelligence, Sushi Go! hits the sweet spot.
