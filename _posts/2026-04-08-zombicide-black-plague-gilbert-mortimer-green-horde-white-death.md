## Overview: What are Gilbert & Mortimer, Green Horde, and White Death?

Zombicide Black Plague launched as a medieval-flavored take on the core Zombicide concept: heroes, zombies, loot, and a lot of dice-powered chaos. The Gilbert & Mortimer duo is part of the rarer character packs that CMON released to spice up campaigns with memorable survivors or antagonists. In the lore of these expansions (as much as a ruleset about undead peasants can be lore), Gilbert and Mortimer bring a specific flavor to the table: two stubborn, likely conspiratorial, human-ish characters whose synergies feel like a sitcom episode in a zombie siege.

Green Horde, on the other hand, is the expansion that drags orcs and their green-skinned friends into the party. It introduces new factions, a slightly different flavor of objectives, and mechanical twists that can make the standard zombie scrum feel more like a skirmish with a brutal, orcish grin. If you’ve played through Black Plague enough times, Green Horde is a welcome reset button that makes you rethink how you approach gates, loot, and the ever-present threat of getting swarmed while your greatsword doesn’t quite reach the last row of models.

White Death is the titular boss of a different flavor of Zombicide expansion, typified by a hulking, near-mythical zombie encounter with a chilling stage presence. In the lore of the game, White Death isn’t just a stat block; it’s a boss fight that requires planning, crowd control, and just a pinch of lucky dice. In practice, facing White Death is where many players either shine or discover a long-buried fear of initiative order. The design intent is to upend standard zombie waves with a memorable encounter that tests your best-laid plans and your ability to adapt on the fly.

## Components and Quality: What You’ll Actually Get

The quality bar in Zombicide miniatures is generally high, and the same holds true for these packs. Gilbert & Mortimer miniatures are characterful, with exaggerated features that translate into clear gameplay silhouettes. The Green Horde introduces more beast-like sculpts with a distinct fantasy-tribal aesthetic. White Death’s model is typically imposing, designed to convey a sense of archetypal dread on the table. The plastic quality tends to be sturdy, though as with any rare pack, you’ll want to inspect for any flash (the little bits of resin or plastic left from the mold) and clean it up before assembly.

As for the gaming tokens, cards, and boards: you’ll see the usual Zombicide chaos—lots of dice, a stack of mission cards, and a handful of character sheets. The card art is well-produced, and the color palette is consistent with the Black Plague vibe: grim-yet-glorious, with enough red to remind you that yes, you are playing a zombie apocalypse themed on a medieval world’s version of “bring your own snacks.” The quality of the art is a pleasant bridge between the grimdark of cannibal zombies and the more whimsical fantasy elements the expansion aims to spice up.

If you’re thinking about painting, you’ll want some patience. The intricate faces on Gilbert and Mortimer cry out for careful brushwork, while Green Horde minions can make for fun, mixed-wash exercises that give your table a pop of color without causing a major headache. White Death, with its dramatic armor and frost-bitten aesthetic, is a perfect candidate for a slightly more ambitious painting session if you’re into winter-themed palettes and icy metallics.

## Gameplay and Balance: How these Add-Ons Fit In

Zombicide is a cooperative survival game. You’re fighting against waves of zombies with your chosen survivors, using weapons, armor, and special abilities to survive. Add-ons like Gilbert & Mortimer, Green Horde, and White Death aren’t simply decorative; they alter the table dynamics in meaningful ways.

- Gilbert & Mortimer tend to introduce synergy-based play. Their abilities encourage teamwork and risk-managed engagement. They reward players who can coordinate moves, set up traps, or create split-push opportunities to thin the zombie crowds before they can overwhelm a line.
- Green Horde shifts the balance by adding non-human antagonists with their own mechanics. Expect new spawn patterns, beasty threats, and perhaps some “armies” that require you to adapt to a changing battlefield where the objective tokens feel less like objective points and more like survival opportunities.
- White Death is a boss who wants to test your final-hour planning. It’s the kind of encounter that makes you rethink your deployment order, which weapons you’ve saved for the boss’ arrival, and whether your fellows can hold a line long enough for someone to slip into the objective room and pull off a clutch victory.

That said, the core game remains approachable. The expansions integrate with minimal friction if you’re comfortable with the base rules. The key is understanding how these new units change spawn dynamics and turn pacing. The result is a more varied game that can keep veteran players on their toes without breaking the fundamental rhythm of the campaign. If you’re building a long-run Black Plague campaign, these elements can help you avoid the dreaded “samey zombie shuffle” fatigue that can creep into any recurring scenario.

### A few practical play tips
- Plan for multiple layers of defense. The White Death fight often requires a staged response rather than a single “nuke the boss” moment.
- Use Green Horde to force players into non-standard formations. A narrow corridor can become your best friend or your worst nightmare depending on how you place Orcs and their minions.
- Gilbert & Mortimer shine in scenarios where you can leverage their pair dynamics. Try creating split objectives with one player drawing fire while the others capitalize on the opportunity to loot or activate a chain of secondary goals.

## Collectibility, Market, and Value: Are They Worth It Today?

These packs are, as you’ve probably gathered, out of print in many areas, which naturally pushes prices upward. If you’re chasing them, you’re paying a premium for nostalgia, rarity, and the potential upgrade to your local gaming group’s status as “the guy who has the good stuff.” The upside is clear: you get unique sculpts, a different flavor of play, and a brag-worthy shelf display. The downside? You’re competing with a global community of collectors who occasionally post “found it at a flea market” stories that feel more like treasure maps than online shopping.

If your budget allows and you’re after a canonical Black Plague experience with a twist, these packs are worth watching for a restock or a seller who’s upgrading their collection. If you’re new to Zombicide, you might want to try other expansions that are currently in print and actively supported. They’ll give you a taste of the same chaotic joy without triggering the wallet alarm.
