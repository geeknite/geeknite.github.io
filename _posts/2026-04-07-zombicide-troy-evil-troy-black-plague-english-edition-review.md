## What is Troy & Evil Troy in Black Plague English Edition?
Troy is the classic heroic archetype—big sword, bigger ego, and a plan that rarely survives contact with the first wave of zombies. Evil Troy is his mirror universe counterpart, a nefarious flip side that thrives on chaos and, occasionally, tipping the balance in a way that makes your team question life choices at the table. In the Black Plague setting, these two figures aren’t just more beef on the board; they act as story accelerants. Their presence shifts the drumbeat from “clear the dungeon, move to the next room” to a more cinematic chase where every turn could reveal a new trap, a new surge of undead, or a cunning plan that goes sideways spectacularly.

Thematically, Troy and Evil Troy play with the undead you’ve already spawned. They reward riskier decisions and punish tunnel-vision heroics. Their mechanics interplay with the standard zombie spawn rules, introducing high-stakes moments that feel cinematic rather than purely tactical. The result is a game that can flip from a routine scavenger hunt into an edge-of-your-seat siege in a handful of rounds.

If you’re a long-time Zombicide fan, you’ll recognize the core DNA here: cooperative play, a dash of push-your-luck randomness, and a lot of “one more turn” energy. What sets Troy and Evil Troy apart is how they orchestrate that energy—the two big bads don’t just sit in a pile of plastic with a stat block; they pull at the narrative strings and insist the players react in real time. It’s a small genre of mechanical design that makes you feel like you’re telling a story together with your friends, rather than playing a game about resource management in a cemetery.

## Setup and Learning Curve
As with most Zombicide expansions, the learning curve is gentle if you’ve played a few sessions of the base game. Troy and Evil Troy come with a pair of new rules that interact with the standard spawning mechanic, but they’re not so arcane that your group needs a full pre-game lecture. The key is to understand how the boss actions escalate when they’re within line of sight of your survivors and how the Boss deck interacts with the regular zombie deck.

A typical setup begins with the standard Black Plague map layout. You’ll place the two bosses on preferred corners of the map, draw their mission cards, and lay out the new spawn tiles. The game then slides into a rhythm that feels familiar yet thrillingly different. The first few turns are usually about getting order and sightlines: who steps into the breach, who fires the crossbow with a prayer, and who ends up feeding a wave of gnarled undead that amuses itself by giggling at your misfortune.

If you haven’t played with a boss before, you might feel a pinch of anxiety during the first couple of boss activations. Don’t panic; the rules are designed to punish rigid plans rather than punish players who think on the fly. The Boss cards push you to consider movement range, line-of-sight, and the timing of your lucky rolls. If you’re a veteran, you’ll appreciate the way Troy and Evil Troy slow the metronome of the game and force you to adjust your usual “go-go-go” playstyle.

For folks who love streaming or sharing their board game nights, Troy and Evil Troy do nothing but elevate the spectacle. The staggered reveals, the boss actions, and the occasional “oh no, not that card” moment translate well to video or photo captures. You’ll get a lot of “boss moments” that your audience will eat up, which is a nice side benefit of a well-designed expansion.

## Gameplay Experience: What It Feels Like to Play
Let’s break down the core gameplay impact. The addition of Troy and Evil Troy changes two core dimensions: tempo and risk. On the tempo front, your round cadence shifts as these bosses bring with them a certain inevitability. You’ll sense a pressure cooker effect—the map opens up, zombies flood in, and the bosses’ actions accelerate the pace toward a cliffhanger finale rather than a predictable number of rounds. On the risk side, you’ll have to decide whether to commit resources to neutering a boss early or absorb a few extra rotters in order to set up a game-winning chain of events later.

A practical example: Evil Troy’s special abilities may redirect attacks or reassign threats across different zones. This means that even if your strongest survivor is trucking toward a safe exit, Evil Troy might swoop in to alter your trajectory with a single, well-timed ability. The result is not just a game of dice and card draws; it’s a puzzle with a pulse that you solve while the map tries to eat you alive.

If you’re new to the concept of “boss fatigue,” this is a perfect entry. You don’t want to burn the boss too quickly, because that would steal the dramatic arc. You want to pace your actions to maximize survivability while still feeling like you’re racing toward a meaningful objective. The design errs on the side of cinematic tension rather than pure mechanical optimization, which suits the game’s broader theme: you’re fighting for your life in a dark, grotesque fairytale where luck and strategy must dance together.

To illustrate the experience, here are some common moments you’ll recognize after a few sessions:

- The first boss surge that makes you realize you’re not just playing a puzzle, you’re playing a horror scene with a timer.
- The moment you realize a seemingly innocuous weapon card becomes your saving grace when the boss sneaks behind your lines.
- The table-wide groans of “we can still pull this off” that turn into cheers when a risky plan finally pays off.

For our community Tuesday night folk, this expansion delivers storytelling more than it delivers a strict optimization path. If you crave clever tactics and a little theatrical chaos, Troy and Evil Troy are right up your alley.

## How It Plays: A Quick Rules Run-through
Whether you’re a veteran or a fresh face, a quick run-through helps set expectations. The core loop remains familiar: choose survivors, embark on a mission, slash your way through zombies, and avoid getting overwhelmed by the undead tide. The Troy & Evil Troy add a few twist cards that affect how waves spawn and how the boss actions trigger. Here’s a compact overview of what to expect:

- Boss mechanics: Troy and Evil Troy each have unique actions that trigger at opposite times, modulating both threat level and the available safe-hand slots on the board.
- Card interactions: The new mission and boss cards interact with existing weapon and armor cards, creating fresh synergies and, occasionally, counterintuitive traps.
- Spawn dynamics: The expansion changes spawn dynamics enough to require you to think about positioning more carefully—suddenly the “safe corner” isn’t so safe when a boss turn aligns with a surge.
- Endgame shift: Boss phases add a layer of narrative urgency as the endgame approaches. You’ll feel the pressure to coordinate a final assault that doesn’t fizzle into a chaotic pile of suffering.

If you want a deeper dive into rules, the best guide is always your group’s shared understanding. If a rule feels ambiguous, pause and agree on a house rule before the die gets cast—this is a game that rewards clear communication and flexible thinking, not rigid enforcement of every minor nuance.

## Strategy, Tactics, and Table Etiquette
The presence of Troy and Evil Troy nudges players toward a few strategic learning points:

- Positioning matters more than you expect. A couple of tiles can make the difference between stalling the enemy horde and a glorious, albeit dangerous, sprint for the exit.
- Resource timing is everything. You’ll want to stagger your best weapons’ usage to align with boss phases. The moment you Realize you’ve got a perfect “burn down the boss on round X” plan, you’ll either reap rewards or watch it collapse spectacularly—usually both.
- Cooperation is king. The bosses force you to talk through threats and plan a multi-step sequence where each player’s action enables the next. In other words, diplomacy and dice luck become the core of your strategy.
- Risk management: sometimes you’ll choose the riskier approach to secure a narrative win. Those moments are where the table really bonds—watching a risky gambit pay off or explode in your face is a story you’ll be telling later with glee.

If you’re new to team-based horror strategic games, try to establish a simple pre-game ritual: a quick check-in to confirm who’s highest risk tolerance for the night, what the favorite weapon is, and whether anyone wants to play a “spotter” role to call out dense zombie swarms before they overwhelm a line.

## The Final Verdict and Recommendations
Zombicide Troy and Evil Troy in the Black Plague English edition stands out as a well-balanced, thematically rich expansion that infuses a bit of cinematic drama into a game structure that could otherwise feel like a repetitive grind. It doesn’t revolutionize Zombicide, but it does reframe the pacing and injects a strong sense of boss-driven tension that most players will find refreshing. If you enjoy narrative heaviness, boss mechanics, and the occasional high-stakes moment where one lucky roll could tilt the entire table’s fate, this expansion is a solid choice.

Who should buy it:
- Groups that have a regular Black Plague session and want a new focal antagonist
