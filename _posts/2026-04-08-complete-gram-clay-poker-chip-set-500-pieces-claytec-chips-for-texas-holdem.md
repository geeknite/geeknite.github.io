Build and Materials: Gram Clay in Action
Clay chips stand out due to their composition: a clay composite that’s baked to a sturdy, yet slightly pliable, finish. The goal is a product that has the heft of something casino-adjacent without the fragility of pure ceramic. The All-Gray Box at a Local Cafe doesn’t quite capture the feel; you need to physically hold them to appreciate the balance.

- Weight: Expect chips in the 11.5–12 g range, give or take a few tenths depending on batch and manufacturing tolerances. That weight is what gives a real “thunk” when you drop a bet into the pot and a solid “clang” when they hit the felt.
- Durability: Clay composites handle the rigors of weekly sessions better than cheap plastic and are less prone to the cracking you might see on stoneware chips. That said, if you’re prone to tennis-player-level vigor with stacks, you’ll still want to avoid throwing them into the air like a ninja—gravity remains undefeated.
- Edge design: A crisp edge bevel helps the color inlays stay legible and reduces the risk of edge chips from rapid shuffles. The texture usually includes a slight ridging around the edge to enhance grip, which is especially useful on slick tables.

Maintenance and Longevity
- Cleaning: Wipe with a soft cloth after a game night. If you’ve got a stubborn chalk residue, you can use a light damp cloth. Do not soak the chips; water can seep into the edges and ruin the color integrity.
- Storage: Keep chips in a dry environment; avoid extreme humidity, which can affect the inlays and the overall look. If you store them in a bag, ensure that the bag has soft padding to avoid edge wear against hard surfaces.
- Wear and tear: It’s typical to see minor edge chipping on the occasional chip after heavy use, but you should not see widespread flaking or rub-off. If you do, consider contacting customer service or purchasing a replacement subset for color continuity.
