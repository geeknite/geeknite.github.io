## Card details in the era of turn-of-the-millennium whimsy

The LV.24 tag, borrowed from the habit of early Japanese and US printings where some Pokémon were printed with levels before the modern practice of "basic/Stage 1/Stage 2" clarity became universal, is a quirky wink to collectors who enjoy the nuance. In practice, LV.24 signals a certain era: the moment when Nintendo, Wizards of the Coast, and anime fans all tried to decide how much personality to squeeze into a 2 5/8 inch card.

This card’s set number is 044, which in many English printings places it early in the base run where the art style featured bold outlines and colors that pop on your kitchen table and, frankly, in coffee shops across the math-obsessed fandom. The card type is Grass (with the era’s penchant for Poison-type synergy on Gloom’s evolutionary line), and the text label includes the Southwestern aesthetic of Erika's gym flair: a hint of endurance, a dash of lavender, and a lot of old-school charm.

## Playability: where a garden gnome meets a battlefield
In vintage formats, energy economy and status effects ruled the battlefield more often than we’d like to admit. Erika's Gloom LV.24 No.044 sits in that fuzzy middle ground where it’s not overshadowed by the big boss cards of later sets but still has enough personality to be a legitimate, if situational, play. The card’s move set—whatever the exact wording—centers around inflicting a status condition (poison) and imposing a gentle nuisance (Stun Spore-like effects). The practical takeaway: Erika’s Gloom LV.24 No.044 is not a modern hyper-efficient piece, but it’s a charming anchor for a nostalgia-focused build that prioritizes longevity, resourcefulness, and the kind of strategic patience that only a 1999–2003 Pokemon Card Game veteran could appreciate.

## The hunt, the art, and the value: collecting Erika’s Gloom
Collectibility in vintage cards is a weird cocktail of nostalgia, print runs, and condition. Erika's Gloom LV.24 No.044 sits in a sweet spot for many collectors: not a “holy grail”, but a card that receives regular attention in community trades and price guides. The No. 044 index gives it a definite identity across multiple print lines; that’s a big win for grade chase and for binder lovers who want to fill a hole in a top-tier set.

What makes this card interesting as a collectible is its potential for misprints and variants. Some print runs included different holo patterns, subtle color shifts, or text alignment variations that can add delirious value to a copy in pristine condition. For the collector, Erika’s Gloom LV.24 No.044 (ungraded) might hover around modest prices, while well-preserved copies with crisp centering and untouched gloss can fetch significantly higher prices in the right market window. For the casual collector, the joy lies not in chasing a fortune but in owning a piece of the era’s aesthetic. The moment you slide Erika’s Gloom LV.24 into a soft sleeve, you’ve got a tangible artifact from the days of binder-sleeved wanderlust and weekend tournament shenanigans.

Because price can swing with time, if you’re new to vintage Pokémon cards, you should approach Erika's Gloom LV.24 No.044 as a fun collector’s piece rather than an investment instrument. The market loves stories, and a card that carries a gym-leader name and a LV tag can spark conversations in online groups and local meetups. In addition to price, consider the card’s authenticity, border quality, and surface gloss. A good rule: if it looks too glossy to be real in the primary region where you live, it might require closer scrutiny or a professional evaluation.

A small note for the seasoned: check for legend-level misprints like misaligned borders or inconsistent text. The early days of the Pokemon TCG were experimental in printing; that means there are some quirky attributes that might be mistaken for rare features. Those curiosities are what fuel the “damn, I can’t believe this exists” kind of joy that drives many collectors to invest in a binder full of Gloom variants. Erika's Gloom LV.24 No.044 is a card that can anchor a conversation about how far the brand has come while serving as a reminder of the charm of older set design.
