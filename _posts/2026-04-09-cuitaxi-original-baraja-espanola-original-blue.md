## What is Baraja Española? A quick primer before we dive into the blue hue
Baraja Española is not your standard poker deck. It’s a 40-card configuration featuring four suits: Espadas (swords), Copas (cups), Bastos (clubs or wands), and Oros (gold coins). Each suit has cards numbered from 1 through 7, and then 10 (Sota), 11 (Caballo), and 12 (Rey). That’s right, no 8s and 9s, because historically someone somewhere decided the 8 and 9 were not “Spanish enough” for the aesthetic or the gameplay. The deck is used for a slew of traditional games such as Brisca, Tute, and Mus in various regional flavors across Spain and Latin America. It’s not just a tool; it’s a social device. It tells you, “we’re going to play a game that requires memory, bluffing, and maybe a little bit of melodrama.”

Cuitaxi’s Original Blue refuses to pretend that a simple card back is enough to carry a night’s mood. The colors aren’t merely decorative; they’re a signal that you’re about to engage in something a touch more ceremonial than your average Go Fish session.

## Care and maintenance: keeping your blue wonder sharp
Like any beloved tool, the Original Blue deck rewards regular but gentle care. Here are a few practical tips:
- Store in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight. Sunlight tends to fade blues faster than you’d expect, and you don’t want your deck to look like a variant of a smurf’s pocket collection.
- Handle with clean hands; oils and lotions can mark the backs over time and create a glossy, fingerprinted history that might hinder clean shuffles.
- Use a card box or a cloth mat on the table. A soft surface reduces edge wear and keeps your shuffles from going rogue when someone trips over a cat (or a dramatic player who slams a card a bit too hard).
- Clean occasionally with a dry microfiber cloth. Avoid water or solvents; you’re not restoring an antique piano; you’re preserving a 40-card piece of social joy.
