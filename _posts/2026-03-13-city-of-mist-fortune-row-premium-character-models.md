## Design and craftsmanship
### Sculpt quality and character
Fortune Row models lean into a narrative-first approach. Each figure embodies a persona rather than a generic archetype. The Dapper Gambler wears a pinstriped coat with a scarf fluttering as if caught in a city breeze; The Velvet Oracle has an oversized hat and a cloak that flows like a rumor you can almost hear in the dark. The Cardsharp poses with a blade-calm confidence, while the Night Archivist carries a tome that seems heavy with secrets. The variety in dialects of menace and charm makes it easy to assign a particular model to known PC concepts or to NPCs you want to launch into your next one-shot.

### Materials and durability
Most Fortune Row minis are cast in a high-quality resin blend selected for crisp detail and a pleasant matte finish. Resin is a double-edged sword: it captures fine lines better than pewter, but it can be a tad brittle if you’re not careful with handling. Expect very fine edges around cuffs, lapels, and accessories; a gentle rinse in warm soapy water before painting helps remove mild mold-release residues. In practice, you’ll likely experience one of two things:

- A small mold line along a seam that’s easy to trim with a hobby knife. 
- A pleasant, stable piece that holds fine sand-and-paint work without cracking at the elbow or ankle.

If you’re a painter who loves heavy shading, these models respond well to contrast paints and a rational dry brush to pick out the delicate reliefs without turning into a muddy mess.

### Scale and bases
The line sticks to the traditional 28–32 mm scale space, which keeps them visually compatible with typical RPG miniatures and sceneries you’d align with City of Mist-inspired layouts. This makes them a good match for standard battlefield grids, narrative display boards, or a coffee-table diorama featuring your favorite noir cast. The bases are round and compact, designed to minimize footprint on a dense tabletop while still giving a solid anchor for dramatic poses. If you want to mix Fortune Row with other companies’ minis, you’ll likely be fine as long as you keep to the same base diameter.

## Painting and customization potential
### Priming and painting tips
If you plan to prime before painting, use a light gray or white primer to keep the fine details legible. Given the resin’s smooth surfaces, a matte primer is ideal to prevent unwanted gloss that can complicate shading. For the initial color wash, use a medium-waint heat: you want the recesses to settle into the lines but not flood the crevices with so much pigment that you lose the blade-edge drama on the Cardsharp.

Layering and edge highlights are where Fortune Row shines. The characters’ outfits present excellent opportunities for color storytelling: velvet blacks with silver trimming, pinstripes that pop when highlighted, and metallic accents that catch the eye as the narrative shifts from alleyway to rooftop. The faces benefit from a pale stone undertone—slightly cooler than your usual skin tone—to give that ethereal noir glow that City of Mist players adore.

### Multi-part or single-piece assembly?
All six figures in the sample kit are designed as single-piece casts with a minimal mounting post. This means less glue, less clamping, and quicker tabletop setup. If you hit a rare casting blemish or need to remove a pin on a few parts, a tiny amount of plastic cement or super glue works well. For those who prefer a no-fuss display piece, you’ll be painting them in place on their bases and calling it done within a few sessions.

### Customization ideas and min-maxing the narrative
Fortune Row is a fantastic springboard for traction between “looks like a hero” and “acts like a myth.” Here are ways to maximize your narrative payoff:

- Assign a feature color to each character’s costume (e.g., crimson for The Velvet Gambler, midnight blue for The Night Archivist) to help players instantly recognize who’s who at the table.
- Create a quick backstory card for each model, tying them to Fortune Row’s mythos and your ongoing campaign arcs. This helps players integrate the miniature into your dramatic beat, turning a pretty figure into a character they care about.
- Use the models as “narrative markers” during scenes: when a player makes a critical choice, move the corresponding model closer to the central map to visually emphasize the decision’s gravity.

## Practical tips for owners
- Pre-wash any resin minis to remove mold release. A soft brush and warm water with mild soap does wonders.
- Prime with a light gray to capture shadows cleanly. Don’t go too dark on the primer; you want the romance of the details to shine through.
- Use thin paint layers; you don’t want to obscure the delicate edges around cuffs and lapels.
- Seal with a matte varnish to preserve the cinematic look under bright room lighting.
- Store upright in a cool, dry place to avoid any warping that might come from high humidity.
