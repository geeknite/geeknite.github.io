## How to play Marvel Codenames
### The setup
The setup is standard Codenames with a Marvel coat of paint. You lay out 25 word tiles in a 5x5 grid. You randomize the team assignment and place red and blue agent markers accordingly. The assassin marker sits in place, ready to ruin your day if someone misreads the board. The hero-themed cards get shuffled into the word deck so that each card presents a Marvel character or a variant title that your spymaster must weave into a clue.

### The roles and turn structure
As with classic Codenames, one player acts as the spymaster for each team. The spymaster sees a key card that tells them which 9 (or sometimes 8) of the 25 tiles belong to their team, which ones belong to the opposing team, which tile is the assassin, and which tiles are neutral. The spymaster’s job is to give a one-word clue that points to as many of their own tiles as possible, plus a number indicating how many tiles on the board the clue covers. The opposing team’s spymaster tries to intercept with better clues. The operative players then guess the tiles one by one, using the clue and the team’s knowledge to flip tiles until they run out of guesses or hit the assassin and end the round.

In Marvel Codenames, the one-word clue needs to connect to the Marvel cards. This makes the job of the spymaster trickier—because many heroes share similar traits or comic book arcs, your clues will need to walk a tightrope between specific and broad. A well-chosen clue can connect two or three heroes on the board and push the round decisively into your corner. A misfired clue can ping a villain from the other team or accidentally reveal the killer assassin and end the round with an embarrassing flourish.

### The Marvel twist on clueing
What Marvel Codenames adds is flavor without breaking the core rule set. Spymasters still want to maximize hits on their own heroes while avoiding enemy tiles and the assassin. The Marvel cards often come with overlapping associations. For example, a clue like hero team up or a well-known character traits set might tie together characters across the board. The trick is to keep the clue as a single word while the actual interpretation spans several cards. The result is the same adrenaline rush as classic Codenames, but with the added gratification of revealing a heroic universe you enjoy.

### Difficulty curve and player count
Codenames scales across a range of players, and Marvel Codenames does not buck that trend. With 4 players (two teams of two) you get a tight, snappy game where each round is brief and the tension is high. As you add more players, the decision making becomes more collaborative and slightly messier in a fun way. If you opt for a larger group, you can even run simultaneous sessions with multiple spymasters in a single room, which creates a delightful chaotic energy that Marvel fans tend to love. The game supports standard team sizes well, and there is enough content variety in the hero cards to keep repeated sessions feeling fresh.

## Tips and tricks for winning Marvel Codenames
### Strategy for spymasters
- Start with a broad, connective clue that targets 2 or 3 easy-to-blind-spot heroes rather than aiming for 9 possible hits. This reduces risk while maintaining momentum.
- When you are unsure, pick a clue that covers two active tiles and one neutral tile instead of risking a blue or red hit on a high-stakes guess.
- Keep track of the board visually. If a villain could plausibly be inferred by a single concept, consider whether that concept will lead your team astray.
- Use hero associations that your team actually recognizes. It is tempting to pull references from the deepest corners of the MCU, but if your team needs a cheat sheet, your clue will slow down the round and ruin the pace.

### Strategy for field operatives
- Quick reads beat long deliberation on a crowded board. If you can confidently identify two or three tiles with a single guess, go for it.
- If your spymaster is uncertain, communicate clearly and efficiently. Silence is not friendship, but a calm nod can prevent a misstep on the table.
- Don’t tunnel vision on the obvious. Some of the best plays come from mid-tier cards that unlock more complex connections without exploding your board in a single turn.
