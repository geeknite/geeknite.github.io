## Assembly: how long, how hard, how heroic

If you’re picturing a weekend project that requires an extra pair of hands and a small crane, fear not. The ORCC trampolines are designed for home assembly without needing a degree in mechanical engineering. Depending on the size (8ft to 14ft), the number of springs, and your tool kit, you’re looking at a typical build window of anywhere from 2 to 5 hours. This is a rough estimate and heavily dependent on your level of pre-assembly caffeination and pet dog willingness to supervise.

A few tips to make assembly less “ordeal” and more “display of craftsmanship”:
- Clear the area. You’ll want a clean circle for the frame and net deployment. A clear workspace is your friend.
- Organize springs and hardware. Lay them out by size so you’re not hunting for a tiny bolt while the kids pretend to launch a space shuttle from the trampoline.
- Watch the alignment. Frame joints should line up cleanly; if a piece doesn’t fit, don’t force it—re-check the parts. Forcing parts is how you end up with a wobbly platform and a grim sense of self-importance.
- Have at least one helper. There’s nothing wrong with a little peer pressure to keep the assembly moving and the jokes flowing.

### Step-by-step essentials (high level)
1) Assemble the frame on a flat, clear surface. 2) Attach legs, ensuring all joints are securely fastened. 3) Mount the frame padding and springs in a consistent pattern. 4) Attach the jumping mat. 5) Install the enclosure net and zipper. 6) Do a test bounce with supervision and a focus on safety.

## Durability and maintenance: keeping the bounce alive

Outdoor gear isn’t a one-and-done purchase; it’s a relationship. You’ll want to keep an eye on mat wear, padding integrity, and the net’s tautness. Here are some practical tips to keep your ORCC trampoline in prime shape:
- Clean the mat and padding regularly. Leaves and small debris collect in the gaps and can degrade materials over time.
- Check springs for signs of fatigue. If you notice a spring doesn’t spring back as eagerly, replace it promptly to avoid uneven bounce.
- Inspect the net for tears or loose attachments. Small rips can become bigger when kids treat the trampoline like a personal gymnasium.
- Weather protection matters. If you’re in a region with harsh winters, consider storing the mat and net indoors during months of non-use to extend lifespan (or at least cover it with a breathable tarp).
- Sun exposure is real. UV rays can fade fabrics; look for color retention and padding resilience over seasons to gauge the product’s long-term performance.

With reasonable maintenance, the ORCC trampolines keep delivering a reliable bounce for years. You’ll get to see your kids’ and maybe your own, questionable dance moves become a quarterly tradition rather than a one-off curiosity.
