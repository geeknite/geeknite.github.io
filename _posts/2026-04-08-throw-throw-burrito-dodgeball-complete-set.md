## How to Play: the Core Ideas Behind the Chaos
If you’ve played a typical deck-builder or party game, Throw Throw Burrito’s concept might feel strange at first. The aim is not to accumulate points through strategic card combos; it’s to survive the volley, collect sets, and keep your dignity intact while being a little ridiculous. The Complete Set enhances this with more cards and the two burritos that physically embody the chaotic spirit of the game.

### Objective
The objective is simple in principle: score the most points by collecting matched sets of cards while dodging burrito hits. Points are awarded for successful collections, dodges, and mastering certain card sequences. The more you enjoy a well-timed dodge and a perfect burrito toss, the higher your satisfaction level climbs. The exact scoring rules are laid out in the rule book, but you’ll quickly learn that the joy is often in bending the rules just enough to keep everyone engaged and laughing.

### Setup
1. Shuffle the deck and deal a starting hand to each player.
2. Place the two burritos within easy reach of all players.
3. Create a central discard pile and a draw pile.
4. Decide on a first thrower, who will begin by flipping a card that triggers a “throw” or “dodge” action.

In the Complete Set, this becomes a little more cinematic. The additional cards add new triggers like “double toss,” “swap fate,” or “mystery burrito morphs,” which can turn a quiet game into what feels like a friendly ritual for chaos lovers.

### Turn flow
A typical turn involves drawing a card, playing a card if you can, and possibly triggering a burrito toss. The exact sequence varies by card effect, but the principle remains the same: you’re alternating between trying to collect cards and avoiding being hit by a burrito.

- If you draw an action card, you may have to perform a throw, dodge, or swap action. These actions create opportunities for your opponents to sting you with a well-placed burrito shot.
- If you draw a throw card, you must either throw a burrito or pass the action to the next player, depending on the symbol on the card. It’s a deceptively simple system that yields unpredictable outcomes every time you reach the table.
- If you’re hit by a burrito, you take a penalty and draw extra cards; if you successfully dodge, you keep your cool and possibly gain a small advantage. The tension comes from whether you’ll be the one delivering the next dramatic toss or dodgy escape.

The mechanics are designed to encourage quick decisions and lighthearted risk-taking. You’ll notice people shouting “Dodge!” in unison, which is a surprisingly satisfying social glue that makes you feel less like you’re playing a game and more like you’re starring in a chaotic improv comedy.

### Endgame and scoring quick tips
As the deck tightens and the burritos fly faster, you’ll want to consolidate your points by targeting matched sets and keeping track of the cards that grant extra points when completed. The Complete Set makes some of those sequences longer and more satisfying, but it can also turn a simple collection into a long chase that tests patience. If you want a fast finish, you can implement a house rule: when a player reaches a threshold of points, the round ends and the highest-scoring player wins. This prevents marathon sessions from devolving into an endless burrito war where everyone forgets what numbers mean.

## Plugin Ideas: House Rules to Turbocharge Your Sessions
Geeknite loves adding a few house rules to standard party games, and Throw Throw Burrito is no exception. Here are some simple adjustments that can significantly alter the pacing and humor without breaking the game’s core charm:

- The Dice of Destiny: Add a six-sided dice to replace some coin-flip outcomes. Roll 1–3 means dodge; 4–6 means you must throw. It creates a rhythm that some players find more engaging than pure card effects.
- Time Attack Mode: Set a timer for each round (e.g., 5 minutes) and declare the winner by most points earned within that window. This keeps sessions tight and energetic.
- Duo-Only Mode: If you have a night with a smaller crowd (2–3 players), implement a relay system where players pair up against a rotating third person. It adds teamwork dynamics and more banter.
- Custom Card Draft: Allow players to draft a couple of extra cards at the start of the game. It introduces strategic choice without compromising the essential chaos.

## How to Teach It: Teaching Dipsticks and New Players Alike
Teaching Throw Throw Burrito is straightforward, but you’ll want to do it with energy. The best learning happens when the room is already fired up with curiosity and a willingness to mispronounce burrito-related terms for comedic effect.

- Start with a quick demonstration: show how to draw, how to play an action, and how to throw. Someone should give a mock throw to illustrate the motion and timing. This reduces the intimidation factor for first-time players.
- Emphasize the dodge concept early: dodges are not guaranteed, and some cards require a bit of risk. It’s important to know when to hold and when to go all-in with a throw.
- Keep the tempo high: set a timer if needed and remind players that the real goal is to have fun and not to master precision. The Complete Set is not about perfect aim; it’s about togetherness through ridiculous plays.
- Use a soft voice for punishment or encouragement: you want the environment to stay friendly, even when someone is DA-BOOM-ing into the next room with a burrito bounce.
