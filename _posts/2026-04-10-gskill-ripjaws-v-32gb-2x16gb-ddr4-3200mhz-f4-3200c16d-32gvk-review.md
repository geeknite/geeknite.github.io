Overclocking and Tuning: A Gentle Ride
-------------------------------------
If you’re into pushing the envelope, the RipJaws V kit provides a reasonable canvass for light overclocking. Here’s what you can expect and how to approach it:

- Stock: 3200MHz CL16-18-18-38 @ 1.35V. It’s a rock-solid baseline that most boards will happily boot into.
- Mild overclock: Pushing to 3400–3600MHz is possible for many kits, with tightened timings perhaps to CL14-18-18-38. Expect higher voltage (1.4V recommended by many guides) and ensure you have a capable motherboard VRM.
- Stability testing: Always run a stability test after any adjustments. A few hours of Prime95 blend or AIDA64 can help uncover instability that your eyes might miss in a quick test.
- Longevity considerations: Running higher voltage and tighter timings can shorten the life of the modules marginally. If you’re chasing peak numbers, treat it as a hobbyist venture rather than a daily driver setting.

If you prefer a safer path, keep it at the XMP profile and enjoy the guaranteed stability. You’ll still get the vast majority of the performance you’re paying for, with less tinkering and fewer late-night BIOS scrambles.

Install Guide: Quick and Easy
-----------------------------
1. Power down and unplug your PC. Ground yourself.
2. Open your case and locate the DIMM slots. Prepare to remove existing RAM if upgrading.
3. Insert the first stick at a 45-degree angle, then press down until the latches click. Repeat with the second stick in the adjacent slot for dual-channel operation.
4. If you’re building on a system that supports XMP, go into BIOS/UEFI and enable XMP Profile 1 (3200MHz CL16). Save settings and reboot.
5. Run a memory stress test (e.g., MemTest86 or your preferred tool) to verify stability. If there are errors, re-seat the modules, re-test, and consider slight voltage/timing tweaks if you’re comfortable.
6. Boot into your OS and verify the installed memory shows 32GB with the correct speed.

This kit plays nicely with most consumer-grade motherboards, and the initial setup experience is typically painless. If you’re a first-timer, don’t panic—the BIOS prompts are often your friend on the learning curve.
