## What's in the Box and What It Looks Like When You Open It

The ZCD007 set is ostensibly a pile of modular floor tiles that snap together into a sprawling city-block vibe while you pretend you understand how line-of-sight works. In the box, you get a curated assortment of hallway tiles, room tiles, staircases, and a handful of specialized tiles that create choke points, broken doors, and the occasional trap you wish you could blame on your players for not reading the rulebook.

- A standard assortment of square and rectangular floor panels, each bearing a distinct floor texture: cracked concrete, tiled floors, sewer grates, and the occasional hazard line that makes your minions whine when they step on it.
- A handful of multi-level tiles for staircases and ladders, letting you build those creaking building interiors that would give even a Brujo a headache.
- A small set of objective markers and token slots that integrate with the base game components without requiring a separate math degree to assemble.
- A full-color cardstock sheet for doorways and wall textures, which is the mini-native art of the set; you can cut along with the included instructions and feel like a tiny Game Designer who forgot to eat lunch.

The first thing you notice is the high-contrast art that CMON tends to do in their tile sets. The textures pop, the edges are clean, and the corners fit snugly enough that you don’t dread picking up a tile and ripping a chunk of it off. But the real magic lies not in the art but in the glut of layout potential. You tilt, rotate, and rearrange to your heart’s content, and somehow it still feels cohesive rather than a puzzle that would make a low-level elf cry.

## Build Quality: Plastic, Cardboard, and a Dash of Bravado

Let’s talk tactile details, because, in the end, tactile is how you judge any tabletop accessory to a large extent. The tiles themselves are a matte cardstock-backed plastic composite. Not as brittle as older CMON tile sets, not as flexible as your grandma’s old knitting needles. They snap together with a satisfying little click that makes you feel like you just saved a life by not misplacing a hex tile in Game Night Hell.

Pros:
- Durable enough for club play without needing to tape a thousand replacement edges.
- The print quality on the tile artwork is crisp; tiny crews of color depth enjoy the crisp grunge aesthetic that appeals to zombie lovers.
- The edges align cleanly, reducing the risk that a misaligned tile ruins a tense moment when you should be sprinting zombies instead of correcting geometry.

Cons:
- A few tiles can be slightly warping if left in direct sunlight, which is your fault for leaving anything near a window like you’re living in a shipping container. Keep them in a box and they’ll be fine.
- The sprue thickness can vary between runs; you might end up with a tile that has slightly stiffer corners, but it’s nothing a little gentle heat can’t fix.

If you’ve handled any Zombicide tile sets before, the physical experience of this ZCD007 set is pleasantly familiar. It’s not premium-level resin or metal money-sink premium; it’s good, sturdy, battle-ready board-game hardware with a dash of that CMON charm that says, We built this for gamers who insist on a little sparkle in their apocalypse.

## Assembly Tips: Getting It Right the First Time

- Start with a small pilot layout to get players comfortable with the tile geometry and door placement. You’ll learn the cheat codes of how the hallways interlock and how to place stair tiles for maximum dramatic effect.
- Use painter's tape or low-tack tape to mark corridors on a larger table before you start building. It saves you from puzzle-frustration while your players argue about fog-of-war rules.
- If you want to preserve tile edges, consider a light sealant or varnish—just don’t go overboard. The goal is to protect the surface while retaining that satisfying click when you connect tiles.
- For storage, use the original box or a dedicated organizer; these tiles aren’t so heavy you’ll injure your back carrying them, but you’ll thank yourself later when you don’t have to jam them back in a tote after a 4-hour session.
