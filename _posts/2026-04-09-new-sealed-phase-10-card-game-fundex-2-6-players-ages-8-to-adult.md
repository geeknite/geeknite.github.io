Gameplay overview

Phase 10 is a hand management game built around completing ten progressive phases. Each phase has a specific criterion such as a run of numbers, a set of the same color, or a combination of both. In practice, you draw a card from the draw pile, discard to the discard pile, and take turns either laying down your current phase or adding cards to existing phases on the table. The goal is to complete the Phase 10 you are working on before the others, but the catch is that you must complete a phase in order. You can only advance after finishing the current phase, which means a little patience, a little luck, and a lot of gentle coercion to convince grandma that a 7-card run is a legitimate Phase 9 replacement.

Rule skim for the uninitiated

The baseline Phase 10 rules are fairly approachable:

- Each player starts with a hand of 10 cards. You draw one, discard one each turn.
- There are ten phases in the game. The number of players determines the pace, with more players meaning more competition for the same bonuses and the same wildcards.
- A phase is complete when you lay down a valid set or run. If you finish a phase, you advance to the next one on your next turn. If you fail to complete, your phase remains the same and the risk of a chaotic steal from the person next to you rises.
- The game ends when someone completes Phase 10 twice or when the draw pile runs out, depending on your house rules.

Mini-tactics and strategy tips

Here are some quick tips to maximize your FUNDEX Phase 10 experience:

- Prioritize early phase completion: Getting your early phases out of the way reduces anxiety when the table becomes a human who forgot to bring a snack and the board game world enters chaos mode.
- Remember wild cards are flexible tools: Use them to complete a needed set or to bridge a gap for a difficult phase. The wild can be your best friend when you are trying to salvage a hand.
- Keep an eye on others phase progress: If someone is close to finishing a big run or a critical set, you can adjust your plays to block them by discarding cards they need, but not too aggressively or you risk getting called out by the host for cheating.
- Manage your hand size: Do not hoard too many cards; you will be forced to draw again and again, potentially giving others the advantage. A lean, efficient hand is a happy hand—like a healthy snack, but with more numbers.
- Use the skip cards judiciously: A well-timed skip lets you disrupt your opponents at the exact moment you sense their next turn will break them. But use skips carefully; otherwise you risk becoming the villain of the family saga.
