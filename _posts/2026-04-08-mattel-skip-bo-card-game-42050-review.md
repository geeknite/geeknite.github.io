## How It Plays: Mechanics and Pace

Skip-Bo is essentially a sequencing engine. Each player has a stock pile at the start that’s face-down. The goal is to play all the cards from your stock pile into the four central build piles in ascending order from 1 to 12. You can only play a card if it’s the next number in line, and you can use Skip-Bo (the wild cards) to act as any number when you’re stuck. The genius of the game is in the tension: do you use a Skip-Bo now to save your turn, or hold it for the moment when you absolutely need it to save your game?

The 2-6 player mode scales nicely: with more players, the draw piles and the chance of someone stealing your momentum increases. Two players is a surprisingly strategic duel that can hinge on a single misplay or a brilliantly timed Skip-Bo play. With six players, you’re in a chaos zone where the table erupts in a chorus of groans, cheers, and desperate last-second plays. The pace is brisk: most rounds finish in 15-25 minutes, which is perfect for a quick session or a game that loops in before a movie night.

### Core decisions you’ll face
- When to use a Skip-Bo card as a generic wildcard or as a specific number to keep your stock pile rolling.
- How aggressively you push for a new build pile versus stalling to improve your hand.
- The order of players’ responses when a middle-card crisis hits—you know, that moment when someone’s build pile threatens to derail the entire table.
