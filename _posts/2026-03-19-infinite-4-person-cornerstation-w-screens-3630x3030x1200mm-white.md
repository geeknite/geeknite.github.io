## Space, Footprint, and Ergonomics: Will It Actually Fit Your Room?
### Dimensions: 3630 x 3030 x 1200 mm
Let’s translate the numbers into human terms. The 1.2-meter height is tall enough to offer comfortable seating for most chairs without making anyone feel like they’re a child at a big table. ### Space Planning Tips
- Leave at least 60 cm of clearance behind each chair so people can roll their chairs without turning the entire desk into a mechanical bull ride.
- Plan for a central power strip and cable raceway under the spine. You’ll thank yourself when you don’t have cables snaking across the floor like spaghetti code.
- Consider vertical storage on the outer edges for shared peripherals: a printer, a docking station, or a hamper for the inevitable paper cubes that somehow survive every office’s digital era.

### Ergonomics for Four: A Coordinated Dance
With four stations facing inward toward a shared screen array, you’ll want to align monitor heights and tilt to match each person’s primary seating position. The risk of “neck gymnastics” goes up when you have multiple users at different heights, but the modularity of the corner design usually means you can fine-tune height with adjustable monitor arms and a shared keyboard tray that’s wide enough for two sets of hands. A good rule of thumb is to align the top of the monitor with eye level for the primary users and tilt others slightly downward to reduce glare. If you’re serious about ergonomics, invest in a sampling of affordable keyboard trays or adjustable chairs with lumbar support. Your future self will send you a thank-you note in a Slack message.

## Real-World Use: When Four People Start Running a Small IT Department
### Collaboration vs. Chaos
In practice, a four-person corner station can either foster natural collaboration or become a chorus of competing monitors and browser tabs. The shared central spine helps keep things orderly, with each user maintaining their own space while still having the option to glance at teammates’ screens for a quick check-in. If your team thrives on ping-pong-like collaboration, this setup can be a godsend; if your crew is more “quietly efficient but occasionally distracted by memes,” you’ll want to integrate a dedicated “focus zone” or a rotating schedule for bringing screens into center stage.

### Acoustic Considerations
A large wood-and-melamine surface can reflect sound, which may amplify your team’s conversations. If your office is already an echo chamber of ideas (and occasional printer jams), you might want to add soft panels or a rug to dampen sound in the room. Noise considerations are often overlooked, but trust us: your colleagues will be grateful when the room’s acoustics don’t turn a quick stand-up into a chorus of “Did you hear what he said?”

### Cable Management Reality Check
We’ve tested with four sets of peripherals, a handful of USB hubs, and a smattering of power strips. The central channel does a decent job of hiding cables, but you’ll still want to invest in a couple of cable sleeves and zip ties for the final minimalist look. A clean cable setup reduces the incidence of “what even is that wire doing under my chair” moments and helps keep the floor free of tripping hazards—as well as your team’s dignity.
