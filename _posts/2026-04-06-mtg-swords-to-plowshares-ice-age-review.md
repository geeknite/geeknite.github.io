## A card that ages like fine black wax

### The basics you already know, or should know, and a sprinkle of Ice Age flavor

Swords to Plowshares is a white instant that says: exile target creature. Its controller gains life equal to the exiled creature's power. Simple, brutal, and somehow both surgical and theatrical at the same time. The Ice Age printing isn’t just a new coat of paint; it’s a time capsule, reminding us that back in the mid-90s, wizards were already willing to print cards that could reshuffle the entire game’s tempo with a single line of text. If you’re unfamiliar with the card’s power, picture removal that doesn’t just answer the moment but also steals your opponent’s life force and uses it to power their next nostalgic email to your mother asking why you haven’t played this game in a while.

This particular Ice Age edition holds a special place in many collectors’ hearts because it’s not just about the power level—it’s about the story you tell when you pull it from a sleeve, the artwork you’re showing your friends, and the way it smells like a long winter of cardboard and enthusiasm. ### How the card plays in today’s meta (in retrospective, with affection)

Even though Ice Age was a different era, Swords to Plowshares remains a standard of removal in many casual and some legacy decks. Its ability to exile a creature rather than just destroy it is a meaningful edge—exile means the board state can’t come back from the graveyard with a few unexiling tricks. In formats like Commander and Vintage cube, Swords to Plowshares is a staple not because it’s flashy, but because it does one thing, and it does it with elegance: it punishes your opponent for playing something big and scary, while you gain a bunch of life to keep you in the fight. In more aggressive formats, it’s a tempo destroyer; in slower formats, it’s a control cornerstone. In practice, this is the kind of spell that makes you grin when your opponent’s best plan folds because your removal is just better than you remembered.

## Ice Age printing vs others: the card you should compare with

You’ll often hear players talk about Swords to Plowshares in relation to Path to Exile or other modern day removal spells. Here’s a quick, friendly comparison you can share at the kitchen table after you’ve had your second cup of cold brew:

- Swords to Plowshares: exile target creature. Its controller gains life equal to the creature’s power. Very efficient, very decisive, and annoyingly resilient in formats that reward long attrition.
- Path to Exile: exile target creature. Its controller searched for a basic land and put it onto the battlefield tapped. The life gain is replaced by a land drop, which can feel more fair to some players and more painful to others, depending on how much you like mana ramps.

In many decks, Swords to Plowshares is the cleaner, more oppressive option when you’re looking for an immediate tempo swing. It’s a knife that not only cuts but also gives your opponent the opportunity to draw a land that might ruin your plan later—except you’re not completely punished because exile is permanent removal and life gain only matters if the game drags on. The Ice Age print is a nostalgic point here: it reminds you that the game has evolved, but some things remain beautifully infuriating.

## Collecting, grading, and storing for the long haul

Great Cond Swords to Plowshares cards deserve careful handling. Here are some practical tips to preserve value and keep your future self from regret:

- Sleeving: choose a high-clarity sleeve to show off the card’s art without hiding the glaze of the Ice Age print. A snug fit helps prevent scratches.
- Top-loading: if you’re serious about value, consider top-loading the card and storing it in a binder with a desiccant pack. Cards hate moisture; moisture hates card life.
- Environment: keep it in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight. The colors can fade if you let the sun autograph your card on the regular.
- Handling: avoid greasy fingers; oils on your skin can degrade the card’s surface over time. If you’re a gamer who snacks during long play sessions, keep a damp cloth nearby.

For those who love to display their collection, a display case with a UV-protective cover gives Swords to Plowshares a place of honor. The Ice Age printing in particular has that tactile nostalgia that makes you want to tell your non-MTG friends about this random artifact you’ve stored behind glass for years.
