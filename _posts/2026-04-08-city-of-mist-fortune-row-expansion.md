## Overview

City of Mist has always been the kind of game that makes you feel like you accidentally wandered into a noir comic book on a late Friday night, when the neon signs are blinking faster than your life choices. Fortune Row, the latest expansion in the City of Mist line, slides into your campaign like a slick cigarette smoke fog that smells vaguely like a solved mystery and an overdue bill at the same time. It doesn’t replace what you already love about the game; it injects new moods, new hooks, and a handful of optional rules that let your table chase coincidence the way a cat chases a laser pointer—intensely relentless, occasionally terrifying, and absolutely ridiculous in the best possible way.

This review attempts to unpack what Fortune Row brings to the table, how it changes the flow of sessions, and whether this expansion is a must-have for players who already own the core rules, or if it’s more of a carnival ride you take with a few friends and then politely apologize to your sleep schedule for the trauma it inflicted.

### What this expansion feels like in practice

Fortune Row isn’t a re-skin of the game. It feels like the game met a jazz pianist who decided to improvise a little—same chords, new riffs, a few surprising detours that occasionally break the usual beat. The designers have clearly listened to groups who adore the “coincidence engine” of City of Mist but wanted more routes to grease the gears. Fortune Row adds new character archetypes, new mechanics tied to luck and fate, and a handful of micro-scenarios that you can sprinkle into an existing campaign or use to seed a one-shot. If you enjoy the feel of chasing a clue that seems to slip through your fingers, Fortune Row will feel like home.

### Aesthetic and mood

Fortune Row leans into a more intimate vibe than some of the broader city-sprawl expansions. It’s less about grand megacity intrigue and more about how a single street can bend reality when luck decides to tilt the board. The art direction nudges toward dark creases of neon reflecting off rain-slick pavement, with a sharper focus on intimate scenes—private eyes at a back-alley poker table, a legendary map that only reveals its secrets under certain conditions, and a set of prop notes you can hand to players to heighten immersion. If you adore the visual language of City of Mist—the way a page can feel like a frame from a comic—Fortune Row intensifies that language without drowning you in more white space.


## What Fortune Row Adds

Fortune Row introduces several features that ripple through both the GM prep and the player experience. Here’s the main menu, so you know what’s cooking before you fold back the wrapper and pretend you’re not hungry.

### New archetypes: the faces you’ll meet on Fortune Row

Fortune Row adds a trio of playable archetypes that slot into the game’s mask-driven structure without demanding a complete rule rewrite. Each archetype has its own mini-move set, flavor, and a handful of signature items that feel both thematically tight and mechanically crunchy in the best possible way.

#### The Gambler of Luck

- Core idea: someone who believes fate can be bet on and sometimes won. They lean into conditional outcomes and can push a roll to escalate luck in a scene, often walking the line between genius and reckless.
- Signature moves: “Bet on It,” which lets a player convert a miss into a leverage moment if the GM grants a twist, and “Roll the Dice, Walk the Edge,” a move that creates a dramatic advantage but carries a cost later.
- Roleplay vibe: slick charm, a tendency to misquote old legends, and a habit of treating every clue like a card drawn at exactly the right moment.

#### The Archivist of Echoes

- Core idea: a character who stores fragments of the city’s memory—the whispers of places, people, and forgotten deals. They’re excellent at connecting disparate clues that others overlook.
- Signature moves: “Memory Lace,” which lets them stitch two disparate clues into a single narrative thread, and “Echo Card,” a resource that grants a small retrospective advantage during investigations.
- Roleplay vibe: patient, reflective, and always with a notebook that seems to record more than the eyes should admit.

#### The Velvet Warden

- Core idea: a protector who uses aura and misdirection to shield the team or manipulate crowds. They’re the “bodyguard with a social plan” archetype, equally at home in a club or a crime scene.
- Signature moves: “Crowd Pulse,” which manages the tempo of social scenes, and “Hidden Hand,” a stealthy option to nudge outcomes without overt aggression.
- Roleplay vibe: calm, commanding, and oddly polite to the point of unnerving some NPCs.

These aren’t revolutionary changes to the core mask system, but they slot cleanly into campaigns that like a slower climb toward a finale rather than a sprint to the last door. They encourage players to think about luck, memory, and social influence in new ways, without sacrificing the game’s fast rhythm.

### New mechanics: fate, luck, and the micro-magics of the city

Fortune Row tightens the weave between the players and the city’s random vitality by adding a handful of Fate mechanics. Think of Fate as a resource that barters with uncertainty: you can spend Fate to tilt a thread in your favor, but you may awaken a second-order consequence that reverberates in later scenes.

- Fate Pools: Small pools tied to scenes or locations, which players and the GM can dip into to trigger dramatic reveals or reroll effects. These aren’t “get-out-of-jail-free” tricks; they’re permanent mood-shifters that reshape how risk feels in the moment.
- Coin of Destiny: A collectible item that characters can acquire during a session. It represents a community’s shared belief in a story and can unlock special options once the coin is earned.
- Fate-Driven Encounters: The expansion provides encounter seeds that rely on manipulating fate, not brute force. You’ll see scenes where the “right” decision is more pivotal than the “strongest” action, and that’s a welcome return to the game’s investigative roots.

### Tools for the GM: seeds, cards, and sizzle

Fortune Row isn’t just a menu of archetypes and re-skins; it hands GMs a toolkit designed to accelerate session prep and heighten tension:

- Scene Seeds: A dozen micro-scenarios tied to Fortune Row’s new mechanics. These seeds emphasize mood, location, and a single twist you can reveal in 15–30 minutes of play.
- Card Decks: A small deck of prompts (fate moments, moral dilemmas, and urban legends) you can pull at opportune moments to inject energy without breaking your flow.
- GM Reference Sheets: Quick-glance references for the new moves, with practical examples. It’s the kind of thing that makes you feel like you’ve been handed a “cheatsheet from a benevolent oracle.”

### Art, layout, and accessibility

Fortune Row’s production values remain in line with City of Mist’s reputation for atmospheric presentation. The layout is clean, with panels that guide you through narrative beats as easily as a well-paced chase scene. The art direction continues to use color palettes that feel like a neon-lit dream and a noir’s morning after. Accessibility considerations are present—clarity in move descriptions, ample examples, and a focus on readability—so groups new to the city don’t drown in a sea of “how does this thing actually work?”

If you’re the kind of GM who uses props, handouts, or screen captures to set a mood, Fortune Row’s assets scale nicely. The included visuals aren’t simply decorative; they double as cues that you can drop into a session with minimal adaptation.

### How it feels on the table: pacing and flow

The expansion emphasizes a slightly slower, more investigative tempo. You’ll run fewer pure combat clashes and more scenes that hinge on choices about fate, memory, and crowd dynamics. For some groups, this can feel like a soothing massage for the mind after a marathon of big-bang gunfights. For others, it might be a tricky adjustment, especially if your table loves fast, tactical engagement. The beauty of a City of Mist session is that it can tilt toward noir, mythic, or pulp depending on the GM’s rhythm and the players’ willingness to lean into mood.

Fortune Row doesn’t force this shift; it nudges toward it in ways that feel natural, not contrived. It’s a seasoning rather than a whole new dish, which is ideal for players who adore the core recipe but want a more complex flavor profile in places.

### Story seeds and adventure hooks

If you’re hunting for ready-to-run material, Fortune Row includes a handful of seeds that are perfect for one-shot sessions or the opening acts of a larger campaign. The seeds are designed to be modular: you can pick one, couple it with a core mystery, and either lock it into your city’s current mood or flip it on its head with a single Fate spend.

Example seeds include:
- The Echoing Auction: A street-level mystery where memory fragments surface as items at a midnight auction. The players must discern whether the memories are true, altered, or engineered by a rogue art collective.
- The Gambler’s Last Card: A high-stakes confrontation where luck itself becomes a weapon. The team must decide whether to push the coin of destiny or walk away with a lesser victory that won’t destroy the city’s faith in its own myths.
- The Velvet Parade: A crowd-control scenario that tests the Velvet Warden’s social powers against a charismatic antagonist who can shape a crowd’s will with a well-timed rumor.

For GM-friendly play, these seeds include recommended scenes, antagonist notes, and tips for weaving Fate into the narrative in a way that never feels forced.

### How it integrates with existing campaigns

Fortune Row slides into existing campaigns without requiring a full rewrite. It’s the sort of expansion you can drop into a mid-campaign arc to recalibrate tension, or you can seed at the start of a new arc to set a mood from day one. If your table loves the core system’s relationship between masks and mysteries, you’ll appreciate how Fortune Row adds nuance without diluting identity. It’s a gentle nudge toward the “what if” questions that keep players involved rather than letting them coast on memory alone.

### The value proposition: is Fortune Row worth it?

If you’re already invested in City of Mist and your group enjoys mood-driven investigations, Fortune Row is a solid buy. It doesn’t crush the price point with a mountain of extra rules; instead, it adds a handful of flavorful options that can elevate a session’s vibe and remind players that the city is a living, breathing character, not just a backdrop for a chase sequence. If your table tends to lean toward long, sprawling campaigns, the Fate mechanics can offer meaningful choices that balance risk with narrative payoff. If your group defaults toward one-shot sessions with a clock that ticks too loudly, Fortune Row might feel a little heavier to fit into a single night—but that’s not a bad thing if you plan your pacing with intention.

For players who adore new toys, the archetypes and Fate features are a true delight. For GMs who relish the craft of scene-building, the seeds and cards offer fresh tools that aren’t gimmicks but legitimate methods to shape a story. In either case, Fortune Row respects the core system’s signature vibe: detective noir meets mythic comic book energy, with a dollop of neon and a dash of metaphor.

## Artwork, layout, and presentation notes

Fortune Row’s design language remains faithful to City of Mist’s atmospheric aesthetic. The printing quality is sturdy and the page layout is friendly to both nibbling on coffee and scribbling notes during a late session. The new cards and sheets are well-thought-out: they will lay flat on a table, which is not something you can always count on with glossy game aids. The typography balances readability with a sense of style that fits the game’s blend of noir grit and mythic grandiosity. If you’ve ever considered running a table where mood and metaphor carry as much weight as dice rolls, Fortune Row will feel like a welcome invitation rather than a chore.

Visuals in the expansion reinforce the mood of “fortune’s edge” rather than simply “neon city.” Expect a few pieces that capture a moment where fate seems to hiccup—an alleyway like a stage curtain, a cryptic poster that hints at a larger conspiracy, and a map whose lines rearrange themselves when looked at from a certain angle. These touches aren’t essential to gameplay, but they contribute to a richer, more immersive session.

## Final thoughts and recommendations

If you’re new to City of Mist, Fortune Row isn’t a hard entry point, but it’s a strong invitation to explore the game’s deeper rhythm. If you’re a veteran who has exhausted a handful of core mysteries and wants to reinject curiosity into your table, Fortune Row provides a well-crafted set of tools that will spark fresh investigations without disrupting the core’s identity. The archetypes offer new angles for roleplay without forcing players into a particular meta; the Fate mechanics reward story choices with meaningful consequences, not just better numbers.

That said, Fortune Row isn’t a must-buy for every table. If your group thrives on high-octane combat or you’re running a campaign where downtime is a scarce resource, you might prefer to wait for a season when you’re ready to slow down and savor narrative texture. If, however, you’re in the camp that loves urban legend vibes, mood-heavy sessions, and the sense that destiny is a character at the table, Fortune Row is a worthy addition to your City of Mist bookshelf.

## Final verdict

Fortune Row is a thoughtful, mood-forward expansion that respects the core game’s DNA while offering new avenues to explore luck, memory, and social influence in the city’s neon glow. It’s not a radical overhaul; it’s a welcomed extension that gives GMs and players more precise levers to pull when they want to tilt the odds toward a story’s emotional core. If you’re chasing a little more artistry in your sessions and you enjoy micro-narratives that hinge on choices rather than brute force, Fortune Row earns its keep at your table.

If you want my blunt bottom line: buy Fortune Row if you crave more mood, more memory, and more chance-chasing moments. If you’re uncertain, consider borrowing a copy from a friend for a weekend test run—you’ll know within a night whether the new dice are your kind of chaos.
