## Introduction
If you’ve spent any time in the wilds of a pawn shop, a convention hall, or the occasional retro game cave, you’ve probably seen The Saboteur on a shelf somewhere. It’s one of those titles that looks like a crowd-pleasing stunt: a World War II setting, a hero who talks in snappy one-liners, and a color palette that basically screams “look at me, I’m stylish.” Released for the PlayStation 3, The Saboteur is a bold blend of open-world action, stealth, driving mayhem, and a surprisingly earnest love letter to noir cinematography. It’s also a game where the color comes in like a secret spice—black-and-white Paris, punctuated by splashes of color when you do something dramatic. And yes, in the spirit of true Geeknite fashion, we’re going to judge it not just by how loud the explosions are, but by how well the game wears its lanyard of clichés and actually makes you smile while you play.

This post is about a PS3 copy in Sehr guter Zustand—OVP CIB, which translates to “very good condition, original packaging, complete in box” for non-German readers. If you’re a collector, you’ll know the drill: a disc that doesn’t mind the occasional coffee spill, a case that still snaps shut like a vintage camera, and a manual that survived the long voyage from the factory to your shelf. If you’re a player who cares about story, style, and a surprisingly satisfying blend of gunplay and parkour, this review also has you covered. And yes, the line between “collector’s curiosity” and “hooked-on-playtime” is thin enough to sprint across with a well-timed roll.

## Visuals: Paris in Black, with Colorful Splashes
The Saboteur’s main visual gimmick is the black-and-white aesthetic that flips to color whenever your protagonist, Sean Devlin, does something bold enough to shake the city out of the Nazis’ pallor. It’s a clever metaphor—like a crime caper filmed by someone who discovered a vintage film stock and decided to punch the color channel with a paint roller. In practice, the effect is gorgeous and a little bit ridiculous, in the best possible way. The city of Paris in 1940s Europe is captured with a mix of photorealistic backdrops and stylized nightlife. The neon signs glow, the gunfights erupt with cinematic flair, and the little details—the license plates on streetcars, the silhouettes in windows, the way smoke curls from a distant chimneys—make the open world feel alive, even when the sun refuses to shine.

For a PS3-era game (and a title that’s now a few iterations old in the eyes of modern hardware), the textures hold up decently. There’s enough detail in the cobbled streets and apartment facades to keep you in the moment, and the color bursts land at just the right moments to remind you you’re playing a game with a sense of humor about itself. It’s not a pristine visual showcase like a current-gen blockbuster, but the art direction hits its mark with swagger. The soundtrack compliments this by underscoring dramatic chase sequences and stealthy infiltrations with a stylish, almost jazz-meets-film-noir vibe that makes you want a cigarette and a trench coat, even if you don’t smoke and you’re famously allergic to both trench coats and cigars. If you’re after a polished, ultra-realistic look, you may be left wanting, but if you want a world that feels alive and cinematic with a dash of color-blasting whimsy, The Saboteur delivers.

## Gameplay: An Oddly Satisfying Sauce of Stealth, Parkour, and Car Chaos
The Saboteur borrows from a handful of genres and blends them into a recipe that should taste terrible but somehow tastes great. It starts as an open-world action-adventure with light stealth, then tosses in rooftop chases, car-like mayhem, and a leveling system that rewards stylish kills and creative takedowns. Sean Devlin isn’t a perfect hero—he’s a swaggering street-smart driver who occasionally trips over his own bravado—but that rough-edged charm is part of the appeal.

### Movement and Parkour
Sean’s movement is a blend of street-level parkour and mobility-based combat. You’ll sprint, climb, slide, and vault across rooftops, using quick-time events and contextual prompts to navigate the environment. It’s not the most precise system on the market, but when you chain a few moves together—vaulting a fence, swinging down a streetlamp, then landing perfectly in cover—the result feels undeniably stylish. The game rewards speed and daring, which is a nice change from the stagnant “wait for the guard to turn away” stealth routines you’ll find in other titles. There are moments where the parkour loop feels a little floaty, but that’s part of its character. You’re not playing a simulation; you’re playing a caper—and capers are supposed to feel a touch outrageous.

### Stealth and Takeouts
Stealth in The Saboteur is functional, not revolutionary. You’ll thread through German patrols and pick off enemies with silenced weapons or non-lethal takedowns when you want to maintain a low profile. The game urges you to be surgical when possible, but if you want to go loud, you can—this is an open world after all. The balance between stealth and chaos is surprisingly forgiving for a game that aims to hit a cinematic, action-packed tone. The cover system works well enough, though the AI sometimes gives you a free pass if you’re a little too showy with your maneuvers. Still, the design encourages you to blend stealth with stylish takedowns rather than simply sprinting into every encounter with a rocket launcher strapped to your back (though there’s nothing wrong with that approach if you’re into it).

### Combat and Car Chaos
On foot, the melee and ranged systems feel serviceable rather than groundbreaking. You’ll trade punches with German soldiers, slide into cover, and use a handful of weak to moderate special moves. The combat is constructed to emphasize tempo and style over simulation. This suits the game’s noir-aesthetic: every encounter should feel like a scene from a classic detective movie, complete with quips and a sense of improvised improvisation. When it comes to driving, the open-world Parisian streets become playgrounds. Vehicles handle with a weighty, arcade-ish feel that’s perfect for the chaos and car chases the game peppers in. It’s not a sim racer, but if you’re in the mood for a reckless joyride through a color-splashed war-torn city, you’ll find it surprisingly satisfying.

### Progression and Rewards
The Saboteur uses a level-up system tied to actions: stealthy approaches, creative takedowns, and completing side activities award points that unlock new abilities and upgrades. The upgrades aren’t revolutionary, but they do a good job of inviting you to experiment with different playstyles. The game nudges you toward a blend of stealth and spectacle rather than pure brute force, which keeps encounters interesting rather than monotonous. Some players may wish for deeper skill trees or more meaningful choice in how you approach missions, but the core loop remains entertaining and the game's pacing does a decent job of staying engaging across its runtime.

## Story, Setting, and Mood: The Noir-Paris Mash-Up
The Saboteur’s narrative is a love letter to classic film noir and pulpy escapades. Sean Devlin is a rough-around-the-edges anti-hero who drinks too much, flirts with danger, and has a moral compass that occasionally spins in a circle. The plot revolves around the resistance against the occupying forces, with a tone that shifts between serious and cheeky. It’s not a groundbreaking epic in the way AAA campaigns try to be today, but it excels at giving you a reason to care about the city you’re wandering through. The writing leans into witty one-liners and melodramatic setup, which serves as a contrast to the grim atmosphere of occupation Paris. If you’re looking for high-stakes dialogue and philosophical contemplation, you might be disappointed; if you want a charismatic rogue and a city that looks like a living painting, you’ll likely be delighted.

The setting—the streets of Paris under siege—feels meticulously designed. The game’s color mechanic does more than look pretty: it functions as a way to punctuate moments of action, risk, and discovery. When you see a car explode in a flare of color, or a guard’s silhouette glow slightly as he’s about to spot you, the world feels reactive. It’s a small thing, but it helps to immerse you in the moment and sells the mood of a rebellious underdog carving out his path in a city that’s both beautiful and dangerous.

## The 2010 PS3 Experience: Performance, Load Times, and Longevity
On the PS3, The Saboteur runs at a steady pace for the most part, with occasional dips in frame rate during intense chases or crowded firefights. It’s not a technological showcase, but it doesn’t pretend to be. Load times are typical of late-PS3 titles and can feel a bit long if you’re impatient, but the world maintains a sense of cohesion even when the engine pauses to shuffle assets. The controls respond with a satisfying tactile feel: sprint, jump, shoot, and pull off a slick takedown with a few button presses. In terms of longevity, the game’s open world and side activities offer a decent amount of playtime without demanding the sort of endless post-game grind you’ll see in some modern titles. If you’re a completionist with a soft spot for noir vibes, the game’s 20-40 hour window can feel just right.

Since you’re playing a PS3-era game, you’ll also want to consider the hardware’s age. If your copy is in Sehr guter Zustand with OVP CIB, that means you’ve got the original artwork, manual, and insert potential, which is always a nice value add for collectors. The packaging often ages like a fine wine or a vintage action figure—imperfect on the outside but still proudly serving its purpose on the shelf. A clean disc with a pristine case is a joy to behold, especially when you’re paying homage to the era that produced it.

## Sound and Voice Acting: A Snack That Fits the Mood
The soundtrack rides a thin line between period-appropriate and modernly cinematic. You’ll hear noir-inflected tracks that swim through the action without overpowering it. The voice acting fits the tone—gritty, a touch roguish, and never overdone to the point of self-parody. It’s not going to win awards for groundbreaking performances, but it does what it needs to do: it sells Sean Devlin’s swagger and the world’s peril with a confident wink. The sound design, including gunfire and the bustling streets, contributes to an immersive, if slightly popcorn-y, action experience. If you like your war-era stories with a healthy dose of personality, you’ll probably enjoy the way The Saboteur handles its audio beats.

## The Aesthetic Equation: Color as a Catalyst
The central gimmick—turning color on during moments of triumph or action—works remarkably well in terms of pacing. It creates a rhythm: calm, tense, explosion of color, and a feeling of momentum. It’s not just a gimmick; it’s a design choice that keeps you engaged, nudging you to perform more bold moves to unlock the next color burst. While some players may wish for more color buckets or different triggers, the balance you’re given generally feels intentional. The result is a game that doesn’t rely on hyper-realistic violence to deliver its punch; it uses visual artistry to emphasize mood and action. In a sense, it’s the cyberpunk of WWII-era gaming: a historical shell with a splash of futurist attitude.
