### What is dry brushing?
Dry brushing is a technique where you load a brush with a tiny amount of paint, wipe off most of it on a palette or tissue, and then lightly drag the brush across the surface to catch the raised detail. The result is a highlight that reads as light catching on edges and textures, without requiring you to paint delicate lines with a microscopic brush. The medium drybrush is sized to cover more ground than a fine detail brush but not so large that you drown your miniature in a tide pool of paint.

This is not magic; it’s math: a bristle in the right position, a stroke with the right pressure, and a color that’s just a tad lighter than your base. The medium drybrush acts as a bullet train for midtone highlights—the jet ski of edge catching, if you will.

### Why the medium matters
The term medium here refers to the brush head size. It’s big enough to skim across shoulders of a trove of armor and small enough to work on a knight’s gauntlet without smearing the entire figure. If you are new to dry brushing, this is a perfect starting point because you can feel the resistance of the bristles and calibrate pressure. If you’re a seasoned painter with the precision of a laser printer, you might use it to grab midtone textures on larger models or to get a quick desaturated wash look without washing the brush in between.

Okay, the dry brush is the star, but the brush is the vehicle. You want bristles that hold a bit of pigment while releasing a controlled trickle of paint, not a brush that defies gravity and dumps the entire color palette onto your model. The Citadel medium drybrush sits in that sweet spot where controlled texture meets practical speed—and yes, it won’t melt your patience if your technique is still a work in progress.

## Build and Design
### Handling and feel
The medium drybrush features a comfortable handle that fits well in the palm and a ferrule that keeps the bristles sturdy. It isn’t feather-light like a precision brush, nor is it a brick on your desk. It sits in that golden middle ground where you can hold it for a long session without cramping, and you won’t feel like you’re wresting a small sword from a combat veteran every time you re-paint a rivet.

### Brush composition
Citadel’s drybrush line typically uses synthetic bristles designed to mimic the feel of natural hair without the maintenance. The medium head has enough stiffness to scrub across textures but enough softness to avoid gouging. The bristles are arranged to allow a light dusting of paint to stay on the bristles and then release with a controlled tap across the model. This is not a brush for paste-level pigment; it’s for a delicate tactile approach that won’t sprint ahead of your control.

### Comfort and durability
With a few sessions under your belt, you’ll notice whether the brush holds its shape or blooms into a miniature porcupine after a long night. In the case of the Citadel medium drybrush, the ferrule remains stable with regular use. If you’re particularly heavy-handed, you may tilt the bristles away from a crisp edge; otherwise, the brush returns to its original shape after a gentle wash. Maintenance is your friend here: clean after use, wipe with tissue, and store flat or bristle-up to prevent warping.

### Cleaning and maintenance
Warm water, mild soap, and a gentle rinse are your allies. Do not soak the brush for long periods; you don’t want the glue to weaken or the bristles to lose their spring. After cleaning, lay flat to dry. If you notice stray hairs after a month, it’s probably time for a replacement; drybrushes aren’t eternal life, but they can last a surprisingly long time if you treat them well.

## How to Use the Medium Drybrush
### Prep steps
1) Prepare your palette and choose a mid-tone or highlight color.
2) Load the brush lightly and wipe off excess paint on a tissue or paper towel.
3) Test on a piece of scrap plastic or a simple miniature head to calibrate pressure.

### Step-by-step technique
- Hold the brush at a slight angle, almost parallel to the surface, with the bristles nearly touching.
- Use quick, short strokes to drag the paint across raised edges and textured surfaces.
- Focus on the outer edges and exposed corners to simulate light catching on metal or fabric.
- If you overshoot, blend by using a very light feathering stroke in the opposite direction.
- For larger flat areas, use a lighter touch and a shorter stroke to avoid pooling.

This is the bread-and-butter of dry brushing: a motion that feels almost playful until you realize you’re painting the entire figure by feel. The key is consistency: the same pressure, the same angle, and a color that reads as a natural postscript to your base.

### Prep, practice, and patience
If you’re brand-new to the technique, practice on a handful of scrap pieces or spare sprues. The aim is not to create a perfect edge highlight on day one but to get your hands used to the brush’s response. You’ll learn how much paint remains on the bristles after you tap off, how much pressure is required to catch raised detail without filling it in, and how to tilt your wrist so you don’t accidentally paint a face with a suit of armor.

### Common mistakes
- Dipping too deep into the paint and trying to apply a heavy wash with a dry brush; you’ll end up with a smeared mess rather than a highlight.
- Forgetting to wipe the brush between strokes; you’ll create a chalky finish that looks like a sculpture of a ghost wearing armor.
- Painting too soon before the midtone dries; you’ll drag paint around and smear it.

### Quick tips
- Less is more: you want an almost dry brush with a whisper of color.
- Use a light hand; you aren’t erasing the model’s sins, you’re hinting at them.
- Temperature matters: a too-cold environment makes paint dry too slowly; a too-warm one makes the paint too runny.
- Test on scrap first, then test again on a hidden seam so your reveal isn't a catastrophe.

## Practical Scenarios
### Terrain highlights
Terrain pieces often benefit from a strong dry brush across stone textures. The medium brush covers more area with fewer passes, letting you catch cracks, rivets, and the edges of bricks. It’s the shortcut to “this looks like stone and not a blob of brown” without dedicating hours to micro-highlights.

### Miniatures in armor
On metal armor or segmented plates, the dry brush can emphasize the edges and create a sense of wear without requiring you to paint all the small lines individually. It’s particularly effective for volume-rich surfaces like torsos and shield rims where edge lighting helps the silhouette pop on the tabletop.

### Reptile scales and fur
For textured surfaces such as scales or fur, the brush can pick up the high points and keep the recessed areas darker, creating depth with minimal effort. With practice, you’ll notice your reptiles and beasts gain a tactile feel that begs to be petted—if your friends didn’t warn you about your hobby desk becoming a petting zoo.

### Wood textures and dioramas
Wood grain responds to a light touch that follows the direction of the grain. The medium drybrush is perfect for catching raised ridges that mimic wood grain without smudging into mush. In dioramas, you’ll appreciate its speed when you need a top-level highlight across a landscape or a base layer of weathered tannins on planks.

### Cleaning up a mistake
If you over-apply, you can use a dry brush with the base color to blend, or go back to a slightly darker shade to patch the area, then re-drybrush as needed. The key is to approach it like a watercolor, not a sledgehammer—you want soft edges and controlled transitions, not a hard line that shouts “I overdid it.”

## Maintenance and care
### Cleaning
Use warm water and mild soap; gently rinse the bristles until no paint remains. Avoid hot water that could distort the bristles; avoid stiff scrubbing that could bend the brush out of shape. A quick rinse after each session extends its life considerably.

### Storage
Store flat or bristle-up to retain shape. A tool rack in the hobby room is ideal for keeping bristles from getting crushed by heavy paints. Keeping the brush clean and out of direct sunlight helps the bristles stay springy and ready.

### Replacement
Drybrushes wear out faster when you are heavy-handed; if bristles begin to shed, it may be time to replace. If you notice the brush no longer releases paint smoothly, don’t try to coax a miracle—retire it and grab the next generation of dry-brushing glory.
