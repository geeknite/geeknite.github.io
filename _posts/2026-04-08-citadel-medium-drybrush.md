## Overview
Dry brushing is one of those techniques that sounds fancy until you realize it is mostly you smearing a nearly dry brush across a model to catch the raised edges. The Citadel Medium Drybrush is designed to be the Swiss Army Knife of this technique. It's not the cheapest or the most luxurious thing in your brush rack, but it sits right at that sweet spot where you can actually do something useful without turning your fingernails into a bad impression of a blue whale.

### What is dry brushing?
Dry brushing is a method where you load a brush with a tiny amount of paint, wipe off most of it on a pallet or tissue, and then lightly drag the brush across the surface to catch the raised detail. The result is a highlight that reads as light catching on edges and textures, without requiring you to paint delicate lines with a tiny brush. The medium drybrush is sized to cover more ground than a fine detail brush but not so large that you drown your miniature in a tide pool of paint.

### Why the medium matters
The term medium here refers to the brush head size. It’s big enough to skim across shoulders of a trove of armor and small enough to work on a knight’s gauntlet without smearing the entire figure. If you are new to dry brushing, this is a perfect starting point because you can feel the resistance of the bristles and calibrate pressure. If you are a seasoned painter with the precision of a laser printer, you might use it to grab those midtone textures on larger models or to get a quick desaturated wash look without washing the brush in between.

Okay, while dry brushing is the star, the brush is the vehicle. You want bristles that hold a bit of pigment while releasing a controlled trickle of paint, not a brush that defies gravity and dumps the entire color palette onto your model.

## Build and Design
### Handling and feel
The medium drybrush features a comfortable handle that fits well in the palm and a ferrule that keeps the bristles sturdy. It’s not feather-light like a precision brush, nor is it a brick on your desk. It sits in that sweet middle ground where you can hold it for a long session without cramping.

### Brush composition
Citadel’s drybrush line typically uses synthetic bristles designed to mimic the feel of natural hair without the maintenance. The medium head has enough stiffness to scrub across textures but enough softness to avoid gouging. The bristles are arranged to allow a light dusting of paint to stay on the bristles and then release with a controlled tap across the model. This is not a brush for paste-level pigment; it’s for a delicate tactile approach.

### Comfort and durability
After a few sessions, you’ll notice whether the brush holds its shape or starts to bloom into a miniature porcupine. In the case of the Citadel medium drybrush, the ferrule retains its form with reasonable use. If you’re particularly heavy-handed, you may tip the bristles away from a perfect edge; otherwise, the brush will return to its original shape after a gentle wash. A word on maintenance: clean after use, wipe with tissue, and store flat or bristle-up to prevent warping.

### Cleaning and maintenance
Warm water, mild soap, and a gentle rinse are your friends here. Do not soak the brush for long periods; you don’t want the glue to weaken or the bristles to lose their spring. After cleaning, lay flat to dry. If you notice stray hairs after a month, it’s probably time for a replacement; drybrushes are not eternal life, but they can last a surprisingly long time if you treat them well.

## How to Use the Medium Drybrush
### Prep steps
1) Prepare your palette and choose a mid-tone or highlight color. 2) Load the brush lightly and wipe off excess paint on a tissue or paper towel. 3) Test on a piece of scrap plastic or a simple miniature head to calibrate pressure.

### Step-by-step technique
- Hold the brush at a slight angle, almost parallel to the surface, with the bristles nearly touching.
- Use quick, short strokes to drag the paint across raised edges and textured surfaces.
- Focus on the outer edges and exposed corners to simulate light catching on metal or fabric.
- If you overshoot, blend by using a very light feathering stroke in the opposite direction.
- For larger flat areas, use a lighter touch and a shorter stroke to avoid pooling.

### Common mistakes
- Dipping too deep into the paint and trying to apply a heavy wash with a dry brush; you’ll end up with a smeared mess rather than a highlight.
- Forgetting to wipe the brush between strokes; you’ll create a chalky finish that looks like a sculpture of a ghost wearing armor.
- Painting too soon before the midtone dries; you’ll drag paint around and smear it.

### Quick tips
- Less is more: you want an almost dry brush with a whisper of color.
- Use a light hand; you aren’t erasing the model’s sins, you’re hinting at them.
- Temperature matters: a too-cold environment makes paint dry too slowly; a too-warm one makes the paint too runny.

## Practical Scenarios
### Terrain highlights
Terrain pieces often benefit from a strong dry brush across stone textures. The medium brush covers more area with fewer passes, letting you catch cracks, rivets, and the edges of bricks.

### Miniatures in armor
On metal armor or segmented plates, the dry brush can emphasize the edges and create a sense of wear without requiring you to paint all the small lines individually.

### Reptile scales and fur
For textured surfaces such as scales or fur, the brush can pick up the high points and keep the recessed areas darker, creating depth with minimal effort.

### Large diorama features
In dioramas, you might want to apply a top-level highlight quickly to simulate light bouncing off large surfaces. The medium dry brush can cover the broad areas while leaving shadows intact.

### Cleaning up a mistake
If you over-apply, you can use a dry brush with the base color to blend, or go back to a slightly darker shade to patch the area, then re-drybrush as needed.

## Maintenance and care
### Cleaning
Use warm water and mild soap; gently rinse the bristles until no paint remains. Avoid hot water that could distort the bristles; avoid stiff scrubbing that could bend the brush out of shape.

### Storage
Store flat or bristle-up to retain shape. A tool rack in the hobby room is ideal for keeping bristles from getting crushed by heavy paints.

### Replacement
Drybrushes wear out faster when you are heavy-handed; if bristles begin to shed, it may be time to replace.
