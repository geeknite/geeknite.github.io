### How to Play: Subtle Differences to the UNO Formula

UNO’s skeleton remains intact: match color or number, play Wilds, draw cards, and avoid being the player with a mountain of cards when UNO is yelled. The BTS Edition injects a handful of flavor-forward twists that feel like a tasteful remix rather than a whole new genre.

- BTS Moment cards: When played, these trigger a short, celebratory moment where players pose or shout a catchphrase. It adds flavor and drama, but it can slow the tempo if your group loves theatricality more than turnover—perfect for a party-night vibe, not a brisk, no-nonsense session.
- ARMY Card: Think of this as a wild card with karaoke potential. When played, the next player must either sing a line of a BTS song or perform a quick action chosen by the table. It’s not a full karaoke edition, but the social pressure can be hilariously delightful. Boundaries are wise here: set expectations about singing in front of friends who aren’t ready for their inner pop star to emerge.
- Mic Drop: The mythical climactic move. When played, the table pauses as the player pretends to drop a mic, followed by a chorus of roars and applause. It’s fun—until someone uses it back-to-back and you’re basically running a stadium vibe in a small apartment.
- Visual cues: Some cards feature BTS silhouettes, lyric cues, or stage imagery. These do not alter the rules but offer constant fan-spotting moments and occasional distraction for non-fans who are polite about their curiosity.

What this means on the table is a lot of giggles, perfectly timed mic drops, and friendly debates over whether a lyric cue qualifies as a valid trigger for a rule. For many groups, this introduces a social layer that can be as entertaining as the card mechanics themselves—turning color-matching into a mini-performance worth its own encore.
