## What is Sushi Go! and what’s special about the Sealed edition?
Sushi Go! is a fast-playing, set-collection card game designed for 2-5 players. The core mechanics revolve around picking one card from your hand and passing the rest to the next player, all while trying to assemble the best combination of sushi cards to score points. The scoring is delightfully modular: Nigiri, Maki, Sashimi, Tempura, Dumplings, and Wasabi cards each contribute to points in different ways, and the strategic art lies in predicting which cards your neighbors will collect and which cards you’re comfortable letting slide by.

The Sealed edition of Sushi Go! adds a twist that brings a new layer of risk management. Instead of drafting from a single shared pool that’s replenished with each pass, players draw from sealed packets or decks that are opened at specific intervals and then passed around. The exact implementation can vary by publisher and regional edition, but the essential feel remains: you can’t rely on a long-term memory of what others have seen. You’re playing with partial information, a few surprise reveals, and a pacing rhythm that invites quick, punchy rounds rather than marathon planning sessions. If you love the taste of the original but crave a little more doom-scrolling of fortune (and not the actual doom of pantry doom, just the dramatic doom of “Did they just take my Wasabi?”), you’ll likely find the sealed variant surprisingly compelling.

This review treats the Sealed edition as a distinct flavor rather than a mere packaging upgrade. The core charm of Sushi Go!—cute art, brisk rounds, meaningful choices—remains intact. The sealed mechanics tilt the balance toward adaptability and observation: you must read the table, but you also must accept that some cards will slip away like a California roll sliding off a plate if you’re not careful.

## Setup and first impressions
- Shuffle the sealed decks (as instructed by your edition) and lay out the initial pool. In Sealed Sushi Go!, you’ll probably see a handful of cards face-down or face-up as per the variant you own.
- Each player receives a hand of cards from a sealed reveal and selects one card to keep, then passes the rest to the left or right depending on the round of the draft. In the sealed version, some rounds may require you to reveal cards before you pass, adding a small pebble of pressure to your decision.
- The game then proceeds with each player taking turns picking and passing, until all cards are claimed and scored. The field resembles a tiny conveyor belt of edible dreams, except you’re not actually allowed to eat any of them—unless you’re playing with a very generous referee.

If you want an ornamental comparison: classic Sushi Go! feels like a quick sushi restaurant rush, while Sealed adds the flavor of a mystery-meets-rotation game where you don’t know what’s coming next until it’s almost too late to react. It’s still approachable for families and groups with varied gaming experience, though the sealed twist might require a few extra explanations for newbies.

## How to play (the quick guide)
- Each round, you are dealt a hand of cards from your sealed deck. You look at your hand, pick one card to keep, and pass the rest in some direction. The direction alternates round to round, which encourages you to track what others might be collecting—and to anticipate what you’ll be offered next.
- You don’t count points immediately; you collect cards across a handful of mini-rounds, then tally scores using the standard Sushi Go! scoring rules. The scoring categories include: Nigiri (points based on type), Sashimi (three of a kind for a fixed score), Tempura (pairs for points), Dumplings (points scale with quantity), and Wasabi (triples an embedded Nigiri card when placed on it).
- In Sealed, some rounds will reveal additional scoring opportunities or hidden bonuses tied to sealed-draw mechanics. These tweaks can reward risk-taking and careful memory, or punish it with a punishing reveal that flips a plan on its head.

The pace is fast. You’ll usually see a full game in 15-25 minutes, depending on player count and how chatty your group gets about their “future dragon roll” puns. That’s classic Sushi Go! energy: light enough to teach to non-gamers, deep enough to entertain hobbyists, and compact enough to bring to a kitchen table, a classroom, or a coffee shop without feeling like a full-blown escape room.

## Scoring overview
- Nigiri: Small cards give you base points; Wasabi multiplies the value of the next Nigiri you play with that Wasabi card. The maximum effect of Wasabi is often a tongue-in-cheek test of your arithmetic skills under mild pressure.
- Maki: Those little green rolls count toward a separate mini-game of which player collects the most. The winner gets a bonus, which can tip the final tally in their favor.
- Sashimi: Three cards equals a fixed bonus; two cards won’t do anything unless paired with the right other combos.
- Tempura: A pair is worth a modest point value; miss the pair and you’re left with nothing, which is heartbreak but not tragedy.
- Dumplings: A little avalanche of points scales based on how many you have. The more you accumulate, the bigger the boost—until the table collectively sighs because you’ve polka-dotted the entire table with dumplings.
- Wasabi: Placing Nigiri on Wasabi amplifies the next Nigiri’s value. The challenge is not to get too fixated on Wasabi and forget to grab a Tempura or Sashimi when the opportunity arises.

The sealed variant can tweak these values by altering how often you see certain card types, so you’ll need to adjust your expectations slightly from a regular Sushi Go! match. The math remains approachable: you can typically compute a round’s expected value quickly enough to adjust your plan on the fly, which keeps the mental gymnastics accessible for players of all ages.

## Strategy and tips (for the brave and the hungry)
- Read the table, not just your own hand. Sushi Go! is as much about predicting others as it is about optimizing your own cards. In Sealed, where certain information is hidden, this becomes even more critical. You’ll want to watch which card types are disappearing first and which players seem to be chasing specific combos.
- Balance immediate gratification with long-term goals. Tempura and Sashimi are tempting because they’re big points if you hit the right numbers, but chasing them at the expense of early Nigiri or Maki can leave you behind in the tail end of scoring. The sealed twist mildly punishes overcommitment to a single strategy and rewards flexible planning.
- Don’t neglect Wasabi. It’s easy to overlook the Wasabi-Nigiri combo when you’re chasing high-value cards, but the multiplier can swing results dramatically if you hit the right sequence.
- Mind the pass directions. Alternating pass directions in Sushi Go! is a staple, but in Sealed you might encounter rounds where the direction is different or where you pass multiple cards at once. Keep track of fences around your decision space so you’re not accidentally helping a rival complete their queen of sushi without realizing it.
- Build a mental “menu” of rounds. Some rounds feel like a steady drumbeat of scoring opportunities; others feel like a sprint to see who can assemble three duplicates or a perfect Sashimi run. Plan for bursts of points, not just consistent, small gains.

## The sealed twist: what it changes, what it preserves
- What works: The Sealed edition preserves the brisk pace and charm of the original while introducing uncertainty that rewards adaptability. The hidden/dealt-sealed cards create a microdrama: you’re often surprised by what’s available, which can be both exciting and nerve-wracking in a good way.
- What to expect: You’ll need to adjust your expectations toward more on-the-fly calculation and fewer long-term forecasts. Because you can’t always predict what will come next, you’ll become more reactive and more forgiving of bad luck—two traits that are surprisingly compatible with party-style gaming.
- Social dynamics: Sealed can enhance the social aspect of the game because players are reacting to similar unknowns. It’s a nice fit for mixed groups with casual players who enjoy a little friendly banter and micro-competition.
