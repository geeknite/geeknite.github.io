The design is readable, elegant, and deceptively clever: you balance immediate gains against future possibilities, and you do it with a constant fear of being last to draft the best combo.

## How to play: a quick refresher and teaching notes

### Setup

- Shuffle the sushi cards and deal a hand to each player.
- Place the remaining deck face down as a draw pile.
- Decide the number of rounds based on players and appetite for speed. Typically three rounds keep the pace brisk while letting you experiment with card combinations.
- Each player keeps a personal score sheet or uses a reference card to track scoring rules.

### The core mechanic: drafting and passing

- On your turn, choose one card from your hand to keep and pass the rest to the left.
- Once everyone has picked, reveal all kept cards simultaneously and resolve the scoring for that round.
- The next round starts with the new hands you receive from the passing process.
- The dynamic is simple for new players but can become delightfully intricate as you chase multi-card combos and fear being the last to draft a crucial piece.

### Scoring: why it matters to plan a few steps ahead

- Nigiri cards award points: the bigger the nigiri, the sweeter the payoff, often depending on accompanying wasabi cards.
- Maki cards give points to the player with the longest stack, creating a nice tension with potential blocks from opponents.
- Dumplings scale with the number you collect, providing a ladder of exponential payoff if you can swing a few rounds early.
- Tempura and Sashimi reward sets: collect the right volumes, and you lock in big chunks of points.
- Puddings come in as a tiebreaker or endgame bonus that punishes plan-less drafting. They aren’t always the first priority but can swing the final tally dramatically.

### Teaching tips for mixed audiences

- Explain one concept at a time: drafting is the core mechanic; scoring is an afterthought; teach by example with a short mock round.
- Use a quick reference card and a simple example board to show how many points you get for each pattern.
- Emphasize that Sushi Go! is a game of information and timing: you know some cards will be valuable, but you don’t know when they will appear in the deck again.
