## Authenticity, Grading, and Acquisition Guidance
### Authenticity markers to spot trouble
If you are buying or trading, be mindful of the usual suspects: misprints, counterfeit foil, dodgy borders, and altered reverses. Typical indicators of authenticity include a consistent holo pattern across the surface, clean borders with minimal printer bleed, and a faithful reproduction of the card’s font and symbol placement. In the SV4a family, the ex branding should be clear but not obtrusive, and the set code should appear in the usual corner with the correct typography. Always compare to high-resolution official imagery when possible and seek a trusted grading service if you want the badge of legitimacy in your binder.

### Grading implications for shiny variants
Shiny cards that are heavily holo often score high in surface and overall aesthetics by grading services. However, grading shiny variants can present some challenges: the surface reflection can complicate the grader's ability to assess centering and edge wear, and the holo surface can create glare in the graders’ photos. If you plan to submit to PSA, BGS, or similar services, be prepared for nuanced evaluation—cosmetic condition matters a lot, and the fragility of holographic layers can influence the final grade. Always ensure you preserve the surface by avoiding fingerprints, oils, and rough handling that could mar the holo finish.

### How to authenticate before you buy
- Check the edge and corner wear under good lighting; the corners on a holo card should be crisp, not rounded or nicked.
- Look for consistent holo distribution across the surface; unusual patches can signal reprinting or tampering.
- Compare the font and placement of the set code to confirmed images from credible sources; even small deviations can be meaningful.
- If buying sealed product, verify the booster box imprint, the seal integrity, and the wrapper colorways that are typical for the region and year of release.
