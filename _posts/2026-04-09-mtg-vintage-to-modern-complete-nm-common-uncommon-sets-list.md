## The NM standard and practicalities of bulk buying
Near mint is a relative term in MTG bulk. In practice, NM for a common or uncommon usually means the card is flat with no wrinkles, rounded corners, or severe scratches. Some sellers grade on the looser side; some on the stricter. A few let the collector’s heart do the grading, and the rest go for a cost-per-card approach. Here’s a pragmatic checklist you can use while evaluating a listing like this:

- Inspect corners and edges: Are there whitening edges? Are the corners perfectly sharp, or is there minor rounding that some would call “near mint minus”?
- Surface checks: Are there heavy scratches on the holo foil or caused by card sleeves rubbing during transit? The layman’s card in a binder is a different beast than a tournament-grade card.
- Creases and gouges: Any bends that could hamper playability? If a stack of commons has a crease that would knock it out of a binder, you’re not getting “NM” anymore.
- Print variations: Some sets have multiple print runs and border styles; you might want to verify you’re getting the version you prefer.

This is where the balance of a four-dollar shipping charge becomes interesting: the seller’s packing quality and the care taken to preserve NM status is often reflected not in price but in handling. A well-packed bulk lot that arrives with the cards in map-perfect condition can be worth more than the sum of its parts. A poorly packed stack that looks like it survived a tumble through a thunderstorm could lead to a buyer circling back with a less-than-NM grievance.

### Modern era (from Ravnica to today)
