## Maintenance and Care
High carbon steel requires basic care to prevent corrosion and maintain edge retention. Here’s what we recommend after unboxing and initial inspection:
- Clean and dry: Wipe the blade with a soft cloth, then ensure it’s completely dry before storage.
- Oil the blade: Apply a light coat of mineral oil or a specialized blade oil to deter rust. Wipe off any excess.
- Inspect the edge: Check for any burrs or micro-chips after practice. If present, lightly strop with a fine stone or leather strop to realign the edge.
- Storage: Keep in a dry place. If you store it in a sheath for a long time, ensure the blade is free of moisture and lightly oiled to prevent rust pockets.

Because this blade is marketed as a practical piece rather than a purely decorative one, standard maintenance routines apply. If you’re a cosplay enthusiast, you’ll probably enjoy maintaining the blade as part of your weekly ritual. If you’re a casual user, think of maintenance as a small ongoing investment that pays off in longer blade life and a better feel during practice.

Note: Avoid aggressive or heavy-duty use with hard materials. This blade is designed for learning, display, and light cutting practice; pushing it into heavy functional territory can cause quicker wear and tear than you’d expect from a budget blade. If you’re chasing industrial-grade performance, then you’ll need to look at higher-end blades with dedicated heat treatment specifications and more documented QC practices.

## Safety and Handling
Safety first, always. A wakizashi is a sharp tool, even if it’s a budget piece. Here are some practical safety reminders:
- Always wear appropriate eye protection and cut-resistant gloves when practicing.
- Practice with a partner or in a controlled space. Using a blade like this in a crowded room is a terrible idea that will result in a dramatic YouTube fail montage at best.
- Check the blade for micro-chips or burrs before use. A small burr can turn into a nasty cut if your grip shifts mid-swing.
- Use a practice target that won’t rebound the blade into you or your surroundings. Foam or synthetic targets are ideal for beginners.

If you’re new to swords, consider taking a basic sword safety class or watching a few reputable tutorial videos before swinging anything sharper than a kitchen knife. And yes, cosplay fights are fun, but they aren’t dodgeball with a cardboard cutout sword. Treat it with respect and keep it away from kids and pets.
