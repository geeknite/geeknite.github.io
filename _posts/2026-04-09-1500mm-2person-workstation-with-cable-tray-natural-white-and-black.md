## Assembly and Setup Experience

The box comes with two sets of hardware, clearly labeled for each seat. The assembly is straightforward but expects two people to be present. Here’s a typical run-through:

- Unbox and lay out all components. Check against the included parts list.
- Attach the legs to the main beam. The black frame slides into place with a satisfying click, and it’s best done by two people to keep things aligned.
- Secure the desktop panels to the frame. If you’re particular about alignment, use a small level and measure diagonals to confirm true squareness.
- Install the cable tray along the underside. Depending on your hardware, you may need to slightly adjust the tray’s position to ensure a comfortable clearance for knees.
- Route cables, tidy up, and test the power sources and monitors. If you’ve got two monitors, you’ll appreciate how the 1500mm length accommodates screen spacing without crowding.

Estimated assembly time: 60–90 minutes with two people and a mild sense of humor. If you’re solo, allocate closer to 120 minutes and plan for a snack break mid-assembly. The instructions are clear enough, but you’ll benefit from a partner who can hold panels in place while you tighten the bolts.

### Practical Tips During Assembly
- Keep track of small parts by using a tray or the packaging’s pocket organization. Losing a bolt is a tragedy. A minor tragedy, but still a tragedy.
- Pre-thread a few bolts before tightening fully. It speeds up alignment and reduces cross-threading.
- Don’t overtighten. The aluminum and steel parts resist warping, but you don’t want to crack a panel or strip a screw. A medium hand at torque is enough.
- Have a set of cable ties ready. While the tray helps, you’ll likely want to secure a few strands above and below for a clean look.

## Cable Management in the Real World

Cable chaos is a real productivity killer. It’s not just about looks; it’s about reducing friction. When you’re two people sharing a desk, a well-thought-out cable system can make the difference between collaborative flow and constant cable tug-of-war.

The included tray does a good job of corralling the majority of the mess, but you’ll still want to plan your device placement. Consider assigning a “per-seat” power strip or a shared hub so you don’t end up with one person monopolizing outlets for a projector and the other fighting for a laptop charger.

For those who feel the cables are plotting against you, a simple approach works: route power bricks to one side, monitor cables to the other, and keep data cables to a separate channel. It’s almost zen-like when you can reach for a keyboard or a USB-C dongle without performing a shimmy between two under-desk towers of cable trees.
