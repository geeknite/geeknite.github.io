## What is Codenames, anyway?

Codenames is, at its essence, a word game dressed in spy paraphernalia. Two teams race to guess their confidential agents based on one-word clues given by their spymasters. The catch: the clue must connect multiple word cards on the table, but some cards belong to the opposing team, some are neutrals, and one is the assassin. The team that uncovers all their agents first wins. It sounds simple, and it is—until someone yells, "One more turn!" and suddenly your conspiracy theory that the word father and the word father-in-law should be synonyms becomes a full-blown haiku about shared experiences with family.

CGE's Complete Set makes the hurdle a little higher by offering a bundled experience: you get the core game, plus two of its most popular sidekicks (the Pictures version, which replaces word cards with pictures, creating an image-based clue puzzle; and Duet, which transforms the chaos into a cooperative two-player firefight where you and your partner are a crime-fighting duo). Some bundles also come with a small expansion pack or extra card sets. If you're new to Codenames, the base game is enough to understand the core mechanics; the rest are optional adrenaline shots.

The design credit goes to Vlaada Chvátil, a designer who seems to log more brain cycles on puzzles than most people log hours at their day jobs. He’s known for games that scale from "fun party game" to "you need a nap after this," and Codenames sits somewhere in the middle: approachable enough that your aunt can play, yet rich enough to fuel arguments about word associations for weeks.

## Start with the basics: how to play (base rules in four minutes)

The core mechanic is elegant in its simplicity:
- Two teams, split decision: spymaster and field operatives for each side.
- A 5x5 grid of 25 word cards lies on the table.
- Spymasters take turns giving one-word clues plus a number, indicating how many cards on the grid relate to the clue. The catch: you want to connect as many of your team’s cards as possible, but you must avoid accidentally revealing the opposing team’s cards, neutrals, or the killer assassin.
- Guessers pick cards one by one, guided by the clue. They can stop early or continue, but one wrong pick ends the turn. If they uncover all their agents first, they win. If they hit the assassin, the game ends in a grossly anticlimactic fashion for that team.

This is where the party vibe shines: there’s room for storytelling, misdirection, and high-fives when the clue lands perfectly and the table erupts in a chorus of delighted groans when a misread clue sends the wrong card flying into oblivion.

The spymaster’s craft:
- A good clue should balance breadth and safety. You want to connect multiple of your team’s agents with a single word, but you don’t want to tip off the enemy or land on the assassin. It’s a game of word psychology, pattern spotting, and sometimes pure luck.
- The best clues feel obvious in hindsight but are challenging without a dictionary in hand. If your clue mentions a concept that’s too niche, you’ll have to explain it, and that drains time and energy from everyone else’s turn.

The field operatives’ challenge:
- You have to interpret the clue while keeping an eye on the board’s layout. The moment you lock onto a plausible connection, you start narrating your own mini-story to justify each guess. This is where the social glue appears: friends bonding over shared vocabulary quirks, wiffle-ball debates about synonyms, and the occasional dramatic sigh when the clue almost works but misses by a hair.

The heart of any board game is its physical presence: the card stock, the clarity of the typography, and the overall art direction. In the Complete Set, you’re likely to notice a few things:

## How to play a session with the Complete Set

Here’s a practical night-night schedule you can steal for your own group:
- Setup (5 minutes): Shuffle the appropriate deck(s) from the base game and optional variants. Lay out the 5x5 grid. Decide teams and designate spymasters.
- Warm-up round (5 minutes): The first clue from the first spymaster is often a test drive. It helps everyone calibrate what kinds of connections work for your group.
- Core rounds (15-30 minutes per round): Play proceeds with alternating turns. Track guesses and keep a running tally of who has more successful clues. In a 60-minute session, you can typically fit two to four rounds depending on group size and the length of each explanation.
- Debrief and discuss (5-10 minutes): After a few rounds, talk about the best clues, the funniest misreads, and what you’d change for the next session. This is where the social glue really shines.

## Who should buy the Complete Set?

- Social butterflies and language enthusiasts who love fast-paced banter.
- Groups that want quick sessions with lots of re-reads and story-building.
- Couples or two-player teams who enjoy cooperative modes and tighter teamwork (Duet).

Who might want to start with a leaner package?

- If you have a large social circle with wildly different group dynamics, the Complete Set’s versatility is worth it for long-term value.

## A few constructive comparisons

- vs Taboo: Codenames emphasizes deduction through clues and conversation rather than direct prohibition. It’s friendlier when someone misreads a clue because the table is already geared toward collaborative problem-solving rather than penalizing players.
- vs Decrypto: Decrypto leans into cryptic clues and synonyms in a more competitive head-to-head style. Codenames is faster, lighter, and more social—perfect for mixed groups where the goal is pure levity and quick rounds.
- vs Werwörter and friends: Codenames is approachable and scalable. It generates laughs quickly and remains approachable even for players who hate heavy cognitive tasks.

Pros and cons recap:
- Pros: Quick onboarding, scalable for large groups, multiple modes for variety, strong social experience, solid component quality.
- Cons: Some word pairings can lead to confusing ambiguity; some players may crave more depth beyond the base mechanics.
