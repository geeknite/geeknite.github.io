## What is Cards Against Humanity?
Cards Against Humanity is a party game designed to push the envelope, riff on pop culture, and celebrate the absurdity of adult conversations we pretend never happened. The core mechanic is deception in reverse: players aren’t trying to be the most clever; they’re trying to be the most shocking, the most outrageous, or the most unexpectedly honest about a given setup.

### Core mechanics
- A round starts with one player drawing a Black Card, which contains a prompt or fill-in-the-blank statement.
- Other players select a White Card from their hands that they think best (or worst) completes the prompt.
- The Card Czar (the judge for that round) reviews the submissions and picks the funniest, most fitting answer. The winner gets the Black Card as a point.
- Rounds keep rotating, and the deck never stops being a fountain of ridiculous prompts.

The base game’s humor leans into irreverence and pop-culture references. It’s not a game about moral high ground; it’s a game about who can conjure the most memorable, most ridiculous pairing from a mix of lopsided social expectations and the sometimes terrifying truth that your friends’ sense of humor may be an acquired taste.

## The Good, The Bad, The Ugly
Like any classic comedy, CAH has a spectrum of strengths and weaknesses. Let’s break it down without pulling punches.

### The Good
- Social glue: It brings people together in a way few games do (or at least, it forces people to broadcast their sense of humor loudly).
- Replayability: With thousands of white cards and a flexible black deck, the combinations feel fresh even after dozens of sessions.
- Low barrier to entry: The rules are simple, and anyone can start playing within minutes. You don’t need a PhD in game theory to get it.
- Dense quotable material: Some lines from CAH become memetic, turning into inside jokes for your friend group that last for years.

### The Bad
- Not for every crowd: If the room is especially sensitive or conservative, CAH can be tense rather than fun. It’s not a game about polite conversation.
- Repetition risk: In long sessions, you can end up seeing similar prompts and punchlines. The trick is to shuffle and rotate, both decks and players, to keep it fresh.
- Expansions are where it gets spicy (and expensive): The base game is great, but expansions multiply both the laughs and the price tag. You’ll want to curate expansions that fit your group’s humor, not just buy every kit.

### The Ugly
- Offensive content: Some combos can cross lines for certain folks. It’s a game that tests boundaries, which is exactly what some people want and exactly what others want to avoid.
- Card ownership drama: When a favorite card disappears into someone’s “I’ll borrow this later” pile (and then never returns), a subtle undercurrent of chaos arises. It’s part of CAH’s charm; just be ready for it.

If you want a hint of how the humor lands in the real world, many groups encounter the same balance of “we’re all adults” and “do we really want to quote this at grandma’s dinner?” It’s a live demo of your friend group’s sensibilities under pressure, and that pressure is precisely what yields the best stories afterward.

## Expansions and House Rules
Base CAH is a strong foundation, but the real spice comes from expansions and house rules. Here’s how to think about them.

### Expansions
Expansions add hundreds (or thousands) of new prompts and answers. They’re a great way to tailor the game to your sense of humor and keep things from plateauing. Some expansions lean into pop culture waves (corporate conferences, reality TV buzz, etc.), while others go for niche themes (nerdy science, fantasy tropes, etc.). The trade-off is twofold: more variety, and more words that you’ll get stuck repeating to death.

Tips for choosing expansions:
- Start with a theme that your group already loves. If your crew worships sci-fi, grab a sci-fi expansion. If you’re all in on adulting memes, a modern-life expansion can be gold.
- Avoid “too close to home” prompts for sensitive topics. You can always skip cards that feel too real or personal.
- Rotate expansions seasonally. It keeps the material fresh and gives you a built-in reason to do a CAH night once a month.

### House rules and etiquette
CAH shines brightest when you set the tone with a few house rules:
- No raping the deck for a single player: rotate Card Czar duties. This ensures a broad sense of fairness.
- Timed rounds: give players 30–60 seconds to pick. Time pressure yields more ridiculous results and stops one or two players from overthinking.
- Rule of “punchline first, agenda second”: the best lines don’t always align with the central theme, but they will crush if they hit just right.
- Respect boundaries: you don’t have to play every card. If a prompt or white card makes you uncomfortable, opt out and substitute a friend’s suggestion to redraw.

House rules can be a fantastic addition, but the magic sits in keeping the vibe fun rather than confrontational.

## A Quick Guide to Setup and Variants
CAH isn’t a complicated beast. With the base game, you’re good to go within minutes, but there are a few variants and optional twists worth knowing about. Here’s a quick guide to get you rocketing toward your next game night.

### Setup basics
1. Gather 4–8 players (the more, the merrier), and pick a Card Czar for the first round.
2. Shuffle the White Cards and Black Cards separately.
3. Deal a fixed number of White Cards to each player (for the base game, usually 10 cards).
4. Draw a Black Card, reveal it to the group, and let everyone select a White Card that best responds.
5. The Card Czar picks the winner, who keeps the Black Card as a point.
6. Rotate the Card Czar and reshuffle as needed. Rinse and repeat.

### Variants and house-rule ideas
- Theme-first nights: everyone plays with a common theme in mind (e.g., sci-fi, fantasy, office life).
- Time-limited rounds: rounds have a fixed duration; the fastest and funniest win more points.
- Blind draws: players submit their White Cards face-down; the Czar reveals all at once to minimize influence and bias.
- Cooperative CAH: create a collaborative challenge where players work together to craft the most absurd combined prompt-and-answer line. It’s not exactly the same game, but it scratches a similar itch.
