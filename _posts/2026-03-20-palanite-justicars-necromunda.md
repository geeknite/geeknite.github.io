### Who are the Palanite Justicars?

In the sprawling, synth-lit lanes of Necromunda, order is often a myth whispered by transit guards and the guy who sells shanks as motivational tracts. The Palanite Justicars stand as one of the most curious and entertaining options for players who want to lean into the lawful-neutral vibe without sacrificing punch in a brawl. Think knights-errant meets public defenders, but with body armor that looks like it survived a magnet factory and a helmet that doubles as a headrest for a long night of paperwork.

Lore-wise, the Justicars are an enforcement faction, a specialized cadre tasked with keeping the peace in a district that has learned to confuse law with improvisation. They’re not just muscle; they’re stylized law incarnate, marching to a beat that screams in a mix of sermon and siren. Their ethos is a peculiar blend of strict adherence to code and a willingness to bend it when it suits the greater good—aka Necromunda in a nutshell. If you like your gang flavored with judicial drama, a pinch of religious iconography, and a dash of bureaucratic nightmare, you are in for a treat.

### Kit and Models: What’s in the Box?

The Palanite Justicars kit is designed to look both ceremonial and practical, a look that says I uphold the law and also I own a plasma baton with a very shiny cap. The models come with a handful of plastic options: grizzled veteran enforcement officers, rookies back from “specialist training” with suspiciously clean armor, and a couple of specialists who specialize in things that might require a roll for sanity. The sculpts are crisp enough to pick out insignia in a single pass, but not so sharp that you’ll cut yourself while cleaning them—necromunda models, bless their resin-free souls, can be delightfully forgiving if you don’t try to shave the mold lines at 3 AM.

From a hobby perspective, the palette invites you to go either ceremonial white and gold with a pop of color, or territory-gnawed and grime-washed neutrals that scream hive police. The weapons range from pistols and basic melee gear to heavier options that look like they could stop a reckless gangers’ tachyon capacitor in its tracks. The kit also includes a handful of flip-screens, banners, and small accessories that scream aesthetic over practicality, which is exactly the vibe we geeks adore when building a display table army with a backstory longer than the last season of a soap opera.

### Painting Tips: Getting the Look Right

Palanite Justicars reward clean lines and high-contrast detailing. If you are painting in a typical Necromunda style, you want your uniforms to read as a unit from afar, but reveal complexity up close. A practical approach: base coat in a light, clean gray or off-white, wash with a deep blue-black or brown, and then pick out insignia and badges with a metallic gold or brass hue. This not only makes the figures pop, but also keeps the ceremonial vibe intact. A good practice is to keep the metallics slightly weathered; these guys are watchers of the hive, not angels of mercy, and the grime is part of the character.

If you enjoy unusual color schemes, consider a two-tone approach: the body armor in one cool metallic, the cloaks or tabards in a saturated color that signals your gang’s sector or precinct. A clean white/cream scheme with a bold emerald or cobalt accent looks sharp on camera and on display alongside the neon-lit board gaming mat. For basing, you’re aiming for urban rubble with occasional bits of neon signage and little bits of refuse that tell a story. A cracked pavement effect plus a few tufts of hardy Heather or Deathworld grass can anchor the miniature in the hive’s environment without drowning the piece in mud.

### Playstyle: Why the Justicars are Fun on the Table

In game terms, the Palanite Justicars tend to favor a balanced, controlled approach to conflict. They’re not the glass cannon people expect from a gang with such a righteous vibe; instead, they bring discipline and reliable firepower to mid-range exchanges. The typical squad loads up with a mix of standard-issue rods and pistols, a leader who can issue commands and a few specialists who can adjust the plan mid-fight. This makes them very good at objective play, where you need steady movement, discipline, and the occasional dramatic takedown of a skirmishing ganger in a doorway glowing with plasma lights.

Where they truly shine is in synergy with their environment. In Necromunda, terrain matters, and a Palanite squad benefits from positions that reward deliberate advancement and cover discipline. Think of them as the chess masters of the underhive: they don’t sprint to the enemy, they step with intention, controlling the lines and shaping the battlefield like a more polite, less murderous game of chess.

### Tactics: How to Use Them in a Game

- Opening the lane: The Justicars excel when you can push a lane with suppressive fire and pinned enemies, making space for a calculated advance. Use your long-range weapons to threaten opposite sides of a corridor, forcing your opponent to split their forces or risk a brutal crossfire.
- Deployment discipline: Do not spill your squad into open ground; Necromunda fights reward obstructed lines of approach. Use push-and-cover, pressing your advantage from a doorway or behind a stack of crates. The longer you stay in cover and move with a plan, the more your opponent will realize you have an actual strategy beyond rolling dice and hoping for good luck.
- Specialist play: Deploy the specialists to disrupt your opponent’s plan—Whether they’re cutting off a straggling unit with a tactical move or holding a critical objective, this is where the Justicars shine. The right specialist can turn a defensive position into a momentum swing, letting your squad apply pressure where it hurts most.

If you want a more narrative, cinematic game, lean into the Palanite Justicars’ story potential. The hive is a brutal theater of law and chaos; playing a squad that embodies the enforcement side gives you opportunities to roleplay, narrate, and occasionally scold your opponent for forgetting about the objective while you recount a long-winded policy that somehow matters in a skirmish about power swords and pistols.

### Scenery, Terrain, and Board Setup

Necromunda is a beautiful chaos of verticality and neon. The Justicars fit nicely into a district with watchtowers, a street market, and a courthouse that looks like it survived a few gauss cannons. For terrain enthusiasts, you can lean into a courthouse set with pillars and balconies, or a high-rise precinct where the Justicars guard the stairwell with a digital badge that glows with a soft, blue light. The key is to remember the Justicars are not necessarily about flashy long shots; they’re about maintaining lines of sight and forcing your opponent to decide between advancing through cover or giving up a defensible position.

If you want to bring a little more drama, you could theme your board around a siege narrative: a square mile of hive district under a maintenance blackout, with the Justicars trying to keep the power grid stable while looters circle like vulture parrots. The narrative payoff is huge—your games feel like episodes of a grim, neon-lit legal thriller rather than simple skirmishes.

### Painting and Assembly: A Practical Guide

- Tools you’ll want: a decent hobby knife, a few files, a fine-tipped brush for insignia, and a reliable bottle of flow aid to help the metallics settle into the model’s recesses. You’ll also want a steady hand for the insignia—these are the sort of details that will either elevate the look or invite a few friendly reprimands from your gaming group.
- Assembly tips: If your kit includes multi-part torsos and arms, assemble the torsos, then attach the weapon arms to avoid glue on the wrong pieces. Dry-fit often helps here. For metallics, a spray undercoat in gray or black with a final dry-brush of silver or gunmetal can help you get that rainy hive vibe without too much fuss.
- Basing ideas: The hive floor look works well with a textured basing grit, small rubble, and a few micro-posters or scraps that imply a society in disrepair yet still functioning. If you want a more dramatic aesthetic, use a wet gloss on the base to simulate a rain-soaked, neon-splashed street—though be mindful not to ruin the base with too much moisture.
