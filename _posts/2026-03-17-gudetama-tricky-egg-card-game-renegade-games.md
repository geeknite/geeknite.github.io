## How to Play: Setup, Turn Structure, and One-Fit Rules

Like many light card games, Gudetama’s appeal rests on approachable setup and a rhythm you can learn in one evening and forget by the next morning, only to rediscover on a rainy Sunday. Here’s a practical roadmap to get you into the thick of the eggy chaos.

### Setup
- Shuffle the main deck. Remove any optional or expansion rules if you’re a purist who hates modular complexity on day one.
- Each player draws a starting hand (the exact number is in the rulebook; you’ll memorize it after your first successful bluff or failed attempt to bluff).
- Place the draw pile in the center. The discard pile forms as an island of memories from every failed plan.
- Decide the starting player by a quick, silly method (rock-paper-scissors, who can make the loudest Gudetama sigh, or who brought the best egg pun). The official rules lean light; your table’s vibe should set the tone.

### Turn Structure
A typical turn in Gudetama involves drawing, playing cards, and potentially triggering effects that impact yourself or your opponents. The core loop goes something like this:
- Draw up to your hand limit.
- Play cards to complete tasks, sabotage other players, or dodge negative events.
- Resolve any effects generated by your cards and resolve any end-of-turn triggers.
- Pass to the next player. Keep the table buzzing with mild chaos and light-hearted blame if you’re playing with friends who enjoy witty banter.

The exact card types vary by edition and any included variations, but you’ll likely encounter a few recurring archetypes: task cards you want to complete for points, event cards that flip the table (in name, not literally), and mitigation cards that help you dodge or dodge-dodge other players’ schemes. The interlocking design means good players learn to balance aggression with defense, and excellent players learn to exploit timing more than to overpower direct confrontation.

### Endgame and Victory Conditions
The game typically ends when a player hits a certain point threshold, runs out of cards, or completes a final big task. Your victory is earned not by producing the most dramatic play but by stacking a few smart, well-timed moves that align with Gudetama’s vibe: minimal effort, maximum effect. In practice, this means you’ll pivot between “I’m just vibing” and “I swear I planned this all along” with equal parts giggles and groans.

As the board fills with tasks and event cards, you’ll want to monitor not just your own cards but the tempo of your opponents. When victory inches into reach, the challenge shifts to risk management. Gudetama won’t shout you down if you misplay; it quietly invites you to reconsider your life choices while you draw the next card.
