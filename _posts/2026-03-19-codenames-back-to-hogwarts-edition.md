### How to Play: Quickstart (the non-spoiler version)
If you already know Codenames, you’ll feel right at home. If you’re new, fear not—the Hogwarts edition is welcoming to novices, pun intended. The core loop remains: one player from each team acts as the spymaster and gives a one-word clue plus a number that indicates how many cards on the table relate to that clue. The rest of the team tries to guess which Character Cards match the clue. You win by finding all your team’s agents before the other team does, while avoiding the Assassin card.

Setup is straightforward:

- Lay out a grid of 5x5 Character Cards on the table. Each card has a name that resonates with Hogwarts lore, but you don’t reveal which house they belong to until guessed. The colors or markers on the board indicate which team controls which guesses; in Hogwarts mode, Gryffindor vs. Slytherin is the default duel, with the other two houses in supporting roles as neutrals that can be used to add spice to the game.
- Each team selects a spymaster. The spymaster studies the grid and the color-coded House tokens while the rest of the team sits ready to shout guesses.
- The starter team gets to pick the first clue. The clue must be a single word that relates to one or more of the 25 visible words on the table, and you announce the number that tells your teammates how many cards are linked to the clue. Yes, you still have to count the plausible connections in your head, which means the coffee you consumed earlier will be judged by the Ministry of Magic.

Cluing rules are intentionally unforgiving—and delightfully punishing—in a magical way. A good clue must connect as many of your team’s agents as possible with accuracy. A terrible clue will either pull in the wrong cards or hint at the Assassin card, which ends your turn in a spectacularly dramatic fashion, like a failed levitation spell that dumps a tray of butterbeer on your lap.

To keep things Hogwarts-y, the clue vocabulary can involve magical terms like spells, creatures, or famous Hogwarts locations, provided the one-word constraint can be satisfied and isn’t itself a house-coded tag that would reveal too much. The joy of this edition is watching your team construct a lattice of associations steeped in Hogwarts culture rather than the grim, modern vibe of a typical spy thriller. It’s cozy chaos, the kind of chaos that makes you wish you had a time-turner to rewind the moment you misread a clue.

### Play Styles: Balanced Chaos vs. Tight Strategy
The Hogwarts edition invites two broad play styles you’ll see at the table:

- The Sharp-Eyed Strategists: These players squeeze every inch of meaning out of a clue. They look for dense, multi-card connections that reward deep knowledge of both the base game vocabulary and Hogwarts lore. They’re happy to risk a slightly obtuse clue if the payoff is a clean sweep of two or three agents plus a safe neutral. This style is best when your team is comfortable with risk and has at least one player who won’t panic when the Dark Mark appears on the table.
- The Charming Chaos Crew: These players love the thematic wackiness this edition brings. ### Strategy Deep-Dive: Clue Crafting in the Wizarding World
Let’s talk about the art of giving a one-word clue that sings. Here are practical tips that work in any Hogwarts game night, whether you’re a first-timer or a seasoned wand-wielder:

- Know your grid. Before you start, take stock of which words are your agents, which are the neutrals, and which is the assassin. The more you understand the layout, the better your clue will hit the sweet spot between accuracy and breadth.
- Build bridges with shared contexts. A good clue in Hogwarts mode might connect two or three words with a single magical concept. For example, a clue like Arithmancy 3 could link a trio of words associated with numbers, riddles, and logical thinking—assuming your grid doesn’t sabotage your plan by including a card that’s disastrously off-theme.
- Use house-based logic sparingly. If your team is disciplined, you can craft clues that align with Ravenclaw traits (cleverness, learning, strategy), but beware: overusing thematic cues can become predictable and invite counterplay from the other team.
- Don’t fear the obvious. Sometimes a clean, straightforward clue that references a widely-known Hogwarts concept works best. The risk of overthinking a clue is real; you want to avoid cornering yourself with a clue that only one card could fit, unless you’re certain your teammates won’t overthink it.
- Watch the assassin. The Dark Mark is the gatekeeper of risk. If your clue nudges a lot of neutral cards or threatens the assassin, you might want to pull back. The best players know when to press and when to retreat behind a protective shield of safe guesses.
