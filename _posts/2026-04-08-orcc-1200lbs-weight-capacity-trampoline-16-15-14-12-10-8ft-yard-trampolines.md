## Safety first: nets, padding, anchors, and good habits

A trampoline is a device meant for joy, not a daredevil test. ORCC includes safety-conscious design features, but the user remains the most important safety component. Here are essential safety features and best practices to keep your backyard fun safe and sane:

- Enclosures: The nets are designed to prevent ejections, with multiple attachment points to resist sag. Regularly inspect the zipper and seams; a stuck zipper can derail a dream trick and cause bruises.
- Padding: Full padding covers the springs and frame to reduce direct contact with metal. Check padding for cracks or tears; replace worn padding to avoid bite-sized injuries from exposed springs.
- Frame and springs: A sturdy frame reduces wobble and edge bounce. Springs should be evenly tensioned and not rusted. If springs begin to feel gritty or show signs of corrosion, it’s time to replace them.
- Surface clearance: Ensure level ground and a clear space around the trampoline. The recommended clearance depends on model size, but the rule of thumb is: more space equals safer jumps. Avoid proximity to fences, trees, and hard points.
- Supervision and age guidance: The 1200-pound rating reflects capacity, not a free pass for heavy use by too many untrained participants. Supervise kids and introduce a simple set of ground rules for tricky stunts. ## Assembly, maintenance, and long-term reliability

The assembly experience with ORCC trampolines is typically straightforward but not something you should rush. Here’s a practical guide to get you through the process without turning your weekend into a jigsaw puzzle:

- Unpack and inventory: lay everything out and verify that all parts are present. Missing screws or a damaged net is not a good way to start your weekend. 
- Frame assembly: the frame pieces click into place with bolts in a configuration that promotes rigidity. Make sure legs align to prevent wobble.
- Spring installation: attach springs to the frame and mat in a balanced pattern, alternating sides to ensure even tension. If you ignore balance, you’ll end up with a lopsided bounce that feels like the world’s run-on treadmill.
- Net and padding: install the net with a taut perimeter and ensure padding covers the springs completely. A loose net or pad leaves you exposed to the raw edge of the frame—the kind of exposure that makes you question your life choices.
- Final checks: test-bounce gently to ensure the unit is level and stable. Tighten any loose bolts to the manufacturer’s torque spec. Periodic inspections after heavy use help maintain reliability across seasons.

Maintenance is mostly about durability: inspect padding for cracks, replace worn nets, and occasionally treat metal parts with a rust-prevention product suitable for outdoor use. If you live in a climate with harsh winters, consider storing the trampoline indoors or under a heavy-weather cover to extend its life.

### A note on assembly time

Expect larger models to require more hands and a little more patience. A couple of hours is a reasonable expectation for mid-sized to larger units, assuming you have a couple of helpers and a plan. Turn on some music, have a snack in your back pocket, and you’ll be done before you know it. Pro tip: use a cordless drill with a low-torque setting for speed without stripping bolts. Also, have a couch nearby for the inevitable post-assembly nap.

## Buyer’s guide: selecting the right size for your yard and crew

If you’re stranded in the “which size should I buy?” dilemma, here’s a simple framework:

- Space first: measure your yard and confirm there’s at least a 6–8 foot clearance around the unit (or more if you live near trees or fences). Consider a layout that doesn’t block a walkway or grill area.
- Borrowed time: if you want to avoid a future upgrade, opt for a larger model from the outset if you have the space and budget. It’s easier to scale down a large trampoline than to add space later.
- Family forecast: consider how many kids and adults you expect to bounce at once. If you frequently court big group sessions, a 14–16 foot model is a smart bet.
- Weight distribution: even with 1200-pound capacity, you’ll get the best bounce and least edge drift when weight is evenly distributed across the mat. Try not to have five adults all stand near the edge to “see who can jump higher”—the outcome is often a surprising thud and a dramatic failure in a tiny corner of your lawn.
