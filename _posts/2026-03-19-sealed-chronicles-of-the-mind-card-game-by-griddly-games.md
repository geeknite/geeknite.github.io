## Core Mechanics: What You Do in a Round
Sealed Chronicles of the Mind operates on a handful of core ideas that merge deck-building instinct with memory-based challenges. Here’s the gist:
- Sealed Pool and Draft: Instead of building a deck from a large, ready-made pool, players draft from a pool of sealed packs, selecting cards to form a personal deck. This creates asymmetry in setup and lets players tailor their strategy to their luck and choices—an elegant way to mix determinism with chaos.
- Resource Management: Cards grant you resources over several turns, which you’ll spend to perform actions, unlock mind tricks, or hinge on memory-based challenges. The tension comes from choosing whether to bank for a bigger turn or spend early to secure momentum.
- Memory and Mind Tricks: A set of cards includes memory upgrade tokens, recall-based actions, and mind-shield mechanisms. You’ll be asked to recall details from earlier rounds, track a sequence, or anticipate what your opponent might do next. It’s like a light cognitive workout with color-coded rewards.
- Scoring and Endgame: The game ends when a certain score threshold is reached or when a round-count is exhausted. Points come not only from card effects but also from successful mental maneuvers—streaks and combos yield higher payoffs. The scoring system tends to reward planning without punishing you for having a few bad draws, which is ideal for family play.

The core loop feels familiar to players who love deck-building but want more narrative-minded, light-to-medium strategy. It’s not a heavy tablebusting engine like Dominion, but it scratches a similar itch by offering meaningful decisions in a compact footprint. If you adore card-slinging with a hint of brain-teasing puzzle, SCM scratches that itch nicely.

A typical turn sequence might look like this:
1) Draw phase: You refresh a portion of your deck and glimpse potential options.
2) Action phase: You play a card or activate a power, often spending memory tokens.
3) Memory phase: You resolve a memory-based mechanic that tests recall, sequence tracking, or pattern recognition.
4) End phase: You discard down to your limit and prepare for the next cycle.

The pacing is well-tuned for a 30–45 minute session with 2–4 players, which makes it a great fit for a late-evening family game night or a quick tabletop meetup without risking civic-level fatigue.

## Play Style and Strategy: For Sealed Deck Builders and Minds Alike
If you’re coming from sealed-deck experiences in other games, SCM will feel familiar but with a friendly twist. Here are some practical strategies you can apply right away, whether you’re a veteran sealed-deck strategist or a curious newcomer:
- Early picks matter, but not too much: The sealed pool is designed to offer strong synergy opportunities, but you’re not locked into a single path on the first pack. The best decks in SCM are the ones that stay flexible for a couple of rounds while you learn the opponent’s rhythm.
- Decode the table: Pay attention to what your opponents are drafting and what their memory challenges suggest they’ll try. If you spot a deck heavy on recall mechanics, you can pivot toward counter-memories or shielding tokens.
- Memory tokens beat brute force: In many rounds, it’s more valuable to secure a memory token or memory-based action than to sweep the board once. The puzzle-y aspect is the game’s heartbeat, and prioritizing it pays dividends in late-game scoring opportunities.
- Balanced risk-taking: Since the pool is sealed, you’ll occasionally face dead-draws. The art is to build a deck that can generate at least some value even when the luck of the draw gives you suboptimal cards.
- Endgame awareness: If you’re closing in on the winning threshold, you’ll want to maximize your late-round effects. The timer and the flow of rounds can be unforgiving if you misjudge the tempo, so think two steps ahead—a trait all good gamers cultivate.

If you’re new to the sealed deck vibe, SCM delivers a gentle onboarding: you’ll learn the mechanics during the first couple of rounds and then feel the “aha” moment as you connect a few card combos. For experienced players, there’s enough depth to sustain a couple of evenings of games before you’ll want the expansion packs to mix in new cards and new challenges.

### Examples of Card Archetypes (Flavor, Not Rules)
- Recall Cards: Triggered by remembering a sequence or pattern, these cards reward your memory with immediate in-game advantages.
- Focus Cards: These help you tighten your next action, reducing variance and increasing precision.
- Breakthrough Cards: High-risk, high-reward cards that can swing the round if timed well.
- Shield Cards: Defensive options that protect you from counter-moves or disrupt your opponent’s plans.

These archetypes aren’t rigid partitions. The deck-building philosophy in SCM is to mix and match archetypes to create a personal vision of how your mind travels through each round.
