## Cleaning and Maintenance

### Quick Clean-Up

After a painting session, rinse the brush under lukewarm water and gently work out the paint with your fingers or a mild brush soap. Don’t be aggressive; you’re not trying to groom a coyote with a wire brush. A gentle rinse helps preserve the bristles and prevents the paint from setting in the ferrule. If you’ve used particularly thick or fast-drying paint, consider a brief soak in a dedicated brush cleaner or a mild soapy solution to loosen the pigment before rinsing.

### Drying and Storage

Lay the brush flat or with the bristles pointing downward while drying. Don’t stand it upright to “drip dry” into the metal ferrule; that’s how you end up with a wobbly ferrule and a sad brush. Once dry, store it in a brush holder with bristles upwards to maintain their shape. Proper storage extends their life and keeps you from the heartbreak of a bristle wand that won’t obey gravity anymore.

### The Soap Debate

Some hobbyists insist on fancy soaps or conditioners for brush maintenance. If you want to dabble, a mild, pH-neutral dish soap diluted with water can do the job. The key is not to soak the bristles in detergent for hours; you’re not giving them a spa day. You’re cleaning off pigment and oils. If you want to keep it simple, warm water and a light rinse usually suffice for the Citadel brush, especially if you’re using thinner paints and not caking your bristles with varnish by accident.

## Practical Techniques with the Base Brush

### The Right Load for the Right Surface

Loading the brush with just the right amount of paint is a skill unto itself. Too much, and you’ll flood details you don’t want to flood; too little, and you’ll need to go back for round two. The magic trick is to dip the bristles into the paint, wipe off the excess on a palette edge, and then deliver a single, confident pass across the surface. You’ll be surprised how often you get a clean, smooth coat with just a light touch.

### Strokes and Strokes Again

Long, smooth strokes are your friend here. Start outside the model’s edge and sweep toward the center to reduce pooling in corners. Don’t press down hard; you’re not trying to drive paint into the plastic; you’re asking the bristles to spread a thin, even film. If you see a ridge forming along the edge, lift on the stroke and let the bristles glide to finish the pass. It’s a dance of light pressure and steady hands, and the brush is your tempo.

### Layering Ahead

Base coats set the foundation for layers and details. Once your base coat is down, you’ll typically do a wash or shade to bring out the recesses, followed by a couple more layers for color depth. The Medium Base Brush helps you achieve an even starting point, but don’t rely on it to “fix” a badly thinned paint job. If your base color looks patchy, adjust the thinning ratio and try again. The brush will respond; it won’t fix bad planning.

## Common Pitfalls and Pro Tips

### Overloading and Splaying

If you overload the brush, you’ll get pooling and bristle splay. This usually shows up as a patchy, glossy surface rather than a smooth coat. The fix is simple: wipe off the excess paint and keep the bristle fan broad and even. A little practice makes this instinctive.

### The Great Drying Trick

Paint dries differently depending on temperature and humidity. If your room is cold or dry, you’ll see fewer brush marks and more drag. If it’s warm and humid, you might get faster flow but with more tendency for pooling. You’ll want to adjust your paint consistency and perhaps slow down your stroke to compensate. This isn’t the brush’s fault; it’s physics in action, and the brush is merely a helpful translator.

### Texture and Surfaces

Textured primed minis can catch extra paint in the high points. The base brush is forgiving, but you might need to do a couple of light passes to achieve even coverage. It’s not the brush’s fault; it’s the model’s texture. The trick is to use longer strokes and to work in sections so you don’t drop a bunch of paint on one rough area and leave a cliff of color on another.
