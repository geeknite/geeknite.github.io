## How to play (Family edition overview)

The core mechanics mirror the classic Codenames: two teams, one Spymaster per team, a grid of words on the table, and a set of clues that tie multiple words together. But the Family Edition tweaks the math just enough to keep it rooted in accessibility:

- Teams take turns with a Spymaster providing a one-word clue and a number. The number tells your teammates how many words on the board relate to that clue. The catch: don’t pick the assassin word.
- The board is a grid (commonly 5x5 for standard configurations), with each word being neutral or belonging to one of the teams.
- Family Edition clue-giving emphasizes safe, age-appropriate vocabulary. You’ll find that some hints are literal, some are silly, and others are surprisingly elegant when you see the connections formed in the moment.
- The weaker, safer rules of the Family Edition often translate into faster rounds, giving you more opportunities to roast your siblings between turns. That’s not a mechanic, but it is a guaranteed side benefit.

A typical round goes like this:

1) Spymaster looks at the board and selects a clue that connects several of their team’s words, along with a number. 
2) Teammates discuss and debate which words best fit that clue without stepping on the Assassin word. 
3) Team taps or points to the words they believe match the clue. If a word is correct, it’s covered with the team’s color. If it’s wrong, the other team may have a chance to steal depending on your house rules. If someone hits the assassin word, the round ends badly—drama ensues, and everyone is reminded to be gentle and patient with one another.
4) Play passes to the other side, and the merry domino effect continues until a team seals their words or until both sides reach a glorious stalemate.

What makes the Family Edition sing is its pacing. The clue word has to be one word and a number, which creates this delightful game of “how far can we stretch this clue without stepping on a landmine?” It’s a test of vocabulary reflexes for kids and a memory game for grownups; the combination is surprisingly satisfying.

## House rules and customization ideas

- Time pressure: Add a timer to each clue round to increase tension and speed. You don’t want to become the “forever clue-giver” who talks for five minutes with no progress.
- Steal rounds: If you want to introduce a little more competition, allow the opposing team a chance to steal after a wrong guess, with a fixed penalty for each steal to keep speed reasonable.
- Word-suggestion rule: Allow a candidate word to be suggested by any player, but the Spymaster must decide whether to use it as the clue or pass on it. This can create hilarious debates about semantics and cross-linguistic puns.
- Theme nights: Build a theme around your family’s inside jokes—each word card could be aligned to a theme (animals, foods, superheroes), making the clue choices more about shared culture than pure vocabulary.
