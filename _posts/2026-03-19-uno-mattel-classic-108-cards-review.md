## Setup and Rules: A Friendly Refresher with Optional House Rules
UNO is famous for its simple base rules, but the 108-card deck invites a little experimentation. Here’s a concise refresher, peppered with optional house rules for players who want a longer, more dramatic experience.

### Basic Rules (for purists)
- Each player starts with seven cards. The rest goes face down as the draw pile; the top card is flipped to start the discard pile.
- On your turn, you must match the top card of the discard pile by either color or number. Special cards add twisty fun: Skip means the next player loses a turn, Reverse flips the direction of play, and Draw Two forces the next player to draw two cards unless they have a matching Draw Two to pass the pain onward.
- Wild and Wild Draw Four cards let you choose the color to continue, and the Wild Draw Four adds a dramatic “you thought you could dodge me?” moment to the table.
- If you’re down to one card, you must shout “UNO!” If another player catches you forgetting, you draw a card as a penalty. This is the ritual that turns casual play into a social performance.
- The winner is the first player to get rid of all their cards. Yes, that’s it. Simple, effective, and somehow still tense.

### House Rules: Spices for the Soul
- Draw 4 mischief: Require players to draw four cards unless they can counter with a color-m matching card and a specific draw sequence—this can slow the game in a fun way.
- Color lock: After a Wild card is played, the color cannot be changed for the next two turns. It adds a strategic layer that rewards memory and precision.
- UNO shock: When someone plays a Wild Draw Four, the next player can challenge the move if they suspect foul play. If the challenge fails, the challenger draws extra cards; if it succeeds, the wild player draws cards instead. It’s trivia-level drama with a side of “I knew it!” shouts.
- Team UNO: For family nights, pair up players into teams. This reduces the chance of one competitive protagonist hijacking the entire game and makes the experience more inclusive for younger players.

The deck’s 108 cards are not just a bigger pile; they’re an invitation to craft the rules you want. The flexibility is a feature, not a bug. If you’re a purist, you’ll love the clean, predictable flow. If you’re a chaos architect, you’ll relish the endless rule variations you can dream up.
