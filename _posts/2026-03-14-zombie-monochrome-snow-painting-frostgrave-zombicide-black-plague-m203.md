## The Concept: Monochrome Snow on a Zombie
The idea of monochrome painting is to push value rather than color. In snow-themed minis, this often means playing with contrasts between deep charcoals, mid-gray midtones, and icy whites, with occasional cool blues or violets to hint at shadows and cold air. A zombie, by nature, is a study in pale tones and weathered textures. Add a snow glaze and you get a creature that looks like it survived a blizzard, only to realize it’s still going to shuffle toward your banner and probably sneeze snow into your face.

In this build, the M203 accessory serves as an anachronistic-but-delicious prop. It’s not historically accurate for the Frostgrave setting, but the contrast between a modern grenade launcher and a medieval-fantasy backdrop is the kind of humor that makes painting sessions unforgettable. It also gives you a chance to practice metallics, rust, and grime on a nontraditional piece—because every undead deserves functional hardware… or at least a convincing pretend one.

## The Kit and the Setup
### The Miniatures
- Zombicide: Black Plague minis bring brutal, modern zombie aesthetics to the table. They’re chunky, expressive, and forgiving for beginners (and for people who drop brushes with a theatrical sigh).
- Frostgrave undead specters offer a more stylized, slightly magical vibe. They’re perfect for exploring pale skin tones and subtle runes etched in stone. Mixing the two requires a delicate balance: you want the undead to read as undead and not as a glitch in a video game.

### The Accessory: M203 Miniature
The M203 grenade launcher is a fun, campy addition. It doesn’t exist in the original zombie tides, but it makes for a striking focal point on a model who’s otherwise wearing snow like a fashion statement. The goal is to treat it as a weathered tool rather than a pristine prop—chips, scratches, and an understated layer of grime that says: “I survive on courage, sarcasm, and questionable decisions.”

### The Palette (Monochrome Snow)
- Base: Neutral gray or mid-gray (e.g., GW Administratum Grey or Vallejo Neutral Grey).
- Midtones: Slightly cooler mid-gray (e.g., Celestra Gray). The aim is to keep things readable without slapping the viewer with pure white.
- Highlights: Off-white or bone white (e.g., Ushabti Bone) with cool tinting.
- Shadow/Deep shadow: Rich charcoal or almost-black (e.g., Black Soot or Nuln Oil wash for the deeper recesses).
- Snow: White with blue or lavender tints for cold lighting (a touch of N prefer: blue-gray washes).
- M203 metallics: Gunmetal, steel, rust accents for realism.

### Tools and Gear
- Good-quality brushes (one size for detail, one larger for dry brushing).
- A wet palette to keep colors sane while you fight the cold metal of your heart.
- Inks and washes (Nuln Oil, Druchii Violet, blue-tinted washes) for shadows and grime.
- A scenic base with snow texture paste or baking soda as alternative.
- A steady hand and a small bottle of patience—the two most valuable commodities in any Frostgrave/Zombicide paint-off.

## Step-by-Step Painting Guide
This is the part where your hands become a kind of weather machine, and your brain becomes an instruction manual for tiny plastic glyphs that refuse to stay still.

### Step 1: Preparation and Assembly
- Inspect the mini for mold lines; remove them with a hobby knife. Sand lightly where needed.
- Prime the model with a neutral gray spray. Gray primer often helps the monochrome palette pop without biasing color choices toward warmth or coolness.
- Assemble the M203 accessory first, if possible, to ensure better access for subsequent painting and weathering.

### Step 2: Basecoating the Zombie
- Apply a mid-gray base across the skin areas. The goal is to create a canvas that can receive both shadows and highlights without burning through to a white bias.
- Paint the clothing in slightly darker gray to help separate the figure from the snow. If you want a hint of Frostgrave’s sorcereous vibe, you can throw a touch of desaturated blue to the shadows of the cloak.
- For the exposed bone and decayed tissues, use a slightly lighter gray with blueish undertones. This gives a lifeless but still readable legibility under the snow cap.

### Step 3: Monochrome Shading and Depth
- Wash the model with a diluted black wash (Nuln Oil or a similar product). Focus on recesses, such as eye sockets, under the brow, and behind torn fabric. Let these areas gather extra pigment to create believable depth.
- Add a second wash with a cool blue-gray if you want to push the cold aesthetic. The blue tint will emerge subtly in the darkest areas, simulating cold air pooling around the figure.
- Highlight raised surfaces with a lighter gray; apply sparing, controlled highlights to edges of the cloak and bones that catch ambient light.

### Step 4: Snow Layering and Basework
- Prepare the base with snow texture. If you’re using texture paste, apply it around the base in a way that resembles a small drift or scattered snow clumps.
- Once dry, dry-brush with a lighter gray to give a frosted look at the edges, followed by a final light touch with off-white to indicate fresh snowfall.
- Blend a translucent blue glaze along the base to evoke cold shadows on the snowcap. This makes the snow look icy rather than chalky white.
- For a more dramatic effect, add a dusting of pure white on the hats and shoulder folds to simulate fresh powder that’s still sitting there, unscuffed by human feet.

### Step 5: The M203: Metal, Wear, and Rust
- Paint the M203 with a base of dark gunmetal. Shadows settle in the recesses, and you’re aiming for a slightly worn look, not a brand-new toy.
- Dry-brush a lighter steel color along the edges and raised surfaces to get the metal catching the light.
- Add a touch of rust at screws, seams, and the bipod joints. A tiny dab of reddish-brown glaze, applied sparingly, will sell the “battle-scarred” vibe without overpowering the piece.
- If you want a worn camouflage look on the M203 for a post-apocalyptic flavor, a light wash of dull green or brown mixed with your steel can produce an industrial patina that reads as weathered but functional.

### Step 6: Final Weathering and Finessing
- Apply a touch of grime on the base edges and the lower legs of the zombie to suggest mud and winter grit. Use a brownish-gray (not black) to keep the monochrome feel intact.
- Add tiny streaks along the torso and limbs to imply movement and accidental falls—a zombie on ice is likely to slip, slide, and hilariously fail at staying upright.
- Consider a glaze toward the face to soften the transition between midtones and highlights. A cool-tone glaze will keep the face readable while preventing a washed-out look.

### Step 7: Varnish and Display
- Use a matte varnish to preserve the soft look of powder snow. If you want a subtle shine on the M203 metallics, you can selectively apply a satin varnish to those parts.
- Mount the miniature on a display-friendly base. A small narrative base—like a frozen cobblestone with ice puddles—adds storytelling value and gives you a place to put a tiny sign that says, “Beware the cold.”

## The Aesthetic Challenge: Frostgrave vs Zombicide
One of the biggest fun challenges with this build is balancing the Frostgrave vibe with Zombicide’s brutal zombie aesthetic. Frostgrave undead tend to lean toward elegant, rune-etched robes and a pale, almost aristocratic grimace. Zombicide Black Plague zombies lean more toward gritty, mass-produced undead with a certain cartoonish heaviness that begs to be painted in bold contrast. Combining the two requires a careful eye for contrast: you want the zombie’s face to read as a lifeless countenance in a snowstorm rather than a ghostly white blob. The monochrome approach helps by keeping the color noise low, but you still need enough tonal difference to prevent the piece from flattening into a single gray blob.

The M203 introduces a modern element that clashes with a medieval fantasy‑ish zombie, yet that clash is exactly the point. It’s a humorous reminder that the apocalypse, much like the hobby, doesn’t care about genre boundaries. The trick is to weather the M203 and give it a slightly worn, functional look that hints at a long period of service in a blizzard-lit siege while still harmonizing with the zombie’s bone-and-snow palette.

## Practical Tips and Common Pitfalls
- Avoid over-bright highlights. Snow looks best when the highlights are crisp but not blinding. You want the white to look like snow that has settled, not a beacon of sun. A common mistake is slapping pure white on everything; your model will read as “glow-in-the-dark snow monster,” which is cute but not accurate.
- When painting faces on zombies, give them a slightly cooler tone. A touch of blue-gray in the cheeks makes them feel cold and undead rather than undead plus sunburn.
- Snow basing can be deceptively tricky. Real snow reflects light differently than model snow. If your snow looks too powdery, you can glaze with a thin blue wash to simulate the cold metals reflecting into the powder.
- The M203 should not outshine the zombie’s face. If it does, dunk the M203 in a duller glaze or weathering to bring the focus back to the creature’s expression. A good rule of thumb: the eye-line should naturally lead to the face, not the launcher.
- Dry brushing is your best friend for snow texture. It adds life without destroying your base color. Resist over-dry brushing to avoid sandy textures that look more like desert camouflage than winter wonderland.
