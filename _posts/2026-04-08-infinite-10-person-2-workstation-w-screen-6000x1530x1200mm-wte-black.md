## Features and specs: what does it actually offer?
### Screen system and display integration
The centerpiece of this system is a built-in display arrangement designed to serve ten users with a single large screen or multiple screens dispersed along the spine. The exact configuration varies by model, but you can expect a main screen with a large viewing angle plus perhaps a couple of smaller displays or mounted panels for shared content. The idea is to allow a facilitator to project content while individuals keep their own work area. Think of it as a conference room and workstation hybrid. The screen system should offer height adjustability and mounting options that align with the ergonomic sweet spot for the group. If you crave ultra-tight integration, check how the screen mount interfaces with the desk surface and whether you can swap in alternative display sizes.

### Cable management and power distribution
Inside the central spine, you’ll typically find a structured cable management system: grommets, cable trays, and a power distribution unit (PDU) that can handle the combined load of keyboards, mice, monitors, chargers, and occasional coffee spills (we all know that the main hazard in open offices is caffeine-vs any wire combo). Efficient cable routing reduces clutter and helps avoid accidental unplugging during meetings. A well-designed unit will also include grommets and access points along the surface so you can route data and power lines without rocky tension or snag hazards.

### Ergonomics and adjustability
The promise here is that each user gets a practical, comfortable working zone while the shared components remain accessible. Ergonomic considerations include leg clearance, monitor height reach, and the ability to adjust chair settings to match each occupant’s height. A ten-person arrangement demands either a flexible seating plan or modular chairs that can be rearranged quickly for collaborative sessions. In practice, many teams report that the central area becomes a social hub during standup or design review, while home bases at the ends provide some privacy for heads-down tasks.

### Acoustic and thermal behavior
Large desk systems can trap heat and amplify noise from keyboards and chair wheels. The WTE Black finish doesn’t inherently fix this, but the system should incorporate some passive acoustic dampening around the central spine and cushion-air gaps in the panels if possible. If your office runs hot, consider adding a few ceiling-mounted air movers or strategic fans to keep the air circulating around the workstation cluster. The goal is to maintain a comfortable environment without turning the space into a wind tunnel.

## Maintenance, cleaning, and care
Cleaning a desk of this scale is less about wiping and more about disciplined housekeeping. A damp microfiber cloth will handle most spills and fingerprints on the surface. For the central spine and cable trays, use a soft brush or compressed air to dislodge dust that collects behind panels. Avoid harsh solvents that can degrade the laminate or loosen edge banding. Periodically check the leveling feet and reseal if you notice creaks or wobble. A regular maintenance cycle will keep the unit looking sharp and performing well for years.

If you have a high-traffic environment, consider adding a lightweight shield or monitor hood at the viewing zone to reduce glare and keep screen content legible for longer sessions. Glass-cleaning is not required, but a microfiber cloth with a gentle cleaner can help maintain that showroom finish.
