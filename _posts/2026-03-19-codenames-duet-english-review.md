## What is Codenames Duet?
Codenames Duet is a cooperative two-player variant built by the folks behind the original Codenames. Instead of competing teams, you and your partner share a single 5x5 grid of word cards and a set of key cards that guide your clues. The goal is to reveal all the necessary words for both players without triggering the “assassin” words or veering off into neutrals so early that you run out of time or stamina. The game scales with you; you’ll feel the board crowded and chaotic in the same moment you realize you’ve started to click as a duo rather than as two weary individuals trying to outguess the other team.

The English edition comes with snappy word pools and a few new twists that make the duo experiment feel fresh. It preserves the core tension of the original—one-word clues and a number that tells your partner how many cards to try to identify—while leaning into the two-player dynamic with a shared sense of purpose. The result is a game that taxes your brain but also rewards your sense of humor when you accidentally reveal a non-target word and both groan in unison.

## How to play: a quick guide
This section is a high level walkthrough intended to get you past the first turn with minimal tears. For the full rules, see the official page linked at the end of this post. Here’s the gist:

### Setup
- Arrange the 25 word cards face up in a 5x5 grid on the table.
- Shuffle the key cards and lay one on the table where everyone can see it, or keep it hidden if you’re playing a high stakes game night and want to feel like a secret agent.
- Decide who will be the spymaster and who will be the field operative (in a two-player setup, you’ll alternate roles each round). There is no need to pretend you’re not in a spy movie; embrace it with a dramatic whisper and a glint in your eye.

### Turn structure
- The spymaster looks at the key and then gives a one-word clue that ties together several words on the board. The clue is followed by a number indicating how many words on the board are related to that clue.
- The field operative then piques their curiosity, chooses words they think match the clue, and points to them one by one. If you guess a word that belongs to your team, you cover it with a token. If you guess a neutral word, place a neutral token; if you guess the opposing team’s word, you’ll shift balance against you; and if you guess an assassin word, that round ends abruptly with a dramatic crash of the table and a loud groan.
- You continue guessing up to the number shown with the clue, plus one extra guess if you want to push your luck. That is, the stakes rise with each guess, and the tension thickens like a good sauce.

### Clues and boundaries
- One-word clues are the order of the day. If you can’t conjure a one-word clue that covers multiple words, you can always go for a longer phrase—but be aware that longer doesn’t always mean smarter. The best clues create a numerically tight cluster of related words in a way that makes sense only to your partner.
- A good rule of thumb is to aim for a clue that captures multiple target words without pulling in too many red herrings. A smart clue turns a handful of ambiguous words into a satisfying chain of correct guesses.

### Endgame and victory conditions
- The game ends when all target words are revealed. In Duet, that means you and your partner have found enough words to unlock the victory condition together. If you hit an assassin word, your session ends in a loss, and you’ll probably have an entertaining story to tell about your dramatic misstep.
- The beauty of Duet is in how quickly a game can finish or how long it can stretch when you and your partner keep finding clever connections. It rewards collaboration, but it also punishes hubris with a well-placed red herring word.

## How it compares to the classic Codenames
If you’ve played the original Codenames with a group of five or more, you’ll recognize the core mechanic: a hint and a guess, a risk assessment, and a splash of chaos as you navigate the grid. But the original is designed for teams, large conversation, and a spicy dynamic where you cheer for your team just as loudly as you groan at the wrong guess.

Codenames Duet distills that flavor into a two-person experience. It trims down the procedural talk, doubles down on the shared state, and makes every word feel consequential. The art remains the same minimal but expressive style, but the game’s pace changes. It’s faster on average because you only have two people thinking through clues, not a small council. It’s slower to recover from a misstep because your partner is equally committed to recovering the path to victory.

From a design perspective, Duet tailors the experience to collaboration rather than competition. That means your banter, your smokescreens, and your almost inhuman capacity for connective wordplay all come to the foreground. The result is a game that can sustain long, satisfying sessions or bite-sized two-round plays between chores and coffee refills.

## Strategy tips for two players
If you want to up your Duet game, here are some practical strategies that don’t demand a lexicon like Shakespeare or a mind like a supercomputer:

### Think in clusters, not in single words
Your clues should attempt to capture several related words at once. The more you can group, the fewer turns you need to reveal them all. A successful clue is like a well-tossed rope that links a handful of words without catching the wrong ones.

### Use cross-connections, but avoid the obvious traps
When you see a cluster of words with a shared meaning, test the clue. But beware: the moment your clue nudges a dangerous word, you’ve given your partner a hyper-specific hint to avoid a possible disaster. The best clues are elegant and contain a dash of misdirection, not a map of every hazard.

### Count your risk, not your love for a clever pun
One-word clues are your friend, but don’t push too many words under the same umbrella. The fewer words you reveal per clue, the more control you have over the guessing path. It’s a balance between ambition and precision—and a steady hand beats a flashy but reckless flourish every time.

### Communicate non-verbally as needed
If your partner looks stumped, switch tactics. Sometimes a whispered nudge or a quick look can steer them toward the right guess without shouting out the intended link. Subtlety can be a superpower here; you just need to practice your poker-face until it’s believable.
