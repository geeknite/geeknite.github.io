## Blade details: material, finish, and edge stance
### Material and heat treatment
1065 steel is the go-to for many budget-friendly blades because it’s relatively forgiving to work with and responds well to typical heat-treat cycles. It’s a higher-carbon variant than simple low-carbon steels, which means it can hold a sharper edge longer and take a respectable edge geometry with proper tempering. It’s not stainless, which means maintenance matters: a dry wipe after handling and a light coat of protective oil will keep rust at bay.

In practice, you’ll notice the blade has a nice, even grind with a crisp shoulder. The temper line (if visible) isn’t dramatic, which is common in mid-range offerings. The sword’s blade face reflects light in a controlled manner, giving you the “polished-but-rigid” look without turning your photos into a blinding beacon.

### Edge geometry and use
This isn’t a performance cutter out of the box. Expect a blunt tip or a partially blunted tip designed more for display or light practice than for heavy cutting. If you’re buying for stage combat or cosplay, you’ll appreciate the safety out of the box, but if you’re considering Indonesian batoning or heavyweight targets, this blade is not intended for it. Always confirm with your local theater or training facility about allowed edge types and safety protocols.

For the curious minds who love numbers, we don’t have an official Rockwell number from the manufacturer here, but the general expectation in this tier is a hardness that keeps the edge doable while avoiding micro-chips in the handle-laminate stage. If you want a stiffer feel later on, you can still consider a professional sharpening service, but ensure the blade’s temper is preserved and the edge isn’t thinned past the loggerhead of your blade’s integrity.

### Full tang and balance
A full tang is a nice feature in this price bracket, which means the blade steel runs through the handle and is not just attached at the grip. This yields a sturdier feel when you’re wielding it for photos or light forms. In hand, the balance sits slightly forward of the grip, which is typical for a blade with a slightly larger blade proportion. It’s not a surgical instrument; it’s a theater prop that wants to look good in motion, which is exactly the vibe you’re paying for here.

## Maintenance and care
The maintenance philosophy for a blade like this is simple: keep it dry, keep it oiled, and keep it out of corrosive environments. After handling, wipe the blade with a dry cloth to remove fingerprints and moisture. A light coat of protective oil will help fend off rust—especially if you live in a humid climate or you drop the blade into a cosplay bag that’s been sweating from a long day of posing.

Do not use abrasive cleaners on the blade, and avoid aggressive scouring that could remove the finish. If the edge ever needs reconditioning (very rare in this tier), consult a professional blade sharpener who understands decorative and functional blades alike. The goal is to keep the blade looking like a hero in a photo and safe to handle in person, not to transform it into a razor.
