### How to play (the fast version)
Here’s the no-fluff guide to the Express experience. We’ll keep it spoiler-light and joke-heavy so you don’t miss the punchlines between the card shuffles.

1) Setup
- Shuffle the deck and deal a fixed number of cards to each player. The exact count varies by house rules, but Express keeps it tight: you won’t need a crew of librarians to organize the draw pile.
- Place the phase cards in a separate, visible pile. Each player can watch the phases rotate as rounds progress.
- Determine who goes first by a quick draw or, you know, a vibe check. The first player starts the action with a display of confidence and the others pretend not to be intimidated.

2) Objective
- The goal remains the same in spirit: complete one or more “phases” by forming card sets that match the current round’s requirements. Express achieves this in a handful of rounds, not in a sprawling marathon of “one more round, I swear.”
- After meeting a phase, you move on to the next one. In many versions, you must complete all ten phases to win. In Express, the pace is what you notice the most: the whole thing can feel like a brisk sprint rather than a marathon, which is exactly the point.

3) Turn flow
- On your turn, you’ll draw a card (or maybe two, depending on the rules you adopt), then choose to either complete a phase or throw in cards to optimize your hand for the next turn. The trick is balancing aggression with the occasional mercy dash toward the rules you’ve chosen to follow.
- You can play cards to the table to showcase your progress, or you can tuck them away and pretend you’re a quiet tactician who never makes eye contact with the shuffle.
- Decks are designed so you’re frequently forced to improvise; the game rewards flexible planning, a little bluff, and the occasional existential sigh when you realize a needed card is “somewhere else.”

4) Completing a phase
- When you meet the requirements for a phase, you lay down the necessary cards and move forward. Express makes this satisfying without dragging on; it’s like finishing a level in a video game but with more sarcasm and less heroism.
- Scoring is modestly simplified from the full Phase 10 experience, which means you won’t need a spreadsheet to track victory points and soul-dragging penalties after every round.

5) Ending the game
- The game ends when a player completes the final phase or runs out of rounds, depending on how you set it up. The quick nature of Express means you’ll likely cycle through a handful of rounds in a single sitting, which is ideal for a 20- to 40-minute lull between work tasks or a chaotic family game night where attention spans are all over the place.

### Strategy tips for success (with a wink)
- Read the room, not just the deck. If your table is high on extroversion, you’ll want to make bold plays early; if it’s a chill crew, you can bide your time and punish reckless optimism.
- Prioritize flexible phases. Some phases are easier to complete with certain card colors or numbers. If you can seed a flexible path, you’ll glide through several rounds without getting stuck.
- Don’t over-commit to a single phase. Express rewards adaptation; if you chase a single goal too hard, you may miss a better opportunity to finish multiple phases rapidly.
- Card memory matters. Track what’s been drawn and what’s still likely to appear. It helps when you’re deciding between a safe play and an aggressive, “I’m going to bluff my way through this round” moment.
