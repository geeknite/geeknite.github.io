## Print run, rarity, and market vibes
Scarlet & Violet 3 (SV3) is a set that a lot of collectors refer to with a mixture of nostalgia and dread—nostalgia because the art direction is often bold and playful; dread because the chase for rare variants can be brutal. Holo AR cards, by their nature, are rarer than standard holo cards, given the combination of alternate art and holo treatment. The JP edition tends to be a little more expensive on initial release than its English counterpart due to tighter distribution, import costs, and the romance of collecting something that only a handful of players ever pull from a pack.

Card 109/108 in this hypothetical SV3 run would be a talking point on collector forums because of the out-of-sequence numbering. We love an anti-promotional rumor around numbers; it creates a story arc for your binder that can outshine the actual gameplay value.

Rarity brackets for such a card would likely be something like this:
- Common: if it exists at all in a non-holo variant
- Uncommon: AR variants that are widely printed
- Rare: holo AR JP edition in a limited run
- Ultra rare: extremely limited print with misprint or mis-number

In the case of 109/108, we could speculate a tiny print run or a misprint edition. These become the kind of cards you show off in online streams while muttering about the mathematics of set design.

Authenticity considerations: When dealing with rare JP SV3 Gloom Holo AR cards, you want to look for:
- The holo pattern alignment across the card edges
- The font and spacing alignment to the set's standard
- The texture of the card; JP prints often have sharper corners and different varnish
- The presence of a factory sticker or watermark that authenticates your copy

Pro tip: if you want to avoid knockoffs, try to purchase from reputable sellers who often include a certificate of authenticity or provide official photos of the packaging. Also be mindful of the possibility of fakes on large marketplaces.
