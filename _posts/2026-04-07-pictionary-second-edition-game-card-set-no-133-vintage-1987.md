## Card stock, wear, and preservation basics
Vintage cards age differently depending on storage, humidity, and sunlight exposure. Here are practical guidelines for keeping No. 133 cards in playable condition:

- Storage: Store the deck in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight. The sun is not your friend when it comes to cardboard and ink; it’s a slow artist of yellowing and faint fading. A labeled, acid-free sleeve or binder is ideal for preserving both card stock and the delicate line work.
- Handling: Avoid excessive bending or flexing. Cards that have survived decades are more brittle than a modern spreadsheet—handle gently, like you’re decoding an ancient, sacred doodle.
- Cleaning: If you must clean, use a soft, dry cloth. Avoid water or cleaning solutions that can warp or smudge ink. The goal is to maintain legibility, not to erase the memories embedded in each doodle.
- Do you replace? If you find a missing card or a compromised card, consider replacements from reputable sources. Some collectors prefer original, untouched cards for display, while others are okay with a high-quality reproduction for playing. The important thing is to keep the deck complete and readable, especially when you want to relive your best guesses and your worst ones, all in one chaotic session.

## Practical care tips for long-term enjoyment
- Display options: If you have a nice glass cabinet or a shelving unit, consider a shallow display case for the No. 133 deck. Having the cards visible is a conversation starter, but keep them away from direct sunlight. A little mood lighting can make the artwork pop without accelerating fading.
- Documentation: Maintain a simple inventory of the cards, noting any rips, bends, or ink smudges. This helps if you decide to sell or trade later on, or if you simply want to recount the deck’s “battle scars” as part of your gaming lore.
- Environment: Keep the deck away from humid basements or stuffy attics. The 80s plastics and inks weren’t designed for climate control in the same way modern materials are. A consistent environment means fewer surprises when you pull out a card for a weekend game.
- Insurance: For high-end collections, you may want to insure your vintage sets. It’s not a necessity for casual players, but for serious collectors it’s a small peace-of-mind investment.

## Where to find and what to look for when buying
- Condition: Mint, near-mint, good, fair, and poor. The grade influences price more than you might expect. Look for straight edges, minimal creasing, and legible text.
