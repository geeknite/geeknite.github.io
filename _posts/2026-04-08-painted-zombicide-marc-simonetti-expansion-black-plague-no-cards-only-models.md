## The Models: Marc Simonetti’s Signature Style
Marc Simonetti’s sculpting style is unmistakable: heroically proportioned figures; elegantly articulated armor; dynamic fabric that folds with gravity; and a sense of motion captured in plastic. The Black Plague sculpts that come from his studio carry that story-forward vibe, a narrative energy that begs for dramatic lighting and careful layering.

When you approach painting these figures, you’re stepping into a slightly different painting workflow than usual. Because the models are larger than the base scale, you can emphasize the sculpt’s storytelling through color choices and shading. Here are some practical tips for working with his sculpts:

- Plan your narrative first: Decide which models belong to the main “crowd” and which are your showpieces; this helps you allocate color variety while preserving a cohesive look.
- Use high-contrast metal for armor: A glossy highlight on edge plates with a darker recess will create the sense of reflective surfaces typical of fantasy armor.
- Cloth is your friend: The capes and tunics provide large surface areas to practice gradient shading, especially if you variate color across the cape sections.
- Flesh and bone: Zombies can benefit from a primary selection (pale flesh) and a second emphasis color (green or purple). Complete the look with subtle veining and moisture effects on lips and gums.

What makes the Simonetti sculpts so satisfying to paint is their storytelling potential. The faces are expressive; the armor often bears ceremonial engravings; the weapons are ornate. You are not just painting a soldier; you’re painting a character who belongs to a legend inside a parchment of steel and bone.

If you’re curious to see how fans interpret similar sculpts, you can explore community threads that discuss color schemes, base textures, and finishing touches. The fan‑led discussion is a great resource for learning new techniques and seeing how others approach the same shapes.

## Painting Techniques and Practical Tips (Expanded)
A good painting plan begins with a robust base and ends with a crisp finishing pass. The no cards approach doesn’t change this; it just makes sure you enjoy every brush stroke and see progress across the entire squad.

- Step 1: Prime and base
  - Prime with a neutral gray or white, depending on whether you want the whites to be brighter or the shadows to be stronger.
  - Apply base coats that are slightly lighter than your final shade to avoid dull results after shading.
- Step 2: Add shading
  - Use washes or thinned paints to define folds, armor recesses, and weapon insets.
  - For metal, a blue‑green or brown wash can add depth and interest to the fabric.
- Step 3: Build depth
  - Add mid-tones in a few well-chosen layers.
  - Paint edges and raised areas with a brighter color to catch the light visually.
- Step 4: Highlights and edge highlights
  - Use sharp, linear highlights on armor and weapons to emphasize the metallic feel.
  - For the cloth, lighter gradient steps can emphasize creases and movement.
- Step 5: Finishing touches
  - Add weathering powders to the bases to simulate dust.
  - Apply a very light dry brush on the outer edges of the miniatures to catch the ambient light in photography.

Case Study: Knight Captain

The Knight Captain is a showpiece in the Simonetti lineup. The armor is not just metal; it’s a canvas for storytelling: scratch lines to show scuffs and battle marks; a heraldic emblem on the shield or cape can be highlighted with gold to invite the eye. For this piece, I prefer a two‑color metallic scheme, such as steel for the main armor and brass for trim, with blue cloth to create contrast.

- Armor: Gunmetal base, then a bright silver edge highlight.
- Trim: Warm brass or gold for regalia, with a deliberate dark wash to define engravings.
- Heraldry: A bright color like royal blue or crimson on the shield to draw the eye.
- Base: A stone platform to emphasize weight and presence.

Case Study: Plague Boss

The boss figures are the narrative anchors. They are often the most demanding in terms of shading complexity. A good approach for the Plague Boss is to lean into rich surfaces with a variety of textures: skin with pale, decaying patches; armor with scuffs and weathering; and an energy glow that can be used to suggest supernatural power. You can experiment with a palette that blends purple hues into sickly greens.

- Skin: Pale with hints of purple veins.
- Armor: Dull brass or gunmetal with a blue wash to bring cool tones into the piece.
- Energy glow: A subtle red or purple aura around the hands or eyes to indicate power.

## The No Cards Concept: Gameplay vs Display
A No Cards, Only Models edition is all about the display and the painting journey; it intentionally removes the gameplay layer to focus on the aesthetics and the craft. While this might seem a radical departure from a game expansion, it nonetheless serves as a compelling exercise in painting technique, color theory, and sculpture appreciation.

From a hobby perspective, painting the entire set as a unified colour story is a fantastic challenge. You can decide a dominant color for the knights, a complementary palette for the zombies, and a secondary narrative color for your bosses. The risk is that you might overwork the pieces and lose contrast if you apply too many glaze layers in a busy composition. The reward is a set of miniatures that looks cohesive on a shelf and can be photographed in a single frame with strong visual impact.

In addition, this approach invites you to experiment with nontraditional color pairs. For example, painting armor in a cool gray-blue with copper edging or gold trim can create a sense of ceremonial, ancient armor rather than generic battle kit, which suits the Marc Simonetti style very well. It’s a creative exercise that rewards thoughtful planning and patient brushwork more than any quick paint job.
