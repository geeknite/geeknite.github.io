## How to play a round
Go Fuc Yourself uses familiar Go Fish mechanics with a few tasteful adult twists. The game is designed to be flexible so you can play with a mix of new and seasoned players. Here is a quick overview of what a typical round looks like:

- Each player starts with a hand of cards. The size of the hand depends on your group size, but the typical range is around five to seven cards. The rest of the deck sits in the center, waiting for fate to decide your social life.
- On your turn you ask another player for a card rank. The goal is to collect sets of four cards with the same rank. Unlike the kid friendly version, the questions can involve adult topics, but everything stays within the realm of consent and social boundaries set by the group.
- If the asked player has any cards of that rank, they must give you all of them. If not, you must draw from the deck and say Go Fish with a wink and a pun. The pun is an essential part of the ritual; it signals that you are embracing the chaos with style.
- The moment you collect four of a kind, you lay down a set and draw from the deck to replenish your hand. The sets create a small victory tableau that looks nice, sounds satisfying, and occasionally triggers the next round of witty banter.
- The game continues until one player empties their hand or until the deck runs out. In either case, the player with the most complete sets wins. The victory condition is simple, the humor is where the game earns its replay value.

## Variants and house rules you can try
Every gamer knows that the real fun starts when you bend the rules until they sing. Here are a few variants that can spice up the session without breaking the game:

- The Gossip Round: Once per game, a player can trade a card face down and reveal a small secret to the table as a form of bluff synergy. The reveal must be approved by the table to avoid crossing lines and to preserve everyone’s dignity.
- The Power Card: Introduce a wild card that can swap entire hands between two players once per round. It can create dramatic comebacks and hilarious misplays, depending on how your table handles the chaos.
- Time Attack: Run a shorter session with a 20 minute timer. This version relies more on quick trades and zingy banter rather than long storytelling, which can be great for high-energy gatherings.

The joy of variants is that they let you tailor the tone to your crowd. A group that loves improv can lean into performance heavy rounds, while a more strategic group can stage a few rounds with tight rules to preserve tension.
