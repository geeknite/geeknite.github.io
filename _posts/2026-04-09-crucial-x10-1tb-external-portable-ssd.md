## Performance and Benchmarks
Speed is the name of the game for an external NVMe drive, and the X10 does not pretend to be shy about it. With USB-C 3.2 Gen 2x2 support, you are looking at theoretical peak speeds in the neighborhood of 2,000 MB/s. In real-world testing on a capable host, you can expect sequential reads near 1000–1050 MB/s and writes around 900–1000 MB/s. That’s not just enough to copy the entire library of your favorite sci-fi novels; it’s enough to move large video projects, game assets, and the occasional backup archive without bringing your workstation to its knees.

### Raw numbers and test conditions
- Sequential read: up to about 1050 MB/s
- Sequential write: up to about 1000 MB/s
- 4K random read IOPS: in the 100k-range
- 4K random write IOPS: in the 90k-range

These figures are achievable on a USB-C Gen 2x2 host with a sturdy, short cable that doesn’t pretend to be a rope. If you plug this into a USB-A port or a budget hub, you will obviously see a drop in performance. The lesson here is not to test a sports car in a standard schoolzone; use the right tool on the right road.

In day-to-day workflows, the X10 shines when handling large media files, local backups, or as a scratch disk for video editing. It is not a magic wand that fixes slow software or a poor drive health; it’s a dependable chassis that can move big chunks of data quickly when connected to a fast host.

For gamers and creative pros, the ability to shuttle terabytes of assets between machines means you spend less time staring at a progress bar and more time making art or finishing a project. The drive stays quiet during sustained transfers, with a negligible hum that most people will tune out after the first 30 minutes of use. It’s not an external soundbar for your desk; it’s a practical tool that respects your workspace and your wallet.
