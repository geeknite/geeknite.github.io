## Strategy and Deck Building Tips
The fun of a starter deck is not just in playing it as is but in using it as a springboard into more nuanced deck building. Here are practical ideas to help you get the most out of BT03 Cross Worlds without needing to become a full time scientist or voyage into a black belt level of rule mastery.

### Early Game Pressure
Your aim in the first three turns is to set up a reliable energy curve and apply pressure on the opponent. This means prioritising cards that either increase your energy production or threaten a cheap but meaningful attack on each active turn. The goal is not to end the game in the first move but to keep your opponent guessing and reduce their options for safe play. A fast tempo line can push you ahead by a significant margin if the opponent cannot stabilize quickly.

### Mid Game Stabilization and Board Quality
As the game progresses, your focus should shift toward maximizing card quality on the board. If you have a couple of midrange threats that can survive a round or two while still developing your board, you gain the luxury of choosing your battles. Mid game is where the deck begins to flex its true potential, as you combine small advantages into a compounding lead that your opponent struggles to erase.

### Endgame Scenarios and Finishing Burst
The finishing portion of a Cross Worlds strategy is not about a single killer combo. It is about maintaining a credible threat pressure while pushing a few clean, efficient turns that leave your opponent with diminishing options. The goal is to present a choice to your opponent that makes their life uncomfortable: either try to answer a credible threat with limited resources or accept that your next turn will likely seal the deal. A well planned endgame keeps you on the offensive and limits your opponent’s ability to rebound.

### Common Pitfalls to Avoid
As with any starter deck, expect to run into a few classic missteps. The most common include overcommitting on turns where you face a strong defensive response, underutilising your energy ramp because you fear leaving your board open, or trying to force a two card combo when a one card line would close the game sooner. The antidote is simple in theory and tough in practice: maintain situational awareness, respect your opponent's potential power plays, and keep a backup plan in your back pocket for those curveball moments that define tournaments.
