## Gameplay Mechanics: Core Rules and House Rules
UNO, at its core, is delightfully straightforward. You match color or number to lay down cards, you dash to change direction or skip players with action cards, and you aim to be the first to empty your hand while avoiding the dreaded UNO call misstep. The 2003 Mattel edition adheres to the classic rules—no fancy side quests, no digital timers, just cardboard chaos and social manipulation in a polite, often humorous fashion.

Key rules recap for new players:
- Each player starts with seven cards. On your turn, you must either match the color or the number of the top card on the discard pile or play a wild card to change the color.
- Skip makes the next player miss a turn.
- Reverse flips the play direction; in two-player games this effectively acts as a Draw Two for the other player, which is a neat conundrum.
- Draw Two forces the next player to draw two cards and skip their turn.
- Wild lets you declare the new color to continue the chaos; Wild Draw Four makes the next player draw four cards and skip their turn but can be challenged if played illegally.
- When you have one card left, you must shout UNO; if another player catches you forgetting, you’ll face a penalty in many home rule sets.

House rules vary widely from household to household, and the 2003 edition is forgiving enough to accommodate a few of them while preserving the core flow. Some families enjoy stacking Draw Two or Draw Four cards for dramatic finales, while purists adhere strictly to official rules and frown upon such shenanigans. Either way, the deck remains a flexible instrument for social fun and a little strategic mischief.

### Tips for smooth play
- Establish an agreed house rule about stacking Draw Twos and Draw Fours before the game begins.
- Decide how to handle challenges on Wild Draw Four; early agreement saves grief later.
- Use the color change strategically when it benefits your position but avoid overuse that makes you predictable.
