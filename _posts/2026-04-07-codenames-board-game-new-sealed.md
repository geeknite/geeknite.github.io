## The Core Mechanics: How Codenames Works

### Setup

- The board begins with a 5x5 grid of 25 word cards laid face up. These are the triggers for clues and guesswork.

- The Spymaster picks a random key card that marks which words belong to red, blue, neutral, and which tile is the assassin. The 25-key cards act as the hidden map for the round.

- The starting team is determined by the key card: red starts with more words to guess, blue has fewer, and the assassin and neutral words are distributed across the grid.

- Everyone else sits around the table with beverages and snacks that are not a hazard to the cards.

### Turn structure

- The Spymaster gives a one-word clue followed by a number. The clue should connect as many of the target words on the board as possible. For example: “Ocean 2” might hint at two words on the grid that have a marine connection (exact matches depend on your board and interpretation).

- The team then discusses and points to words on the grid that match the clue. They may discuss among themselves for knowledge and common sense.

- The Spymaster can be kind with their clue or cruelly ambiguous, but they cannot give any additional hints beyond the clue and the number.

- The round continues with the team selecting words to reveal. If a blue word is selected (assuming blue started), that word is crossed off the grid for the blue team. If a red word is revealed, the red team gains a step toward victory. If a neutral word is chosen, play simply passes to the other team. If the assassin word is revealed, the team that revealed it instantly ends in defeat.

- The goal is to identify all the red or blue words before the other team or before you cause an assassin reveal. The tension builds as the board changes with each guess, and the conversation becomes a kind of comedic microplay where the players attempt to coax others into word choices.

### The end conditions

- The game ends when all the red or blue words are found or when the assassin is revealed.

### Example Round

In a typical round, your Spymaster slides a one-word clue across the table and an audible groan erupts from teammates who realize that they must connect “Ocean” and “Boat” and maybe “Shore” with a single clue. The Word-Association Olympics begins. Some players will propose connections to literature, science, pop culture, or any creative tangent that makes sense. The best moment arrives when the team lands on a word that turns out to be an exact match for their clue, while a few others get close but miss the mark by a few degrees of semantic deviation.

## Strategy and Tips: Making You Look Smart (Even When You Feel Lost)

- The best clues are short and precise. A good clue connects two or three words with a strong but not overly obvious relation. If you can line up two or more words with a single strong association, you can swing the round in your team’s favor.

- Think about shared categories. If the board has clues like “Ocean,” “Shark,” and “Boat,” you might tie them to a background knowledge domain such as marine terms or popular culture.

- Avoid obvious pitfalls. If the board has an assassin or a lot of words that relate to your clue, be aware that a misstep can ruin you. The beauty of the Spymaster is risk management; cunning is optional but helpful.

- The art of crowd-sourced guesswork. Team members often propose links that the Spymaster had not expected. Embrace the spontaneity—some of the best moments are when the group discovers a novel link.

- The power of misdirection. A well-chosen clue may direct players to multiple words while accidentally guiding one to the assassin. The more you practice, the more you’ll be able to anticipate the board and protect your team.

- The advantage of a sealed box. The fact that the copy is new means you’re starting with pristine, untouched cards; the likelihood of wear or confusion with misprints is lower. Your clues can be sharper, your edges cleaner.

- Sorting your thoughts. If you’re playing with kids, you’ll want to keep your clues simple and direct. If you’re playing with adult friends who drool at puns, you can push the boundary a little more and watch the hilarity unfold.

- Timing matters. There’s no timer in standard Codenames, but you can set one for a party vibe. A 25-second per turn window keeps the energy high and prevents the discussion from dragging on.
