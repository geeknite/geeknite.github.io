## The gameplay loop: movement, exploration, and discovery
Ori is a game about movement as much as it is about story. The core loop involves exploring a map, acquiring new abilities, accessing previously unreachable areas, and returning to earlier sections to unlock hidden paths. The movement controls are a masterclass in well-calibrated physics: you feel the weight of each jump, the momentum of your dash, and the relief of landing softly on a mossy platform after a narrow escape from a stretching, branching corridor.

The upgrade system—the collection of energy cells, skills, and spirit orbs—feeds into a satisfying upgrade path. Each new ability opens up more of the forest, revealing additional lore bits and environmental storytelling. The Definitive Edition’s tweaks make these upgrades feel more meaningful; they often unlock not just new movement options but new traversal routes that alter your mental map of the forest. It’s a design decision that rewards curiosity and patience, two virtues that never go out of style in a good platformer.

Combat in Ori is not heavy on DPS; it’s about timing, positioning, and resource management. You’ll rarely find yourself in a pure brawl; instead, you’ll orchestrate a dance with the forest’s dangers, using light and shadow to your advantage. The boss encounters are some of the best moments in the game—apparent tests of skill that feel fair and earned, with mechanics that teach you what you need to know rather than bellow at you with inscrutable complexity.
