## What is Sushi Go! Pick & Pass?

Sushi Go! Pick & Pass is Gamewright’s playful riff on the card-drafting vibe that made its siblings famous. The goal remains delightfully simple: assemble the best sushi “menu” by drafting cards and passing the rest to the left. The mechanics are compact, approachable, and designed to generate smiles as often as scoring opportunities. The Pick & Pass moniker nods to the core action—each player selects a card (or set of cards, depending on the variant you’re playing) and then passes the remainder along to the next player. Rounds unfold with the speed of a conveyor belt and the drama of a group chat when someone realizes they passed the perfect dumpling to the rival who just needs one more card to hit the sashimi jackpot.

This version is designed for 2-5 players and is friendly to families, classrooms, and late-night gamers who want something quick, cheerful, and a little spicy in its tactical flavor. It captures the essence of the Sushi Go! family—colorful art, quick rounds, and the satisfying moment when you realize you’ve put together a killer combo—without requiring an advanced degree in math or a master’s degree in grumpy rule tweaks.

### Setup: Fast and Friendly

Setting up is gloriously straightforward. Shuffle the deck, deal a handful of cards to each player (the exact number scales with player count), place the score track within easy reach, and decide a starting player. The table stakes are low: you’ll have a quick briefing on the flow—repeat until the deck is exhausted, with everyone passing cards after each pick. ### How to Play: The Draft, the Pass, the Pep Rally

The core loop is simple: you have a hand of cards, you pick one, you pass the rest to the left. When everyone has revealed and passed, you score the round and set up for the next pass with the new hands. The game flows with a breezy momentum that makes the entire table lean in with anticipation rather than exhale with fatigue.

- Turn structure: On your turn, select a card (or pair, depending on your variant rules) from your hand and place it face down in front of you. Then pass the remaining cards to the next player. Once all players have revealed their picks, the hands rotate and you dive into the next round.
- Round cadence: You play through multiple rounds until the deck is exhausted. No long theoretical debates about sequencing your 14-card engine here; it’s all about pocket-sized decisions that add up. The speed keeps players engaged, and the boxy length of the rounds ensures you get to the end with a sense of triumph rather than a brain-cramp.
- Scoring cycles: Cards come with simple scoring conditions. You’ll be juggling sets, averages, and wildcards that push you toward collecting specific combos while trying to guess what others are chasing. It’s a balancing act—commit early to a plan or pivot when new information (or an excellent poop Emoji of a card) arrives on the table.

### Scoring: The Flavor-Packed Math That Feels Like Cheating at Times

Scoring is the real spice of the experience. Each card alliance has its own points, and your job is to assemble as many of the right combos as you can. Here are typical categories you’ll see, though note that individual print runs may tweak numbers slightly:

- Nigiri: A single card cluster worth a base number of points; multiple Nigiri cards amplify your total. The more you stack, the more you savor those points.
- Maki Roll: A track that rewards the player who ends with the longest Maki roll at the end of the round. It’s a micro-battle within each round that can swing early leads or force a strategic block move.
- Sashimi: A small-set collector’s dream. If you manage to grab three Sashimi cards, you’ll be showered with big points. It’s a high-risk, high-reward tactic that tests your ability to plan ahead and predict others’ drafts.
- Tempura and Dumplings: Classic, smaller collectibles that reward you for achieving a specific pair or stack. They’re the bread and butter of the midgame, giving you reliable footholds even if your big combos slip away.
- Pudding (if included): A longer-term scoring device that you tally at game-end. Pudding rewards can swing the final score and give players something to chase even as the rounds end.

The joy of Pick & Pass is that you’ll enjoy the same core math repeatedly but with different card distributions, which keeps the brain engaged without requiring a PhD in Sumurai Sushi or anything similarly silly. The game remains accessible while still offering satisfying, quasi-optimizing joy for those who enjoy chasing a perfect synergy or a perfectly denied combo.

### Strategy and Tactics: How to Build Your Peppered Plate of Points

- Early planning, late execution: Begin with flexible picks. Target a few core combos in the early rounds, but don’t lock yourself into a single path too early. The moment you see a rival eyeing your intended pattern, you can pivot and go after a secondary route that still preserves your total potential.
- Block and bluff (gently): There’s value in passing a card that could complete someone else’s set, especially if you can anticipate their next move. It’s not about being cruel; it’s about keeping your own score climbing while preventing your rival from sprinting ahead.
- Manage risk: If you’re behind on a major combo, you might pivot toward lower-risk, consistent gains rather than gambling on a big but fragile engine. It’s all about risk management and reading the table’s mood.
- Pacing and table talk: Because this is a fast title, use the downtime to chat. A quick joke about the “sushi stock market” or a faux trade negotiation can break a tense moment and remind everyone that the point of the game is to have fun.
