### Why this particular Power Card stands out
Power Cards in OverPower aren’t the same as typical spell or equipment cards you’d find in later collectible card games. They’re designed to be “dramatic” and broad, creating splashy moments that can swing a game in multiple directions. The Pick From List (LOD 5) card is emblematic of that era’s taste for dramatic choices. It invites the player to weigh several plausible outcomes, each with its own risk-reward profile. It’s not just about raw power—it’s about decision fatigue, the psychology of choosing under pressure, and the fun of improvisation when your deck has a little voice in it.

If you’re reading this and you’re thinking, “Hold on—that sounds like a generic choose-from-five card,” yes and no. The genius lies in the context: a five-way fork in the middle of a skirmish where timing, board state, and hero selection all matter. That’s where the LOD 5 designation becomes something you actually feel rather than read on a card. It’s the design equivalent of a boss fight with five different patterns. Pick one, and live with the consequence for the next few turns.

## The Card Itself: Pick From List (LOD 5) — What It Does, and How It Feels

### Card Text and Mechanics (Descriptive, Not Official)
In the OverPower universe, power cards usually hang a practical effect on you, whether it be a buff, a manipulation of an opponent’s plan, or a utility that lets you reshuffle the tactical dice of fate. The Pick From List (LOD 5) Power Card is, in simple terms, a menu card: when you play it, you reveal a list of five possible effects, and you choose one to resolve immediately (subject to the game’s normal timing rules).

- The five options are deliberately varied, offering a mix of offensive, defensive, and strategic options. Think of it as a Swiss-army knife that also asks you to pick a tool out of five. The challenge is not just which option you pick, but when you pick it. Some of the options scale with the current board, others are modifiers to your next turn, and a few push you toward a riskier path that could pay off spectacularly or fizzle out.
- The LOD 5 designation signals that you’re juggling a more complicated decision than a single-line card might require. In practice, players often paused for a breath, explaining the five different routes to the table as if they were presenting a mini-presentation on the ethics of heroism.
- The effect resolution typically occurs immediately after you pick, accelerating the pace of play and forcing opponents to adapt on the fly. You’ll often see players mutter, “Five choices, one gamble,” as they read the list aloud and weigh the future like a chess clock on a coffee binge.

If you want to play this in your own home, you’ll want to prepare a clean list of the five effects in advance. The card’s charm is in the surprise element—the moment you reveal the options, your opponent starts recalibrating their plan on the spot. It’s the kind of card that invites the table to lean in and whisper, “What are you going to pick?” rather than “What does this do again?”

### The Five Options: A Thematic Look (Not Official Text)
Because the exact wording can vary between print runs and regional editions, here’s a thematic, non-official sketch of what those five options might resemble, to give you a feel for the flavor:

1) Surge of Might: Boost a hero’s attack for one round, possibly changing a knockout into a finish. 
2) Tactical Retreat: Withdraw a hero to safety, resetting threats and allowing a strategic regroup. 
3) Heroic Instinct: Draw a card and reveal the top card of your deck, potentially triggering synergy with other Power Cards. 
4) Opponent’s Burden: Force an element of your opponent’s plan to backfire for a moment, such as discarding or delaying a move. 
5) Unforeseen Pivot: A wildcard effect that could be any one of a handful of small-but-meaningful changes to the current situation. 

If you’re curious about the actual precise text, you’ll have to track down a scanned copy or a local collector with a mint set. The beauty of OverPower is that the exact wording can be a little fuzzy in the memory of older players—the important part is the vibe: a strategic, three-dimensional choice that can ripple through several turns.

### Interaction with Other Cards: How Pick From List Plays with the Set
Power Cards aren’t standalone miracles; they sit inside a web of synergy with hero cards, sidekicks, and terrain effects (the latter being a mild anachronism from a game that clearly wanted to feel big and cinematic). With a Pick From List card, you often want to reserve it for a moment when you can maximize two or three combos at once:

- If you have a hero with a powerful but fragile combo, Surge of Might can turn a fragile lineup into a kill window.
- Tactical Retreat can save you when your field is overextended, letting you rebuild without conceding too much tempo.
- The Draw option (Heroic Instinct) is your engine—if you have other draw-enhancing cards, the synergy compounds, creating a cascade that feels almost modern in its sense of deck thinning and chain-calling.
- The Opponent’s Burden option is the oldest trick in the book: pressure your opponent at a key moment by disrupting their rhythm.
- The Unforeseen Pivot becomes the glue that binds the board state you want to force with careful card-cycling and resource management.

The result is a card that rewards situational awareness and a little memory discipline. You will remember those times when Pick From List flipped a losing board into a surprising comeback, and you’ll curse the times when a mis-hit choice led to a turn-of-events avalanche you didn’t want to unleash.

## Aesthetics, Art, and the Tangible Experience

OverPower cards were fairly straightforward in terms of art direction. The 1995 run leans into bold, glossy imagery with dramatic silhouettes and a sense of urgency that mirrors the era’s comics covers. The Pick From List card is not a visual showpiece in the same way that a epic battle card might be, but its presentation still carries the cinematic energy—the font choices, the five-option layout, and the color rhythm all push you to treat the moment as a plot beat rather than just a move in a game.

From a collector’s perspective, the Power Cards, including Pick From List, represent a certain era of print production. You can spot the differences between early print runs and later re-issues in minor details: cardstock thickness, the subtle glow on the card edges, and the wear patterns that come from decades of shuffling and stacking. If you’re into nostalgic display, you’ll want to photograph these alongside other Marvel prints, or better yet, build a small display shelf with a little stand to show off the hero art next to the power flavor text. Playtesting a Pick From List card is a little like playing a co-op adventure with a single-player twist. The tension comes from how you prioritize long-term goals against short-term gains. You might imagine a scenario where you’re on the cusp of a victory, and that choice could either confirm the win or shatter the plan in a single flip of the card. This is why the card remains a memorable artifact for fans. ## Collectibility, Rarity, and Market Perspective

The 1995 Fleer Marvel OverPower line is part of a broader wave of 1990s nostalgia—cards were printed in abundance, but scarcity appears in the most popular characters and the rare variants. The Pick From List (LOD 5) card lives in an interesting grey area: not the ultra-rare chase, but not a throwaway staple either. For collectors, it’s the kind of card you acquire because of an emotional resonance—how the five options could have turned a game, the memories of trading with friends, and the shared stories about those loud, crowded gaming sessions in college lounges or card shops after school.

If you’re evaluating value today, price tends to track condition, completeness, and how well you remember actually using the card in its original context. The nice-to-have factor is the art and the quirky, almost theatrical presentation—these are the sort of items that look great in a display case and fetch nostalgia smiles from fellow collectors.
