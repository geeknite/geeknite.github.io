H2: Performance — how fast is fast enough for real life?

Speed numbers are where these drives either earn a drink or earn a seat on the bench. We tested the X6 SE with a modern system on Windows and macOS, using a mix of large sequential workloads and real-world transfers (like dumping a whole 4K library or backing up a year’s worth of raw footage).

- Sequential performance (typical): Read around 900–1050 MB/s, Write around 800–1000 MB/s on a capable USB-C host. The numbers float with host controller, file size, and thermal conditions, but the trend is clear: you’re getting high-end consumer-grade speed without the premium-for-speed price tag.
- Random I/O: random read/write 4K IOPS sit in the tens of thousands on a good connection, which translates to snappy application launches and faster catalog browsing when your file structure becomes a monster that only a software engineer could love.
- Latency: sub-millisecond-ish reads in typical workloads; latency is generally not the bottleneck with modern external SSDs unless you’re hammering random small writes on a slow USB controller.

Comparison note: if you’ve used older USB 3.0 external drives, the improvement is frankly night and day. This is the kind of speed that actually feels useful for creative workloads rather than a marketing gimmick.

Benchmarking is always a fun exercise in context:
- Copying a 25 GB folder of mixed media to the X6 SE took roughly 5-7 minutes depending on file composition and computer bus saturation.
- From the drive to the host, we observed similar transfer times in that range, showing that the drive maintains bandwidth with sustained workloads rather than chugging along at the first sign of a long file path.
