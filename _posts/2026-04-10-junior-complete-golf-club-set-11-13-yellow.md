### Ergonomics and grip size
The grip size in junior sets is a crucial factor that many buyers overlook in favor of “cool design” or “lowest price.” Grip circumference should be comfortable for small hands—usually around 4.0 to 4.5 inches in diameter for 11–13-year-olds, but the exact dimension depends on hand size. A grip that’s too fat makes it hard to hold the club squarely, and a grip that’s too thin can lead to rapid wrist fatigue and awkward swings. The Yellow set we’re reviewing tends to offer a slightly oversized grip to accommodate growing hands while preserving control, a design choice that’s especially helpful for new golfers who are still discovering their swing plane.

Grip material matters too. Most junior sets use a resin-based wrap or a soft-tack compound that provides a gentle tack without being sticky in hot weather. The benefit is a reliable grip without requiring constant re-gripping from sweaty hands after three holes.

## Safety and age-appropriateness

Safety first. Junior clubs, especially when kids are growing as fast as a software release, can become a hazard if poorly sized. Here are some practical safety tips:

- Ensure the length is appropriate: the club should reach the base of the wrist when the kid stands straight with arms relaxed at the side. If the club head can poke the child in the thigh, it’s too long. If it’s a hair too short, the kid’s stance will be awkward and maybe dangerous on full swings.
- Check for sharp edges: mass-produced sets are engineered with rounded edges and protective finishes, but it never hurts to inspect the ferrules and any exposed metal for burrs.
- Proper storage: use a stand bag with padded straps to prevent the clubs from banging into each other during transit. A little padding goes a long way toward quiet practice sessions and less clanging when you roll the bag across the garage floor.
- Sunscreen and hydration: this is outdoor gear; sunglasses, sunscreen, and water bottles keep the session safe. If your kid is chasing a yellow ball, you’ll want to see their eyes behind those shades.

## Maintenance and care

Long-term value comes from maintenance. Here are a few easy steps to keep the Yellow set performing well:

- Wipe clubs after use to prevent dirt buildup and rust from moisture.
- Store in a dry place; avoid leaving the bag in a damp trunk overnight.
- Check grips for wear; replace grips as needed to maintain a comfortable feel and reliable grip.
- Use headcovers; not only do they protect the clubs, but they also make the set easier to identify in a crowded bag.
- Clean the shafts with a damp cloth to remove sweat and oils that can affect grip feel over time.

If you want to extend the life of your junior clubs, consider investing in a simple club-cleaning kit that includes a mild soap, a soft brush, and a microfiber cloth. It’s the sort of kit that makes you feel like a real-life golf tech, minus the lab coat and the “don’t touch the balls” sign.

## How to pick the right size and setup for your kid

Choosing the right size is more art than science, but there are a few rules of thumb:

- Have your kid stand naturally, with arms relaxed at their sides. The grip end should be around the kid’s hip height for most drivers and irons. If you stand the club up against their hip and there’s a comfortable amount of space, you’re in the right zone.
- Check the stance. If the kid has to bend too much to reach the ball, the club is too long. If they’re standing straight and the club head is hovering well above the ball, it’s too short.
- Consider adjustment options. If you anticipate growth over the next year or two, a set with adjustable length or upgradeability will save you from buying a new set every season.

And yes, you’ll still want to bring the kid to the shop to try the clubs in person if possible. The feel of a grip and the balance of a club can be hard to judge online, and nothing beats the “this feels right” moment when you hold the club in your own hands.
