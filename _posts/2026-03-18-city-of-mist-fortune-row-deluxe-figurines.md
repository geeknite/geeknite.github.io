# City of Mist: Fortune Row - Deluxe Character Figurines

City of Mist fans are no strangers to neon rain, trench coats, and the sense that every alleyway hides a story waiting to be told. The Fortune Row, a rogues' gallery with more swagger than your local barista, has finally stepped into the light in deluxe figurine form. Are these five little statues the perfect companion to your noir tabletop sessions, or are they just shiny distractions that look great next to your commemorative city map}? Either way, we’re here to unbox, inspect, and—most importantly—groan at the puns these figures inspire.

## Unboxing and first impressions

### Packaging: a noir present in a glossy shell

Opening the Fortune Row deluxe box is almost a miniature narrative in itself. The outer sleeve features a neon-drenched skyline with rain-slicked streets, punctuated by a single, dripping question mark. Inside, each figure rests on a foam cradle, with a glossy insert that hints at their character traits—like fortune-tellings, high-stakes gambles, and morally questionable decisions. It’s the kind of packaging that makes you feel you’ve just unwrapped a story, not just a bunch of plastic people (though, let’s be honest, the plastic is pretty darn nice).

### First touch: weight, balance, and the “ooh” factor
The figures feel sturdier than expected for resin-and-paint pieces. Weight distribution is well handled; bases are stable enough to survive the occasional table-nudge without a dramatic migration to the carpet of doom. The paintwork immediately signals that this is a collector-focused release rather than a casual “display if you have a dog who won’t touch your minis.” In short: they pass the “do not crumble when you sneeze near them” test with flying colors.

### The first glance SFX: design language
The sculptors lean into the noir-energy of City of Mist with sleek silhouettes, crisp lines, and subtle textures that suggest rain-soaked fabrics, leather, and arcane tattoos that you’ll need a magnifying glass to truly appreciate. The five figures present a spectrum from enigmatic to flamboyantly theatrical, which is exactly the mood you want when your GM calls for a dramatic reveal in the middle of a chase scene.

## Sculpt quality, materials, and craft details

### Materials and durability
These are resin-based pieces with enamel paints, not cheap chump-change plastics. The resin gives sharp micro-details—think wrinkles in a scarf, glint in a monocle, and the precise edge of a blade that might never draw blood in your campaigns but definitely draws admirers on your shelf. The enamel paint offers clean color separation with subtle shading that makes fabrics and metallics pop without looking like a toy store threw up on your figurines.

### Detail fidelity: the good kind of sharp
If you zoom in (carefully, because you might want to preserve the finish), you’ll notice micro-chisel detail in the hair, capes, and jewelry. The faces are characterful rather than photorealistic, which is exactly what you want for a tabletop display: expressive enough to give you a vibe, subtle enough not to terrify your painting skills if you want to tweak them later.

### Base design: support that tells a story
Each figure sits on a circular stand that’s been etched with a tiny emblem related to that character’s gimmick. The bases aren’t just cosmetic—some of them include a faint, engraved street motif that hints at the Fortune Row’s nocturnal enclave. It’s a small flourish, but it helps the display feel coherent rather than a random grab-bag of heroes and villains.

## Paint, finish, and displayability

### Colorwork and panel lining
The color scheme leans into the neon-noir palette: deep blues, purples, electric pinks, and the occasional brass touch for metalwork. The shading is deliberate but refined, giving depth to folds and creases without veering into “every crevasse is a separate gradient.” The panel lining is tasteful, which is a win—too heavy, and you risk turning the figures into toy-store diorama cats; too light, and the details vanish.

### Weathering and character mood
A few weathered accents—scuffs on boots, rain-slick shoulders, and the occasional chipped edge on a weapon—lend authenticity without looking like the figures came straight from a 1990s tabletop game refight. If you’re into making your noir figures look like they’ve survived a city-shaking chase, you’ll appreciate these subtle touches.

### Display practicality
The figurines sit well on display shelves, office desks, or the infamous “nerd corner” of your living room. They pair nicely with photos, posters, and even the occasional crumbly snack you’ll inevitably drop while brooding over what your character’s next move should be. The finish resists fingerprints better than you’d expect for painted resin, which means fewer frantic wipe-downs after friendship-bracelet-level photoshoots.

City of Mist is all about myth and memory—stories you tell to make sense of the chaos. ## Gameplay vs. display: do you need them for play?

If you’re playing City of Mist with a rules-light, storytelling-first vibe, these figurines aren’t necessary for gameplay. They don’t come with official stat lines, nor do they alter dice outcomes or card draws. In that sense, they’re best described as high-quality display pieces that bring a tactile, visual dimension to the city’s rogues and icons. If your table embraces the “show, don’t tell” ethos, these can become a delightful focal point during the “character reveal” moments—especially for new players who are still wrapping their heads around the power of mythic archetypes.

However, if you’re a collector or you want a dramatic centerpiece for photo sessions, this set shines. The balance between display quality and practical tabletop presence is surprisingly good for a collectible item at this price tier. It’s not a “board game upgrade” in the most practical sense, but it’s a worthy companion for the mood and storytelling that City of Mist embodies.

### Price and tiering expectations
Deluxe figurines—five characters, with display bases and neon-noir charm—sit at a premium end of the hobby spectrum. If you want an occasional “wow” moment on your shelf or in your campaign’s intro cutscene, they deliver.

### Longevity and collectibility
The resin-and-enamel finish holds up well with careful handling and occasional dusting. The sculpts don’t rely on trendy paint schemes that go out of fashion after a season; they tap into a timeless noir aesthetic that should age gracefully with your collection. In a field crowded with flashy decals and gimmick finishes, Fortune Row’s mature design choices feel durable and tasteful.

### Resale and value proposition
If you’re the type who likes to curate a curated library of premium figurines, this set can be a long-term investment for your display cabinet. If you’re more of a “one-and-done” buyer who expects every purchase to sprint toward a game session, you might view them as a premium ornament rather than essential gear. The value, as with many collectibles, rests less in immediate gameplay utility and more in the storytelling aura they generate over time.

## Tips for painting, display, and care

- Protective storage: When not on display, consider a small glass cabinet or a padded box to prevent dust buildup and accidental knocks.
- Cleaning: Lightly dust with a soft brush. Avoid aggressive solvents; if you need to refresh the sheen on armor, a quick wipe with a microfiber cloth is usually enough.
- Display dioramas: Pair with a faux-stone or rain-soaked backdrop to maximize the cinematic effect. A tiny LED strip behind the display can mimic neon signs and amplify the mood.
- Photography tips: Use a shallow depth of field to emphasize the character silhouette, then reveal the details with gentle post-processing that doesn’t flatten the painting’s depth.
