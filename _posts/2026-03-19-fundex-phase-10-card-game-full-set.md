## How the Phases Work in the Full Set
### A Quick Primer for the Uninitiated
Phase 10 is a game about completing specific sets of cards in a sequence of ten friendly challenges. Each phase is a pattern you must lay down on the table, and the phases become progressively trickier as you move forward in the game. In the Full Set, you will encounter a robust list of phases, each with its own requirements, some of which lean into the possibility of flexible interpretation. That means you might be forced to improvise a little, which is half the fun and half the reason to bring a notepad to the table to keep track of who owes what to whom after multiple rounds.

The core mechanic revolves around drawing, discarding, and reorganizing your hand to assemble the required phase on your turn. If you can do this, you advance. If not, you pass that round, keep a growing pile of cards you are trying to forget, and you watch the other players lock down a phase that makes you rethink your life choices. It is competitive in a friendly way, like a group of friends trying to decide who gets to brag about finishing their tax forms the fastest.

### Common Phase Archetypes in the Full Set
- Two sets of three (or a similar two-group requirement) that you can assemble with cards of the same color or varied colors, depending on the specific phase
- Runs of consecutive numbers across colors, which encourage players to watch card flow rather than simply stacking up power cards
- Mixed phase patterns that require both sets and runs, nudging your decision-making toward balance rather than brute force

These archetypes are designed to reward planning and risk assessment. However, the Full Set also introduces some variability with wild and skip cards that can shift the momentum in surprising ways. It is not a stealthy trick; it is a design choice to keep the game lively and to prevent a late-night stalemate where one player clings to a single strategy like a lifebuoy on a calm but unremarkable sea.
