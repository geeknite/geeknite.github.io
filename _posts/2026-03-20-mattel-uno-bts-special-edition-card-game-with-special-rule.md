### The Special Rule
Here comes the hook called the `Special Rule` in BTS land. This is the rule that changes tempo, not just tempo of the music. In practice, when the Special Rule card is in play, a player can trigger a mini-challenge that often involves performing a quick BTS-inspired action (a two-second dance move, a quick chorus line cue, or a signature pose). If you are someone who hates dancing in front of friends, this rule can be safely ignored or toned down with a house rule. ## How to Play with the Special Rule
### Setup and Player Count
The BTS UNO edition supports 2-10 players, which makes it a natural fit for a game night of friends, family, roommates, or that one person who refuses to admit they are not a winning spreadsheet wizard. Each player starts with seven cards, standard UNO rules apply for the draw pile, and the game proceeds with the usual color/number matching. The similarity to classic UNO is intentional: you recognize the rhythm, even if the deck wears a different outfit.

### Turn Flow
On your turn, you must play a card that matches either color or number, just like normal UNO. If you cannot play, you draw one card, per standard rules. The twist is a potential activation of the Special Rule when the designated card appears or when players opt into it via the cue cards. Triggering the Special Rule adds a shout-out moment, a little performance, or a playful challenge. If the group agrees to a lenient tempo, the performance can be treated as part of the fun rather than a penalty. If someone is camera shy or anti-dance, the house rule can convert the moment into a simple team‑based challenge: the team that loses the round completes a quick BTS trivia question instead of performing.

### Endgame and Penalties
As with regular UNO, you shout UNO when you have one card left and win by playing your last card. The Special Rule can amplify the endgame: last-minute group dances, a chorus line reminder, or a cheeky pose that gets captured on a phone camera (consenting participants only). For families with younger players, you can swap the on-stage moment for a high-five or a thumbs-up. The point is to keep it light, fast, and inclusive rather than turning it into a talent show.

## House Rules and Variants to Try
- The Encore Minute: every time the Special Rule is triggered, the group has exactly one minute to perform or trivia before continuing. This keeps the energy high but prevents the night from turning into a full-blown talent contest.
- The Color Shift: impose a rule where, after a color change, the next player must describe their chosen color in a fun, BTS-esque way. It’s silly, it’s light, and it’s surprisingly memory-friendly.
- No-Dance Option: for players who opt out, a simple silent clap is permitted as an alternative to dancing or singing. It’s respectful, quick, and still produces a moment of shared humor.
