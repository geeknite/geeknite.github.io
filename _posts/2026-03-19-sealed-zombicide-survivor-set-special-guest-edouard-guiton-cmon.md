## Unboxing and First Impressions
Opening a sealed Zombicide box is a little like opening a door to Narnia if Narnia smelled faintly of plastic and ambition. The shrink wrap crackles, the box art stares back at you with a confidence that only official license art can muster, and the little plastic trays inside promise a mini diorama of doom. In this Survivor Set, the focus is on a balanced crew: a handful of survivors with distinct silhouettes, a pair of particularly gnarly-looking zombies, and a few token extras that scream “use me in a campaign tonight, or else.” The first unboxing impression is all about build quality and weight. If you have a large kitchen scale, you can practically weigh the shelf-life of your entire gamer night in grams.

The survivors themselves sport Edouard Guiton’s signature playful design—sharp lines, dynamic poses, and a level of character that makes it hard to decide which miniature to paint first. The sculpts capture that CMON aesthetic: almost too much detail for the lazy hobbyist, just enough for the serious painter who likes to cram a tiny world into a 28-millimeter footprint. Characterful expressions, practical gear, and enough personality to give your table talk a boost during setup. And yes, Edouard’s influence is visible in the silhouettes—more unique silhouettes than your standard generic survivor cadre, which is a win for players who want to tell their crew apart without having to memorize the color-coded bases.

The zombies look the part: hulking, slow, and surprisingly expressive for being basically walking death on sprue. They’re not just walk-bread; they’re the kind of undead that makes you pause and wonder if you left your own character in the car to go to the fridge for a snack. The quality here is consistent with CMON’s recent standards: crisp sculpts, clean mold lines, and a paint-friendly texture that invites highlight passes and dry-brush experiments. If you’re a painter, this set is a delight. If you’re a gamer who wants to throw a sheet of tokens and a couple of dice at the table and start blasting zombies, you’ll still be satisfied by the crisp cards and the crisp punch of the board game components.

## Rules and gameplay notes
Zombicide remains the cooperative puzzle where you balance risk, resource management, and the timing of your big-chain reactions. The Survivor Set doesn’t reinvent the wheel so much as reframe the wheel with Edouard Guiton’s personality attached to it. The survivor cards will typically feature unique abilities, flavor text, and synergy with the rest of the squad. You’ll want to pay attention to how these new characters interact with the existing rules: action economy, card draws, and the all-important “end of turn” phase where you tally your zombie count.

If you’re new to Zombicide, think of it as a tactical board game with a shared objective: survive the round, complete the objective, and avoid getting your crew devoured. There are plenty of mini objectives that can change the tempo of the game: rescue survivors, secure a safe house, retrieve a crucial artifact, or simply clear a path through the horde. The new set adds fresh flavor through its characters and scenarios, offering new tactical combos for experienced players and a gentle ramp for newcomers.

For those who like to obsess over rules minutiae, you’ll find that the new cards come with updated text and revised clarifications. The presence of Edouard’s signature style shines through the narrative flavor on the cards and the new ability interactions. It’s not a radical redesign of existing mechanics, but it does give players who enjoy character-driven play a new hook to build around.
