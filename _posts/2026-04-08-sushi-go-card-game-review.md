## How to play in a wink

Sushi Go! is all about choosing a card each turn, passing the rest, and watching your future self hope for the right synergy on the next draw. The flow is a loop of quick decisions and swift handoffs, designed to prevent analysis paralysis and maximize table chatter.

### Setup

- Shuffle the deck and deal each player a fixed hand size. The standard game uses the same number of cards per player per round, and the round structure is built so the game ends after three passes.
- Each player keeps their hand secret from the others, because a little mystery makes the next pass feel electric.
- Set aside a space for your collected cards as you draft and score.

A note on player counts: as soon as you cross into three or more players, the decision space grows, and so does the excitement. With two players, the game becomes a sly duel of memory and prediction; with five, it’s a carnival of scuttling hands and accidental malapropic banter about “Which fish is that again?”

### Turn structure

- Everyone selects one card from their hand and places it face down in front of them.
- All players reveal their chosen cards simultaneously.
- The chosen cards are added to your scoring area for this round, and the remaining cards in your hand are passed to the next player.
- The process repeats until all cards are exhausted from every hand.

That “pick one, pass the rest” mechanic is tiny but mighty. It creates a constant tension between what you want to keep and what you’re worried your neighbor will nab, all while you get to pretend you’re negotiating a treaty with a plate of sushi-themed glory.

### Scoring

- Nigiri cards provide base points. Wasabi can triple the value of your next Nigiri if you manage to chain them—so planning a little timing drama can pay off in a big way.
- Maki cards award points for having the most Maki. In a tie, a small advantage might go to one side or another depending on the round.
- Tempura pays off with pairs; Sashimi pays off with triples. The big payoff arrives when you orchestrate a multi-card combo that makes your table mates mutter, “How did they pull that off again?”
- Pudding cards don’t contribute to score until the end of the game, but they can swing the final tally in surprising ways. Pudding is the plot twist you didn’t know you needed.

### End of round and game end

- After three rounds, Pudding scores are tallied, and the total pudding count becomes a potential swing factor.
- The player with the highest cumulative total wins the game. It’s rare that a lead is unassailable early, which keeps everyone clinging to a glimmer of hope until the final shower of points lands.
