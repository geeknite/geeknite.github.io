Performance and specifications
--------------------------------
This is where the X10 shines on paper and, to some extent, in practice. The spec sheet lists 2,100 MB/s and 2,000 MB/s for read and write respectively, tied to USB-C 3.2 Gen 2 connectivity. In real-world terms, that’s fast enough to copy entire 4K video projects, your Steam library, or tens of thousands of RAW image files in a single second-like memory sprint. We’ll break it down into practical tests and what you can actually expect.

- Sequential read/write: Expect around 2.0–2.1 GB/s reads and roughly 1.8–2.0 GB/s writes on a capable USB-C 3.2 Gen 2 port with a balanced cable. If you’re plugged into a USB-A ecosystem, you’ll see a drop-off to well under the 2 GB/s threshold, which isn’t surprising given USB-A limitations.
- Random I/O: For game assets, large photo libraries, and video editing caches, the random 4K-64K performance matters more than raw sequential speed, and the X10 handles these with a steady hand, though it’s not a tiny NVMe drive in terms of latency. Expect snappy response for loading assets and editing timelines on the road.
- Thermals: The drive remains cool under typical workloads. It won’t glow like a starship’s engine, but it won’t fry your palm either during extended transfers. If you’re doing sustained 4K video cuts, you might notice a mild warmth after longer sessions, but nothing alarming.

Compatibility and USB-C considerations
---------------------------------------
- Compatibility: Works with Windows, macOS, and Linux systems that support USB mass storage devices. For macOS users, you’ll need to consider formatting if you need TRIM or APFS-specific optimizations; otherwise, HFS+ or exFAT will get the job done for cross-platform portability.
- Cables and ports: To hit the full 2,100 MB/s speeds, use a USB-C to USB-C 3.2 Gen 2 cable connected to a compatible host or docking station. If you’re on a laptop with USB-C but a slower controller, you’ll still see excellent performance, just not the peak advertised numbers.
- Power delivery: Portable SSDs like the X10 are bus-powered, drawing power from the USB port. In practice, this means you won’t need an external power brick, but if your laptop is in a power-saving mode or the USB port is unusually throttled, you could see reduced performance. Nothing out of the ordinary for bus-powered storage, but keep it in mind during long, sustained backups.

Practical tips for buyers
-------------------------
- Check your host USB-C port: If you want to hit the 2,100 MB/s number, you’ll need a USB-C Gen 2 or better controller in your laptop or dock.
- Use a good cable: Don’t cheap out on cables when you’re trying to maximize speed. The wrong cable can cap your throughput at a fraction of the read speed.
- Consider your workflow: If your day-to-day is heavy editing or large backups, the X10’s speed will save you time. If you mostly copy small files and occasionally back up, you’ll still enjoy quick transfers.
- Cross-platform data handling: If you’re using Windows and macOS in a mixed environment, use exFAT for the drive to avoid filesystem headaches. If you need native macOS performance, format for APFS or use a cross-platform approach with a compatible cross-OS tool.
