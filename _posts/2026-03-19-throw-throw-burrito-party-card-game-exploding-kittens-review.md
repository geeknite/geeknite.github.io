## What is Throw Throw Burrito?

Throw Throw Burrito is a party card game from the creators of Exploding Kittens. The core mechanic is a mashup of memory matching and dodgeball, played with two plush burritos and a deck of cards. Each player tries to collect pairs of identical cards while avoiding cards that trigger a burrito duel. When a burrito duel card appears, players fling their burritos at each other and, ideally, avoid hitting their teammates in the process. It is chaotic, energetic, and wonderfully absurd—the kind of game that turns a quiet living room into a makeshift arena, complete with referee shouts and the occasional dramatic tumble into a couch-laden pit of victory and defeat.

This is not a game for silent contemplation. It is a game for yelling, laughing, and secretly hoping your friend loves you enough to not retaliate after you whiff a throw and accidentally bean the cat-shaped lamp. The spectacle is the point, and the card art leans into the goofy voice acting and silly punny names that Exploding Kittens fans know and love.

## Setup and Basic Rules

Setup is a breeze, which is exactly what you want when you are surrounded by people who think a burrito is an appropriate meditation tool. Shuffle the deck, deal a handful of cards to each player, and lay the burritos within reach of everyone. The objective is simple: collect pairs by playing matching cards and avoid incurring penalties that lead to a burrito duel. The twists come fast and furious: players can surprise each other with attack cards, dodge cards, swap cards, and, yes, cause chaotic burrito duels that evoke a certain primal giggle you thought was buried under a pile of work emails.

A typical turn involves playing a card from your hand to either collect a match or initiate a burrito duel. If you reveal a burrito duel, you and the other players may have to fling burritos at one another based on the card instructions. Dull, you say? Not for the players who love dramatic flair and the occasional dramatic fall into a chair or a cat post that looks dangerously like a throne. It is messy, ridiculous, and glorious in the way only a game that involves fake food can be.

### How to Win
The winner is the player who has collected the most pairs by the end of the round, all while surviving a flurry of burrito duels and not losing their sense of humor. The game rewards bold plays and requires you to gauge your opponent’s temperament: are they the type who will yell “incoming burritos” and then perfectly dodge? Or are they the type who will shrink away in fear and drop a burrito into their own lap? The social deduction aspect is light, but the mischief is thick enough to be felt across the room.

## Strategy: When to Hold Them, When to Roll with the Burrito

Let us be clear: the strategic depth is not the point here. The fun of Throw Throw Burrito arises from knowing when to push for a match and when to sprint toward the escape route. However, a few strategic tips help you maximize your laughs and your odds of survival:

- Observe the tempo: If the table is in high-energy mode, you can be bolder with your plays. If the room is quiet and tense (the kind of quiet that follows a failed dodge), you might want to slow down and savor the moment, letting your opponents reveal their plans.
- Manage your space: Keep the burritos within reach, but not so close that you are an obvious target. The best players treat the burritos as extensions of their mind games, using the prospect of a throw to mislead opponents about their next move.
- Bluff and misdirect: Part of the fun is pretending you are about to go all-out on a puppy-dog meme throw, only to pivot into a strategic card play that saves you from an imminent duel. The audience loves a good feint, and your friends will cheer for you even as they plan their revenge.
- Team up (slightly): In some rounds, pairing up against a louder player can be entertaining. It is not cheating; it is theater. The goal is to maximize laughter while preserving some friendly rivalry.
- Card memory matters: While this is not a pure memory game, tracking which cards have already appeared helps you anticipate whether a duel is likely next. It is not foolproof, but it adds a layer of strategy that keeps the game fresh after multiple rounds.
