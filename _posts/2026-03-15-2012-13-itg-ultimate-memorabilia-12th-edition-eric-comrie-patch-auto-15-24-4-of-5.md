## Autograph and Patch Quality: A Closer Look
The autograph itself, in many of these ITG products, is hit-or-miss on the exact pen stroke. This particular Eric Comrie signature reads well enough that you won't need a magnifying glass to tell who signed it—though some collectors still prefer to zoom in with a phone camera to confirm legibility. The patch quality matters, though; if you are paying for a 15/24 piece, you want a patch that shows a substantial chunk of jersey and not a sliver that would require you to squint to deduce the color blocks.

In person, the patch presence matters more than the patch color. A nice patch with vibrant color that is clearly from the team jersey increases desirability, while a tiny, vague patch can dampen the perceived value even if the serial number is scarily low. This card hits a nice middle ground: a noticeable patch with a color block pattern that hints at authenticity and care in design.

## Grading, Authentication, and Authentication Denial or Validation
If you are a grader by trade or hobby, you know the pain and joy of authentic autographs and patch cards. The ITG line has a history of strong design but not always a consistent grading outcome across pop reports. For a patch auto, you want to verify two things: autograph legibility and patch integrity (no loose threads or patch fraying). It helps to have a professional grading service inspect it before you commit to a flip or hold.

In the absence of a formal grading service, you can still perform a basic authentication routine: compare the signature style to known exemplars of Eric Comrie's autograph from similar ITG releases, check patch color block pattern against the jersey era, and verify serial numbers by cross-checking the card's numbering that ITG included in their product sheet. It won't guarantee a PSA 10, but it will help you sleep better at night if your display shelf is near your sofa where you binge late-night card shows.

## Market Value: Price Per Pixel and Per Patch
Markets for vintage-ish patch autos can swing wildly depending on the day of the week and whether you catch a collector with a caffeine high. The 15/24 serial number is a strong selling point; it conveys scarcity and a sense of exclusivity. However, Eric Comrie's career trajectory can affect price; if he becomes a perennial backup goaltender, the card may still retain its premium due to the patch autography, but the market might be less forgiving than the more famous star players.

When evaluating value, consider these factors:
- Serial number scarcity: 15/24 is attractive; 15 is not the first two digits in a cookie recipe, but it’s close.
- Patch size and color: multi-color patches fetch higher values; a patch with meaningful color block is more desirable.
- Autograph visibility: legible, clean autographs perform better in resale.
- Overall condition: corners, edges, surface; the premium stock helps, but wear can still degrade value.

As always, do not rely on one listing. Compare several online marketplaces, take note of price tracks, and calibrate based on the card's condition, nation of sale, and whether the listing includes a certificate of authenticity.

## Display, Storage, and Presentation Tips
This card deserves a display case or a top loader with a thick outer sleeve to preserve the glossy finish and foil accents. Avoid direct sunlight for long durations, as light can cause colors to fade, especially on the foil accents. Consider a magnetic-pinch presentation in a small acrylic stand if you want it to be the centerpiece of a desk or shelf. Pro tip: keep moisture away; a dehumidifier in your card room can do wonders to maintain surface integrity over the years.

If you are feeling fancy, pair it with a small digital frame that rotates your top five patches and autographs. The Geeknite showroom of the future is a wall of patch autos with matching LED backlights; this is a card that could lead that parade.
