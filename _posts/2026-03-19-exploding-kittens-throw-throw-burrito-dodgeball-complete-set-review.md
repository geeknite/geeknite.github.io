## How to Play: The Two Modes, The One Party Atmosphere

The Complete Set essentially gives you two distinct, chaotic, but enjoyable experiences:

### 1) Throw Throw Burrito Mode (Two-Player to Party-Size)
In this mode, players sling the foam burritos at each other while trying to avoid drawing cards that trigger “explosion” events. It’s a speed-reading exercise with fog-of-war: you sprint through the deck, you flip a card, you shout a dare, and you fling a burrito in your friend’s direction with a comedic flourish that would make a slapstick comedy director proud.

Key mechanics include timed rounds, a handful of “dodging” cards, and “flip” cards that ensure you’re constantly adjusting your strategy on the fly. It’s not a game of perfect aim; it’s a test of who can maintain balance while midair burrito trajectories threaten to unleash chaos in your living room. The learning curve is gentle enough for casual players but the chaos keeps the more competitive folks engaged.

### 2) Dodgeball Mode
This is the flip-side of the Burrito mode, where the cardboard and foam collide in a more sports-centric, albeit cartoonish, experience. Teams are formed, rules become more about dodging, catching, and not stepping on last night’s pizza slice, and the game meanders into a friendly competition where you’re not just throwing stuff—you’re playing strategy as a sport that exists solely in a living room.

What stands out here is the social dynamic. You’ll find yourself negotiating peels of laughter with the same intensity you’d normally reserve for a game night staple like Codenames or a big party board game. The set pieces—the burritos, the cards that cause “explosion” events, and the occasional multipliers—don’t just slow you down; they force you to adjust and improvise in real-time, which is essentially the essence of good party gaming.

To help with human factors, we’ve included visual cues in the deck: the “hot zone” cards that encourage you to aim for the far side of the room without becoming a hazard, and “safe zone” cards that remind players to pause, breathe, and gel with the chaos. If you’re a control freak, you’ll be tested; if you’re a chaos theorist, you’ll thrive.

For a more structured breakdown, here are a few quick rules-tips you’ll want to keep handy:
- Always designate a “ref” to manage the timer and ensure the burritos stay in their lanes. The ref also acts as a gentle adjudicator for disputes over “was that a catch?”
- Consider setting up a soft barrier or cleared boundary to reduce slip hazards. Your ankles will thank you after the first 10 minutes of near-miss hilarity.
- Don’t be afraid to create silly house rules for your group. The game’s humor thrives when you lean into the ridiculousness rather than try to police every moment.

## Flow, Pace, and Strategy: How to Maximize Your Fun

The tempo of Throw Throw Burrito and Dodgeball is the gift that keeps on giving and the headache that your landlord might not appreciate. You bounce between moments of frantic sprinting and purely ridiculous “Did I just win by catching a burrito in mid-air?” glory. Here are some practical strategies to help you squeeze maximum mileage out of your two-player-to-small-team sessions.

- Keep your eyes on the board and your burrito in motion. The joy of this set is that, even when you’re essentially failing at a toss, you’re still delivering a performance that’s entertaining to everyone else.
- Coordinate with teammates for the dodgeball mode. It’s not just about throwing; it’s about baiting opponents, creating openings, and shouting dramatic martial phrases to increase the vibe.
- Use misdirection. If you pretend you’re about to sling to your left, you’ll often cause your opponent to overcommit to a block, opening up a better shot elsewhere.
- Don’t sweat the rules too hard. The beauty of this set lies in the chaos. If a moment feels right for ignoring a minor rule for a moment, do it—so long as the group agrees and laughs. Gaming is a social contract with built-in exceptions for fun.

We also experimented with different room layouts. A larger room makes it easier to swing wide, which increases the comedic factor; a smaller space makes it a speed-run of who can dodge the fastest and who ends up wearing a burrito like a victory sash. Both variants are enjoyable, but the latter tends to produce the most fist-pump moments.
