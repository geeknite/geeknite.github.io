## Collectibility, Grading, and Care: How to Treat This Like a Precious Artifact
Near Mint is a tricky beast. It requires a careful eye and consistent lighting. Here are practical steps to verify and preserve a Gloom AR 109/108 SV3 in near-mint condition:

- Inspect under bright, neutral light. Look for whitening at the corners or edge scuffs. In a holo card, check for surface scratches that disrupt the holo pattern. A dull edge is a sign of circulation, not a badge of honor.
- Slab if possible. Professional grading adds a level of credibility that’s often worth the fee if you’re considering selling or trading high-value items. The grade will be more than a number; it’s a promise to a future buyer that you didn’t roll out an ape-sized damage dice while transporting it.
- Store safely. Use top-loaders, then a sleeve, and then a solid binder page. For Japanese cards, some collectors prefer semi-rigid sleeves to preserve the border. If you can invest in a proper binder with acid-free pages, you’ll outlive several generations of your own card hobby ramp-up.
- Temperature and humidity matter. Avoid damp basements and sauna sauna experiments. The card hates moisture—unless you’re trying to create the legendary “water-logged holo” misprint, which is a thing that exists in myth and overpriced eBay listings.

Pricing and market dynamics are volatile, especially for SV3 variants and language-specific releases. Japanese cards can carry a premium due to print run size, aesthetics, and collector demand for language-specific artwork. The price trajectory can be influenced by nostalgia cycles, appearances in tournament promo decks, and the sheer whim of collectors who decide that a certain flame-wreath on a card border is the aesthetic they want to chase for the next decade.
