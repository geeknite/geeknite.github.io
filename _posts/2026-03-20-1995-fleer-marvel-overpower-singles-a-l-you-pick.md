Introduction
If you grew up in the mid-90s (or if your nostalgia sensor still tingles at the whiff of bubblegum and old card-stock), you probably remember Fleer’s Marvel Overpower. It was a superhero skirmish game where you combined heroes, villains, and phenomenally over-the-top powers to crush your opponent in gloriously flashy fashion. The A-L singles are the alphabet soup of the set: a selection of characters whose names begin with A through L. You pick them, you draft them, you deploy them, and you hope your opponent doesn’t have the one-liner to shut down your favorite combo.

This post is a deep dive into the 1995 Fleer Marvel Overpower Singles for letters A through L. We’ll cover what makes these cards sing (or screech), how to assemble thematic teams from this slice of the catalog, and how to leverage the quirks of era-appropriate design to your advantage in today’s nostalgia-driven skirmishes. If you’re looking for the perfect retro deck or just a comical stroll down memory lane, you’ve parked in the right shade of radioactive green connectors.

What is Marvel Overpower, and why does A-L matter?
Overpower was built around a streamlined, fast-playing engine designed to let players feel like they’re wielding cosmic might without needing a mechanical engineer’s degree to understand it. The system reduced complexity for quick head-to-head duels, and a big part of its charm was the expansive roster of Marvel characters—heroes, anti-heroes, and villainous baddies—each with unique powers, attack values, and synergy potential.

The A-L pool is where you first encounter many fan-favorites (Ant-Man, Black Panther, Captain America, Daredevil, Elektra, Iron Man, Loki, etc.) and a handful of less obvious picks that reward creative drafting and tactical timing. These cards aren’t just numerics on a page; they’re little mini-portraits of the characters you love, reimagined in a 1990s trading card style with bold art, chunky borders, and enough flavor text to make you smile wryly at how dramatically everything is described.

Note: This guide assumes you’re exploring singles from the original 1995 print run and focuses on A-L to help you narrow your hunting grounds. If you’re compiling a complete set, you’ll still want to explore M-Z, but the A-L window has plenty of punch to stand on its own.

The A-L Singles: A quick map
In Overpower, the roster isn’t simply “who has the highest attack.” Card design often rewarded synergy, team composition, and the way powers interact with your opponent’s board state. Here are the sort of archetypes you’ll encounter when you look at A-L:

- Ant-Man (A): A surprising value pick with scaling or situational powers that shine in fast, micro-control decks. Ant-Man is a classic example of a card that rewards out-thinking your opponent rather than brute-forcing the table.
- Angel (A): An aerial presence that can alter the ground game or provide a different angle of attack. Angel’s kit often leans into mobility and positioning. If you’re building a squad that likes to outmaneuver the board, Angel is your wingman.
- Black Panther (B): A staple in many collections thanks to his balance of offense and defense and the possibility of synergy with tech-like or Wakanda-themed strategies. Black Panther embodies a certain sleek efficiency—almost like he’s the Marvel version of a well-tuned sports car.
- Black Widow (B): An agility-forward pick with tricky moves and sometimes meta-level disruption. She’s the kind of card that punishes over-commitment and rewards precise timing.
- Captain America (C): The old-school powerhouse that tends to anchor a team with leadership-like effects or straightforward brute force. Captain America can be a moral compass on your board, keeping your plan honest and your lines solid.
- Colossus (C): A defensive rock with a surprising amount of staying power and the kind of durable presence that discourages your opponent from rushing you down a lane.
- Daredevil (D): The street-level hero who thrives on misdirection and clever reads of the game state. Daredevil exemplifies the “smart control” archetype—talented at disrupting opponents’ plans while keeping his own approach clean.
- Doctor Doom (D): Doom struts into the game with big, showy interactions and a pinch of villainy that can swing the board in dramatic fashion. He’s the kind of card that invites you to build a plan around an all-out, show-stopping moment.
- Elektra (E): A blade-wielding presence that often trades off speed and precision for big impact when you set up the right combos. Elektra rewards timing more than raw stats.
- Fantastic Four members in the A-L slice (often including legacy members with specific role claims): The Thing, Mr. Fantastic, Invisible Woman, Human Torch—various archetypes that can form a flexible core depending on your theme. In A-L, you’ll encounter characters that push a team-building approach where synergy matters as much as raw power.
- Ghost Rider / Gambit (G): Flavors of chaos and flair, with special effects that lean into the unpredictable. They’re the kind of picks that make your deck feel like a rollercoaster ride—exciting, risky, but memorable.
- Hulk (H): A wrecking crew on legs—typically a high-impact card that can swing momentum if you’re not prepared to answer. Hulk decks often thrive on momentum and timing.
- Hawkeye (H): A utility line that likes to poke and project options across the board. A Hawkeye pick can be a keystone in a control deck that outlets damage across multiple angles.
- Iron Man (I): The iconic tech strain of Overpower with gadgets and built-in resilience. Iron Man can offer a stable midrange threat that scales up with upgrades and specific picks elsewhere in your team.
- Iceman / Jubilee / Juggernaut (I/J/J, depending on set): Each of these characters represents a different flavor—raw power, burst disruption, and relentless pressure respectively.
- Loki (L): The trickster king who loves to twist the plan in unexpected ways. Loki decks often revolve around misdirection, bluffing, and forced errors in the opponent’s setup.

These examples illustrate the versatility of the A-L pool: you can lean into a heroic, team-based motif, chase a more villain-centric board, or try to embed a meta-style strategy that exploits specific interactions. The important thing is to identify a theme you enjoy and then locate singles within A-L that reinforce it rather than fight it.

Building around A-L: picks, synergy, and counterplay
A-L Singles aren’t a random grab-bag; they’re a playground of potential synergies. When you assemble a deck, consider at least three axes of strategy:

- Theme and flavor: Do you want a Wakanda-tech vibe with Black Panther + Iron Man + Shuri (if present in the subset)? Or do you want the city-block grit of Daredevil + Elektra + Hawkeye? Thematic cohesion often translates to more predictable, repeatable plays.
- Range and tempo: Some characters favor quick skirmishes and early pressure (Ant-Man, Angel), while others shine in late-game scenarios (Captain America with a rally mechanic, Hulk in a heavy-hitting finisher role). Balance your curve to avoid being stunned by a single fatal swing from your opponent.
- Counterplay: A-L’s diverse roster allows you to craft stoppers to opponent strategies. If your meta is heavy on flying or mobility, you may want Colossus or Hulk as a defensive hedge. If your foe leans on tricksy interrupts, Daredevil or Black Widow can offer disruption to slow them down.

Drafting tips for A-L
- Prioritize versatile cards: Pick characters that can function across multiple lines of attack. A-L has a handful of “swiss army knife” cards that can adapt to your need in a given match.
- Don’t neglect support-sized cards: Some of the most impactful interactions come from cards that aren’t the biggest hitters but enable combos or protect your board.
- Consider alignment: If you have a strong synergy with a particular archetype (speed, brute force, or control), ensure your A-L picks reinforce that archetype rather than scatter your energy.

Character Spotlight: A through L deep dive (select picks)
To give you a practical sense of what these singles do in play, here are quick releases for a handful of notable A-L cards you might consider when you pick a squad:

A – Ant-Man: The miniature mastermind
Ant-Man is often underappreciated in drafts because his value becomes evident only when you look at the synergy in a swarm-based plan. He excels at punishing over-aggression with micro-control tricks and can be the glue that holds a tight weave of cards together. If you’re building a “small but mighty” team, Ant-Man is a great anchor. His presence creates a micro-army effect that keeps pressure on your opponent while you line up more expensive plays behind him.

A – Angel: Wings of strategy
Angel adds a different dimension to the board—mobility and a way to threaten from angles your opponent may not expect. In the right lineup, Angel can force your opponent to split their resources, letting you capitalize on weaker flanks. If you enjoy hit-and-run play, Angel is your go-to pick for ensuring you’re never trapped into a straight horse race on a single lane.

B – Black Panther: Sleek power
Black Panther is the clean, efficient hero who makes your mana feel like a well-oiled machine. His kit often rewards clean execution and punishes clumsy plays. In a world where “cool” sometimes means “flashy,” Black Panther stays grounded while offering sustained value across the match.

B – Black Widow: Precision and disruption
If you like control-oriented games, Black Widow is a natural fit for the A-L pool. She thrives in a plan that seeks to deny your opponent their moves and set up precise counterpunches. A Widow-led deck can be the most satisfying, because every turn you feel like you’re peeling away the layers of your opponent’s strategy.

C – Captain America: Rally the troops
Captain America often brings a leadership effect that can strengthen your entire squad. He’s the moral compass in the roster, but also a legitimate power turn generator when combined with certain streaks. If your deck’s tempo relies on “getting to the next big threat,” Captain America helps you hit that target with more consistency.

C – Colossus: The durable shield
Colossus offers a rock-solid presence that your opponent must eventually pry loose. In A-L terms, he’s the defensive backbone that lets you weather the early swings while you set up a bigger plan for the late game. Colossus is especially satisfying when your opponent tries to overwhelm you with numbers and you can hold the line with elegance and brutality.

D – Daredevil: The art of disruption
Daredevil rewards careful play. He shines when you’re trying to sculpt the flow of the game rather than force a direct clash. In a sense, Daredevil is the chess player of the A-L subset: you bait, you feint, and you strike exactly when the time feels right.

D – Doctor Doom: Villainous spectacle
Doom is the spectacle pick, the one that turns heads and often turns games. When used well, he creates pressure that forces your adversary into the wrong move at the wrong time. Doom decks are about imposing your will—whether through raw threat or cunning manipulation—so you feel like you’re playing a villainous mastermind rather than a conventional hero squad.

E – Elektra: Precision strike
Elektra is a character that rewards exact timing and surgical plays. If you like to slot her into a combo window where one precise hit decides the match, Elektra will be your favorite. Her presence underscores the theme that in Overpower, the best wins are sometimes the cleanest, most precise wins.

F – The Thing / Mr. Fantastic / Invisible Woman / Human Torch (some sets include variations in the A-L window): FF synergy
If you’re chasing a Fantastic Four vibe, you’ll find a blend of bulk, stretchiness, and sun-made energy. The Thing and Mr. Fantastic typically offer robust board control and adaptability, Invisible Woman provides strategic positioning or protective effects, and the Human Torch can dish out aggression while adding a dash of hazard to the opponent’s plans. A-L gives you a taste of that family dynamic and how different FF flavors interact on a mid-90s battlefield.

G – Ghost Rider / Gambit: Chaos and charm
Ghost Rider and Gambit are the heart of the “wild card” archetype. They can bend the game to their will in the right circumstances, and their draw or damage windows create memorable moments you’ll still be talking about at conventions 20 years later. If you enjoy risk and reward, G is your playground.

H – Hulk / Hawkeye: The big hitter and the reliable sidearm
Hulk embodies the “beatdown” vibe—when he lands, your opponent will feel it. Hawkeye, on the other hand, offers precise damage deployment and can keep a plan alive when you’re short on big hits. The combination of Hulk’s raw impact with Hawkeye’s measured application yields a threatening balance for mid-to-late game scenarios.

I – Iron Man: Tech-based resilience
Iron Man is a staple of many playgroups who enjoy tech-based synergy. He’s not always the flashiest pick, but he can be incredibly dependable, delivering a mix of offense and defense that scales once you’ve set up a few of his supporting hardware cards. If you like “build-your-own-arc reactor” vibes, Iron Man is your guy.

J – Jubilee / Juggernaut: Burst and force of nature
Jubilee brings burst potential and tempo swings, making her a great pick for decks that want to capitalize on quick, decisive plays. Juggernaut, meanwhile, is the unstoppable force: he’s terrifying to face if you can get him off-to-a-good-start and keep him rolling with the right buffs. The J picks in A-L set a tone of aggression with a few clever counters available to the prepared mind.

K – Kingpin / Killmonger: Control and anti-hero swagger
Kingpin can be a control king in the wrong hands, forcing your opponent into tight spots. Killmonger provides a different flavor—anti-hero swagger with potentially explosive effect when used with proper timing. The K cards remind us that the Marvel universe thrives on complexity and gray-area leadership.

L – Loki: The trickster supreme
Loki rounds out the A-L window with misdirection and strategic deception. If you enjoy games where you misread your own card as much as you misread your opponent’s, Loki is a joy to pilot. His value often lies in leading your opponent down a path that looks good from a distance but collapses as soon as you react to it with a perfectly timed twist.

Drafting practical: constructing a reliable A-L deck
If you’re heading into a casual draft or a friendly tournament with a stack of A-L singles, here are practical guidelines to help you maximize your picks:
- Build around a core synergy: Choose 3–4 cards that clearly support a primary strategy (speed, control, or brute force). Then add 6–8 flexible cards that can slot into multiple roles.
- Leave room for counterplay: Include a few cards that specifically counter popular A-L-based strategies within your local meta. It’s the idea of “counterplay within your own deck” rather than just “counterplay in the format.”
- Protect your plan with redundancy: You don’t want to rely on a single combo to win; you want multiple threats that can win if your opponent disrupts one area. Redundancy is a budget-friendly way to ensure you don’t fall apart when faced with targeted disruption.
- Practice timing: Many A-L cards rely on the timing of their special abilities. Write a mental checklist for your rounds: when to play a buff, when to push a big hit, when to preserve a key blocker, and when to stage a lockdown move.

Collectibility and fandom notes
A notable aspect of the A-L set is the lived memory value. The 1990s were a time when card art exploded with color and earnest enthusiasm for richer superhero mythologies. The A-L pool captures a spectrum of heroes and villains that a mid-90s collector would be itching to own, display, and occasionally duel over in dorm rooms, comic shops, or convention hallways.

If you’re chasing value, focus on:
- Condition and borders: The era’s printing quirks mean you’ll see a fair number of off-border or slightly miscut cards. A well-preserved A-L card can fetch more than you’d expect if the artwork is iconic and the card is rare enough.
- Foil variations: Foil or chrome variants are a target for many collectors. If you’re into shiny odds and ends, keep an eye out for these rarities—though availability varies by region and edition.
- Character popularity: Classics like Captain America, Black Panther, Daredevil, Iron Man, Loki, and Hulk often hold steady demand due to enduring fan interest.
