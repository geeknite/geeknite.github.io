## Box contents and sculpt quality
### The lineup of figures
The Special Guest Lot arrives with a blend of survivors and zombies designed to complement the existing Zombicide base set. While the exact number can vary slightly by region or edition, the core of the box tends to present 8-12 miniatures: a handful of survivor-types (hard targets with improvised gear) and a broader crew of zombies (runners, fatties, and the occasionally grotesque variant). From a practical standpoint, these minis are CMON’s standard: solid plastic with clean mold lines, a degree of flash that’s easy to trim, and robust bases that resist warping during transport. The “CIB” (Complete In Box) aspect means you’re getting the figures as originally packaged, with all the character cards and tokens aligned to the promotional promise. It’s a good sign for hobbyists who don’t want to rummage through a loose bag of leftovers at a con. The density of details is notable but not insane—there are places where the sculpt demands careful painting and others where you can get away with a quick wash to capture the mood.

### Paintability and tabletop presence

Parrillo’s pieces tend to benefit from a strong red or teal accent—colors that pop under lead-light sources and photography. From a gameplay perspective, the design language is still Zombicide: push the line with a bit of style but don’t complicate the core rules. You’ll want to probe some scenarios where special guest pieces subtly alter threat levels or provide a tactical twist—without turning the game into a “prove you can paint by the book” exercise. The balance between look and play remains the heart of the package.

## The CIB experience and packaging
### What “Complete In Box” delivers here
CIB signals that you’re buying a self-contained experience. For collectors, this matters: you aren’t chasing down loose parts from eBay or rummaging through a dozen different expansion blisters to assemble a complete set. The packaging is designed to protect the minis during transport, and the box art leans into the guest-artist prestige, which is a nice marketing angle for friends who ask about your “art collection” rather than your “game library.”

In the real world, this matters when you’re setting up a display shelf, or when you want to resell later without dealing with the “unmatched runner duplicates” problem. The presentation is part of the value proposition, especially for fans who collect signature lines or who want to stage a coffee-table display that still functions as a game piece.

### Assembly and priming considerations
The minis in this lot require standard prep: wash resin or plastic where appropriate, trim mold lines, and possibly pin some pieces for stability on dramatic poses. The complexity isn’t outlandish; you won’t need a professional-grade kit to get a solid tabletop result, but if you’re chasing “museum-perfect” finishes you’ll appreciate the extra time. A few of the body accents—like gauntlets or ornamental bones—are delicate, so patience is advised. If you’re a hobbyist who likes to paint while watching a weather-themed sci-fi show, this box is your ideal companion: enough challenge to be satisfying, not enough to induce a meltdown when the airbrush misbehaves.

## Painting and storage tips
### Quick-start painting guide
- Prime in a neutral gray or white to maximize shadow detail.
- Start with base mid-tones for skin or armor, then build up with mid-tone washes (blue-gray or brown depending on the piece).
- Use a high-contrast highlight on raised edges to emphasize the sculpt’s drama; Parrillo’s lines benefit from a bright edge, while Guiton’s pieces respond well to crisp metallic highlights.
- Seal with a matte varnish to preserve brushwork and reduce glare on photography.

### Storage and transport considerations
If you’re transporting to gaming clubs or conventions, consider a dedicated foam tray or a tray with layered compartments. The box’s internal layout is friendly, but a small foam upgrade keeps the more dramatic pieces safe from accidental breakage during transit. You’ll also want to label the boxes if you borrow them to friends who “forget” they’re still in your car after a long con day. Trust me on this: the zombie you forgot about is a heavier passenger than you expect after a long drive.
