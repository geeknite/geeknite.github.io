## Painting and storage tips
### Quick-start painting guide
- Prime in a neutral gray or white to maximize shadow detail.
- Establish base mid-tones for skin or armor, then build up with mid-tone washes (blue-gray or brown depending on piece).
- Use high-contrast edge highlights to emphasize the sculpt’s drama; Parrillo’s lines respond to bright edges, while Guiton’s pieces respond well to crisp metallic highlights.
- Layer washes to add grime and weathering without muddying the overall read.
- Seal with a matte varnish to preserve brushwork and reduce glare on photography.

### Advanced painting notes and color recipes
If you want the look of a polished studio piece, consider these approaches:
- Parrillo pieces: base coat in a desaturated flesh tone, a purple-lue shadow wash in the recesses, then a very bright highlight along raised surfaces using a pale skin tone. Edge highlights should be crisp and selective, especially on blade edges and armor trim. For a cinematic pop, use a controlled red glaze on exposed skin areas or on cloth accents to evoke heat and danger.
- Guiton pieces: lean into clean whites and metallics. Undercoat white, shade with a blue-gray wash in the recesses to deepen the contrast, then pick out metallics with a non-oil-based metal color (or a metallic acrylic) and finish with a fine-tipped brush to pick out rivets and seam lines.
- Non-metallic metal (NMM) accents can be used sparingly on armor edges for an extra flare without overwhelming the sculpt’s natural texture.

### Storage and transport considerations
If you’re hauling these to clubs or conventions, a dedicated foam tray with layered compartments is worth it. The box’s internal layout is friendly, but a compact foam upgrade helps keep dramatic pieces safe from breakage in transit. Labeling is your friend; you don’t want to resurrect a zombie you forgot you loaned to a friend months ago. A little planning saves a lot of heartbreak after a long con day.
