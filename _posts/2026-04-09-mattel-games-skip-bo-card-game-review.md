## Introduction
Skip-Bo is the kind of game that sneaks into your weekend with the stealth of a friendly librarian. It looks simple on the surface: a deck of number cards, four build piles, stock piles, and a handful of wilds. But under the hood, Skip-Bo is a miniature engineering problem: you manage tempo, you optimize discard flow, and you race against your own capacity to keep four piles moving up from 1 to 12. The secret weapon is that the rules are breezy enough for a kid to learn in one sitting, yet they reward careful planning and flexible thinking. In the Geeknite universe, Skip-Bo is that reliable, no-drama friend who always shows up with snacks and a plan.

## What is Skip-Bo?
Skip-Bo is a timeless card game from Mattel that has survived decades of family game nights. The objective is to shed your stock pile by playing cards onto four common building piles in ascending order from 1 to 12. You can use any card from your hand or from your discard piles, and you can use wild Skip-Bo cards as any number to complete sequences. The energy of the game comes from the balance between luck of the draw and the tactical choices you make with the cards in hand. The result is a fast, forgiving, and surprisingly satisfying experience.

## Components and Setup
A typical Skip-Bo deck contains 162 cards: numbered cards 1–12 in four colors, plus 18 wild Skip-Bo cards that can stand in for any number. The build piles are the focal point, while the stock piles are your personal resource that you must shrink to win. Setup is embarrassingly simple: shuffle, deal each player a stock pile (with a few cards face down and the top card flipped), give each player a hand, and lay out four central build piles. The first player to shed their entire stock pile wins. The tactile sensation of dealing, shuffling, and revealing a new card can feel oddly ceremonial, like the high five you give yourself when your plan does not explode into chaos.

## Rules At a Glance
The core rule is straightforward: you must build four piles in ascending order from 1 to 12 using cards from your hand or discard piles. The Skip-Bo wilds can fill gaps as needed. When you play a card from your hand or discard piles to the building piles, you draw back up to your hand size. If you cannot play any card on your turn, you must draw from the draw pile and pass the turn.

### Core Objective
Build and exhaust your stock pile before your friends do. The more you can chain, the quicker you approach victory.

### Build Piles
Four piles in the center, each starting at 1 and moving up to 12. A pile ends when it reaches 12, freeing up space and opening the possibility of new piles.

### Stock Piles and Hand
Your stock pile is your personal resource. It holds the cards you are trying to shed, and often your last few plays determine the pace of the game.

### Skip-Bo Wilds
Wilds are flexible and potent. Use them to bridge gaps, complete long sequences, or rescue a near-lost round.

### Turn Structure
Turns are simple in concept and surprisingly rich in potential: play as many valid moves as you can, draw back to your hand size, and pass to the next player.

### The Art of Card Management Is Pretend
The art of card management is less about obsessive counting, more about reading the table and anticipating your future draws. Keep a mental map of which numbers are likely to appear next and how a single wild card can unlock several moves in a row.

### Stock Pile Tactics
Your stock pile is both an asset and a risk. The faster you shed it, the more control you have over the game's pace. Be mindful of the top cards and plan several moves ahead.

### Wild Card Timing
Wilds are late-game accelerants. Hold them for moments when you can unleash a cascade of builds, or when a threatened pile needs a dramatic rescue.

## Variants and House Rules
Many families and game clubs modify Skip-Bo to suit their pace. Common variants include timed rounds, no wilds, or limited draws. The core remains the same: you shed stock piles and aim to complete all sequences before your opponents.

## A Sample Round
Here is a hypothetical sequence to illustrate how a typical game might unfold. Not a real game, but a plausible one:
- You start with four visible cards in your hand and a stock pile of ten cards. The top of your stock reveals a 5. You hold a 1 and a 2 in your hand.
- You play a 1 onto the first build pile, then a 2 onto the same pile—progress! You draw back up to your hand limit.
- Your opponent reveals a wild Skip-Bo on their turn, which they place on a new build pile as a 4, creating a chain.
- You draw again and find that you can place a 3 on your second pile. The action continues with everyone trying to push piles toward 12.
- The tension rises as a wild card sits in your hand and you realize you can end the round by shedding your stock pile with a last few plays.

## Common Mistakes and How to Avoid
- Mismanaging the stock pile order: the top stock card can be the key to a potential win.
- Wasting wilds: save wilds for big plays, not small early wins.
- Overlooking discard piles: your discard piles are resources for future turns.
