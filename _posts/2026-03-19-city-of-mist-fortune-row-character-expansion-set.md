## Overview: what Fortune Row actually is
Fortune Row is not a new core rulebook. It’s a character expansion set designed to give your players more masks, more flaws, more mythic hooks, and—let’s be honest—a few more ways to reach for the dramatic close-up camera shot in a crowded alleyway. In City of Mist terms, you don’t get a new city block; you get a new set of lenses for seeing the block. The expansion focuses on two big ideas: identity and luck as a narrative tool.

### Theme and tone
Fortune Row leans into a mythos of luck—both good fortune and bad luck—as a living, breathing force that characters can cultivate, borrow, or steal. The new character options are designed to play with that motif. Expect aliases that hinge on improbable coincidences, a few masks that scream “I was totally born under a comet” and a handful of arcanities that feel like whispers in a crowded club: not illegal, but absolutely not something you want your mother to hear you say out loud.

### What you get in the box
- A roster of new character archetypes (thematically aligned with Fortune Row’s luck-based motif)
- New masks, backgrounds, and a few signature moves that tilt the odds in quirky directions
- A handful of complications and plot hooks designed for quick integration into ongoing campaigns
- A small set of art cards and quick-reference sheets for streamlined combat and social interactions
- Optional GM tools designed to thread Fortune Row characters into existing storylines without tide-turning chaos

If you’re a GM who loves weaving through players’ choices with a scenic detour or a player who wants a character whose power is basically narrative mischief, Fortune Row is your new best friend—and possibly your worst enemy if your party decides to go all edge-detective on the city’s secrets.

## New characters: who shows up and what they bring
Fortune Row introduces several new masks and associated powers. The characters feel deliberately cinematic: they’re designed to be seen, heard, and argued about in the same breath as the city’s most infamous legends. Here are a few representative examples (names anonymized for spoiler-free appetite):

### The Lucky Stray
- Theme: A fortune-teller who apparently only foretells good outcomes for others when their own life teeters on the edge of ruin.
- Hook: The player can manipulate minor probabilities—think “glancing blow” instead of game-breaking miracles.
- Narrative niche: Frontline comic relief that doubles as a moral compass, albeit a slightly crooked one.

### The Velvet Gambler
- Theme: A mask of risk and romance who seems to always land on their feet—sometimes literally on the rooftop across the street.
- Hook: Can re-roll a failed attempt by offering a trade: a scene beat, a secret shared with the GM, or a stolen moment with a major character.
- Narrative niche: The city’s charm offensive, charming villains and players into storytelling choices that feel fated… and then dramatically undercut fate with a clever twist.

### The Quid Pro Quoi
- Theme: A cunning broker of favors and consequences, who always has a second plan and a price tag.
- Hook: Introduces a “favor economy” where players accumulate and spend favors to unlock narrative perks.
- Narrative niche: Moral quandaries and social intrigue—the mask that makes players question whether they’re the heroes of the story or merely the easiest bet on the night’s crime roulette.

There are more, and if your table loves a good moral hazard, you’ll be delighted by the way these masks encourage roleplay while nudging the session into interesting, unpredictable places.

## Mechanics: how Fortune Row changes the game
Fortune Row is designed to be a nervous system for your sessions—nerves, not a backbone. It doesn’t rewrite the core combat system but injects mechanics that influence social scenes, investigation beats, and the timing of “moments” in a scene. Here are the core mechanical shifts you’ll feel:

### Masked power and narrative currency
- Each new mask has a compact mechanic that aligns with the luck motif: you’re inviting fate to intervene, but you own the cost.
- Narrative currency (a step above standard play) lets players burn a point to force a scene beat, reveal a clue, or pivot a conversation in a way that feels cinematic but not abusive.
- The balance is maintained by GM controls: you can cap how often the currency is spent and set soft limits to avoid power creep.

### Complications and misfortune tracks
- Fortune Row expands the complication pool, but with a twist: complications are now more tightly linked to the expansion’s theme, giving players more opportunities to justify their choices and GM more hooks to pull on.
- Misfortune can be turned into a plot twist or a character arc pivot, which makes sessions feel like a living, breathing pseudo-mystery rather than a mere chase sequence.

### Investigation and social combat boosts
- Characters gain a toolkit that helps with social encounters, persuading witnesses, or extracting information via subtle, luck-based twists rather than brute force.
- The expansion encourages players to think in terms of narrative stakes, not just dice results. It’s less “roll to win” and more “roll to reveal.”

If you enjoy the City of Mist vibe—the blend of noir, myth, and personal mythologies—Fortune Row nudges you toward deeper character-centric storytelling, with a gentle reminder that the city’s fate changes faster than the weather when you’ve got a charmeuse mask in play.

## Design and art: a quick look
The art direction in Fortune Row continues City of Mist’s signature blend of moody noir photography overlays and hand-drawn mythic iconography. The new character cards have a slightly different palette, leaning into golds and midnight blues, with a matte finish that’s almost velveteen to the touch. The typography is legible and stylish, which matters when you’re trying to remember a new mask’s special ability while a chess club of city detectives grill your character for motive.

Small touches matter here: the icons for each mask sit in a corner like a lucky rune, a tiny visual cue that helps players quickly identify the relevant mechanics during a tense scene. It’s the kind of polish that says, “This kit was designed by people who actually run games, not just paint pretty pictures.”

## Content balance: is it all glitter and gold coins?
Balance is the eternal whisper in the City of Mist product cycle. Fortune Row doesn’t crash the party with a dramatic overreach; instead, it injects more masks, more narrative levers, and a few hooks that can scale with the table’s appetite for drama. The new content is generally well-tuned for mid-to-high maturity campaigns: characters should feel potent, but not invincible, and GMs should feel empowered to say yes to a scene beat but also to say, “Let’s roll back to the scene you’re missing.”

If your table tends toward experimental storytelling with lots of “what would happen if…” moments, Fortune Row slots right into that appetite. If your group is not into improvisational storytelling, you might find the expansion’s emphasis on narrative flexibility a little buffet-like: plenty of choices, but you’ll need to curate what your table can digest in a single session.

## Thematic integration: how well it fits your city
Fortune Row leans into the City of Mist’s core conceit: the city is a living organism, and the masks are its heartbeat. The expansion’s content is designed to slot into existing arcs without forcing a new campaign format. That means you can drop Fortune Row into your ongoing game without tearing up your session notes, but you should be prepared to adjust your players’ expectations about how often a “lucky break” will occur and how the city interprets such luck in different neighborhoods.

For players who adore conspiracy vibes, Fortune Row gives you more threads to pull—more masks that can be deployed by the GM to create a plot’s domino effect, and more opportunities for players to improvise scandalous backstories that intertwine with city lore. It’s a good fit for a group that enjoys long arcs and multi-session character development.

## Integration with other expansions
If you’re already riding the City of Mist wave with other expansions, Fortune Row plays nicely with them, though you’ll want to be mindful of overlap in terminology and mechanics. The masks and narrative currencies are distinct enough to feel fresh, but there are shared motifs (mythic motifs, urban legends, and the city’s political landscape) that can cause occasional duplication if you stack too many expansions at once.

A practical tip: treat Fortune Row as a “character flavor pack” rather than a heavy mechanical overhaul. Use it to deepen your players’ identities and to seed new plot hooks that can be integrated into existing arcs. If you’ve got a long-running campaign, Fortune Row can serve as a refreshing pivot point, allowing for a season of character-driven storytelling before you pivot to a different thematic expansion.

## How to get the most out of Fortune Row
- Start slow: pick one or two masks that best fit your current party dynamics and weave them into a few scenes. Let the others breathe in the background, waiting for their moment.
- Use the narrative currency strategically: don’t just give players a “freebie” every session; reserve it for decisive scenes where a choice would genuinely alter the story direction.
- Encourage player collaboration: the masks in Fortune Row tend to reward character synergy. Create moments where two players’ masks interact in surprising ways, providing mutual benefits and conflicts alike.
- Prepare plastic stakes: the city’s denizens should react to the players’ reputation and luck, and you should reflect those reactions in the scene’s tension and tone.

## Summary recommendations
- If your table loves character-driven narratives with cinematic vibes and a wink to fate, Fortune Row is a strong addition. It doesn’t replace the core mechanics; it enhances them by giving you more lenses to view the city through and more ways to craft moments that feel larger-than-life.
- If you’re new to City of Mist, start with the core rules, and then consider Fortune Row as a mid-to-late-stage purchase once your group has built a stable rhythm and is eager to explore deeper character identities.
- If your campaign is already sprawling with multiple expansions, Fortune Row provides a chewy, character-centric infusion that can anchor a season’s arc without destabilizing your overall system or requiring a major streamlining pass.

## Final thoughts: is Fortune Row worth it?
Yes, but with the caveat that it’s a character expansion first, a storytelling catalyst second. It shines when your players lean into the city’s mythic underbelly and when you as GM enjoy leaning into the “what if” moments that make City of Mist feel like a living graphic novel rather than a fixed set of rules. If you crave more masks, more narrative leverage, and more dramatic moments where luck isn’t just a roll of the dice but a character in the scene, Fortune Row will respect your time and reward your ambitions.

If you’re ready to deepen the city’s shadowy glamour and give your players a fresh toolkit for crafting myth, you’ll want Fortune Row on your shelf. It’s not a must-have for every table, but for those who practice noir-ish myth-telling with a love of dramatic reversals, it’s a little treasure that’s worth the hunt.
