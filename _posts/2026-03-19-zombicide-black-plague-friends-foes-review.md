## Overview: what is this expansion really about?

Zombicide Black Plague is a fantasy-horror mashup where knights, swashbucklers, and rogues face down legions of rotters, blights, and toxic bruisers. The Friends and Foes expansion rattles the cage even more aggressively by adding a new set of survivors who are, in some cases, your party members, and in others, your worst nightmare in the form of variants and enemy types. Thematic focus here is on social dynamics within the apocalypse; friends become foils, foils become allies, and the entire board becomes a stage for moral tests involving power, trust, and the occasional slapstick misfortune.

Why this matters? Because Zombicide lives and dies by its tension. The base game does a masterful job of teaching you how fragile a plan can be when you open the box and realize your best ally is standing in a pool of green goo while a giant chainaxe throws a knife behind your back. The Friends and Foes expansion leans into that drama—asking players to juggle loyalty and survival while the undead scream their way toward you. It is not a gentle nudge into greater complexity; it is a full-on leap into a more cinematic campaign feel.

If you enjoy thematic ambition and you want your co op nights to resemble a heist movie montage where everyone has a moment to shine (and a moment to be bitten in a dramatic fashion), this is your jam. The design team at CMON understands why players fall in love with Zombicide: it marries quick tactical decisions with big moment outcomes. Friends and Foes channels that energy through new faces, new enemies, and a handful of optional rules that let you tailor the chaos level.

## What's in the box: components, minis, and tokens

The expansion brings a carefully curated mix of content designed to integrate with the base game without requiring a full replacement of your existing setup. Expect to find:

- A fresh cohort of survivor miniatures that slot into your roster with distinct class-like flavors (think rogue scout, holy knight who mutters pious encouragements as a two-hander cleaves through a mob, and the skeptic who questions your moral compass every time you roll a critical fail).
- A new batch of enemy miniatures that ratchet up the pressure. The foes are not merely reruns of the base encounters; they have bite mechanics and AI quirks that will make you reconsider when to sprint or when to hold a line for one more turn of misdirection.
- A handful of new tokens and cards that introduce fresh effects, global modifiers, and mission-specific twists. Some of these are sneaky, some are majestic, and all of them make you pause mid-planning to say, I did not expect this board to become a Shakespearean stage about trust and misdirection.
- Dual-sided survivor boards with upgraded art and new ability trees that shimmer with the promise of more tactical depth. This is not fluff; it is real design work to give you meaningful choices each time you play.
- A compact rules appendix that is merciful compared to the labyrinthine pages of some expansions. It does not replace the base rulebook but enhances it, like a stylish upgrade kit for your favorite sci fi helmet.

The physical quality remains in line with CMON’s reputation: sturdy boards, glossy art that pops on the table, and minis that survive the occasional tabletop tumble. The cards are thick enough to shuffle without the typical wear in a night of chaotic dice rolling. The overall production value reinforces the sense that you are not just playing a board game; you are managing a little cinematic production where you are both director and lead actor in a zombie-infested melodrama.

From a reading of the content, the balance appears designed to challenge players who already know the base game rhythm. It is not a gentle nudge toward “easier nights”; it is a robust invitation to escalate the stakes, negotiate trust, and invent new strategies to outthink the undead and your party mates at the same time.

## How it changes the gameplay experience

If you know Zombicide Black Plague well, you know the rhythm: plan, roll, hope for a good combo, survive the inevitable chain of bad dice luck, and repeat. Friends and Foes adds a twist that shakes up the predictable tempo without turning the game into a runaway chaos incident. The key changes include:

- New survivor dynamics: Some survivors act as moral pivots, offering choices with consequences that ripple through the session. You might be faced with a mission that rewards a high-risk, high-reward path at the cost of short-term safety for the squad. This nudges you toward long term planning and risk assessment rather than pure card-draw luck.
- Enhanced cooperation angles: The expansion introduces mechanics that reward coordinated plays. You will want to talk through corridors, coordinate spikes in attention to draw a herd away from a critical objective, and time your actions to maximize environmental advantages. In short, the expansion rewards real teamwork and punishes reckless, lone-wolf play styles with a deliciously poetic physics of consequences.
- Foes with personalities: The new foes aren’t just bigger numbers; they have traits that require you to adapt your approach. Some enemies may ignore certain lines of engagement, forcing you to move into new formations or deploy poison-tipped traps with surgical precision. The threat system feels earned and not merely inflated complexity.
- Mission variety and pacing: The new missions inject dynamic pacing. Some missions feel like a classic dungeon crawl, with a pulse-pounding build toward a climactic encounter, while others pivot into a tense cat-and-mouse exercise across narrow hallways. You get the sense that the expansion is designing a night of play with palate-cleansing variety instead of repetitive grind.

All these elements cohere into a session that feels more cinematic, less mechanical. You are not simply clearing rooms; you are orchestrating an ensemble performance where every actor (and zombie) has a role that shifts the stakes mid-scene. That is the heart of the expansion: you are playing in a bigger sandbox with more dramatic opportunities.

## The art of balance: is it too chaotic or just right?

Zombicide Black Plague thrives on a balance between chance and strategy. Friends and Foes knows that players crave meaningful choices; it supplies more doors than a cathedral, but it does not slam those doors in your face without the light of a plan. The new cards and tokens give you options that matter, but they do not render the basic rules obsolete. The designers have avoided turning this into a pure puzzle, where every move must be calculated to the millimeter. Instead, they lean into the joy of improvisation, letting clever play mingle with lucky outcomes to produce memorable storytelling moments.

From a difficulty standpoint, this expansion is accessible to players who have a firm grasp of the base game. It adds a layer of complexity but does not break the learning curve. The learning curve is steeper than the base game, yes, but if you are here for the long game—where tactical nous and social negotiation drive outcomes—Friends and Foes is your companion piece. It invites players to practice more nuanced risk management, more deliberate turns, and more meaningful cooperation. The result is often a session that feels longer and more gratifying, even when the dice are unfriendly and the undead decide to stage a dramatic coup on your weakest link.

## Components, setup, and teardown: a brief practicality guide

Setup remains straightforward, though not lightning fast. The box is not a time sink in itself, but you will need a moment to assemble all the new survivor sheets, lay out extra tokens, and ensure you have the right foe variants prepared for the mission at hand. The good news is that the layout is clean and intuitive, and the map overlays fit neatly with the base game boards. If you already know how to run a session of Black Plague, you will transition smoothly into Friends and Foes without consulting the rulebook every other turn.

Teardown is similar to setup in terms of time, but with more pieces to collect and repack. You will likely appreciate having dedicated organizers or small zip bags to keep track of tokens so that the chaos of the night does not turn the table into a scavenger hunt for your command tokens. The practical advice: invest in good organizers, keep a dedicated player mat out for the noncombatants, and designate a token wrangler who can help keep track of threat level and mission modifiers as the night spirals forward.

In terms of aesthetics, the new minis and art land with high polish. The color palette aligns with Black Plague’s medieval-meets-gothic vibe, while the faces on the survivors look expressive enough to help players quickly identify who is likely to fall into the next megazombie ambush. If you value the spectacle of your tabletop experiences, the Friends and Foes expansion does not disappoint.

## Solo play, co op depth, and replayability

If you are a solo player trying to wring maximum value from every box, Friends and Foes has something to offer. The expansion’s AI and mission variety can stretch a solo run into a contemplative exercise in resource management and board control. The new survivors and enemies provide enough wrinkle to demand different heuristics from cycle to cycle, which means a solo campaign can feel substantially different from a group night.

Co op depth is where this expansion truly shines. The interactions between allies and antagonists become an emergent property of the session rather than a scripted bone thrown at you by a fixed scenario. You will be rewarded for communication, timing, and tactical patience. The more you embrace coordinated plays, the richer the experience becomes. It is a classic case of the whole being greater than the sum of its parts, especially when those parts include a couple of extremely bad dice rolls that somehow create the perfect storm for a dramatic rescue mission.

On the replayability front, this expansion earns its keep. The addition of new survivor archetypes means different party compositions yield different outcomes. The revised enemy pool ensures that no two nights feel identical, even if you replay the exact same mission. If you love the loop of: plan, execute, adapt, survive, and then tell a friend the ridiculous story that followed, then this box will stay in your rotation for a long, long time.

## A quick critique: where it might miss the mark

No expansion is perfect, and Friends and Foes is no exception. A few caveats worth mentioning:

- It increases setup time and requires a bit more space on the table. If you are playing in a cramped apartment or in a living room that doubles as a gaming closet, you may want to reorganize your space to accommodate the larger footprint.
- The learning curve is steeper than the base game. New players will benefit from a slow ramp-up and a dedicated mentor at the table who can help decode the better lines of play. If you prefer a quick 60 minute sprint, you might be better served starting with a shorter mission before diving into the expansion’s deeper options.
- Some mission variants feel strongly narrative rather than purely tactical. If you play with a group that thrives on optimization, you might encounter scenarios where saving a single survivor becomes almost impossibly tricky. This is not a bad thing; it is a feature that pushes players toward story-driven decisions rather than pure dice luck, which some groups will adore and others will find frustrating.

## Thematic integrity and presentation

Zombicide Black Plague already delivers a rich fantasy-horror tone, and Friends and Foes leans into that mood with more dramatic stakes and morally ambiguous choices. The expansions’ survivors often wrestle with trust, loyalty, and what it means to save the day when your own fate feels precarious. The enemy design amplifies dread by not only being deadly but by presenting new tactical challenges that test your ability to manage multiple threats at once. The direction here is a well-executed ambiance upgrade: you feel like you are not simply playing a game night but inhabiting a vivid, pulpy world in which every decision carries a weight beyond the immediate dice outcome.

From a narrative perspective, that is the victory. The mechanics are not just a garnish; they support a storytelling layer that elevates both teamwork and individual hero moments. Some of my favorite moments came from planning a risky maneuver that paid off in a single, cinematic turn, turning table talk into a chorus of cheers and groans as the last stand finally tilted into victory.

## Comparisons to other expansions and the base game

If you own only the base Zombicide Black Plague, Friends and Foes will feel like a significant step upward in terms of both complexity and satisfaction. It does not render the base experience obsolete; it adds a robust layer that can be toggled on or off depending on your preference for difficulty and narrative depth.

Compared to other CMON expansions, Friends and Foes stands out for its balance between narrative flavor and mechanical depth. Some expansions lean heavily into extra cards or new environments; others chase a more numeric increase in difficulty. This one occupies a sweet spot where new characters and enemies feel integrated into the story rather than tacked on as a numeric hurdle. If you are a completionist, you will want this for the variety it provides; if you prefer a leaner experience, you can mix and match with a careful selection of components to preserve a brisk pace while still enjoying the flavor boost.
