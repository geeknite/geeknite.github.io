## Cable management and accessibility

In a workspace this large, cables can become a villain wearing a tuxedo. The Infinite system provides built-in channels along the underside of the wings and through the legs to route power, network, and display cables into discreet lanes. It’s a solid approach for the initial deployment. The real test comes after a few months when devices multiply and the cable queues start to resemble a conga line that never ends.

Practical tips:
- Label cables during week one and stick to a color scheme that maps to the wing and station numbers.
- Invest in cable combs, Velcro straps, and modest cable trays that won’t turn into a dust bunny farm.
- Keep a spare set of power adapters and display connectors on hand; central screens aged gracefully by software updates, not by heroic improvisation.

## Performance in practice: meetings, sprints, and all-day use

We put the system through a suite of scenarios: ten-person daily standups around the central display, two-person paired programming at adjacent wings, and independent work with the central pane serving dashboards. The layout nudges a conversational dynamic—people can see each other’s body language across the room and the central display provides a shared context.

In longer sessions, a potential downside emerges: audio bleed. With ten people in proximity, nearby conversations can mingle with the primary meeting audio, turning a focused design review into an accidental roundtable. If your space presents acoustic challenges, consider adding sound-absorbing panels or a lightweight ceiling cloud for the central area. A white-noise machine can also be a cost-effective backup. The aim is to preserve clarity of discussion without stifling creative energy.

## Assembly, maintenance, and upgrade paths

A two-person installation crew can typically set the base configuration in roughly 4–6 hours, assuming a clean workspace and a well-planned cable map. More time is needed if you insist on precise color-coded heat maps for every cable or if you anchor the system to a concrete floor rather than relying on adjustable feet. Maintenance is where modular furniture shines: you can replace a damaged panel, swap a wing, or upgrade the central display without tearing down the entire system.

The central screen is the energy-hog hero here, but modern displays are designed to be swapped in as technology evolves. Expect incremental upgrades to peripherals and improved cable interfaces; you won’t likely need a full teardown to refresh the core experience.
