Dimensions and layout: space planning in the real world
-------------------------------------------------------
The spec reads 9000 x 1530 x 1200 mm. Yes, that is a lot of numbers, and yes, your eyes deserve a small vacation. Let us translate this into practical terms. The 9-meter-long footprint means the setup spans across a generous wall-to-wall arrangement, or it can be configured as two parallel wings with a shared screen in the middle for visual synergy. The 1,530 mm width gives a comfortable seat triangle for each workstation, enabling two workers to share a central space without elbow combat. The 1,200 mm height is on the taller side for a desk system, which means better reach for wall-mounted screens and improved posture when paired with adjustable chairs.

The modular concept is one of the strongest selling points. Instead of a single monolithic desk, this is a two-workstation-on-each-side arrangement that can be reconfigured quickly if you decide to re-parent teams, swap roles, or accidentally hired a group of enthusiastic project managers who insist on reorganizing every two weeks. The central screen provides a focal point for collaboration, while the outer stations keep individual autonomy intact. If you have a room that measures roughly 9 by 2 meters, you could squeeze in two of these units with a shared visual space, or you could run only one wing and leave the rest of the wall for whiteboards, blue tape, and decorative plants that secretly judge your product roadmap.

Cable management and accessibility
-----------------------------------
In a workspace this size, cable chaos can be a villain in disguise. The Infinite system offers built-in cable channels along the underside and through the legs, intended to route power, network, and display cables into discreet channels. In practice, the system works well for the initial setup; the tough part is after a few months when dozens of devices accumulate and the queues of cables start to behave like a conga line. The recommended practice is to label cables during the first week of deployment and to invest in a handful of cable combs, zip ties, and perhaps a gentle reminder to your users that the floor is not a dragon’s lair for spaghetti cables.

Ergonomics and user comfort
---------------------------
This is where the two-workstation concept shines. Each seat pair has dedicated space to adjust chairs, monitor arms, and keyboard trays. The central screen acts as a shared collaborative canvas, which is excellent for pair programming, design critiques, or brainstorming sessions where everyone wants the same big diagram in their line of sight. The chairs we tested with the unit offered good lumbar support and a decent range of height adjustability, though as with any modular system, seat height will interact with the desk height. A mismatch can yield a shoulder shrug moment when someone enters a heavy design review phase. The moral: alignment between chair height, desk height, and display angle is crucial for comfort over long days.

Performance in practice: meetings, sprints, and all-day use
-----------------------------------------------------------
We ran a variety of scenarios: daily standups with ten participants around the central screen, pair programming at adjacent stations, and independent work with the central display showing shared dashboards. The shape of the layout encourages a conversational dynamic without blocking lines of sight. The central screen ensures that even if folks drift into separate conversations, there is a common ground for status updates and KPI dashboards. In longer sessions, the adjacency helps reduce friction compared to a sea of isolated desks; the downside is a potential spillover of audio from adjacent pairs during intense debugging sessions. If your office has acoustic challenges, additional sound-dampening panels or a white-noise machine can be a wise investment.

Assembly, maintenance, and upgrade paths
-----------------------------------------
Setup time for a team of two with basic hand tools hovered around 4 to 6 hours for the base configuration, assuming a clean workspace and a crenellated map of cable routes. Longer installation times occur if you insist on every cable being labeled with color-coded heat maps and if you decide to anchor the entire system to a concrete floor rather than using the included adjustable feet. In terms of maintenance, the system is designed to be modular. Replacing a damaged panel or swapping a wing for future expansion is reasonable. The central screen is a power-hungry hero, but modern displays love modern connectors, so you should be able to swap to upgraded panels or add more peripherals down the line without a full teardown.

Practical tips for deployment
------------------------------
- Map the room like a chessboard: identify focal points, traffic lanes, and zones of privacy.
- Use the central screen as a collaboration hub rather than a control center for everything. Not every decision needs to be on a big display, or you will end up with a living whiteboard that never finishes a thought.
- Invest in cable management and field service. In the long run, the difference between a tidy system and a spaghetti disaster is a few hours of planning and a couple of cable trays.
- Schedule a pilot phase with a whiteboard, a few teams, and a lot of sticky notes. The goal is to learn how your teams actually work with the system, not how you imagine they should work.
