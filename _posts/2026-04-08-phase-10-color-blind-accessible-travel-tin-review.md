## What is Phase 10 and why a color blind friendly deck matters
Phase 10 is essentially a marathon of ten mini-goals. Each phase requires a specific combination of cards, such as sets of three or runs of seven, and players must complete them in order. The deck is designed with color coded suits and numbers that help you track your progress visually. If you are someone who perceives colors differently or finds greens and blues that look the same in certain lighting or screens, color blind friendly features can be a literal game changer. It isn’t just a nicety; it’s about making the game inclusive so families with different visual needs can share the same table without someone shouting, I can’t tell which color that is because the lighting in the living room is suspiciously like a nightclub.

In the color blind friendly version, the designers emphasize patterns, shapes, and high-contrast elements to convey information beyond color. In a card game, that means you can distinguish suits and phases not simply by hue but by how the card looks and the patterns on its face. It turns the usual red vs blue battle into a friendly geometry class where triangles, squares, and dots are your guides. This is terrific for family game nights, where kids may mix with adults who use reading glasses or those who pretend not to need them but secretly do when the Guild of Phase-10 Rule Lawyers convenes.

From a usability standpoint, color blind accessibility reduces the cognitive load. Instead of memorizing that green is the “wild color” and relying on a color wheel that would make a color-blind person want to file a complaint with the Wavelength Commission, you can see patterns. The result is less squinting, less “is that a 7 or a 9?” arguments, and more actual playtime. In practice, this means your grandma can participate in a stage of the game without needing to borrow your highlighter and a flashlight to verify each card.
