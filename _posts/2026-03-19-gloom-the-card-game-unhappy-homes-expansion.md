## What is Unhappy Homes Expansion?

### New components and their roles

The Unhappy Homes expansion brings a handful of new components that feel thematically integrated rather than tacked on as a gimmick. In short, the box adds two main ideas: (1) a couple of new card types that broaden the kinds of misfortune you can inflict or mitigate and (2) a “house” mechanic that lets players attach a home to a family, creating a dynamic where the house itself adds modifiers to gloom outcomes.

- House cards: 4 distinct dicey domiciles, each with its own personality and a set of bony arms that reach out to affect gloom in unique ways. Some houses punish people who are already sad; others reward players who drag happiness out of others. In practice, these cards become a focal point for strategy and table talk because they’re visible, tangible levers that change how you think about both luck and social manipulation.
- Home aura and mood cards: The expansion adds a small stack of mood-altering cards that can swing the tone of a round between bleak comedy and existential dread. They’re designed to interact with both existing gloom cards and the new house cards, creating mini-chains of effects that can swing the game’s momentum in a surprisingly organic way.
- Incident and haunting cards: A handful of cards that represent misfortune or comic tragedy delivered by the house itself. These cards often trigger when certain conditions on the board align, so you might be forced to reveal an incident that makes your own family’s gloom rise to the top, even as you try to drag others into your private sad parade.
- Tokens and markers: Some tokens payment for the activated house effects, used to track ongoing influence or to remediate particular curses. They are small but actually useful, which is a pleasant surprise in a game that sometimes leans heavier on art than function.

In sum, you’re getting more ways to interact with the core mechanic of gloom: manipulating sadness and happiness. The new pieces feel balanced, not overbearing, and they do a good job of making the “house” feel like a character rather than just a decorative prop.

### Thematic core and how it lands

The Unhappy Homes expansion leans into the theme that the home itself can be a character with moods, quirks, and long memories. The art style stays consistent with gloom—slightly macabre, warmly silly, and always readable enough that you don’t spend two minutes deciphering a face on a card. The homes themselves are fictional micro-dramas: a creaky Victorian that seems to sigh every time a family member draws a gloom card, or a sunken bungalow that smugly hands out bonuses to players who manage to upset everyone around the table. The tone isn’t cynical for cynicism’s sake; it’s a comic, theatrical look at how the spaces we live in shape our choices, even when those choices are to doom a different family more than our own.

If you’ve played gloom before, you’ll recognize a few motifs from the base game recast for a new flavor. The expansion doesn’t rewrite the math of gloom; it reframes the math with a stronger emphasis on the interdependencies of houses and households. It’s possible to build a strategy that’s almost entirely about house management, but the real juice comes from balancing those choices with the inflated stakes that the stories prompt.

> For readers who crave the lore: you’ll find touches that feel like they were written in a corner cafe by a gamer who loves both literature and interior design. ## How to set up and play with Unhappy Homes

### Setup time and table presence

Setting up with Unhappy Homes is not much more complicated than base gloom. The instruction booklet is concise and forgiving; it emphasizes that you don’t need to tear out the old deck by the roots to enjoy the expansion—the two sets slot in neatly. Practically, you’ll do the following:

1) Shuffle the house cards into the gloom deck or separate them into a house-specific pile, depending on your table’s whim.
2) Shuffle the mood, incident, and haunting cards into their appropriate piles as described in the booklet.
3) Place house tokens or markers in the designated spots on the board or playing area.
4) Deal the standard gloom starting hands for each player and begin play as you would in the base game, with the added option to trigger a house’s effect when certain conditions arise.

The upshot is that the setup is minimal friction if you already know gloom; the expansion wants to be a gentle nudge towards more table talk and less dead air when someone is thinking about their third gloomy move of the round.

### Core gameplay changes in practice

The core gloom gameplay—play gloom cards to reduce happiness for opponents while building your own happiness up or down—stays intact. Unhappy Homes adds a few new layers:

- House alignment and influence: Each house has a primary mechanic that interacts with the happiness counters on the gloom cards. Some houses boost the effect of a particular family’s gloom, creating synergy (or sabotage) opportunities for players who can time their cards to match the house’s quirks.
- Residences as traps and boons: The house often activates at a pivotal moment, either when a family hits a threshold of gloom or when a specific card is played. The trick is to anticipate the moment when the house’s effect will swing the table the hardest, then either parry the wave or ride it to the shore of someone else’s misery.
- Inter-house interaction: Because there are four houses, there are some natural focal points in the table. If multiple players are invested in different houses, you’ll get a little tug-of-war that can be oddly satisfying—like a melodrama where everyone is pretending to be the most rational person in a haunted house.

Overall, the gameplay remains quick, with many rounds finishing in under an hour even when you add the expansion. The new cards add to the pacing rather than bogging it down. The risk you’ll encounter is that with more moving parts, there’s more room for misreading a card’s effect, so a moment of careful reading is still essential. The rules are not obtuse, but there is a small learning curve worth embracing because it unlocks the expansion’s best moments.

### Aesthetics and components worth noting

The card stock is sturdy, with art that avoids feeling gimmicky while still delivering the gloom persona. The new house cards are visually distinct enough to recognize at a glance, which matters when the table is in a fever pitch of laughter and despair. The tokens are small but clear, the text is readable, and the edge-bleed on the card art looks intentional rather than sloppy. If you’re particular about presentation, you’ll appreciate that the expansion doesn’t feel like a cheap add-on; it feels like a crafted extension of the gloom universe.

## Gameplay depth: how Unhappy Homes changes strategy

### House-centric strategies

If you lean toward house-centric strategies, Unhappy Homes rewards players who think in terms of boards and zones. You’ll want to plan your turns so that you don’t just dump gloom on the board, but also set up the house for maximum impact on the next few rounds. Some noble heuristics to consider:

- Time your escalations: Don’t unleash a cascade of sadness the moment the house becomes active. Let a few other cards set up a chain reaction that makes the house feel like the true orchestrator of misfortune.
- Align with others’ miseries: If you see someone building to threshholds that the house loves, you might be able to position a few extra gloom cards that push their happiness just enough to trigger a powerful house effect in your favor.
- Don’t ignore your own family: The expansion is generous enough to allow you to manipulate your own gloom in ways that set up house effects. Don’t be a martyr of misfortune; be a cunning strategist who can leverage your own sadness to maximize tribal chaos.

### Interaction with base game cards

The new cards complement existing gloom cards rather than replacing them. You’ll find that some new incidents and mood cards pair particularly well with the houses, creating micro-arcs that wrap around the currents the base game had already established. The result is more of the same, but with a twist of theatricality. If you enjoyed the meta-game of sequencing and card synergy in gloom, you’ll find the expansion adds a playful layer of planning without turning the game into a spreadsheet.

### Player counts and social dynamics

Unhappy Homes scales fluidly from 2 to 4 players in the standard gloom framework, though with the house effects sometimes triggering more dramatically with more players at the table. In a full 4-player game, you’ll feel the expansion’s bite as multiple houses interact and compete for attention. In a two-player game, the houses often become a tug of war that demands a different kind of finesse: you’re pushing for cause-and-effect while the other player blocks your moves with their own misfortune engine. The expansion remains approachable at all counts, and that’s a big win for a game that can sometimes feel capricious in social dynamics.

### Solo and cooperative considerations

For players who adore gloom as a solo challenge or who experiment with variants, Unhappy Homes scales down nicely to a quiet, tense puzzle. You’ll still be managing a deck and a house, but the social feedback loop is less intense, which can be a relief if you adore gloom’s vibe but not the chaos of a crowded table. The instructions include some variant rules that accommodate solo play or light cooperative wins where you’re all working together to avoid a catastrophic combined gloom, which in practice translates to a surprisingly tense and satisfying chair dance around the table.

## Comparisons: Unhappy Homes vs other gloom expansions

Compared to other gloom expansions, Unhappy Homes sits somewhere in the middle in terms of complexity and impact. It doesn’t overhaul the core mechanics the way some larger expansions do; instead, it offers a concentrated baker’s dozen of new strategic tools that feel distinct enough to justify their own play sessions. If you’ve tried the gloom expansions that introduced new suits or new scoring rules, you’ll recognize the difference: Unhappy Homes is more about adding a narrative engine to the core game rather than injecting an entirely new victory condition. That can be a relief if you like the crisp pace of gloom and don’t want to burn out the engine with too many side quests.

Two quick head-to-head notes for the curious:
- It often feels more forgiving for new players than some of the heavier gloom expansions because it emphasizes narrative choices and timing rather than forcing players into a single rigid strategy.
- It scales well for seasoned players because the house mechanics reward careful planning and social bluffing, which is where gloom truly shines as a social experience.

If you already own several gloom expansions, I’d still recommend adding Unhappy Homes to keep the table engaged. It’s the fresh coat of melancholy you didn’t know you needed until you tried it under a dramatic lamp with a full tabletop. If you’re choosing between expansions, consider which mood you want this year: a new scheme to chase or a new stage for your old schemes. Unhappy Homes gives you both in a compact and thematically coherent package.
