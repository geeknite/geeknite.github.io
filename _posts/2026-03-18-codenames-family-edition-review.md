## What You Get: Components and Setup

Inside the box you will find the standard 25 word cards laid out on the board in a 5 by 5 grid, two sets of agent cards in red and blue, a handful of neutral cards, and a number of helpful stand-ins for the family-friendly tone. ### The Board, Tokens, and Key Card

The board is a tidy square with a simple grid that makes it easy for players to scan cards at a glance. The key card, which reveals which words belong to which team for a given round, remains a central piece of the puzzle. The key card setup is quick: place the grid, show the starting color to the team that will go first, and you’re ready to roll. The flow remains true to the original’s quick-start ethos, a boon for players who want to jump into the fun without a lengthy tutorial.

### Player Roles and House Rules

Two teams, red and blue, take turns. One player per team acts as the Spymaster, the only player who can see the key card and who gives clues. The rest of the team tries to identify their own words on the board. House rules naturally emerge around a kitchen-table playgroup: you can add a rule about time-boxing guesses or allow a short time-out between clues if laughter is running longer than the clue should land. The Family Edition is particularly forgiving for new players, which makes it a great gateway game for friends and relatives who want to dip their toes into social gaming without feeling overwhelmed.

### Setup Time and Replayability

Our setup time hovered around 3 to 5 minutes, depending on how many kids are sorting the cards and how ferociously your cat wants to inspect the pile of tokens. The first round is often a gentle introduction to the rule set, and after that, the pace climbs as players start to anticipate the kinds of clues their teammates will appreciate. Replayability is decent for this kind of game; you can mix up which words appear on the board, switch the starting color, and invite new players to rotate Spymasters. ## How to Play: The Core Flow

### The Quick-and-Dirty Round Structure

The core loop is straightforward, which is part of the charm here. A Spymaster gives a one-word clue followed by a number that indicates how many words on the board relate to that clue. For example, a clue could be single-word and the number indicates how many words are tied to that clue. The team then discusses and names words aloud, placing the appropriate color markers on the board. If the team lands on a word that belongs to their own color, they continue guessing until they run out of allowed guesses or decide to stop. If they hit an opponent word, that word is revealed for the other team. If a neutral word is revealed, the turn ends without a win for either side. The goal is to reveal all of your team words first and avoid big missteps that could hand victory to the rival.

### Clue-Giving Strategy (Kid-Friendly Edition)

Clue giving in the Family Edition emphasizes accessibility and shared knowledge. Look for connections that resonate with a broad range of players, starting with obvious ties like pets, foods, or common activities. Avoid overly abstract links that only a handful of players will understand. The best clues in this edition are short, clear, and anchored in everyday life. If you are stuck, a safe tactic is to point toward a couple of concrete words and one or two more general terms that still fit the family theme. The key is to balance inclusivity with cleverness so that both adults and kids feel good about the clue while still leaving some room for guessing.
