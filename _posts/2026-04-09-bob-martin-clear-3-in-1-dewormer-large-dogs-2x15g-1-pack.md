## How to use and dosing basics
Dewormers, especially “3 In 1” formulas, require careful dosing. The exact dose for large dogs is usually weight-based, and the label will tell you minimum and maximum ranges. In practice, you’ll find yourself following these steps:

1) Read the label. Yes, really read it. A lot of the frustration with pet meds comes from assuming you know the dosing by osmosis. The label will give you the recommended dose for your dog’s weight category and whether you should dose with food.
2) Measure accurately. Paste formulations can be finicky. If you’re handing your dog a paste, a small dab might get licked off the spoon, but the rest needs to be squeezed onto a dosage tip or into a small amount of tasty food.
3) Administer with minimal drama. Some dogs accept paste like a pro; others act as though you’ve just offered a napalm sandwich. The trick is to align the dose with a normal mealtime routine or a favorite treat to mask the medicating moment.
4) Observe for side effects. The most common non-catastrophic side effects are mild vomiting, mild diarrhea, or temporary lethargy—often nothing to panic over. If you notice persistent symptoms, call your vet.
5) Clean up and celebrate. Once the dose is delivered and your dog seems back to normal, do a tiny victory dance in the kitchen (quietly, so the neighbors don’t hear your life’s soundtrack).

## Side effects and safety notes
No vet product is perfect, and dewormers are no exception. The most common, non-serious side effects reported in owners’ diaries (and by extension, our imagination) include mild nausea, transient vomiting, or softer stools for a day or two. If your dog exhibits signs of an allergic reaction—swelling of the face, hives, difficulty breathing—seek veterinary care immediately. Always ensure you’re using the correct product for your dog’s weight category and age. If your dog is pregnant, lactating, or has pre-existing medical conditions, a vet consultation is essential. Remember: the safety profile of any dewormer is best interpreted by a professional who knows your dog's medical history.
