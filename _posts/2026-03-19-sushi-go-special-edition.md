## What is Sushi Go Special Edition all about

Sushi Go is a quick drafting game for 2 to 5 players (the base rules remain friendly to small and large groups). Each round, players pass a hand of three cards around the table, select one card to add to their platter, and pass the rest to the next player. After a few rounds, you tally up your points based on the scoring rules for different sushi types: maki, nigiri, tempura, sashimi, puddings, and curiosities that sometimes show up as bonus cards in the Special Edition. The Special Edition adds a few variants that shake up scoring patterns and encourage more aggressive drafting, which in practice translates to more tense face-off moments and more dramatic reveals when you finally reveal your endgame tally.

## How to play in 60 seconds or less

1) Each player starts with a small display of cards in hand. 2) Pick one card to keep and pass the rest to the neighbor. 3) Repeat until the deck is exhausted. 4) Reveal and tally scores for the round. 5) Repeat for a few rounds until the end of the game. 6) The player with the most points wins.

For those who enjoy the deeper meta, there is enough room in the Special Edition to experiment with nuanced drafting. You can opt for a tight, minimal approach in which you attempt to squeeze a few high-scoring combos each round, or you can go for a broader, higher-variance strategy that risks more but pays off with spectacular rounds when the stars align. The key is to communicate with your table without turning it into a cutthroat tournament; Sushi Go is at its best when the pace stays breezy and the jokes stay friendly.

## Strategy and tips

- Read the table, not just your hand. Observing what your neighbors are collecting can help you decide whether a card is worth risk or if you can safely pass it along for someone else to snap up.
- Early rounds matter, but the late rounds decide the mood of the table. Don’t oversell a lead in the middle rounds; you want to keep options open for the final tally.
- Balance your meal. It is tempting to chase a single high-value card, but the real points usually come from a blend of dish families and combo bonuses.
- Use the new variants as weapons carefully. A well-timed scoring card can swing the endgame, but misusing it might just waste a turn and give your opponents the edge.
- Don’t forget pudding. In many versions of Sushi Go, pudding can swing the final score tape. If you are close in points, a pudding pack can tip the scale in your favor at the last moment.
