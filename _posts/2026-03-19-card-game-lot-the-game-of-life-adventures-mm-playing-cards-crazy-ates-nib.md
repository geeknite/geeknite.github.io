## Condition and Packaging: NIB vs. Opened
The NIB label is half brag, half caution. New In Box can mean pristine if the box has been kept in a climate-controlled bunker, or it can still be a little rough if someone stored it under a sink and prayed for humidity control. In this specific lot, the Crazy Ates NIB appears to have the best box presentation, with crisp edges and a box lid that still feels more confident than a veteran player at a tournament. The Game of Life Adventures deck shows more signs of shelf life: minor creases along corners, slight discoloration on the card backs, and a few minor scuffs that do not compromise readability but certainly tell a weathered tale of weekend gaming sessions gone long into the night. The M&M Playing Cards deck, being mass-market and frequently jostled in bagged bundles, has the most typical signs of wear: a few dull spots on the edges and some surface micro-scratches on the card faces.

From a practical standpoint, if you are a flipper or a display collector, you want to separate the ideal from the functional. The NIB Crazy Ates will fetch higher attention on display shelves and photo sessions, while the Life Adventures set might be better served as a functional prop for a game night archive. The M&M deck can be used for casual play or repackaged into a display with a candy-counter vibe that makes your guest list smile and want to raid your snack stash.

In terms of value, packaging plays a big role in the collector market, but condition is king. If you intend to sell the lot or part of it, a clear description of box condition, card stock wear, and any factory defects will help you price realistically.
