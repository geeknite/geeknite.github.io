## The Quick Pitch
If you like dwarves, treasure, and trust issues, Saboteur is the party game you never knew you needed. It drops you into a mine where the gold is real, but the loyalty is optional. Some players are miners chasing glittering gold through a tangled web of tunnels. Others are saboteurs who love misdirection, dead ends, and the occasional pickaxe-shaped pun. The goal is simple on the surface: be the first to reach the gold and claim your treasure. The problem? You can't tell your friends from your enemies, and every turn you draw another card that might save the day or ruin your plan with the elegance of a goblin using a fork to pick a lock.

This is the game that makes you question your favorite fellow gamer: whether they’re truly your ally or just very convincing at pretending they are. The answer is often maybe, which is exactly what the game wants from you.

Two things to know up front: Saboteur thrives on social dynamics and short rounds. It plays fast with a big group, slows down with a small one, and becomes a comedy of errors when you least expect it. It’s also surprisingly strategic: the more players you have, the more dramatic the misdirection, the more precious each path card becomes, and the more ruthless your teammates will be about blocking a tunnel that might lead to gold.

## What is Saboteur, Exactly?
Saboteur is a light-to-midweight card game about dwarves mining for gold. The twist is the hidden roles: some players are miners who want to reach the gold vein; others are saboteurs who want to derail the mining operation—either by sabotaging tunnels, creating dead ends, or manipulating the path with sneaky actions. The tension springs from the fact that no one knows who’s who, except the saboteurs themselves who quietly smirk behind their beards.

The goal is to build a path from the mine entrance to a treasure goal card. Each round, players draw and play cards to lay down tunnels, lay track, or sabotage their rivals. When a path reaches a goal card with gold, the miners who contributed to that path share the spoils. If the saboteurs manage to derail every path so that none reach gold, they win by default. It’s silly, it’s dramatic, and it’s the rare game where the matrix of trust shifts as quickly as a goblin rolling a die.

The base deck is a cocktail of path cards (straight tunnels, corner tunnels, and dead ends), action cards (block, break, fix, replace), role cards (miner or saboteur), and gold nuggets (points to claim at the end of a round). There are typically 3-6 players as the sweet spot for maximum chaos, though the box readily accommodates more players. The rules are approachable; the setup is deceptively simple; and the tempo can swing from breezy to nail-biting in the space of a single draw. That’s the charm of Saboteur: it doesn’t demand your full cognitive load, but it rewards social cunning in ways that make you feel like a master strategist—even if you mostly mouth off about how the tunnel should go “this way, obviously.”

## The Mechanics Behind the Mayhem
To truly appreciate Saboteur, you need to understand three things: hidden roles, the path-building core, and the sabotage control panel (aka the action cards).

### Hidden Roles: Miners and Saboteurs
At the start of a round, each player receives a role card. The miners want to connect the entrance to a goal card containing gold. The saboteurs want to derail that plan—sometimes by shaping a perfect tunnel, sometimes by ensuring the path leads to nothing but dead ends. The suspense lies in how long you can keep a straight face while someone else ponders over whether you’re the saboteur or the miner. The classic dynamic is a little like a board game version of a heist comedy where everyone pretends to be a master thief while occasionally checking if the guy next to them has the loot map.

The roles are secret, so you’re never entirely sure who is helping you and who is pretending to help while quietly turning off the lights. The more players you have, the more the saboteur strategy becomes a mosaic of misdirection. It becomes a social puzzle where you’re not just deciding which card to play next, but who you can bluff into believing your plan is legitimate.

### The Path-Building Core: Cards That Create Tunnels
Path cards form the actual tunnels leading from the entrance to a goal. They come in various shapes—straight, corner, and end pieces—and the aim is to connect the entrance with any of the goal cards available in the center of the table. The miners lay down cards to build a path; the saboteurs throw in walls, dead ends, and misdirections to waste turns and disorient the other miners. The result is a patchwork of tunnels that can be beautiful, chaotic, or hilariously wrong, depending on who plays what and when.

Some turns will be clean and efficient; others will demand a re-draw, a rearranged tunnel, and a dramatic moment where someone plays an “extend path” card and suddenly the table erupts in cheers and groans because the path has become absurdly long. The variety of path cards means no two rounds feel the same, and the same path card can produce entirely different outcomes depending on when it lands and who is watching you with narrowed eyes.

### Action Cards: Sabotage, Repair, and Seriously Questionable Decisions
The action cards are where Saboteur earns its reputation for absurd, delicious chaos. They let you block a tunnel, break a card someone just placed, fix a break, opt to replace a card in someone else’s hand, or even peek at another player’s role card under certain situations. The dramatic irony is that sometimes the best action is not to use an action at all but to pretend to plan a disaster so that your neighbor will panic enough to lay down the wrong card.

The balance is crucial: if you’re a saboteur, you want to be surgical in your intervention, not reckless. If you’re a miner, you want to use action cards judiciously, saving them for when you can actually influence the path in front of you rather than shouting at the table about “the path that must be this way.” Your choices in these moments define the round’s mood: is it tense and strategic, or is it a gleeful circus of missteps and improv comedy?

### Turn Structure and Flow
A typical round unfolds in a cycle: roles are revealed, path cards are drawn, decisions are made, and the tunnel grows. Each player has a hand of cards from which to choose. You can place a path card to extend the tunnel or play an action card to hinder a neighbor. The round ends when a path reaches a goal card with gold, or when the path stagnates because the table unanimously forgets how to count to three. It’s dramatic, tiny, and surprisingly intense for a game that fits in your pocket and most of your patience.

The tempo of the game is what makes it shine. It’s possible to have a quick, brisk round where the path is built in a handful of turns or a longer, more drawn-out session where every card feels loaded with consequence. You don’t need to be a math savant to enjoy Saboteur, but you do need to cultivate a little poker face and a love for dramatic reveals.

### Strategy and Social Dynamics
What makes Saboteur truly satisfying is the interplay between hidden information and social deduction. A key strategic thread is risk management: when should you back a path that looks promising? When should you snap a path away because you suspect a saboteur is trying to lock you into a dead end? And which signals should you read from your tablemates—the way they draw, the cards they hesitatingly hold, or the way they lean back with a suspicious smile while telling you their plan?

For miners, practical strategy involves coordinating with teammates to maximize the number of people contributing to a successful path. Saboteurs want to sow doubt and misdirection, encourage the group to waste time chasing dead-end paths, and ensure at least one path ends in a broken dream. The thrill comes from real-time miscommunication and the thrill of the moment a player finally says, hey, this path might actually work, only to realize the plan is spiraling into glorious chaos because another player had a better idea all along.

A few practical tips for new players:
- Don’t reveal your role early. The moment you claim you know where the path should go, you become a magnet for suspicion.
- Use action cards strategically. A well-timed block or break can derail a strong path and create space for a cunning switch of plans.
- Watch for patterns. If a player seems to know exactly which card to play at the perfect moment, they might be the saboteur or simply a player with a knack for reading the table. Either way, adjust your strategy accordingly.
- Embrace the chaos. Some rounds will be messy; that’s part of the charm and probably a sign you’re playing with a great group.

### Components, Art, and Theme
Saboteur’s aesthetics lean into warm, slightly cartoonish dwarven charm. The cards are sturdy and not precious; the art leans into the humor of a dwarven mine that’s a little too crowded and a lot too suspicious. The color palette is earthy, with gold glints that catch the eye and remind you why you’re digging in the first place.

From a design perspective, the game nails the balance between ease of learning and depth of play. The rules are short—maybe two pages in a typical setup—and the variety of cards means that players won’t be bored even after a dozen rounds. It’s the kind of game you can teach in five minutes but still enjoy for 20, 40, or more minutes depending on your group’s dynamics.

### Variants and Expansions: Do You Need Them?
There are expansions and variants that add more paths, more roles, and more ways to complicate your life. The core idea—hidden roles, path-building, and sabotage—remains intact, but the additional complexity can either spice up a regular game night or overwhelm new players who are still learning the base rules. If you’re new to Saboteur, start with the base game and let the social dynamics do the heavy lifting. If you’re craving more chaos and more cunning, explore expansions with care and curiosity, not bravado.

### A Round-By-Round Breakdown (A Practical Walkthrough)
- Step 1: Setup. Shuffle the deck, deal role cards face down, deal a starting hand to each player, reveal the entrance card, and place the path cards face down in a draw pile.
- Step 2: Role reveal. Each player looks at their role card in secret. If you’re a saboteur, your mission is to blend in until a critical moment; if you’re a miner, your mission is to push that path toward a gold-laden goal.
- Step 3: The build. On your turn, you draw a card and decide whether to place a path card to extend the tunnel or play an action card to hinder a neighbor. The table’s energy rises with every card placed that twists the path away from safety and toward danger.
- Step 4: Endgame. When a path reaches a goal card with gold, the miners on that path win the gold; if the saboteurs prevent any path from reaching gold for a full round, they celebrate their own devious victory. And so the cycle repeats, sometimes with new players, sometimes with the same crew who still can’t believe the tunnel ends up here.
- Step 5: Scoring and rotation. Each round yields gold nuggets, and after a series of rounds you tally up the points. The person with the most gold is crowned the gold-smuggling champion and is allowed to bask in the glory, optionally roasting the others in good-natured fashion, or maybe both. It’s a cycle of joy and chaos that never seems to end well, which means it ends at exactly the right moment—at least until the next game night.

### Saboteur in Different Settings: In-Person, Online, and Anywhere with a Table
Saboteur plays best in a live setting where you can read micro-expressions, the sly glint in a friend’s eye, and the way someone quietly slides a path card into a corner. It translates surprisingly well to a video-call setting if you have the right camera setup and a table where everyone can reach the draw pile, though some of the best moments come from the chaos of a shared physical space. A lot of the humor comes from the physicality of the actions—the sarcastic reveals, the muffled bets on whether someone will “accidentally” show their role, the dramatic piling of path cards as you argue for a route to the gold.

It scales gracefully from a small, intimate session to a raucous, high-energy night with a dozen players. The core mechanic—hidden roles plus collaborative tunnel-building—delivers big laughs, surprising twists, and the occasional guilt-trip when someone tries to sabotage the path and ends up just sabotaging their own plan.
