## Grading, Condition Nuances, and What NM Implies
Near Mint means the card has minimal handling wear, clean surface, sharp corners, and a centering close to 55/45 or better in most cases. But vintage NM is nuanced. In Gym Heroes Japanese cards, centering is king, and the difference between a solid 9 and a solid 10 can hinge on two tiny corner nicks that a casual eye would miss entirely. When evaluating Erika’s Gloom LV. 24, buyers often look for:
- Crisp border edges with minimal printing flaws on the border itself.
- A clean surface with no major scratches or scuffs that affect the artwork. Minor print lines are common and acceptable in NM; they don’t ruin the art’s charm.
- Centering that isn’t stepping over the threshold into “off-centered” territory. The historical standard for Japanese sets tends to prize symmetrical presentation, and that symmetry matters when you’re chasing top grades in the collection.
- A stamp or foil if applicable. Gym Heroes cards didn’t always feature heavy holographic foils like the modern era, but a well-preserved print can still feel “new enough” to enchant a collector.

## Display, Storage, and Care for a NM Erika's Gloom
Storing a vintage NM card requires gentle handling and a plan for long-term preservation. Here are practical tips to keep Erika’s Gloom looking pristine:
- Use sleeves designed for vintage cards with acid-free materials. Consider a soft sleeve to minimize surface contact and reduce the risk of microabrasions.
- Place the card in a rigid top-load sleeve or a semi-rigid magnetic-closure binder page designed for graded cards. You want to keep it flat and shielded from bending.
- Store in a cool, dry environment away from direct sunlight. Sunlight can cause fading and color shift over time, especially for the pastel greens used in this art.
- Avoid stacking too many heavy cards on top of it. The corners are delicate, and repeated pressure is the enemy of minty corners.
