## The Guest Artists and Their Visual Language
### Lucio Parrillo and the Bold, Gothic Realism
Parrillo’s work is a swirl of dramatic lighting, sinewy limbs, and bold color choices that make a zombie look like it could star in a blockbuster fantasy epic. In the Special Guest Lot, his sculpts are designed to be instantly recognizable on the table a pair of survivors whose cloaks, leather, and weaponry look like they survived a dungeon crawl with a brush-wielding wizard and a caffeinated painter shouting Stop dithering and paint the eyes already. Parrillo’s influence on the design language leans into the heroic, the tall, and the striking silhouette. On a board full of gray plastic, Parrillo’s pieces pop with a confident silhouette that says I am not just a zombie, I am a story about to begin.

### Edouard Guiton and the Painterly Kinetic Energy
Guiton’s style, on the other hand, leans more towards dynamic action poses, and a kinetic energy reminiscent of mid-20th-century pulp art thrown into a zombie blender. In the Special Guest Lot, look for musculature that has the sense of motion frozen in a moment after someone shouts for reinforcements and the zombified side finally gets the drop on your survivors. The contrast with Parrillo’s panels creates a two-artist dialogue: one with the epiciose of fantasy, and one with the swirl of action and grit. The result is not a uniform look but a deliberate juxtaposition that can be fun to paint side by side.

## Paintability and Tutorial Ideas
This section is where your inner painter may start flexing. Parrillo and Guiton’s pieces provide a mix of textures leather, metal, natural fabrics, and bone. You can experiment with color temperature to create contrast between the earth-toned survivors and the more vibrant guest characters. A common approach is a three-color base plus wash plus dry brush for highlights but you can mix in some selective edge-lining to bring out details on the folds and the buckled belts. If you are into anime-inspired cell shading these sculpts also respond nicely to sharp color breaks though you should do a quick test on a spare from your stash to ensure you don’t wash away the sculpt’s fine lines.

A good primer flexible paints and careful pre-shading can yield results that pop on the table.
