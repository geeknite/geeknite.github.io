## Installation: a practical, not an epic quest
Note: Always disconnect the car battery if you’re poking around near electronics or airbags. The following steps are generalized; your exact model year may have minor variations in clip counts or screw sizes.

### Tools you’ll likely need
- Small flathead and Phillips screwdrivers
- Trim removal tool (or a plastic card you can bend without snapping)
- Socket set with 7mm or 8mm (depending on your clips)
- A flashlight and a clean microfiber cloth

### Step-by-step install guide
1) Prepare the workspace: clear the center console, lay a soft mat to prevent scuffs, and switch off the car so nothing spontaneously beeps at you.
2) Remove the current gaiter and frame: gently pry around the edges with a trim tool, releasing the clasps or screws that hold the around the shifter surround. Don’t yank; clips can break.
3) Inspect the mount points: count clips, screws, and any adhesive pads. Make sure you know what needs to be relocated or reattached on the new piece.
4) Clean the area: wipe away dust with a microfiber. A clean surface is your ally; dust loves to hide behind trims like a mischievous gremlin.
5) Fit the new frame: align the clips with their corresponding holes. It should drop in with a satisfying little click without forcing anything. If something doesn’t align, don’t force it—empty your toolkit, pause, and recheck alignment.
6) Attach top edge: secure any top clips or screws to hold the gaiter frame in place. A loose top edge will cause the gaiter to slip and look like a DIY project that deflated halfway through.
7) Reinstall the gaiter: slide the boot back over the shifter, making sure the top edge sits flush inside the frame without twisting the fabric. A kinked gaiter will catch on the inner walls and annoy you every time you shift into third.
8) Test, test, test: start the car, cycle through all gears, and confirm the gaiter sits properly with no rubbing against the knob or the console. If you’re hearing squeaks, you likely have a misalignment or a clip you forgot to engage.

### Common snags and quick fixes
- Snug but not tight: If the frame feels loose, recheck the lower clips and ensure the top edge is fully engaged. Sometimes a tiny shim or a thin rubber spacer from your kit can help fill a micro-gap.
- Gaiter binding: If the gaiter binds when you move the shifter, check that the boot is oriented correctly and not twisted. A small twist can catch on the frame or on the knob base.
- Trim discoloration: If the finish looks uneven after installation, a quick wipe with a mild interior cleaner can restore uniformity. Do not use harsh solvents on faux leather or coatings—these can degrade the surface faster than your ex’s attitude.

### What to do if clips don’t align
If your year/model uses a slightly different clip pattern, you may need to swap in a universal frame with adjustable clips. Some vendors offer tweak kits to retrofit a nonstandard shifter surround. In that case, keep the receipt and ask customer support about clip compatibility before you attempt an improvised fix on the drive home from the store.

## Maintenance and longevity tips
- Regularly wipe the frame and gaiter with a mild interior cleaner. Avoid solvent-heavy products that can degrade the surface.
- If you notice creasing in the gaiter, you can gently stretch it back into shape while the material is warm (car parked in sun, or a warm day). Don’t over-stretch; you want a natural look and feel, not a stretched-out mess.
- Inspect clips and screws during routine maintenance or when you replace air filters. A loose frame can rattle and wear faster than you’d expect, especially on rough roads.
- If you have a dog or a lot of traffic around the car, consider covering the gaiter with a light protective spray designed for interior plastics to keep it looking fresh.
