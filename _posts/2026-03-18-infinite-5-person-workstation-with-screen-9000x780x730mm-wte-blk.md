## Design and Build Quality
The first thing you notice is that this thing is big. It dwarfs the standard single desk as if you replaced a small city block with a lounge chair. The 9000 mm length means you can spin a meeting around with almost no one feeling left out. The construction feels sturdy, with metal frames that creak gracefully under load rather than complaining like a wallet full of receipts after tax season. The finish is matte black with a white accent line along the edges, giving the entire rig a modern industrial vibe without shouting at people about how much money was spent.

The surface is a composite that resists scratches, but the real test is how well it handles daily use. You will have laptops, monitors, docking stations, random office paraphernalia, and a large amount of coffee mugs distributed across the length of the desk. Our testing involved placing five workstations worth of peripherals, plus a test monitor, and a couple of plants that were clearly not edible. The surface held up well, with only minor scuffing after a week of heavy use. If you are planning a long term deployment of this piece in a busy environment, expect to see signage and tape marks in certain hot zones—these are not defects, just the visual language of an active team planning sessions.

The screen mentioned in the product name is a thoughtful addition rather than a mere marketing gimmick. It integrates into the central zone and acts as the focal point for shared dashboards or a central KPI board. We found the viewing angle to be surprisingly good from most seats, which is a relief given that a single screen faces multiple people and, occasionally, a cat that strolls into the office for the occasional laser pointer session.

## Ergonomics and Comfort
The ergonomics are a mixed bag, as you would expect with a desk designed for multiple users. Five people means five different body heights, leg lengths, and preferred monitor distances. The central shared screen is an ergonomic balancing act: if you sit too close, you block the view for others; if you sit too far, the text becomes a blur and the team loses faith in Agile estimates. The design solves this by offering adjustable monitor arms in the central zone that can tilt and swivel but keep everything pleasantly stable when a teammate bumps the desk during a sprint planning session.

Legroom is surprisingly decent given the scale. The under-desk space is organized with a cable management trench that runs the length of the desk. It keeps power strips, USB hubs, and the occasional forgotten umbrella in check. Cable management remains one of the more tedious tasks in any large desk setup, yet this model makes it reasonably painless thanks to labeled channels and removable panels that let you do cable shuffles without needing an electrical engineering degree.

Comfort comes from the ability to position chairs in a way that maintains visibility across the screen and reduces neck strain for longer sessions. The five-person layout encourages cooperative work while preventing the classic problem of someone physically blocking another person when discussing a design idea. In practice, the configuration lends itself well to stand up talk breaks, quick whiteboard brainstorms, and spontaneous demos that do not require a separate meeting room.

## Assembly and Setup
If you are the kind of person who enjoys assembling furniture as a weekend sport, this will feel like a gratifying challenge. The kit arrives in multiple large boxes with clear labeling and a surprisingly calm instruction booklet that avoids the dreaded DIY porn of exploded diagrams. The key to a smooth assembly is to designate an open space for the entire thing, then assign a lead organizer who understands the joke about Ikea hex keys.

We laid out the parts, sorted the screws by size, and began with the central spine that holds the five workstations together. The spine is the backbone of the whole system, and taking a little extra time here pays off in long-term rigidity. The desk sections lock into the spine with a click that feels satisfying but not aggressive. It is not the same as a flat-pack chair, but it is close enough to make you feel like a master builder without needing a degree in macro engineering.

The electrical wiring is the sensitive part. A long run of power cables is included, with a guide that helps you avoid tangling like a bowl of spaghetti. We recommend routing the cables along the trench and using the provided cable ties to keep everything neat. You will need basic tools like a screwdriver and a rubber mallet for minor alignment of panels, but no heavy power tools are required for typical office assembly. The process from unpacked boxes to fully functional is roughly a half-day job for two people, with a full day if you insist on turning it into a social event with snacks and a camera crew.

## Materials, Durability, and Maintenance

## Practical Tips for Deployment
- Start with a floor plan drawing that marks the central monitor zone and five user positions. This helps you test sight lines and avoid a cluster where someone cannot see the screen without turning around.
- Invest in under desk storage where possible. The trench cable management is robust but benefits from labeled bins and easy access to power strips without crawling under the table every day.
- Consider adding footrests or adjustable chairs to improve comfort, especially if your team spends long hours in sit mode.
- Create a rotation plan for meetings. The shared screen is ideal for large standups, but you want to avoid the scenario where everyone looks at the same person in a never-ending glow of screen reflection.
