## What this expansion brings to your gloomy table

The Unhappy Homes expansion for Gloom introduces a fresh motif: haunted houses that actually modify how you play the cards in front of you. Instead of just adding new cards and a few tweaked rules in isolation, this expansion stitches its content into the core game with thematic motifs and mechanical twists that feel purposeful rather than gimmicky. You will notice three broad design ideas at play: persistent house effects, new family dynamics, and a handful of room-driven synergies that reward careful planning and ruthless misdirection.

The result is a product that invites you to narrate your misfortune with a bit more structure, while still letting you ad-lib your own melodramatic soap opera. This is not a “feel good” expansion; it is a “feels bad, but in a funny way” expansion. If your group thrives on sarcasm, storytelling, and a strong sense of theatrical tragedy, Unhappy Homes will be your jam. If your playgroup is more into dry Euro vibes, you’ll still groan with appreciation at the clever interactions, even as you try to manipulate fate into your favor.

### The box contents in a nutshell

- A set of new House Cards, each representing a distinct location with its own mood and malediction. Think haunted kitchen, ghostly nursery, cryptic attic, and a foyer that never stops sighing.
- Room and Object cards that can be slotted into houses (or dropped into the discard pile, depending on your mood and level of petty vengeance).
- Ghost cards that introduce ephemeral haunt effects, forcing you to adapt your plans on the fly.
- Curse cards that escalate drama in memorable ways: you might flip a curse mid-story and suddenly realize that you have been dragging a spectral white elephant through the entire sequence.
- Family trait cards that interact with the house effects, creating comedic-but-terrifying synergies.
- A couple of new reference sheets so you do not have to squint at tiny print while the house creaks and your relationships crumble.
- Tokens or dials to track the new house effects and, yes, to measure your heartbreak in painful inches of despair.

The physical quality remains solid. Card stock is sturdy enough to survive the inevitable shuffles and near-disastrous table flips when someone bursts into a dramatic monologue about their doomed family. The art continues to lean into the macabre with a wink, maintaining the same dark whimsy that fans already adore. The design keeps things accessible for new players while still providing meaningful depth for old hands who love to micromanage doom allocations and the order in which cards are revealed.

### How it threads into the base game

Unhappy Homes is designed with compatibility in mind. You can integrate it seamlessly into your base Gloom sessions without needing to overhaul existing rules. The core mechanic remains the same: players aim to make their family cards as unhappy as possible while steering their opponents’ families toward the bright, shining light of misfortune. The expansion adds new layers without forcing you to learn a brand-new game system from scratch.

- House effects are persistent but optional—you can decide if you want them to be active for a particular round or for the entire game, depending on your group’s appetite for dramatic continuity.
- The new family trait cards interact with both the House and Room cards, creating opportunities for multi-card combos that can snowball in entertaining ways.
- Curses add an extra layer of timing. When you anticipate a key moment to drop a curse on an opponent, you may alter the balance of power in a single, glorious reveal.

If you have played through multiple sessions of base gloom and you crave more extended campaigns or a longer chain of misfortunes, this expansion scratches that itch. It invites you to build longer narratives and to deploy cunning symbolic threats that feel satisfying to unleash on your tablemates.

## How to set up and run a session with Unhappy Homes

Setting up Unhappy Homes is largely the same as base Gloom, with a few thoughtful additions to accommodate the extra cards and the house-theme mechanics. The vibe remains breezy and chaotic, which means you can get into a game quickly, yet there is enough texture to keep the more patient players engaged.

1) Shuffle the new House deck separately from the base gloom deck, and place them in their own draw pile. If you like chaos, shuffle them together and go full inferno mode, but we recommend a clean setup to understand interactions first.
2) Deal the appropriate number of Family cards to each player, just as you would in the base game. If you are curious about longer play, consider a house-based draft phase where players pick their starting house before the rest of the family cards are dealt.
3) Add Room, Object, and Ghost decks to the table. It is here that you start discovering the house-specific effects and the eerie synergies that will shape the entire session.
4) Place any curses into their own small pile. You will pull from them at dramatic moments, so keep them accessible but slightly out of sight to maintain suspense.
5) Start the game and let the melodrama begin. The new House effects provide a faint whisper of dread that lingers across turns, ensuring you know a haunting is on the horizon.

Pro-tip for busy tables: keep a simple “haunt tracker” to remind players when a house effect triggers. It can be as simple as a 4-column sheet on a post-it or a single laminated card that slides around the table. You want the aura of doom without devolving into a house rule tangle that requires a degree in enchanted jurisprudence to adjudicate.

## Strategy and narrative tips

Gloom, by design, rewards storytelling and strategic sacrifice. Unhappy Homes enhances that dynamic by offering new levers that can be pulled in the moment of decision. Here are a few practical and humorous strategies to maximize your gloom while keeping a smile on your face:

- Build around a house’s core sentiment. Some Houses are all about domestic despair; others are about social humiliation. Align your curses and your room selections with the house mood to exploit synergy bonuses.
- Plan a two-step misfortune. The beauty of this expansion is the way it invites you to stage a dramatic two-turn plan: turn one to set up a cursed chain, turn two to unleash the final, devastating punchline.
- Use Ghosts and Objects to create “shadow corridors” of doom. Ghosts can delay a decision, push back a tragedy, or accelerate a heartbreak with a well-timed reveal.
- Exploit family trait synergies. When you synergize a house effect with a family trait, you can create a narrative arc that feels like a mini-arc in your own doomed soap opera. The audience at the table will cheer and wail in perfect harmony.
- Brainstorm a theme for the round. A quick, shared agreement on a silly theme—like “haunted kitchen week” or “attic of abandoned ambitions”—can transform a tense execution into a memorable performance, which in turn nudges the gloom into theater rather than mere despair.

If you like to micro-optimize, you can also consider keeping a loose running tally of “gloom value” per house. This is purely for players who enjoy crunching numbers in their head while their character’s story spirals into melodrama. For more casual tables, focus on flow and storytelling; the gloom value will accumulate in due time, often in unexpected, hilarious ways.
