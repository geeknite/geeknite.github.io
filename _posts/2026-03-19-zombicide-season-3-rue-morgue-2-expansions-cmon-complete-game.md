## What’s in the Box and what the expansions add
Rue Morgue is a particular slice of Zombicide that leans into a metropolitan, abandoned city vibe with additional factions mixing into the undead chorus. ## Setup, learning curve, and first impressions
Like many CMON games, Rue Morgue rewards careful setup but isn’t a total brick to teach. The rulebook uses clear sections for setup, a quick-start guide, and then the long punchy sections that seasoned players will skim. If you’ve played other Zombicide sets, you’ll recognize the base mechanics: players control survivors, you activate a number of survivors each round, you perform actions, you draw encounter and zombie cards, and you watch the zombies pile into your positions as if the city itself is plotting against you.

The learning curve is moderate. You’ll want a round or two to explain what a survivor’s action suite looks like, how the two expansions modify the move sequences, and what the new special zombies do in terms of threat level. The two expansions are designed to be additive rather than disruptive, which is a big win for players who want to escalate the game gradually rather than jumping straight into heavy, multi-column boss fights. If you’ve played the base game, Rue Morgue with expansions introduces new threats and new synergy windows without turning the table into a full-blown calculus exam.

On the table, setup takes roughly 15 to 25 minutes depending on group size and how meticulous you are about sorting the new tokens and miniatures. The modular board tiles in Rue Morgue ensure that no two sessions feel identical. You’ll end up with a city block where back alleys hide both opportunities and traps and where the clock is always ticking—survivors often have limited ammo and must plan for turn sequences that allow for safe loot collection while the next wave of walkers shuffles in.

Remember to keep the rain effects and the neon lighting in mind if you play on a dim table. It helps with immersion, and it reduces the risk of stepping on a misplaced zombie miniature that you accidentally kicked while arguing about line of sight. Yes, this game can escalate into an animated argument about who should have used the stun pistol on that last zombie who just sprinted past your guy. Welcome to Rue Morgue’s world: it’s loud, chaotic, and gleefully unpredictable.

## Core mechanics and why Rue Morgue feels different in Season 3
Zombicide’s core arc is familiar: players cooperate to survive wave after wave of zombies, gathering gear and completing objectives. Rue Morgue adds flavor by injecting two expansions that tilt the balance toward more dynamic urban survival play. The core mechanic of turning action points into movement, search actions, and weapon use remains intact; you still manage line-of-sight, range, and fear tokens. The expansions adjust the tempo by introducing new zombie types and fresh objective triggers, which means you’ll be juggling multiple threats at once rather than sweeping a simple corridor of zombies in neat rows.

What makes Rue Morgue feel special is the urban setting. The street grid, the secret backrooms, and the occasional vertical element on the map create new routing challenges. You’ll often encounter missions that hinge on controlling chokepoints or performing synchronized actions across multiple survivors. It’s a dance of timing, risk, and a pinch of good fortune, and the expansions give you more steps in that dance.

The tactical depth is solid but not overwhelming. If you enjoy planning a few moves ahead and then pivoting when a card throws a wrench in your plan, you’ll feel right at home. The expansions give you more opportunities to experiment with different team compositions. You might run a heist-style squad with a mix of specialists, or you could field a lean team that relies on heavy weaponry and crowd control. Either way, the synergy is the real hook: placing a gun here, moving a sneaky survivor there, and timing a door-blocking tactic to prevent a critical zombie rush from collapsing your perimeter.

One notable improvement in this complete package is the balance of difficulty. The base set had its teeth, and the expansions take a careful bite without breaking the jaw. The designers structure the scenarios so that you’re never guaranteed a win just by pure luck nor doomed by a single wrong turn. It’s a steady climb that requires attention and some luck with dice, but never feels like a broken puzzle you cannot solve. This is the sweet spot for a mixed group of players—some who want to plan and others who want the thrill of chaos.

## The two expansions: what they uniquely bring to the table
Expansion A: More Survivors and Options
- Adds a handful of new survivor profiles with distinct abilities that change how you approach movement, looting, and melee. Some are better at pushing through crowds, others excel at helping teammates in distress. The key is to pick a squad that complements one another. The expansions encourage you to experiment with synergy, which is part of the charm of Zombicide.
- Brings new equipment cards that appear less frequently than in the base game, ensuring you feel the crunch of resource management more often. It also creates a few emotionally charged moments when a critical tool shows up just as you thought the team would be fine without it.

Expansion B: The Zombies Speak, and the City Responds
- Introduces new zombie variants with unique movement patterns or special abilities. These variants aren’t gimmicks; they alter your lane control and force you to re-evaluate safe zones on the map.
- Adds encounter cards that throw curveballs into standard objectives. You’ll face scenarios where you must defend a location while a secondary objective tries to drag you away from the front line. It’s a mental gym session in the best possible way.

Together, the expansions push Rue Morgue from a solid, tense co-op into a living, breathing city where every room has a decision and every alley hides a consequence. They’re designed to be mixed and matched, so your group can escalate gradually or dive straight into midfield chaos depending on the mood and the players’ willingness to risk a bad dice roll.

## House rules, variants, and how to tailor the chaos
Every gaming group has its preferred spice. Some ideas to tailor Rue Morgue for your table:
- Shorter nights variant: reduce the number of objective cards drawn per mission to speed up the game and lean into quick, decisive runs.
- Hard mode: introduce a custom rule where one zombie type gains a faster movement rate after every round, roughly simulating a “citywide panic” mechanism.
- Coop drama mode: encourage players to split into two small squads with shared objectives; reward cross-squad collaboration with a bonus loot reveal if the two squads synchronize their actions perfectly on a single turn.
- Solo tuning: if you’re playing solo, mimic a two-controller approach by controlling two survivors in parallel and switching focus between their actions, with a rule that you must give equal attention to both to avoid one character from stagnating.

These house rules aren’t mandatory, but they can spice your long sessions. Rue Morgue’s modular design makes it forgiving enough to allow for experimentation without ruining the core experience.
