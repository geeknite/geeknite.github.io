# Sushi Go Party! Deluxe Review

If you thought Sushi Go was cute and fast, Sushi Go Party! takes the speed dating of snacks and makes it a dinner party with a glow-up. This Deluxe Pick & Pass Card Game from Gamewright brings more menus, more options, and more chances to yell out Nigiri with wasabi across the table. In short, it is what happens when you take a simple card drafting mechanic and toss in a buffet line worth of choices, all wrapped in colorful art and a surprisingly sturdy box.

## Overview

Sushi Go Party! is a stand-alone follow-up to Sushi Go, marketed as the deluxe, party-friendly version. It keeps the core idea of pick and pass, where players choose one card from their hand, place it down, and pass the rest to the next player. After everyone reveals their chosen cards, you score based on combos formed by the cards from the menu card you selected. The Party edition expands that concept by offering a rotating set of menu boards; at the start of each game, you choose from a handful of distinct menus or mix your own for a custom experience.

The components are the star here: a handful of printed menu boards, a stack of beautifully illustrated sushi cards, scoring chits, and a chunky dose of color that makes every round feel like a carnival of edible icons. The multicolored theme is not just an aesthetic flourish; it helps players quickly identify card types and potential combos, which is a blessing when you are trying to keep up with your aunt who yells at the table like a competitive eater during finals week. You also get a lot of re-playability due to the menu variation, which means you can bring Sushi Go Party! to multiple gatherings without it feeling stale.

### Setup and Turn Structure

Setup is a breeze for a party-game: decide how many menus you want to use, lay out a board for each player, shuffle the sushi deck, and assign the chosen menu board. The number of players affects how many cards each player is dealt per round, and the objective remains the same: maximize your points by forming the right combos on your chosen menus.

Each round, you get a hand of five or more cards depending on player count. You pick one card secretly, place it in front of you, and pass the rest to the left. Then all players reveal their chosen cards in a synchronized moment. The cards you collected get laid out in front of you, scoring at the end of each round according to your active menu. The process repeats for a number of rounds equal to the menu board design. The result is a fast, tense, and occasionally chaotic rhythm that will remind you of a real sushi queue at peak lunch hour – minus the long lines and the existential dread.

### Menu Variability and Customization

The big selling point of Sushi Go Party! is the selectable menus. You can choose standard ones or mix and match to create a house variant that fits your crew. Some menus reward you for collecting similar items, others push you toward diversifying; a few blend both strategies with special bonuses for combos that feel ridiculously satisfying when you pull them off. This means your first game can be a gentle introduction, while future games can escalate into a strategic gauntlet where you plan turns two or three moves ahead.

On the topic of customization, you can design your own menu if you want the game to mimic a specific dinner party vibe. Want a sushi crawl across the Rainbow Roll zone? You can do that. Yearning for a fancy feast table with extra points for rare fish? You can do that too. The modular menus give you elasticity that most card games lack, turning a simple pick and pass into a dynamic planning exercise and table talk generator.

### Strategy and Card Management

Strategy here is a balancing act between short-term gains and long-term scoring potential. Some menus reward you for high-velocity grabbing of singles like Nigiri and Sashimi early in the game, while others push you toward collecting multiple cards of the same type to unlock a high scoring ceiling. The presence of the Chopsticks card adds a layer of misdirection; if you can sneak it into your hand and later swap in more valuable cards, you can swing a round's outcome dramatically.

However, the catch is that you never truly know what your neighbors will draft. The thrill is in reading the table: who is eyeing dumplings? Who is chasing wasabi or soy sauce? The pick and pass flow rewards bluffing and timing. You might pass a card you actually want because you realize your neighbor values something else more and vice versa. It’s a little mind game wrapped in cute octopi and salmon roe palettes, which is exactly the vibe we need after a long day of staring at spreadsheets.

### Player Count and Accessibility

Sushi Go Party! scales well from 2 players up to about 8 or 12 depending on the menu boards used and the edition you own. The base game is already quick and breezy; Party Edition is even more nimble when you have a reliable group that appreciates spare minutes and quick decisions. For families, it’s a fantastic gateway into the world of card drafting without needing a masterclass in probability. For hardcore gamers, the customizable menus offer enough depth to feel strategic without bogging you down in math nerd minutiae.

That said, if you have players who want to optimize to the letter, you might hear a few rounds where people debate the optimal combo on every menu board. You know who you are, champ. The game rewards cunning, but it doesn’t punish you for fun—only the occasional miscalculation if you misread your tablemate’s intentions. It’s the kind of party game that makes you wish you could draft at the speed of thought while still crafting a delightful plate of pretend sushi.

### Artwork, Components, and Overall Feel

The multicolored art is bold, bright, and distinctly non-threatening for younger players. Card icons are clear, which is essential when you’re trying to figure out if you need to chase oyster nigiri or a particular set of maki. The physical components feel solid; shuffling the deck is a joy, and the boards sit sturdy on the table rather than sliding around. The theme lands with a goofy but affectionate wink, which suits Geeknite’s vibe perfectly: we love a game that doesn’t take itself too seriously while still offering real decision points.

If I had a gripe, it’s that with a very large menu pool, some combinations can produce runaway scoring opportunities if players are not paying attention. The deluxe edition improves this by smoothing out balance with improved card stock and readable scoring tracks, but the underlying tension remains in the hands you receive and the choices you face every round.
