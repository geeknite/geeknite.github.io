Gloom 2nd Edition is the kind of game that sneaks into a gaming night wearing a funeral veil and a wink. It wants you to feel vaguely bad for a fictional family of gloomy souls while secretly staring at the table and thinking, this is the kind of thing I could somehow turn into a lifestyle blog about: exclusive gloom, candlelit chaos, and despair with a side of humor. ## Overview

Gloom 2nd Edition is a light, family-friendly (well, downcast-friendly) card game from Atlas Games. The core mechanic is melancholic storytelling laced with ruthless optimization: you want your characters to die early and painfully so your score climbs higher as their gloom increases. The cards tell stories of doomed families, each card a little vignette of woe, whimsy, and wicked puns. The gloom mechanic is simple enough for a first-time player to groan at and clever enough to keep seasoned players from shouting at the rules like a late-night caffeine-fueled referee.

In 2nd Edition, the art is punchier, the text a touch clearer, and the in-game humor sharper. If you have previously experienced the first edition, this version dials up the readability and streamlines some of the fiddly bits without taking away the mischief. The game plays 2 to 4 players and typically finishes in 60 to 90 minutes. It scales surprisingly well, which is a fancy way to say it doesn’t die of loneliness when the table grows from two to four people who all want to tell a story about their very sad family.

> A quick spoiler-free vibe check: if your group enjoys black humor, tragic outcomes, and gentle card-counting mischief, this is your jam. If you hate fun and prefer solemn, soul-crushing seriousness without any punchlines, you might want to watch from the foyer.

## What you get in a used, complete copy

When you buy a used copy, you are buying a piece of history that smells faintly of peppermints and regret. Here is what to expect and what to check:

### Card stock and artwork

- The artwork in 2nd Edition is a step up from the original in terms of contrast and readability. The gloom feel is modern but still classically moody. The cards are not glossy enough to cause glare during a sunny gaming session, which is a boon when you are trying to keep a straight face while explaining exactly how your undead matriarch regrets every life she didn’t quite ruin.
- In a used copy, you want to inspect the corners for wear. The art on the backs should be intact, and there should be no sudden creases that would affect readable text in the gloom-inducing cards. Look for any subtle warping that might affect sorting during game play. If the cards show consistent edge wear but everything remains legible and sturdy, you’ve likely got a solid deck.

### Card insert and components

- Gloom 2nd Edition uses a compact, tidy deck system. The insert, if present, should keep the deck sorted into families and gloom tiles. A missing insert is not a dealbreaker, but it can make shuffling a little… dramatic. If you receive a loose box with all tokens in a bag, that is fine as long as nothing is bent and the tokens are complete.
- Tokens, gloom dice (if any), and any fate markers should be present. In a used copy, you may find tokens with slight scuffing; this is cosmetic and does not affect function. If any tokens are missing, you may still have the core experience, but you’ll need to improvise or negotiate a small price discount.

### Dice and tokens

Gloom 2nd Edition is a card game with a minimal rule set and a lot of narrative power. The tokens and any accompanying dice are mostly optional accessories that sometimes help track gloom levels or mood. In a used copy, ensure that any tokens are legible and that there are no missing indicators for track markers.

### Rulebook condition

The rulebook is where a lot of the magic lives. A well-loved used copy will still have crisp pages, clean diagrams, and minimal creasing. If the rulebook is dog-eared, make sure you can still read all examples. A thumbed rulebook often means the game was played with joy, which is a good sign. If there are sticky notes or dog-eared sections, that is a badge of honor, not a red flag. It just means someone tried to annotate the gloom for better storytelling.

### Box integrity and inserts

A sturdy box is a blessing. Look for a box lid that sits square and a base that hasn’t collapsed under years of being stacked in a closet with a copy of a DnD player's guide and several mysteriously sticky coaster sleeves. A box that closes cleanly is a joy in the age of moving houses and inconsistent shelf space. If the insert is missing entirely, you can still play, but the organization gods will judge you silently.

## Setup and first impressions

Setting up Gloom 2nd Edition is a delightful exercise in foreboding. You deal each player a starting hand of gloom cards and character cards, then you start narrating your family’s misfortunes. The goal is simple: accumulate a higher total gloom than your rivals and, ideally, finish with a story that makes everyone laugh and cry at the same time.

### Step-by-step setup (at a high level)

1) Separate the deck into the family cards and the mood/gloom cards. 2) Hand out initial characters to each player. 3) Shuffle the gloom deck and place it within reach. 4) Determine a starting player. 5) Prepare the score track. 6) Start telling a story that will culminate in a glorious chain of inevitable tragedy.

In a used copy, you may see paper inserts that show potential misprints or porch-scarred corners from the previous owner. That is part of the charm. The game is designed to welcome imperfect artifacts and still shine as a storytelling engine. If you come from a background where pristine newness is holy, you might bristle a bit. If you come from a background where you happily turn a thrift store score into a room full of giggles despite its dents, you’ll fit right in.

## Gameplay loop and strategy

Gloom 2nd Edition revolves around a deceptively simple loop: collect gloom, tell a story, minimize your own family’s happiness, and maximize your opponents’ misery. The heavier the gloom, the better your score, but you want to be careful—if your own family’s misfortune hits a weather-like ceiling, you’ll lose points before the round ends. The balance between storytelling and card management is where the game earns its stripes.

### Core mechanics in a nutshell

- Storytelling: Each turn, you narrate a fragment about a character, weaving in gloom effects that increase misery. A well-timed flourish can swing the talk-track and influence the mood of the table.
- Card drafting: You draft gloom cards and place them on your family cards to increase their misery. The art and wording provide hooks for storytelling, which means you’ll be improvising more than you expect.
- Hidden information: Much of the strategy comes from not revealing too much about your cards too soon. The other players’ reactions and guesses drive a surprising amount of pressure and fun.
- Endgame and scoring: At the end of the round, you reveal all gloom cards on your families and tally misery. Scores rise and fall with dramatic flair, which can lead to triumphs that feel earned or collapses that feel deliciously petty.

### Tips for maximizing efficiency (and laughs)

- Start with a strong personality: A character who is terrible at social events but somehow charming in despair can anchor your storytelling. Let their gloom define the narrative early.
- Slow and steady wins the race—into the mausoleum: Early gloom can be risky, but a controlled buildup can lead to a dramatic finish. Don’t be shy about letting others move the rug under you and then watching your tale twist into a glorious, ironic ending.
- Read the room: Gloom is half rules, half improv. If your table is more comedic than dramatic, lean into punchy lines and quick cuts rather than long epic sagas.

### When to push and when to hold back

Gloom rewards bold storytelling, but there are strategic moments where restraint pays off. If you reveal too much about your gloom early, others will adapt and you’ll lose the surprise factor. Conversely, a perfectly timed lament can trigger laughter, sympathy, and most importantly, a spike in your gloom score. The trick is to find the rhythm that fits your group: some tables prefer a rapid-fire, show-stealing narration; others savor slow-burn gloom that builds tension across several turns.

## How to evaluate a used copy for value

When assessing a used copy, consider the following:

- Price vs. condition: If the price is significantly discounted due to wear, the game may still offer excellent value. If the rulebook is dog-eared but complete, you’re still in good shape. If the cards show heavy creasing or staining, you might want to negotiate.
- Completeness: Confirm all characters and gloom cards are present. Missing cards alter the balance and could dramatically curb the fun.
- Player count compatibility: Ensure the box supports the player count you want. If you only have two players, the game is still playable, but your options are narrower.
- Storage quality: If the box has sustained damage, think about whether it affects the play experience. A well-worn but functional box can be a badge of honor, but you don’t want a lid that keeps springing open mid-game.
