The Marvel edition is built around the same core as the original Codenames: two teams, a Master of Spies, and a grid of 25 words that can point to anything from Iron Man to the laundry list of your to-do items. The twist, of course, is Marvel characters. The deck is loaded with heroes, villains, places, and tech that rhyme with your own pop culture bones. The result is chaotic, silly, and surprisingly elegant when you manage to thread a clue through a dozen characters without being the villain in your own story.

## What’s in the box
The Marvel edition keeps the same box layout as the base game but sprinkles in glossy, action-packed art. The primary advantage is that the characters you recognize from the MCU are not arbitrary: you’ll find a lot of Spider-Man, Iron Man, Captain America, Thor, Hulk, Black Widow, and the occasional villain cameo that should trigger a groan from your group.

Inside the box you’ll find:
- 200+ double-sided Agent cards featuring Marvel characters and pulp-style art
- 25 word cards, with new art hints that lean into superhero clichés
- 1 blue/ red key card to show which team is guessing
- 1 gray assassin card (the Deadpool of troubles, sometimes)
- Instructions and a few handy reference sheets

## How to play
### Setup
Like most party games, setup is a micro-assembly: shuffle the 25-word cards and lay them in a 5 by 5 grid. Decode who is who by drawing a key card that reveals which words belong to which team. The spy masters sit across from their teams, armed with a stack of clues that often include one Marvel reference and a whole lot of risk. The board is laid out; the teams choose colors, and the game begins.

### The turn structure
- Each turn, one spy master looks at their key card and chooses one clue to guide their team to multiple words on the board. Clues must be a single word and a number; the number tells you how many words on the board the clue references. The trick here is to avoid the assassin word and the opponent words.
- The team can keep guessing until they either hit a wrong word, decide to stop, or exhaust the number associated with the clue. If a correct word is found, the turn continues up to the limit; missteps mean a quick swing to the other team. It’s a delicate dance of logic, memory, and a healthy dose of luck.

### End of round
- The round ends when either team identifies all of its words or when someone triggers the assassin word, ending the round. The next spy master rotates in, and the game continues.

## Marvel flavor and theme integration
The licensing adds a lot of flavor without turning the game into a pure fan-service event. The Marvel deck is full of character names, though you’ll often find that some names feel more iconic than others, like Captain America or Spider-Man. The synergy between words is where the magic happens: abundance of names that can be clued through a shared theme (e.g., a character’s ability, a movie plot, or a location in the Marvel Universe). The art direction leans on bright colors and action lines, which creates a party vibe that is visually striking across the table.

## Strategy and tips
- Don’t chase a perfect clue: The board is a buffet of options, not a dictionary. A single clever clue like New York, 4 can connect Spider-Man, Iron Man, and the Avengers-themed nicknames, but you’ll also risk stepping on an assassin if you’re not careful.
- Use Marvel knowledge to your advantage: If your team has seen enough MCU movies, you can make connections between characters and events that would not be obvious to non-fans. Just don’t overdo it; you don’t want to turn the table into a spoiler zone.
- Balance team-friendly vs. high-risk clues: If your teammates struggle with a theme, move to safer but smaller clues to maintain momentum. The thrill lies in successfully linking two or three words with a single clue, not in becoming a human decoder ring.

## Multiplayer vibes
The social energy is the big win here. This edition shines at parties, family nights, and those awkward post-game conversations where everyone pretends to understand your brilliant pun. It scales well from 4 players to large groups; the trick is to rotate spy masters frequently enough that someone else gets a turn to bask in glory or risk being humiliated on social media.

## Artwork, components, and quality
The art is bold, comic-book-inspired, and a little chaotic—perfect for capturing the energy of a Marvel property. The quality of the cards is good, with a slightly glossy finish that stands up to repeated shuffles. The key cards are clear enough to see from across the table, which means fewer squinted eyes and more time to make bad jokes. Construction-wise, the box is sturdy and easy to pack away, though the fan in me would have liked a few extra storage inserts for the spare cards. If you travel with friends, the compact packaging is a blessing rather than a curse.

## Comparisons and variants
- Core Codenames vs Marvel: The core gameplay remains the same, so if you’ve played original Codenames, you’ll feel right at home. The Marvel version adds theme and a lot of character density which can both help and hinder. For some groups, the characters become a shorthand that speeds up the guessing; for others, the names are so loaded with pop culture baggage that it becomes almost impossible to decouple concept from reference.
- Other variants (like Codenames: Pictures, Codenames: Deep Undercover) lean into different kinds of wordplay. Marvel isn’t necessarily the best gateway edition for newbies, but it is a terrific entry point for Marvel fans who want a party game that doesn’t require memorizing 5,000 science facts.

## House rules and optional twists
If you want to mix up the typical flow, try a twist: allow a single-turn guess with two clues if the team is confident. Or play with a round limit to discourage marathon sessions. For those who crave more interplay, you can implement a “team swap” rule where after every assassin reveal, the opposing team can steal one word, mirroring a dramatic movie exchange.

## Accessibility and language notes
The Inglés edition is crafted with an English-speaking audience in mind, which means a lot of the humor and pun logic hinges on wordplay that English players recognize instantly. For players who are learning English or who speak another language at home, some clues may land a little more ambiguously. That’s part of the charm and the challenge: the game rewards flexible thinking and shared cultural shorthand. If you’re hosting a multilingual group, consider using bilingual clueing where possible and accept that some puns will land in a second language. The Marvel license adds a further hurdle for non-fans, but in a good way: it invites everyone to weigh whether a clue lands on “iconic character” or “movie moment,” which is a surprisingly satisfying cognitive load.

## Replayability and group dynamics
One of the biggest wins of this edition is the way it scales with your group. A four-player round can feel intimate, with two spy masters sharing the burden of guessing. A larger group, however, can turn into a carnival of guesses, mispronunciations, and bursts of laughter when a clue clearly lands in an unintended universe. The Marvel theme adds new memory anchors that help players recall which characters belong to which words. You’ll find yourself recalling specific movie moments and character abilities as if you were in a trivia night, which is a wonderful bonus for fans.

If you want to bring superhero vibes into a social word game, this edition is a solid choice, delivering both comic relief and tense did-we-guess-it moments. It’s fast, fun, and surprisingly clever when the right players bring the right energy.
