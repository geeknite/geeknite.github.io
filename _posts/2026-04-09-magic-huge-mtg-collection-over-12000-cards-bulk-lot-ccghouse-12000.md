### Sorting and inventory: the first real challenge
Sorting is the core of this experience. Without a plan, you’ll spin your wheels for days and end up with a mountain of unsorted cardboard. A few recommended strategies:
- Quick triage: separate into four piles—lands, commons, uncommons, and rares/mythics. You’ll be surprised how many “hooded stranger” rares lurk in the deep of bulk.
- Rare and mythic screening: use light-sorting to skim for shiny edges and foil hints first. This gives you quick snacks of potential value to pull out before you bury yourself in a sea of basics.
- Color and set tracking: group cards by color identity and set. This helps if you’re looking to reconstruct a specific format, like Standard for a limited window or EDH for a pre-constructed commander theme.
- Condition notes: mark damaged souls and misprints so you don’t attempt to use them for anything but bargaining chips at a yard sale.

A practical inventory method: a two-tier approach. Build a master list of the playable/common pool, and separately maintain a “specials” folder for rares, foils, and the handful of cards that appear to be worth a closer look. If you’re tech-savvy, you can pilot a basic CSV inventory with fields like card name, set, rarity, condition, expected value, and notes. If you’re not chart-savvy, a simple notebook or a whiteboard will do, as long as you commit to it.

## Handling Damage, Condition, and Authenticity

Damage is the color of reality when you’re dealing with bulk. Expect edge wear, binder rings hugging the corners, slight warps from humidity, and occasionally a sticker from a prior owner that somehow stuck the entire card to a binder page. A simple rule: if you’re planning to play, you want cards in good to near-mint condition for your real decks, and you want to separate everything else for bulk sale or trade. If you’re a collector, you’ll want to be even more meticulous in your triage.

Authenticity concerns can be overstated but not ignored. In bulk, you’ll sometimes encounter misprints, counterfeit-looking cards, or promos that may not be genuine in the strict sense. If something looks too good to be true or seems suspiciously valuable for its stated set, treat it with skepticism and consult known reference sources or a trusted local store before making a big decision.

Storage is another dimension of bulk. Plan for stacking boxes, Binders, and deck boxes. Consider the following:
- Card sleeves for playable piles to prevent further damage during handling.
- Stackable storage bins with dividers for easy classification (color identity, set, rarity).
- Humidity control to prevent warping and mildew in damp climates.
- A tidy workspace where you can physically spread out this many cards without causing a small earthquake in your house.
