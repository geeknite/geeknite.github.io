## What makes the 88s special? Design, indices, and a little history
### Design and aesthetics
The 88 in the deck’s name isn’t just a number; it’s a vibe. The “Blue Jumbo Index” variant is a rare flavor of Bicycle decks produced to appeal to those who value legibility over tiny elegance. Jumbo indices mean each card’s numeral and each face’s pips are printed larger than typical modern Bicycle decks. It’s like upgrading from an ordinary highway to a scenic byway where the road signs shout at you with friendly flair. The end result is a deck that’s easy on the eyes from across the table, particularly for older players or those with less-than-stellar vision after a long gaming night.

The artwork on Bicycle decks has always walked the line between classic and modern. On the 88 Blue Jumbo, you’ll notice the familiar corner indices on the number cards but with a larger footprint. The court cards—King, Queen, Jack—get that vintage Bicycle treatment: clean line work, bold silhouettes, and a surface that photographs well under game lamps. The blue tone of the tuck box and the back design tend to be slightly more saturated in older print runs, giving the deck a pop that modern iterations attempt to replicate but rarely match in the same tactile way.

### Material and finish
USPCC historically used a stock that feels substantial without being too stiff—an invitation to riffle and fan. The finish historically used on many Bicycle decks is “air-cushion” or something akin to it, which translates to a comfortable feel in hand and a pleasing snap when you throw a few cards into a fan. On the 88 Blue Jumbo, you’ll often find a finish that remains relatively durable even after years of storage in a hobby box; the edges show less fray than some of the thinner modern stocks. If you’re a cardist who loves smooth motion, you’re likely to notice the way the blue backs catch light and how the jumbo pips respond to your fingers. If you’re a magic-minded reader, you might find the finish friendly for certain classic palms and flourish sequences—though beware: the Jumbo Indices can sometimes interact with certain misdirection tricks by giving away the index too readily to a trained eye.

### Printing and color fidelity
The Ohio production line that produced many Historical Bicycle decks has a reputation for consistent color and print quality. With the 88 Blue Jumbo, the palette centers around a deep blue—more saturated than a lot of modern “blue” varieties. If you’re a photographer, you’ll love how the color reads in macro shots, where the corner pips and faces pop with a slight sheen. The back design tends to be symmetrical, which is great for peeks, shuffles, and that satisfying moment when you see a perfect deck domino effect in a video or a real-life demonstration.

## Caring for your vintage deck: storage, handling, and display
### Handling tips
- Wash hands, dry thoroughly, and handle the deck with care. Vintage stock can be more brittle, and the jumbo sizes may suffer more from repeated bending if you are a heavy shuffler.
- Use a soft cloth or microfiber rag when cleaning faces or edges, and avoid harsh chemicals that could degrade back designs or inks.
- When fanning, practice light, controlled motions. Jumbo indices can make the deck feel “bigger” than it actually is; you don’t need to palm a full 88 to get a nice fan—though it’s fun to try.

### Storage considerations
- Keep decks in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight. UV light can fade the blue tones and make the white borders look dingier over time.
- Store in a protective case or a humidity-controlled environment to avoid warping. A simple card collector case with a silica gel pack can do wonders.
- If you keep multiple vintage decks, consider labeling them by era and variant to avoid mixing up boxes that look similar—which is a real temptation when your hobby table resembles a small library of colored paper.

### Display ideas
- A glass display case with a bright LED strip can highlight the blue hue and the bold jumbo pips.
- A staged game night setup with a selected few vintage decks around a central card trick or board game can create a narrative for guests who visit your hobby space.

Cardists, if you’re into flourishes, may appreciate the deck’s balance and feel, but the Jumbo Index can alter the “flow.” If you’re building a video routine, the Jumbo pips could be a feature that reads well on camera; if you’re doing a close-up, you might opt for a standard-index deck to avoid distraction.

## Authenticity, condition, and how to tell a real from a pretend
### Signs of authenticity
- Back design consistency: Vintage Bicycle decks have a distinctive back pattern—mirror-like symmetry with precise alignment. If the back looks off-center or misaligned in ways that seem unusual for a Bicycle deck, question it.
- Seal integrity: The original tuck seal should be intact and crisp. If the seal is damaged, re-seal or verify its period with the packaging details.
- Card stock weight: The feel should be sturdy but not overly rigid; age can lead to a slightly more brittle edge, but a well-preserved deck maintains a playable feel.

### Common fakes and how to spot them
- Recolored blues: Some counterfeit decks attempt to mimic the blue hue but end up with slightly washed or off-kilter colors. Compare with authentic images from reputable sources.
- Uneven print quality: Vintage pieces tend to have a consistent print quality given the era and method. If you see obvious print run defects that look modern or machine-made, be skeptical.
- Missing or altered text: Pay attention to the tuck box’s typography and any state or location marks (like Ohio) that historically accompany the deck. Variants with altered branding may be fakes.
