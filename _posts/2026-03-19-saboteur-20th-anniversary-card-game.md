## How to Play in 30 Minutes or Less

Saboteur thrives on a simple, repeatable cycle: lay path cards to connect from the starting point to a treasure, decide who you trust—or pretend to trust—and use action cards to derail the honest miners or protect your own route to glory. The 20th Anniversary edition maintains the core loop, but with a few tweaks to keep the “one more round” vibe intact.

### Turn Structure (H3)

1. Draw a card from the deck.
2. Play a path card to extend the tunnel, or a special card to influence the board or players. If you draw a card that would break the path you’ve been stitching, you may need to pivot your plan and improvise a little; improvisation is basically rule #1 among dwarves.
3. Discard any cards you don’t want to keep and pass turn to the next player.
4. If the path leads to a treasure, those players vote to determine who sees the treasure first or if it’s even there at all (because in Saboteur, sometimes the treasure is a mirage conjured by the table’s collective paranoia).

The process repeats until the treasure is claimed by a group, or a clever saboteur forces a misfire and sends the table into chaos. The 30-minute clock is more of a social timer than a real one; you’ll know you’ve been sucked into Dwarven Negotiations when you’ve spent two rounds discussing whether or not your comrades deserve the glory of a gold tile.

### Roles and Objectives (H3)

- Miners: Work together (or pretend to) to create a path that reaches the treasure. Your victory hinges on quietly guiding a route without giving away the treasure having been found.
- Saboteurs: This is where the fun begins. Saboteurs try to derail the route, misdirect the group, or pin the blame on a different player. Your job is to be the perfect mix of honest-seeming and ultra-suspicious.

There can be several saboteurs in a game depending on the number of players. The dynamic tension comes from having multiple agendas in the same tunnel, which is basically the dwarven version of social deduction in a cave made of cardboard.

## Mechanics and Design: Why It Feels Familiar Yet Fresh

The core mechanic of Saboteur is path-building with a dash of player interaction. The 20th Anniversary edition doesn’t radically change the bones of the game; instead, it polishes and refines the ride:

- Path Cards: These form the literal routes to treasure, and their arrangement can make or break a run. Some cards feature bridges, others trapdoors, and a handful allow you to block a tunnel while pretending you’re merely “adjusting map coordinates.” The path cards demand both foresight and a little bit of luck, because you’ll often draw a card that changes your entire plan mid-round.
- Role Cards: Clear roles help players know which side they’re on, but not exactly what each neighbor is thinking. That ambiguity is the lifeblood of the game: a good bluff can keep your team’s path safe while slowly choking the life out of another player’s plan.
- Action Cards: These include adding or removing cards from the path, peeking at other players’ cards, or forcing a discard. The cheeky nature of these cards is what makes Saboteur replayable: you can use them to protect a route, sabotage someone you don’t like, or just nudge the table toward chaos for the sake of entertainment.
- Balance and Pace: The Anniversary edition has a few balance tweaks that feel natural. The distribution of role cards remains a tense secret, but you’ll notice that certain combinations appear with a little more or less frequency than before. This helps prevent stagnation and encourages more aggressive play in later rounds, which is exactly what you want in a game about dwarves who love drama almost as much as gold.

Overall, Saboteur remains a wonderful gateway to social deduction wrapped in a family-friendly package. It’s light enough to teach in one sitting, yet rich enough to keep a room of adult players from devolving into pure chaos—though that chaos is part of the charm. The Anniversary edition pulls this off with a wink and a nod to longtime fans.

## Strategy and Bluffing: A Gentle Guide to Scheming

If you’re new to Saboteur, start with the simplest advice: don’t overshare your intentions. The moment you reveal that you’re a miner with a path towards the treasure, you’ll be a magnet for suspicion. The real skill is in painting a plausible story while playing cards that advance your own goals and, ideally, make your neighbors look suspicious of each other.

- Early game: Set up a flexible tunnel. You want to have a few options so you’re not trapped if a sabotage card hits the table. This is where you practice your diplomacy face and your “I’m totally not sabotaging you” voice.
- Mid-game: Use action cards to protect your route while sowing seeds of doubt elsewhere. A well-timed card can redirect a group’s focus, letting you slip into the treasure’s glow.
- Late game: Decide whether to reveal the treasure with a flourish or to keep your plans vague to maintain the drama. If you over-commit, you risk being caught in a lie; if you under-commit, you lose the dramatic prize you were chasing.

One of the game’s joys is how different personalities handle bluffing. Some players lean into the performance aspect, delivering monologues as they twist the board; others prefer a quiet, almost robotic approach that relies on micro-expressions and a well-timed smear of doubt. Either way, the 20th Anniversary edition rewards players who lean into the social theatrics without turning the table into a screaming parrot cage.
