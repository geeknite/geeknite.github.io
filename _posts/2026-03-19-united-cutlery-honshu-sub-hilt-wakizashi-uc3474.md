Unboxing a budget Honshu is a ritual, folks. In the box you’ll typically find the wakizashi itself, a lacquered scabbard (saya), and a modest set of screws and a stand if you’re feeling fancy. Again, this is par for the course for UC’s Honshu line and not a knock against the product but a reality check for the curious shopper.


## Quick Specs and What They Mean

Below is a practical quick-spec dump you can skim if you’re shopping during a caffeine-fueled Saturday. These numbers are typical for the Honshu Sub-Hilt family and should be treated as approximate rather than exact lab results:

- Overall length: roughly in the 18–22 inch neighborhood. This makes it a compact little dagger-sibling to the longer katana offerings and perfect for display or cosplay waggery.
- Blade length: around 11–14 inches. Short enough to be easily handled but long enough to give you a solid silhouette when sheathed in the scabbard.
- Blade material: commonly stainless or 1065 carbon in budget lines; don’t expect pristine high carbon edge-holding, but do expect a blade that looks the part and isn’t a fragile toy.
- Edge: usually non-surgical sharp out of the box; some reviewers report only a light polish—enough to slice paper if you apply a light touch. If you want a razor, budget for reshaping or a professional sharpening, and perhaps a torch to heat-treat your expectations.
- Guard: a robust looking tsuba-style piece with a darker finish. It’s theatrical rather than practical protection, which suits cosplay and display well.
- Handle: wrap that simulates ray skin, over a steel or zinc-aluminum core; the result is a grip that feels substantial, even if it’s not a sub-0.5-inch thick slice of ergonomic perfection.
- Scabbard (saya): wooden core with lacquer; a reasonable fit for the blade with a snug slide in and out, though you’ll want to avoid forcing it if it sticks—just like you don’t force a plot twist on a good story, you let it unfold naturally.

The important takeaway here is that UC3474 is designed for look and vibe rather than battlefield-grade performance. If your cosplay needs a blade that photographs well at arm’s length, this is a solid candidate. If you want a wired-for-battle, precise steel tool, you’ll likely want to look elsewhere.

## Scabbard, Display, and Care

### Saya and Mounting
The saya is smooth to the touch with a lacquer finish that resists fingerprints; remember that lacquer can scuff if you rub it aggressively. The connection between blade and saya is snug but not problematic; you won’t be muttering curses while drawing in a photo booth. If you’re using it for display, a standard wall mount or a small stand will show off the blade’s silhouette nicely. The scabbard’s color harmonizes with the handle, creating a cohesive look that works well in photo backdrops or a glass case.

### Maintenance Tips
- Wipe the blade with a soft, dry cloth after handling to minimize fingerprints. If you plan to display it long-term, you may apply a light coat of oil recommended for display swords; use sparingly.
- Avoid exposing the blade to corrosive environments or salt spray; it’s not designed for marine adventures or heavy outdoor use.
- Check the wrap periodically for looseness. If you notice the wrap slipping, you can reseat it carefully or consult a local prop-sword professional for help.
- Never attempt to sharpen the blade aggressively. The UC3474 is designed for looks; if you want an edge, it’s best to have it professionally tuned by a swordsman who knows the material and finish.

### Display Versus Active Use
For display, UC3474 shines. For cosplay, you’ll love the look and the way it photographs alongside costuming textures like leather, foam, and faux fur. For active use or training, you should invest in a purpose-built practice blade and training equipment. The difference between a cosplay prop and a training weapon is myth-and-legend-level serious when you’re in the dojo or at the con floor ready to duel your own glow-in-the-dark sword prop.
