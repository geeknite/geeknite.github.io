## Why Folios? The Design Philosophy Behind Accessory Lot
City of Mist is a game built around mythic identities and personal legends. The system thrives on quick data access and an emphasis on narrative hooks rather than crunchy numbers. Folios that mirror this ethos can feel like a lifeline rather than a burden. Son of Oak’s pack leans into a few core design choices:

- Clarity over clutter: Each folio presents the core identity, the best-bits of the myth, and a couple of quick conflict prompts without folding your gaming table into a data-entry swamp.
- Aesthetic coherence: The art direction nods to noir alleys, neon signage, and mythic symbols. It’s not just “pretty art” in a vacuum; the visuals cue the mood and help frame a story hook at a glance.
- Print-friendly but durable: The pages are made for print, but the design tolerates rough handling. If you’re printing at home on standard inkjet stock, you’ll be surprised at how well they hold up in a sleeve.

The result is a product that feels like an expansion of the City of Mist universe rather than a static add-on. It’s a tool, not a souvenir, and that makes a meaningful difference when you’re trying to weave a campaign together without a binder full of ancient runes.

## Playability: How It Affects Game Night
A product this explicit about playability should pass a basic test: does it reduce friction without reducing fun? In my sessions, the folios did the following:

- Reduced table talk time around character basics. Instead of hunting for a sheet, players could simply point to their folio and say, “That’s my myth.” The rest of the table could focus on questions that mattered to the scene, not to the stat block.
- Created faster NPC engagements. NPC folios include quick hooks like faction ties or a rumor they’re spreading. This made social scenes loop more smoothly—no awkward pauses while someone fumbles for a line to say as an alias.
- Linked personal myths to on-table consequences. City of Mist thrives on the tension between who you pretend to be and what your myth compels you to do. Folios help highlight that dynamic with just a glance.

Of course, no tool is perfect. If you’re one of those players who loves 18th-level micro-meta-story arcs, you might find that folios condense some complexity. That’s not a flaw so much as a design choice: you’re buying a launchpad for ideas, not a complete script for every session. The best use-case is to let folios spark micro arcs: a rumor that expands into a side quest; a relationship that becomes a running thread across multiple sessions; a personal myth that collides with a citywide mystery.
