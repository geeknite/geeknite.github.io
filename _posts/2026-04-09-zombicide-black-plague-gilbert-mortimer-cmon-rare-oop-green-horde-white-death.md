## Gilbert & Mortimer: The duo that looks like it wandered off a Victorian postcard

### Who are Gilbert & Mortimer?
Gilbert and Mortimer aren’t the typical knights with shiny helmets and a war cry. They’re more like a pair of gentlemen adventurers who traded in their monocles for crossbows and leather armor, and somehow didn’t lose their sense of propriety while the zombie apocalypse lurched in from the fog. In the game, they’re a two-figure combo that merges into the Black Plague experience as a rare, coveted duo (as opposed to the standard survivors you unlock in the base game). They bring a distinct flavor: clever, a little bit sneaky, and with abilities that feel designed to poke holes in the zombies’ carefully laid plans.

If you’ve played through a few Zombicide campaigns, you know that the “rare” status isn’t just about shiny plastic. It’s about the little stories those miniatures tell as you plop them on the board: Gilbert might neutralize danger with a witty quip that distracts the undead; Mortimer could be that risk-loving risk-averse teammate who somehow makes the impossible look easy. Put together, they provide a duo that can bend certain scenarios to your advantage, given you’ve got the patience and the market prices to prove it.

### The gameplay flavor they bring
Gilbert & Mortimer aren’t simply cosmetic. In the board game’s ecosystem, they offer synergy that can change your early-game choices in meaningful ways. The player using them might find themselves splitting the team to create strategic fires and fog-of-war distractions, or they might rely on the duo’s specific mission cards that reward calculated risk rather than brute force. They’re not invincible, but they’re cunning, which is half the battle in a zombie-infested fantasy realm where brute force often leaves you clutching a splintered spear and a hope and a prayer.

### Rarity and collectible status: why everyone loves to chase them
Here’s the thing about rare OOP CMON items: the value isn’t just monetary. It’s about the memory of the hunt. When Gilbert & Mortimer first popped up as a scarce set, collectors treated them like a limited edition poster you’d sworn you’d frame properly “someday.” Someday arrived as a skyrocketing price, a few “mint on card” fantasies, and enough marketplace drama to fill a season of a soap opera nobody asked for. The OOP tag isn’t just a label; it’s a tiny, brag-worthy badge you can flash at your next game night while everyone jokes about who paid the mortgage for your hobby habit.

The practical takeaway? If you’re a completionist, Gilbert & Mortimer are a holy grail you’ll chase the way you chase that last piece of mozzarella in the pizza box: with a steady heart and a stubborn grin. If you’re a casual player, you’ll enjoy their flavor and their dynamics once you’ve secured a copy at a price that won’t require selling a kidney in the black market of board-game retailers. And if you’re a pure collector who loves the aesthetics, the sculpts themselves—dapper outfits, expressive faces, and a certain theatricality—make for a great display piece on a shelf that already looks like a diorama from a museum of oddities.

### The miniatures: quality and aesthetics
The Gilbert & Mortimer crew typically arrives as two distinct sculpts, each with that classic CMON style: crisp lines, clean faces, and enough texture on their gear to make you wonder if your tabletop needs a safety inspector. The paint job in the typical pre-owned market photos looks splendidly vintage—the kind of look that says “we’ve seen a thing or two, and we’ll tell you about it if you listen.” The detail is good enough to satisfy the hobbyist who loves to convert, prime, and paint them in a style that nods to Victorian necro-adventures without crossing into cosplay territory.

In terms of model quality, these miniatures won’t disintegrate in your hands, and they’ll stand up to the usual rough-tabletop life. They’re not likely to win any “most dynamic pose” awards, but they don’t sulk on the base, either. If you’re hunting for a set that looks good in a glass cabinet and plays well in a dungeon-crawling campaign, Gilbert & Mortimer strike a nice balance between display appeal and functional tabletop use.

## Green Horde: Welcome to the party, orcs with manners and menace

### Theme and mechanics
Green Horde is the expansion that adds a slice of green chaos to your Black Plague table. Orc zombies, chaotic hordes, and new encounter cards mean you’re not facing the same predictable zombie responses every time. The aesthetic is distinct: wood-and-metal accents, crude armor that somehow looks menacing rather than silly, and a swarm dynamic that forces you to scramble. The mechanical impact is straightforward but effective: extra threat, new paths, and a set of tokens that make the board feel more alive (and more dangerous).

For Gilbert & Mortimer players, the Green Horde introduces new tactical layers. You’ll want to plan your routes with an eye for evacuation space and choke points. The orc minis can overwhelm you with numbers, so the duo’s brains become even more valuable as you manage the board’s geography rather than just piling bodies onto the table.

### Cards and minis: what’s in the box for Green Horde
The expansion adds new zombie types and encounter decks that lean into the wild, unpredictable nature of the green threat. The miniatures share that 28mm scale that makes them comfortable on standard zombie-horror boards, while the token mix adds new ways to track damage and status effects. The overall vibe is a little more feral than the core game, which is a good reminder that in Zombicide, not every wave of the undead is politely marching in a straight line.

### How it plays with Gilbert & Mortimer
Gilbert & Mortimer gain from a more dynamic environment. They can use the Green Horde’s new situational cards to set up pinches and traps, making their cleverness even more valuable. The aesthetic of the orcs, with their rugged design and chitinous shields, provides a nice contrast to the aristocratic vibe of the duo, which makes the board feel more alive and a touch more cinematic.

## White Death: A snowbound curveball in a zombie apocalypse

### Who is White Death, and why does a board game care?
White Death is a nod to a harsher, colder theater of war—perfect for a winter campaign where the undead look slightly more menacing under a frost-light. In the Zombicide ecosystem, White Death is an expansion that adds a different flavor of challenge: environment-driven obstacles, arctic hazards, and a distinct set of mission cards that reward careful movement and resource management. The contrast between White Death’s icy aesthetics and Gilbert & Mortimer’s Victorian charm creates a delicious tonal clash on the table.

### Interaction with Gilbert & Mortimer
In campaigns that mix White Death with Gilbert & Mortimer, you’re playing a sort of “two detectives in a snowstorm” scenario. Mortimer’s flair and Gilbert’s methodical approach feel perfectly suited to a setting where every move could freeze the party in place or force you to improvise a clever workaround. The synergy isn’t about raw power; it’s about managing risk, leveraging environment, and letting the duo’s planning shine when the board becomes a chessboard of frost and fear.

## Gameplay implications: does Gilbert & Mortimer change the game?

### Core rules compatibility
In practice, Gilbert & Mortimer slot into Black Plague without requiring a full rulebook re-install. They function within the core mechanics—turns, action economy, zombie spawns, and the inevitable “oh no, there are too many zombies” moment. Their abilities don’t overturn core strategy; they augment it. The duo tends to reward planning, careful positioning, and timing—a classic recipe for a game that rewards players who think three turns ahead instead of just swinging swords now.

### Interactions with Green Horde and White Death
The Green Horde’s extra threats push the duo to use their planning capabilities more aggressively. It’s easy to fall into a trap where brute force seems like the simplest answer, but the Green Horde environment invites you to think about space, crowd control, and safe routes more than ever. White Death compounds the challenge with environmental hazards that can punish reckless advances. Gilbert & Mortimer’s brains-and-cobblestones approach becomes particularly valuable in winter campaigns and snow-on-board scenarios where a single misstep can derail the entire round.

### Replayability and scenario variety
With the two expansions, you don’t just get more pieces—you get more scenario variety. The rare nature of Gilbert & Mortimer adds to the feeling that you’re in a “special event” rather than a routine session. ## Painting, hobby, and display value

### Painting tips for the duo
If you’re into miniature painting, Gilbert & Mortimer offer a nice canvas for contrast. You can lean into Mortimer’s flamboyant vibe with rich reds and golds for trim, while Gilbert can be treated with cooler tones—charcoal over steel and a touch of weathering to emphasize the stoic, older gentleman identity. The key is to keep skin tones natural and avoid over-saturation on the already busy outfits. A light dry brush on belts and buckles helps the texture pop without distracting from the overall silhouette on the table.

### Display and shelf appeal
A well-painted Gilbert & Mortimer commands a moment on a shelf dedicated to “bizarre collectibles” in your games room. They pair nicely with other rare CMON items and can serve as a reminder that the hobby isn’t just about winning—it’s about curating a story that looks great in photos or during a live stream. If you’re mindful of lighting and a glass case, they can be part of a tribute wall to your favorite CMON-era releases.

## Value, market status, and practical buying tips

### Market reality for rare OOP items
As with many CMON rarities, pricing fluctuates with market demand, condition, and the degree to which a seller wants to humiliate your wallet with a “final sale.” The more minty and sealed an item is, the higher the price—often leading to a two-tier market: pristine collectors’ copies and well-loved, played-through pieces that still look fantastic but don’t scream “you paid what for a zombie duo?”

### Practical buying advice
If you’re chasing Gilbert & Mortimer, you’re balancing price against the joy of ownership. Consider setting a maximum bid or price point to avoid the eye-watering moment when a box climbs past your mortgage payment. Also, watch for bundled deals including Green Horde and White Death; sometimes sellers will offer a discount when you buy multiple expansions together. And don’t forget to inspect pictures for primer issues, broken bases, or bent cards—these things can ruin a perfect night of zombie hunting before you roll your first die.
