## How to play: core mechanics

### The general flow

The core of the game remains familiar: players ask others for cards to complete sets. But the flavor text and adult-oriented prompts add a level of meta-humor that makes every request feel like a tiny social dare.

- On your turn, you choose a player and ask for a card rank.
- If that player has any cards of that rank, they must hand them over, and you go again.
- If they do not, you must \"Go Fish\" from the draw pile. Draw one card. If you draw the card you asked for, you go again; otherwise, it becomes the next player's turn.

What makes this version different is that every successful or failed draw might trigger a pre-loaded phrase in the rulebook: fun lines, sound-bites, or prompts that push players into storytelling or embarrassing-but-harmless anecdotes.

### Variants and house rules

There are several variants to fit different groups:

- The \"Truth or Clause\" variant: every time you complete a set, you must tell a truth or accept a dare taken from the flavor deck.
- The \"Flavors of the Sea\" variant: different fish species grant different degrees of laughter or consequences, ensuring that some fish cards prompt goofy dances or exaggerated proclamations.
- The \"Limit Break\" variant: when a player completes a set, the table votes whether to allow a funny, mild disfluency to count as a \"wild card\" for the round.

### Strategy notes

The game rewards social awareness, not just memory. If you have a table full of people who know their audience, you can maximize laughter by tailoring the prompts to someone\'s personality. Don’t press someone too hard if they are not comfortable with certain jokes; the joy of a party game is that it should bring people closer—without pushing people out of the room.

## Practical tips for hosts and players

### Setting boundaries before the game begins

- Set a time limit for jokes and floor time.
- Define what counts as a \"line\" that should not be crossed.
- Decide how to handle a disagreement or discomfort.

### Keeping the game moving

One of the biggest challenges of humor-based games is letting the laughter subside long enough to actually finish rounds. A quick timer for dares or a pre-determined number of jokes per turn can help your group keep momentum.

### Balancing play and party dynamics

The host should act as a facilitator, not a referee. The role of host includes ensuring that new players understand the rules and that everyone has a chance to contribute to the fun. This is a game where inclusion is as important as competition.

## Accessibility and inclusivity notes

Go Fuc Yourself is designed to be accessible at a glance, but humor is heavily reliant on social context. For groups with varied reading levels or non-native English speakers, consider providing simplified prompts and pairing players who can translate or rephrase jokes. The game also benefits from clear visual cues: if a prompt relies on a spoken punchline, a quick text-slash-card version can help those who are shy. If a player uses assistive devices or needs accommodations, you should adapt rules to ensure everyone can participate in a comfortable way. The table should remain a place where everyone can have fun, not a stage where someone feels singled out.
