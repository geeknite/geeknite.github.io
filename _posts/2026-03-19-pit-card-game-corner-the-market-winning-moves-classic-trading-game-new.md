## What’s in the box and what’s new
When you crack open the Winning Moves edition, you’re greeted with the familiar Pit energy: a deck of commodity cards that feel suspiciously chaotic in the best possible way, a compact trading surface (or a big, kitchen-table battlefield depending on your furniture), and the iconic trading bell that sounds less like progress and more like a tiny coin-operated siren of doom. Corner The Market aims to lean into that real-time frenzy by introducing two major updates:

### The Corner tokens and market layout
First, you’ll notice Corner tokens that players can call during negotiations. These tokens act like little “market access cards,” granting temporary advantages or triggering special events when certain combinations of commodities are met. The effect is not just “hurry up and trade” but “strategy under fire,” because you’ll need to decide when to burn a token on a desperate trade or hold it for a bigger payoff later in the round.

Second, the market layout itself hints at a more tactical feel. The four, five, or sometimes six commodity groups (depending on the edition) are color-coded with punchy, almost neon vibes. They want you to shout and gesture at your neighbor with the satisfied confidence of someone who’s finally cornered the sugar market while accidentally cornering the sugar packet with your own elbow. It’s silly in the best possible way and yet somehow still feels like a high-stakes stock exchange on a game night.

### The revised rules and pacing
Pit lives and dies by its pace. The original thrives on speed, energy, and a chorus of trades all at once, with a timer sometimes doing more harm than good. The Corner The Market edition nudges the pacing with a few tweaks:
- A slightly clearer call-and-response for trades to reduce argument time and keep the chaos flowing.
- Slightly longer rounds to give players a moment to savor a well-timed trade without devolving into pure bedlam.
- Optional rule variants for veterans who want an extra layer of strategy on top of the shouting and chaos. You can toggle these in during setup or after a few rounds if you want to level up your own table’s chaos meter.

## How to play (quick refresher)
If you’ve never played Pit or you’ve only played the tamer version on a rainy Sunday, here’s a high-level refresher. This is not a substitute for the full rulebook, but it’ll get you in the game fast so you can start shouting and pointing at your friends before they realize you’ve already cornered the market on coffee beans.

### Objective
The aim is simple: accumulate a complete set of one commodity by trading with everyone at the table. The moment you get a full set, you shout a loud, primal victory cry and hope the rest of the players are too busy giggling to object. In Corner The Market, there are extra dynamics around “corners” and tokens that can nudge you toward victory faster or crash you into a hilarious mug-clinking loss. Either way, chaos is guaranteed.

### Setup
- Shuffle the commodity cards and deal them out to players so that everyone starts with roughly the same number of cards.
- Place the Corner tokens within reach of all players.
- Decide if you’re using the standard rules or the optional variants. It’s a good habit to set expectations before the first sweep of cards hits the table.
- Mount the trading surface as you prefer: a table, a board, or a clear floor with appropriate space. This is a game that benefits from room to wave your hand dramatically as you propose a trade.

### Turn structure
1) In real-time fashion, players start trading. The moment you have a card that someone else needs and you’re willing to trade, you shout the trade. The goal is to assemble a complete set of a commodity—this is where the chaos factor becomes the star of the show.
2) Other players monitor and attempt to hijack trades that could benefit them more. The secret sauce of Pit is that there are no turns in the traditional sense; it’s a spontaneous auction of wits and bluster.
3) When someone completes a set, they call “corner” and must hold onto that corner until the end of the round or until a specified rule in your version triggers a new bidding war. The room erupts with a mix of applause, mock outrage, and occasional shoelaces catching on chair legs in your best living-room theatre.

While these steps sound chaotic, the fun lies in how fast and how loud you can negotiate while keeping a straight face long enough to avoid spilling cola on your friend’s cat poster. It’s a performance. It’s a math-free math party, and it’s glorious.

### Trading tips and strategy (the what-not-to-do list)
- Communicate like a telepathic auctioneer: You don’t need perfect information to win; you need the confidence to pretend you have perfect information.
- Watch the table, not just your own cards. The moment you notice two players exchanging looks, be ready to jump in with a quick trade. Your timing matters more than the specific cards you hold.
- Use Corner tokens strategically. Don’t waste them early unless you have a plan that will pay off in the next few moves. You want to feel clever, not lucky.
- Don’t be a hero with a single commodity. Diversify your investments. That way, even if someone corners your favorite fruit, you still have other sets that can blossom into a victory.
- Have fun with the banter. Pit is a social experiment with a bell. The more you lean into the chaos, the more you’ll enjoy the game.
