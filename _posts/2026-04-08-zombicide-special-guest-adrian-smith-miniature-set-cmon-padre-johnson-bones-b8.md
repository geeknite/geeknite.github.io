## Unboxing and First Impressions

Unboxing begins with the familiar CMON packaging: a sturdy cardboard spine, a clear plastic window, and enough safety blister packs to make a pharmacist anxious. The Padre Johnson figure sits on a sprue of medium complexity; you can tell the sculptor took extra care with the iconography on the vest, the rosary beads, and the nail-pierced cross motif that hints at a backstory where religion and the undead share a complicated relationship. The Bones B8 figure is more experimental in its silhouette, with a leering stance and a base that looks part ritual altar, part puzzle—exactly the kind of thing that makes paint-masters sigh with equal parts joy and terror.

## The Minis: Design and Scale

Adrian Smith's style shines through immediately. The sculpt carries his trademark bio-organic textures: sinewy muscle, cracked bone, and a palette that leans into bone-white with bruised purples and sickly greens. The result is a set that photographs well and presents a sturdy challenge on the painting table. If you like your monsters to have a spine-tingling, almost architectural detail, you will fall in love with how the Bones B8 figure catches light and shadow in a way that makes your camera scream with delight.

The kit includes all the usual Zombicide extras: a set of zombie spawn tokens, a couple of survivor cards, and the standard dice that make you feel like you are rolling your own small fate. The cards have the same polished CLI as other CMON products, with clean typography and a touch of stylized art that ties back to Adrian Smith’s aesthetic. It is, in other words, a well-rounded package that does not try to pretend it is something else. It is a specialty set for a hobby that loves to show off.

The minis themselves go together with more ease than you might fear. The Bones B8 figure is a classic Reaper-like resin feel translated into CMON plastic—snug sockets, minimal flash, and an air of robust durability. The Padre Johnson model is a little more delicate, with tiny insignia and a delicate cross on the belt buckle. If you are a gluing purist, you might want to pin the torso and leg joints for extra stability, but in most cases a dab of good plastic glue and a careful clamp will suffice. If you are painting with a beginner’s brush, you will still be able to pull off a nice result with a little patience; the sculpt's texture does the heavy lifting, letting your blues, browns, and bone whites sing.

In terms of scale, these minis align with the standard Zombicide fighters: close to 32 mm heroic scale, with a slightly exaggerated silhouette to read well on camera and on the table. The base diameters are wide enough to ensure stability on chaotic boards and to support dynamic poses without tipping over during a tense moment when your survivor goes runner-up to the last zombie. The overall footprint is not excessive, which means you can include them in a standard CMON board setup without crowding out your other favorites.

## The Artistic Direction and Flavor

Now, let us talk about the artistic direction. Adrian Smith’s contribution brings an epic fantasy vibe to a modern zombie apocalypse. This is a marriage of two genres that seldom share a meal: grimdark fantasy and fast-paced zombie survival. The result is a pair of minis that feel like they could star in a graphic novel—Padre Johnson as the weary cleric with a questionable past, and Bones B8 as a creeping undead not-quite-thing with a face that promises trouble if you look too closely. If you want to stage a moment that looks great in photos, this set is a gold mine. The paintjob possibilities range from high-contrast, heroic tabletop heroes to a dirty, grungy, “I have survived three site evacuations and two earthquakes of rotten weather” look.

Gameplay wise, the set slots into Zombicide as a set of unique survivors with bespoke abilities, or as a special zombie variant depending on how your group adjudicates guest content. It is worth noting that guest minis often require a little house ruling to maintain balance across the campaign; you do not want to overpower the group with a single hero that can shoot, heal, and dismember zombies in a single action. The design philosophy here leans toward giving Padre Johnson a robust, forgiving ability set that rewards tactical movement and teamwork, while Bones B8 offers a more menace-focused presence that can disrupt a plan but can be neutralized with careful targeting and the right card combinations. If you are used to standard Zombicide pacing, you may need a few sessions to recalibrate the rhythm with these two guests on board.

## Gameplay and Utility

The value proposition is nuanced. If you are a collector who wants to own the Adrian Smith edition of Zombicide, this is a must-have. If, on the other hand, you are primarily in it for the gameplay experience, you will still appreciate the novelty factor. The set does not radically change the core mechanics of Zombicide; it enhances the flavor, gives you a couple of hero figures who look spectacular when painted, and adds a touch of narrative weight to your campaigns. The price point, while not the cheapest on the market, is fair given the licensing, the production quality, and the sculpting finesse. If you buy many expansions, you know the cost of good tabletop art, and this set lands in the mid-to-upper range, but with quality that justifies the spend if you care about table presence.

Display and painting tips. The best approach to these minis is to plan a color story that respects Adrian Smith’s palette but also fits your personal style. Start with a dark undercoat, then build up bone whites or cold grays for the exposed bone textures. For Padre Johnson, consider a weathered cleric palette: muted browns for the robe, bone-white accents for the rosaries and crosses, and a touch of metallic for the buckles. You can push the shadows with a quick blue glaze to make the suit and armor look more cinematic, or go high-contrast with a red accent to signal danger and authority. The Bones B8 model benefits from a non-metallic metal approach or a glossy dark wash in the crevices to emphasize its sinewy, almost otherworldly texture. If you are into weathering powders, a pinch of ash and soot on the base can anchor the model in a post-apocalyptic vibe.

Storage and maintenance. After you finish painting, consider storing these minis in a dedicated display tray or an air-tight storage case. The last thing you want is a honking clang as you reach for a binder with your Zombicide cards and you discover Padre Johnson has decided to tilt a little and leak his marvelous aura across your entire board. The Bones B8 figure, with its slightly more fragile silhouette, benefits from being stored away from sharp edges and heavy books—trust me, I speak from experience. A little cotton between the mini bases in the foam tray can prevent scuffs and keep your paint job intact.

## Practical Tips for Assembly

A few quick notes to make the process smoother: wash the plastic to remove the mold release before assembly; use thin plastic glue to avoid excess seepage; pin the Padre Johnson joints for extra stability if you’re playing a campaign with heavy zombie resurrections; and use a fine brush to sculpt the textures on Bones B8 so you don’t miss the tiny sinew lines. If you are a hobbyist who likes a challenge, try a two-tone skin palette for Bones B8 with a sickly green base and a purple undertone; it reads well in photos and adds a supernatural touch to your table narrative. For those who prefer a faster route, a clean airbrush base coat, followed by a wash and a dry brush, can yield excellent results in a single weekend.
