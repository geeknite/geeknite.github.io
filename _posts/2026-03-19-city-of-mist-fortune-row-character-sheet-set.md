## Overview and what makes Fortune Row tick

Fortune Row isn’t just a place on a map. In City of Mist, it’s a mood, a pocket of destiny, a neon-lit alley where destiny, myth, and modern life collide like two super-powered magnets. The Fortune Row Character Sheet Set leans into that vibe with a design language that says: we respect your need for readability while also encouraging you to write aggressively about your own mythic potential. The sheets are designed to be used in long, campaign-scale arcs as well as in tight one-shot missions; it provides flexible templates, placeholders for relationships, and a mythic ladder for power sources.

In the granularity of design, you’ll see a split between what I’d call “mythic locator fields” and “mundane support fields.” The former invites you to fill in your Source of Power, your Name of Fortune, your Masks, and your Rookery of Rivals—these are your core identity hooks. The latter keeps track of mundane-but-critical things: Stress, Conditions, Clues, and the ever-necessary Relationship Strings with NPCs. The balancing act here is smart: you get enough long-haul scaffolding to run a complex arc, but not so much that you drown in the ledger before the second act. In geek terms: it’s the difference between a dungeon map that’s too busy with icons and one that invites you to draw your own dungeon graffiti.


## What’s in the set and how it’s laid out

## Design and usability: readability, accessibility, and practical use

### Visual style and readability
The Fortune Row sheets strike a balance between thematic flourishes and legibility. Font choices are modern, with slightly condensed letterforms that maximize rows per page without sacrificing readability. For players with visual impairments or those who prefer larger type, the aspect ratio of the sheets is forgiving enough that you can print a larger draft without the sheet feeling unwieldy. The color palette uses a high-contrast combination of dark backgrounds with bright accent colors for headings and keys. This makes it easier to scan the sheet in a dim gaming room, where the GM might be wearing a hoodie that used to be a trench coat in a previous edition of your group’s lore.

### Layout flow
The page layout follows a predictable rhythm: identity blocks at the top, mythic paths in the middle, and relationship/plot hooks along the bottom. This layout is deliberate for players who want to build a coherent arc across sessions. It also helps when you’re playing online and screens are taking up your real estate; the consistent flow reduces the cognitive load of swapping sheets mid-session.

### Accessibility considerations
The design team included tactile-friendly checkboxes and sufficient white space to reduce confusion during intense moments. If you’re printing on standard Letter or A4 paper, the margins are generous enough to accommodate a three-hole punch without destroying field labels. For digital play, many of the elements translate well to PDF fillable form fields, which means you can use your favorite PDF editor to type directly on the sheets if you don’t want to scribble in ink.

## How to get the most out of Fortune Row at the table

- Use the relationship blocks to build a web of NPCs who care about your character’s fate. This creates ongoing tension and motivates long-term goals beyond the current scene.
- Treat the Mythic Path section as a living document. Let it evolve with your character’s growth or the city’s shifting fortunes; update it after pivotal scenes rather than waiting for an in-game “downtime.”
- Integrate Clues and Fate tracks into your GM planning. If you’re worried about pacing, you can pre-log a handful of clues for future acts and then reveal them as the story progresses, using Fortune Row’s layout to show how each clue spins into the next.
- Encourage on-table scribbles. The physical act of marking sheets can cement a sense of agency and ownership for players, which is essential for emotionally charged city-alchemy moments.
- If you’re playing online, pair the sheet set with a shared digital whiteboard. You can port the core sections into a collaborative document, which helps keep everyone on the same page during complex plot turns.
