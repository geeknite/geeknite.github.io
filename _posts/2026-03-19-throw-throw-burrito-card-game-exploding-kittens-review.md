## What is Throw Throw Burrito, exactly?
Throw Throw Burrito is the brainchild of a company that turns chaos into commerce and somehow makes it feel educational. At its core, it’s a competitive party game that uses a deck of cards and two soft foam burritos. The objective? Collect matching burrito cards while dodging the relentless assault of burrito-flinging chaos. It’s not a strategy game, it’s a sport—aliased as “dangerous soft projectile diplomacy” by some of our more dramatic colleagues.

The premise is simple: players draw and play cards that either force you to take a hit, force someone else to take a hit, or trigger a mini-round where burritos fly like tiny fluffy meteors. The game thrives on unpredictability, laughter, and maybe a few bruised pride moments when someone you didn’t expect to be athletic suddenly becomes a burrito ninja. If you’re into calm, methodical strategy games, this one isn’t for you. If you’re into bonding with friends through chaos and foam, you’ve found your tribe.

## Setup: less ritual, more chaos
Setting up Throw Throw Burrito is refreshingly quick. You shuffle the deck, lay out a few cards in the center (the burrito targets), and hand each player a burrito to call their own. The game starts with a party vibe that’s almost suspiciously wholesome given the impending chaos. A typical round looks like this:

1) Each player draws a card and quickly reads the instruction—because in a game about throwing foam burritos, you don’t want to be the one who misreads a “Catch” as a “Throw.”
2) When you flip a card that involves a burrito, the nearby arrows of fate (and your fellow players) decide who’s throwing and who’s getting hit. 
3) The round ends when a burrito is thrown and either caught or struck by a thrower, triggering the resolution on the card. 

The setup time is short enough that you can move from “what’s this ridiculous game?” to “I will immediately forget the rules and make poor life choices with my friends” in under five minutes.

## How to play (the high-speed, no-snooze version)
If you’ve played Exploding Kittens, you’ll recognize the vibe: cards with wild illustrations, a sense of humor that leans into absurdity, and a mechanical flow that rewards quick thinking and quicker reflexes. Here’s a high-speed walkthrough of a typical turn:

- Draw a card or play a card from your hand. Cards generally fall into three buckets: attack cards (must throw a burrito at someone), catch cards (avoid a penalty by catching a burrito), and effect cards (special actions like swapping hands or forcing a target to perform a dramatic catch).
- If a burrito is launched into the arena (i.e., someone throws one), the target must either withstand the hit or save themselves via a catch. If caught, the throw is neutralized; if not, penalties apply—usually a loss of special tokens or a “life” from your health tracker.
- The goal is to accumulate specific burrito cards that match, either by hitting opponents or by catching burritos to collect the matching sets. The first player to clean out their burrito set or be the last one standing (depending on the variant you’re playing) wins.

The real joy is the interplay of daring throws and panic catches. It’s called Throw Throw Burrito for a reason: there will be throws. I’ve witnessed players channel their inner gymnast, bouncing between furniture and living room lamps to avoid a foam projectile that somehow finds its way around the coffee table like a rogue comet. The game rewards quick reflexes, but it also forgives the occasionally ridiculous misthrow with a hearty laugh and a shrug.

## Variants and house rules: customizing the chaos
Like any good party game, Throw Throw Burrito shines brighter when you tailor it to your crowd. Here are a few tried-and-true variants and house rules that Geeknite editors have endorsed over the years:

- Short rounds, maximum chaos: remove a few cards that prolong the game and aim for 10-15 minute bursts of glorious pandemonium. Great for a busy game night when attention spans are… being held hostage by a streaming service.
- Safe play and safety first: who needs a bruised ego when you can trade a bruise for a clever pun? Establish a quick “head, shoulders, elbows” safety protocol, and keep the burritos pointed away from faces. This is not a martial arts curriculum; it’s a party game with soft projectiles.
- The “catch and swap” mode: if you catch a burrito, you get to swap a hand with another player. It’s like a very silly version of musical chairs where chairs are replaced by who has the burrito receipts.
