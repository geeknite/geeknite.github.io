## Overview: What is OVERPOWER, anyway?
OverPower by Fleer was one of those 1990s experiments where developers tried to turn comic book fantasy into a collectible card game with the vibe of a trading card bazaar and the micro-mechanics of a board game. It borrowed the idea of “heroes” that you gather, “villains” to defeat, and “mission” cards that push the narrative forward, all while sneaking in the rules gauntlet: misprints, foils, and those tiny stat lines that looked perfectly reasonable on glossy stock but felt like cryptic runes when you first tried to use them in a match.

The concept of a “Hero Mission” subset implies a focus on scenario-driven play where the hero team has to complete tasks, foil schemes, or outlast a villain’s machinations. In practice, that means you’ll see a lot of synergy cards, some level-up tokens, and a cluster of mission cards that encourage a chain of plays rather than a one-turn, straight-damage kill. This lot we’re examining lives in that space—75+ cards’ worth of heroes, villains, mission cards, and combat contingencies that sparked a whole generation’s imagination about what a superhero deck could feel like.

If you want a quick external primer, the older encyclopedias of comic-nerd culture will tell you that OverPower had a handful of expansions, chase foils, and a style that leaned very heavily into the mood of 90s Marvel comics. Some folks consider it a cult classic, others remember it as a charmingly chaotic time capsule, and all of us who played it remember that dice-rolling, power-level-wrangling, and “Are you sure that’s allowed?” energy. ## Condition and Grading: What to Expect When You Open the Tin
Let’s be real: 90s card stock was a weird beast. Some cards have a glossy finish that still looks vibrant, while others have the telltale “clicky” texture that only shows up after a few decades of fandom and storage in non-archival conditions. A lot of this is cosmetic rather than game-breaking; you’re not buying a pristine modern hologram, you’re buying a slice of history that may have lived in a backpack, a couch arm, or a lunch-box for more years than you’re comfortable admitting.

- Edges: Expect some whitening or softness, a few nicks, and maybe a corner bend on some non-foil cards. It’s not a catastrophe; it’s a memory. If you’re building a display, you can frame the better examples and preserve the rest in protective sleeves.
- Surface: Most of the time, surface wear shows as light scuffs. If a card has been stored in a semi-humid environment, you might see minor spotting or dulling. Again, not a crime against the hobby, just a reminder of the rainstorm of time and the gentle friction of flipping through a lot at a garage sale.
- Foils and misprints: If there are foil variants in your lot, they’re likely more valuable as conversation pieces than as reliable game tokens because foils in a late-90s printing can be inconsistent in color and reflectivity. Misprints exist, too; some folks love these for their quirky charm, while others treat them as quirky defects. Either way, they tell a story about the production realities of the era.

For vendors and collectors, it’s essential to photograph edges, corners, front-and-back color, and any foil variance. A good listing will note the rough grade, while a more expansive sale might include a few cards laid out to show the typical wear pattern. ## Display, Storage, and Care: Keeping the Magic Alive
A lot of 90s cards survive in surprising condition when you consider how many years they’ve spent in shoe boxes and kitchen junk drawers. If you’re lucky enough to own the lot, proper storage can preserve it for years to come:

- Sleeving: Use acid-free card sleeves to protect each card if you plan to handle them often. Consider top-loaders for the standout heroes and mission cards you want on display.
- Binders vs. boxes: A binder with archival sleeves is a great showpiece for the heroes, while a sturdy storage box keeps the entire batch organized for your next game night. Label the sections by type (Heroes, Villains, Missions) to make retrieval easy.
- Climate matters: Keep the cards away from humidity and direct sunlight. The glossy finish can fade, and the colors can shift over time if exposed to heat. A cool, dry corner is your best friend here.
- Display strategy: A few standout cards on a shelf, framed with a small caption about the era, can be a delightful nod to friends who visit your space. It’s not just about keeping cards: it’s about telling a story with your collection.
