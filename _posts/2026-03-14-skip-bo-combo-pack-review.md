## How to play a round
Skip-Bo is a race to clear your stock pile by playing cards onto four central building piles in ascending order from 1 through 12. The deck contains normal number cards and special wild cards called Skip-Bo that can substitute for any card when building piles. The top card of each stock pile is visible to you, while the rest sit face down. Strategic play involves choosing when to reveal cards, when to discard to your own piles, and how to use wilds to maximize your turns.

In the combo pack of 2 you can treat each deck as a separate game that shares the same goal. You can also run a dual-table tournament by assigning each table a deck and then letting the winners from each table face off in a final round with a shared draw pile. Either approach adds social flavor and pacing variety to your night.

Turn structure is straightforward: on your turn you may play any number of cards from your hand, from your stock pile, or from your discard piles onto the building piles. You can also draw from the draw pile as needed to replenish your hand. The wild Skip-Bo cards add flexibility, but they should be used sparingly to avoid giving your rivals the exact move they needed.

The two deck format doubles opportunities for clever discards, risky plays, and the occasional misstep you will want to recount in your group chat later. It is common to feel a rush when you slide a sequence onto a building pile with a well-timed wild, followed by the satisfaction of emptying a stock pile shortly after. And yes, there will be a moment when you misread a card and realize you could have saved a wildcard for a future turn. That is the moment you learn patience, and probably also to blame the dog for sitting on the table while you shuffled.

## Strategy and tips
Strategy in Skip-Bo sits on a spectrum between efficient card management and opportunistic improvisation. Here are practical tips that tend to help most players, whether you are using a single deck or the dual deck setup in the combo pack:

- Prioritize building piles that you can complete quickly. If you can push a 1 through 4 sequence with a single move, you probably want to do it rather than staging a more elaborate sequence that risks breaking your momentum.
- Use wild Skip-Bo cards to fill gaps that would otherwise stall your turn. The trick is to hold them until you have a sequence that really needs them, rather than using them on a minimal run early in the turn.
- Manage your discard piles efficiently. They are not just places to dump excess cards; they are potential sources of future plays. Keep track of what you have shown and what remains hidden.
- In the combo pack, keep two mental calendars. One deck may be friendly to a certain play style, and the other may reward a different approach. Switching focus between decks can yield surprising advantages.
- Keep an eye on opponents stock piles. If you notice someone is close to finishing, you may adjust your plan to disrupt their momentum with a well-timed discard or card play that benefits you while hampering them.
- Pace matters. Skip-Bo rewards turn economy. The more you can do on a single turn without giving away an easy move to the opposition, the more you will feel the rhythm of victory.

Common mistakes include overusing wild cards, trying to force long runs without sufficient cards to back them up, and underutilizing discard piles that could unlock a chain of plays. The dual deck setup makes it easier to test different strategies without reshuffling the entire table, but it can also tempt chaos if you try to optimize both games at once without keeping track of the specific state of each deck.
