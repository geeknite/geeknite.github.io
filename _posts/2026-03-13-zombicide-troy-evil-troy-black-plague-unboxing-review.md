## Zombicide Troy & Evil Troy Black Plague New and Sealed: A Geeknite Unboxing and Review

Welcome fellow dice whisperers and mini painting enthusiasts to a deep dive into a box that almost begs to be torn open with the zeal of a rampaging zombie horde. Today we examine Zombicide Black Plague with the intriguing duo of expansions called Troy and Evil Troy from Guillotine Games. New and sealed means the plastic has not yet learned to fold itself and run away, which is a good sign when you want to extract expensive plastic warriors and the promise of endless tabletop glory.

![Zombicide Black Plague New and Sealed]({{ '/images/zombicide-black-plague.jpg' | relative_url }})

First thoughts straight from the shrink wrap: the box is heavy enough to double as a tiny club if your car battery runs low on power and you need to fend off a late night zombie scenario. The art on the cover is bold, and the color palette screams medieval plague times with a splash of neon to remind you that this is still a game for grown ups who enjoy a good pun and a little chaos.

For those who have not encountered Zombicide before, the Black Plague line is a dark fantasy reimagining of classic zombie survival with a medieval twist. The base game introduces a living map, weapon cards, and a cadre of knights and peasants who are either heroic survivors or delicious morsels for the undead. The Troy expansion adds a new thematic layer, while Evil Troy introduces a pinch of chaos that makes players second-guess their loyalty to the free world. Together, they create a scenario flow that can feel like a roller coaster built by a bored necromancer with a sense of drama.


### Unboxing and First Impressions

The unboxing process is part ceremony, part puzzle. The rulebook sits at the top like a stern librarian guarding the sacred path to victory. Beneath that are the plastic miniatures, each one packaged with a little care that signals you will probably need a magnifying glass to admire every detail. If you are into painting miniatures, this is a dream. If you are more of a casual player who just wants to swing swords and watch the crusaders dodging zombies like bad memes, you will still have enough visual candy to keep you engaged for a long campaign.

The Troy expansion ships with a new cadre of survivors who look ready to sign up for a bad idea and a worse day. Evil Troy brings a cadre of antagonists and traitors that can flip the board state faster than a goblin flips a coin. The components feel sturdy. The plastic is crisp, the bases are clean, and the punch boards feel robust enough to survive several tabletop sessions without becoming a landfill of tiny plastic disasters. The sealed nature means there is a premium smell of new plastic mixed with cardboard adhesives. It is the olfactory perfume of tabletop glory, and if you are into sensory experiences, you will enjoy the moment when you peel away the protective wrap and reveal the dungeon that lies ahead. The color palette is a little grim, but that is the vibe Zombicide is chasing here, a world where knights clash with the undead and somehow the plague ends up being the garnish rather than the main course.


### The Box Content and Component Quality

The base box holds a surprisingly large array of miniatures. We are not talking about shy little pewter figurines here; these are chunky plastic characters with enough personality to justify a few paint sessions. The hero survivors come with distinctive silhouettes and a level of character design that makes you want to read their backstory and probably write fanfic about their adventures in the plague-ridden lands. The zeds are plentiful and varied, giving you a steady stream of antagonists to mow through with swords, axes, and the occasional holy relic that does not actually exist but should.

In terms of components, the board tiles are sturdy and have a tactile heft that prevents slipping during enthusiastic turns. The tokens are clear and well labeled, which is essential for quick play. The cards are printed on good stock with readable icons. The art direction maintains that medieval-meets-movie-poster vibe that Zombicide has cultivated across multiple editions. It is not subtle, but it is effective. You want to feel like a knight fighting for your life while a zombie apocalypse unfolds around you in glorious, over-the-top color.

The Troy expansion introduces new survivor kits and a few new enemy types. The named survivors — with knightly badges and plague doctor vibes — bring a sense of gravity to the game. Their special abilities and gear cards mix things up considerably, offering new routes to survival that do not rely solely on luck of the dice. Evil Troy, on the other hand, is designed to disrupt co operative play in delightfully chaotic ways. It is the kind of mechanic that prompts players to ask themselves if a teammate is actually a traitor or if the traitor is simply someone who forgot to take a turn. The expansion design encourages tension and adds a layer of social deduction possibilities without forcing a heavy rumor mill at the table.


### Thematic Fit and Narrative Flow

Zombicide Black Plague is built on a strong theme that places undead invasions in a high medieval setting. The art and design choices reinforce the sense of danger and drama while preserving the lighthearted feel that keeps it accessible for new players. With Troy and Evil Troy, you get a more dynamic narrative arc. The knights are not just doing hero stuff; they are navigating betrayals, moral choices, and the occasional blunder that costs a few lives but teaches valuable lessons about team coordination.

The Troy expansion tends to emphasize formation play and strategic positioning. You will be thinking about map control, choke points, and how to protect a healer who never seems to survive the first wave of undead. Evil Troy, by contrast, injects uncertainty into planning sessions. It injects misdirection, hidden agendas, and moments where you question your own memory of who has the best equipment. These two expansions do not simply add more tiles and more zombies; they alter how players interact with each other and how the box rotates between leadership and anarchy on the table.


### Gameplay Experience and Pacing

Once you dive into a session, you quickly feel the rhythm of Zombicide Black Plague. Players take turns activating a squad of survivors, moving them across a modular board, gathering gear, and fighting waves of zombies that surge along the streets like an angry tide. The core mechanics are that simple to grasp but hard to master. The action economy rewards careful planning but also punishes overconfidence in the blink of a zombie eye. The Troy expansion adds new mission objectives that require you to adapt quickly to changing board states. The Evil Troy expansion keeps you honest by injecting misdirection and traitor mechanics that force players to coordinate more than they would like to admit.

The synergy between the two expansions creates some memorable sessions. There are moments when your carefully laid plan is undone by a stray zombie surge or by a traitor who reveals themselves at the worst possible moment. This is not a game designed for the faint of heart. It is for players who like the thrill of organizing a counterattack while quietly praying that your teammate did not just sabotage your best strategy. The combat feels weighty without tipping into fiddly; you have enough decisions to feel meaningful without spending the whole day calculating probability trees. The dice, cards, and miniatures cooperate in producing that satisfying moment when you land a critical hit and watch the board dynamics shift in your favor, at least for a single turn before the next surge.


### Setup Time, Playtime, and Replayability

Setup takes a little longer than the average board game thanks to the extensive components. It is a feature not a bug, however, because the longer setup is balanced by the depth of the missions and the variety of enemy types you will encounter. With Troy expansions included, there are more mission paths and dynamic board states, which means you can replay the campaign multiple times without feeling like you are stuck in a rut.

A typical playthrough runs anywhere from 60 to 180 minutes depending on player count and how aggressively your dice choose to cooperate with you. The expanded content can push a single session toward the upper range, but the payoff is a more nuanced story and a greater sense of accomplishment when you finally push the last necromancer into the afterlife. For gamers who like to stretch their campaigns over weekends or who enjoy painting and customizing miniatures, the combination of Black Plague with the Troy expansions offers significant long-term engagement.


### Storage, Quality, and Longevity

Storage is not glamorous but it matters. The box is roomy enough to hold base components plus expansions with a little shuffling. If you plan to store it with the minis assembled, you may want to consider a dedicated insert. The quality of plastic looks robust enough to survive consistent gameplay, but as with any tabletop project, there is a better chance of longevity if you treat it with care and avoid dropping the box down the stairs during an epic unboxing montage.

If you are a painter or a hobbyist, the presentation begs for a few sessions of priming and shading. The knights and monsters are built with enough surface area to justify a paint job, which will in turn enhance the overall immersion of the session. If you are not into painting, you can still enjoy the aesthetic while leaving the miniatures in their factory plastic well enough alone. Either way, the box is a centerpiece that invites discussion and strategy at the table.


### Rule Clarity, Learning Curve, and Player Interaction

Zombicide Black Plague can be learned relatively quickly by new players, but the expansions do add layers that require some attention. The Troy faction introduces new mechanics around territory control and hero synergy, while Evil Troy requires players to think about hidden agendas and team dynamics in more complex ways. The rulebook is surprisingly readable for a game of its scope; it presents core concepts clearly and then expands on the nuances as you progress through missions.

Player interaction tends to be high in this system. You will be negotiating, trading, and sometimes planting a trap for a would-be traitor. The social dynamics add a fantastic dimension to the experience, turning what could be a pure tactical exercise into a memorable storytelling session where the fate of your crew hangs on both your dice and your diplomacy. This is not a solo-friendly game in practice; the fun comes from the interplay between players as much as from the tactical combat itself. Budget a couple of practice rounds to get everyone acquainted with the mechanics and the pacing of play. You will thank me later when someone does not commit a cardinal sin and you all survive a pivotal encounter.


### Comparisons to Other Zombicide Sets

If you have played other Zombicide sets, you will notice the Black Plague line has a more restrained, grounded feel compared to some of the more bombastic modern Zombicide releases. The medieval setting provides a different flavor of chaos, where swords beat down zombies and magic rarely acts as a deus ex machina. The Troy additions push this further, providing new survivors and a fresh layer of strategy while Evil Troy shakes things up with morally grey decisions. Compared to earlier Zombicide editions that leaned heavily into generic fantasy, this box feels more cohesive in its theme and less likely to slip into the uncanny valley of mismatched art.

For players who prefer lighter, more casual co op experiences, this might feel a touch heavy. For those who crave a deeper strategic challenge with occasional dramatic betrayals, this combination is a treat. If you are wondering how it stacks up against a purely modern zombie setting, the old guard of knights vs undead provides enough novelty to make revisiting a classic scenario feel fresh and relevant.


### Accessibility and Setup Tips

Here are a few tips to maximize your experience with this set. First, dedicate a clean, well-lit play area. The miniatures reward careful painting and you want to avoid jabbing your knee on a stray sword while tracking activation sequences. Second, separate event cards, gear cards, and zombie tokens into clearly labeled containers. The organization will save you dozens of minutes per session and make the initial loadout less chaotic. Third, consider a short practice round with a small squad to acclimate new players to the turn order and the interplay between expansions. Finally, establish a house rule for traitor reveals during Evil Troy missions to ensure the tension remains fun rather than personal.

### Final Verdict and Recommendation

If you crave a robust, thematic co op experience with a dash of social tension and the promise of many replayable campaigns, Zombicide Black Plague with the Troy and Evil Troy expansions is a strong candidate for your shelf. The quality of the components feels premium enough to justify the investment, and the expansions add meaningful new decisions that keep sessions fresh rather than rote. Setup time is non trivial, but the payoff is substantial for players who enjoy puzzle-like scenarios, tactical combat, and the occasional dramatic betrayal that makes your group talk for days after the table is cleared.

If you already own Black Plague and are on the fence about expanding, the Troy expansion is a smart add, offering new survivors and a fresh layer of strategy. Evil Troy adds spice and social dynamics that escalate conflict and injects memorable moments into your campaigns. For newcomers, I would recommend starting with the base game to learn the core mechanics, then gradually incorporating the expansions as you get comfortable with the rules and the pacing of play.
