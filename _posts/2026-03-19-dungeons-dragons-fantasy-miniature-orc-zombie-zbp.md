## Unboxing and assembly

If you are the kind of hobbyist who loves a good unboxing video, this is a model that does not disappoint. The Orc Zombie 4 arrives in a plastic sprue with a handful of limbs, a torso, an optional weapon hand, and the gnarly pieces that make it unmistakably undead. The level of detail on the muscles, bone decay, and the shredded fabric edges encourages you to use a fine detail brush and a steady hand. The downside is that a zombie with this much character tends to come with a degree of assembly complexity. Expect tiny connectors and the possibility of a peg fighting with a limb if you’re not precise. Here is a pro tip: dry-fit first, then decide which limb goes where. If a head refuses to sit flush, use a very small amount of putty to fill the gap and sand after it cures. The goal is a pose that feels natural and not like a puzzle piece that refused to cooperate.

One thing to note is the portion of the model that might require pinning, especially if you want a dynamic pose. Zombicide pieces are not typically heavy on chonky metal bits, but undead heft plus a raised arm can cause a hairline bend in the lower limbs. Don’t be afraid to pin and green-stuff micro-joins; it will reward you with a stable mini that can hold up to the kind of table talk that happens during a critical fail.

The assembly journey is part of the fun. ## Painting and color theory

Painting an undead orc is a fantastic way to demonstrate a few core techniques without getting overwhelmed. Here is a practical recipe that yields a convincing undead look without turning the orc into a popcorn-colored catastrophe:

- Base coat: a mid-green orc skin tone, such as a forest green or olive shade. The aim is a hue that reads as alive at first glance but decays under close inspection.
- Flesh shadow: a slightly cooler shade in the deeper recesses protected from direct light, which adds depth to muscles and rotting tissue.
- Flesh highlight: a lighter, warmer green or a bone color mixed with a touch of green to simulate patchy skin decay.
- Undead details: wash with a diluted brown or sepia to emphasize bone textures and the leathery texture of shredded skin. A touch of blue or purple can be used around the exposed bone areas to simulate cold necrosis.
- Armor and weapons: rust and weathering are essential. Use a base metal color, then apply a rust wash. Build up highlights along the edges to simulate edge wear and old battle damage.
- Cloth and leather: desaturated browns or grays with a light dry brush on raised edges imitate worn fabric and leather that have seen too many campaigns.
- Base: basing is an opportunity to tell the dungeon story. Sand and static grass can imply a dirty ruin floor, while small pebbles and tufts add texture without overwhelming the figure.

If you want a quick-start palette, here is a suggested order you can translate into your preferred brand: base coat green, wash brown, highlight with light green, rust for armor, dry brush bone for exposed bone, and final edge highlight around the armor and teeth.

For painters who like to push the envelope, you can add a little luminescent green or blue glaze on the eyes to give a haunting stare that glows under candlelight, a classic trick for setting the mood at a gaming table. The point is not to oversaturate; the goal is to suggest age, rot, and a diet consisting mostly of adventurers who forgot to bring holy water.
