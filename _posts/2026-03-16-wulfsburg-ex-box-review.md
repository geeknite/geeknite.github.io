Wulfsburg: CMON’s city of politics, power struggles and howlers, now in the Fair EX Box. If you’ve ever wondered what it would be like if a werewolf uprising had a municipal planning committee, this is the game for you. The EX edition promises more modules, more variability, and enough cardboard to conjure a minor moat around your gaming table. In this review, we’ll dive into what makes Wulfsburg tick, what makes players howl, and whether the EX box is worth your money (and your neighbors’ sanity).

## Aperçu général
In Wulfsburg, players take on the roles of city leaders trying to steer a town under constant threat from the hidden werewolf faction. The game blends area control, engine-building, and a dash of negotiation with a lot of subtle cruel smiles. The box ex ("EX" for extra, extraordinaire) includes modular boards, extra character cards, and a few “moon phase” events that can upset even the best-laid plan.

### The Box contents and first impressions
The EX edition is the star of the show. The box is sturdy, with a nice dichotomy between the ceremonial red and the wolf-pack gray. The tokens feel good, the meeples are cute and menacing at the same time, and the board has a level of detail that says \"we spent the budget on the art\" (which is often a good sign). Inside you’ll find:
- 1 modular game board (two boards that can be combined in multiple layouts)
- 5 player boards with distinct starting powers
- A deck of action cards that powers the engine
- 120 influence tokens, 60 rage tokens, 8 moon tokens
- 12 werewolf tiles with abstract but charming art
- Moon dial and event track
- The EX expansion pack, including new scenarios and optional rules

Oh, and the rulebook, which is… a reasonably sized PDF if you want to avoid turning pages in a dimly lit basement.

## Gameplay: core loop, pacing and how the city breathes
### Setup rapide (et que tout le monde puisse rire en attendant)
Setup takes about 10-15 minutes for a 4-player game, which is enough time to open a bag of snacks and pretend you’re about to play a heavy strategy epic. The module selection in the EX box lets you tailor setup: basic city layout, add Moon Phase events, or throw in a “Night of the Howls” scenario if you’re feeling dramatic.

### Le cœur du jeu: l’action et l’enchaînement
Each round combines calendar-like planning with real-time adaptability. Players draft action cards to place influence on districts, recruit scouts, or unleash a moon event that can tilt the board. The action cards create a sense of engine-building: you assemble a combo of district influence, resource management, and sudden power-ups that can snowball into a convincing lead.

But beware: the werewolf track rewards deception. The “wolf” aspect is not just a flavor; it’s a moral enough impetus to cause players to question their career choices: “Should I reveal I’m the Werewolf? Will that cause a coup or a new alliance? And will my brunch order be the first casualty in this political saga?”

### Pouvoirs et asymétrie
Each player starts with a unique power that nudges how you influence districts. The asymmetry is not as extreme as in a dedicated asymmetric game, but enough to reward different playstyles: a diplomat who excels at negotiating influence, a hunter with more leverage in the outer districts, a seer who glimpses moon events. The EX edition adds additional powers via its expansion characters, each with a distinct swing in pace or endgame condition.

### Interaction et conflit
Wulfsburg rewards interaction—sometimes with a cooperative vibe (we all want to keep the city safe) and sometimes with a more chaotic “evict-your-neighbor” vibe. The negotiation mechanic is reminiscent of classic Euro-games—share information, bluff a little, and hope your ally doesn’t become a partisan foe mid-game. It’s also a good game to watch for table talk. The memes will flow as you accuse someone of “planning the Night of the Howls” while your own secret scheme is quietly hovering on the moon dial.

### Solo et rejouabilité
There’s a solo variant included or easy to adapt. The modular board and the variety of character cards means no two games feel identical. The Moon Phase events in the EX box add a layer of unpredictability that keeps the table from going to sleep after a few plays. Replayability is strong for a mid-weight strategy game; if you have a regular group that enjoys negotiation puzzles and a bit of “doom-doni” planning, Wulfsburg will spend plenty of nights dominating your shelf.

## Art et composants
### Esthétique et thème
The art direction is moody and atmospheric, with a European gothic vibe that fits the werewolf theme without becoming cartoonish. The typography and iconography are clear; the moon dial is a nice tactile piece that makes the passage of time feel real rather than abstract. You’ll catch yourself appreciating the non-tacky design choices, which often is what sells a mid-weight game more than the campaign word-lust of a few glossy cards.

### Matériaux et durabilité
Plastic tokens feel sturdy, boards are thick and well-laminated, and the player boards are compact but legible. The EX edition’s new art variants hold up well, and the extra little figurines? They’re more cute than terrifying, which is perfect for a family or a more casual group that doesn’t want to feel like a war council.

## Readability and learnability
### Tutoriel-serie et ressources
The rulebook is fairly well structured for a mid-weight Euro with negotiation. There’s a quick-start guide that helps new players dive into the mechanics, but expect a few “ah-ha” moments when you realize a particular combination of power and district control can swing the tide of the game.

## Conclusion et recommendation
If you love a puzzle that sits between negotiation and engine-building, with enough political intrigue to keep the table honest and enough werewolf-y chaos to keep it interesting, Wulfsburg EX Box is a solid choice. It scales well, offers a strong box of extras and a handful of memorable moments on every table. It won’t be the fastest game on your shelf, and it certainly won’t make your electricity bill spike from the number of candles you’ll need to set the mood. But it delivers a satisfying blend of strategy, interaction, and whimsy that will make you grin and groan in equal measure.

Pros:
- Strong core loop with an elegant balance of engine-building and negotiation
- Excellent art direction; components feel premium
- EX Box adds meaningful variability and scenarios

Cons:
- Not a party game; requires thoughtful turns and some table talk
- 5-player games can get messy and slow
- Learning curve is gentle but can be long for complete newcomers

Final recommendation: Wulfsburg EX Box is a recommended pick for groups who enjoy mid-to-mid-high weight strategy with social negotiation. If your gaming group wants a heavy dose of politics, strategic planning, and a dash of werewolf chaos, this is a great fit.

- Quick tip: Start with a base setup (no Moon Phases) to learn the core engine, then add modules gradually. It will help players acclimate to the pace and the decision fatigue that is inherent in a complex, multi-mod game.
