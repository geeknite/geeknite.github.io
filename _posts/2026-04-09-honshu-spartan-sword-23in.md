## Build, Materials, and the Tang Debate

The headline specs are simple: 23 inches overall length, 7Cr13 stainless steel blade, full tang, and a scabbard that’s the sort of leather-and-foam combo that says, “I’m serious about look, not about pretending to actually cut titanium.” The steel grade 7Cr13 is a budget-friendly Chinese stainless commonly used in budget to mid-range blades. It’s not a high-end carbon steel like 1095 or D2, but it’s easier to maintain and less prone to rust when you forget to dry it after your “let’s do a midnight cosplay photoshoot” session.

From a materials perspective, 7Cr13 behaves like a well-behaved, slightly less temperamental street racer. It can take a decent edge with the right sharpening, and it’s relatively forgiving for beginners who want a blade that doesn’t demand a blacksmith’s apprenticeships to maintain. Don’t expect surgical-grade performance here, but do expect a blade that looks perfectly enough to be mistaken for something you’d see in a mid-tier fantasy film budget. It’s the kind of steel that makes you feel like a hero in an indie adventure game, not a battle-tested veteran of a knight’s tourney.

The blade geometry leans toward the practical fantasy aesthetic: a straight, single-edged blade with a strong spine, a slightly widened mid-section near the tip, and a point that’s designed for stabbing rather than fine, surgical cuts. That makes sense for a sword marketed to cosplay and display: you want presence more than lab-grade cut precision. If you’ve ever looked at a steel blade and wondered how much fantasy you can wring out of it, this blade politely says, “As much as your heart desires, but let’s stay within the realm of reality.”

The full tang is a big deal for budget swords. It means the blade runs the full width of the handle, giving you a sense of rigidity and control that the cheaper “full tang-ish” designs sometimes lack. Practically, it translates to better balance and handling when you do the classic “pretend to duel with your roommate” maneuver. The tang, though not a technical marvel, is solid steel that will hold up to display, cosplay handling, or a casual fenced-in sword-fight in a living room, provided no one actually intends to hurt anyone—always the prudent disclaimer in any nerdy weapon review.

Balance is another interesting talking point. A 23-inch sword with a sturdy blade and leather-wrapped grip tends to lean slightly toward the hilt in a upright pose, giving you good control during slow, cinematic strokes while preserving the dramatic arc of the blade when you swing in all-caps excitement for a serviceable cosplay shot. ## Maintenance and Care: Keeping Your Spartan Sharp (ish)

The last thing you want is to treat your blade like a family heirloom and then forget it in the closet for six months. Stainless steel like 7Cr13 is more forgiving than carbon steel, but it isn’t invincible. Moisture, fingerprints, and plain old neglect can tarnish the finish and dull the edge over time. Here are a few tips to keep your Honshu Spartan sword looking sharp and feeling sturdy:

- Wipe it down after handling. A microfiber cloth and a light coat of oil will prevent fingerprints and rust spots. You don’t need a blacksmith’s arsenal for this—just common sense and a little elbow grease.
- Avoid leaving it in the scabbard in humidity-heavy environments for prolonged periods. If you’re storing it, air it out occasionally and keep it in a dry place.
- If you’re actively sharpening it, use a proper blade file or stone with care. Don’t go full “Lancelot on a cheese grater” on a budget blade. A gentle touch and a few passes will do.
- Cleaning is easier if you disassemble gently, but if you’re not comfortable, you can still maintain the blade with a wipe-down and oil. The goal is minimal corrosion and maximum gleam when you strike the cosplay pose.

In short: treat it like the cosplay star it is. A little care goes a long way toward preserving the aesthetic and the structure. The blade won’t thank you with a wine glass, but it will thank you with a clean edge and a display that looks like it belongs on a high-fantasy shelf.
