### Ergonomics and comfort: space to breathe, room to move
With eight people sharing a single footprint, space planning is the real star here. Each workstation has an ample width to accommodate a primary monitor, secondary devices, and the occasional ergonomic accessory. The depth (1530 mm) provides enough space for keyboard and mouse deviation without turning into a pajama party on the keyboard tray. The height (1200 mm) is tall enough to avoid that “or else I’ll get eye strain” zone while still being friendly to standing desks in adjacent modules.

We tested a cross-section of body types and working styles, from developers who live in a code cave to designers who like to tilt their chairs back and pretend they’re giving a TED Talk. The result: a surprisingly comfortable seating dynamic when you distribute chairs with proper lumbar support and a Diversity of Posture policy (a.k.a. encourage dynamic sitting). The central screen wall encourages eye contact and shared focus during group sessions, but you’ll want solid awareness around glare. A reflective office window or overhead lighting can cause hot spots on certain monitor configurations. If you go for a matte finish on the screens and a low-gloss veneer on the oak, you’ll limit glare without dulling the personality of the space.

As with any large ergonomic project, micromovements matter. The key is to position chairs so that no one has to crane their neck like a curious giraffe in a study hall. The modular design helps; you can rotate teams, swap modules, and rejigger the layout as your product roadmap evolves. It’s a living, breathing desk ecosystem—like a small city with a central plaza where the coffee cart visits you on your break.

### Assembly, delivery, and a little patience
Let’s get real: a desk this size is usually delivered in multiple crates. Plan for a couple of hours of unboxing, followed by a planning phase where you decide whether to set up the two modules in a straight line, a right-angle configuration, or a funky “L-shaped learning lounge.” The official assembly window is generous—think a long afternoon or a productive weekend—so you’re not juggling furniture as if you’re solving a Rubik’s cube aboard a storm-tossed spaceship.

We recommend two people for the heavy lifting, a padded workspace (to avoid dinging the oak while you wrestle with the frame), and a dedicated cable-sculpting pro to tame the jungle of wires. The cable management system works well if you plan ahead: route power, data, and display cables as you assemble; otherwise, you’ll end up with a tangled ecosystem that looks like a Christmas-charm nightmare in February. A little pre-planning goes a long way here.

### Cable management and tech integration: keep the flow, lose the mess
One of the standout features is the centralized trunking system that runs the length of the desk. It’s designed to handle power outlets, data lines, USB hubs, and display cables in a way that keeps the top surface clean. The grommets are generously sized, which is handy for modern chunky power adapters or the occasional charging brick you forgot to pack in your bag. We tested worst-case scenarios: multiple laptops, a couple of docking stations, and a monitor arm with a bulky VESA plate. The result was a neat, organized underbelly that’s far easier to navigate than your new year’s resolutions.

Of course, no cable-management system is perfect. If you’re planning a truly “plug-and-play” environment with a lot of dynamic reconfiguration, you may want to invest in additional cable sleeves or vertical slack management to avoid the spaghetti apocalypse. The desk invites you to code, design, and brainstorm rather than fight for power sockets, which is exactly the kind of design-minded empathy you want in a collaborative space.
