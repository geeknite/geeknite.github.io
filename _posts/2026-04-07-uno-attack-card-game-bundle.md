## How to Play: The Core Loop
UNO Attack is still UNO at its heart, which means you want to shed your hand by matching colors or numbers, with a small twist courtesy of the launcher. Here is the core loop you will repeat, again and again, with varying degrees of intensity:

- On your turn, you either play a valid card from your hand or draw if you cannot play. The objective remains shedding all cards before the others, which is as simple as it sounds and as dramatic as your table allows.
- If you can play a card that matches the previous card by color or number, you do so. Simple, traditional, and the backbone of UNO, even in the presence of chaos.
- If you cannot play, you draw from the deck as usual. The tension grows because someone may get a lucky draw that upgrades the energy level at the table.
- The launcher comes into play in a couple of different ways: sometimes it may dispense a single card, sometimes a cascade of several cards, and sometimes nothing happens at all. The unpredictability is the point, and that is where the laughter tends to spike.

The actual chaos is all in how players react to the launcher. You are not just playing cards; you are simultaneously moderating a tiny chaotic device that can alter the pace of the round in seconds. The thrill comes from the social dynamics: who remains calm, who starts to bluff about their card count, and who takes a small victory lap after a sudden cascade reduces the field to a dramatic handful of players. Expect lots of exclamations, impromptu celebrations, and the occasional freak out when a card dumps onto the table like confetti at a parade.

## Gameplay Nuances and House Rules
A few nuances and optional rules can elevate or derail the experience depending on how you use them. Here are some practical measures you can apply to tailor the experience to your group:

- Tie breaker rule: In case two players end up with zero cards on the same turn, a quick race to shed one extra card can break ties. This keeps the pace up and the competition clean.
- Expansion deck strategy: If you are using the extra deck, you can choose whether to mix and match or to keep the expansion as a separate sub game. The hybrid approach allows for varied rounds with different dynamics.
- Quick start rule: For groups that are new to UNO Attack, a three round introductory mini-game can ease players into the flow. The aim is to reduce first timer intimidation while still preserving the core energy of the experience.

House rules often make or break the social experience. The best approach is to discuss a couple of simple modifications before you start and then let the chaos unfold with confidence. We recommend trying the quick start and the simple tie breaker on your first session, then evaluating how to layer in expansions once you know your crowd.
