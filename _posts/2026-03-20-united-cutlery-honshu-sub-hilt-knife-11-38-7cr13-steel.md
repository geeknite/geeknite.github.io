## Design and build quality
### Blade geometry and steel
The Honshu Sub Hilt uses 7Cr13 stainless steel, a common workhorse for budget blades. It’s not going to rewrite the metallurgy textbooks, but it’s perfectly adequate for everyday utility tasks, light cutting, and the occasional prop-destruction demonstration at a con. The steel tends to hold a decent edge for simple tasks like cord-cutting, paracord slicing, and opening snack packaging after a late night of gaming. If you’re planning something heavy-duty, you’ll want to temper your expectations and perhaps upgrade to a higher-grade steel when your budget allows.

From a maintenance standpoint, 7Cr13 is forgiving. It resists corrosion reasonably well if you dry the blade after use and occasionally apply a thin layer of oil. If you’re the type who forgets, you’ll notice minor staining or micro-oxidation after exposure to humidity, but that’s part of the knife’s “vintage” charm in some circles. In short: if you’re a casual user, you won’t feel cheated. If you’re a steel snob, you’ll want something with more chrome, vanadium, or a fancy heat treatment—this blade isn’t pretending to be that knife.

### Handle and grip
The handle is a Black TPR over a basic substrate. TPR is one of those terms that feels like it should be the name of a sci-fi villain—yet it’s basically a soft, grippy rubbery material. It’s comfortable in hand, provides decent traction even when wet, and cushions minor vibrations when you’re performing a quick slice test on a piece of drone packaging (don’t judge—sometimes you need to practice cutting power cords in a safe, controlled environment).

The sub-hilt guard is an aesthetic and functional feature. It’s not a full tang cross-guard, but a little extra protection to keep your knuckles away from the blade on heavy cuts. It adds a touch of elegance to the silhouette while providing a reassuring flourish to your inner ninja. For those who appreciate ergonomics, the handle radius is pleasant, though long-term fatigue is possible if you’re using this knife for extended sustained cuts. It’s not meant to replace a dedicated fixed-blade user’s primary tool, but it’s comfortable enough for day-long use during a convention photoshoot or a weekend camping trip.

### Build quality and finish
United Cutlery tends to deliver budget-friendly models with a confident look and a sturdy feel. The Honshu Sub Hilt is no exception. Joints are solid, the blade runs true with no noticeable looseness, and the finish is consistent across the blade. Overall, the build feels sturdy enough for display and practical use in safe scenarios.

## Care and maintenance tips
- Clean and dry after use: 7Cr13 is stainless, but it isn’t invincible. A quick wipe down with oil on a soft cloth helps prevent micro-rust in high-humidity environments.
- Sharpening: A basic stone or guided sharpener will bring back belt-kink sharpness. Expect standard retention for casual tasks; don’t expect surgical-grade edge longevity.
- Storage: Keep it in a sheath when not in use to protect the blade and the sheath. Avoid long-term moisture exposure.
- Handling: Always keep fingers away from the edge, and never use the knife as a pry tool to avoid bending, chipping, or existential crises about your tool’s purpose.
