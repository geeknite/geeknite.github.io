## Condition and Grading: What You’re Really Buying
The phrase great condition can be slippery in the vintage world. It depends on the card’s storage history and how much it enjoyed binder life under a desk lamp. Here are practical benchmarks to apply when evaluating a nine-card lot like this:

- Edge durability: whitening, chipping, or dinged corners. Light handling marks are common, but significant edge wear can tank value.
- Centering and borders: early MTG borders sometimes show minor misalignment. A clean border enclosing the art with no bleed is a strong sign of quality.
- Ink integrity: faded ink or discoloration in the title or mana cost is a red flag. A clean, legible face with saturated color is a win.
- Surface scratches: fine scratches are expected; multiple scratches or dull gloss typically lowers value more than you’d expect.
- Print anomalies: misprints or unusual text lines can be a conversation piece for collectors; document them with photos if possible.

If you’re new to grading, treat these nine cards as a learning exercise. Build your own mental checklist, then apply it next time you sample a vintage lot. There’s no substitute for hands-on experience, and every Lot teaches you a bit more about balancing story and price tag.

## Care, Storage, and Display: Keeping Value Likely Long-Term
A nine-card lot benefits from smart care:

- Sleeves: high clarity sleeves and a rigid top loader for display-worthy cards. Consider double-sleeving for added protection when flipping through a binder.
- Storage: keep away from heat, humidity, and direct sunlight. The longer you protect pigment, the longer your cards stay vibrant and legible.
- Handling: clean hands and minimal handling reduce risk of oils and dirt.

If you’re displaying these cards, label them with set origin, approximate year, and notable features. It adds to the experience and helps you remember why you paid a particular price at a particular time.
