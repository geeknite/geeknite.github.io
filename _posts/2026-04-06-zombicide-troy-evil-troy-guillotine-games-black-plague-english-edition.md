# Zombicide: Troy & Evil Troy — A Geeknite Deep Dive

Welcome back to Geeknite, the place where the undead are loud, goofy, and surprisingly strategic. Today we dive into the Troy & Evil Troy expansion for Zombicide Black Plague English Edition, a pairing that makes the labyrinthine streets of the dark ages feel a little bit like a kitchen sink opera with a dash of Monty Python. If you thought your group was chaos incarnate, wait until you unlock Troy and Evil Troy, two siblings from the same end of the alley who have a talent for catching you off guard while the zombies jam to your doom.

## What is Zombicide Black Plague with Troy & Evil Troy?

Zombicide Black Plague is Guillotine Games punchy interpretation of zombie mayhem in a medieval fantasy setting. The base game already dumps you into a sprawling city map, a stack of metallic miniatures, and a quest to survive waves of undead while grabbing loot and completing objectives. The Troy & Evil Troy expansion adds two named survivors with unique abilities that riff off each other in ways that will make you grin, groan, and occasionally cackle as your newest chaos agents.

Troy is the valiant, noble knight type with a soft spot for mercy, which in zombie-land translates to a punchy set of melee moves and leadership triggers that can turn the tide when your team needs a morale boost. Evil Troy, on the other hand, is the cheeky counterpart who delights in risk and mischief, letting you gamble for extra rewards, sometimes at the cost of a near apocalypse. Put these two on the board and you are playing not only for survival but for a dramatic arc that would make any tavern tale jealous.

## Components and Quality

The box opens with a careful collision of miniatures that look like they leapt out of a tavern fight and decided to go on a vacation to a dungeon. Troy and Evil Troy come with a pair of character boards, relevant equipment cards, a set of unique skills, and a handful of new quests to test whether you can survive long enough to watch the story unfold. The miniatures themselves are crisp, with the usual medieval zombie aesthetic that Zombicide fans know and tolerate with a raised eyebrow. The sculpts capture the humor of their personalities — Troy as a sturdy, perhaps overconfident champion; Evil Troy as a snark machine wearing a gleeful expression when chaos is afoot.

The tiles, tokens, and cards conform to the Black Plague base game design. The faithful color palette and frost-bitten metal hues do a fine job of making the expansion feel cohesive with the core set, while still giving Troy and Evil Troy a distinct identity. The quality is high. It is not a plastic-relief dreamland, but the pieces are durable enough to survive the occasional gamer who learns the hard way that dice can be loud when rolled with a flourish.

## Rules Integration and Setup

Adding Troy and Evil Troy to a Black Plague session is not a full rewrite of the rules, which is a relief for anyone who has a limited brain fog tolerance after a long day at the job. The expansion uses the base game rules as a backbone, with a handful of new interaction rules that feel intuitive rather than gimmicky. Setup remains similar to a standard mission, with a couple of extra steps to place the new tiles and tokens, and then you look around your table and realize you now have two dynamic personalities to manage in addition to your standard crew.

Troy has a standard survivor profile: he moves, fights, and uses his leadership ability to exhort the group. Evil Troy adds a risk-reward mechanic that can trigger on certain actions. The tension arises because Evil Troy benefits from chaos in the dungeon while the other survivors may have a different idea of how to win. When you pull a card from the new deck, the luck factor increases, which can be a good or a bad thing depending on whether you rolled the right skulls last turn.

The synergy with the base game is a highlight. The two new characters can operate as a paired duo or as separate agents with their own agendas. The design leans into the storytelling aspect that the Black Plague line has always claimed to be good at: you are not just laying traps and killing zombies; you are weaving a narrative that could lead to a heroic moment or, more likely, a hilariously chaotic one. The missions still deliver the same bite sized goals with a reasonable balance between luck and strategy, and the Troy pair adds a new flavor to the pacing.

## Gameplay Feel: Laughs, Strategy, and Light Terror

Zombicide likes to pitch you into a loop of planning, dice rolling, and cross-eyed luck that feels unfairly balanced in the most delightful way. The Troy expansion amplifies that by injecting two distinct personalities who want different things from the same board. Troy is the dependable anchor who can rally the group when the odds look impossible. He shines in straight combat and in executing concentrated teamwork with your other survivors. Evil Troy is a chaos engine wearing a halo of mischief; he tempts you to take risks that could yield big treasure or big trouble, depending on how you read the room.

The combination creates a rhythm that can be both exhilarating and terrifying. You might plan to storm a crypt with two fearless fighters, only for Evil Troy to advocate for setting a trap and luring the undead into a perfect ambush. It is silly, yes, but it requires you to think about your next move in layers rather than just go for the strongest dice roll. The humor comes from the way these two characters interact with zombies, crates, and each other. The tone remains perfectly in line with Zombicide: it is not just about fighting undead; it is about telling a story with your friends around the table. In a hobby where you can overanalyze the rules to death, Troy and Evil Troy remind us to loosen up enough to enjoy the chaos.

## Balance and Replayability

A good expansion adds depth without tipping the entire game on its head. Troy & Evil Troy manages this by adding a couple of new mechanics that feel additive rather than disruptive. The two new characters bring new strategies that can alter how you plan your turns, but they do not overshadow the base game dynamics. The missions included with the expansion scale nicely with player count. At a table of four or five, you get that classic Zombicide pacing: a bit of planning, a few hair-raising moments, and a race to finish the objective before the map becomes a tomb of doom.

Replayability is a core feature of Zombicide, and Troy & Evil Troy extends this by ensuring you have different personality-driven decisions each match. You can pair Troy with other survivors for a strong leadership dynamic or let Evil Troy spice things up with the risk reward playstyle. The variability in each session means you can return to the same map and still feel a different sense of urgency and possibility. If you are a completionist who collects every figure and minis to paint, this expansion offers a couple of new sculpts to add to your shelf, which is the kind of dopamine hit that keeps the hobbyist flame alive.

## Accessibility, Teaching Time, and Player Experience

New players can jump in with some help. The rulebook provides quick references to new mechanics and a handful of sample scenarios that can be triaged quickly. The learning curve for the expansion is not steep; it is designed to ride on the coattails of the base game's clarity, which is a win for newcomers and veterans who are tired of rummaging through extra pages. The components are clearly labeled, the icons are straightforward, and the turn order remains simple enough to keep everyone engaged without resorting to appendix slog.

For players who enjoy house rules, Troy & Evil Troy adds flexible options that can be folded into a campaign mode. It is easy to incorporate their dynamic into your ongoing story arc without upsetting the pacing of standard missions. If you like to narrate your games, you will find the expansion a gold mine for improv and character moments.

## Value, Availability, and Packaging

The physical edition of Troy & Evil Troy sits in the same price bracket as other Zombicide expansions. If you own the Black Plague core set, this is the kind of expansion that breathes new life into your sessions without requiring a complete reset of your board game library. For fans who already own a few expansions, this one slots in easily, offering a different flavor of chaos without becoming a full on rework.

Availability can vary by region and retailer. If you are chasing a copy, check with your favorite local game shop or common online retailers that stock Guillotine Games products. The expansion often sees reprint cycles that align with the base game, so don’t panic if you see it out of stock for a moment; the zombie apocalypse can be a little dramatic at times, but it is not an endless summer.

## Comparisons to Other Zombicide Expansions

There are many expansions for Zombicide Black Plague, from the big monster packs to mini-campaigns. Troy & Evil Troy occupies a middle space in terms of scope and price. It offers recognizable personality dynamics without turning the game into a monster-size campaign. In that sense, the expansion is ideal for groups who want to deepen the storytelling angle while keeping the board manageable. It is not the beefiest expansion in the line, but it is among the most character-driven, which for many players is the real draw.

If you have already tried other expansions that introduce wild new mechanics, you may be pleasantly surprised by the restrained elegance of Troy and Evil Troy. They feel like a natural extension rather than a radical detour, which is the sweet spot for players who want a little more flavor without heavy retooling.

For painting enthusiasts, this expansion offers a reason to try alternative basing, a chance to experiment with metallics for armor, and a few dynamic poses that look good on the display shelf.

## Final Thoughts and Geeknite Verdict

In the end, Troy & Evil Troy is a surprisingly well-balanced addition to Zombicide Black Plague English Edition. It provides strong character dynamics, scalable gameplay, and enough jam to drive sessions to exciting conclusions without turning the board into a math homework exercise. The humor remains light, the theme remains coherent with the base game, and the expansions slot in with a level of polish that reveals Guillotine Games expertise in balancing thematic content with mechanical clarity.

If you want to invest in an expansion that adds personality to your survivors without requiring a full break from your existing campaign, this is a strong candidate. It is especially recommended for groups who love a good narrative beat as much as they love a near misadventure in a tomb full of hungry zombies. Troy and Evil Troy manage to feel essential without overshadowing the rest of your crew, which is a trick many expansions fail to master. The result is a product that earns its place on the table and on the shelf next to its core set.
