## How to Play: A Refresher for Veterans and a Tutorial for Newbies
If you’ve played Cards Against Humanity before, Version 2.0 won’t reinvent the wheel; it refines the wheel, sharpens the spokes, and adds a few UK-specific knobs for flavor. The core gameplay remains: one player is the Card Czar each round, the others play white answer cards in reaction to the black prompt card, and the best (or most hilariously inappropriate) match wins the round.

### Setup and Basic Rules
- Shuffle both decks and deal each player a fixed number of white cards (usually 10). If you’re playing with a larger group, you can adjust to 7 or 8 to keep rounds snappy.
- The Card Czar flips a black card that contains the prompt. This is the “question” card of the round.
- Each player selects one white card from their hand that they think best answers the prompt and places it face down.
- The Card Czar collects the submitted cards, reads them aloud in a theatrical fashion, and selects a winner for that round.
- The winner keeps the black card as a point; the round resets with the new Card Czar (clockwise or any order you prefer).

### Gameplay Variants and House Rules
Version 2.0 bundles a few house-rule variants to accommodate different play group sizes and sensitivity levels. Some common options include:
- The Safety Card: requires players to avoid extremely offensive prompt/response pairings. It’s a soft guardrail to keep the game from venturing into genuinely uncomfortable territory.
- The Time Limit: to keep things moving, you can impose a 30-second clock for card submissions. This heightens the spontaneity and dumps a bucket of chaos into the room.
- The Slogan Rule: players can veto a black card that’s deemed too edgy for the group’s mood; when vetoed, the Card Czar must draw a new prompt.

### Typical Round Length and Group Dynamics
In groups of 4–8, rounds typically run 10–20 seconds per submission, plus a little extra time for the Card Czar to deliver a dramatic read. The more players you have, the more surprising the outcomes—this is where UK edition often shines: given the syllables and cultural references, some combinations produce hilariously speedy snap judgments, while others leave the room muttering, “That’s a stretch, mate.” If your group has more reserved members, you’ll definitely want to set expectations ahead of time to prevent the game from turning into a boring shift at a paperwork factory.
