## What is Sushi Go! and why it matters in your life
### Quick elevator pitch
Sushi Go! is a fast, family-friendly drafting game where players pass cards around a table to build the best combination of sushi dishes for points. Each round, you pick one card from your hand and pass the rest to the next player. The twist? Your choices ripple through the table as cards change hands and fate decides that your future happiness hinges on whether you drafted the right nigiri or accidentally handed your friend three maki rolls in a row.

Sushi Go! is not going to single-handedly save the world from climate change or bring about world peace. But it will teach you some basic strategic ideas: card counting, risk assessment, and when to say to heck with it and take a dumpling that pays off in the long run. It’s a microcosm of drafting theory in a package small enough to fit in a backpack, a purse, or that anxiety-inducing, perfectly-stacked desk drawer where you keep all your game night snacks.

### The components you actually care about
- A deck of adorable sushi cards featuring nigiri, maki, sashimi, tempura, pudding, wasabi, and more.
- Score sheets (or a mental scoring system if you hate math as much as you hate soggy seaweed).
- A simple rulebook that fits on a napkin, which is perfect since you will be eating anyway.

The design is intentionally cute, not coy, and the gameplay is designed so that you can learn the ropes in minutes while still getting enough strategic depth to keep your brain engaged for a full evening of gaming. It is the kind of game that makes you feel clever without requiring a math degree or the patience of a saint to remember a bazillion card interactions.

## Card types and how they score you points
### Nigiri: tiny powerhouses with tiny footprints
Nigiri cards are the tiny rock stars of Sushi Go!. They come in three flavors: egg, salmon, and squid, each worth different points. The catch? You only get the points if you successfully collect as many of the same type as possible in your current meal. The more you collect, the better your potential score, but if you spread yourself too thin, you might end up with a handful of puny nigiri that barely buys you a cup of tea in the afterlife of culinary glory.

### Maki: the multi-card multiplier
Maki rolls are your multi-point bets. In each round, you track who has the most and second-most maki rolls. The winner? A nice chunk of points that scales with your competition and your ability to read who is likely to overtake you. The fun part is that the lead can swing wildly—the same deck that gives you a lead in one moment can tumble into chaos the very next round as other players snap up those elusive maki rolls.

### Tempura and Sashimi: the long-game duo
Tempura rewards you for collecting a pair; sashimi rewards you for collecting triples. Both require you to think in terms of rounds, not just one hand. Tempura is the cautious, patient player's dream: if you can snag a clean pair across rounds, you are laughing all the way to the pudding. Sashimi, on the other hand, is a sprint to the finish—the more triples you pull off, the bigger your payoff. But beware: missing the window on either card type can leave you with nothing but seaweed and regret.

### Pudding: the sneaky, endgame pivot
Pudding is the optional expansion that slides in during the final scoring. It’s unique because it is not about the current round’s dish value but your ability to score pudding points at the very end. This means even if you had a disastrous run of rounds, you can still claw back victory by cleverly collecting pudding cards and hoping other players forget that desserts exist. It’s the culinary moral of the story: dessert matters in the end more than you thought.

### Wasabi: the one-card power boost
Wasabi acts as a multipliers card: if you get a nigiri card with wasabi on it, its value doubles. It’s a classic example of a single card changing the entire narrative of a hand. The trick is to time your wasabi correctly—don’t waste a one-card wasabi on a low-value nigiri, but also don’t block your opponents from using it when it would ruin their dinner party.

### How all the pieces fit together for scoring
Sushi Go! rewards you for planning ahead and executing a consistent strategy across multiple rounds. The scoring is not arcane, but it rewards a balanced approach: pick a dish type you want to build toward, keep track of what your opponents are collecting, and don’t forget to glance at pudding as the game approaches the finish line. The best players can juggle multiple priorities at once: chasing maki dominance, hitting a sashimi triple, and ensuring that their pudding cards don’t vanish into the ether of other players’ strategies.

## Strategy: drafting with style rather than disasters
### Reading the table: how many players and what it does to your choices
The number of players heavily shifts the value of certain cards. In a 2-player game, you can often predict what your opponent will take next round and plan a counter-move accordingly. In a 4-player game, a single bad pick can cascade into a meltdown, and suddenly you are clutching a plate of dumplings that nobody wants because the table has decided that it is your turn to suffer. The key is to not get locked into one path. Be flexible, keep an eye on your opponents’ tendencies, and be mindful of what remains in the deck as you approach the midgame.

### The three-phase decision trick
1) Early phase: prioritize flexible, high-yield cards with low risk. This often means chasing a few nigiri basics or small maki sets that can be built upon later. 2) Midgame: you now know more about what others are collecting. Use this phase to pivot toward overlooked synergies like sashimi triples or temporary building blocks such as dumplings or wasabi combos. 3) Endgame: pudding and heavy scoring wrappers come into play. Make sure you aren’t left with a squandered hand and a dessert you forgot to guard. This is the moment where the game’s pace accelerates and your heart rate climbs to match the intensity of a spicy tuna roll.

### The art of the bluff and misdirection
Sushi Go! rewards deception in small doses. Sometimes you pass up a card you want because you suspect your neighbor will snag it and keep you from completing a set. Or you pass a high-scoring card to bait your opponent into misreading the table. The social element—reading expressions, shrugging with confidence, and pretending you didn’t see the two-drawer plan you actually had—adds a playful layer to what is otherwise a clean, fast drafting system.

### A practical round-by-round plan for new players
- Round 1: pick a flexible starting card that doesn’t box you into a single strategy. A balanced approach is often the safest.
- Round 2-3: start tracking opponents’ choices. If you notice a lot of sashimi going around, maybe pivot toward sashimi triples or pick up a reliable tempura pair.
- Round 4-5: this is where you cinch the pudding and pin down the maki leaders. Don’t chase everything; prioritize what remains in the deck and the pudding’s endgame value.
- Endgame: tally up and optimize. If you’re close in points, a last-second pudding card may tip the scales. If you’re not, don’t panic, just savor the victory of having survived another round of adorable culinary suspense.

## Common pitfalls that derail your tasty strategy (and how to avoid them)
- Overfocusing on one card type: It is tempting to chase a dream of perfect sashimi with a single card type, but this often collapses under the weight of a competitive table. Keep a flexible plan and a few alternative paths.
- Forgetting pudding endgame value: The endgame score can swing the game dramatically if pudding cards were neglected earlier. Don’t forget to plan toward the pudding ladder as you approach the last rounds.
- Misjudging the round: Sushi Go! moves quickly, and a mistaken pick can leave you scrambling for mediocre points later. Trust your initial instinct but be prepared to adjust on the fly.
- Not paying attention to opponents: If you ignore what others are going for, you risk walking into a trap you could have avoided. The best players keep mental track of the board and adapt their plans accordingly.
