## Features that matter

### Cable management and power distribution

One of the standout features is the cable routing system. Expect a central spine or a series of vertical channels that keep power cords, USB-C hubs, and the occasional ethernet line from turning the desk into a spider web of doom. The better systems offer integrated power or modular power strips with surge protection, plus USB-C quick charge for staff who insist that their laptops absolutely must charge at a rate that will impress a MacBook Pro from 2019. The downside? If you’re the kind of team that constantly rearranges the seating chart, those cables may need re-tidying after every sprint review. Fortunately, most systems are designed for quick reconfiguration, so you can blame the agile process for your cables’ little rebellion.

### Modularity and expandability

Modularity is the star here. You can adjust panel heights, swap out privacy screens, and reconfigure seating layouts as your product teams re-prioritize. Some configurations can morph from a straight line into a horseshoe or U-shape, promoting impromptu stand-ups or user research sessions that require space for participants and a flip chart that could double as a wall. Expandability also means you can add more work surface if you onboard another new hire without having to replace the entire system. It’s not a unicorn feature, but it’s pretty close when you’re a growing team with shifting needs.

### Standing options and ergonomics

If you’re chasing a stand-up-friendly setup, check whether the system offers height-adjustable legs or add-ons that enable standing over longer sessions. The best variants provide smooth height transitions and a stable surface when raised. The last thing you want is a wobble during a critical planning session, which would turn your well-planned roadmap into a graph of anxiety spikes. When standing, consider the impact on monitor height and keyboard tray alignment so you don’t end up with neck cramps and a new category of chronic shoulder tension.

### Setup and space requirements

First, space planning matters. A ten-person desk system won’t magically appear in a closet. The measurement game is real: you’ll want a floor plan that accounts for clearance around the desk (for chairs and foot traffic) and a decent radius for the team’s movement during high-energy stand-ups. We found that an overall footprint of around 15–20 feet in width and 6–8 feet in depth was a comfortable minimum for a two-row or horseshoe configuration with a small corridor behind. If you squeeze more, you risk the inevitable “one person’s elbow is someone else’s coffee zone” situation. If you go for a larger room, you gain the ability to add lounge seating and a whiteboard corridor—two more features that can turn a tactical review into a joyful brainstorming festival.

### Stability under dynamic loads

The mechanical core of the desk is surprisingly sturdy. Ten adults shifting positions, leaning on the surface during design critiques, and the occasional mock sprint test created a lot of dynamic load. The desk held up, with only minor flex in the lighter panels when the room’s energy peaked. If your team includes a heavy-handed designer who tests the panel by pressing a finger to the edge to see if it’s truly resonance, you may want to ensure you’ve got reinforced joints and a robust panel connection. The good news is that you can adjust the configuration on the fly without needing a toolbox and a second mortgage on office supplies.

### Privacy vs. collaboration balance

A recurring theme was the tension between privacy and collaboration. People liked the ability to slide privacy screens between seats when deep work was required, but the moment someone asked for feedback, the screens came down like a well-trained social butterfly. The ten-person setup shines when you have a clear process for quick collaboration: designate a central screen wall for demos, use movable partitions to create temporary meeting pods, and keep the rest of the row as a shared surface for notes and quick sketches. The result is a living space that can morph from quiet-focused work to a high-energy review without the furniture fighting back.

## Setup ideas and hacks

- Build a flexible “center stage”: Use a larger central monitor wall or a whiteboard rail to keep the team aligned during crits and planning. This becomes the shared canvas for the group while the individual desks remain personal space for deep work.
- Create zoning with movable panels: Block off a quiet zone for focused tasks and a social zone for stand-ups without needing a room divider or a hallway. The trick is to let the team place panels where needed and then move them when priorities shift.
- Embrace cable choreography: Run power and data along the central spine and out to each workstation. If your office is prone to cable chaos, invest in color-coded cable sleeves to help folks quickly identify the right cords for their laptops and docking stations. The result is organization that reduces desk clutter and the “armageddon of charging cables” dread.
- Active collaboration corners: Reserve a couple of stations for quick client reviews or partner sessions. A small seating cluster or ergonomic stools in a corner can become the go-to place for micro-sprints and lively discussions without turning the entire room into a coffee shop.
- Lighting that travels with you: Install adjustable task lighting at key spots to avoid that office glow that makes a spreadsheet look like a crime scene. The right lighting makes stand-up meetings feel less like a battle and more like an epic debugging session with better eyesight.

## Maintenance and durability

Durability matters when you’re hosting sessions that feel like a small orchestra of devices and humans. The components should resist daily wear and tear: chair wheels, surface scratches, and the occasional spill that requires a rescue operation using a napkin and a heroic sigh. The 10-person workstation tends to hold up well under routine cleaning and occasional rearrangements. If you factor in a quarterly check-up—tighten a few screws, realign the panels, and swap out any damaged grommets—the system can sustain active use for years without becoming an eyesore or a health risk.

In terms of maintenance, you’ll want to plan for two things: 1) quarterly inspections of the panel connectors to ensure no wobble creeps in, and 2) a simple reorganization ritual every six months to re-evaluate how your team actually uses the space. Our team found that retaining a small “desk stewardship” ritual—where someone takes charge of rearranging the layout after a project wrap—keeps the space dynamic and prevents it from turning into a static, monolithic block of furniture.
