## What is the Special Guest Box?
The Special Guest Box is CMON’s way of acknowledging a designer’s influence on a game line, and in this case, Edouard Guiton has left behind a signature flavor that fans didn’t know they were missing until they opened the lid. The box promises a handful of new miniatures, a couple of “experimental” sculpts, and a slew of scenario cards that nudge Zombicide’s base rules in a direction that is equal parts elegant strategy and gloriously chaotic shenanigans.

Contents, in spoiler-safe terms:

- A set of limited-edition miniatures featuring Edouard’s design fingerprints: angular helmets, expressive faces, and poses that imply “I’m planning a strategic barbecue, who’s bringing the groceries?”
- A curated assortment of new survivor cards with quirky quirks and occasional dramatic quotes that you will swear you invented to accompany your own hobby habit.
- Two or three party-puzzle objective cards to spice up co-op play and ensure you get to the end of a mission while arguing over who forgot to bring the correct color dice.
- A handful of branded tokens, dashboards, and a small, tactile book that reads like a designer’s love letter to the box art. And yes, there is a gloss on the cover that will catch the light just enough to pretend you’re looking at a tiny neon billboard advertising an underground zombie club.

## The Unboxing Experience: A Tiny Theater of Cardboard and Hope
Unboxing is a ritual. The Special Guest Box starts with a lid that opens with a satisfying sigh, as if the box itself is exhaling after surviving the long journey from CMON’s warehouse, where it was lovingly supervised by a team of people who clearly respect fine dice-ology and efficient plastic. The inner tray is a compact, well-lit micro-stage for miniatures that feel almost too stylish for the undead. It’s not just “take out the models”; it’s “perform the unboxing dance.”

From the first glimpse, Edouard Guiton’s influence is palpable. There’s an intentionality to the posing that screams “story before stats.” You can imagine a short, dramatic vignette: a survivor bracing their stance, another peeking around a corner with a blade that has just the right amount of etched wear to imply backstory. That backstory, in the context of a board game about killing zombies with friends, is a refreshing change of pace from the usual “one more generic torso” approach we’ve grown accustomed to.

The cards smell faintly of new cardboard and old game-room glory. You’ll appreciate the tactile feel—the stock is robust enough for heavy shuffles and furious fan flics when you realize you’ve drawn the same card you drew last game. If you’re a completionist, you’ll want to sleeve these babies because Edouard’s card art deserves long-term archiving, not a wear pattern from your sweaty gaming sessions.

The miniature quality, as with many CMON releases, is a mixed bag in the best possible way. Some sculpts carry an editorial flair that makes you question whether you’ve been playing a zombie game or watching a kinetic sculpture exhibit. Others? They’re simply incredibly well-detailed, with enough personality to justify a tiny interview panel on the back of the blister. The “special” guest minis are not just functionally different; they feel like characters who could have their own side quests if the box were a little longer and the campaign a lot more dramatic.


## Design Philosophy: Edouard Guiton’s Touch on Zombicide
Edouard’s approach to game design tends to blend clean silhouettes with narrative flourishes. In this Special Guest Box, the balance is front and center: you get crisp, readable miniatures that perform well on the tabletop, plus enough flavor text, unique cards, and scenario prompts to spark creativity without derailing the core mechanics.

From a design perspective, the box nudges players toward thematic immersion. The survivor cards carry one-liners that are clearly the result of a designer who has spent countless nights arguing with paint pots about whether a zombie should have a smile or a scowl (the correct answer being both, depending on the day). The new scenario cards are not just “here’s a new objective” — they feel like they were written to be performed, to be acted out in a living-room theatre of roleplay and roars.

If you’re a metrics person, you’ll notice the balance changes are not heavy-handed. The new rules adjustments feel like an invitation to experiment rather than a mandate to abandon the base box. The design choices encourage players to try new tactics, test novel synergies, and, perhaps most importantly, to argue about which survivor name best suits their playstyle. Spoiler: it’s always the loud engineer who insists on optimizing the grenade economy.

## Artwork, Theme, and Production Quality
The theme work is where Edouard’s influence becomes most visible. The linework on the cards and the painterly textures on the miniatures reflect a style that’s both cinematic and a touch whimsical in the way that only a designer who loves the medium can achieve. The box art itself is a conversation starter. It’s not just a product shot; it’s a scene.
