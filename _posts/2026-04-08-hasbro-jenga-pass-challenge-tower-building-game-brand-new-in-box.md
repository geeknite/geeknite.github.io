## How the Pass Challenge works in practice

### Setup and first impression
Setting up is as straightforward as a grocery list: stack the blocks tightly, form the base, and ensure the tower stands tall and elegant. The first player chooses a starting block from any level, removes it, and then passes the tower to the next player along a designated path. Each time a block is removed, the players rebuild the tower by sliding the blocks back into place on the high levels. The goal is simple: don’t let the tower crash while enjoying the chaotic ballet of teammates gripping and sliding with varying degrees of precision.

The PASS mechanic introduces a social dynamic that standard Jenga lacks: communication. You have to call out which block you’re removing, warn teammates about impending instability, and, depending on your mood, flirt with disaster in a very dramatic fashion. It’s not just about who can be the last block thief but who can orchestrate a safe handoff without turning the table into a chaotic sculpture of splinters and applause.

### The rhythm of play
One of the joys of this game is the tempo. A well-timed pass can keep the tower upright for a surprisingly long stretch. A poorly timed pass, however, can cause a gentle or not-so-gentle cascade. The timer adds a sense of urgency—your team has a window to pass and reposition blocks, or you’re going to watch gravity do its thing while you sip your beverage and hope your chair doesn’t betray you.

Strategy emerges quickly, but so does chaos. Some players go for the “safe blocks” on the lower levels, others treat the tower like a puzzle and chase blocks with the most potential to destabilize the top. It’s a game about risk assessment under social pressure: do you pick a block that threatens to topple everything, or do you play it safe and let someone else later pay the price? The board is a stage, and the blocks are the props in a tiny existential comedy where the punchline is a tower that collapses in a dramatic puff of wood dust.
