## What is Baraja Española?
Baraja Española is a family of card decks used across Spain and many Latin American countries for a variety of traditional games. The core idea is simple and delightful: four suits, a handful of face cards, and a crisp, sturdy deck that refuses to bend when you’re trying to bluff your way through a brisca trick. The four suits are:

- espadas (swords)
- oros (coins)
- copas (cups)
- bastos (clubs or rods, depending on your locale)

A typical 40-card Baraja Española includes the numbers 1 through 7, plus 10 (sota), 11 (caballo), and 12 (rey) in each suit. You might hear each face card referred to culturally as a sotain the context of some traditional games, or as a caballo when someone wants to sound fancy at the table. In many games this exact setup—no 8s and 9s—creates a rhythm distinct from the 52-card world you’re probably used to. Now, what does it mean to have two full decks of this tradition on your table? It means you can run bigger groups, greater pass-backs, and more dramatic misreads of a hand you should have seen coming a mile away.

Two-deck sets often present either two copies of every card (80 cards total if each deck is 40) or two complete 40-card decks that you can shuffle together for big, chaotic games. Either way, you’re increasing the combinatorics, not shrinking them, which is exactly the vibe we geeks crave.

### The card values and their quirks
Understanding Baraja Española card values is essential before you try to bluff your way through a brisca hand. In the 40-card version, the numeric order is 1–7 plus 10, 11, and 12 in each suit.
