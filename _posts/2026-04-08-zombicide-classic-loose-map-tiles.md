## How to evaluate a preowned tile bundle

Evaluating loose map tiles requires a balance of enthusiasm and practicality. You’re not just paying for cardboard; you’re paying for the potential of tabletop storytelling and the joy of sorting by edition like a tiny, undead-themed library. Here are essential checks and methods to assess value and condition when you’re eyeing a preowned stash:

### Visual inspection: what to look for

- Warping and bending: Light warping is common with older tiles, especially if they’ve spent time under humid conditions. A slight curl isn’t necessarily a death blow, but pronounced warping can affect your ability to lay flat boards during setup.
- Surface wear: Look for scratches, scuffs, or attempts at re-laminating with tape. Surface wear often indicates heavy use; mild wear can add character. The key is to ensure no deep gouges or creases that could snag models or obstruct movements.
- Color fading: Sun-exposed tiles might have faded colors. If the tiling relied on bright contrasts (think neon accents or high-contrast corridors), fading can alter readability during play.
- Backing integrity: Check the backs for creases or signs of water damage. Back damage can cause tiles to sit unevenly or slide apart when you’re shuffling the board.

### Physical checks: a tactile approach

- Thickness consistency: Run your finger along the tile edge. If you notice one tile significantly thinner or thicker than others, it might indicate a resizing or a re-gluing episode from a prior owner. This can complicate stacking and sliding as you play.
- Corner integrity: Tiles should click together neatly. If corners chip, you’ll see gaps that disrupt visual continuity on your board. It’s not a pure gameplay showstopper, but you’ll notice it during setup and teardown.
- Labels and markings: Some tiles might have pencil marks, doodles, or even quarter-sized stickers. While these are usually harmless, they can sometimes obscure important terrain symbols or spawn points. If you’re buying, request a discount and a clean slate if possible.

### Edition-based considerations

- Art and iconography: Tiles from different editions may display slightly different icons for doors, obstacles, or spawn zones. This isn’t a deal-breaker, but it’s worth knowing if you plan to mix tiles across editions in a single campaign. Some players love the mixed-edition chaos; others find it confusing.
- Terrain density: Earlier tiles often prioritized denser interior layouts. If the batch you’re eyeing includes a lot of interior corridors, you’ll get that classic claustrophobic feel—great for tense close-quarters combat.

### Functionality over perfection

Ultimately, you’re buying tiles to stage a game. If the tiles lie flat, the art is legible, and the edges connect without gapping, you’re in a good position. If a tile has a goofy paint scratch that adds character rather than obscuring a symbol, that’s a feature, not a flaw. The real question is whether you’ll enjoy creating scenarios with them, and whether the price justifies assembling a mosaic you’ll want to stare at while a dozen tiny zombies clamber across the table.

## Value, price, and how to negotiate a good deal

Preowned tile lots are often priced by perceived rarity, total tile count, and condition. A five-edition bundle, if well cared for, can be a superb bargain compared to hunting down each edition separately. Here are tips to maximize value:

- Ask for a high-resolution photo set: Close-ups of corners, edges, and any inner artwork will help you gauge condition more accurately than a blurry listing. If the seller refuses to provide clear images, consider it a red flag and walk away with a confident swagger.
- Check for missing tiles: Inventory can be tricky. A few missing tiles are not the end of the world if you’re comfortable with substitutions or making your own with blank tiles. Missing pieces might also be a bargaining chip—negotiate a bit down if they’re missing a significant portion.
- Bundle with related components: If there are other bits and bobs (rulebooks, standees, or tokens) included in the lot, factor their value into your price. Sometimes the extras can skew the price in a favorable direction.
- Consider shipping risk: Loose tiles are more prone to damage in transit. If possible, ask for bundled insurance or local pickup. A seller who won’t offer safe packaging is a seller you might want to avoid.

Value is context-dependent. If you’re a completist with a growing Zombicide library, a well-kept five-edition tile stash can be priceless. If your shelf space is limited and you’re chasing minimalism, you may want to cherry-pick the best of the best editions and thrift out anything that looks tired.

## Care and maintenance to extend tile life

Preowned tiles aren’t disposable by default. A little care goes a long way toward preserving their look and feel for years of undead-slaying sessions. Here are practical care tips:

- Store flat, in a dry environment: Humidity is the enemy of cardboard. Use a few baking sheets between stacks or place tiles in a shallow box with a flat weight on top to maintain flatness. If you’re in a humid climate, consider desiccant packs.
- Avoid direct sunlight: Prolonged exposure can fade artwork more than a few sessions would. If you’re displaying tiles, keep them away from windows or use UV-protective sleeves for display purposes.
- Clean gently: If tiles pick up stray pencil marks or dust, use a soft brush or microfiber cloth. Avoid solvents or water on the surface; many tile backs are not sealed to be water-safe.
- Use tile sleeves or protective storage: For especially prized tiles, use archival sleeves on the front to preserve artwork. For the backs, don’t block any manufacturing marks that might be useful for future ID purposes.

Maintenance isn’t glamorous, but it buys you time. Time is the one resource undead cannot steal from you, unless you’re playing a marathon campaign with an overly enthusiastic group of players who forget to take breaks.
