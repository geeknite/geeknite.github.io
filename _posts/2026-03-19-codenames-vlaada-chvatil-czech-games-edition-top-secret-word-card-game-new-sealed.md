Introduction

Codenames is a party word game that spins on a single tiny axis: the power of one word to conjure a sprawling network of associations. The rules are deceptively simple, which makes it either perfect for a casual game night or a personal proof that your brain is a chaotic but lovable algorithm. The game places two teams, red and blue, against each other. Each team has a spymaster who sees a secret key that marks 25 word cards on the table. The spymasters alternate giving one-word clues followed by a number. The clue points to several of their team’s words on the table. The goal is to guess all your team’s words before the other team wins, all while avoiding the other team’s words, a bystander, and especially the assassin word that ends the game immediately—boo, hiss, you lose.

Codenames is part of a lineage of Vlaada Chvátil games that feel like the designer pulled you aside at a sci-fi convention and whispered: here’s a clever word game where you fight with meta-linguistic vibes and bragging rights. Chvátil has a knack for making brainy concepts approachable and wildly entertaining in equal measure. It’s a design that shines brightest when you’ve got a group with good chemistry and roughly a dozen opinions about whether the word “bananas” can be connected to “monkeys” and “space.” If you like word games with a dash of strategy and a pinch of chaos, this sealed copy is probably worth the dopamine spike you’ll feel when you whisper your perfect clue and your team actually gets all the words in one shot.

The game comes in a sealed box that feels like it’s hiding a very confident secret. How to Play: The Quick Version

If you’ve never played Codenames before, the rules are compact enough to fit on one piece of printer paper, which is nice for those situations where your group has a “rules are suggestions” vibe. Here’s the gist:

- The Spymaster for the respective color gives a clue consisting of a single word and a number. The number indicates how many words on the board relate to the clue. For example, if the clue is “Ocean 3,” there are three Word Cards that tie to the sea or ocean theme.
- The clue must be a single word; no compound phrases unless your group is ready to argue about whether “spaceflight” counts as one word or two. The Spymaster must avoid giving a clue that directly names an opponent’s word. This is where the fun and potential accusations begin.
- Teammates guess Word Cards one by one, using the clue as their compass. They flip the Word Cards to reveal whether a card matches the red team, blue team, a bystander, or the assassin. If they hit the assassin card, the turn ends and the opposing team gets control of the board. If they reveal all of their team’s Word Cards first, they win. If they only reveal bystanders for too long, the other team gets precious progress toward victory.
- The game ends when one team has found all of its agents or when the assassin is revealed.

The beauty of this system is that you learn a lot about your team’s collective mind. The same clue can unlock wildly different associations depending on who’s listening: a linguist who loves etymology, a gamer who worships wordplay, an improviser who treats the table like a stage, or the neighbor who uses “ma’am” to describe almost everything. The outcome is a mirror held up to your group’s shared vocabulary and the hilarity that occurs when those vocabularies collide.

Why It Works

Codenames works because it blends social deduction, vocabulary, and a pinch of strategy into a compact package. It’s not about who has the largest vocabulary or who can conjure the most obscure reference. It’s about aligning mental maps with your teammates. The Spymaster’s clue can be brilliant or risky, and that tension—anticipation, misreads, the occasional roaring laughter when someone misinterprets “banana” as “Banana Republic” and a cascade of other words—creates memorable moments that outlive the game. It’s the kind of game where a single, perfect clue can turn a table full of strangers into a team that argues over whether a “quartz” is a rock or a jewel as if it’s the most profound philosophical question of our age.

The Spymaster Challenge: Tips and Tricks

If you find yourself in the Spymaster role, you’re basically hosting a tiny, high-stakes TED Talk about your team’s shared associations. Here are some quick tips that helped groups we played with:

- Start with broad connections: a clue that ties to multiple words broadly reduces the risk of hitting your own cards. For example, a clue like “Adventure 3” might map to several action words and places, but you’ll need to be careful about what it could imply for the assassin side.
- Consider the table’s vocabulary: if your team uses a lot of pop culture references, leaning into that can be an effective strategy. If your team is more literal-minded, go for safer, more obvious connections.
- Don’t overcommit: a clue with a large number is a trap that invites your team to guess for too long and risk a misstep into the assassin region. A well-timed smaller clue can lead to a clean win just as well as a big, risky one.
- Read your team’s tone: the best clue-giving sometimes isn’t the cleverest word but the one that gets the group laughing and momentarily breaking their own carefully constructed plan. Laughter is a powerful clue in itself.
