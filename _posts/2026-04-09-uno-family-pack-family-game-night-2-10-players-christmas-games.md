## Rules, setup, and house rules

### Setup basics
- Shuffle all decks together (if you have multiple decks). If you’re using a split-package configuration, decide how many decks to pull from for your game size.
- Deal seven cards to each player (adjust if you want shorter rounds).
- Place one card face up to start the discard pile. If the first card is a special action card, you apply the effect immediately or re-draw and reshuffle depending on your household preference.
- The remaining cards form the draw pile.

### Core UNO rules (as they apply here)
- Match the color or number of the top card on the discard pile to play a card.
- If you can’t play, draw one card (or more if house rules allow it) and your turn ends.
- Special cards (Skip, Reverse, Draw Two, Wild, Wild Draw Four) retain their classic effects, but you’ll find these adapted for multi-deck play in some versions of the family pack—usually with an optional rule to prevent instant chaos in the first turn.
- When you have one card left, you must shout UNO. If another player catches you forgetting, there’s usually a penalty draw. It’s tradition; resist the urge to turn this into a courtroom drama.

### Family game rules and variations
To keep the game fun and accessible for everyone, try a few light modifications:
- Team play: In large groups (6–10 players), pair up players into teams of two. Teammates share a hand; the goal is for one team to reach UNO together. It’s a goofy change that encourages collaboration and silly celebrations when someone yells UNO.
- Quick rounds: For family gatherings with limited time, use a timer (e.g., 15 minutes) and tally points. The team or person with the most points after the timer wins a small prize.
- Holiday mode: Add a seasonal card into the mix (from a spare deck or a home-made add-on). The card can trigger a “gift swap” or “reindeer dash” event—rotate turns quickly or force everyone to swap hands—just to keep the holiday chaos contained within a fun framework.

### How to handle late-game drama
UNO is a game of ebbs and flows. In a family setting, it’s not just about who wins but who gets to tell the best story about the final card. Some tips to keep things friendly:
- Keep language light and fun; avoid personal jabs.
- If tempers flare, call a 2-minute “cool-down” break and offer hot chocolate or cookies to reset the mood.
- Celebrate near-wins with goofy gestures (sound effects, exaggerated claps, or a quick victory dance). It’s surprising how much a silly ritual can defuse tension.
