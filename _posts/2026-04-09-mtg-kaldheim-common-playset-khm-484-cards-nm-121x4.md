## What is a Common Playset?
In Magic: The Gathering terms, a common playset is four copies of every common in a given set. For Kaldheim, that equals 121 distinct commons times four, for a grand total of 484 cards. It's basically the entire common ecosystem of a set, all in one tidy, four-fold package. The result is a bulk treasure trove that lets you prototype decks, test strategies, and have enough material to run a year-long draft party with your friends and their cats.

## Why People Buy Bulk Commons
- Budget deck building: A four-copy-per-card bulk gives you the chance to assemble budget versions of new archetypes without dropping a mortgage on the more expensive rare and mythic cards.
- Proxies and testing: You can test a lot of ideas quickly, which is perfect for those who like to jam four games a night to see if a deck is actually viable.
- Collection completeness: Owning a complete common playset means you can claim parity with your friends when they brag about their rares, and you can prove you had a plan all along.

## Sorting and Organization
- Color-first, then mana cost, then card type and text. This is a handy method that scales well as you go from a single box to a dozen. Your structure should be intuitive enough that you can locate a specific copy of a specific common within minutes, not hours.
- Storage options: Binders with four-card pages, boxes with dividers, and labeled bins for each color. Four copies of each card means a substantial chunk of table space, so you’ll want to optimize your sorting to minimize the wasted cubit of real estate.
- Text notes: Some commons share similar names or effects across colors; a small note field can help you quickly identify the exact card you want to pull.

## Deal Breakdown: Price and Value
- Price structure: Bulk commons are rarely a treasure chest waiting to explode with monetary value. The value is often very low per card, typically a few cents or less, depending on the card. The real value is the ability to build decks, the convenience of accessibility, and the time saved by not hunting through singles.
- The opportunistic value: A few Kaldheim commons may spike in price due to tribal synergy or a modern shell, but most will remain in the realm of bulk. If you’re chasing top-dollar flips, look for a few outliers rather than the entire set, and expect a few pennies here and there rather than a fortune.
- Price-checking: If you want to verify current values, consult external resources like MTGGoldfish or TCGPlayer, but don’t expect a windfall; this is bulk, not a lottery.

## Potential Deck Building with Commons
- Budget mono-color decks: A four-copy-per-card bulk set can yield multiple copies of a single color, which helps you target a mono-color or two-color plan. You can start with a stable curve and then fill in the rest with the rest of the commons.
- Multi-color synergy: Kaldheim’s color identity and the Foretell mechanic provide opportunities to pair colors for a flexible early-to-mid game plan. With 484 cards, you’ll likely find enough synergy to build an interesting deck with minimal rares, and perhaps a few cheap rares to augment the base plan.
- Tribal themes: Some commons support specific creature types or tribal synergists. You can draft a tribal deck using only commons, and then incorporate a couple of rare or mythic cards for curve and finishing power.
- Limited value: In sealed or draft, the common pool becomes a crucial part of your deck-building. The right subset of commons can push you into aggressive tempo, while a different subset can slow the game with blockers.

## Tips and Tricks
- A consistent labeling system helps you locate things quickly. A simple color-coded scheme (C1, C2, etc.) keeps you from losing track of your duplicates.
- Inventory tracking: A simple spreadsheet or a few checklists can keep you sane. You can log the card name, color, and quantity, and a note about which deck you’re planning to slot it into.
- Slow and steady sorting: It’s easier if you build a rhythm. Do a color pass, then a type pass, and finally a text pass to capture evergreen interactions.
- The joy of the process: The process is a large part of the value of the purchase. It’s not just about the cards; it’s about the mental game you play with yourself while sorting.
- Use duplicates strategically: Duplicates are not worthless; if you find a few duplicates that pair well with Monocolor or two-color decks, you can build a project around them without chasing rare staples.
