## What is the Hogwarts Edition

Codenames Card Game — Back to Hogwarts Edition takes the classic tug of war between two teams and drops you into a magical campus where professors, mischief, and magical creatures collide with wordplay. The setup mirrors the familiar 5x5 grid of word cards, but with a twist: every word card is now a Hogwarts themed term or phrase. Think things like basilisk, quaffle, patronus, transfiguration, aoe incantation names, and a few cheeky in-jokes that only long time HP fans will catch. The board becomes a map of Hogwarts life rather than a plain word field, and the spy masters use clues that lean on spellcasting, House allegiances, and the occasional inevitable misfire.

The core mechanics stay true to Codenames: two teams, red and blue, with a spymaster giving one word clues along with a number, helping teammates guess their agents without stepping on the assassin card. The Hogwarts edition spices up the flavor but leaves the game’s skeleton intact. That means if you know the original rules, you can jump into this edition with minimal confusion, but there is enough theme here to feel fresh and occasionally delightfully ridiculous.

If you are curious about the roots of the game, you can explore the classic Codenames rules on the official site and see how this edition riffs on those foundations. Both demand memory, predictive skills, and the ability to shrug off a bad clue with class.

## How to play: quick start guide

If you already know Codenames, you can skip ahead to the House Dynamics. If not, here is a rough map to get you from empty table to tense, hilarious play in under 10 minutes.

### Setup
- Lay out the 25 word cards in a 5x5 grid, all visible to both teams.
- Each team designates a spymaster who sits on opposite sides of the table facing the grid.
- The red and blue teams are assigned a fixed color, with the assassin card hidden among the board riches.
- Shuffle the Hogwarts-themed clue deck and place it within reach of the spymaster.
- Decide on who goes first and reveal the corresponding color on the clue deck. The starting team gets 9 word picks in a 5x5 grid; the other team gets 8.

### The turn and clue mechanics
- The spymaster gives a one-word clue followed by a number. The clue word must relate to one or more of the word cards on the table. The number indicates how many cards on the board are connected to that clue.
- Teammates then try to guess those cards one by one, staying within the limit of the clue number plus potentially one extra guess if they think they know more than the clue suggests. You know the drill: guessing wrong ends your turn and may switch the momentum to the other team.
- If a team guesses an agent card, that card is removed from the board, and the team continues guessing as long as they have numbers left to call. If they hit a neutral card, their turn ends, and the other team gets a chance to guess. If they hit an assassin card, the game immediately ends in the other team’s favor. Expect dramatic reactions when a perfectly reasonable clue collapses into a jumbled misfire.

### The house flavor and clue strategy
The Hogwarts edition nudges you toward clues that connect to wizard lore, spells, magical items, and a healthy dose of house-specific vibes. You can play clean, clever clues like Diagon Alley or Patronus that link multiple cards in one go, or you can lean into the chaos with something more cheeky like Quidditch or Horcrux that carry risk but potential payoff. The key is to balance clever cross-referencing with clarity, because a clue that is too broad can help the other team or doom your own team if misinterpreted.

## Strategy tips: getting the most out of your clues

- Use multi-card connections: Look for clues that tie together several word cards that are clearly your team’s agents. If your red team has three solid red cards connected through a single concept, you can swing momentum quickly by choosing a well-timed wide net clue.
- Mind the room for misdirection: Hogwarts terms can be tricky. If you call something like basilisk, know that it could anchor both sinister and fear-based associations. If you want to minimize risk, lean toward more specific connections like spells or magical items that have distinct imagery.
- Manage the assassin risk: In the Hogwarts edition, the assassin is a reminder that even the Dark Arts have a cost. When you are close to victory, avoid giving a clue that could inadvertently reveal the assassin. This is where your spymaster brain should peek out and whisper a cautious mantra: clarity is kindness, misdirection is drama.
- Practice with the house dynamics: The houses add a flavor layer that can influence clue choices. You might find yourself favoring speed and boldness as a Gryffindor, or cunning and plan-ahead play as a Slytherin. This is less about your actual house affiliation and more about the personality you want to embody for the round.
- Don’t underestimate simple, clear clues: A well-chosen single word that maps cleanly to two or three cards can crush a round. The time you spend chasing a perfect multilevel clue can be replaced with a concise cue that teammates can latch onto quickly.
