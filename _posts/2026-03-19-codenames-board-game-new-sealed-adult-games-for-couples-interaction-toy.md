## What is Codenames, Really?
Codenames isn’t a complicated machine; it’s a clever social puzzle wrapped in a 5x5 grid and a deck of deceptively innocent words. The basic premise: two teams, two spymasters, one grid of 25 cards. The spymaster gives a one-word clue followed by a number, telling their team how many words on the grid relate to that clue. The catch? Among the 25 words are the opposing team’s words, innocent bystanders, and—this is the tricky bit—the assassin. You want to avoid those last two because misplacing a clue could end your turn, give the other team a free point, or end the game if you reveal the assassin.

In a standard 15–word grid, players must connect abstract ideas to concrete words, often requiring leaps of logic, shared experiences, and a healthy tolerance for ridiculous associations. The thrill of the game comes from watching your teammate connect the dots in just the right way, or from witnessing a misread so spectacular that you both forget the original clue and start laughing about why the word “pasta” appears next to “umbrella.”

For our adult editions, the bones don’t get heavier, but the jokes can get sharper. ## Setup and Components
In Codenames, setup is almost too easy to ruin. Shuffle the 25 codename cards and lay them out in a 5x5 grid. Then assign one spymaster to each team; one person sits on the “-red” side, the other on the “blue” side. The remaining team chooses a starter and the associated color blocks as their team’s words to guess. The blue team’s spymaster and the red team’s spymaster each have a key card that marks which 9 words belong to their team, which words are neutral, which belong to the other team, and which card is the assassin. The other players must rely on the clues the spymasters give to identify their team’s words while avoiding pitfalls.

Tools included with the sealed copy:
- 200 or so small codename cards with one word each
- 25 grid cards (in some versions these might be separate or combined)
- An assassin and neutral symbols printed on some of the grid cards
- A rulebook that is almost a single-serving manual in the event of a power outage
- The starter card, which marks which color begins

The component quality matters in the sense that you want the words to be legible from across the table, not just when you lean in with the face 3 inches from the card. The cards should be sturdy enough to survive repeated shuffles, fan-out reveals, and those inevitable table crashes when a cat knocks the board onto the coffee table.

Pro tip: If you plan to play with a couple, consider a rotating guest rule that the “host” chooses the starting color each round to keep the game exciting, and to avoid any unfair blame games.

## How to Play (A Quick Refresher)
Codenames is not a long or complicated game to learn. The core loop is:

- Spymaster for each team sits on their own side with a secret key.
- The spymaster gives a one-word clue + a number.
- Team members discuss and select that many words on the grid.
- If a word belongs to their team, it’s covered with their color card; if it belongs to the opposing team, it’s covered with the other color; neutrals are neutral; the assassin ends the game in defeat.

In two-player couples-friendly play, you’ll find a rhythm: the spymaster tosses out a clue, the partner scours the grid looking for connections, and you celebrate or commiserate as the board reveals more red and blue cards. The pace tends to be quicker than a typical family game night, since the players are often more focused on the social cues and the banter rather than winning by any means necessary.

## Strategy Tips for Couples
Here are practical tips drawn from many nights of friends, partners, and mispronounced words:

- Start with safe, high-coverage clues. If you’re red team, aim for clues that clearly link two or more red words and avoid any clue that might refer to blue or assassin words.
- Use shared experiences as anchor points. If you both have a favorite vacation, a clue tied to that trip can unlock multiple words together.
- Mind the tempo. Don’t rush; take a beat to observe your partner’s reaction. A lot of the fun comes from reading facial expressions and the tiny tells that say, “That clue was on the mark,” or “No, that’s not the right direction.”
- Establish boundaries. If you want to keep things PG-13, agree on what kind of content is permissible as you set up the house rules.
- Practice makes better. The more you play, the more you train your brain to spot connections you wouldn’t think of spontaneously.
