## What’s in Exploding Kittens, and what’s in the Party Pack?

Before we get into the used-vs-new debate, let’s set expectations with a quick overview. The core Exploding Kittens game is a family-friendly, fast-turning card game for 2-5 players (some variants scale up with house rules). The objective is simple: avoid drawing an Exploding Kitten card while using a buffet of wacky action cards to outplay your friends. Cards like See the Future, Defuse, and Attack keep the game light, fast, and occasionally ridiculous. The humor is goofy, the artwork is intentionally over-the-top, and the tension builds in fits and starts until someone dramatically turns a desperate Defuse into a miracle save or, more often, into a comedic flop.

The Exploding Kittens Party Pack is designed as a party-friendly expansion or standalone expansion, depending on how you want to interpret the card zoo you’ve just opened. It typically includes a larger deck, more variety in action cards, and tweaks that accommodate bigger groups. If you’ve played the base game with a group of friends who love chaos, the Party Pack can feel like a radically upgraded version of that same chaotic energy—more players, more cards, more chances to pull a face-palm moment when a plan comes together and then promptly unravels.

In a high-level sense, the Party Pack doubles down on the core emotions of Exploding Kittens: surprise, suspense, and that sweet moment when someone says, “Hold my beer,” and you watch the table erupt in laughter and light chaos. The exact counts of new cards aren’t as important as the vibe: more room for mischief, more opportunities for wacky combos, and more ways to bluff your friends into thinking you’ve got a plan—then watch it implode in the same way a soufflé does when you sneezed near the kitchen.

## Used vs Brand New Sealed: What does that even mean here?

This is the moment where “used” and “brand new sealed” collide in a glorious paradox. If you’re buying a used-but-sealed Exploding Kittens box, you’re most likely dealing with a box that has not been open or played, but may have been stored in a location where the seal wasn’t airtight forever. The pros: you’re probably looking at a lower price, you might snag an item that’s out-of-print or hard to find, and there’s a certain romance to owning a nearly-new artifact that survived the ages of couch gaming. The cons: there’s a potential risk of seal integrity issues, possible missing-in-box items (cards can be miscounted), and the anxiety that comes with buying something that’s essentially a mystery wrapped in a trivia card about cats and ninjas.

Brand-new sealed, on the other hand, promises that you’re the first person on the planet to break that seal, breathe that cardboard life, and chuckle at the first “See the Future” you play with the sheer joy of a person who has never had to replace a missing card. Brands-new items often come with guaranteed seals, fresh card stock, and the comfort of knowing nothing in your set has seen the existential crisis of a party where a toddler decided to nap on a deck.

In practice, the decision to buy used brand-new sealed comes down to three things:

- Condition check: Is the outer seal intact? Are there any dents or moisture marks? If it’s sealed and pristine, you’re generally safe. If the seal shows signs of life (tiny rips, resealed edges, or suspicious tape), you should treat it as a potential red flag and inspect further.
- Completeness: Are all cards present? Are any card sleeves, dice (if included), or tokens missing? A sealed box gives you some confidence; a resealed or used box requires meticulous counting and possibly comparing with the official component list.
- Warranty and return policy: If you’re buying from a reputable seller, a return warranty can offset fears. If you’re buying from a marketplace without protection, your odds of a mystery box go up dramatically.

We’ll dive into a practical checklist in a later section, but for now, let me spare you from the drama: if the price is too-good-to-be-true and the seal looks suspiciously confident in its reseal job, it’s probably your cue to walk away and instead chase a verified new copy or a well-known seller with return protections.

## Strategies, house rules, and the art of keeping a straight face

No guide to Exploding Kittens would be complete without a few practical tips. This isn’t a protracted meta, just a few moves you can steal from your own table to keep the laughter rolling:

- Create a plausible “safe” plan, then pivot at the last second. People love the drama of watching a carefully laid plan collapse in a single draw.
- Use the See the Future cards to read the moment before you draw. If you sense a trap, you can soften the risk with a Defuse or a well-timed Nope.
- Consider a house rule: one reroll per game per player. It doesn’t break the game, but it creates dramatic rescue moments and fewer early endings.
- In a Party Pack game, vary the rotation to avoid predictable patterns. The more players you have, the more you’ll want to mix and match special cards to keep everyone guessing.

If you’re into deeper mechanics, there are fans who love to map out the probabilities of exploding draws, the odds of using Defuse at the right moment, and the most reliable Nope replies in different deck configurations. ## Condition checklist for a used box

To ensure you’re not walking into a consumer horror story, use this quick checklist before you finalize the purchase:

- Seal integrity: Is the shrink wrap intact? Any resealing evidence should be clearly disclosed by the seller.
- Box condition: Any crushing, moisture spots, or bent corners? A slightly bent corner is usually tolerable; a crushed corner might indicate rough handling.
- Card stock and finish: Blunt edges, warping, or obvious wear on the card surfaces? If it looks clean, you’re in the green zone; if not, reason for discount.
- Completeness: Does the listing mention all base components and any extra Party Pack additions? If you’re counting, do a quick physical tally once opened (if the seal is broken for inspection, you’re allowed to verify).
- Return policy: A solid seller should offer a reasonable return window if you discover missing cards or other issues after receiving the item.

For a used-but-sealed copy, you’re looking for signs of storage rather than used gameplay wear. If you want the most peace of mind, ask for a photo of the inside of the box flaps and a close-up of the card backs. If the seller hesitates or provides vague photos, that’s a red flag that you probably want to walk away and seek a trusted retailer instead.
