## What is the Citadel Medium Shade Brush, exactly?
You might be picturing a brush with a name that sounds like it belongs in a spellbook. And you’d be half-right. The Citadel Medium Shade Brush is designed to apply medium-density shading products—think washes and thinned-down inks—across the model’s surface with controlled, broader strokes rather than the tiny, surgical injections of a fine detail brush.

### Design and feel
The handle is comfortable in typical Citadel fashion: not a monument to ergonomics, but a sensible, balanced grip that doesn’t make your hand feel like it’s doing parkour in a snowstorm. The ferrule is snug, not wobbly, which matters when you’re dragging the brush across a model’s surface and the bristles want to spread unpredictably. The bristle bundle is larger than a fine detail brush but smaller than a big, fluffy painter’s brush, which is exactly what you want for shading work where you’re trying to maintain soft, feathered edges rather than a blunt, heavy-handed stroke.

### Bristles and responsiveness
The Medium Shade is typically built from synthetic fibers designed to hold a fair amount of wash without splaying too aggressively. The bristles have a springy tip that returns to its original shape after a stroke, which means you can cover a fair amount of surface without reloading constantly. The brush’s size lets you reach recessed areas or panel lines with confidence, and you can keep a consistent angle to avoid streaks—unless you’re going for deliberate streaks as a stylized effect, which—let’s be honest—can be a vibe if you’re painting something like a sci-fi tank with battle damage.

### Size and purpose (in a sentence)
If you’re shading large plates, cloak surfaces, and scenic bases, the Medium Shade balances coverage and precision. If you’re doing delicate feathering around a face or a nose highlight, you’ll probably switch to a finer brush. If you’re glazing or doing quick, uniform washes across a squad, this brush becomes your new best friend, the Gandalf of your painting toolkit—wise, a little mystical, and occasionally grumpy about your optimistic glaze ratios.

## How it fits into your painting workflow
Let’s map a typical shading workflow and see where the Medium Shade slips in with the grace of a well-timed power-up.

### Step 1: Primer and base coats
You’ll start with a clean model. The Medium Shade brush isn’t a base-coat beast; you’ll want something with absorbency and control for layering the paint. Base coats should be done with a proper brush (or airbrush, if that’s your vibe) and allowed to dry thoroughly. The shading brush will come into play after you’ve got your base layers down and you’re ready to add depth.

### Step 2: The shading phase
This is where the brush earns its stripes. You’ll load your Medium Shade with a thinned-down wash or ink. The goal is to create soft, controlled shadows in crevices, under capes, along armor recesses, or around musculature on fantasy beasts. A couple of key tricks:
- Load lightly: you want the brush to carry pigment, not be dripping with it. Too much paint means pooling in every little nook, which defeats the purpose of shading.
- Use angled strokes: tilt the brush to follow the panel lines and recesses. The medium-sized brush head helps you avoid painting the entire surface, which is exactly what you don’t want when you’re shading.
- Build in layers: shading is about gradient depth. Start darker where you need more depth, then move to lighter passes to blend the edge of the shade into the base color.

### Step 3: Blending and glazing
After laying down the shade, you’ll often want to blend the edge with the base layer. The Medium Shade’s bristle stiffness makes it feasible to do a soft feathered edge without risking a volcano-like blot of paint. If you’re feeling fancy, use a clean, slightly damp brush to gently feather the outer edge of the shaded area. For glazing, thin your glaze well and apply in thin, slow passes; the goal is a seamless transition rather than a chalky fade.

### Step 4: Touch-ups and cleanup
Shading can reveal where you rushed or where your brush control wandered into the wrong lane. If that happens, switch to a fine-detail brush for precise edge work or a small dry brush to lift excess pigment. Clean your Medium Shade immediately after use; don’t let washes dry in the bristles unless you’re into the crunchy-bristle aesthetic—the world already has enough crunchy things, and your brush doesn’t need to be one more.

## Practical tips and tricks from the bench
Here are some real-world tips that help you get the most from the Medium Shade brush, even if you’ve had a rough day at the hobby table:

- Tip 1: Pre-wet the bristles lightly. A damp brush holds more wash at surface tension, which reduces the risk of pooling in every crevice. Just don’t soak it like you’re about to baptize the miniature; you’re not doing a baptism, you’re reducing glare.
- Tip 2: Use a consistent angle. A slight tilt shows up as a soft line of shade along the edge of armor or cloak seams. If you’re just dragging the brush in straight lines, you’ll get flat shading that looks, frankly, lazy.
- Tip 3: Let the brush do the work. Don’t press hard. The point of shading is not brute force; it’s intelligent, gentle application. If you notice the brush skittering or leaving behind streaks, reload with less paint.
- Tip 4: Keep a little water on standby. A few drops can help you smooth the edge in wet-blending fashion. It’s not cheating; it’s technique.
- Tip 5: Test on a spare sprue or a test model. If you’re new to shading a particular color family, it helps to practice on a non-crucial piece before you ruin your prized display mini.

## Maintenance and care: keeping the Medium Shade happy
All tools deserve love, and brushes are no exception. A well-cared-for brush lasts longer and behaves more predictably:

- Clean promptly after use. Wash with lukewarm water and a gentle brush soap. Rinse until the water runs clear and there’s no soapy residue.
- Shape the bristles and dry properly. After washing, gently reshape the bristles and lay the brush flat to dry on a clean towel. Storing with the bristles facing down can cause warping over time.
- Don’t soak the ferrule. Water entering the metal ferrule invites rust and looseness in the bristles. It’s not a good look when you’re doing the final highlight and the brush goes wobbly.
- Rotate your brush stock. If you only ever use one brush, it’s bound to fail at the worst moment. Build a small rotation so you’re never left with a single point of failure.
