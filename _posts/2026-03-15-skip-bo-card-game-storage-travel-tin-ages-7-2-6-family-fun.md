## How Skip-Bo Works (A Friendly Refresher)
Skip-Bo is all about sequencing. The core mechanic: players build piles on the table in ascending order, using cards from their hand or from the top of their stock piles. A winner is the first player to empty their stock pile. The wild cards (Skip-Bo cards) can stand in for any number, which opens doors to dramatic comebacks and, occasionally, dramatic outbursts from the kid who didn’t get their turn fast enough.

Key elements: your hand management, the tempo of discards, and those tiny tactical choices that force you to run the math in real time. The Travel Tin doesn’t reinvent the wheel here; it just makes it easier to carry the wheel around and pretend you’re a portable wheel of gaming destiny.

### Setup and initial play
1) Each player is dealt a set number of face-down stock cards. 2) A few stock cards are turned face-up into a starter pile—this creates the initial tension because you can almost taste the possibility of a quick finish. 3) The top card of each stock pile is turned face-up to guide your early play. 4) Players draw back up to a hand size so you’re never staring at a blank hand while your opponent orchestrates a perfect run.

Pro-tip: keep the pace steady. The game rewards calm, deliberate plays as well as those moments of wild improvisation when you realize you can squeeze out a few extra cards by using a wild in a clever way. The Travel Tin’s compact design encourages a quick start, but it won’t force a breakneck pace unless you want it to.

## Strategy, Tactics, and House Rules
The beauty of Skip-Bo is that it teaches you to balance immediate gain with long-term planning. Here are a few tips tailored to the Travel Tin experience:

- Mind your hand: you’ll often be holding several playable cards and a few dead ends. Try to discard cards that unlock future turns, not just the ones that feel like a cheap win.
- Watch the wilds: Skip-Bo wilds are valuable, but remember they can be used strategically in multiple ways. Don’t burn them on a marginal move—save them for a critical moment when a single wild could seal a victory.
- Stock management matters: the fewer cards you have left in your stock pile, the more pressure you feel. Use that pressure to your advantage, but don’t misread the table and give away a win with a careless mistake.
- Tempo over brainburn: the Travel Tin’s design is perfect for quick rounds. If you’re tempted to out-think the table, you’ll slow the game and risk a bluff from younger players who start turning the table’s facial expressions into a strategic game of “aha, they’re overthinking it.”

If you want to spice things up, you can introduce light house rules like allowing two meets per round, or giving players a few extra draws when the stock pile is almost empty. These tweaks can breathe new life into the experience without turning the game into an existential crisis about turn order.
