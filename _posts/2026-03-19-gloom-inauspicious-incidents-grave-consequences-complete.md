Introduction: a game about tragedy, in the most cheerful way possible

Gloom is a game where you want your characters to suffer, and your opponents’ families to be cursed with even worse fates. The goal is to have the gloomiest, most misfortune-ridden family line by the end of the game, all while keeping your own heirs sunny and ignored. The mechanics are elegant: transparent cards, a touch of hand management, and a little storytelling that lets you weave soap-opera-level drama with a tilt of your head and a grin. The base game is tight; the expansions, Inauspicious Incidents and Grave Consequences, push the mood lighting up to full midnight glow. This post is the complete lantern-lit tour through the combined package, with jokes, tips, and a strategy map to help you navigate the fog of bad endings.

H2: What is Gloom, really?

Gloom is a card game from Keith Baker that uses transparent cards to depict families that accumulate negative circumstances like a backlog of unreturned library books. Each player gets a family, each member wearing a different misfortune, and the aim is to cause as much misery as possible to your own family while avoiding giving the other players the satisfaction of seeing their characters prosper. The catch? Your actions affect everyone at the table, and the darkest humor is what threads the core experience together. It’s a game about tragedy that doesn’t punish you for laughing at it.

H3: Core mechanics in a single paragraph

- Everyone gets a family and a set of negative scenarios. Your job is to manipulate cards so that your family ends up with the most gloom at the end, while ensuring others accrue less gloom than you. 
- Cards are transparent, physically stacked on the table so you can read everyone’s fate without peeking at their hand. It’s a gleefully theatrical mechanic that doubles as a practical method for players to monitor everyone’s doom in real time. 
- The events, like the font of misfortune itself, are presented with a gleeful punchline. The humor comes not from cruelty but from the exaggerated whimsy of calamity. It’s a comedy of manners, only the manners are morbid, and the jokes revolve around how badly your characters can react to their own mortal misfortunes.

H2: Inauspicious Incidents — what it adds to the table

Inauspicious Incidents is an expansion that broadens the scope of misfortune by injecting a suite of incident cards that alter how gloom is spread and managed. The incidents aren’t merely “more of the same”; they’re accelerants and curveballs that force players to reconsider how they shape their families’ sagas.

H3: What the Incidents card adds

- New incident types that trigger during play, changing the timing of gloom shifts. Some incidents cause glee to hinge on a specific suit of cards, turning the table into a tense dance where everyone tries to time their gloom precisely. 
- Thematically, these cards lean into the macabre yet lighthearted vibe that Gloom thrives on. They feel like a stage’s dramatic entrance: you don’t know what’s coming, but you know it’s going to be deliciously awful.
- Incidents also give players more agency to interrupt or accelerate the doom countdown, adding strategic depth without breaking the breezy, storytelling format the game is built on. You’re not just a number cruncher; you’re a master of melodrama.

H3: Design notes and how they influence play

From a design perspective, Incidents respect the established rhythm of Gloom while widening the space for improvisation. They reward both clever card placement and clever banter. The humor is more refined here—the expansions lean into the theatricality rather than the shock value, which makes for better party vibes and fewer moments where you’d rather not be at the table. The fate of your characters becomes a running joke that everyone contributes to, and that collaborative storytelling adds a lot to replays. 

H2: Grave Consequences — the cemetery of clever misery

Grave Consequences is the second expansion in the complete package and introduces a cemetery of new mechanics, a set of gloom-enriched death outcomes, and more ways to twist the knife with gleeful theatrics. If Inauspicious Incidents is about curveballs, Grave Consequences is about the long game: how do you plan for a late-game swing when the board is already a mosaic of misfortune?

H3: The bones of the Grave Consequences expansion

- An expanded deck of “grave” cards that alter endgame scoring and can dramatically shift who ends up with the most gloom. The grave cards aren’t just flavor; they’re weighted tools that require you to foresee, paradoxically enough, a future you might never reach because the current turn could end the story prematurely.
- New character types and new ways to apply misery to family members in ways that feel fresh, not recycled. The art remains a delight—a perfect balance of whimsical macabre with clean, legible icons that keep the table oriented under the glow of the dimmed lights.
- A few clever toggle rules (like optional endgame modifiers) that let you scale the tone of the game from lighthearted tragedy to operatic catastrophe. It’s like adding a tilt to the see-saw, but the see-saw is made of cards and the caboose is a punchline.

H3: Why Grave Consequences matters for strategy

Grave Consequences adds a new layer to risk management. You’ll learn to balance early misfortunes against late-game swings, because the endgame can be decided by the smallest misfortune that grows into a runaway score. It rewards players who can plan several turns ahead without tipping their own hands—an accomplishment, given the table’s predilection for dramatic reveals. If you like your strategic depth with a side of dark humor and a pinch of melodrama, Grave Consequences delivers with grace and warmth (well, as warm as gloomy can be).

H2: The Complete Experience — pairing both expansions with the base game

Putting base Gloom together with Inauspicious Incidents and Grave Consequences creates a richer, deeper tapestry of sorrow and laughter. The base rules remain incredibly approachable; the expansions simply add more levers to pull, more jokes to crack, and more dramatic reveals to stage. Player count and playtime stay friendly: typically 2–5 players with sessions ranging from 45 minutes to an hour depending on how freely you improvise the family saga. The complete package shines in that it increases variability—every game night can pivot on a different mood, unknown to the players at the start. 

H3: Setup and session flow with the complete set

- Setup remains delightfully quick. You shuffle the gloom decks, assign families, and set up the central fear bar that tracks who is undeniably getting the worst of it. 
- The Incidents deck introduces fresh chaos, so you’ll want to keep a mental note (and a bragging-right-to-remember-whose-misfortune-was-first). 
- Grave Consequences nudges you toward long-term storytelling by offering endgame complications that can swing the tally in surprising ways, which makes the final reveal feel earned rather than contrived. 

H2: Artwork, humor, and the Keith Baker voice

Keith Baker’s writing for Gloom has always been the backbone of its charm. The misfortune cards are not just cruel; they’re witty, punchy, and often gleefully absurd. The humor lands because it’s anchored in character-driven storytelling rather than random puns. The expansions retain this voice while adding new metaphors for despair that still feel light on their feet. The art supports the mood without overshadowing the gameplay, which is key to a game that relies on reading a table’s energy as much as reading any card. The tone remains consistent across the base game and both expansions, preserving the sense that you’re watching a tiny, melodramatic opera with a table full of dramatic readers.

H2: Accessibility and replayability

Gloom already has high accessibility: rules are simple, the math is light, and you can laugh your way through any misfortune. The two expansions keep that accessibility intact while improving replayability through more variants and endgame possibilities. There are moments when a player reaches for the top card only to realize the next card could flip the entire table’s mood, and that moment of shared suspense is where Gloom really wins. If you’re worried about complexity, the cost is minimal: you gain more flexible endings and new funny outcomes without turning the game into a math lecture.
