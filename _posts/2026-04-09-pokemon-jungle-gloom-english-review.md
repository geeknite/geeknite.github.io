## Overview: The card in the Jungle era
The Jungle set, released after the original Base set, was known for its bright borders, chunky logos, and a sense that the game was still discovering what it could be in the age of dial-up internet and chunky card stock. Gloom, the infamously fragrant Grass-type, snuck into the jungle with a design that’s as meme-worthy as it is functional. The English edition No. 37/64 marks a moment when card art quality started leaning toward more dynamic scenes and less static portraits. The card is a classic case study in how 1990s Pokémon card design balanced character art with readable statistics on a compact card.

### Artwork and aesthetics: a perfumed postcard from the 90s
The Gloom artwork in Jungle is a splashy, almost cartoonish take on the Pokémon’s signature aura. The card shows a grumpy little flower Pokemon in a dimly lit background with purple and pink tints that feel like the sort of poster you’d hang in a gamer den in the late 1990s. The English print preserves the soft shading, but the border styling is a bit more aggressive, a side-effect of the era’s fascination with “big color” on a small card. If you’ve collected earlier sets, you’ll notice the Gloom art in Jungle is less dramatic than later evolutions, but it exudes a certain “pink lemonade in a Pokéball” charm that is hard to resist. The surface texture on the English print is typical of the time: slightly glossy with a pale refusal to glare in bright light, which makes it easier to scan and photograph when you’re compiling a digital archive.

### Card text and abilities: the move-set romance with semantics
Gloom’s text in Jungle era cards typically reflects a low-power, utility-first approach. The English No. 37/64 Gloom carries a couple of attacks that require energy to power, with effects that feel like they come from a simpler era of the game. You’ll notice the flavor text in smaller print, a little hint about poison or stench mechanics that players later realized were better documented in the card’s later printings. For new collectors, the takeaway is that Jungle Gloom is more about the historical vibe and the art than about winning tournaments with a single spiky play. If you’re trying to build a deck around Gloom, you’ll be limiting yourself, but the card shines as a nostalgia piece and as a conversation starter in a binder.

#### Rating the text: clarity, flavor, and the era’s constraints
- Clarity: The text is readable at a glance; not every word is essential to modern play, but the flavor adds to the card’s personality.
- Flavor: High. The Gloom vibe – a little bitter, a little purple – is captured with a consistent art direction that resonates with fans who grew up with the Jungle set.
- Utility: Moderate for casual play, low for serious competitive decks by today’s standards.

### Collectibility: rarity, print quality, and market vibes
In Jungle, card rarity is a big driver of value. The No. 37/64 Gloom sits somewhere in the middle of the pack regarding scarcity, but condition matters more than a single print line does. The English version has that universal nostalgia factor; it’s the kind of card people pull out at card shows to spark conversations about the late-90s design choices. A clean, well-centered copy with sharp corners and minimal whitening will fetch a premium in the right market, while off-center prints with edge wear will settle into a reasonable price bracket for casual collectors. If you have a sealed Jungle booster box, you might not want to dive into the price ladder for a single copy, but you’ll likely want it for your mental scrapbook of the era.

### Grading, handling, and preservation: the real work of an old card
A Jungle Gloom in English is typically a mid-90s print with cardstock that remains surprisingly resilient if you store it properly. The border may be a tad prone to whitening if the card has seen a lot of sunlight, and the surface can acquire micro-scratches from years in a binder. The best practice is to sleeve the card in a high-clarity, acid-free sleeve, put a top-loader for rigidity, and store it in a climate-controlled environment. If you’re into grading, expect that the card’s edges will tell a story about a decade of adoption: a little whitening on the corners, a small print line, and perhaps a faint gloss irregularity from years in a binder. None of this is a deal-breaker for the right collector, but it does affect value.

### Photography & scanning tips for the modern collector
- Lighting: Use diffuse lighting to avoid hotspots that wash out the art. The Jungle Gloom’s purple hues can turn greyish in harsh light.
- Angles: A 45-degree angle photograph captures the card’s surface texture better than a straight-on shot. You want to reveal the subtle gloss and any whitening on the corners.
- Macro: Get up close on the card edge to highlight centering and edge wear; this is where the real tells come from for grading.
- Scanning: If you scan, do it at 300–600 dpi and save in a lossless format to preserve the color of Jungles pink-lavender palette.

## Gameplay context: how Jungle Gloom fits into the era’s meta
While this card shines as a collector’s piece and a nostalgia trigger, the strategic value in modern deck-building is, frankly, limited. The Jungle era was a time of experimental card design; there were fewer consistency fixes, and some mechanics that later became central were still in flux. Gloom’s role in a deck was often as a placeholder or as a part of a larger strategy that grouped Grass-type Pokemon with basic support cards. If you’re building a themed nostalgia deck for a local tournament, you might consider pairing Gloom with stronger evolutions in the same line, or simply using it as a centerpiece for a binder display and a conversation piece about early Pokemon Trading Card Game history.

### Notable prints and how colors tell a story
Different print runs of English Jungle Gloom may show subtle color variation due to printing batches. The lighting on the card, the saturation of the purple aura, and the exact shade of the border can shift from one copy to another. Collectors often prefer “purer” color representation, which means you want a copy that sits well in photos: not too pink, not too washed out. This is the kind of variation that makes multi-copy collectors chase a near-mint perfect copy across years, and it’s also the reason you’ll see people debate the “best-looking” Jungle Gloom.

## Language editions: English vs Japanese vs reprints
Gloom is a fan favorite across languages, but the English Jungle print (No. 37/64) offers a distinct flavor compared to its Japanese counterpart and any later reprints. The Japanese prints sometimes feature alternate card border art or subtle art crop differences; the English prints preserve a more faithful reproduction of the Jungle era’s design language. If you’re building a cross-language collection, you’ll want to factor in print variants: Japanese versions may be more scarce in certain markets, and reprints with revised text may differ in subtle ways. For many collectors, the English Jungle Gloom is a gateway card that opens up an entire era of chocolate-colored sleeves and neon border art.

## Packaging, pricing, and market vibes
The Jungle era is a darling of nostalgia markets. Grading woes aside, a clean No. 37/64 Gloom will fetch a fair price among players and collectors. The price will be influenced by whether you have a standard edition versus a foil or holo variant; Jungle itself had a selection of holofoil versions that rose in price as collectors chased the most dramatic visuals. If you’re new to collecting, a practical approach is to treat the English Jungle Gloom as a “value nostalgia card” that’s worth its weight in good conversation and binder value rather than a guaranteed investment. Always check for centering and edge wear; in many cases, the eye-catching art and the card’s iconic pose will do far more to impress your friends than its actual battle prowess.

## Care for your Jungle Gloom: practical tips
- Store away from direct sunlight; it will turn the color sappier than a grape soda if left in a window for weeks.
- Use soft, buffer-friendly sleeves; avoid multi-layer thick sleeves that might cause glare in photos.
- Keep binder pages away from moisture; Jungle cards are surprisingly robust, but the worst enemy is humidity and the wrong binder.
- Consider a UV-resistant display case if you’re showing your collection off at meetups.
