## How to play: quick start guide

### The core loop

If you already know UNO, you’re halfway there. The goal remains simple: be the first to get rid of all your cards. On your turn, you match either the number or the colour of the card in the discard pile, or you play a special card from your hand that declares a new rule. The twist in UNO Flip is that some action cards affect both sides of the deck, and flip cards swap the entire game to the dark side. This means you need to adapt your strategy on the fly, reading not only the cards left in your hand but also what the visible cards on the table signal about the next flip.

During the light-side phase, you’ll encounter familiar foes such as Skip, Reverse, Draw Two, and Wild cards. When the light side flips to dark, those same action concepts apply but with new penalties and new strategic opportunities—like a sudden rainstorm of Draw Five that nobody asked for. It’s chaotic, it’s loud, and it’s surprisingly good for developing quick math and memory—skills that are suddenly more valuable when someone’s about to threaten you with a late-night UNO that you’ll regret for weeks.

### Turn structure and the Flip mechanic

A typical turn involves drawing if you cannot match, or playing a card to reduce your hand. The Flip card is the meteor that redefines the entire gameplay. Play a Flip card, or draw a Flip in response, and the deck flips from light to dark, and with it the available action cards may switch around. This means you must constantly reassess your position: who is close to going out, who has the power to change colour on the next move, and who is about to unleash a cascade of consequences that would make a soap opera blush.

The Flip mechanic is what elevates UNO Flip above standard UNO. It’s not just a gimmick; it changes the risk calculus. Do you hold onto a Wild card to pivot after a flip, or do you unload it early to deny a strategic move to your rival? The decision matters, and the game rewards players who can read the table’s mood as the cards morph from bright and friendly to moody and dramatic.

### A sample turn, to avoid the rules-lawyering paralysis

On a typical light-side turn, you draw if you can’t match, then you try to dump a few cards with a well-timed combination. Suppose you’re sitting on a Wild and a handful of blues. The table’s low on blues, and you’re watching the next flip closely. You might hold the Wild for a critical moment when the deck flips; or you might play it now to lock in a new colour and force the next player to deal with a tougher choice. If you notice the person to your left is blocked by a clump of red cards, you might steer the table toward red on the next couple of turns—just enough to flip the swing of power before your own hand empties. The actual decisions are wonderfully human: bluff, memory, and a little showmanship all collide in a way that keeps the game light but surprisingly tactical.
