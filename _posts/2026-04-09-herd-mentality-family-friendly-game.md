## What is Herd Mentality?
Herd Mentality is a party-style game designed around a simple premise: players answer prompts with what they think the rest of the table will say. The catch is that you don't answer for yourself; you answer for the group. The person who best predicts the majority answer earns points. The twist? If your guess matches the most popular answer, you score; if not, you miss out, and the real crowd is the judge. It’s a friendly clash of wates of thought that becomes a mirror of your group's collective quirks.

This is a game about social perception rather than pure logic or trivia. It rewards empathy and a dash of mischief. Children can enjoy it by aligning with their friends and adults can enjoy pondering the social dynamics of the family unit. The humor often comes from the ridiculousness of the answers and the way siblings, cousins, and parents converge or diverge in their predictions. You’ll find yourself saying, often with a grin, “Of course we all pick the same thing—how else would the herd behave?”

For fans of party games, Herd Mentality slots neatly between the quick-fire energy of phrases games and the more introspective vibe of social deduction. It scratches that itch for rapid-fire social interaction while staying accessible to players who might not love heavy strategy or long chalkboard explanations. It is a communal experience that invites everyone to contribute, fail, and laugh together.

## How to Play: Round Structure and Flow
Herd Mentality unfolds in rounds, each with a simple rhythm that you will recognize almost instantly if you have played party games before. Here is a no-nonsense walkthrough suitable for hosting a family night:

### Setup
- Shuffle the prompt deck and place it within easy reach of the group.
- Decide who starts as the first predictor. The starting player is often the most willing to lead the chaotic chorus, but you can rotate roles to keep it fair.
- Each round requires a predictor and the rest of the players who will propose what they think the majority would answer. The number of players affects the dynamics; more players usually means a richer palette of predictions.
- Hand out scoring markers or tokens. Set a target score if you want a little longer drama or a simple, low-stress race to the end.

### The Guessing Mechanic
- The predictor looks at the prompt and considers how the herd would respond.
- Each non-predictor writes or says their guess for what the majority would say.
- Once all responses are revealed, the group votes on which answer they think is the actual majority. Sometimes the real majority is obvious; sometimes there are multiple near-equal contenders. The tension is where the comedy happens.
- Points are awarded to players whose guesses match the real majority. The predictor earns points when their prediction lands on the actual herd consensus, creating a meta-game of prediction against prediction.

### Scoring and Ending
- The scoring system is simple: predict well, and you climb the leaderboard. If your answer matches the majority, you gain points; if not, your points stay where they are.
- The game typically ends when a player reaches a set number of points or after a pre-determined number of rounds. The winner is the person with the highest score, but in practice the real prize is the memories of the goofy responses and the shared laughter.

For players who want a technical headline: the scoring cadence encourages dynamic participation without requiring a mathematician's brain. The churn of predictions and the ensuing debates are the real reward. The fastest rounds feel like a sitcom episode's cold open, the longer ones feel like you’ve accidentally wandered into a family council about the best ice-cream flavor.

## Round Flow: A Quick Example
Let us paint a quick mental picture so you can imagine the flow without needing to memorize a dozen specifics.

- Prompt: “What would grandma say if you asked her to choose a pet?”
- Predictor’s guess: the cluster around “a calm cat with a good nap schedule.”
- Other players write their guesses: “a hammy parrot,” “a dignified old tortoise,” “a dog who fetches the newspaper.”
- The majority answer is revealed. It’s often the oddball choice that gets the room roaring, but every now and then grandma surprises everyone by picking the most sensible option. The key is not the choice itself but the shared reasoning that led to the guess.
- Points are awarded to those whose guesses align with the actual majority, which might be a unanimous mild response or a surprising counterpoint. The happiness comes from seeing whether your tribe converges or diverges.

## Hosting Tips: Running a Smooth Family Night
- Keep rounds short: the bounce-back energy is higher when rounds are tight. A timer can be a hero here. If you’re using a rough-and-ready time limit, 60 seconds per round tends to feel just right.
- Rotate the predictor role to give everyone a shot at leadership. This keeps the energy fresh and prevents one person from monopolizing the fun.
- Embrace the chaos with a gentle hosting style. If someone’s guess spirals into a ridiculous tangent, celebrate it. The laughter is the point, not the perfection of the answer.
- Use a simple scoring track so kids can participate without needing to do arithmetic on the fly. A whiteboard or sticky-note board is perfect for visualizing the herd’s trajectory.
- Leverage a light pre-round ritual: ask a quick, silly question before the prompt to get people warmed up and engaged—like what fictional character would be the best babysitter.

## Variants and House Rules
Geeknite audiences appreciate a bit of tweakability, and Herd Mentality provides graceful space for house rules without breaking the core mechanic. Here are a few friendly variants you can try:
- Quick-Draw Variant: Each round, players must present their guess in a single sentence. This forces concise predictions and adds a pinch of improv.
- Collaboration Night: Teams of two predict together, then you compare the team’s prediction to the group majority. This works great for younger players who want to team up with a parent or older sibling.
- Memory Lane: Add a twist by storing a few rounds for a post-game discussion about which predictions were the most on-point and why. It’s a light way to build social reflection into a kid-friendly format.

To keep things fresh, you can rotate prompts with your own family branding. A few custom prompts about family routines, favorite snacks, or fictional universes your group loves can be a blast and a nice nod to your shared microculture.
