## Materials, Craft, and the “Raw Wool” Advantage
### The Wool and the Way It Moves
The core of this piece is raw wool—a fiber that hasn’t been dyed to death and then re-dyed with a kaleidoscope of chemical processes. Raw wool carries its own natural palette: creams, soft whites, and the occasional tiny fleck that looks like a mini galaxy. The term “raw” here is a badge of honor: it signals a lack of heavy processing, which many of us secretly crave when we’re scrubbing and vacuuming tracks of glitter that managed to migrate into every corner of life.

For those who love the texture of natural fibers, raw wool is—pun intended—a warm hug. It’s resilient, naturally flame-retardant to a degree, and surprisingly forgiving of everyday spills (blot, don’t rub; treat like a friendly eco-spill). The long-time wearer in your home, your cat, your dog, or the neighbor’s aerodynamic potted plant will appreciate that the wool fibers stand up to traffic. We’re not saying this rug can survive a superhero landing, but it’s ready for the daily marathon without flinching.

### Hand-Woven: The Human Element
A hand-woven rug implies a looped, patient process, often built on a loom that’s part ancient craft, part modern zen. The artisan’s rhythm—over, under, over, under—creates a tapestry where each knot is a tiny timestamp of a maker’s day. The geometry is not a machine-made exactness; it’s a living, breathing line that sometimes wobbles minutely, which is exactly why it feels alive rather than clinical.

The bohemian influence means there’s room for minor asymmetries. If you’re one of those people who counts threads as therapy, this rug will be a majestic instructions-in-bedtime-story for your brain. If you care about sustainability, know that hand-woven pieces like this often come with fair-trade or cooperative production vibes that support local artisans and rural economies.

### Construction and Longevity
Geometric patterns rely on precision. In a hand-woven piece, that precision is achieved by skill and patience. The weave creates a sturdy foundation that tends to resist crushing in high-traffic zones. It’s not a shag load of fluff—this rug’s pile is balanced to maintain legibility of the design while still offering the beloved underfoot hug. The wool fiber tends to recover well after compression, which helps when you drag in the stroller, the vacuum, or that suspiciously heavy laundry bag labeled “for science.”

The combination of ivory-grey coloring and a geometric motif makes maintenance less frantic. You don’t need to fear coffee spills or mud prints as much as you would with a high-contrast or solid color rug. Still, a little care goes a long way: quick blotting, turning the rug occasionally to even out wear, and a respectful distance from direct sun if possible. Wool loves sun less than your house plants do, but it will fade gracefully if you push it into the sun’s path for years.

## Care, Maintenance, and Longevity: Keeping It Looking Sharp
### Everyday Upkeep
- Vacuum with a gentle nozzle on low suction to avoid pulling on the fibers.
- Rotate the rug every 3-6 months to promote even wear, especially in high-traffic zones.
- Blot spills immediately with a clean, dry cloth; avoid rubbing, which can push liquid deeper into the pile.
- For durable cleaning, use a mild wool-safe cleaner following the manufacturer’s instructions. Always test on a small, inconspicuous area first.

### Sunlight, Pilling, and Wool Quirks
Natural wool wrinkles under heavy sunlight if exposed for long periods. If your space is sun-drenched, consider rotating the rug to minimize uneven fading. Wool can pill with heavy use, but regular grooming with a soft-bristle brush will keep the surface fuzzy-free and looking brand-new longer. The texture you enjoy—the tactile loops and the little texture variations—are part of the charm, not a defect.
