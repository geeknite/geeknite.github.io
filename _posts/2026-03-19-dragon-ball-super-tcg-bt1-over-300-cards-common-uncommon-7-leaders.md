## Common vs Uncommon in BT1: a dance of accessibility and depth

Common cards are the workhorses of any set. They’re the bread, the breadsticks, and the occasional garlic knot. They form the backbone of your early rounds, provide the basic resources, and create the scaffolding that lets your mid-game plan take shape. Common cards in BT1 tend to be straightforward in terms of cost curves and timing windows. They’re the reliable base your deck rests on after a grueling weekend of card-sorting. They won’t usually single-handedly win you a game, but they’ll help you get there with fewer misplays.

Uncommon cards are the spice rack. They introduce new effects that can drastically shift the math of the match. The uncommons in BT1 add more robust activation windows, better multi-card synergies, and sometimes a subtle tempo swing that can snowball into a win when used at the right moment. The key with these cards is learning to recognize the micro-interactions that turn a two-card play into a turn-3 or turn-4 advantage.

## Card design and mechanical vibes: what BT1 actually feels like at the table

Design-wise, BT1 balances the familiar with the novel. You’ll recognize baseline resource mechanics that trigger consistent lines of play; you’ll also encounter new timing windows and interaction rules that reward players who pay attention to the small print. The common cards give you dependable outcomes: you know what you’re getting most of the time. The uncommons, by contrast, often require you to think about two or three steps ahead, which is exactly what makes a casual match feel like a well-planned heist movie rather than a random brawl in a coffee shop.

One of the more interesting aspects of BT1 is how it handles tempo. The better uncommons not only push for a favorable exchange on the current turn but also set up future turns that can catch your opponent off guard. If you’re a fan of chess with a sprinkle of sci fi, you’ll appreciate these small but meaningful two-step advantages that accumulate over a couple of turns.

Another notable design decision is the balance between removal and protection. In BT1, removal is present and potent, but so is the ability to create sticky boards and multi-turn threats. The seven leaders each have a slightly different approach to pressure, but the common thread is that the set rewards players who think in terms of boards and sequences rather than simply counting the biggest one-turn payoff.
