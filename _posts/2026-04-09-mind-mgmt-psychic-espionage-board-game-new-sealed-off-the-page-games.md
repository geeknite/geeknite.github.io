## How the game actually works
### Core mechanics
Mind MGMT is a game of hidden roles with a twist: you will be secretly trying to decipher who is aligned with the Mind MGMT program and who is not. There are two kinds of players in this game: the sleepers and the agents. The sleepers know almost nothing about the true state of affairs, while the agents have special information but must be careful about revealing too much. The mix is like a spy thriller where half the cast is reading from a different script than the other half.

The action cards are where the magic happens. Each turn you will choose to perform actions such as gathering intel, challenging a decision, or attempting to manipulate the storyline to your advantage. The twist here is that you can be manipulated by the person you trust most, which is an innate recipe for paranoia. And paranoia, as we all know, is a great seasoning for a party game.

### Game flow
- Phase 1: Intelligence gathering
- Phase 2: Influence and misdirection
- Phase 3: Resolution and suspicion check

The flow is not strictly linear; players can react to reveals and adapt. This dynamic keeps everyone involved, and prevents the game from turning into a bureaucratic meltdown where someone nags about rulebooks.

### Social deduction with a brain burn
Mind MGMT is less about who is green or red and more about how you present yourself under pressure. It asks you to make a series of cunning calls about who to trust, who to back, and how far you can push to produce a favorable outcome for your side. You will be testing your poker face, your deduction instincts, and your willingness to risk a strategic misstep that could blow the whole operation.

## How to optimize a first game night
If you want your first session to hit that sweet spot where tension and humor coexist, consider these tips:
- Gather a group that enjoys talking and listening as much as they enjoy playing and bluffing
- Set aside a quiet space where you can discuss strategies without shouting over a TV show
- Use a timer to keep rounds moving but leave space for a big reveal when someone finally calls a bluff
- Don’t overanalyze every move in the first game; let the narrative breathe and let players grow into their roles

## House rules and optional twists
If you want to experiment with your group, there are a few fun additions that can lift the experience:
- Introduce a short timer for each discussion round to keep things brisk
- Add a rule where one player can issue a temporary false identity to another player once per game
- Allow players to swap one card with a neighbor to simulate slipping information through the network

These twists give you the chance to tailor the vibe and tempo to your table, whether you prefer a fast-paced cat and mouse chase or a long, slow burn of suspicion.
