## The 15 Walkers: A Gallery
Here we go—15 unique faces of doom for your gaming table. For each walker, we include a quick note on paint approach and tabletop role. The goal is to give you enough detail to paint confidently while keeping the narration entertaining.

### Walker 1: The Grinless Grunter
This is a squat, rotting figure with a big jaw and a grin of teeth. The silhouette reads easily even in low light. When painting, start with a dark base and build up to bone highlights. A quick wash brings out the teeth and the hollow eyes. If you want extra character, add ragged cloth bits in muted color so the model looks like a scavenger who has survived the first two nights but not the third.

### Walker 2: The Blood-Tide Wanderer
A taller model with more sinew showing through decayed skin. The color palette leans toward dark reds and purples. A careful red wash on the exposed muscle areas makes the model pop on the table. The key trick here is to avoid over-saturating the red so you keep a gritty, realistic feel rather than carnival-night gore.

### Walker 3: The Drooling Stalker
This one has a long arm and a hunched posture. The paint plan emphasizes texture on the skin with stippling and a subtle blue-gray wash to suggest cold corruption. Consider a gloss treatment on the saliva edges if you want a slightly sleazy, glistening effect under the table lights.

### Walker 4: The Amber Mire
A walker with swampy tones and a greenish tint to the skin. Use a blue-green glaze on the deeper recesses to evoke a damp, swampy feel. Dry brushing with a pale yellow-bone color will highlight the bone structure beneath that swampy skin.

### Walker 5: The Ashen Biter
A pale, almost corpse-white look with darkly shaded eyes and a slightly parted jaw. A bone-done palette with a dark wash creates a stark, haunting gaze. If you struggle with achieving true pale tones, try layering thin off-white with a touch of blue-gray for the base coat before the final highlight.

### Walker 6: The Mire Stalker
A walker with a camouflaged, swampy freckling of browns and greens. This one benefits from a stippled base that makes the skin texture read as spoiled earth rather than flat color.

### Walker 7: The Cracked Grasp
A medium build with revealed bone plates along the forearms. When painting, push contrast on the bone to make the armor-like plates pop against rotting flesh.

### Walker 8: The Soot Runner
This figure has a soot-black coating that reads almost like burnt skin. A wet earth wash over the black base can reveal softened edges and keep the miniature from looking flat. Consider a rust pigment on the exposed metal bits if your Abominator’s smoke trails are part of your table legend.

### Walker 9: The Frostbound Cremator
A chilly palette with blue-gray skin and a chilly white highlight. A touch of pale blue glaze on the extremities gives a frostbite impression without losing the undead feel. You can add a frosted snow effect to the base to tie this one into winter campaigns.

### Walker 10: The Rusted Sentinel
An almost steampunk zombie with alloy-like ribs. A metallic wash on the bones followed by a rust wash on the armor plates creates a nice contrast that catches the eye during combat.

### Walker 11: The Vein-Night Lurker
A slim but dangerous silhouette with exposed veins. The highlight should be on the raised tissue to emphasize the parasite-like look. A pinkish wash along the veins provides a pop against the gray skin.

### Walker 12: The Gutted Lookout
A taller, gaunt figure with extra rib cage textures that read well from across the board. The goal is to show menace with negative space—let the dark recesses do the talking.

### Walker 13: The Rot-Eyed Watcher
The eyes are the star here. A bright white or pale blue eye with a black pupil reads clearly and gives a sense of watching and waiting.

### Walker 14: The Bleached Howl
A pale, almost bone-white body with a muted brown tinge to the hair. The contrast helps the model stand out in a swarm rather than disappear into a gray blur.

### Walker 15: The Silent Shambler
The final piece is all about posture. A low, slow lumbering stance captures the sense of a zombie that moves with deliberate, creeping menace. A quick glaze on the joints will help the muscles and tendons read as rotted but alive.

## Painting and Assembly Tips
If you are assembling for the first time or just re-basing an existing army, here are practical tips that actually help.

- Cleaning and trimming: Use a hobby knife to remove the mold lines. Do not rush here; the better your prep, the crisper the final paint job.
- Prime: A standard gray or black primer works great. If you plan to field bright color schemes, a gray primer will help the colors pop without looking chalky.
- Base coats: Layer on midtones to give depth, but keep the shadows. The teeth and exposed bone areas benefit from a slightly warmer base color to accentuate texture.
- Washes and glazes: A dark wash (sepia or brown) on the recesses enhances the undead look. Use glazes sparingly on the skin to avoid pooling in the wrong places.
- Highlights: For Walkers, edge highlighting with bone or pale gray can dramatically lift the model. For the Abominator, concentrate on the armor plates and the ridges for a more intimidating silhouette.
- Basing: Gravel and tufts work well for Walkers; plaster rocks and grit can be used for terrain-ready basing. A little soil pigment around the feet helps sell the idea of a battlefield where these walkers were spawned.
- Sealing: A satin or matte finish preserves the grim aesthetics. A light gloss on the eyes can make the gaze feel alive in a creepy way while keeping the overall look grounded.

Storage and transport: The included bases are sturdy, but if you travel to game nights, consider a shallow, snug foam insert to prevent movement. A little extra padding goes a long way to keeping the Abominator from leaning and the Walkers from becoming a tangled mob in transit.
