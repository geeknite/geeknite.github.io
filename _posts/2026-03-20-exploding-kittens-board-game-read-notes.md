## Rules Primer: The Core Loop with Read Notes Flair
If you already know the card game, you will recognize the core loop: you draw cards, you hold onto action cards, and you avoid triggering the exploding kitten. In the board game, the board adds a layer of spatial tension, but the backbone remains the same: manage risk, trade shots, and lean into the humor when you survive a near-miss by inches. The Read Notes lens asks: what exactly do you need to know to start playing without needing a PhD in kitten kabooms?

### Turn structure in plain terms
- Each player starts with a hand of cards and a pawn on the board. The objective is to navigate the board, use action cards to manipulate others, and avoid drawing the literal exploding kitten card.
- On a turn, a player can either move their pawn on the board or play an action card before or after moving, depending on card type. Movement is more about risk management than racing to a finish line; the board includes spaces that intensify the danger or grant you tiny rewards.
- After you act, you draw a card from the draw deck, which reintroduces risk into the mix. The tension escalates as the deck shifts, and you find yourself weighing a cowardly draw against a bold move.

### Card types you will actually use
- Attack and Skip: You can interrupt someone else’s potential run or skip your own turn to disrupt the flow of the round.
- See the Future: A quick peek at upcoming draws helps you decide if you should push your luck or retreat to safety.
- Favor and Peace: Cards that barter small favors or temporarily reduce damage show the social negotiation aspect that keeps the table lively.
- Explode and Defuse: The iconic kit kat meets a grim fate, and your defusal tools typically involve other cards that let you dodge the worst outcomes.

## Reading the Rule Book: Common Pitfalls and Best Practices
- Pitfall: Misreading the timing on action cards. The cure is simple: pause for one breath and confirm whether the effect occurs before or after the draw. The pain of mis-timed actions can haunt a table for the rest of the night, so clarifying early saves you from a cascade of misplays.
- Best practice: Agree before you start that you will read card names aloud in character. This is not a requirement, but it creates a shared sense of theater and reduces arguments about card interpretation.
- Pitfall: Underestimating the length of a session with a full table. It can stretch longer than expected, especially if you are enjoying the banter. The cure is to set expectations before you begin or to schedule a time-box for the session to prevent it from mutating into a marathon.
