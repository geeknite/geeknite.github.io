## Brewing 101: The Ritual of Getting It Right

There’s a science, a little bit of alchemy, and a dash of theater to brewing Meadow Green properly. Here are the baseline guidelines, followed by a few nerdy extensions for the aspiring tea architect.

- Water quality: Soft water is kind to green tea. If your tap tastes like a rock candy shop, you’re in luck because you’ll be able to taste the minerals deciding to heckle the leaves. If your water is hard, consider using filtered or spring water.
- Water temperature: Green tea loves not-quite-boiling water. Think around 75–85°C (167–185°F). If you overshoot, you’ll drag astringency into your cup that might crash the party.
- Steep time: Common ranges are 1–3 minutes. Shorter times emphasize freshness; longer times extract more body but risk bitterness. For Meadow Green, start at 2 minutes and adjust by 15-second increments until your palate reports victory.
- Tea quantity: A teaspoon per 8 ounces is a friendly default. If you’re a scale person, aim for about 2–3 grams per 250 ml. This keeps you from brewing a cup that could double as a lab sample.
- Multiple infusions: Green teas often blossom in subsequent infusions. The second or third steep can reveal hidden citrus or vegetal notes that didn’t show up on the first pass. A true Meadow Green enjoys a tiny afterlife in the teacup.

There are many breathless debates about cup size, infusion times, and vessel material. Stainless steel retains heat, porcelain preserves aroma, and glass lets you admire the color. If you’re seeking a pure, unadulterated meadow experience, go with a clear glass teapot to watch the emerald steam do its origami routine before your eyes.

## Brewing Tech: Multiple Infusions and Flavor Maps

One of the joys of Meadow Green is how it responds to multiple infusions. Your first steep may deliver the clean, bright voice you expect, while subsequent steepings reveal hidden layers—sometimes a whisper of orchid, sometimes a citrus chord that wasn’t present at first sip. Here are a few strategies:

- Short, repeated infusions: 1–1.5 minutes per infusion during the first two pours can yield a chorus of notes that evolve with each cup.
- Temperature hacking: If your first batch tastes flat, raise the temperature slightly for the next infusion (within the 75–85°C window). If it tastes bitter, lower the temperature and shorten the steep time.
- Vessel matters: A glass teapot makes it easier to time the infusions visually, while a Yixing clay pot can mellow the tea and develop a slightly earthier complexity with repeated use.

Document your results as if you’re running a small, caffeinated experiment. You’ll notice patterns—notes that appear in certain leaf lots, or the way certain harvest years respond to a gentle steep. Welcome to your own flavor science lab, with fewer beakers and more teacups.

## Storage: Keeping the Meadow Fresh

Meadow Green Tea is best stored in a cool, dry place away from light. Oxygen and light are the thieves of flavor, so keep your tins sealed and in a cabinet that doesn’t double as a sunlit stage. If you buy in bulk, consider transferring the leaves to an airtight container that blocks light—brown glass jars or opaque tins are ideal. Avoid strong-smelling spices nearby; green tea can pick up neighbor aromas like a curious tech geek at a hackathon.
