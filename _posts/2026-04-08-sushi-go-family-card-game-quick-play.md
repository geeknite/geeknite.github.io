## How to play in under 20 minutes: a quick-glance guide
### The setup
- Shuffle the deck and deal each player a hand. The exact number depends on player count, but don’t overthink it—this game lives on speed.
- Place the draw pile in the center and prepare for a passing dance that will feel like a musical chairs version of a sushi bar.
- Decide on a starting player; the rest of the table is just a supporting chorus line.

### The drafting dance
- On your turn, choose one card from your hand to keep face down in front of you.
- Pass the remaining cards to your left (or right—depending on the round and house rules you adopt) and repeat with the new hand you receive.
- After everyone reveals simultaneously, collect your cards and tally any immediate effects, then proceed to the next round.

### End of round and end of game
- After several rounds equal to the deck size or as dictated by player count, you move into the final tally. The game ends after a round where the last cards are drafted and revealed.
- Score the various patterns: two tempuras, delicious nigiri combos, Maki advantage, and the pudding endgame bonus. The scores add up quickly, and the table’s energy rises in proportion to who managed to snag the most adorable sashimi cards.

### A few strategy notes for the practical-minded
- Balance risk vs reward: hot combos (like two tempuras or multiple dumplings) can propel you ahead, but chasing them at the expense of other stable points can backfire.
- Read the table: if someone is clearly chasing a high-value set, you can sometimes foil them by taking a card that blocks their path, even if it isn’t your top choice.
- 2-player play nuance: with two players, the drafting becomes a fast-paced duel where each decision carries more weight per round. The game still maintains its rhythm, but expect slightly faster cycles and a more intimate table talk.

## Mechanics deep dive: what makes the drafting sing
### Card drafting and pattern building
Sushi Go Family leverages a simple yet elegant drafting system where each turn offers a single decision with immediate visualization of potential scoring patterns. The core fun comes from the tension between what you want now and what might come in the future as you pass cards around. It’s a miniature puzzle: how do I secure a stable point chunk while also preventing my opponent from building the same pattern? The balance between short-term gain and long-term strategy keeps the game engaging round after round.

### The “set collection” rhythm in bite-sized portions
Unlike heavier drafting games, Sushi Go Family keeps the scoring patterns bite-sized and approachable. Two tempuras can be worth more than several single items, but you only get them by playing the long game of collecting those two specific cards. The same applies to dumplings—the more you collect, the bigger the payoff, but those cards aren’t the easiest to grab once the deck starts thinning out. This creates a natural arc: early rounds feel casual, mid rounds introduce tension, and the last few rounds can tilt the scoreboard in a dramatic, satisfying way.

### Endgame tension: puddings matter
Pudding cards, scored at game end, inject a delightful late game twist. They force you to think about the long tail of the session and sometimes reward players who took slightly riskier options earlier to secure pudding-based gains later. This mechanic prevents the game from devolving into a purely ephemeral sprint; it gives you a reason to remember what happened in round three and to hedge your bets as the end approaches.
