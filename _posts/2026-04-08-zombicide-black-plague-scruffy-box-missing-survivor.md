## The Missing Survivor: Why It Matters and How It Impacts Play

In Zombicide Black Plague, a team of survivors collaborates to push back a tide of undead. Each survivor brings unique abilities, equipment options, and roles — scout, medic, heavy weapon specialist, the entire fantasy RPG mirror-ball that makes co op gaming shine. A missing survivor isn't just a figure: it is potentially a missing plan, a missing synergy, and for some players, a missing story beat you were desperate to write in your next gaming session.

First, we must identify what a missing survivor means in practice. If you play with four players, you probably rely on a spectrum of survivability: someone to sprint, someone to heal, someone to draw aggro, and someone to quietly become the true MVP by performing essential tasks that keep the team alive longer than the rest of you deserve. If one member is not present, the team must adjust on the fly. You can house-rule a substitute: the missing figure can be a placeholder named after your favorite sitcom character. But that undermines the impartial balance designed by the game. A substitute person with a similar profile can work, but it is not ideal.

There are two main school of thoughts here. The first is pragmatic: if your campaign is about a single session, you can game the system by using a different survivor with comparable save/dodge stats and call it a day. The second is purist: you pause, contact the vendor, and request a replacement. If you are anything like me, you realize you are not playing a war movie where every prop is essential; you can improvise, you can adapt, but you must acknowledge that you are altering the expected flow of the game. That is where the fun stops for a moment and the geek inside you sighs deeply and whispers, “Well done, Sherlock, you have found a loophole.”

Replacement policies vary by retailer and by publisher. In many cases, board game manufacturers offer a straightforward replacement for missing or damaged parts, especially for new products that have left the factory with the wrong sprue or a mispackaged survivor. The process typically involves sending photos of the box contents, the sealed/unsealed state of the packaging, and your order number. Some will require proof of purchase and a serial number or batch code visible on the box rear. The good news is that modern companies understand that a missing piece is not the end of your narrative; it is an opportunity to practice good customer service and to remind yourself that your hobby is supported by people who eat, sleep, and bleed packaging tape to keep your cardboard dreams intact.

For the impatient among us, let me share a practical plan you can adopt this weekend:

- Step 1: Stop swearing at the box. You just unboxed something that is designed to create immersive chaos. Your keyboard can wait.
- Step 2: Photograph the contents in order: boards, zombies, tokens, dice, standees, and the missing survivor piece. A photo helps the support team understand your exact situation faster than you can say zombie apocalypse.
- Step 3: Gather your proof of purchase. A receipt or order confirmation matters, especially if you bought from a retailer with a strict policy on missing components.
- Step 4: Contact the retailer first, then the publisher if needed. If the retailer passes the buck, go straight to the source. In many cases, you can request a replacement part instead of returning the whole box, which makes the entire process less painful than a bite from a particularly persistent zombie.
- Step 5: If you want to keep the vibe authentic, you can patch the gap with a temporary proxy. The survivors are still there; you just have a nameless placeholder or a tiny custom-painted mini to fill the role for that session. It’s a fun DIY moment that makes your friends ask if you have a secret modeling hobby and if you are going to run a diorama of the siege next.

As far as policy and practice go, I highly recommend keeping notes of your correspondence and tracking the expected replacement timeline. Some stores ship replacements within days; others require a longer lead time while the terrifying logistics of spare zombie hands get sorted out. The important thing is to preserve the narrative you want to tell about the game rather than letting a missing piece turn into a mysterious artifact that exists only in legend.

## A Practical Unboxing and Setup Guide for the Time-Pressed Player

Time is money when you are a gamer who wants to start slaying the undead as soon as humanly possible. Here is a quick, practical guide to get your Zombicide Black Plague session up and running even with a missing survivor piece:

- Plan the table layout ahead of time. The boards are modular, and you can usually get a session started with a compact layout rather than the sprawling metropolis you might imagine from the box art.
- Sort your components the moment the box lands on the table. This reduces the post-box chaos and helps you spot the missing piece early. A little organization goes a long way when your dice are screaming for blood and you need your survivors to appear in order.
- Create a temporary standing rule for the missing survivor. For example, you could designate a placeholder survivor with a card that summarizes the missing piece's ability. It is not perfect, but it is a fun narrative device. If your group is prone to meta-game talk, the placeholder can become a running joke that adds a little personality to your session.

## Handling the Missing Piece: A Step-by-step Replacement Plan

The practical plan for missing components in a brand-new game is surprisingly boring in a satisfying way. It is basically customer service tenacity with a dash of nerd enthusiasm. Here is a detailed step-by-step plan that has worked for me several times:

1) Photograph everything you have, including the exact spot where the survivor was supposed to appear. The photo helps the support team understand the issue without back-and-forth emails that feel like a rescue mission.
2) Collect purchase details: order number, date of purchase, and retailer name. If you bought the game in a shop, have your receipt handy. If you bought digitally or through a platform, gather the order ID from your account.
3) Contact support for the publisher or retailer. Be concise, friendly, and specific about the missing piece. Mention the exact product name, edition, and the missing piece as you understand it. If you can, attach the photos to your message.
4) Ask for a replacement part rather than a whole replacement box if possible. This saves time and reduces the shipping weight for everyone involved.
5) Keep a copy of all communications. If the process stalls, don’t hesitate to escalate to a supervisor. Your patience is a virtue, but you also deserve your missing survivor back in action.
6) In the meantime, you can craft a fun substitute. A stand-in figure with a little paint and a name card can keep the game flowing while you wait for the proper piece to arrive. You will probably find this not only tolerable but also oddly charming, especially when the table suddenly feels like an ad hoc miniature-figuring club.

Having walked several such replacement paths, I can say that the actual time to replacement can range from a few days to a few weeks depending on the stock situation. The more you document and the sooner you reach out, the faster you will be able to reclaim the original balance of the team. And yes, there is a tiny pang of disappointment when you realize your play session is not going to be exactly like the pristine marketing video, but that is the price of enjoying a hobby that cares about its players enough to fix itself when things go sideways.
