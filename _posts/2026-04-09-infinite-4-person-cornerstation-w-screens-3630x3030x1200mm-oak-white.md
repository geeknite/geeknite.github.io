### Screens and privacy panels
The W Screens, as the name suggests, are privacy panels built into the desk to create four individual work zones. They are not computer monitors; they are separation devices. The goal is to reduce glare from your own screen while offering a semblance of personal space across a shared surface. In practice, they help squelch the “I can hear your keyboard scuffle” vibes that plague open-plan setups. If privacy is not your thing, these panels can be configured to allow open sightlines; if you want to stage a debate about who forgot to mute during a meeting, you have the option to flip between modes with a few cable-friendly moves.

### Cable management and power integration
Every desk of this magnitude has to solve the inevitable wireless vs wired question: how do we feed these four corners without turning the whole thing into a black-hole of cords? The Infinite Cornerstation includes a cable management trough and separate channels by which you can route power and data to each workstation pod. Expect a few hours of tidying if you want the look to be immaculate, but be prepared for the classic desk-drawer ritual of discovering the unknown USB cable you swear you saw two years ago. Pro tip: label your power rails, run the high-voltage stuff in the outer channels, and keep your data cables cleanly separated from power to minimize interference during a heated gaming session.

## Assembly and Setup
### Unboxing and initial impressions
If unboxing videos taught us anything, it’s that furniture is a test of patience and a comedy of misread instructions. ### Step-by-step assembly and tips
The build is modular. You assemble the corner unit first, anchor it to the wall or floor, then lay down the occasional panels and screens. The trick is to align the corner curvature so all four seats feel equally included without someone feeling left out near the wall. Our tip: sort your screws by size, lay out panels on a soft surface to avoid scratches, and do a test-run before you commit to the final screw-tightening. You don’t want to discover, halfway through, that you’ve assembled the “left wing” to the “right wing” and now you have a giant, wobbly sandwich.

### Ergonomics and comfort during long sessions
The desk height sits in a comfortable range for typical office chairs, with enough clearance for cable routing under the seats. For longer work days, you’ll want to ensure that monitor height is appropriate for each station. The privacy screens help reduce distractions, while the open areas encourage collaboration when you’re in “group project” mode. Ergonomics-wise, four people sharing a single unit always means compromises; you’ll want adjustable chairs, properly spaced monitor arms, and a plan for occasional stand-up breaks. Remember: your back will thank you later when the assembly is complete and you aren’t hunched over a single, glorified work surface.

## How to get the most from your cornerstation
### Room placement and lighting tips
Place the desk in a corner with natural light to minimize screen glare. If possible, keep the wall behind the screens free of heavy artwork or clutter which could reflect into the workspace. If you have a home theater nearby, consider acoustic panels to dampen ambient noise and make four-person collaboration feel less like a chaotic boardroom and more like a well-tuned team in a sci-fi lab.

### Cable setup and power management tips
Labeling is your best friend here. Use color-coded zip ties and a labeling system on all power rails. Group high-draw devices on a separate circuit if you can. The last thing you want is a tripped breaker during a live stream or critical team call. A simple giga-stranded cable management approach can help you maintain a neat surface while providing enough slack to adjust seats and screens without disassembly.
