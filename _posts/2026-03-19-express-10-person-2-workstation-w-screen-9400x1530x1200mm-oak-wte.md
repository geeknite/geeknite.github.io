## Design and Build Quality: The Good, the Bad, and the Gigantic
### Materials and Finish
The oak veneer on this thing has a nice grain that reads as “finished product with actual tree meaning.” It’s not one of those cheap laminates that whisper “I’m going to peel at the edges” after you sneeze. The edges are squared off with a gentle bevel, which is appreciated if you plan to walk into the desk at 3 a.m. while wearing a VR headset and chaotic sock choice. The core structure seems to be a sturdy metal frame wrapped in a veneer that can take a reasonable amount of abuse from office chairs, cable ties, and a curious cat.

I spent a good chunk of time testing how the surface handles everyday marks and the occasional spill. Coffee, tea, and water are inevitable guests in any office. The oak veneer held up surprisingly well with careful cleanup and a basic furniture cleaner—no dramatic war stories required. If you’re the type who spills Tuesday’s dream on a daily basis, you’ll want a quick-draw cleaning kit and a lot of elbow grease. Still, this is better than most office-grade desks where you need to hire a professional to buff out a scratch the size of a dime.

### Assembly and Modularity
This beast ships in a way that makes Indiana Jones roll his eyes: lots of boxes, a hex wrench, and a set of allen keys that seems to be as old as the Andes. My advice: assign one person to be the “box opener,” one person to “hand the tool,” and one person to “judge the number of cables.” It’s not a one-man job, unless you’re into intense yoga posing and the vow of “I’ll only cry on a useful surface.” The assembly manual is straightforward enough—pictures, minimal words, and an alarming number of washers that look identical. A few fitment adjustments were necessary to square the long panel with the central screen, and yes, you might need a second pair of hands for the longer modules.

### Screens and Connectivity: The Screen-in-Desk Concept
Yes, the product name includes “With Screen.” The central screen is built into the midsection and is designed to be a focal point for two workstation lines. It’s not a cinema screen, but it’s large enough to hitch a small movie night on a Friday if your team agrees not to watch nature documentaries for once. The screen’s brightness and color reproduction were surprisingly useful for both design work (color-critical tasks) and gaming sessions where someone insists on “just one more round.” The screen is best described as a “functional centerpiece” rather than a home theater centerpiece. If you’re after a wall-mounted behemoth, this isn’t it; if you want a practical, integrated display that makes the desk feel like a command center, this is your vibe.

### Cable Management: A Heroic Climb Up Mount Cablemore
The cable management solution is, frankly, the hero we didn’t know we needed. There are channels, grommets, and cable trays that help keep the chaos to something manageable—ish. In our test environment, the desk didn’t magically solve all cable tangling, but it did reduce the “spaghetti tangle” factor by about 60%. When you’ve got up to ten workers snagging their devices in a shared space, there will be cables. The design tries to corral them; you still need to invest in cable sleeves, zip ties, and the patience of a saint who’s learning to enjoy the sound of a cable loom snapping in slow motion. A final recommendation: invest in a robust cable-management kit and label your cables with color-coded tags. Trust me, future you will thank past you when someone asks, “Where does this specific HDMI cable go?” and you don’t have to do the scavenger hunt of 900 cables.

## Space Planning and Ergonomics: Comfort in a Colossal Shell
### Ergonomic Bliss (or Not-So-Blissful Realities)
Ergonomics rules in theory, but in practice, a 9.4-meter bench invites you to test every hypothesis about posture, reach, and the myth of “I’ll be more productive if everything is closer.” The desk’s height is adjustable, which is essential because not everyone shares a single baseline. If you’re sharing with colleagues with wildly different body heights, you’ll appreciate the ability to tailor your position. The key is to set a neutral posture that balances arm reach, monitor height, and the possibility of accidentally bumping the central screen with a chair wheel. If you’re not careful, you’ll discover new and creative ways to misalign your neck, which, frankly, is a gift to imagination but not to your chiropractor.

### Screen Positioning and Multi-User Dynamics
With the screen centrally located, two workstations can operate around a shared hub. The idea is neat; the execution requires coordination. The screen should be at an optimal height for the tallest user and at an angle that reduces glare across the entire zone. In practice, you’ll end up rotating chairs, nudging monitors, and developing a new sport—desk acrobatics. For teams that thrive on collaboration and occasional disagreements about color grading, the central screen becomes a communication focal point—if your team can agree on what color gray actually is. When it works, it’s collaborative magic; when it doesn’t, it’s a chorus of, “Turn the brightness down!” and, “No, we didn’t say brightness; we said contrast.”

### Chair Coverage and Personal Space
Ten people sharing a long bench is an exercise in social dynamics. You’ll inevitably get the friend who hogs the corner, the person who needs two extra feet of elbow room to type with confidence, and the coworker who is always exactly in your line of sight when you’re trying to code. The desk’s footprint supports adequate personal space, but the social space around it is where you’ll negotiate etiquette in the same language as your version control system—tests are green, but moods are amber. The key to success is establishing “zones”: a calm zone for deep work, a collaboration zone for creative sprinting, and a “hands-off if I’m in the middle of a bug” zone that’s mostly respected after coffee has cooled down.

## Storage, Organization, and Real World Debris Management
Storage is a big deal in a 10-person setup. You want enough drawers, shelves, and cubbies to avoid the “where did I put the stapler” conspiracy. The Express workstation features integrated storage options along the sides and central modules that offer a combination of open shelves and closed cabinets. Our test run found that open shelves are perfect for showing off quirky gadgets, 3D-printed doodads, and the occasional emergency snack stash. Closed cabinets help hide the chaos, but you’ll still need separate organizers for the ever-growing pile of paperwork and the “I definitely filed this last Thursday” pile.

Pro tip: label everything. If you label it with a nerdy joke (e.g., “Objects in desk drawers may be heavier than they look”), you’ll make the day a tiny bit more fun and a tiny bit less dull. The key is to balance accessibility with a sense of organization that won’t crumble at the slightest breeze or the arrival of a new intern who thinks “ergonomic” is a dance move.

## Durability, Maintenance, and Longevity: Will It Survive The Office Apocalypse?
As with any furniture designed to accommodate a crew, durability is crucial. The frames feel sturdy; the oak veneer handles a daily barrage of office life with a quiet dignity, even as someone tests the “suppressing reality” by tossing a swivel chair in a dramatic arc. Maintenance is straightforward: wipe with a soft cloth, avoid harsh chemicals, and consider applying a mild conditioner every few months to keep the surface from drying out and becoming a playground for static electricity and the occasional stray hair from your cat that thinks the desk is a giant scratching post.

In terms of long-term resilience, you’re looking at a piece that should withstand daily usage for years, assuming you don’t attempt to move it alone through a doorway that’s two centimeters too narrow. The true test of durability is in the ability to adapt to new team sizes and reconfigurations—one of which will inevitably happen when someone leaks a new budget spreadsheet and everyone starts moving furniture around in a panic.
