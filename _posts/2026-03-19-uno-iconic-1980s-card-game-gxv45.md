## Gameplay experience: classic UNO with a retro glaze
### Setup and first impressions
To set up, you deal each player seven cards and lay the rest into a draw pile. The top card starts the discard pile, and then you’re off to the colorful races. The GXV45 rules stay faithful to Uno’s core: match color or number, play action cards to disrupt the pack, and strategically unleash a Wild to flip the table (metaphorically speaking, unless you’re playing with your best friend who secretly wants to reverse the entire night).

What GXV45 adds is the 80s vibe. The language is the same, but the packaging and card art invite you to lean into a more flamboyant, party-friendly mode of play. The result is a game that feels friendly enough for kids and chaotic enough for adults who still remember cassette tapes as if they were yesterday.

### The rhythm of play
UNO has always been about tempo: who’s counting cards, who’s bluffing with a Wild, who’s about to draw a handful because someone drew a Draw Two and then a Skip in the same turn. GXV45 keeps this rhythm in spades. The action cards—Skip, Reverse, Draw Two, Wild, Wild Draw Four—land with satisfying pops when you slap them onto the pile. The neon-themed art helps players quickly locate color groups in the chaos, which may or may not be a blessing depending on how competitive your group gets.

### Variations and optional house rules
UNO is famously flexible with house rules. GXV45 invites you to experiment with a few 80s-tinged twists: speed rounds where everyone passes their hand after two minutes, or a “neon draw” hour where every time someone forgets to say Uno, you shout neon and draw two. If you want to keep things breezy, keep the standard UNO rules and enjoy the box’s era-specific mood.
