### How to play (the retro-flavored basics)

UNO’s core mechanics are deceptively simple, and the 1980s edition sticks to that. If you can match the color or number of the top card on the discard pile, you play. If you can’t, you draw. The action cards toss chaos into the mix in ways that feel natural, not forced. Here’s a quick refresher with the retro lens:

- Each player starts with a hand of seven cards. The rest of the deck forms the draw pile.
- The top card of the draw pile starts the discard pile. Players take turns matching either color or number to the top card.
- If you can’t play, you draw one card. If that card can be played immediately, you may do so; if not, your turn ends.
- Skip: The next player loses a turn. It’s a blunt tool for punishing someone who looks too confident with their color choices.
- Reverse: In a two-player game, this acts like a skip. In larger games, it flips the direction of play, which can turn the board into a minor cyclone of strategy arguments and laughter.
- Draw Two: The next player draws two cards and loses their turn. The psychological impact can be severe when you’re drawing toward a new color but the group is loaded with red cards anyway.
- Wild: The player declares the color that continues play. In retro packs, the color declaration moment feels akin to a small theatrical performance—there’s a flourish and a smile that says, “Yes, I will turn this game into my personal color-coordinated chaos.”
- Wild Draw Four (if included in your edition): The next player draws four cards and loses their turn, with the caveat that you must have no other playable card of any color. This card is a dramatic gesture and, frankly, the adult version of a dramatic mic drop.

The game ends when a player runs out of cards. But in UNO Retro, endings tend to be less like a cinematic climax and more like a party that’s about to wrap up but refuses to admit defeat. You can set a target score (e.g., first to 500 points) if you want to stretch the evening, which is a fun twist for those who crave a little long-game commitment.

- Five-second rule: any player who takes longer than five seconds to decide must draw a penalty card. This keeps the game moving and injects a little comedic tension as players hustle their brains into gear.
