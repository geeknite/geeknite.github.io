Design and Build: First Impressions

The blade is described as high carbon steel, which historically means a material that holds an edge when you stare at it cross-eyed and whispers, Yo, I can cut. If you’re into a long, steady stroke for slicing practice or dramatic cinematic entrances, this blade gives you a plausible stage presence without needing a gymnasium-sized cutting space.

### Handle, grip, and balance

The handle is described as a TPR (thermoplastic rubber) grip, which is a fancy way of saying it’s grippy, forgiving, and not likely to melt under hot dojo lights. TPR handles are great for beginners who want a secure hold when they misjudge their own strength and accidentally perform a windmill of enthusiasm. The grip texture is comfortable enough for long practice sessions, but it’s not a luxury wooden wrap; this is a practical option that shrugs off sweat and rainy training days. The balance point sits predictably along the middle of the hand, giving you leverage without forcing you into a contortionist pose. In short: it feels like a weapon designed for actual use rather than a prop on a shelf.

Maintenance, Care, and Longevity

### Cleaning and oiling basics

High carbon blades demand some TLC, and this one is no exception. After use, wipe with a soft cloth to remove fingerprints and moisture, then give the blade a light coat of mineral oil or a dedicated blade oil. This helps prevent rust and preserves edge integrity over time. The TPR handle won’t rot like natural wood, but it’s still wise to wipe it down and keep it dry. Don’t let the blade soak in liquids for extended periods; you’re not marinating a steak, you’re maintaining a tool that wants to be sharp when you call its name.

### Storage and humidity considerations

Store the blade in a dry environment with a stable humidity level. If you live in a coastal area with salty air, consider a desiccant pack in your display or storage area. The goal is to keep the blade from oxidizing while it’s not in play. In Geeknite fashion, treat this blade as you would a prized arcade stick: display when you want to show it off, store it securely when you don’t; don’t leave it leaning against a spot where your dog can mistake it for a chew toy.
