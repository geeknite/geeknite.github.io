## The new rules and the Elite Cards
### What are the Elite Cards?
The Elite Cards are six or so edition-specific action cards that slot into the standard UNO rules to give players a new toolbox. Think of them as the secret sauces you’d expect in a sports-themed kitchen: there are play-altering effects that reward timely aggression, but they aren’t so overpowering that they derail the game’s core balance.
- End Zone (Wild Draw 4-ish): A wild that can only be played if the current color is “wiped” from memory and the player shouts something that sounds like a touchdown. It forces the next player to draw a number of cards equal to the number of players minus one, but only after you declare the color. It’s chaotic in a fun way.
- Two-Point Conversion (Skip + Reverse): A card that can skip or reverse once, representing a sudden switch in the game’s momentum. It’s a helpful tool that can turn a tense moment into a celebratory flip as you watch someone’s strategy topple like a domino line in a tunnel of cheerleaders.
- Penalty Flag (Color Change): A color-change card with a sports-flavored twist. Instead of choosing a single color, the player announces a color and a “penalty flag” rule that adds one more card for the next player if they don’t comply. It’s cute, it’s cheeky, and it yields some dramatic comebacks.
- Touchdown! (Draw 4 bonus): A flashy version of Draw Four that also grants you a tiny advantage: after you play it, you may look at the top of the draw pile and rearrange the top five cards, a mini-miracle that feels surprisingly powerful in late-game chaos.
- End-of-Quarter Reset (Wild): This wild resets the round’s vibe by shuffling everyone’s hands in a way that creates new possibilities—but be warned, it can backfire spectacularly if you misjudge the moment.
- Hail Mary (Plus One): A single card that adds an extra card to one player of your choice, with a wink toward the final big plays of the night.

Because these cards are rarer than an autographed football on eBay at game day, the game stays accessible. You won’t see these effects consistently enough to wreck the core UNO experience, but you’ll feel them enough to alter late-game decisions in a way that keeps players engaged rather than resigned to grim inevitability.
