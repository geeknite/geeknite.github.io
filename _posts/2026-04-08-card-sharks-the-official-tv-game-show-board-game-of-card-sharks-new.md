## How to play: a quick-start guide to not messing up friends

This section is a digestible primer if you’re bringing Card Sharks to game night and want a least-common-denominator start before you start adding silly house rules.

### Objective
Reach the top money level by correctly predicting whether the next card will be higher or lower than the current card, while answering questions that unlock your ability to reveal new cards. It’s the show’s core mechanic, distilled into an approachable tabletop form.

### Basic turn flow
1. The dealer/shark (host-adjacent facilitator) flips a starting card and places a marker on the money ladder.
2. A player (or team, depending on your house rules) guesses higher or lower for the next card. If correct, you advance on the ladder and claim that round’s money; if wrong, you pass the turn, and the opponent might get a crack at a strategic recovery.
3. Answer a question from the Card Cards deck, which can influence card draws or provide additional choices (like multiple bets or a second flip).
4. If you hit a “bust” zone (you’re out of money or fail to meet a threshold), your round ends. The goal is to survive multiple rounds and accumulate the top tier money tokens.

### The turn structure: fast, but not furious
The game emphasizes cadence: quick questions, faster decisions, and the occasional dramatic reveal when a card lands face-up. The real drama comes from the “is it higher or lower?” decision that begins with a safe card and ends with a riskier pick as the ladder climbs. The tension is the fun part; the actual math is a background hum that your brain tunes in subconsciously by the third game.

### House rules and how to tweak the chaos
- Time-box the question rounds: two minutes per round, because nothing kills a night like a gambler’s remorse and an open-ended Sudoku puzzle in your head.
- Team mode: pair up players and alternate guesses. This is great for larger groups and for keeping the conversation lively when someone inevitably overthinks a two-card decision.
- The “Shark Attack” variant: when a player busts, you add a tiny humorous penalty for the rest of the round (like wearing a silly hat or reciting a nerdy fact about probabilities).

### Probability, risk, and a little bit of strategy
Card Sharks is a light-weight probability exercise, not a full-blown math class. You’ll learn quickly that early ladders reward caution, while higher ladders demand bold bets and a flair for dramatic misdirection. The show’s logic—more risk can yield more reward—aligns with the board game’s design, but you’ll often find yourself choosing entertainment value over pure statistical advantage. And that’s absolutely fine; board games are a social sport first and a math annex second.
