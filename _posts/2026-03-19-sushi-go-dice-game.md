## Introduction

Brace yourself, adventurer of snacks and strategist of snack-sized decisions: Sushi Go! Dice Game has rolled onto the table with a clatter of tiny edible dice and a whiff of mad genius. ### What is Sushi Go! Dice Game?

In this speed-drafting dice game, players roll a handful of sushi dice and draft dice to build the best menu of nigiri, maki rolls, and side dishes before the table goes quiet with a satisfied burp. The aim remains the same as the card game: collect sets and combos to maximize your score. But in this version, your fortune is decided not by collecting card combos, but by evaluating dice faces that flicker with the edible glory of sushi. The result is a compact, fast-paced party game that can handle 2 to 6 players and fits in a backpack of snack-sized glory.

## Components and Setup

### What's in the box
- A colorful set of dice, each etched with sushi icons: nigiri, maki, gunkan, and a few quirky sides.
- A small score pad and reference card showing how to tally points.
- A handful of food-tongue-inspired tokens and a little discard tray for the dice that didn’t quite land in your favor.
- A compact rules booklet that reads as if a sushi chef wrote it during a particularly intense service.

The core idea is deceptively simple: roll dice, draft a few, pass the rest, and try not to let your rivals steal your favorite combinations. Some dice are “hot” with big scoring options; others are more modest but steady, like a reliable eel roll that never fails to disappoint (in a good way).

### Setup notes

The setup takes about a minute. Shuffle the dice, place them within reach, give each player a little score tracker, and decide who is the starting chef (i.e., who rolls first). The first player rolls a subset of the dice to create a pool; everyone chooses a die to keep and passes the remaining dice to the left. The process repeats a couple more times, until all dice are drafted. Then the scoring begins, with players tallying points for sets of sushi, combos with side dishes, and special bonuses.

### Table presence

Visually, Sushi Go! Dice Game pops with the same bright, cartoony vibe as the card version, but with a tactile twist: dice. There’s something inherently satisfying about watching a little piece of sushi dice tumble and bounce toward your napkin. It’s the culinary equivalent of rolling a crit in a dungeon game — you hope for the spicy tuna, but settle for a crunchy cucumber roll with a wry grin.

### Accessibility considerations

The game is approachable for kids and adults alike, though it does reward pattern recognition and risk calculation. It’s not a heavy strategy game, but it has enough depth to keep your inner sim traveling for a few rounds. If your group is new to drafting or dice games, this is a gentle, digestible introduction with a healthy splash of whimsy.

## How to Play: A Walkthrough

### Turn structure and flow

Each round consists of a drafting sequence followed by a scoring phase. On your turn, you roll a subset of your dice. You then pick a die to keep from the face-up pool and pass the remaining dice to the person on your left. The goal is to assemble a set of faces that maximize score cards, just like forming a menu of the best sushi. The number of rounds is finite, but the number of opportunities to snatch the perfect combination feels infinite when you’re hungry for a win (and a snack).

### The faces and what they do

- Nigiri faces yield points based on the type and sometimes require combinations for maximum effect.
- Maki faces grant points if you collect enough copies; the more you have, the more you’ll gloat to your neighbors.
- Side dish faces (like edamame or gyoza) provide smaller bonuses but can be the tiebreakers that tilt a tense scoreline in your favor.
- A few rare faces might trigger special bonuses, rewarding you for collecting a variety of sushi types or for matching patterns across rounds.

The exact scoring cards are where the theme and the math fuse. You’ll find yourself calculating a multi-move plan: do I chase the big Nigiri payoff now, or do I grab a few minor gains and hope the table fights over the rest on the last turn? It’s a delicious dilemma, and that’s where the fun hides.

### Example round

Let’s imagine a quick hypothetical exchange. Round one starts with a pool of six dice. You roll them and see a flashy Nigiri with a big point value, two Maki dice, a small Gunkan drop, and a duo of side-dish symbols. You pick the Nigiri, pass the rest. The next player does the same, and so forth. By the time the dice have circled the table twice, your options have shifted from “grab the big payoff now” to “secure the set that will crush the tiebreakers.” The tension builds, the table laughs, and you realize you’ve eaten enough sushi in your head to know what not to order when you’re hungry again five minutes later.

### Scoring and end-of-round tally

At the end of each round, you tally points from your collected dice according to the face values and the scoring cards. The rules give you a handful of shortcuts for combos and sets; if you’re lucky, you’ll snag the coolest bonuses for assembling a rainbow of sushi types. The final tally across rounds gives you the winner’s sashimi-grade bragging rights. The rules aren’t complicated, but the scoring does reward careful planning and a dash of luck — the two things that make snack-based tabletop fights so endearing.

## Strategy and Tips

### Early game focus

In the first couple of rounds, you’re laying breadcrumbs. Your decisions should lean toward flexible, low-risk gains. Choose dice that leave you with multiple plausible paths for the later rounds. Don’t get married to a single type of sushi too early; the dice may betray you, and you’ll look silly sighing about “my precious nigiri” while someone else quietly clocks a sweet set of maki bonuses.

### Mid-game pivoting

When more dice have circulated, start reading the table. If you notice others chasing a specific combination, you can choose to block by taking a die that would break their rhythm. It’s not purely antagonistic; it’s about adjusting to the energy around the table. Some rounds are about risk management: do you gamble on a high-scoring path that requires two perfect rolls, or do you bank reliable points from smaller sets?

### Endgame clinchers

The last rounds are where you separate the pros from the pretenders. It’s tempting to chase a flashy end-game objective, but often a quiet, consistent approach wins the day. Keep an eye on your opponents’ totals and their potential to explode with one strong roll. If you can deny them a single critical die while piling up small gains for yourself, you’ll walk away with the table’s respect and a grin that says, I came; I rolled; I conquered.

### House rules and variants

Some groups like to loosen up the rules with a few “house” twists: faster rounds, hidden scoring cards, or alternate die faces for extra chaos. If you want to remix, do it in the spirit of the game: keep the pace quick, keep it silly, and don’t ruin someone’s appetite with overbearing rules. The joy of Sushi Go! Dice is its nimble design — any tweak should be in the service of speed, laughter, and snacks.

## Comparison to Sushi Go! Card Game

If you’re already a fan of Sushi Go! Card Game, you’ll notice a few key shifts:

- The tempo is faster. Dice add a crisp, tactile layer that cards lack, and the rolling itself introduces a little chaos that keeps things fresh.
- Scoring feels more modular. In the card version, you chase specific sets; in the dice version, you chase face values that align with scoring cards. It’s similar in vibe but with a different flavor of decision-making.
- Social dynamics change. Cards hide information more effectively; dice expose a bit more of the table’s appetite for risk. You’ll see players lean into bold plays you hadn’t anticipated because the dice gave them a window to chase big payoffs.

In short: if you enjoy the original for its warmth and speed, the Dice version scratches a similar itch with added tactile glee. If you’re skeptical about losing some depth in translating from cards to dice, you’ll be relieved to find that the core tempo remains brisk, forgiving, and highly entertaining for casual game nights.
