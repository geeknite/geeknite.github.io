Material and durability
-----------------------

Durability depends heavily on material choice. Acrylic and PET panels tend to be lighter and shatter-resistant (great for accidental desk dives or a print-out of your to-do list that never gets printed). If you’re looking at tempered glass, you’re in a different league: heavier, scratch-resistant, and potentially more premium-feeling, but less forgiving if you need to reposition it on a whim. In most product listings, you’ll see thicknesses ranging from 2 to 4 millimeters. At Geeknite, we tested a mid-range 2.5–3 mm variant. It’s sturdy enough to survive the occasional elbow-nudge from a neighbor who insists they “just need to borrow your charger for one second” but not so thick that you’ll feel compelled to hire a mover to rearrange your desk.

The base and mounting system deserve scrutiny as well. If it’s a clamp-on shield, check the clamp’s width and the material it’s built from. Plastic clamps? Cute, but slippery when your desk’s surface is crisp and clean. Metal clamps? Durable, but you’ll want to avoid marring your desk with the clamp’s bite if you’re prone to anxiety about surface blemishes. Some designs come with stand-alone bases, which can be handy for desks without edge clamps or for you to pretend you’re working on a “server rack” in your home lab. The bottom line: ensure the mounting system is compatible with your desk thickness and surface finish and that it stays put during keyboard tapping and the occasional sprint to the microwave for lunch.

Comparisons with other desk dividers
-----------------------------------

There are plenty of desk divider options on the market, from crystal-clear acrylic panels to frosted PET sheets and even fabric-covered boards. Here’s a quick compare-and-contrast to help you decide:

- Acrylic vs PET: Acrylic is a bit more robust and scratch-resistant, but can show fingerprints more easily if you don’t clean it regularly. PET is lighter and often more budget-friendly; it’s easier to replace if you nick it, but it may feel less premium. Your choice depends on how rough your desk life tends to be.
- Full-length screens vs. partial panels: Full-length privacy screens can offer more privacy and reduce screen visibility from the sides, but they can also encroach on your personal space. Partial panels like the 20x12 model provide a middle ground, shielding your work area without turning your desk into a box.
- Stand-alone bases vs. clamps: Stand-alone bases are flexible and portable; clamps save desk space and keep things neat but require a compatible desk edge. If you move offices frequently, go for clamps; if you rarely move, stand-alone bases are fine.
