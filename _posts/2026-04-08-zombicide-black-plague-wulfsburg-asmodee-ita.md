## What's in the box
Wulfsburg doesn’t reinvent Zombicide’s wheel; it adds a well-oiled crank to make that wheel feel more alive and gnarly. The box includes four to six new urban tiles that form a compact, asymmetric map when combined with base boards. The tiles are printed with the same moody, medieval vibe we’ve grown to love: blackberry windows, timber-framed houses, and the occasional ominous banner fluttering in your imagination. The quality of the card stock is solid; not the brittle business-card feel you get with some cheaper expansions, but something that can survive a crippling round of “we forgot to shuffle the zombies.”

There are new miniatures representing the inhabitants of Wulfsburg—think townsfolk who look at danger with the resigned humor of a player who has seen one too many critical failures. The monsters are clearly inspired by wolves and werewolves in a medieval setting, giving players a familiar but fresh threat. The art direction is cohesive with Black Plague; if you own the base game and the other expansions, you’ll notice a consistent palette, a shared feel, and a sense that you’re stepping into a slightly more grim version of a fairy tale.

The Italian edition by Asmodee ITA handles translations with care. Rule summaries on the cards are faithful and readable, and the player aids are legible in a way that won’t force you to press your nose against the page. If you’ve ever played a language-barrier game, you’ll appreciate the quality of translation here; it speeds up turns without sacrificing the dread of a gnarly zombie horde. If you’re new to Zombicide, however, the learning curve remains steep; a dedicated gaming night is recommended to avoid learning fatigue.

Coach’s tip: before the first session, sort the new tiles, mark where each tile links to different parts of Wulfsburg, and set expectations about the wolves’ ferocity. In practice, the wolves don’t just bite—they plot revenge, then demand a snack.

### What’s inside and how it feels on the table
The expansion introduces a handful of tangible changes that ripple through setup and play. You’ll find a handful of new terrain tiles that slot into the existing map to create urban mazes—tight streets, dead-ends, and a couple of open plazas that invite high-risk hero pushes. The new cards bring objective variety, spawn scheduling variations, and encounter events that feel both thematic and tense. The card backs and fonts maintain readability across languages, which is a blessing for ITA players who don’t want to squint through rule clarifications that look like ancient runes.

The extra tiles also change LOS (line of sight) calculations in meaningful ways. There are more opportunities to trap zombies behind doors or use doorways as pressure-release valves when a wolf pack comes roaring down a street. The net effect is a more dynamic tactical canvas where your decisions matter more than pure luck of the draw.

### The Wulfsburg design philosophy
Zombicide: Black Plague – Wulfsburg is a love letter to players who want to raid a macabre town with a dose of strategy and plenty of adrenaline. The design team has done a clever job balancing new elements with the core rules so the expansion doesn’t feel like a rule-heavy detour. You’ll notice four or five new terrain types plus a few key mechanics that alter pathing and line of sight. The core idea is to tighten the tempo by forcing players to think about doorways, chokepoints, and the timing of zombie waves as they traverse the Wulfsburg streets.

The new map tiles create interesting decisions: do you push your survivors into a narrow alley to lure zombies into a controlled trap, or do you risk exposing them to a pack that can quickly outrun your team? The answer depends on your party’s loadout, and the wolves are more than happy to punish reckless bravado. This is where the expansion earns its keep: it forces your crew to share information and coordinate movement, otherwise you’ll watch your heroes become a patchwork of blade-scarred survivors.

In terms of difficulty, Wulfsburg sits in the “hard but fair” category. If you’ve played base Black Plague to its cruising speed, you’ll find the added threat punishes solo runs and rewards teamwork. The wolves push players to use the terrain and to manage the damage intake from the hordes. It’s not about who hits harder—it’s about who dodges longer to finish the mission and escape with your sanity intact.

## Game play experience: pacing, turn order, and strategy
The arrival of the Wulfsburg expansion doesn’t slow down the core game; it re-choreographs the dance. The turn order remains the same, but the options for positioning, sprinting, and grabbing objectives become a little more nuanced. The new tiles encourage careful planning over reckless heroism; you’ll want to map safe routes for your survivors and identify secure zones where you can hold out when the horde arrives.

One of the clever things about Wulfsburg is how it integrates with the base game’s cooperative mechanics. You don’t need to be an expert to enjoy the expansion, but you do need to be comfortable discussing your plan aloud and not turning every encounter into a solo monologue. The wolves punish bravado by shrinking the space between players, making careful coordination not just nice but necessary.

Asmodee ITA’s edition handles card text and iconography in a way that is accessible to Italian-speaking players. The cards feel crisp and legible, with readable fonts that scale well across different print runs. If you’re bilingual or monolingual in Italian, the design choice helps keep the pace brisk rather than bogging down the table in translation.

A typical session with Wulfsburg will feel longer than base Black Plague, but that extra time is well spent if you enjoy the sense of crescendo as the wolves move from the outskirts to the city center. The added cards provide enough variety to prevent the same encounter pattern across multiple playthroughs.

Small mechanical note: the expansion introduces additional spawn mechanics that require you to think about timing. If you’re used to the standard spawns popping up at regular intervals, you’ll notice a few twisty rules that require a moment of analysis. We recommend reading the rules aloud with the group during the first session to ensure everyone is on the same page. It pays off later when the wolves start streaming from alleyways and you realize you forgot to set an objective.

## Artwork and components review
The art direction is consistent with the rest of Black Plague. The Town of Wulfsburg evokes a grim fairy tale vibe, with moonlit streets, timber-framed taverns, and signage that looks like a mash-up of medieval signage and heavy-metal poster art. The miniatures are robust and expressive; the survivors wear their armor with a sense of grim determination, while the wolves look hungry and efficient. The sculpts aren’t overly busy, which makes them ideal for younger groups and new players who fear the sight of dozens of tiny teeth.

The components feel sturdy in hand. The cardboard tokens are thick enough to survive a few rounds of shoving, rummaging, and not-quite-remembered rule checks. The insert is well designed to hold the tiles and cards in a functional order, which makes setup less of a headache and more of a “we’re here to play” moment. If you’re a collector who loves neat organization, you’ll appreciate the small touches: color coordination on the town tiles, keyed icons on the player aids, and a general sense of premium packaging.

One caveat: the Italian edition uses Italian card backs for certain cards, which is fine if you intend to keep your games in the Italian language, but if you frequently lend to friends who don’t read Italian, you might want a bilingual setup. If you plan to use fan translations or language mods, the Wulfsburg pack plays nicely with those as well, provided you’re comfortable adjusting references.

In the end, the physical experience complements the gameplay well. You’ll leave the table with a sense of having explored a new part of the Black Plague world, rather than simply adding another generic zombie tile to your stack.

## Solo and co-op viability
Zombicide is, at its core, a co-op game with team-based objectives. The Wulfsburg expansion maintains that spirit while adding a dose of risk to solo players who take on the expansion by themselves. In a solo run, you’ll feel a noticeable uptick in difficulty, particularly if you’re trying to maintain a route through the new village lanes and manage wolf spawns efficiently. The expansion is less forgiving to solo players because the wolves compress space and force you to juggle multiple threats at once.

For groups, Wulfsburg shines. The balance between new allies and the wolf threat can create memorable moments: a plan that collapses under a sudden Howling Event, followed by a quick pivot to secure a critical objective. The best sessions are the ones where players communicate, leverage terrain, and coordinate the timing of their actions to prevent choke points from becoming death traps.

If you’re considering a long-running campaign or a mixed-ability group, Wulfsburg offers enough variety to keep sessions fresh. The cards provide enough variety to prevent the same path to victory from repeating itself across multiple plays.

## Value and longevity
In terms of value, the Wulfsburg expansion sits comfortably in the “worth it if you play often” category. If your weekly or monthly gaming nights lean toward co-ops and you’ve already built a comfortable base of Zombicide players, adding Wulfsburg can significantly extend game nights. You’ll have fresh maps, new tokens, and a few new mechanics that delay the stagnation of the base game.

On the other hand, if you’re a casual player who enjoys an occasional session or two, the expansion may feel like 30–40 dollars of content that you’ll pull out a few times a year. It’s not the same as a full purchase of a brand-new game, but it adds depth to the Black Plague universe. For those with a growing shelf of Zombicide content, Wulfsburg provides a compelling reason to revisit the base game’s themes with new spatial puzzles and a more intense late-game cadence.
