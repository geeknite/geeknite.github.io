## How to play with the color blind deck
The gameplay of Phase 10 remains the same at its core. Here is a quick refresher for new players and a reminder for veterans who forgot where they placed their lucky rabbit foot last game night:
- Each player gets a hand of cards, and players attempt to complete a series of phases in order. A phase might require sets of the same number or runs of consecutive numbers. The exact phases depend on the version you own.
- On a turn, you draw one card, then discard one. You can lay down completed phases on the table when you meet the requirements. The goal is to be the first to complete all phases.
- When a phase is completed, it is set aside and your progress continues with new phases until the end of the round.
- The deck includes wilds and sometimes skips, which can complicate things for a brief moment but add flavor to risk management on a late round.

With the color blind deck, you will notice that the cues replace or supplement color with shapes. This means that when you are deciding which cards to play for a phase, you can rely on symbol alignment rather than color matching. It is especially helpful when you are playing in rooms with suboptimal lighting or when you have a rowdy crew that keeps turning the lights off during negotiations for who gets to go first.

If you are new to Phase 10, the travel tin is a nice starter kit. If you are a long time fan, the symbols add a refreshing new dimension that still preserves the chaos of a good Phase 10 grind session.

## Accessibility in practice
The color blind deck is not a miracle fix that will turn color-blind players into mind readers of the card deck. It is, however, a thoughtful improvement that reduces the cognitive load during play and helps everyone participate more evenly. Here are some practical takeaways:
