## How to Play in 60 Seconds or Less
If you’ve ever watched a cooking show and thought, “I could totally do that,” Sushi Go! is your gateway drug to card drafting cuisine. Here’s the short version, with a little extra geek-wink for readers who enjoy the crunchy details:

### Setup
- Shuffle the deck and deal a hand of cards to each player (the exact number depends on player count), face down.
- Place the remaining deck in the center as a draw pile.
- Decide who will go first; that person becomes the first passer. The common rhythm: you pass the entire hand to the next player, pick one card from your hand, then pass the rest along. Rinse and repeat until all cards have been chosen.

### Drafting and Play
- On your turn, select one card from your hand and lay it in front of you to keep track of your choices.
- Pass the remaining cards to the next player. Your goal is to assemble a personal combination of sushi cards that will score the most points by the end of the round.
- The catch: you can only choose one card per pass, and you must keep the rest for the next player. This creates a chain of small, deliciously tough decisions that are the true heart of the game.
- End of round: tally points by following the scoring rules on the reference card. The board game vibe here is “simple, but not trivial,” which is exactly what players want when they’re escaping from real life for a half-hour.

### Scoring Cues
Sushi Go! uses a mixture of straightforward and tricky scoring lines. Nigiri cards score directly with a modest point value, but their usefulness often rises or falls based on partner cards like Wasabi or Sashimi sets. Maki rolls offer a progressive pleasure—more Maki icons on your card stack means more points when you compare totals with other players. Tempura and Sushi combos reward you with set-based bonuses; Pudding cards (in most versions) offer a long tail of scoring that can feel like a late-game boss battle if you neglect them early.

### End of Round and Next Round
Once every player has drafted all the cards in their hand, the round ends. You tabulate points, discard everything, and start a fresh deck for the next round. The number of rounds depends on player count, but in practice you’ll be tearing through rounds in about 15–25 minutes, depending on how often someone brags about not needing instructions and then has to ask for them anyway.

### Two-Player Play and House Rules
Sushi Go! plays surprisingly well with two players, but as the player count grows, the table talk escalates into delicious banter and strategic mind games. If you’re going 2-player, you can either call a time-out and simulate a two-handed drafting duel or use a slightly expanded scoring variant to keep things spicy. ## The Good, The Bad, and The Snackable
Like any snack, Sushi Go! has both bite-sized pros and the occasional crumbly edge.

### The Pros
- Quick to learn: The rules are approachable for kids but dense enough to satisfy adults who enjoy a good munch-and-score session.
- Portable: The compact deck slips into a bag or backpack as easily as a pack of wasabi packets slips into a lunchbox. It’s the perfect filler game for travel, flights, or long lines at the bank where you want to pretend you’re doing something productive.
- The drafting concept is instantly replayable: Each round brings new combos and new decisions, which means you’ll rarely fall into a single mold of optimal strategy.
- Family-friendly and accessible: It’s easy to teach, unlikely to cause heavy disagreements, and the kind of game where a kid can whoop a grown-up and feel like a tiny victory champion.

### The Cons
- The luck factor can be real: Sometimes you simply don’t get the cards you need, and there’s not much you can do about it beyond preparing for next round. This is not a flaw so much as a reality of a 15–25 minute game with a small card pool.
- Assembly line of passing: For some players, the constant passing can feel a bit fiddly, especially with the smallest hands. If you’re not into micro-management, you may want to call a quick “card swap” break mid-game to reset the rhythm.
- Theme on the table is cute, but not deeply thematic: If you’re chasing a heavy narrative or heavy strategy, Sushi Go! may feel more like a party slider than a grand epic. It’s a palate cleanser, not a multi-course feast.

### Who Should Buy This?
If you’re a busy gamer with a craving for a bright, digestible card game that doesn’t demand a full afternoon or a PhD in mathematics, Sushi Go! is your friend. It’s ideal for families, couples who like to snack while they strategize, students seeking a quick game after class, or friends who want to bond over adorable little sushi faces while pretending to be serious strategic gamers.

## Strategy Tips for Getting More Crunch per Crunch
While Sushi Go! can feel like a purely luck-of-the-draw exercise, there are thoughtful decisions you can make to improve your odds of triumph without turning your kitchen table into a math lab.

- Read the room (and the cards): Quick glances at what other players are collecting can tell you whether you’re on track to score big with a particular combo or if you should pivot to a different strategy. If everyone seems to be chasing Nigiri-heavy hands, your best move might be to collect Maki or Pudding to secure a late lead.
- Plan for end-game scoring: Items that have late-stage value, like Pudding or long-term combinations, are easy to overlook in the heat of the moment. Keep a loose eye on end-game potential so you don’t drift into a “score-everything-now” trap.
- Balance risk and reward: Some cards have immediate high-value potential (like powerful Nigiri combos), but you may regret chasing them if they starve your later rounds of important pieces. Weigh the short-term payoff against your remaining rounds.
- Don’t overcommit: The compact nature of the deck means you’ll often have to pass a card you like. It’s better to deny a strong option to an opponent than to cling to a card that doesn’t fit your overall plan.
- Adaptability wins: Your success hinges on your ability to pivot as the draft evolves. If you’re stuck in a single plan, your opponents will outpace you. Flexibility is the spice in Sushi Go!’s sushi curry.

## In-Depth: Two Quick Variants You Might Enjoy
- Two-Player Sandbox: Although the core game is designed for 2–5 players, a courteous improv version can keep the edge sharp when there are only two of you. The approach is to halve the number of cards in each hand and tweak the round count, or simply play multiple rounds with a running score. The end result is a tense, fast-moving micro-draft with more mind games than a reality show finale.
- House Rule: The Chopstick Twist: If you have a Chopstick card, you can exchange it for two cards in your hand on a later turn. It’s a small twist that can create more dynamic turns and make you feel like a sushi economist managing your “resources.”
