## How to Play with up to 10 Players
Up to ten players means more laughter, more gasps, and more dramatic pauses when the bomb approaches. The core mechanic remains the same: you draw cards, use action cards to save yourself, pass blame, or blow up your neighbors. The Party Pack adds rules and space so that everyone has a fair shot at turning the tide before the last cat blinks.

- Seat order matters less than your ability to bluff, feign surprise, and memorize which players have been eliminated. Keep track of who has a defuse card and who has used up a skip that could bite them later
- Turn structure remains simple: draw a card, play any number of action cards, and decide whether to risk a draw from the cat stack or target a neighbor. The social dynamics are where the magic hides in between the cards
- The bigger group means more opportunities for misdirection. A clever plan could involve convincing a player to draw from a specific pile, or orchestrating a chain reaction where a player uses a defuse at the worst moment for someone else

Setup is the most critical part. Before you start, lay out the tempo and set expectations. The EK system thrives on quick rounds and rapid escalation. In a 10 player game, you need a loud timer and a calm, polite reminder to keep things moving. If you have ever tried to run a meeting with more than eight people where someone keeps muttering about agenda items, you already know what EK at scale feels like—only with more cats and fewer PowerPoint slides.

If you are new to the EK universe, fear not. The Party Pack is designed to ramp you up gradually. Start with 2-3 players to learn the rhythm, then invite more friends who will shout at the screen during the penalties. The table becomes a dramatic stage where each player plays their hand with a flourish worthy of a televised improv show. ## Variant Modes and House Rules
The Party Pack is flexible, and you can tailor the chaos to your crowd. If you prefer speed, run a sprint variant where rounds are shorter and the cat cards are clipped to avoid long windups. If you want to lean into betrayal, introduce designate-the-defuser where players can swap defuse cards in a high stakes negotiation. If your crew loves roleplay, add flavor text to each card or declare your own nonsense rules for dramatic effect. Remember, the point of Exploding Kittens is to create shared jokes, not to break the table or cause actual harm to your friends.

Here are a few ideas that work well in practice:

- The Coordinated Panic Rule: every time someone uses a defuse, everyone must raise a glass or a cup and shout a silly victory phrase. It’s the social glue that keeps the table engaged during longer sessions
- The Cat Basket: assign each cat card to a player, who must maintain a running commentary about their pulse while the card is in play. It’s silly and surprisingly effective at diffusing tension
- The Spectator Edit: for players who are waiting their turn, appoint a designated narrator who provides running commentary about who might explode next. The narrator can influence decisions by adding dramatic effect, not real strategy

If you want more concrete house rules to try, check our sketched variants in the linked post. Also consider peeking at official guidelines on the EK site for additional ideas and cautionary notes about keeping things friendly and safe for players of all ages.

## Strategy and Tips for Ten Players
The larger the table, the more strategies you can deploy, but you also face more noise, more opinions, and more chances for a rogue card to go rogue in your face. Here are practical tips to maximize fun and minimize table chaos:

- Observe the table dynamics. In a crowd you will notice who trades information for power, who is always a threat, and who is simply there to enjoy the chaos. Use this to your advantage when selecting targets for action cards
- Keep a mental count of defuse cards and bombs. It is not foolproof, but it helps you time your defuse moments and avoid chasing the wrong swing card
- Use cards to control tempo. If you sense a big moment coming up, save a few defuse or skip cards for the right moment to either survive or knock someone else out of the game
- Don t forget to manage the real social tension. The biggest threat in EK is the fear of the next draw, not the next card. The drama comes from waiting for the reveal and the reactions when the bomb lands
- Manage table talk. A loud, playful voice is not a distraction; it is a strategy tool. You want to craft a scenario where your opponents misread your intent and waste valuable cards

A good starting point for 10 players is a short first round, a quick adaptation of rules for newcomers, and then a gentle ramp up. Bump the deck size a little to keep pace, then add your own house rules for laughter during the latter stages of the game. The Party Pack makes this kind of expansion easy by offering a ready to play deck that supports a large group without constant rule lookups

## A Quick Notes on Setup and Logistics
While the standard setup for EK is fast, the Party Pack requires a touch more planning to maximize fun. Here are quick steps to run a smooth session:

1) Clear a large table. Ten players require each person to claim at least a cushion seat and a clear line of sight to the center of the action. If you are playing in a living room, consider moving coffee tables and clearing snacks into a dedicated corner so the game box has breathing room

2) Do a quick demo round. The first round is a hell of a show; the second round is where you lock the rules into memory. Make sure everyone understands the defuse mechanic and what happens when a bomb hits the table

3) Assign a moderator. In a ten player game, someone should help keep the pace, remind players of the rules, and maintain a light, fun atmosphere. This is not the time to test your memorization of every card; a quick reminder is often enough to keep the game moving

4) Use house rules with caution. A good house rule can speed things up, but too many rules can ruin the magic. Start simple and gradually layer complexity if you have the energy and the attention span to manage it

5) Consider a time cap. If you want to protect your evening from becoming a marathon, set a time limit and a soft end. You will still get the chaos you wanted, but with a controlled arc that doesn t threaten your sleep schedule
