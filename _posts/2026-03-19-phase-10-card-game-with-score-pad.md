## What is Phase 10 and what’s in this Mattel package?

Phase 10 is a card game with a built in progression. It borrows from rummy and dances with a to-do list of challenges to complete. The objective is simple on the surface: complete 10 distinctive phases before your peers. Each phase is a specific combination of cards, such as two sets of four, a run of five, or a mix of both. The catch? You must complete the phase exactly as written to move on to the next one. It sounds easy until you realize there are little traps tucked into each hand that can derail your plan faster than you can say phase ten.

Mattel’s Phase 10 card game with a score pad adds a tangible tally to the chaos. The box includes the standard deck of Phase 10 cards, a card guide that explains the permissible phase patterns, and the score pad, which is neither optional nor decorative. The score pad is the neat twist here: it lets you keep a running total across rounds, which adds a layer of strategy beyond simply finishing the phase first. If you have ever played a game night that devolves into a numbers war, the score pad is your notepad of doom—also your lifeline if your patience is wearing thin.

## The score pad: why it matters and how it behaves in practice

The score pad is the star of this version. It tracks players, phases completed, and the cumulative points across rounds. In practice, it works like this: after each round, players tally points based on their remaining cards and the phase outcomes. The person who completes their phase first does not automatically win the round—points can swing based on who finishes early but also who finishes with fewer penalty cards in their hand. The score pad creates a scoreboard narrative that follows players beyond a single round and into the next, turning a simple race into a series of micro-dramas.

Pros of the score pad:

- Encourages long-term strategy: do you chase a high-scoring phase that might leave you with a handful of cards, or do you finish a low-risk phase and reduce your risk for the next round?
- Adds accountability: you cannot pretend you forgot you still owe three points from a card you discarded in round two.
- Visual feedback: seeing your score creep up (or down) provides a satisfying sense of momentum, even if your tablemates are too busy arguing about whether the third wild card should count as a six or a seven.

Cons of the score pad:

- It can feel a little heavy for younger players who simply want to finish their phase and drop the mic.
- If you have a large group, the score tallying can stretch a round longer than a normal conversation about memes.
- The pad’s layout sometimes requires finger gymnastics to locate where pen marks should go, especially if you are trying to write while dodging a hand of cards being shuffled around your face.

In short, the score pad does not just exist as a ledger; it acts as a narrative device. It gives your group something to talk about after each round, something to argue about, and something to measure your personal growth as a gamer who somehow still enjoys a family-friendly thing after a long day at the office where you looked at spreadsheets for eight hours straight.

## Gameplay overview: setup, phases, and turns

### Setup

- Shuffle the deck and deal ten cards to each player. The rest of the deck forms a draw pile; the top card is flipped to start a discard pile.
- Each player keeps track of their own phase sheet (which should be obvious on the score pad). In our testing, a quiet moment where the group acknowledges the phase list can prevent chaos in round one when people forget whether they are aiming for two sets of four or a sequence of five.
- The first player starts by drawing a card and discarding one. The action continues clockwise. The phrase that defines Phase 10 is simple: you either complete the phase or you don’t. If you complete your phase, you move on to the next phase in the sequence. If you cannot complete your phase, you draw and discard again until you can, or the round ends when the draw pile runs out.

### Phases overview

The 10 phases form a roadmap that players must follow. Each phase has a clear set of requirements, and the challenge lies in executing them while also preventing opponents from stealing your thunder with well-timed discards or strategic skipping cards.

- Phase 1: two sets of four
- Phase 2: one set of four plus one run of four
- Phase 3: one set of four plus one run of five
- Phase 4: one run of seven
- Phase 5: one run of seven plus one pair
- Phase 6: one run of eight
- Phase 7: one run of nine
- Phase 8: two sets of five
- Phase 9: one set of five plus one set of four
- Phase 10: one set of four plus one run of four

If you have played the original Phase 10, you will recognize these as the classic nine plus the ten. The trick is to navigate them without giving away your path to the finish while also exploiting the opportunities that come from your opponents’ misses.

### Turn structure

On each turn: draw a card, decide whether to keep it or not, arrange your cards to visually reflect your current phase, and discard. If you discard a card that helps someone else finish their phase, you will hear about it—usually in the form of gentle sarcasm or celebratory jeers depending on your group’s vibe. The core tension is in whether you should go for a risky high-value card that might help your own progress or protect yourself by discarding something your opponents would rarely want.

### Scoring rules

At the end of each round, players tally up their cards that remain in hand. The score pad records these penalties for the round, and a completed phase typically yields fewer points than getting stuck holding cards while everyone else whizzes through the ten-phase list. The eventual winner is the one who completes all ten phases with the smallest total score. If you enjoy drama, you will enjoy watching the scoreboard swing as players chase confidence and confidence wavers when the cards refuse to cooperate.

## Strategy and tips to maximize your Phase 10 experience

### Early phase planning

If you can map out the first three phases in your head, you are already ahead of the curve. The trick is to avoid getting blindsided by a big run that you cannot finish in time. Start by focusing on the phases that require fewer cards or that grant you early control of the board. If your cards look like a mess, do not panic. Phase 10 is a long game with ten milestones; sometimes the best move is to bank a decent but safe phase to secure a foothold before the storm hits.

### When to hold vs run

There is a moment in every Phase 10 game when you have to decide between a fast finish and a longer con of a run that could net you more points but costs you a lot of risk. If your opponents are close behind, you might want to push for a quick finish to minimize their scoring damage. If you have a comfortable lead, you can take calculated risks, since the score pad will tell you whether you can afford a few extra cards in hand without painting a big red target on your back.

### Blocking opponents

The suspense of Phase 10 often comes from the interplay between players who discard cards that could help someone else. Your job is to balance finishing your phase with not giving your rivals a perfect card that advances them. Discarding a wild card is a double-edged sword: it can ruin someone else’s plan if they crouch to grab it, but it can also foil your own run if you rely on that exact card for your own phase. The general rule is to be mindful of what is left in the deck; the more cards that can complete a phase, the more strategic your discards must become.
