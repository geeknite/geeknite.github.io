## Painting Setup: Palette, Tools, and First Pass

If you’re about to embark on this piece, here’s a practical setup list that keeps you sane:

- A base coat of a mid-gray or pale tan to create a neutral undercoat that helps light reflect without washing out the details.
- White or off-white primary coat for the snowy sections; consider mixing in a touch of blue-gray for depth.
- A limited metallic palette for the M203: gunmetal, silver, and a hint of rust for weathering.
- A wash for shading: cool gray or a blue-gray wash to push the recesses into shadow without making everything look blue-trozen.
- Snow effects: white pigment powders or a white acrylic with a splash of gloss medium for icy sparkle; for tracked snow you can dry-brush with pure white and then glaze with a tiny touch of diluted white glue.
- Varnish: a matte satin-minimal gloss spray to protect the finish once you’re satisfied.
- Brushes: a couple of fine detail brushes for the M203 and a broader brush for coats and the base.

The first pass should be a clean base in the mid-gray family so the white bits can sit on top and still have room to breathe. Remember: in monochrome snow schemes, the contrast lives in the tonal differences, not color swings. That means your lights shouldn’t be pure white right away; they should read as a pale, wind-burnished gray or bone-white so you can push shadows into the nooks with glazes and washes without losing the feel of the piece.

To give you a sense of how this plays with light, a quick lighting tip: place your light slightly to the left and a bit above; this will create gentle shadows along the scarf edge and under the hood that help the texture read as carved ice rather than plastic. The goal here is to create a narrative that the snow has been compressed by the zombie’s weight, and that the metal of the M203 has seen a few seasons of corrosion and weather.

## Step-by-Step Painting: From First Coat to Finished Glow

1) Basecoat and underpainting
- Apply a neutral gray basecoat to the body and a slightly lighter gray for the armor and clothing. The snow portions get a crisp off-white.
- Use a thin brush to define creases and folds; don’t get too heavy-handed with the lines—subtlety is your friend here.

2) Monochrome shading
- Apply a cool gray wash to the recesses, focusing under the arms, the base of the legs, and the underside of the scarf. This pushes depth without introducing color shifts.
- If you see any warmer tones peeking through, it’s a sign you need another light glaze of cool gray. The goal is a palette that feels cold rather than beige.

3) Snow base and texture
- The white sections are not pure white at this stage. You want a slightly blue-tinged white for the base to mimic winter light. A light dry-brush of off-white on the raised areas adds dimension.
- For snowfall accumulation, dab on a light snow paste or static grass with white pigment to create a fluffy, powdery texture on shoulders and helmet rims.

4) M203 metallics and weathering
- The M203 should be a focal point with a metallic undercoat. Start with chainmail or gunmetal, then highlight with silver on the edges that catch the light.
- Weathering can be achieved with a tiny bit of brown wash around edges and an intentionally placed rust speck here and there. You don’t want a rusty zombie; you want a zombie that has witnessed the long winter of battle.

5) Final glaze and highlights
- A micro-glaze of very pale blue or cool gray across the raised surfaces helps unify the piece and keeps the snow from looking flat.
- Dry brush a final white highlight on the most elevated surfaces to mimic ice sparkles catching the light.

6) Base and scene cohesion
- The base is where a lot of the “story” lives. A snowy, icy base with rock elements and footprints can anchor the figure. Add subtle shading at the base where the snow meets the rocks and a thin wash along edges to suggest ground shadow.

## Lighting, Contrast, and Tonal Range in Monochrome Snow

In a snow palette, tonal range is king. You want the highlights to glow without becoming a beacon of glare. A practical approach is to split the figure into three ranges: shadow shadows, mid-tones, and highlight edges. The shadow areas (underarms, inner folds, and the lower belt) get a blue-gray shade or a cooler gray wash. Mid-tones stay within the grayscale family but avoid pure mid-gray; introduce a touch of the lighter mid-tone to keep things cohesive. Highlights on the raised surfaces—the cheeks, the top of the hood, the top lip of the M203—get a brighter white with a hint of cool blue mixed in to simulate frosted light.

One trick we love for this scale is glazing. A wafer-thin glaze of a slightly blue-tinted white, brushed carefully across the edges, can push the sculpture into a more cinematic frosty feel. It’s a subtle technique, but your display shelf will thank you with a sigh of relief whenever someone steps back to admire the piece without squinting.

## Assembly Tips: Fit, Gaps, and Handling the M203 Accessory

The M203 is a chunky piece and, as with many accessory components, the fit can require a touch of patience. A tiny amount of green stuff can help bridge the seam where the launcher connects to the arm. Sanding the contact surfaces lightly before assembly reduces the risk of a visible line. If you’re using super glue, apply a small bead and hold the connection for a few seconds to ensure a secure epoxy-like bond without warping the pose.

If you’re building this for a display piece, consider sealing the model before you start the weathering and snow effects. A clear matte or satin seal helps keep the paint and powders from dissolving into a melted mess when you’re brimming with enthusiasm at 3 AM. And yes, it happens to all of us.
