## 3D Doors: The Real Doors to Victory (and Injury to Spoilers)

Let’s talk about the elephant doors in the room: 3D doors. These aren’t cute little cardboard flaps; they’re sturdy plastic hulks that add verticality to your dungeon crawls. They have the heft of a small weapon and the clearance of a well-loved table edge. When you slam one of these babies shut on a cluster of nosy zombies, you’ll feel the difference between “mangy cardboard” and “that door could stop a speeding halberd.”

Pros:
- Durability: You won’t tear them during a dramatic “open door, all zombies charge” moment.
- Aesthetics: They look rad on stream-cut camera angles and in photo mode.
- Tactile satisfaction: There’s something delicious about hearing that plastic click as you seal a doorway.

Cons:
- Setup time: They require a bit more patience than flat cardboard doors. If you’re the fumbly type with model-kit credentials, you’ll respect the process.
- Storage: They take up space. If your play area doubles as a storage rack, you’ll need to plan for the extra bulk.

In practice, the 3D doors shift the pacing a notch. Rooms feel more dramatic; the fear of “What’s behind this door?” becomes a physical, audible element rather than a vague anxiety about a cardboard rectangle. You’ll find yourself debating door placement with your teammates mid-round, which is either excellent coordination practice or a crafty excuse to procrastinate on closing a door during a critical round. Either way, it’s entertaining.

If you want to gauge broader sentiment about 3D doors, the general response tends to be: “Yes, please, more of these in future editions.” It’s not a polarizing topic. People like doors that don’t fold under the weight of undead exoskeletons. And yes, they’re shiny. Shiny is good.

## Plastic Tokens: A Small Step for Tokens, A Huge Step for Tabletop Fun

Plastic tokens replace several of the older, flatter chits. They add texture, weight, and a satisfying audible cue when you flip them to reveal a new status effect or mission objective. The tokens are sturdy, colorful, and likely to survive a few accidental spills (or a cat’s curious whisker—the real enemy in most households).

Advantages:
- Easier readability during heated rounds: you don’t squint at a tiny icon to confirm a survivor’s status.
- Reduced wear on cards and boards: the tokens handle the heavy lifting and the occasional “two-player stacking” moments much better.
- Aesthetic consistency with the 3D doors and miniatures; everything feels unified, which matters when you’re trying to explain to a group of friends why your board looks like a small, fancy museum of medieval zombie warfare.

The primary caveat with tokens is storage and organization. If your current solution is a shoebox labeled “Misc,” you’ll want something a tad more organized. It’s not a dealbreaker, but a good insert or small tackle box will keep the tokens from becoming a scavenger hunt mid-game.
