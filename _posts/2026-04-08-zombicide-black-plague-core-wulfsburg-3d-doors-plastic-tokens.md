## Overview

Zombicide Black Plague is the fantasy-molded cousin of the zombie apocalypse genre. It drops you into a grim medieval world where heroes swing axes, axes swing back, and the undead keep knocking at the door—literally, sometimes with 3D doors. The Core Box provides the base game: an array of survivors, a swarm of walkers, some armored zombies, and mission after mission to chain into a long night of cooperative chaos. The Wulfsburg expansion scales that chaos up with new scenarios, more survivors, and, you guessed it, wolves that behave badly when the map tiles aren’t gridded to your liking.

This particular edition review includes a keen look at two big hardware upgrades you get in the box: 3D doors and plastic tokens. If you’ve ever muttered, “I could make nicer doors out of foam core at home,” then you’re going to smile a sly smile while placing a 3D door that actually stands up and clacks satisfying when you slam it shut on a wave of zombie reinforcements.

> Quick tip: if you’re new to Zombicide, don’t skip the tutorial missions. They’re not just a warmup; they’re a ritual that convinces you that your friends are capable—just maybe not as capable as the rad 3D doors imply.

## What’s in the Box: Core + Wulfsburg

The Core Box is a compact siege engine of cardboard, plastic, and the occasional moment of dread. It contains:

- A full faction of survivors with color-coded bases and plastic miniatures
- A good pile of zombies: walkers, runners, and the occasional screamer for extra mischief
- Cards for weapons, equipment, and mission objectives
- Rulebooks, reference sheets, and enough dice to start your own casino night in a gymnasium
- The promised 3D doors (in this edition, they are a feature to be carefully assembled and placed)
- Plastic tokens that replace some of the older cardboard chits

The Wulfsburg expansion slides into the mix with:

- New survivors and new mission scenarios
- Additional enemies and special objectives that bring a different tempo to the table
- A new tile layout that can be merged with the Core board in a variety of ways
- Your standard rulebook addendum, clarifications, and a few delightful pain points to discuss for weeks after the last zombie is cleared

The sheer physical heft is a delightful reminder that you’re not playing a “soft” tabletop game; you’re coordinating a team of medieval badasses who occasionally forget to close doors and keep their shields up. And yes, the 3D doors alone will spark at least three “Oh wow” moments per session.

## 3D Doors: The Real Doors to Victory (and Injury to Spoilers)

Let’s talk about the elephant doors in the room: 3D doors. These aren’t cute little cardboard flaps; they’re sturdy plastic monstrosities that add verticality to your dungeon crawls. They have the heft of a small weapon and the clearance of a well-loved table edge. When you slam one of these babies shut on a cluster of nosy zombies, you’ll feel the difference between “mangy cardboard” and “man, that door could stop a speeding halberd.”

Pros:
- Durability: You won’t tear them during a dramatic “open door, all zombies charge” moment.
- Aesthetics: They look rad on stream-cut camera angles and in photo mode.
- Tactile satisfaction: There’s something delicious about hearing that plastic click as you secure a doorway.

Cons:
- Setup time: They require a little more patience than flat cardboard doors. If you’re the kind who fumbles with model kits, you’ll respect the process.
- Storage: They take up space. If your play area doubles as a storage rack, you’ll need to plan for the extra bulk.

In practice, the 3D doors shift the pacing a bit. Rooms feel more dramatic; the fear of “What’s behind this door?” becomes a more physical, audible element rather than a vague anxiety about a cardboard rectangle. You’ll find yourself discussing door placement with your teammates mid-round, which is either excellent coordination practice or an excuse to procrastinate on breaking a critical door during a critical round. Either way, it’s entertaining.

If you want to see how other players felt about 3D doors, the general response tends to be: “Yes, please, more of these in future editions.” It’s not a controversial stance. People like doors that don’t fold under heavy zombie exoskeletons. Also, they’re shiny. Shiny is good.

## Plastic Tokens: A Small Step for Tids, A Huge Step for Tabletops

Plastic tokens replace several of the older, flatter chits. They add texture, weight, and a satisfying audible cue when you flip them over to reveal a new status effect or a mission objective. The tokens are sturdy, colorful, and likely to survive a few accidental spills (or a cat’s curious whisker, which is the real enemy in most households).

Advantages include:
- Easier readability during heated rounds: you don’t squint at a tiny icon to confirm a survivor’s status.
- Reduced wear on cards and boards: the tokens handle the heavy lifting and briefer “two-player stacking” moments much better.
- Aesthetic consistency with the 3D doors and miniatures; everything feels unified, which matters when you’re trying to explain to a group of friends why your board looks like a small, fancy museum of medieval zombie warfare.

The primary caveat with tokens is storage and organization. If your current storage solution is a shoebox labeled “Misc,” you’ll want to upgrade to something a touch more organized. It’s not a dealbreaker by any means, but a good insert or small tackle box will keep the tokens from becoming a scavenger hunt mid-game.

## Gameplay Impressions: Core Mechanics Meet Wulfsburg’s Twist

If you’re already versed in Zombicide, you know the formula. A team of survivors fights off an ever-spawning zombie horde with dice-driven actions, weapon cards, and the occasional “clear a room” meme that becomes an inner circle of howling excitement every round. The Core Box maintains this rhythm with a steady beat: group coordination, dice rolls, and the constant threat of being overwhelmed by more zombies than you have actions to deal with.

Wulfsburg doesn’t rewrite the rules so much as it adds new notes to the same song. You get new missions that feel distinct from the base scenarios, requiring different tactics, choke points, and a willingness to split up or group up in creative ways. It’s not a total overhaul; it’s more of a playlist update: same core mechanics, but more tracks to jam to while you fight off undead werewolves (or werewolf-adjacent threats, depending on your memory and the map you’re playing on).

A few tactical takeaways:
- Territory control matters. The more you secure doors and choke points, the more you’ll delay the zombie wave and reduce overrun risk.
- Resource management is king. The deck of equipment and weapon cards can swing a mission from “we’re in trouble” to “we’re gold-plated heroes” in a few turns.
- Do not neglect the outs. In Zombicide, sometimes the best plan is to pivot: a quick fallback to a safer room, a sprint to a scouted objective, or a strategic sacrifice to save a stronger teammate for the finale.

The synergy between Core and Wulfsburg is where this set shines. The new tiles and scenarios create a layer of replayability that keeps the game fresh after multiple sessions. If you’ve hit a plateau with the base game’s campaigns, this expansion pack is likely to rekindle your love for the grim, gothic vibe of Black Plague without losing the familiar rhythm you enjoy.

## Setup, Playtime, and Table Presence

Setup time is a factor in any boxed set with deluxe components. The Core Box plus Wulfsburg can be assembled into a playable state in roughly 15-25 minutes for a clean, no-stress session, assuming you’ve labeled the pieces and aren’t auditioning new music in the background. With the 3D doors and tokens, you’ll likely spend a little longer on assembly, but the payoff is shorter in actual game time because you’re not staring at cardboard pieces trying to figure out where to place a tokenboard.

Playtime per session varies with player count and mission complexity. A typical 60-90 minute game night can stretch into 2-3 hours if you decide to chase a multi-stage campaign. The game scales well from 1 to 6 players, which makes it a rare “any size group” winner for a co-op game night. The more players you have, the more you’ll laugh at the chaos of dice and the more careful you’ll be about door placement—perfect for friends who want to argue about strategy and then immediately execute it with surprising synergy.

## Replayability and Modularity

What I like about Core + Wulfsburg is the modular embrace. You can mix and match mission tiles from both sets to create custom campaigns. It’s not just that you can run the same two or three missions endlessly; you can craft your own sequences, which is a delight for players who enjoy puzzle-solving under zombie pressure.

Here are a few ideas for maximizing replayability:
- Create your own “boss room” scenarios using the 3D doors as part of the final objective multi-step. The physical presence of a sealed door adds narrative weight to the climactic moments.
- Run a rotating survivor roster. Rotate players in and out, with each new player taking a fresh set of gear cards. It changes how your group negotiates risk and incentives.
- Try variant difficulty modes. If you’re a veteran, raise the bar with house rules for zombie spawn rates or introduce a countdown timer to simulate a “city-wide evacuation” panic.
