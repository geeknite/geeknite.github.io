# Zombicide Special Guest Edouard: A Glorious Box of Zom-banter

Unboxings are like first dates: you tell promising lies about the chemistry, you hope the surprise at the bottom of the box isn’t a functional fuse, and you pretend that you’re totally calm while your pulse tries to audition for a heart-attack remix. Today we’re opening a sealed box from CMON: Zombicide Special Guest Edouard, designed with Edouard Guiton’s signature flair. If you’re unfamiliar, Edouard is the kind of guest designer who shows up with a coffee mug that reads, “I sculpted more hobbits than your grandma baked cookies.” In other words, a creator who brings not just models but personality. Does the game inside live up to the guest-star hype, or is this a cameo that should have stayed on the cutting-room floor? Read on, dear reader, for a geeky, giggly, slightly obsessive teardown.

## Unboxing and First Impressions

### The Box Feel and Theme Shake

Right away, the box exudes CMON’s familiar mix of glossy art and brisk, no-nonsense layout. The cover art features Edouard’s signature style: a little more gloss on the minis, a little more attitude in the zombie silhouettes, and a cheeky wink in the typography. If you’ve seen Edouard’s other work, you’ll recognize that cruelly precise shading and a knack for giving everything a dash of cinematic menace. The packaging is sturdy enough to survive the modern-day apocalypse—by which I mean the weekly curbside recycling run, not the actual undead apocalypse, which this box is far more prepared to handle than I am at carrying it up stairs.

Inside, the content arrangement is efficient: rulebook on top, followed by the new miniatures, tokens, dashboard overlays, and a few plastic trays that jam every time you shut the lid as a friendly reminder that you really should have kept the box flat on the table. The color palette leans toward Edouard’s preferred grays and earth tones with splashes of crimson that aren’t just eye candy; they’re callouts for where to poke your play sessions when the alarm goes off and you realize your dice have betrayed you again.

### The Build: Components at a Glance

The box includes a typical CMON quality check: sturdy miniatures, thick citadel-grade plastic, and crisp, sculpted detail. The new set features a handful of hero miniatures with alternate sculpts that proudly display Edouard’s design fingerprints. You’ll notice cleaner armor plates, more aggressive postures, and weapons that look like they mean business—like they heard you say, “I’m only going to roll one critical hit today,” and responded with a scoff that says, “Not while I’m here.” The zombie cast feels punchier, with distinct silhouettes to keep track of who’s who during a chaotic late game. The card stock for equipment and mission tiles is solid; you won’t ruin a play session by bending a cardboard overlay while you stare at the grimace on a zombie’s face.

![Zombicide Edouard new minis]( {{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/zombicide-edouard-minis.jpg )

If you’ve opened a Zombicide box before, you’ll notice a few changes that signal a slightly different design language: fewer pieces per character, more clearly labeled action icons, and the presence of a couple of new mechanics that feel like a natural extension rather than a radical pivot. The tokens are clear, the dice feel satisfyingly clacky, and the plastic components snap together with enough resistance to feel premium without requiring a medieval blacksmith’s biceps to assemble.

## What’s Inside: The Box Contents in Depth

### The Rulebook and Scenarii

The rulebook is a concise but dense package. It doesn’t threaten you with room-sized paragraphs; instead, it sticks to the crucial bits, which is exactly how I like my zombie games: lean on rules, heavy on action. The scenarios section is where Edouard’s influence shines. Several new mission templates appear, designed to leverage the table-covering zombie density that CMON games tend to encourage. The scenarios emphasize timed objectives, cooperative coordination, and a dash of narrative flavor that makes you feel like you’re steering a blockbuster zombie flick rather than simply exterminating undead cannon fodder.

### The Minis: Champions, Survivors, and the Things That Haunt the Box

As mentioned, Edouard’s guest-stamped minis are the headline. They’re not merely “new models”—they’re a demonstration that a guest designer can push a long-running engine into new emotional territory. The survivors stand with bold silhouettes and dynamic poses, while a few of the zombies look almost comically menacing—the kind of design where a stumble becomes a horror moment on the table. The paint job is clean; if you’re one of those players who paints every miniature to showpiece status, you’ll be thrilled with the base sculpting and ready-to-paint textures.

### Cards, Tokens, and Theater of War

New equipment cards add some spicy synergy: weapons that unlock short bursts of advantage but trade those advantages for apparently the most inconvenient charges to reload. The overlay boards and tokens are crisp, easy to read, and arranged so that new players won’t spend five minutes staring at a token and wondering if it’s a trap card or a medal. In short: CMON’s (and Edouard’s) team did not ship a bag of gimmicks with a few shiny minis; they shipped a coherent bag of tricks that you can actually use in a game night instead of one that sits on a shelf collecting dust until a friend’s birthday happens to coincide with a zombie apocalypse movie marathon.

### A Quick Personal Playthrough Note

If you’re the type who gleefully reads every card front-to-back before a game, you’ll appreciate the clarity of the new action icons and the minimal “oops, I forgot what that symbol means” moments. The design team has clearly tested the flow of play under time pressure, which is a big win for games that promise not to turn your living room into a logistics hub for a small army of undead figurines. While not every twist lands perfectly for every group, the improvements feel purposeful rather than ornamental.

## Edouard Guiton’s Touch: The Guest Designer’s Imprint

Edouard is known in the community for injecting a bold visual style and a penchant for high-tension moments into his projects. In this Zombicide edition, his influence is felt in three key places:

1) Thematic cohesion: The survivors have a narrative through-line that’s easy to thread into a “you vs. the horde” arc. This isn’t a random assortment of zombies and neon cards; it’s a lived-in world where each mission tells a story with rising stakes. 
2) Visual storytelling: The miniatures’ poses and basing choices create cinematic tableaus. You can almost stage a quick stop-motion reel after a decisive turn—proof that board games can be as photogenic as they are strategic. 
3) Mechanic texture: There are little mechanical prompts that feel designed to complement Edouard’s flair for dramatic moments—like synergy-enriching combos that trigger in the late game, giving weary players a moment of triumphant narrative payoff.

If you’re a fan of Edouard’s other work, you’ll smile at these touches. If you’re new to his style, you’ll still get that sense of “this isn’t a generic zombie crush-fest”; it’s a product curated with a storyteller’s beat, not just a sculptor’s skill.

## Rules and Gameplay: What’s New, What’s Familiar, and What It Means for You

### Core Mechanics Retained
Zombicide stays faithful to its core: team-based, action-queue gameplay with a dice-chuck element, where each turn you optimize moves to maximize your survival odds while the horde piles on. The core rules need little re-learn for veterans, which is a big plus for a box that might lure back lapsed players and new players alike. If you know the baseline game, you’ll jump in and start farming zombies with fewer “how do I even start?” moments.

### New Bits and How They Shift the Pace
There are a few new mechanics designed to offer late-game micro-dramas. A couple of “leap” actions allow survivors to cover ground more quickly, but that speed sometimes triggers more zombies in range. This creates a fun push-pull rhythm: you want to sprint, but you’re leaving your allies behind or triggering a spike in aggression from the undead. The scenario design clearly leans into this, giving players moments where a single decision pivots the entire game state. It’s not revolutionary, but it’s a tasteful evolution that feels consistent with Edouard’s penchant for dramatic storytelling.

### Difficulty and Accessibility

For newcomers, the new materials are approachable and the rulebook remains clear and friendly. For seasoned Zombicide fans, there’s enough nuance to reward multiple plays without feeling like you’re replaying the same mission on a different map. The difficulty curve is smartly tuned to preserve tension without tipping into “unplayable with a cold start.” If you’re a “hardcore grognard” who enjoys the grind, you’ll probably house-rule a few bits to fit your group’s speed, but you’ll still appreciate the puzzle of the new mechanics.

### Replayability and Longevity

This edition shines in its replay potential. The new sculpts and scenario templates give you a dozen distinct play patterns before you even start mixing in old boxes and expansions. The variety helps clubs and families alike avoid the “we have seen it all” malaise. If you’re into collecting, Edouard’s box offers a sense of continuity with the designer’s other works while still standing firmly on its own feet.

## Components Quality: A Clarity Check for Your Tabletop

The physical quality of this box is a reaffirmation of CMON’s reputation for producing robust, visually engaging games. The miniatures are cleanly cast with crisp details; the plastic is sturdy; the card stock is thick enough to survive a few careless shuffles without showing wear early on. The base inserts keep everything organized, which is crucial for a game with a healthy number of components. Assembly time is short, wittily enough; you can get from “box opened” to “fight your way out of a horde” without needing a pre-lecture on how to assemble the army’s battle lines.

The art on the cards and boards carries the Edouard stamp—dramatic lighting, moody color choices, and a few cheeky panels of humor that remind you to enjoy the chaos rather than fear it. The box’s interior layout is more intuitive than the last few Zombicide releases I’ve opened, which means fewer “where did I put that hex tile again?” detours mid-match. If you’ve got a bevy of friends who treat setup as a solemn ritual, you’ll appreciate the speed with which you can get a group into the action.

## The Visuals: Art, Miniatures, and The Mood They Create

The cover art pops with a cinematic glow and Edouard’s signature shadow play; the interior art carries that mood into the table with purposeful, mood-boosting detail. The zombies are varied enough to keep the table visually interesting, and the survivors’ sculpts capture expressive moment frames—like a small action-film montage in three dimensions. For a game that’s as much about storytelling as it is about rolling bones, this visual package makes the table feel alive. It’s not just about “how many zombies can we fit on this square” but about the scene you’re creating together with your friends.

If you’re the sort who takes photos mid-game (hands still smeared with plastic glue and coffee rings on the table), you’ll have a field day: the minis photograph beautifully under standard living-room lighting, and the character ensembles help you narrate who’s got what to lose when the bite hits the fan.

## Value, Longevity, and Group Engagement

Does a box like this justify its price tag? In short: yes, for the right audience. If you’re a die-hard Zombicide fan who already owns most of the core set and expansions, Edouard’s edition is a “must-have” upgrade that adds bite and atmosphere as much as it adds fresh play options. If you’re more of a casual gamer who collects boxes for the shelf appeal, you’ll still get an enjoyable play experience here—though you may not feel compelled to squeeze in 20+ plays before moving on to the next big thing.

The replayability is reinforced by the mission variety and the modular map design. The new scenarios scale reasonably, so a group that’s comfortable with mid-level difficulty can keep the session engaging without needing to hire a professional mathematics tutor to optimize every turn. If you run a regular game night, you’ll likely return to Edouard’s edition for a few campaigns, then mix in with other Zombicide boxes to keep the puzzle fresh.

For table space, this box requires a modest footprint. It’s not the hugest box on the shelf, but it’s not a travel-lite variant either. If you’re bringing it to a club or a convention, you’ll want a dedicated play surface to keep the map pristine and the tokens in their proper lanes. The rule-of-thumb: more space equals more dramatic zombie annihilation, and this box rewards a big, honest table.

## Comparisons: How It Stacks Against Other Zombicide Sets

Compared to the core Zombicide boxes, Edouard’s edition leans more into cinematic storytelling and designer flair. The game remains faithful to the core mechanics that long-time players love, but the integration of new tools and sharper components adds a layer of polish that can feel like a fresh coat of paint on a familiar car. If you own multiple Zombicide chapters, you’ll appreciate how this edition slots into your collection as a high-quality, thematically rich add-on rather than a standalone marvel you’ll admire from a distance.

That said, if you’re not a fan of guest-designed editions, you could find the difference subtle. The appeal here is that Edouard’s signature style isn’t an overbearing gimmick; it’s a natural enhancement that improves theme, table presence, and the “story moment” feel without sacrificing the careful rhythm of the base game’s turns.

## The Final Verdict: Should You Buy It?

- If you crave narrative-driven zombie chaos with a designer’s touch, this edition delivers with gusto. The minis are stylish, the scenarios are well-paced, and the production quality respects your table space and time.
- If you prefer ultra-tight, math-heavy tactical games with near-zero narrative fluff, you might prefer to dip your toes into a few plays rather than diving into a full campaign.
- If you’re somewhere in between, you’ll probably fall in love with Edouard’s style and the way it reshapes the mood of your Zombicide nights without sacrificing the core gameplay that keeps you coming back.

The product lands with more “oomph” than the average sequel and still respects the family of players who adore the franchise. It’s not a radical departure; it’s a confident, stylish upgrade that enhances cohesion, plays smoothly, and gives you a couple of new tricks to keep the apocalyptic party lively.

## Final Thoughts and Recommendations

- Best for: Players who enjoy a dash of cinematic drama, fans of Edouard Guiton, and groups looking for a solidly produced, story-forward zombie experience. Families and clubs with a regular rotation of players will benefit from the streamlined setup and clearer icons.
- Not ideal for: Pure optimizers who want nothing more than raw numbers and minimal theme; players who already own every edition and don’t want to mix old content with new rules.

If you’re in the target audience, Edouard’s Zombicide is a noise you’ll want to hear. It lands with a confident swagger, a handful of clever new bits, and a design that proves a guest designer can leave a lasting fingerprint on a beloved system without rewriting the whole thing from scratch.
