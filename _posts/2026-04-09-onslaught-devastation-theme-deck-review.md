## Strategy and playstyle: how to ride the Devastation wave
If you’re new to preconstructed decks, Devastation’s approach might feel like a brisk ride rather than a long, methodical climb. The deck emphasizes tempo and pressure: you don’t want to sit back and chug along with a block-heavy strategy; you want to step on the gas and keep opponent decisions tight and uncomfortable.

### Building around a theme
The first lesson in playing Devastation is to lean into its core theme: do not over-think it. Your job is to put bodies on the board and apply pressure while sequencing your threats so that your opponent cannot keep up. This means you’ll need to avoid overusing removal on irrelevant threats and instead conserve it for the moments when your board’s momentum is on the cusp of collapsing under a larger threat.

### Early game decisions
In the opening turns, you want to maximize your efficiency. A one-drop attacker that can trade favorably with the opponent’s early plays is a gold mine. If you can establish plate-spinning tempo with a couple of cheap creatures and minimal mana commitments, you’ve set yourself up for a turn-four or turn-five window where your mid-game threats begin to matter. In this zone, your removal and pump tricks become critical; you want to protect your faster threats and keep the opponent from stabilizing.

### Mid-game pivot points
As the game drags into the middle turns, Devastation typically reveals its second act: you either seal the deal with a potent finisher or you maintain a constant, unpleasant board pressure. The key is to align your threats with the deck’s available mana and to use your instants and tricks to exploit misplays or overextensions from your opponent.

### Late game and recovery
If you feel like you’re behind, do not surrender. The deck often has enough resilience and a few late-game helpers to push back into a winning position, especially if you’ve kept a couple of removal spells and a surprise pump in your hand. You’re playing for the long game, but you’re not playing it slowly. You want to remain aggressive enough to keep the opponent off balance, even when resources dwindle.
