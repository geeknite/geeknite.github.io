## Gameplay in a Nutshell
Sushi Go is a drafting game where players pass cards around the table and pick one card each round. The goal is to assemble the best sushi combinations to score points. The rounds are brisk, the decisions are quick, and the game punishes indecision with the constant reminder that you could have had the sweet dessert roll instead of a bland cucumber roll if you had just chosen differently a few seconds earlier.

### Setup and Flow
- Shuffle the deck and deal a hand to each player.
- On each turn, you pick one card and pass the rest to the next player.
- After multiple rounds, you tally up scores based on different sushi combos.
- The player with the most points wins.

Two things keep Sushi Go exciting: the chase for high scoring combos and the pressure of passing a hot card that you know someone else will snap up before your neighbor even realizes it exists.

### Core Mechanic: Draft and Pass
The core mechanic is elegant in its simplicity. You see a hand, you choose one card that will fit into your evolving menu, you pass the rest, and you do this again until the deck runs dry. The trick is to anticipate what your neighbors might collect and time your picks so you end up with the best overall collection rather than a pile of half complete combos that look impressive on the surface but score poorly in the final tally.

### Player Count and Pace
- 2 to 5 players.
- Typical playtime is 15 to 20 minutes, which makes it ideal for a quick weekend game or as a warm up before a longer session.
- The pace stays snappy because there is no heavy setup or complex strategies to memorize. It is a perfect gateway game for families and casual gamers, yet it has enough micro-strategy to keep hobbyists engaged.

## Teaching Deductions and Quick Play Tips
If you are the designated teacher of the group, you want to minimize rule explanations while maximizing fun. Here are some tips:
- Start with a quick one minute briefing: you draft, you pass, you score. No need to dive into every scoring nuance at once.
- Use a couple of examples to illustrate the main scoring opportunities: nigiri combos, maki rolls, tempura, and sashimi. A short demonstration goes a long way.
- For groups that ADORE strategy, point out the timing of a chopstick card if your version includes it. It creates a mini game of who gets to pick twice when the opportunity arises.

Humor tip: the game presents you with the culinary wonders of Japan at a speed that will make your brain ping with the same energy as a wasabi kick. You will not only feel like you are racing to build a tray of sushi but you will also be racing to keep a straight face when your buddy reveals a ridiculous round score that makes everyone else groan and giggle at the same time.
