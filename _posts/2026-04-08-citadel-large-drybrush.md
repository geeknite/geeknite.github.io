## What is a drybrush and why a large one
A drybrush is the skateboard of miniature painting: you push pigment onto ridges, textures, and raised edges rather than solid fills. The trick is to have enough bristles touching the surface to deposit the paint softly, but not so much paint that you’re basically painting with a wet mop. The large variant, like the Citadel Large Drybrush, is designed to cover more surface area with fewer passes. It is the equivalent of trading a scalpel for a sledgehammer when you need to highlight large negative space or rough terrain without committing to a full base coat with a finer brush.

The benefit is speed and consistency; the drawback is you can overdo it if you are not mindful of the brush load and the angle. Think of it as a performance booster for texture: it gives you a broad, dramatic effect quickly, but it wants you to respect its reach and not mistake it for a precision instrument. When used correctly, it can turn a plain textured base into something that looks like a battle-worn landscape, not a smeared paste with a hint of regret.

## In use: real world testing
### Setup and paint loading
Dry brushing is not about painting—it's about removing paint. The Large Drybrush is designed to hold a modest amount of paint, then release it across texture with controlled light pressures. We recommend a light dip into the paint with a quick roll on a paper towel to offload excess pigment. If you want a quick highlight across a stone ruin or a raised dragon scale, this brush will save you a lot of swipes. We found it performs best with thinner dry paints and slightly moisture-starved surfaces. If you load it heavily, you risk pooling and muddy highlights on textured areas. If you load it too lightly, you will be doing more passes than is pleasant on a lazy Sunday afternoon. Finding the middle ground is where the magic happens.

We also tested with a few different paint brands and a couple of specialty dry pigments. The Large Drybrush played nice with standard Vallejo, Army Painter, and Citadel lines, though you’ll notice slight variation in the way each brand dries and adheres to texture. The key is to adapt your load and pressure to the paint’s viscosity and your texture’s roughness. On very rough stone textures, a lighter touch and a shallower angle produced the most natural-looking highlight: a gentle, sunlit edge rather than a chalky halo. On smoother surfaces, you may need to lift more pigment off the bristles to avoid a shiny wash that fights with the texture’s natural depth.

### Technique tips
- Angle matters: tilt the brush so that only the trailing edge makes contact with the raised texture. This reduces paint application to a fine, even skim rather than a full-on sandwich of paint and texture.
- Use quick, confident strokes: the brush head is broad; you want broad, directional strokes that catch all the texture in one pass rather than a jittery peppering of dots.
- Use contrast as your friend: dry brushing on light grey or bone with a darker wash creates dramatic texture without much effort.
- Layer with care: you can build up a weathered effect by applying multiple passes with gradually lighter tones. The Large Drybrush loves a multi-step highlight, but it does not require you to do every edge in a single go.
- Clean between sessions: mineral deposits and pigment build-up will accumulate if you skip cleaning. A quick rinse, warm water, and a gentle reshape of the bristles will preserve performance.
- Dry brushing vs washing: think of the Large Drybrush as a tool for dry texture. If you want a deeper shadow in crevices, consider a wash later or a different brush to apply the base shade before dry brushing.

## Techniques and projects: what to try first
### Terrain basing and large textures
Take advantage of the large head by loading a small amount of paint and applying across wide surfaces like rocky ground, plinths, or girders. The aim is to create a quick, organic texture rather than neat, controlled lines. You can achieve a weathered stone look or a dusty desert base with minimal strokes. If you want to push the look further, pair it with dry pigments and a light dry brush of lighter tones to create depth where you want it.

### Creature scales and armor plates
For raised scales or armor edges, you will still want to use a smaller brush for crisp edges, but a few judicious passes with the large drybrush can simulate worn metallic sheen and general weathering across large flat panels. The trick is to avoid overloading and to keep the final coat thin. The brush’s big head means you can do the bulk of the work in fewer passes, saving you time for the important details like eyes and insignias—yep, we see you grinning and pretending that your monster needs a dramatic focal point.

### A quick weathering technique
One quick method is to start with a base midtone, wash in a darker color, and then dry brush the raised textures with a lighter color. The Citadel Large Drybrush helps you keep the darks and lights distinct by letting you skim over the top without pushing into the recesses too deeply. The result is a believable, battle-worn surface that looks like someone actually spent an hour with a brush rather than an espresso cup and sheer stubbornness.

### Layering for depth
If you want deeper texture with fewer layers, consider a three-step approach: base midtone, mid-contrast wash, then two light dry-brush passes with progressively lighter tones. The Large Drybrush shines when you’re chasing a weathered look across large surfaces. You’ll be surprised at how a few well-placed strokes can imply age, grime, and history without turning the piece into a muddy soup.

## Weather, maintenance, and longevity
Brush care is not glamorous, but it’s the foundation of consistent results. Care for your brush as you would for a friend you actually like spending time with.
- Rinse after every session, reshape the bristles, and store flat or with the bristles pointed upward to avoid warping.
- If you neglect cleaning, pigment can dry between bristles, which can lead to uneven application or frayed tips.
- Periodic conditioning with a light brush soap is not a luxury but a small investment in your future painting sessions. And yes, you can still be the person who leaves your brush in a mug of water overnight, but be prepared for a bit of a bristle rebellion in the morning. Tidy brushes are happy brushes, and happy brushes mean better textures with less argument.

Troubleshooting quick hits:
- Bristles splaying after a few sessions? This is usually a sign of either overloading or using overly aggressive pressure on textured surfaces. Scale back load and loosen your grip a fraction.
- Muddy highlights on textured areas? Wipe the bristles more aggressively on a clean cloth or paper towel, and consider thinning the paint slightly more than you think you need. You can always add a second pass if necessary.
- Bent ferrule or loose bristles after a drop? A quick check and reseating of the ferrule usually fixes it; if not, Citadel customer service tends to be friendly about replacements for lightly used tools.
