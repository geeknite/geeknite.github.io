### Partially painted status: what it means for play and display
The set I opened shows signs of loving paintwork, but it is far from fully finished. The partially painted minis look charming; they give a quick “board presence” that makes the session pop, but the details aren’t crisp across the entire suite. The armor trims have good contrast, but some of the faces and clothwork need a second pass to avoid looking like “grey plastic with brown washes.”

Pros of partially painted minis:
- Quick setup: You can start playing faster without waiting for a big painting session to finish.
- Aesthetics on display: A few well-placed highlights can make the table look amazing without committing to a full-scale painting marathon.

Cons of partially painted minis:
- Uniformity challenge: Mixed paint levels across the board can look uneven and occasionally distracting during tense moments.
- Value for future painting: If you’re a completionist, you’ll want to revisit each figure with more detail to maintain uniform quality across the box.

If you want to start a painting journey without scaring away your group, I recommend tackling the knight figure first. The large surface area and strong silhouette make it a satisfying early project. The Huntsman can be trickier due to daggers and fine linework, but a mid-level brush and a steady hand can yield dramatic results that lock in your table’s color scheme.

### Painting tips for the ambitious (and the mildly lazy)
- Plan your palette around a cohesive color story. Pick three anchor tones for the armor, a leather/burlap shade for tunics, and a metallic highlight that hits the edges. This keeps you from ending up with a muddy army.
- Prime in good light. A white or gray primer helps your colors pop and makes shading easier.
- Thin your paints. Zombicide minis reward glazes and washes that settle into crevices rather than thick blobs that hide details.
- Use contrast for quick effects. A well-chosen contrast color on cloth or leather can make a model look complete without hours of shading.
- Dry brushing edges on the knight’s plate armor creates a sense of wear without overdoing it.

## Practical table presence and setup tips
- Pre-plan your table layout: a few ruins pieces, a couple of watchtowers, and a central boss board piece can act as the centerpiece. Then spread your survivors across the map in such a way that you’re always within two zones of at least one other hero—cooperation is your best weapon against a bad dice night.
- Color coordination matters: If your knight is metallic and your Huntsman uses earth tones, you can create a visually pleasing contrast that makes each figure easy to identify during chaotic turns.
- Lighting matters: In a room with strong overhead lighting, you’ll see the paint job pop on the metallics and gemstones. If your lighting is dim, you’ll want brighter contrast on the minis so they still tell their stories on the board.
