## Materials and durability: will they survive your hobby life?
Durability is partly about the material (plastic versus metal) and partly about how you handle them. As a loose box, these minis are typically plastic or lightly metal; either way, you’ll want to consider:

- Handling with care: If you’re transporting these to a gaming session, a padded container is your friend. Loose minis in a bag can be a brawl with dice and snack wrappers, especially if you’re a table-hopper who plays in various cafes. A magnetic tray or a small foam insert would be a good upgrade for the future.
- Post-paint durability: With primer and a sturdy paint job, these minis can stand up to a couple of campaigns. If you’re planning heavy warbands and frequent re-paints, you’ll want to plan for possible touch-ups or re-basing after table shenanigans. The real test is how well they take a wash—expect the deeper crevices to drink up shade and reveal the character in the sculpt.
- Joining and stability: As loose minis, there’s no guarantee of pre-attached bases for each figure. You may need to base them yourself—sand, texture paint, and a bit of glue can go a long way toward stability, preventing nudges from turning into topple-down moments mid-walk of a zombie horde.

All told, durability hinges on your care and finishing steps. If you’re planning to permanently mount them or frequently transport them, a quick base and seal coat will extend their life nicely. If you’re a casual painter who only plays a table or two per month, these should be fine with light handling and a standard matte sealer.

## Painting and assembly: tips to get the best out of the five
Because you’ve got five distinct survivors in loose form, you’ve got a tasty little project set. Here are practical steps to getting the most out of them without turning your hobby into a full weekend of existential dread:

- Prep and clean: Lightly wash or scrub the models to remove any mold release residue. A quick acetone-free wipe or a gentle rinse will do. Dry thoroughly before priming.
- Prime options: Black or gray primer tends to highlight the sculpt’s depth nicely. If you’re feeling adventurous, you can go for a zenithal spray (light from above) to boost shadow contrasts without extra steps.
- Color palette: You don’t need a million shades to start. A core palette with a few washes and one or two highlights will bring out the characters’ personality. Think leather browns, muted steel, blood-red accents, and earth-toned fabrics. The goal is readable contrasts, not a rainbow parade on your tabletop.
- Washes and shading: A good wash (sepia, black, or brown) will settle into the recesses, giving you a more realistic armor and cloth texture. A second dry-brush lightens edges to catch the light from your game lamps. This contrast helps you identify characters at a glance during frantic rounds.
- Basing: The base shape matters for stability. You can opt for a simple textured base or go full-on fantasy with rocks, moss, or impromptu dungeon floor tiles. Since these are loose, I’d recommend consistent basing across your five minis for a cohesive squad look.

If you’re new to painting, this set serves as a nice practice field. The five minis offer enough variation to learn shading, edge highlighting, and color layering without feeling overwhelmed. If you’re a veteran, these looser minis let you experiment with basing schemes and weathering without the pressure of a pricey resin kit. Either way, you’ll end up with five minis that look good on a table and feel personal because you touched them.

## How to store and transport your 5-piece squad
Storage matters more than you’d think when you’re dealing with loose minis. Consider:

- A padded storage tray: A small foam insert cuts down on wobble, and a magnetic or peg-based tray helps keep figures upright during transport.
- Individual basing: If you opted for bases you can remove, ensure you have a stable base option to prevent tipping. A shallow, uniform base makes it easier to pack them without damage.
- Separate liquids: Keep your primer, washes, and paints in a dedicated toolbox so you don’t accidently paint your lunch. If you’re sure you’ll never use the same painting station twice in one day, you’re a miracle worker or a hobby lunatic—and I salute your dedication.

Pro tip: label the underside of the bases with a tiny symbol or color; it makes gameplay rounds faster when you have five survivors moving around a dice-dotted dungeon. This saves mental energy for the important things: killing zombies and telling crabby goblins to stand down.

## Painting tips and playing tips together: a practical combo
- Start with a clean palette: pick three base colors (e.g., steel, leather brown, and muted green) and keep them consistent across the five minis. This creates a coherent squad appearance.
- Use a limited wash approach: one or two washes (dark brown and black) will automatically enhance depth without muddying your color choices. If you want to push contrast, a targeted sepia wash in the armor creases creates a weathered look that reads well from across the table.
- Highlight smartly: dry-brush the edges with a lighter tone to bring out edges. Focus highlights on edges of armor, blade rims, and shield rims to give crisp readability when you’re passing minis to your opponent for line-of-sight checks.
- Shield details: if your minis include shields, a small metallic outline or a tiny emblem in gold or brass can add a regal touch to an otherwise rugged survivor. It’s the little details that show you’re taking this seriously—without being precious about it.
- Base your characters cohesively: add stone textures or dungeon floor elements to bases for a consistent battlefield feeling. If you prefer narrative consistency, you could create a small legend for each hero’s origin and reflect that on the base texture (e.g., stone flags for a knight, muddy earth for a woodsman).
