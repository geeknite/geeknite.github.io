## Rules and Gameplay Experience

### Core rules recap

UNO is a classic shedding card game built around matching the top card of the discard pile by color or number, with a handful of action cards to spice things up. The standard four color system (red, green, blue, yellow) provides the palette for quick decision-making and dramatic swings as Wild cards force you to change color on the fly. The 30 Year Anniversary Edition does not attempt to reinvent the wheel; it attends to the basic grammar of UNO and adds a light period flavor that makes the experience feel a little more ceremonial than a standard run of the mill game night.

Key rules to keep in mind:
- On your turn, you must play a card that matches either color or number on the top discard. If you cannot play, you draw one card.
- If you play a Draw Two or Wild Draw Four, the next player must draw two or four cards (respectively) unless they can counter with a similar card. This back-and-forth is where UNO becomes a social sport rather than a simple card game.
- Skip and Reverse cards alter the order of play; in a four-player table, the flow gets chaotic in a satisfying way.
- When you have one card left, you must shout UNO; failing to do so can invite a penalty if another player notices and calls you out.
- The Wild cards are the strategic heart of UNO: they allow you to decide the color for the table and, in the case of Wild Draw Four, can deal a devastating blow if played at the right moment.

### House rules and variant notes

While the base rules are straightforward, UNO has long encouraged local variants and house rules. The 2001 edition is no different in spirit. A few common variants you might encounter or adopt include:
- 7-0 variant: whenever a 7 is played, the player can pass the hand to another, and when a 0 is played, players pass to the next. This variant can dramatically change the late-game dynamic and keep players on their toes.
- Stacking Draw Two and Wild Draw Four: some households allow stacking, meaning a Draw Two can be countered by another Draw Two, which can lead to long chain sequences of draws until someone finally declares UNO and wins with a final card. If you hate chaos, skip stacking, but if you love the chaos of a well-timed wild draw, this is the way to go.
- House rule for last card: some folks enforce a mandatory action if you fail to say UNO; others ignore it. The 30 Year Anniversary Edition responds best to a clearly agreed-upon house rule before the first card is drawn.
