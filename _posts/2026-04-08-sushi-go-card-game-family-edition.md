## What is Sushi Go! Family Edition?
Sushi Go! Family Edition is a streamlined, kid-friendly variant of the classic drafting card game where players pass a hand of sushi cards and pick one to keep, then pass the rest along. The goal is to assemble the best combination of cards to maximize points by the end of each round. The Family Edition tweaks the UI/UX for younger players: bigger, bolder artwork, simpler scoring, and a handful of quick setups that get you to the table faster than you can shout, “Wakarimasu!”

## How to Play: The 5-Minute Masterclass
If you’ve played the standard Sushi Go!, you’ll recognize the core loop: each player picks one card from their hand and passes the rest to the next player. Repeat until all cards are drafted. Then tally scores and reset for the next round. In Family Edition, we keep the core mechanics but simplify the scoring and emphasize quick rounds so the table doesn’t devolve into a debate about what “sushi” actually comprises.

### Setup
1. Shuffle the sushi deck and deal a hand of cards to each player (the exact hand size depends on player count; the quick-start guide will tell you the default).
2. Decide on the number of rounds and the target score. Common targets are 20 or 25 points for a short family game, with 15-point rounds for younger players.
3. Determine who starts as the conveyor (the player who picks first for the round). This can rotate clockwise or be chosen at random.

### The Draft: Step by Step
1. The active player selects one card from their hand and places it face down in front of them.
2. The remaining cards are passed to the next player in the chosen direction.
3. Everyone does the same, picking one card per turn, until all cards are drafted.
4. At the end of the round, players reveal their cards and tally points according to the simplified scoring rules for this edition.

The scoring for Family Edition is designed to be intuitive:
- Nigiri cards award points directly (1-3 points for different types).
- Maki cards grant bonuses if you have the most or second-most Makis by the end of the round.
- Dumplings scale: more dumplings equal more points, but you can’t hoard them like lifeboats in a dessert storm.
- Tempura and other small scoring cards contribute small but satisfying benefits.
- There are penalties for conflicting sets or for holding onto dubious choices too long.

If you want a quick rubric on how to maximize points, the quick-start card includes recommended strategies for families—though it won’t teach you how to become a dramatic purple-haired anime chef. You’ll learn by playing, and that’s part of the charm.

### End of Round and Next Round
After everyone has drafted all cards from their hand, tally scores for that round and set up the next round by dealing a fresh hand. The player to the left of the conveyor typically starts next round. Rotate accordingly so that everyone gets a turn granting them both the “I got the roll” glory and the “I got stuck with the wasabi” guilt.

### Winning the Game
The game ends when a player reaches or exceeds the target score after a round ends. If two players tie, you can run a tiebreaker round or declare a joint winner—depending on how dramatic your family is about sharing the victory hot plate. The Family Edition generally ends quicker than the original for a calmer, more cooperative vibe, but with enough drama to keep it entertaining.

## Strategy Tips for Family-Mode Winners
Here are a few practical tips to help your squad step into the role of “Sushi Sensei” without becoming a classroom lecture:
- Prioritize flexible cards early: If you can grab a card that will play nicely with multiple potential sets, you’ll have better odds when the hand rotates.
- Balance short-term gains with long-term potential: A single sweet dumpling may win a round, but a well-timed maki sweep can swing the entire match.
- Read your table, not just the deck: Watch what others are collecting. If you see your sibling accumulating multiple nigiri for a big round, you might pivot toward a card that denies them a big payoff.
- Keep it light: The Family Edition rewards quick, clean scoring—don’t overthink every move. The goal is shared fun, not perfect optimization.
- Use the quick-start guide as your cheat sheet: It’s designed to keep the game moving along when younger players are still learning the rhythm.

If you’re teaching a younger player, consider pairing the drafting action with a simple post-round discussion: “What was your favorite card this round? Which card do you wish you’d kept?” It’s a gentle way to introduce strategic thinking without turning the dining room into a tense lab.
