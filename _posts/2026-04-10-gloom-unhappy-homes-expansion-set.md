## Overview: what this expansion actually does
Gloom’s Unhappy Homes expansion is not a standalone game. It is a mansion-sized add-on for your existing gloom toolkit. It introduces new house boards, new family cards, additional twists on the doom track, and a couple of brand-new mechanics that tilt the balance between “mirthful misery” and “mundane heartbreak.” If you’ve played base Gloom, you know the itch: you want to maximize the number of unfortunate fates for your opponents while scrambling to hide your own family’s misfortunes behind a suitably dramatic story.

The expansion leans into the idea that a home isn’t just a place where you live; it’s a puzzle where every room adds a tiny catastrophe. The rules themselves don’t become more complicated so much as they become more flavorful. That flavor is key: Unhappy Homes is all about atmosphere—those lilting Gothic vibes, creaking floors, and the sense that your house might be judging you for your choices in a way that a human never would.

## What’s in the box: components and visual storytelling
The Unhappy Homes box contains a handful of content designed to make your gloom feel less solitary and more architectural. You’ll find:

- 3 new House boards: Think new street corners of your neighborhood where doom can get comfortable and stay awhile.
- 12 new Family cards: Each family has a distinct home life and a set of misfortunes that are fit for a soap opera, not a census.
- 8 new Story cards: These are narrative prompts that encourage you to weave a short tale around each card flip. Spoiler: some stories end with a punchline and a sugar-free coffee afterward.
- 2 new Doom tracks: A couple of fresh ways to escalate gloom that interact differently with the original doom cards.
- 10 new Mansion tokens: Small circular reminders that the house itself might be collecting your tears as payment.
- 1 collector’s rule sheet: Because you will forget to apply at least two rules during your first game, so a reminder sheet exists to gently mock you.
- 1 set of decorative tokens: Aesthetic touches that won’t affect gameplay but will definitely affect your mood when you arrange them on the table like a little haunted art installation.

The production quality is up to par with Gloom’s typical standard: sturdy cards, crisp artwork, and a cohesive color palette that says we are here to wreck your day with style. The art direction leans into Victorian gloom with a wink of cartoonish humor, so you won’t mistake this for a horror anthology—this is a party that knows it’s a party, even if the party is dressed as a tired-looking ghost in a cardigan.

## Box contents and setup: how to get started without chaos
Setting up Unhappy Homes follows the same spirit as base Gloom: you deal with gloom, you hide gloom, you hope your opponent’s gloom is the one that wins the game in the cruelest way possible. The new House boards each come with a dedicated setup that tells you where to place mansion tokens and how the mood tracks will interact with the rooms you reveal.

Here’s a practical walkthrough:

- Choose a House board for the round and lay it out in the center. These boards function like mini stages for your misfortunes—each one adds its own flavor of doom. 
- Shuffle the new Family cards and distribute them according to the house rules. Some families intentionally tilt toward certain rooms, so pay attention to those room cues; you will want to exploit them when your competitor starts a dramatic sob story about the foyer.
- Prepare the Story and Doom decks as instructed. Story cards are a delight: they demand you to narrate a tiny parallel plot that aligns with the family’s mischief. It is not required for you to be a novelist to enjoy these, but it helps if you are part pirate and part poet of sadness.
- Place Mansion tokens and mood trackers as directed. The tokens don’t just crowd your table; they are a tiny reminder that your furniture has opinions about you.

A lot of the joy of setup comes from the thematic prompts that players exchange as you flip cards. The expansion nudges you to talk, to argue, and to laugh at the absurdity of your own gloom. It’s not merely a set of cards; it’s a stage for your table to become a tiny, terrible household where the sofa has the final word.

## Mechanics and design: how Unhappy Homes changes the math of misery
The core mechanic of Gloom—making your family’s lives as miserable as possible while trying to avoid giving your opponents better fates—remains intact. Unhappy Homes adds a few strategic twists that you will either love or loathe depending on your appetite for complexity.

- House specific effects: Each House board introduces a unique effect when a new room is revealed or when a certain mood token is placed. These effects can amplify Doom or bend the timing of who gets first relations to death and taxes (in gloom, the death part tends to win the game).
- Mood Track weaving: A new mood track runs through the board, with mood tokens that can spread from room to room. The mood tokens act as persistent doom pressure. They’re not overly punishing, but they force you to consider long-term plans rather than quick sprints for quick points.
- Story card synergy: Story cards pair with family cards for tiny subplots. These subplots can grant bonuses if you can narrate a convincing, grim mini-story in two sentences or less. It’s silly, yes, but it’s delightfully engaging for players who enjoy roleplay-lite moments.
- Doom track upgrades: The two new doom tracks create alternates to the base doom card deck. These tracks tend to lengthen the game and increase the laughter at how slowly everyone is dying. They also occasionally prompt decisions that feel morally questionable—like choosing which family to doom first when all of them deserve it equally.

What this means in practical terms is a slightly longer, more narrative-driven session that gives you more opportunities to swing a round with a single dramatic reveal. It preserves the core balance of base Gloom while nudging players toward a more storytelling oriented table presence. If you liked core Gloom’s theatrical misfortune, you’ll feel right at home here—and if you didn’t, you’ll at least appreciate how the new mechanics encourage you to lean into character performances rather than just flip cards.

Artwork remains a high point. The painters did their homework: you’ll spot stained wallpaper, cobwebbed chandeliers, and a family portrait that seems to have aged with angst. The boxes, tokens, and boards all carry that house-with-history feel that makes you want to own the expansion not just to play it, but to keep it on display as if the gloom itself were an art installation.

If you have players who love heavy lore or who enjoy dramatic group storytelling, Unhappy Homes pays off in spades. ## Value for money: is this expansion worth it?
As a general rule for board game expansions, the value calculus hinges on how much you will actually play with the new content. Gloom’s Unhappy Homes excels here because it adds meaningful, replayable content without inflating the core rules into a labyrinth. The House boards feel distinct enough to justify their own plays, and the new family cards alone dramatically increase the storytelling potential at the table. If you’re a dedicated Gloom player who enjoys exploring “what-if” scenarios for every family, the expansion is a no-brainer.

That said, if you’re a casual Gloom player who pops in for a quick game once a month, it might feel like an extra ornament rather than a necessary upgrade. The price-to-value ratio is favorable for players who want to deepen their gloom and expand the theater of misfortune. For those who prefer minimalism, you can still enjoy the base game and add this expansion later when you crave a richer mood orchestration.

## Strategy tips and table tips: how to maximize Unhappy Homes
- Lean into the rooms’ synergies: pay attention to how the House boards’ room types complement the families’ misfortunes. Some families benefit from triggering a specific room at a precise moment, which can swing a round in your favor if you time it correctly.
- Tell the stories, but don’t overdo it: the narrative prompts are a joy, not a requirement. A well-timed two-sentence story can unlock a bonus without derailing the flow of the game. Practice delivering a punchline in under five seconds to keep the table engaged and responsive.
- Balance doom and audience: remember the person reading the most haunting card might be you. Avoid the trap of doom-stacking your own family to the point that the table loses sympathy points; your goal is to keep the gloom dynamic while not turning the mood into a courtroom drama.
- Use mood tokens to create late-game drama: the mood track can become a pacing tool. You can accelerate a late-game finish or cause a dramatic reversal—just be ready to adapt when your plan goes up in smoke (and smoke can be very dramatic in a Victorian drawing room).
- Pair with casual storytelling sessions: if your group enjoys improv, use the Story cards as a prompt for short, improvised scenes. It’s amazing how quickly you’ll discover a rhythm for turning gloom into laughter, which is precisely the healing balm this hobby sometimes needs.
