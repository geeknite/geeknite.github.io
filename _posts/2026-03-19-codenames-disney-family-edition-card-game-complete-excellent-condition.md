### How to play (the quick refresher)

- Split into two teams and choose a spymaster for each side. Spymasters sit where their team cannot see the grid but can see the assignment cards on the table.
- Flip the Disney character cards to form the grid. The standard grid is 5x5, though this edition can scale up or down depending on the rules you agree upon. Each agent card shows a Disney character, scene, or symbol.
- The spymaster gives a one-word clue and a number. The one word should hint at multiple agents on the grid that belong to their team. The number indicates how many cards on the grid the clue applies to.
- The field operatives discuss and try to guess cards related to the clue. They have to avoid guessing the assassin (which ends the round immediately for their team) and avoid selecting opponent cards that would help the other team.
- The round continues until one team has revealed all of their agents or the assassin is revealed.

The Disney edition keeps the pace lively. You may pepper your clues with a dash of movie trivia or a nod to a character’s catchphrase. Just be mindful of spoilers if you are playing with younger players who have not seen every film yet. It is all about balance. If you overdo the movie references, the clue becomes obvious to the opposing team, but if you under do it, you risk losing the fun factor. The best clues feel smart without turning into a pop quiz about your family’s collective memory of Pixar plotlines.

### The game's difficulty curve and your strategy toolbox

Disney, by nature, tends toward approachable charm. In a puzzly word game, that means the difficulty curve remains comfortable for families while still offering a bit of a challenge to the seasoned players who enjoy a good deduction session. The one-word clue mechanism is the same as classic Codenames, but with Disney-flavored tokens, your brain gets to the correct target using a little cinematic logic. The trick is to create multi-agent clues while minimizing the risk of the assassin. It’s a balance of risk and reward, like choosing to go for the extra cookie from the cookie jar with a strict parental rule in place.

A good strategy tip: begin with safety. A clue that points to two or three characters is often the sweet spot. If you can prompt your teammates to identify two or three connected characters with one well-chosen clue, you dramatically improve your odds of success. If your clue wins you the round by connecting five characters at once, you are the hero who will be remembered for a snack that night. But be mindful: some clues might be too obvious and give away too much to the opposing team. The art of the clue is to connect multiple agents without making it too obvious to your rivals. In Disney terms, it is like referencing a crowd-pleasing scene without spoiling the ending of the film you are referencing.
