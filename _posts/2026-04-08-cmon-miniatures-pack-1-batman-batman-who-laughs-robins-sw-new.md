## Painting and Assembly Tips
If you are a painter who treats minis like tiny canvases, Pack 1 offers you a clean slate and a chance to flex a few advanced techniques. The Batman figure paints up well with metallic accents on armor and a matte black base layer that makes the cape read as a living shadow. For the Batman Who Laughs, you may want to experiment with a two-tone scheme: a grim, bone-chilling armor color paired with the red glow in the eyes and the neon green gloss on the face plate. The contrast will sell the freaky vibe and give you a ready-made showpiece for your display shelf when you are not conquering your local gaming table.

Robins SW invites a brighter palette without losing its edge. You can push the red, blue, and gold on the suit while still letting the character breathe through the base and a few weathering tricks. The alt-universe Robin also makes for great contrast with a metallic weapon or gadget that pops against the more muted cape tones. If you are new to assembly, the build process is straightforward: trim flash with a hobby knife, dry-fit the pieces to get the angles right, then glue once you are confident the pose is locked in. A quick green stuff seam treatment around joints goes a long way to make the kit look seamless on the battlefield.

From a painting perspective, these minis reward a medium approach: block color, wash, highlight, and then a few edge picks. The Batman sculpt loves a crisp edge highlight on the cape and armor plates, while the Batman Who Laughs benefits from a touch of sponging to simulate a corroded, organic texture that reads as nightmare metal. Robins SW, with its more dynamic silhouette, can be brought to life with a gradient on the red and a careful metallic accent on the gadgets. If you want a quick-start method that yields tabletop-ready results without spending twelve hours on each model, try a three-step approach: base coat, washes to bring out rivets and shadows, then a selective dry brush for raised edges.
