## Sculpting craft and aesthetic: what Edouard brings to the table

### Sculpting language: dynamic, not static
Edouard Guiton’s style leans into kinetic energy. The poses aren’t simply standing with a weapon; they’re mid-action: sprinting with a baseball bat raised high, a zombie pausing mid‑roar as if it’s evaluating your shelf space, or a survivor backpedaling with a spray can in hand. The lines are clean, the silhouettes readable from across the dining table, and the scale is consistent with the rest of the Zombicide plastic line. If you like your minis to look like they could leap into a scene and steal the camera, these hit the sweet spot.

### Anatomy and proportion: exaggerated but honest
Disproportion is an art form in miniature sculpting, and Edouard uses it with intention rather than excess. Limbs are elongated enough to read as motion, while torsos carry a sense of weight that makes a crouch or a lunge believable on the 28mm table. This isn’t a “hero with a giant hammer” approach; it’s a “fighter in a desperate situation” approach, which pairs well with Zombicide’s chaotic pacing where you’re more concerned with how many zoms are about to pile on than with the realism of every joint. The heads vary in size just enough to add character without tipping the models into cartoonish caricature.

### Texture and micro-details: texture you can see and feel
The surface textures—cloth, leather, armor plating, and grime—are applied with a painter’s eye for layering. This matters a lot when you’re prepping a batch for speed painting at your local club. The micro-details are clear but not aggressively fragile; you can push a brush across a cape without fear of root canal-level misalignment, and you can dry-brush the leather to bring out age without accidentally turning the model into a movie poster for “The Silence of the Dimes.”

## Painting and prep: from box to tabletop sparkle

Prep is the boring-but-crucial part of any new miniature line, and Edouard’s minis are no exception. Here’s a practical rundown:

- Cleaning: A quick soak in warm water with a dash of dish soap to loosen any flash, followed by a careful rinse and air dry. The plastic is forgiving enough that you won’t need to go full-on spa treatment, but a light touch with a scalpel will remove any extraneous sprue tags.
- Assembly: The parts align with a satisfying click, but you’ll likely want to pin some of the more dynamic poses if you’re transporting the game in a backpack. A tiny bit of green stuff or model putty can help close gaps where the legs and torsos meet. If you’re new to assembly, Edouard’s line gives you more tolerance than some ultra-tiny sculpts but still rewards careful work.
- Priming: A light gray or matte white primer helps the paint sit evenly on the textured surfaces. If you like a darker base to bring out the grime, a neutral black primer is fine too, but you’ll be trading some highlight intensity for depth.
- Painting: Edouard’s faces and hands respond beautifully to a mid-range flesh tone and a careful wash to bring out the eye sockets. The armor and clothing textures respond well to dry-brushed highlights, which can be a great speed paint technique for groups of minis you want to push onto the table quickly.

As with any new mini line, your mileage will vary with your preferred painting style. If you’re going for a “museum-shelf” quality, you’ll spend more time on blending and edge highlighting. If you’re a gamer first, you’ll be delighted by the dramatic silhouettes and crisp details that read cleanly at 1–2 foot distance—exactly the sweet spot for a crowded gaming table.
