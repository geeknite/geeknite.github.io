## Sculpting craft and aesthetic: what Edouard brings to the table

### Sculpting language: dynamic, not static
Edouard Guiton specializes in kinetic energy. These minis feel like they are midflight, midbreath, or midcomeback from a zombie groan. The silhouettes read at a glance, which is crucial when your table is a war zone of many similarly sized figures. The drama is controlled rather than gaudy, which makes them a perfect match for regular Zombicide battles that demand clarity under pressure.

### Anatomy and proportion: exaggerated but honest
Exaggeration in miniatures is an art form, and Edouard uses it with intent. Limbs are extended for readability at 28mm, while torsos carry the weight that makes a lunge or a backstep feel real on the table. This isn’t a caricature league; it is an action‑movie moment that preserves balance with gameplay scale. Heads vary just enough to give personality without tipping into cartoonish territory, letting painters capture expressions that read at arm’s length rather than via a magnifying glass.

### Texture and micro-details: texture you can see and feel
Every cloth, leather, and rusted metal piece carries a painterly invitation. The textures are robust enough for speed painting if you want a tabletop army to glow, but they reward careful work if you enjoy deep wet blending and layered washes. Those micro-details are not fragile; you can dry brush a cape and still keep the sculpt readable on a crowded table. If you like to paint a batch of minis and keep a sense of cohesion across a squad, the textures offer a reliable palette to explore your preferred techniques.

## Painting and prep: from box to tabletop sparkle

Prep is the boring-but-crucial part of new minis, and Edouard’s line is no exception. Here is a practical rundown you can skim between coffee refills:

- Cleaning: A quick soak in warm water with a dash of dish soap to loosen any flash, then a careful rinse and air dry. The plastic is forgiving enough that you won’t need a full spa treatment, but a light touch with a sharp hobby knife will remove stray sprue tags.
- Assembly: Parts align with a satisfying click, but you may want to pin the more dramatic poses if you travel with the game. A touch of putty can close gaps where limbs meet torsos. If you are new to assembly, these sculpts offer a comfortable balance of tolerance and reward for careful work.
- Priming: A light gray or matte white primer helps paint sit evenly. If you prefer a darker base for grime and depth, a neutral black primer is fine, just know you might trade some edge highlights for shadow depth.
- Painting: Edouard’s faces and hands respond beautifully to a mid‑tone flesh and a careful wash to emphasize eye sockets. Dry brushing on armor and fabrics brings texture without turning every model into a glossy poster of the future. If you like speed painting, you can batch paint and keep a consistent look across a small squad while maintaining punchy contrasts.

As with any line, your mileage depends on your style. If you crave a museum‑level finish, you will invest more time in blending and edge work. If you are gamer‑first, the dramatic silhouettes and crisp details are perfectly readable from a distance and read well under table lighting, which is exactly what you want when the table is a battlefield of clattering dice.
