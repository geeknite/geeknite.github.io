## Design goals and core features of the dashboard

The dashboard is designed around a few clear goals that align with how we actually play Zombicide Black Plague at the table:

- Readability at a glance: You should be able to tell who is wounded, who has free actions, and who is staggering toward the next encounter with a single look. 
- Stability in play: No sudden topple overs when the orc shambles by and flips the table. The tray keeps tokens and cards aligned, even during a heated group photo or the occasional victory dance. 
- Easy customization: Each hero in Zombicide has unique stats and gear. The dashboard should accommodate different types and scales without reprinting the entire thing. 
- Tidy integration: It should slot into neatly with existing terrain, miniatures, and terrain. No one wants a dashboard that looks like a city dump after a long scavenging mission. 
- A touch of personality: The aesthetic should feel like a piece of gear from a grim fantasy world rather than a sterile board game accessory. 

Key features you will likely appreciate include the following:

- A dedicated slot for the hero card at the top edge, with space for essential stats like health, stamina, and action tokens. 
- An area for the current status effects with an easy to read icon system. 
- A compact wound counter and fatigue counter integrated into the lower section. 
- A row of modular slots for equipment cards with a quick swap mechanism so you can update gear between rounds without disturbing other components.
- A separate section for order tokens and card draw triggers so the turn sequence stays crystal clear. 
- Ample room for dice, tokens, and loot counters that frequently accumulate at the end of a round. 
- A clean, modular look that works with both dark forest aesthetics and gritty urban dungeon vibes. 

The tray adds a few surprises as well:

- A main tray for minis with a shallow wall to prevent a catastrophic spill when a zombie lunges into a melee. 
- A second sub-tray for countdown timers and mission-specific tokens. This helps prevent misplacement when the board fills with green plastic. 
- Optional dividers that can be swapped to accommodate different hero card sizes and equipment sets from various expansions. 
- A finish that accepts paint and light weathering so it can match your terrain palette or your mood of the night.

## Materials and print settings for FDM printers

If you are new to 3D printing, you might be surprised at how much variance there is between different printers. Your PLA may print like a dream on one machine and refuse to cooperate on another. The dashboard is designed with a sweet spot in mind: solid enough walls to hold their shape, thin enough walls to keep weight in check, and small enough features to be printable on most hobby machines. Here are the practical guidelines we suggest:

- Layer height: 0.12 mm to 0.2 mm. The finer the layer, the smoother the surface around the hero card slot. If your printer is known for rough edges, 0.16 mm is a safe middle ground. 
- Wall thickness: at least 1.6 mm to ensure rigidity. If you plan to paint or weather the piece, a slightly thicker wall will hold up better to handling. 
- Infill: 15 to 20 percent is typically enough for the dashboard frame; the tray can go slightly higher if you want extra weight to prevent shifting during play. 
- Material: PLA is standard for clean edges and easy printing; PETG is great if you want more durability and a tad more flex to resist cracking under pressure. If you go with PETG, consider 235 C nozzle temperature and 70 C bed temperature to maximize adhesion. 
- Supports: minimal or none for the main body; some sections might benefit from light support for overhangs around the card slots. Remember to orient for strength where the hero card will be slotted. 
- Post processing: light sanding of edges plus a quick coat of primer helps the details pop. A touch of spray paint in your chosen theme completes the look.

## Printing considerations and troubleshooting

Printing a dashboard and tray is not a heroic act of magic; it is basic engineering. The main challenges tend to be warping on tall sections, overhangs around the card slots, and achieving a tight fit between the hero card and the slot. Here are a few practical tips to boost your success rate:

- Bed leveling matters. A well leveled bed yields consistent layer adhesion, which translates to straight edges and snug fits for the hero slider. 
- Print orientation matters a lot. Placing the card slot area flat on the bed minimizes the need for supports and yields cleaner edges. If you cannot avoid an overhang, compact support settings are your friend. 
- Filament storage is not glamorous but important. Store filament in a cool, dry place to avoid moisture-induced issues that can cause surface roughness and dimensional instability. 
- Print slowing down the critical parts. For the hero card slot, experiment with a slower print speed to retain precision. 
- Calibration is your friend. Run a small test piece if you are unsure about hole fits, then scale up. It is cheaper than reprinting an entire dashboard due to a misalignment. 

If you run into a gap between the panel and the tray, try a light sanding of the slot edges and a slight increase in printer accuracy. If you use PETG, you may experience stringing you did not anticipate; a retraction tune helps address this.

## Maintenance and durability considerations

3D printed components require a little care to stay in top form. Here are a few maintenance tips to keep your dashboard looking sharp over many campaigns:

- Clean is better than rough. Wipe down the surface with a soft cloth to remove dust and fingerprints. 
- Avoid aggressive chemical cleaners. Mild soap and water is all you need. Harsh solvents can degrade the plastic or remove paint detailing. 
- Inspect joints regularly. If you notice a loose joint, apply a small amount of plastic compatible adhesive to restore strength. 
- Store safely. Keep the dashboard and tray in a protective container when not in use to prevent accidental damage during storage or transport. 

With proper care, your dashboard will outlast several campaigns and still look good enough to brag about at the next gaming meetup. 


## Practical workflow for your table setup

To help you incorporate the dashboard into your normal play routine, here is a simple workflow you can adopt:

1) Set up the table with a clean play area. Position your dashboard in the center align with your hero seat. 
2) Place each hero card into the top card slot. Add the health and action counters next to it. 
3) Place equipment cards, status effects, and wound counters in their designated zones. 
4) During turns, update tokens as necessary and adjust the hero card to reflect new stats. 
5) When a round ends, tidy up the tray, ensure no tokens have wandered, and reset for the next round. 

The result is a calmer game flow and a table that feels less like a zoo of zombie tokens and more like a survival scenario where heroes actually know what they are doing.
