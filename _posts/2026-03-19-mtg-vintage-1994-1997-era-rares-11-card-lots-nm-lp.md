### How to evaluate NM/LP status in these lots

NM/LP is a useful shorthand—but you should not treat it as a guaranteed perfect scorecard. Here are practical steps to evaluate the condition when you open the mail or the photos:

- Take a close look at corners and edges. Minute whitening or tiny bends can be a marker of the card’s life thus far. In 1990s printing, some cards were more forgiving than others, so pay attention to border jitter and edge whitening.
- Check the surface for scratches or printing flaws. Cards from the era can have subtle surface issues that are not obviously visible in low-res photos. A true NM/LP card should have a smooth surface with minor, nearly invisible flaws.
- Inspect the back for consistent color and any cutting anomalies. Some sets used different collector-numbering schemes or back designs; ensure there’s no misprint or heavy whitening on the backside.
- Look for signs of reprinting or restoration. In some cases, unscrupulous sellers might attempt to misrepresent a card’s authenticity by altering the borders or corners. If something looks off, ask for close-up photos or request a video to inspect the details.

If you are a newer buyer, consider asking for an extended return window or a short-term trial to confirm that the lot meets the NM/LP standard you expect. A seller who is confident in their product should be comfortable with straightforward verification, photos, and, ideally, a transparent grading rubric.

### Practical shipping and handling tips

Because these are older cards, players and collectors want to protect them as if they were the last working porcelain mug before the end of the world. Here are practical tips to ensure your NM/LP 11-card lot arrives in the promised condition:

- Use top loaders and semi-rigid toploaders, plus a sturdy mailer. In vintage MTG, the border corners need protection from flex and shipping wear.
- Include a descriptive note about each card’s visible condition, ideally with close-up photos. This reduces the chance of miscommunication and post-shipment drama.
- If you are selling, photograph the lot with natural light. The darker the photo, the more risk of misperception about edge whitening or corner damage.
- Consider adding a return policy that makes both you and the buyer comfortable. A 7-to-14-day window works for many collectors, especially if you request return shipping in the event of a misrepresentation.

If the shipping part sounds mundane, that is because it is: the arc of a successful vintage sale often lands on the alignment of good photos, reliable packaging, and timely communication.
