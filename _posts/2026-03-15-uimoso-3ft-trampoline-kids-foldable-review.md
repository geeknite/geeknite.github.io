## Safety Features and Considerations
Safety is not a marketing checkbox; it is a parenting philosophy. Here is what you should consider when you bring a Uimoso 3FT Trampoline into the home:

- Weight limit and user age: Most 3FT trampolines are designed for small children with strict supervision. Check the manufacturer guidelines for weight limits and age ranges. Do not let a taller teen or a parent use it for workouts unless the model explicitly allows it. You would not repurpose a baby bottle as a protein shaker, so don’t do the trampoline equivalent with a tall teenager.
- Supervision: The best trampoline experiences happen with an adult within reach. The promise of a quick bounce should never lead to careless play or the belief that a parental check-in is optional.
- Floor protection: If you are bouncing on a hard surface, spread a play mat or thick rug underneath to reduce floor damage and provide extra grip. The goal is to avoid a bounce that surprises the cat and ends in a tumble of holiday decor.
- Shoes and socks: Many parents prefer socked feet or grip socks for traction and comfort. Shoes on the mat can slip and cause missteps that are not fun for anyone involved.
- Indoor-outdoor use: If you plan to use outdoors, consider wind, sun exposure, and surface stability. A windy day can turn the trampoline into a sail, a scenario that is not recommended for small humans or patio furniture.

While the build quality seems solid, remember that no toy is a substitute for good judgment. If your child approaches the two-hands-on-floor bounce with wild enthusiasm, it is wise to remind them of the basic safety rules and to halt operation if the ground is slippery or the area around is cluttered with toys, furniture, or a stray rubber chicken that somehow found its way into your playroom.

## Setup and Assembly: A Quick Start Guide
Here is a practical path to get you to the bounce zone without sacrificing parental sanity. Notes assume the box contains all hardware and the necessary bolts.

1) Unpack and inventory: Check that you have the frame sheet, foam padding, mat, legs, and any optional safety accessories. Lay everything on the floor to confirm you are not missing a component.
2) Attach the legs: The legs usually screw or bolt into the frame. Tighten securely with the included tool. Do not overtighten and strip the thread—nobody wants to call in a tiny mechanic to fix a cross-threaded problem during a playdate.
3) Attach the padding: Slide or clip the edge padding around the frame to cover springs or sharp edges. Ensure the padding sits flush and does not bunch up, which can create a tripping hazard.
4) Secure the mat: The jumping surface should be centered. Check that all anchor points are fastened and that there is no slack in the fabric. A well-centered mat reduces the chance of twisting ankles during a playful jump.
5) Fold test: If your model folds, practice the collapse and expansion in a clear space. Ensure no fingers get caught and that the folded unit can be moved by an average adult without fear of it snapping open like a comic book trap.

Typical assembly time sits at around 10 to 20 minutes for most adults, depending on your dexterity, the stubbornness of your parts, and how loudly your toddler asks for a snack mid-assembly. Pro tip: keep a small container of snacks handy; it is a known morale booster when building something with more bolts than a DIY IKEA shelf in a storm.
