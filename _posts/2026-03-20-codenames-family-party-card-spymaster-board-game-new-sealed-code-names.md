### The Rules in Plain English

- Each team designates a Spymaster who provides one word clues followed by a number indicating how many board words relate to that clue. The number can be one or more, but you should calibrate it to your group s comfort level with risk and ambiguity.
- Team members then take turns guessing board words connected to the clue, one card at a time. If you land on a red agent card or the blue agent card or the assassin, the turn ends accordingly and those cards are revealed to everyone at the table.
- The Family Edition emphasizes kid friendly vocabulary and pun friendly humor. It s designed to be more accessible and less likely to produce awkward conversations that you d rather avoid with Aunt Linda or Uncle Bob. You re not allowed to use profanity and you should try to keep clues within the family s shared frame of reference.

The rules are straightforward enough that you can explain them in one breath and then spend ten minutes laughing at the results of your own attempts to be clever. And that, friends, is the essence of a good family party game.

The Family Edition nudges you toward shared experiences, common knowledge, and the kind of humor that makes people groan and cheer in equal measure. It s a dynamic dance: you give a single word that hints at multiple words, your teammates deliberate with their own inside jokes, and you watch as a cascade of guesses either falls into a coherent chorus or splinters into chaos. The chaos can be delightful when it s managed with good humor and a willingness to laugh at your own misreads.

### Strategy for Spymasters Getting More With Less

The best clues connect two or three board words without dragging in anything dangerous. If you cram in too many words you risk hitting the assassin. The trick is to choose a clue that resonates with everyone at the table and hinges on a shared vocabulary. You ll find yourself leaning on familiar categories: family hobbies, shared memories from vacations, or popular children s characters that both young players and adults can recognize. The goal is to create a mental map that your team can traverse quickly rather than constructing a sprawling labyrinth that looks clever but collapses under pressure.
