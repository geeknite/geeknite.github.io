## What is a drybrush and why the Medium matters
Drybrushing is the art of dragging a nearly dry brush across a surface to catch raised details with minimal paint on the bristles. The goal is to leave most of the surface untouched while allowing the high points to pick up color. The result can look like weathering, grime, or a crisp highlight depending on your technique and color choice. The brush size, bristle stiffness, and the amount of paint you load on the brush all factor into the final effect. The Citadel Medium Drybrush is designed to strike a balance between stiffness and softness, a capability to pick up texture without digging into the base layers.

This particular brush fits into the middle ground: not a featherweight for ultra-fine textures, not a monstrosity that would double as a miniature-size plow. It’s meant to be a generalist tool for midrange texture: cloth folds, armor edges, fur texture, ragged banners, and a touch of grime where you want the eye to rest without shouting. The idea is consistency. If you have multiple models to batch-paint, you’ll want a drybrush that doesn’t surprise you with wildly different results from one session to the next. The Medium aims to be the dependable workhorse—like a loyal goblin with a hammer rather than a berserker with a chainaxe. It may not be the flashiest brush in the cabinet, but it can get the job done without drama, which is a good thing when you’re painting a dozen miniatures and your playlist is stuck on a 90s power metal loop.

## The brush itself: design, bristles, and handling
What makes a drybrush good, beyond the brand name and hype? It starts with the bristles and the ferrule. Citadel typically uses synthetic bristles designed to hold a little grip on paint while staying relatively stiff—enough to grab texture without flinging pigment across the room. In practice, you want the bristles to be uniform, with no stray hairs sticking out like an awkward cosplay prop. You want a handle that feels balanced in your grip, not a medical device that requires a full stance and a math degree to use. The Citadel Medium Drybrush hits a middle ground here too: not a twig, not a baton, and not a party favor you got at a convention booth.

The ferrule should be tight and secure, preventing bristle creep. A loose ferrule is a speedrunner’s nightmare because once the bristles start to splay, you’ve lost control of the paint load and the texture becomes a surprise attack rather than a planned tactic. In our session, the ferrule stayed solid, and the bristles returned to their original shape after a clean, denser-than-expected paint pickup. That matters because you’ll be dunking this brush into paint jars, wiping on palette paper, and swiping across surfaces with varying angles. A wobble-free brush makes a huge difference when you’re trying to achieve consistent results across multiple panels of a model.

Handling-wise, the grip should feel natural. If your hand cramp triggers after 20 minutes of working small scales, it’s not the brush’s fault—it’s your posture. Still, the Medium Drybrush’s handle length and balance feel supportive for long sessions. It’s not too long to feel unwieldy and not too short to require you to hump your model like a turtle. Comfort matters because you’ll be repeating the same motion with dozens of touches, each one contributing to a final texture that communicates weathering, age, or battle wear.

## How to use the Citadel Medium Drybrush: a step-by-step guide
Drybrushing is part science, part witchcraft, and part ritual. Here’s a practical method to maximize your results while avoiding some common bonehead mistakes.

### Prep the model and the paint
- Clean your model to remove oils from your fingers and any mold release residue. If it’s a plastic kit with a lot of tiny nubs, a quick scrub of dish soap and a warm rinse helps the pigment adhere better.
- Choose a color that you want to highlight or texture. For example, a light gray or off-white works well on armor plates, stone, or bone; browns and ochres suit leather and wood. The color you pick will be the color that shows up on the raised textures after the drybrushing passes.
- Thin your paint slightly under the assumption that you will be applying it with a very dry brush. This prevents clumps and ensures the texture reads cleanly rather than as a muddy smear.

### Load, then almost clear the brush
- Dip the brush’s tip into the paint just enough to cover it. The goal is not a fully saturated brush but a lightly loaded one.
- Wipe off on a scrap sheet or a paper towel until you see minimal paint left on the bristles. Twist the brush to distribute the pigment evenly across the bristles. You want a nearly dry brush, not a wet mop.

### Test on a failed piece of plastic if you must
- If you’re unsure, test on a spare sprue or a hidden area. The goal is to learn how much pigment transfers with your pressure. Too much paint on the brush and you’ll see a chalky, unnatural highlight. Too little and you’ll miss the texture entirely.

### Apply with a light touch
- Lightly drag the brush across the raised details, moving in a direction that follows the model’s form. You want the paint to catch the top edges and raised surfaces while leaving the recesses relatively untouched.
- For larger flat surfaces, use a quick, sweeping motion rather than long, slow passes. Short, choppy strokes help catch micro-textures and avoid a heavy smear.
- If you’re aiming for a gritty texture, you can layer multiple passes, letting each layer dry. The second layer can be slightly different in tone to add depth.

### Clean up and inspection
- Wipe the brush thoroughly after use. If you notice a lot of pigment sticking to the bristles, it can be a sign that your brush is overloaded or your paint was too thick.
- Inspect the model from multiple angles under proper lighting. If you see any blotches or obvious over-highlighting, you can retouch with a small amount of the base color or your top highlight color to blend.

## Technique notes: what to expect and what not to expect
### Consistency and control
The Medium Drybrush aims to deliver a consistent texture across different panels. This is essential when you’re painting massed infantry rather than a single centerpiece. The brush’s stiffness helps pick up texture without disabling the underlying shading. You’ll find that the resulting highlight reads more like subtle weathering than a stark edge highlight, which is exactly what we want for certain aesthetic goals. You won’t get the razor-thin edge you’d expect from a dedicated edge brush, and that is not the point of a drybrush. The point is to catch raised details with a controlled, soft glow.

### Layering textures
If you’re blending multiple textures, start with a lighter drybrush and work toward darker tones. You can go any direction with color, but the principle remains the same: leverage the bristles to grab the raised surface while preserving the recesses. The Medium Drybrush handles this well, giving you enough of a “brush stroke” look to be intentional without turning your model into a chalky caricature.

### Surface considerations
Plastic, resin, and metal all behave differently. Plastic is forgiving; resin can be chalky; metal takes pigments differently depending on the surface’s roughness and primer. In our tests, the Citadel Medium Drybrush performed reliably on standard plastic minis with a smooth surface after a good primer. On textured surfaces, it still did a solid job, though you might need a couple more passes to get the same highlight intensity on deeply textured areas.

## Comparing to other brands: where this brush sits in the ecosystem
We’ve all faced the choice of whether to use a Citadel brush or pick up something from a competitor. The Medium Drybrush makes a case for itself in this head-to-head arena:

- Citadel Medium Drybrush vs. Generic Brand Medium: Citadel tends to have more uniform bristle density, which yields a more predictable texture. Generic brands sometimes deliver a stiffer brush that retains more paint, which can be a blessing or a curse depending on your tolerance for mess.
- Citadel Medium Drybrush vs. Fine Drybrushes: If you want micro-textured highlights, a fine drybrush is your friend. The medium, however, provides broader texture capture with less risk of overdoing edges. It’s a good compromise for mid-range textures.
- Citadel Medium Drybrush vs. Large Drybrushes: The medium is less likely to pollute the entire model with too much pigment and is better for controlled work on mid-size surfaces. A large drybrush can be great for larger areas but can quickly produce an uneven distribution if you’re not careful.

In short, if you’re building a painting toolkit for a variety of models, the Citadel Medium Drybrush stands as a reliable baseline. It isn’t claiming to be the absolute best at every task, but it’s a solid all-rounder that can cover a lot of ground without demanding you switch tools mid-project.

## Pro tips and common pitfalls
- Don’t over-load the brush. The helm of drybrushing is discipline. A little goes a long way.
- Watch lighting. Harsh light can make you overestimate the amount of pigment left on the bristles. A softer, natural light helps you see the true effect.
- Be mindful of clear coats. A matte finish tends to mute drybrushed texture slightly; applying gloss can ruin the feel. If you’re going for grit, keep a matte or satin finish on top.
- Use a test piece. A bit of sprue or an old model can save you from disaster on a prized centerpiece.

## Maintenance and care
A brush’s life is governed by how you treat it after the session ends. Here are some practical habits:
- Clean immediately after use. Rinse with lukewarm water, then gently squeeze the bristles to remove pigment. Avoid twisting or pulling to prevent bristle loss.
- Use mild soap occasionally. If you’re using acrylics, a gentle brush cleaner can extend the life of the bristles.
- Dry properly. Lay the brush bristle-down or flat to prevent water from seeping into the ferrule, which can loosen the glue and cause bristles to shed.
- Store in a way that protects the bristles. A brush rack or a dedicated slot will keep the bristles aligned and prevent deformation.

## Final verdict: is the Citadel Medium Drybrush worth it?
If you value dependable texture work without investing in a large collection of specialty brushes, the Citadel Medium Drybrush is a solid pick. It’s not gimmicky; it’s not a drama queen; it does what you expect with consistent results and reasonable ease of use. It’s a practical tool for hobbyists who paint a lot and want predictable results across multiple projects. It’s also a good fit for beginners who want to experiment with texture without getting overwhelmed by brush choices. The brush shines when you apply technique, not when you rely on it as a miracle solution. The texture you see on the model is a product of the brush’s design plus your paint choice, your technique, and your patience. If you treat it as a helpful instrument in your toolkit rather than a magic wand, you will likely be satisfied with the results you achieve.

That said, no brush is perfect for every application. If you’re chasing razor-thin highlights on extremely fine details, you might want to add a smaller, finer brush to your set. If you’re aiming for heavy weathering on large textures, you might prefer a larger, stiffer brush to push pigment into deeper crevices. The Citadel Medium Drybrush isn’t trying to replace those tools; it is here to fill the gap between base coats and precise edge work, to help you get texture in a consistent, manageable way.
