## Screen integration and privacy
### Central panel vs. individual monitors
Some variants offer a central privacy screen, while others allow two independent monitor configurations. The central screen acts as a glare shield and a privacy curtain for your coworker’s screen-time secrets (aka, what you don’t want to see when they’re deep in gaming tabs). If you need maximum collaboration, you may want to go for two separate displays. If you crave privacy and glare reduction, the central panel is a surprisingly underappreciated feature.

### Height, tilt, and glare management
The panel height should be adjustable to avoid neck strain. If you’re sharing, you may want one monitor perched slightly higher than the other to account for different eye levels—this reduces neck tilt and encourages better posture across the board. For glare, angle the panels and screens to minimize reflections from overhead lighting or sun angles. A small anti-glare film on the monitors can help if your workspace is sun-exposed.

### Practical tips for privacy without isolation
If you love collaboration but also need focus, use the central panel during joint sessions and flip it to privacy mode when deep work calls. The desk becomes a chameleon: open and bright for team sprints, closed and focused for deep work sprints.

## Ergonomics deep dive
### Eye level and neck posture
Monitor height should align with eye level, and screens should be angled to minimize glare. If two people have notably different heights, consider individual monitor risers or a modular monitor arm system. The goal is to avoid neck strain and the inevitable whispered complaint about “my screen being too low” after a long sprint.

### Keyboard and mouse placement
Elbows should rest at roughly 90 degrees, with wrists neutral. If the creaking noise of two elbows colliding becomes a thing, you might want to stagger the keyboard placements or install slim trey extensions that offer a little extra reach without pushing the monitors into your lap. Cushions or wrist rests can help reduce fatigue over long sessions.

### Sitting vs standing
A dual sit-stand setup is ideal if both users want the benefits of movement. Pro-tip: set a routine—stand for a block, then sit for the next. If your office life includes frequent video calls, a rising desk makes you look more dynamic (and less like you’re stuck in a chair that’s evolved into an extension of your legs).
