## Box contents and physical condition: what’s inside and what to expect from the open-box experience?
As with any vintage open-box item, you’re primarily paying for the possibility of what once lived inside and how well it kept its secrets. The standard Second Edition Pictionary set included a deck of clue cards, drawing pads, the timer, and the pencils or markers (depending on the exact printing). Box 133, in this open state, presents a few realities:

- Card deck: expect some edge wear and the occasional bend in the corners. None of these are likely to ruin a game-night vibe, but they do say, “we’ve had a party.” If you’re a purist who wants pristine, this is your red flag; if you’re a romantic who wants stories, this is your backstage pass.
- Timer: the iconic sand timer, if present, might show a floaty drift in sand distribution—i.e., the timer is still functional but not a precision instrument used by a NASA countdown technician.
- Pads and pencils: the drawing pads may show light creasing, and the pencils could have a few stubs. If you’re lucky, you’ll get a fresh pack from a friendly dealer; if not, you’ll at least have a mood-improving scent of chalk and endless sketch potential.

The VTG (Very Thoroughly Grown) descriptor for this item, when applied to vintage games, is less about retail grade and more about how the item felt when you first opened it in the 1980s. VTG here implies the set remains a reliable time machine—good, with minor jitter, and clearly loved by someone who treated it as a living object rather than a tax-write-off. Caveat emptor: open-box items by nature are sold “as-is,” and the joy of them is often proportional to the buyer’s tolerance for minor imperfections that add character rather than subtract value.

Here’s a practical checklist I use for open-box vintage game items like this one:

- Inspect edges for tears that could indicate prior shuffles through a fireworks show of excitement.
- Check card backs for uniform color and absence of heavy mildew stains. A little yellowing is part of the vibe; a lot of mildew is a red alert.
- Look for missing components. The deck, pads, and timer should be present. If something is missing, you’ll want to adjust the price or the story you tell your gaming circle.
- Consider the packaging’s structural integrity. An opened lid that still closes with a satisfied snap is a good sign; a lid that won’t stay shut is the universe’s way of telling you to keep your receipts.

In practice, Box 133’s open-box condition can still deliver an outstanding “feel” for your collection. The box art and the tactile feel of the cards carry more than their face value. ## Market considerations: value, rarity, and how to buy with confidence
Vintage Pictionary sets exist in a market that loves nostalgia as much as it loves game design. Card sets labeled Second Edition and open-box variants frequently attract collectors seeking history as a tangible thing—an object they can touch, share, and occasionally trip over when rearranging a display shelf.

There are a few practical tips for buyers navigating this market:

- Compare conditions within the same edition. A boxed set with a pristine inner deck may be worth more than a mint outer package with minor interior wear; the opposite can also be true if the interior cards show consistent, pleasing patina that screams “authentic vintage.”
- Always verify the presence of all components. Missing cards or a missing timer can significantly impact value and playability. If you can source a replacement timer and a handful of extra cards, you can sometimes recoup value by creating a “near-complete” set that still carries the vintage vibe.
- Ask for high-resolution photos if you’re buying online. The difference between “slightly faded” and “coffee-stained” is in the details. A seller who can provide crisp images will help you assess condition more confidently.
- Consider your display and storage method. Vintage sets tend to become more valuable when kept in stable environments; humidity, direct sun, and extreme temperature swings can all degrade color and material quickly.

In terms of price, Box No. 133 sits in a mid-to-high spectrum for open-box Second Edition sets. It’s not the rarest variant, but it carries a distinctive aura that can appeal to both collectors and players who want to bring a slice of the 1980s to their living room. The key is to balance your desire for nostalgia with your patience for minor imperfections. If you’re buying for a long-term shelf, a little card edge wear can be a small price to pay for a high-spirited memory factory.
