## Unboxing and components
A sealed copy of Sushi Go! feels like unwrapping a tiny edible parade. The box is compact, the art is cheery, and the card stock holds up to the inevitable “shuffle, fluff, repeat” abuse from enthusiastic table mates. Inside you’ll find:
- 108 cards packed with bright, friendly sushi illustrations
- 1-page rule sheet with succinct, no-nonsense guidance
- A compact footprint that makes it ideal for travel, dorm rooms, or a cramped kitchen table where negotiations about who gets the last dumpling get tense

There’s no need for a bulky board or a lot of accessories. The game relies entirely on the cards, the draft mechanic, and a scoring system that’s easy to tile into your brain without a cheat sheet. Keeping everything in a single deck means you can slip Sushi Go! into a bag with a mischievous confidence—you’re prepared for a 15-minute culinary duel at a moment’s notice.

Here are the major card types you’ll encounter, in roughly the order you’ll care about them during play:
- Nigiri (egg, salmon, squid): single cards with obvious values, sometimes amplified by Wasabi
- Sashimi: sets score more the more you collect, rewarding patient planning
- Tempura: two-of-a-kind yields a fixed score
- Maki: points awarded to the player with the most Maki cards
- Dumplings: a simple ramp up in points as your stack grows
- Wasabi: a multiplier for the next Nigiri you play
- Pudding: a late-game decision ballast that affects final scoring

If you’re a visual person, this is the moment to picture a rainbow of little plates and a chorus of “pew pew” sound effects as you pass a card to your left and hope your neighbor doesn’t swoop your dream combo. ## How to play
Sushi Go! runs on a single, elegant mechanic: pick a card from your hand and pass the rest to your neighbor. The turn order and simultaneity keep everyone in the loop without lengthy teach-backs.

### Setup
- Decide how many rounds you’ll play. The classic version uses three rounds, with the total score determining the winner.
- Shuffle the 108-card deck and deal a hand to each player. The number of cards dealt per player decreases as more players join the table: two players get 10 cards each, three players 9, four players 8, five players 7.
- Place any pudding cards aside for endgame scoring; they’ll come back into the meringue of decision at the end.

### Turn structure
On each turn, you simultaneously pick one card from your current hand and place it face-down in front of you. Then everyone passes the remaining cards to the person on their left. When all cards in a round have been played, you reveal all your chosen cards and add the points from the cards you collected. It’s a fast cadence that creates micro-competitions around each card type.

A few tips that feel like magic when you first notice them:
- Wasabi works as a multiplier, but you need to draw a Nigiri after picking Wasabi to maximize value.
- Sashimi requires patience; two Sashimi cards will not net you points until you’ve collected a third for the big payoff.
- Tempura is all about pairing; if you miss a second Tempura, you’ll likely regret it later.
- Pudding should be tracked across rounds; it’s not a single-round scoring card but a weighty tiebreaker at game end.

### End of round scoring
At the end of each round, you tally your points based on the card combos you’ve managed to assemble. The scoring rules are straightforward, but they reward strategic prioritization:
- Nigiri cards: basic values tend to be 1, 2, 3, or higher depending on the subtype; Wasabi temporarily amplifies the next Nigiri you play, which means you can set up a high-score swing if you time it right.
- Tempura: two Tempura cards yield a fixed score; if you only have one, you don’t score from Tempura that round.
- Sashimi: set collectors—three Sashimi cards count as a big win for that category.
- Maki: the player with the most Maki cards gets bonus points; the second-most also gets some points. If there’s a tie, it splits the reward.
- Dumplings: a simple ladder that rises with each additional Dumpling in your hand.
- Pudding: counts at game end; the player with the most Pudding cards gains points, while the least gets penalties. This creates a surprising tension across all rounds.

The cadence of low-stakes rounds makes it easy to wrap a few games into an evening without fatigue. It’s not about monumental decisions; it’s about spotting tiny opportunities—like a single card that unlocks a minor but satisfying sequence you can replicate in subsequent rounds.

## Strategy and experience
Sushi Go! is more about pattern recognition and anticipation than about brute force. It invites you to guess what your neighbor might want, what they’re likely to pass, and how you can build value from the cards that eventually cycle back to you.

### Drafting and passing dynamics
A core skill is to read the table without staring at your own cards like a caffeinated detective. You’ll want to pass a card that you suspect someone else will grab immediately, freeing you to chase a better combination next turn. It’s a dance that rewards flexible thinking and a bit of humor about your own mis-predictions.

### Card synergies and memory
- Wasabi + Nigiri: a classic boom-burst combo. If you can guarantee a higher value Nigiri after applying Wasabi, you’ll feel a delicious sense of vindication when your neighbors realize your sushi actually packs a punch.
- Tempura + Sashimi: both are sets, and their success depends on the flow of cards across rounds. The trick is to avoid overcommitting early and risk losing out on later rounds when the board rebalances.
- Dumplings: a nice suspense card; you can build a few in a round to crest into a high score if you manage to draw the right sequence across turns.
- Pudding: a late-game friend or foe. If you’ve been hoarding Pudding cards, you’re likely to pull away at the end; if you have none, you’ll be watching the scoreboard anxiously as others pull away.

### The social layer
Sushi Go! shines most when played with a mixed group. The quick rounds create a light, competitive energy—everyone is in the game, and every decision can alter the outcome. The social tension is gentle—no table-flipping moments over a missing power card. It’s more like a friendly sushi race where the prize is bragging rights and a tidy memory for future games.
