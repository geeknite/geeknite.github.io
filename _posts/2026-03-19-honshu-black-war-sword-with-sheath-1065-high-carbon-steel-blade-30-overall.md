## Build and Material: The 1065 Carbon Steel Story
1065 high carbon steel is a classic alloy known for edge retention and ease of sharpening. It’s not stainless, which means it wants a little more love in maintenance. The steel composition typically emphasizes a good balance of hardness and toughness, which translates to a blade that can hold a respectable edge while being forgiving if you’re still learning to stop at the edge of the target and not its wooden frame.

Pros of 1065 in this context:
- Ready-to-sharpen: If you enjoy the ritual of honing, 1065 accepts a sharp edge with a simple whetstone routine.
- Toughness: The carbon level isn’t so high that you’ll feel like you’re carrying a brittle implement. It’s a comfortable middle ground for practice and display purposes.
- Affordable: Part of what makes a sword like this accessible is the steel itself isn’t extravagantly exotic, so you’re not paying for a lab-coat-level alloying process.

Cons:
- Rust risk: It’s not stainless steel, so moisture is your enemy. You’ll want to wipe after exposure to rain or poolside display sessions. A light coat of oil helps keep the shine honest and the corrosion at bay.
- Edge maintenance: Expect to touch up regularly if you’re using the sword for practice rather than display. The edge will thank you for the patience you grant it with ministrations of a stones and strops.

## Maintenance and Care: Keeping the Edge Honest
1065 steel rewards simple care. Here are pragmatic steps to keep your blade performing and looking sharp:
- Wipe down after handling: Use a dry cloth to remove fingerprints and moisture, especially after exposure to humidity.
- Oil lightly: A thin layer of mineral oil or sword oil helps prevent rust. Don’t drown it; you want a micro-level protective film, not a pond on the blade.
- Sharpen with purpose: When you want to sharpen, do it with a whetstone or sharpening steel. Start with a coarser grit and move to finer grits for a clean edge. If you’re a novice, practice on a less fancy blade first.
- Store right: Keep the sword in a dry, cool environment. If you’re displaying it on a wall, consider a climate-controlled space; avoid basements that leak and attics that bake in the sun.

A common question: does the black finish hold up under polishing and cleaning? It does fairly well in normal conditions. Do not expect it to be scratch-resistant if you’re actively training with a dull blade against hard materials. Treat the finish with respect; treat your elbow grease to the same level of respect, and you’ll be happy with the overall appearance.
