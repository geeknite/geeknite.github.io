## Build and Material: The 1065 Carbon Steel Story
1065 high carbon steel is a classic alloy known for a solid blend of edge retention and toughness. It’s not stainless, which means moisture management is part of the deal. This isn’t a lab-grade supersteel; it’s the kind of steel hobbyists actually encounter at home, in the workshop, or at a convention booth. The balance tends to lean toward a blade that holds up under practice and occasional showpiece use while remaining accessible for maintenance.

Pros of 1065 in this context:
- Ready-to-sharpen: 1065 accepts a keen edge with basic whetstone work.
- Toughness: The carbon level provides a forgiving edge that resists chipping under typical practice as long as you respect the target.
- Accessibility: It’s not a mysterious alloy—easy to source and maintain with common tools.

Cons:
- Rust risk: Not stainless, so moisture is your enemy. Wipe after exposure to rain or mist, and consider a light oil coat to keep rust at bay.
- Edge maintenance: Regular touch-ups are part of the hobby, especially if you’re practicing on tougher targets.

### Behind the Steel: 1065 Anatomy and Performance
1065 carbon steel sits in a practical middle ground. It typically runs around 0.60–0.70% carbon content, which contributes to a robust, tough blade that can hold a decent edge while absorbing a little shock without shattering. The microstructure of 1065 is mostly pearlite and ferrite, with enough carbon to form a hard edge but not so much that you’re courting brittle fracture. In practice, you’ll notice:
- Edge retention that benefits from regular honing and a light oil film after use.
- A willingness to be sharpened with standard whetstones without requiring exotic grits.
- A balance between rigidity and toughness that makes it adaptable for hobbyist practice as well as cosplay demonstrations.

## Seasonal Care Calendar
1065 steel isn’t a museum piece, but it does thrive with a little seasonal care. Here’s a practical calendar to keep rust at bay and edge performance high:
- Spring: Humidity is up; wipe after outdoor storage. A light oil helps guard against early-season moisture.
- Summer: Heat and sweat can accelerate corrosion; wipe, dry, and re-oil after training sessions in hot weather.
- Autumn: Moisture risk increases in damp climates; consider silica in display cases and re-check the leather sheath for stiffness.
- Winter: Cold air dries hands; re-oil after use to prevent micro-pitting from dry air and condensation when moving between heated rooms.
