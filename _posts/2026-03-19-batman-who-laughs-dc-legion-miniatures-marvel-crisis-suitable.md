## The Figure and the Base: Sculpt, Scale, and Stance
Legion Miniatures are celebrated for their sculpting detail, and Batman Who Laughs brings a unique set of micro-sculptural challenges to the table. The figure sits on a base that should communicate a narrative as clearly as the pose itself. Here are the core considerations:

- Proportion and silhouette: Does the final pose communicate the chaos of Batman Who Laughs, or does it drift into a generic villain pose? A successful piece balances a recognizable silhouette with the MCP-like dynamics that gamers expect.
- Armor and texture: The Wilson-suit of the Laughs’ look (a nod to his spore-fungal aesthetic and the multitudes of his mind) benefits from high-contrast textures—glossy capes, matte skin, and a splintered metallic palette on gear that reads as both a weapon and a tech artifact.
- Base storytelling: In an MCP-laden universe, the base serves as a stage curtain. A cracked stone texture with spores erupting from the crevices can imply the “laughing” influence spreading through the battlefield. Alternatively, a shattered glass texture can imply reality bending under the villain’s insane logic.

A well-executed base is a kind of micro-narrative: it tells you where the mini belongs in the story, even before any dice are rolled. If the base screams “doom and gloom,” you’ve set the tone for a game narrative that feels like a dark chapter in a crossover saga.

Sculpt-wise, the Batman Who Laughs in this piece favors dynamic tension over pin-up flair. A slight tilt of the upper body, a foot planted on the jagged base, and a menacing tilt of the head create a “I’ve already turned your plan against you” mood that translates well when seen from a gaming table. ## Painting Strategy: Color Theory and Application
Color carries emotion in miniature painting almost as much as sculpt does. Batman Who Laughs is a perfect playground for color psychology: darkness, menace, a hint of gleaming chrome, and a pulse of sickly green energy that hints at the insemination of fear.

### The palette
- Primary: deep charcoal black with charcoal-to-ink gradient, to accent the cape and suit edges.
- Accents: the signature green of toxin-infused chaos, used sparingly to avoid washing out the silhouette.
- Metallics: gunmetal or silver trims that catch light in certain angles, suggesting weaponized tech and broken morals.
- Flesh tones: pallid, almost corpse-like, with slight blue shading in the recesses to imply the cold, calculated nature of this villain.
- Whites and insignia: a stark white onto certain elements to create focal points, along with Joker-esque teeth whitening on the mask for a sense of haunting grin.

### Techniques that shine
- Layering and glazing: thin layers of color to build up a luminous depth, especially on the cape where the light should travel along the drape rather than sit opaquely.
- Edge highlighting: fine edge highlights on armor plates to enhance crispness without making the miniature look too techy or sterile.
- Wet blending: between black and charcoal for soft velvet transitions on the cloak; use this for the “motion blur” look to imply movement, even when the figure is stationary.
- Freehand accents: a tattered cloth motif or spores sprayed from the base in a pale green can tie the character to its fungal theme without overpowering the main silhouette.

### Making it read in MCP’s cityscape
Marvel Crisis Protocol frames its characters in cinematic action poses. The Batman Who Laughs piece should feel like a freeze-frame: a moment where the villain’s plan has begun to bend reality. The color choices should support this narrative: the greens should feel hot and dangerous, the blacks should recede into the background, and the whites should glow with a cold light. A well-balanced palette helps ensure that the figure remains legible at typical MCP table distances (roughly 1.5 to 2 feet away), while still offering up the “squint and you’ll see something wicked” detail when players lean in to roll dice.

MCP’s rules are written for Marvel characters; the core difficulty of mixing brands is not “can you physically place a DC mini on an MCP base?” but rather “will this cross-brand concept enrich or erode the storytelling and table dynamics?”

## Build Notes and Practical Tips
If you’re inspired to embark on a similar build, here are some practical tips to help you translate this concept into your own tabletop showpiece:

- Start with a solid pose: The figure should read as aggressive, but not so contrived that it looks awkward on a typical MCP table. A slight forward lean, a raised weapon or hand, and a cloak that wraps around the leg create a dynamic silhouette.
- Select a base texture that communicates mood: cracked stone, molten lava, or fungus-inspired ribbing all work as narrative pillars. The base should complement the paint job without overpowering the figure.
- Use glazing to build depth: thin glazes of blue on the recesses, green on the energy aura, and warm browns on the wood or rock textures will separate layers visually and keep the model readable at distance.
- Weathering and storytelling marks: subtle chipping on metallic elements and light weathering on boots and edges create the sense of a battle-worn villain who has walked through many battles and many moral lines.
- Lighting and photography: clear, diffuse light helps the greens pop and reduces harsh shadows. If you’re sharing images online, a three-point lighting setup can reveal textures effectively without washing out the color depth.

If you want to replicate the look, you’ll likely rely on a mix of acrylics and water-based viewer-friendly mediums for shading. A small airbrush can help with soft gradient transitions on capes and larger cloth areas, while fine-tipped brushes will let you crisp the facial details and the cape edges.
