## Cleaning and Maintenance

Cleaning outdoor textiles is part art, part science, and part “holy heck, how many leaves did we collect this week?” The polypropylene surface makes cleanup fairly straightforward. A power wash might seem tempting for stubborn stains, but a gentler approach is often better for longevity. A simple hose or a garden sprayer with a mild detergent can handle most grime without warping the rug’s shape. After washing, lay it flat to dry in the shade. Direct sunlight every once in a while is fine for drying, but prolonged sun exposure can cause color fading, especially if you’re using a rug with lighter tones.

For stubborn stains such as oil or grease, a degreaser or stain remover designed for outdoor textiles can help. Test on a small area first to ensure there’s no color bleed. If you happen to spill something particularly curious—a dye transfer, for instance—blot gently and rinse. Do not rub vigorously as this can fray fibers along the edges. The rug’s non-porous surface helps prevent deep staining, which is great during a barbecue or a spontaneous art project by the kids. It’s not magic, but it’s close enough to feel like it sometimes.

Storage is simple: roll it up or fold it along its longer dimension and keep it in a dry place when not in use for extended periods. If you have a covered patio, you might keep it on the floor year-round, but if your climate gets heavy snowfall, you’ll want to store it safely to avoid cracking from cold air and moisture—although the rug isn’t designed for arctic winters, it’s not exactly delicate either.

We recommend periodic checks for fraying or loose edges. A pair of scissors, a bit of glue, and a careful trim can fix minor issues before they escalate into a bigger problem. The goal is to extend the rug’s life so you can enjoy your outdoor space without the constant fear of tripping over a loose edge.
