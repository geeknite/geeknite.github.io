## How to Play with a Toy Story Twist
UNO is not complicated. It is a card game about slapping your fate with a number card and praying that you do not have to draw a million cards while your friends cheer you on like you just discovered the cheat code for life. The Toy Story Special Edition does not rewrite the core rules, but it adds flavor so that the experience feels like you are playing UNO inside Andy's Toy Box rather than a bland living room.

The standard UNO rules still apply: match color or number, play a Skip, Reverse, Draw Two, or Wild card, and try to be the last player standing with a hand of cards that does not include your doom. The Special Edition adds a couple of community edition cards. They are optional and designed to inject humor rather than balance. Here is a quick overview of how they typically function in play. It is not an official rulebook, so treat it as the whimsical interpretive guide that happens when you forget your copy of the actual rules and decide to wing it with imagination.

- Andy's Toy Box card: When played, all other players pick up one card unless they can counter with a Toy Story action card. This creates chaos and laughter, especially if someone is trying to hoard red cards for a dramatic finish.
- Woody's Jump Over: A wild card effect that allows you to switch hands with the person to your left, which is particularly delightful when the left-hand person is reality TV level dramatic.
- Buzz Lightyear Draw: A variant of Draw Two where the target gets to draw two cards plus a bonus one for the aura of space exploration. The effect feels like a mini space mission in your living room.

If you have a friend group that loves theme nights, you can use these as the main rules, and then revert to the standard UNO whenever you want to restore order and sanity. The house rules are flexible and designed to slow down the pace enough for a good joke or a friendly roast of your own competitive tendencies. The real joy of these cards is not in the mathematical optimality but in the storytelling that unfolds as Woody pulls off a dramatic color-change and Buzz shouts to infinity and beyond before you draw the lethal penalty card.

### Game Setup and Flow
Setting up is simple: shuffle the deck, deal seven cards to each player, and place the remainder as a draw pile. The top card flips to begin the discard pile. The rest of the play sequence remains untouched: match color or number, draw when necessary, and call UNO when you have a single card left. The Toy Story add-ons come into play as optional cards. You can either add them in right away or set them aside for a special occasion like a family dinner where someone forgot to bring snacks and suddenly UNO becomes a strategic battle for chips and bragging rights.

During play, you will see the familiar patterns of UNO: color changes, strategic draws, and the inevitable chaos when someone refuses to play their last card and instead saves it for a moment that will earn them a laugh and a glare from the other players. The Toy Story cards inject moments of humor. Imagine Woody's face in the corner of your thought process as you decide which card to throw down. The emotional resonance is more entertainment than mathematics, but that is where the charm lies.
