## The MOANA Twist: Special Cards & Rules
Uno has always thrived on a simple mechanic: match color or number to empty your hand. The Disney MOANA Edition 2 adds a few flavor cards to shake the default flow without turning the table into a mechanical nightmare of rule-checking at every turn.

### Special Cards That Spark Joy (and Mild Strategy)
- Treasure Map Card: A new twist that lets you peek at the top two cards of the draw pile. This is a nice risk-reward card for players who can handle a dash of memory and light misdirection. It’s not optional cheat, it’s just a nudge towards better reading of the board.
- Coconut Palm Block Card: A defensive play that can block a player from calling UNO when they’re about to push out a last card. This introduces a bit more interaction and makes players think twice about shouting UNO. It’s a light, flavorful balance between offense and defense.
- Moana’s Wind Card: A wild card that can change the current color and potentially skip a few turns depending on house rules. It adds a sense of momentum, very thematically appropriate for a voyage across the ocean of family game night.
- Maui’s Demonstration Card: A humorous penalty card that triggers a goofy action (like making everyone pose heroically for 5 seconds) instead of a strict penalty. It’s the kind of card that keeps the vibe relaxed and silly, which is perfect for younger players.

The new cards do a nice job of keeping UNO’s core tempo while giving families something new to discover without requiring a full-blown rulebook review before every game. The flavor text and artwork are charming enough to feel special but not so busy that it distracts from actual play. In practice, the special cards encourage more interaction and create opportunities for comeback turns, especially when players are down to their last few cards.

### How Rules Play Out at the Table
The MOANA Edition 2 keeps the base rules intact for the most part: players match color or number, you draw cards when you can’t, and you shout UNO when you have one card left. The house rules you might adopt with kids—like mandatory high-fives for yellow cards or singing a short lyric when someone calls UNO—are optional, but they do help keep energy high and the mood light.

From a gameplay perspective, this edition shines in the way it guides navigation through hand management. The Treasure Map card is a subtle but very effective way to keep players engaged and thinking strategically about when to risk looking at the top of the deck. The Wind Card’s ability to change color can be a lifesaver in a late-game scenario where you’re forced to improvise with the color you can’t dodge.
