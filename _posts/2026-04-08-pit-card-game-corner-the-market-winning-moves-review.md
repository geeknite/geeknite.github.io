## How to play (the short version)

Pit Card Game uses a fast-paced trading cycle where players try to throw down trades to swap cards with your neighbors. The objective isn’t simply to spread chaos; it’s to out-trade everyone around the table so that you can ditch cards and declare a victory. The flow typically looks like this:

1) Draw a starting hand and set the market status. 
2) On your turn, you do a sequence of trades—aim to line up trades that force your neighbors to hand over cards they desperately need.
3) When you’re able to rid yourself of all cards (or hit the agreed-upon hand-size condition), you shout your victory and hope your tablemates don’t stage a mutiny. 

The corner-the-market mechanic is invoked when a player acquires a set of specific market cards, triggering a special effect that can swing the game in dramatic fashion. It’s not purely luck; there’s a lot of reading your opponents, bluffing, and timing your trades just right.

To get a sense of the tempo, imagine a group of friends playing a speed version of “economics class” where everyone’s professor and you’re all slinging colorful fruit bars across the table while shouting price quotes. ## Strategy and tips: how to win without selling your soul

- Learn the market signals: Some market cards react to specific commodities. Recognizing when to push a particular trade can net you a few precious cards while your rivals chase their red herring trades.
- Trade aggressively, but not stupidly: The goal isn’t to out-trade with the fastest tongue; it’s to out-think your opponents about what they can afford to give up on the next turn. If you can force a couple of advantageous trades by feigning weakness, you’ll get into a position where your opponents overcommit and you come out on top.
- Timing is everything: In Pit, the moment you exit your last trade is a micro-drama. Don’t be the person who calls victory on the second-to-last card; hold a little longer and ensure you can drop your last card in a single decisive move.
- Manage your hand size: If possible, avoid clogging your hand with extra minor cards at the end. The bigger your hand, the more you risk running out of options on your final round. Your goal is to balance variety with the comfort of a clean exit.
