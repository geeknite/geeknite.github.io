## Overview: Exodus and the Common-Uncommon Reality

Exodus, released in the late 1990s as part of the Tempest block, sought to weave the dark, storm-tossed world of Dominia with more aggressive, tempo-driven gameplay. The common and uncommon cards in Exodus carry the weight of that design philosophy: cheap to cast, often with efficient stats, and the capacity to shape early board states. The typical Exodus common might not win a match on its own, but it can push a game into a position where your midrange plan becomes a slam-dunk if your opponent misreads a curve or forgets you’re swinging with two creatures and a pumped aura.

From a collector’s standpoint, a “nearly complete common/uncommon set” is a romantic notion. It implies you’ve got a robust core—enough to draft or build casual decks that aren’t embarrassing to bring to a kitchen table session, yet not so heavy with rares that you need a second mortgage to finish the binder. The sweet spot is the feeling of completeness without the debt-driven dread of chasing a chase foil. Exodus offers a landscape where that balance is plausible, but not without some pain and some hilarious near-misses.

## The Common Core: What Makes a Card a “Common” in Exodus?

In this section, we’ll zoom into common cards, what they’re trying to accomplish, and how they age. Commons are the glue that helps us answer a timeless question: can I play something on turn 1 that doesn’t make me regret life choices? Exodus commons are a mixed bag of early-game bodies, momentum engines, and the occasional surprise role-player that sticks around long after the game is decided.

### Early Game Velocity and Hole-Filling Bodies

Exodus includes a number of commons designed to hit the board early and threaten to scale up if the opponent doesn’t respond. These cards often have efficient mana to power out small, evasive or aggressive creatures, or cheap spell effects that create immediate board pressure. If you’re building a nearly complete set, you’ll want several of these to ensure you can draft a coherent early curve and keep your table friend from grabbing all of the “two-drop” bread-and-butter cards.

### Removal, at Common? Sometimes, but Carefully

One topic that always comes up with Exodus is its reception of removal spells at common. The set errs on the side of value rather than raw efficiency in many cases; you’ll see inexpensive removal options that trade 1-for-1 or set up two-for-one exchanges when paired with a steady stream of creatures. The challenge for a nearly complete collection is spotting those sweet removal helpers who age well—cards that still feel relevant in casual play and don’t vanish into the dustbin of MTG history when you’re trying to finish your binder.

### Tribal and Theme-Driven Commons

Exodus contains a handful of tribal synergies—think creatures of a similar race or creature-type that reward you for committing to a board with a specific theme. Some commons push you toward that tribal idea, making them not just drop-ins but potential cornerstones in a cohesive deck. If you’re collecting the commons, those tribe-centric cards are worth stashing because they support a fun, cohesive playstyle that’s accessible to new players and entertaining to older hands who enjoy a themed deck.

## Color-by-Color Breakdown: The Commons and Uncommons You’ll Likely Run Into

This section is not a strict census. It’s a practical guide to what you’ll actually encounter when you start sorting Exodus commons and uncommons into a binder named after a mid-tier fantasy deity. We’ll cover general tendencies per color and the kinds of green-and-purple parlor tricks you’ll learn to love or tolerate.

### White: Grounded Values and Small But Steady Bodies

White commons in Exodus are often about efficient creatures with protective or tempo-based abilities. You’ll see cheap creatures that help you stabilize the early game, and a handful of uncommons that pivot into combat tricks or small lifelink-tinged effects. If you’re assembling a near-complete white subset, you want a handful of these: a couple of strong two-drop bodies, a serviceable combat trick, and a few role players who can win games by attrition rather than pure speed.

### Blue: Counter-Play, Card Draw, and Tempo Engines

Blue common and uncommon cards in Exodus typically push into tempo and control play, but with a twist: the set often rewards smart decision-making and value trades. You’ll find a few cheap counterspells that feel fair rather than oppressive, and some card-draw or rhythm-altering effects that help you setup your late game. A nearly complete blue set is a delight for those who love puzzles—the cards don’t always win on the spot, but they make the game’s tempo feel like a chess match with better art.

### Black: Removal, Chaos, and Graveyard Psychology

Black’s commons and uncommons in Exodus lean into disruption and ritual-style play. You’ll encounter efficient removal, some life-drain twists, and the occasional stuffer that makes the opponent groan. The trick here is to collect enough of the black toolkit to enable a couple of boards where the opponent’s hand is suddenly a liability. If you’re building a near-complete set, you’ll want to chase those low-cost black cards that keep pressure on while you set up bigger plays.

### Red: Aggression, Burn, and Quick Wins

Red in Exodus is all about urgency. The commons deliver fast starts, some direct-purge damage, and a few one-off tricks that catch the opponent off guard. Uncommons in red often amplify the tempo and can enable quick wins if you’ve got a couple of cheap-red spells and a small creature swarm. Completing the red subset is satisfying because the gameplay tends to be straightforward yet satisfying—the kind of deck where you grin when your opponent looks at their draw and sighs, “Of course that happened.”

### Green: Ramp, Resilience, and Curious Beasts

Green’s common and uncommon cards in Exodus often focus on big creatures arriving a little too late for their own good or on efficient ramp that helps you accelerate out of nowhere. The trick with green is to identify those staples that survive combat and taunt your opponent into overcommitting. For the nearly complete green set, you’re hunting for cards that offer reliable board presence, a little sand-in-the-gear synergy, and perhaps a creature that’s just good enough to justify a splash in a casual deck.

### Multicolor and Artifact Considerations

Don’t forget Exodus isn’t all about monocolor perfection. You’ll see a few multicolor concepts that reward you for sticking to a theme. Uncommons in these slots can be surprisingly potent, offering a wedge of synergy that can elevate a casual deck into something surprisingly coherent. Artifact-supporting cards appear too, and once you start thinking in terms of colorless vs. color-supporting, you’ll realize why some players chase complete commons/uncommons first and reserve the chase rares and mythics for later expansions.

## Binder Habits and Storage Rituals: Practical Nerdiness

Binder organization matters. A nearly complete set deserves a comfortable, readable binder. If you’re a geek who loves categorization, you can separate commons by color and by sub-theme: tempo, stall/control, ramp, artifact synergy. You’ll thank yourself later when you’re hunting for a mid game play you drafted years ago and only remember the vibe of the card rather than its exact name. A practical approach is to keep core cards near the front and special cards near the back—the ones you’re proud of or likely to lend for a co op night.

As you go, you’ll also want to consider a few storage strategies: sample decks that feature your near complete subset, a small playset of each color for quick pickup games, and a few spare sleeves to protect nostalgia. It’s a silly thing, but it creates a ritual that makes the hobby feel both manageable and glamorous—the way a well organized bookshelf makes you feel like a wizard of bibliographic order.

### Storage Suggestions That Don’t Require a Roof Over Your Head
- Use color coded page protectors for each color family and a separate section for multicolor and artifacts.
- Label with short keywords: tempo, ramp, removal, inevitability. Your future self will thank you for not needing a scavenger hunt every time you want to play a retro deck.
- Rotate a small, rotating “deck of the month” with your near complete subset featured to show off to friends and family at game night.
