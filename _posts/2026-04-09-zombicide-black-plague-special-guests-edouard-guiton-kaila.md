Ah yes, the apocalypse with style. Zombicide Black Plague is the kind of game that makes you feel like you accidentally opened a portal to a very well dressed undead ballroom. Each player is a hero with a unique set of skills, and together you sprint through scenarios to kill the undead, rescue survivors, and accumulate enough loot to justify your increasingly questionable life choices.

### What makes Black Plague tick

Zombicide Black Plague builds on the classic Zombicide engine but gives you a medieval twist that makes knights fear skeletons with more swagger than a knight at a cosplay gala. The game combines simultaneous tempo planning with turn based action, and the synergy between players is what really sells it. You can be a hulking berserker who cleaves through a pack, a healer who keeps the party from turning into a crunchy snack, or a rogue who sneaks around the shadows and steals everything that isn’t nailed down. The keys to success are simple and brutal: coordinate with your team, use your environment, and pray to your chosen board game deity that your dice align with your will.

The special guests for this session bring a unique energy to the table. Edouard Guiton, a founder who literally helped bring this zombie carnival to life, provides a meta perspective on design decisions and the history behind the gears. Kaila adds a fresh, tactical lens that reframes some of the standard meta strategies. Their presence turns a routine setup into a lively, theory crafting binge where the phrase balance is a myth becomes a running joke at the table. If you ever wanted to hear a designer talk about how a goblet shaped the blade slider of a zombie in a pitch meeting, this is the session you want to watch, live or in text form.

### Setup: the calm before the scream

Zombicide Black Plague does not pretend to be a light weight. The setup can feel like a ritual, but once you’re through it, the game rewards you with cinematic chaos. The board is modular, built from a bunch of polyomino style rooms, corridors, and hazard tiles. The goal is to illuminate a theme: you are the last line of defense, trying to outlast the plague while not stepping into a pit trap that would somehow doom your day with the grace of a well timed misstep.

With Edouard and Kaila at the table, the setup includes a short, almost ceremonial briefing on the scenario you are playing. They remind you that you’re not just playing a board game; you are participating in a living museum of zombie design decisions, and yes, there is a tiny thrill in that. The dice have a presence, the foretellers of luck, and you can tell a good game by how little you need to persuade others to re-roll. The table feels alive as we place the first token, and the mood shifts from casual hobbyist chatter to the high stakes world of halted time where you must crush the uprising before the tide of undead becomes a red sea of regrets.

### Gameplay mechanics: synergy, chaos, and a little bit of math

#### The core loop

At its core, Black Plague blends simultaneous planning with turn based action resolution. Each round, you choose actions for your survivor, usually a couple of moves like move, search, or attack. You resolve those actions with dice that determine success or failure. The death of a zombie is not a quiet moment; it is a symphony of choked screams and plastic clattering as limbs topple in glorious fashion. The presence of two guests adds new layers: a meta discussion about the most efficient burn of action points and the ethical question of whether an orc knight can truly be a good friend when you need him to stand in a doorway and shield a mage from a zombie horde.

Edouard often spurs the team to think about the design intent behind each obstacle. Why is this tile here? Could the hazard be a more flavorful narrative device? Kaila challenges the tactical norms with counterpoints that push you to consider corner cases: what if you have a single remaining survivor and a wave of zombies barreling down? The conversation becomes as instructive as it is entertaining, and you realize you might be learning something while rolling for your life. The game rewards teamwork; if one person is stubborn about a path, the others will either coerce them with logic or hilarious begging for mercy.

#### Characters and progression

Survivors in Black Plague are colorful and specialized. A knight can swing a blade with medieval bravado while a hunter sneaks through shadows like a lantern with better sense. The growth system — typically tied to experience points earned by killing zombies and accomplishing scenarios — adds an RPG flavor. The more you level up, the more tricks you unlock. This creates a satisfying sense of progression that makes even bad dice days feel redeemable, which is crucial when your table is host to a couple of guests who treat every roll as a plot twist.

Edouard and Kaila offer their own perspectives on leveling up: Edouard might speak to the design philosophy behind class balance and the decision to make certain survivors more powerful in late game. Kaila could push for a narrative angle, arguing that certain survivors feel more heroic when they write their own legends in the chaos. The result is a conversation that blends technical design talk with storytelling flair. You leave the table with a few new ideas about how to run your own campaigns and a few more ideas about how to scream with joy when your fighter lands a perfect dice chain to clear a room.

#### Cooperation and conflict

The cooperative nature of the game can lead to genuine bonding moments. It is common to have a debate over whether to escort a frightened survivor or push forward to pull back a stronger line. The guests help keep these debates sizzling by injecting anecdotal wisdom: Edouard shares a designer anecdote about a notorious failed experiment that became a beloved rule tweak; Kaila adds a counterexample of a scenario that felt too easy until the group discovered a cunning trick. The dynamic is reminiscent of a podcast in some ways: a healthy mix of information, humor, and enthusiastic disagreement, all under the umbrella of a shared goal — to survive the night and claim the prize.

### The components: plastic, paint, and a dash of chaos

The miniatures in Black Plague are the star of the show. They are flamboyantly styled, with enough weapon variety to justify an entire hall of board game art. The artwork is a mixture of grimdark medieval and cartoonish charm that keeps your inner goth happy while still letting you giggle at a zombie with a crown or a skeletal knight wearing mismatched armor. The board tiles are sturdy, the zombie spawner cards are dramatic, and the loot tokens gleam with that satisfying RPG loot sparkle that makes you want to pocket every shiny square you can find.

Edouard and Kaila both comment on the physical quality with the fondness of parents describing a well made toy. The weight of the box, the crispness of the insert, and the tactile satisfaction of sliding a survivor model from the tray all contribute to a sense that this game was designed to be played not just once, but again and again with different people and different strategies. The GitHub level of precision is replaced with a more tactile designer energy: this product is meant to be used, abused, enjoyed, and then repacked with a little flourish of pride.

### Box opening, setup, and first turns: a mini ritual

Opening the box is a little ceremony. You count the number of zombie models, sigh a little at the size of the dice bag, and promise to be careful not to crumple the map tiles the moment you pull them out. The first turns are where a lot of the magic happens: you get to test drive your survivors, see how the cards interact with the board, and figure out where the first zombie horde will appear and how quickly you will regret that decision. The guests bring their own first turn strategies to the table, often testing the edge cases: can you block a corridor with a single human shield and still have enough mobility to run away from a sprinting zombie who insists on romance with your kneecap?

The humor here lands well. Edouard offers a retrospective on how certain early prototypes led to the final rule set, and Kaila provides a fresh perspective on how players in a modern group might interpret those rules with a pinch of narrative risk. The synergy makes what could have been a dry tutorial into a playful, educational warm up that leaves everyone with a smile and a handful of excuses for why their dice rolled so dramatically wrong.

### Scenarios, balance, and replayability: does it live up to the hype

Replayability is the heart of a game like Zombicide Black Plague. Each scenario offers a different map, a new cascade of threats, and a fresh reason to coordinate with your team. The scenarios vary in difficulty, length, and the kind of chaos you will unleash on your own foolish bravado. The duo of Edouard and Kaila frequently teases out the tension between planning and improvisation: sometimes the best path forward is the one that feels nearly suicidal but ends up delivering a cinematic victory when the cavalry arrives just in the nick of time.

From a design perspective, the balance feels deliberate but not oppressive. Some players will crave a meta where certain survivors are clearly superior; others will celebrate the idea that any group can triumph if they communicate well and keep their eyes on the clock. The guests remind us that balance is never truly perfect in a living game system; it is a story you tell with a little bit of luck and a lot of teamwork. The result is a game that can be approachable for newcomers while still offering depth for veterans who want to min max their way to a heroic ending.

If you like talking about strategy, the post game chat becomes a festival of what ifs and could have beens. Edouard often points out how certain expansions or alternate rules can tilt the balance further toward one side or another, while Kaila pushes back with counterpoints about what those changes do to the game’s pacing and narrative feel. The result is a thoughtful exchange that makes you reflect on your own table talk and maybe sharpen your negotiating skills for future gaming nights.

### Visuals, theme, and immersion

The medieval fantasy plague theme is not merely a wrapper; it is the architecture of the entire game experience. The zombie models are charismatic yet terrifying, the knights look like they belong in a tavern brawl after a long night of plundering ruin, and the board tiles evoke a crumbling castle under siege. The soundscape is verbal more than auditory, of course, but the moment you hear a zombie groan, you can practically smell the stale air, the candle smoke, and the distant clang of armor. It is a mood piece as much as a strategy game, and the guests do a commendable job of leaning into that mood without turning the session into a cosplay contest.

Edouard might reflect on how the art direction informs player expectations, while Kaila highlights moments where the narrative thread emerges — a survivor narrowly escaping an open drum of zombies and the visceral thrill of a risky search action that yields a life saving item. The combination of visuals and story helps the game feel like a living, breathing nightmare you actively participate in rather than a mere series of numbers on a card.

### Box to table experience: time investment and social vibe

A big factor with Zombicide Black Plague is the pace. It can swing from chill to chaotic fast, depending on how many players are at the table and how well you handle a missteps in your tactical planning. The social dynamic during these sessions is part of the fun. You witness the comedic chaos of failed pushes and botched searches, and you cheer when a plan that looked doomed comes together in a glorious, cinematic moment. The special guests keep the energy high, guiding the conversation with anecdotes, sometimes encouraging you to take a dangerous risk for the glory of the moment and sometimes coaching you to bail out the party before a perfect storm of undead arrives at your doorway.

This is not a game for solo play purists, but the experience scales nicely to 2 to 4 players, and with the right group you can coax a surprisingly long playing session out of a single scenario. The more you play, the more you unlock the rhythm of the game, and the more you appreciate the design discipline that kept your eyes on the prize while still letting your dice do their dramatic worst.

## How it compares to other Zombicide editions

When you compare Black Plague to other Zombicide versions, the medieval aesthetic immediately sets it apart. There is a certain elegance in the way swords and axes clash with zombie skulls in a dance of death. Other editions may lean into modern or futuristic settings, but Black Plague has a narrative charm all its own. It is less about shootouts and more about tactical positioning, crowd control, and recognizing a moment when you need to pivot from offense to defense and survive the next couple of turns.

Edouard and Kaila bring their love for the franchise to the foreground here. Their insights shed light on why Black Plague became a fan favorite and how the design language of Zombicide continues to evolve while staying faithful to its roots. If you are deciding between Black Plague and another edition, consider what kind of table you have: do you crave a grim, mythic vibe with heroic artwork and big, cinematic turns, or do you prefer something with a more modern urban zombie feel? The guests make these distinctions palpable and worth considering before you pick your poison.

### Final thoughts and a recommendation from the guest chair

If you love cooperative experiences that combine strategic planning with the thrill of cinematic chaos, Zombicide Black Plague is a strong pick. The base game delivers a robust core loop, a satisfying progression system, and enough table turning moments to keep a group engaged over multiple sessions.
