## Card Basics: Gloom in the Jungle

In the Jungle set, Gloom is the living proof that Pokemon’s design team can make a flower-loving droopy creature look mysterious and nostalgic at the same time. Gloom is a Grass-type creature that evolves from Oddish and then goes on to become Vileplume. In English Jungle, it is card number 37 out of 64, a placement that places it squarely in the middle of the card pool—perfect for shuffling, not too flashy, but with enough charisma to catch the eye of a kid who just learned to say Stun Spore.

What you get on the card is a Gloom that embodies the era’s aesthetic: simple, bold art, clear type iconography, and a layout that screams classic TCG, early days. The Jungle Gloom typically features two attacks designed to inflict status conditions and wear down the opponent’s board tempo. If memory serves (and memory serves many of us well here), the two attacks—Stun Spore and Poison Powder—mirror Gloom’s poisonous, sleepy vibe: a touch of danger mixed with a dash of whimsy.

From a mechanical standpoint, Gloom in Jungle occupies a sensible space in your deck: a stage 1 Grass-type with enough staying power to stall while your bench fills with the right follow-ups. Gloom’s energy cost aligns with the era’s colorless energy flexibility, allowing you to slot in a variety of Grass-energy activations without needing a perfect pinned-down energy curve. That is the beauty and the frustration of late-90s design—simple enough to learn quickly, but quirky enough to become the hallmark of a good memory.

## Card Text, Type, and Battler’s Potential

Gloom’s English Jungle card is a stage-1 evolution that follows Oddish and precedes Vileplume in the evolutionary chain. In terms of mechanics, it lives in the era when a single card could deliver either a status condition or a poison effect through two different attacks. Gloom’s type is Grass, which means it’s typically weak to Fire—an irony given how many Fire-centric decks were fighting in those early days. The card’s retreat cost generally sits at a modest value, reflecting the design ethos that you should be able to cycle Gloom in and out of the active position without too much energy investment.

The two attacks commonly associated with Jungle Gloom are Stun Spore and Poison Powder. Stun Spore is your classic paralysis move: it has a chance to immobilize the opponent’s active Pokemon, buying you crucial turns to set up your bench and accelerate toward your Vileplume. Poison Powder, on the other hand, sets your opponent on a slow burn—Poison continues to tick away, which means your opponent’s options become more limited as they wait for a remedy, a retreat, or a clean knockout run. The combination is a simple concept with real deck-building implications: do you prioritize stalling, or do you tilt toward guaranteed status pressure? In Jungle, your energy requirements are forgiving enough to let you mix and match with other Grass types, or even colorless energy to give you flexibility when your draws aren’t perfect.

From a strategic perspective, Gloom is a card that rewards patience and planning. It asks you to think in terms of tempo: how many turns can you buy before your opponent’s board position becomes untenable? With Stun Spore’s potential to stall and Poison Powder’s slow bleed, you can win endurance games where your opponent runs out of options. On the flip side, if you draw into Gloom late or get rushed by an aggressive deck, you’ll need to rely on your other Pokemon—perhaps your Vileplume line or even a bulky Oddish that can stall long enough to bring Gloom into the late game with a vengeance. The Jungle era rewarded flexible play, and Gloom sits squarely at the intersection of reliability and nostalgia.

To put it into perspective for modern readers, think about how a well-timed status on your opponent’s Pokemon can force suboptimal plays: they’re forced to heal, retreat, or risk a knockout that you’ve set up by board control. This is not a hyper-modern, all-damage meta; this is an era where subtlety and board management mattered, where a single status effect could influence the flow of a match more than a big two-digit KO number. If you want to revisit those strategic vibes, consider pairing Gloom with other Jungle staples like Vileplume—whose own presence in the metagame could amplify the value of your status effects by ensuring you keep pressure on the opponent’s side of the board.

For those who want to see a play-by-play, we can break down a hypothetical turn sequence with Gloom on the active: Turn 1, you lay down a growth plan. Turn 5 and beyond, you start stacking Poison Powder or move toward a bench-based plan with your Vileplume or Oddish. The key is to manage your energy, manage your bench, and read the meta of Jungle where status effects, not just raw numbers, defined success.

Collecting Tips: How to Care for Your Jungle Gloom

- Protect with sleeves that respect vintage stock. The old stock is more prone to whitening at the edges, so consider soft sleeves and a proper binder page to prevent edge damage.
- Store away from sunlight. The purple tones in the Gloom body and the green hues in the Jungle art can wash out with prolonged sun exposure. If you own a holo variant, sunlight also risks fading the holo shine, so keep it shielded.
- Use top loaders for display and avoid over-handling. It’s tempting to flip between cards, but the less you handle the surface, the better your Gloom will look decades from now.
- Keep track of condition: corners, centering, and surface are your main battlefronts in grading, but remember that raw nostalgia carries its own weight in market dynamics—don’t over-think the numbers; judge the card by its charm first.
- Consider bundling Gloom with its evolution, Vileplume, to create a cohesive display of the line’s early evolutionary chain. A neatly presented set often yields more appreciation than a solitary card that sits in an awkward binder pocket.
