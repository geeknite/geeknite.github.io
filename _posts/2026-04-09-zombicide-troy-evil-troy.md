Overview: What is Zombicide Troy & Evil Troy?

Troy & Evil Troy is an expansion for Zombicide Black Plague, Guillotine Games' darkly clever spin on the zombie apocalypse where heroes are less about staying alive and more about collecting shiny loot while muttering under your breath about the inevitability of death. The expansion introduces two new survivors: Troy, the classic frontline brawler whose sword slash is as satisfying as landing a critical hit in a boss fight, and Evil Troy, a corrupted version whose skill set leans into duality and risk-versus-reward tactics. Think of them as two sides of the same coin: one side screams charge, the other whispers corruption, and both sides are essential to unlocking some of the expansion's most entertaining moments.

The box also includes new equipment cards that reflect their thematic flavor, encounter cards that nudge the board toward more aggressive play, and tokens that track the new mechanics. The mood is dark and a little heroic, with a splash of cheeky humor that is very Zombicide: the kind of vibe that makes you grin while you pivot a shield to block an oncoming swarm and then realize you just wasted a perfectly good action card because you forgot to check the box for the extra loot.

What comes in the box?

- 2 miniature survivors: Troy and Evil Troy, sculpted with the unmistakable bravado and that eerily calm corrupted look that Evil Troy sports.
- 2 unique survivor boards with class-specific abilities that open up new tactical avenues.
- A full set of equipment cards tailored to the two heroes, including some items that synergize with the Evil Troy mechanic.
- New encounter cards and tokens to refresh the board with fresh challenges.
- A few mission tiles and objective markers to help you weave the expansion into a campaign experience rather than just a one-off skirmish.

The minis are beautifully molded: the black-plague aesthetic remains consistent with the base game, with a slightly more detailed finishing pass that gives the two figures personality without breaking immersion. The Evil Troy model reads as a clone of Troy but with a darker, sharper silhouette and a gleam in the eye that suggests the presence of a moral dilemma the players will be shouting about every other round. If you enjoy painting, both greys and muted metallics will pop nicely here; if you play primed plastic and fair-weather painters, these still look terrific on the table without the need for a marathon painting session.

How Troy & Evil Troy fit into Black Plague's mechanics

Black Plague operates on the basics of squad tactics, dice-driven combat, and zombie wave management. Expansions typically add more options rather than replace core rules, and Troy & Evil Troy is very much in that spirit. The two new survivors come with specialization that encourages a slightly different approach from the standard survivors you may be used to. Troy’s card set leans toward mobility and frontline resilience, turning him into a moving shield that soaks up hits while dishing out clean damage. Evil Troy, by contrast, benefits from being used in ways that sometimes require you to take a risk to unlock bigger rewards—this is not a mere flavor addition; it is a mechanical invitation to embrace a degree of chaos.

From a gameplay perspective, the expansion emphasizes two recurring themes in Zombicide: synergy and tempo. First, synergy: many scenarios reward you for pairing two powerful but differently aligned characters who can complement each other’s weaknesses. Troy and Evil Troy push this idea further by offering explicit interactions on the hero sheets that can trigger extra actions, extra loot, or safer access to certain high-value equipment. Second, tempo: this is the emotional clock of Zombicide. When you add Troy & Evil Troy, you get a couple of turns that feel like you are piloting a small mythic drama—one turn you push forward, the next you risk a misstep that could tip the balance against your group. The net result is a more dynamic game with moments that feel meaningful rather than purely chaotic.

The hero cards and equipment: what to expect

Troy’s equipment line breathes life into the standard weapon kits. Expect items that lean into close combat supremacy, with buffs that help him cut through zombies with a flourish. These aren’t just stat bumps; some items grant temporary damage boosts or improved movement, which makes Troy a reliable anchor for a fast-moving assault. Evil Troy’s equipment, on the other hand, includes a couple of “curse”-charged items that carry a risk-reward payoff. You might get a big attack boost at the cost of a doom token or a chance to trigger a special event that can swing momentum in the survivors' favor or doom them to a harsher zombie surge. The risk element is not gratuitous; it feeds into the thematic tension and creates memorable decisions.

Strategic take: how to deploy Troy & Evil Troy

The two heroes shine when used in tandem in the mid to late game windows. Early on, they can establish a strong presence on a choke point or a contested objective, with Troy drawing heat and Evil Troy unlocking a potential power spike if the players manage the risk correctly. The key to success is not simply putting both on the board and praying for good dice; it is about reading the board, leveraging the new tokens, and timing the Evil Troy risk windows to align with favorable board states. If you enjoy puzzle-like turns where every choice matters, Troy & Evil Troy is going to feel like a fresh itch you need to scratch.

Scenarios and campaign potential

One of the big draws of Zombicide expansions is how they seed ideas for campaigns that feel cohesive rather than a series of random one-offs. Troy & Evil Troy offers a handful of scenarios that clue you in to how the two heroes influence outcomes and how the mirror dynamic can be used to create a narrative arc across missions. Some scenarios lean into the moral ambiguity of Evil Troy, forcing players to weigh loot against the risk of triggering a wave of tougher zombies. Others leverage Troy as the steady, frontline force, proving that the simplest choice is often the bravest one. Together, they provide a nice beat for a mini-campaign or a longer run when you pair the expansion with additional Black Plague missions and perhaps a few custom scenarios of your own.

Durability and presentation: components are sturdy and visually appealing

In terms of build quality, Troy & Evil Troy follows the standard of Guillotine Games' Zombicide line: sturdy plastic that can take a beating, with clean miniatures that hold details well after a few episodes of board bashing and table edge catastrophes. The artwork remains consistent with Black Plague's moody, medieval-arena aesthetic, giving the game a consistent look on the table. The new cards and tokens feel well integrated; they are not overly fiddly to manage and they slot into the base game's sleeves and organizers without causing chaos during setup.

A note on balance and difficulty

Like many expansions, there is a risk of oversaturation or power creep with two very potent heroes entering a base game that players know inside out. In my testing, Troy & Evil Troy did not unbalance the game in a single session, but it did tilt some mission choices toward riskier, more aggressive play. The Evil Troy mechanic can create tense moment-to-moment decision making; it rewards bold play but punishes overconfidence with a burst of aggressive zombie spawns if the players mismanage the risk. This is precisely the kind of design that makes Zombicide memorable but may not be ideal for complete newcomers. If you are crafting a night of games for a mixed group, consider running a brief pre-game chat about when and how the Evil Troy risk cards are drawn to avoid an early power swing that derails the session.

Visuals, assets, and your table: how it looks on the board

The minis are crisp and the two new heroes are visually distinct, which aids player identification on crowded boards. The sculpts capture that over-the-top heroic vibe while staying faithful to Black Plague’s gritty fantasy tone. The new encounter cards add flavor with thematic names and short flavor text that elicits a few chuckles and a few groans in equal measure. The tokens are clearly color-coded and durable enough to survive a few table tumble incidents. If you love the look of the core game and want something that keeps the vibe intact without bending the aesthetic toward pop fantasy, Troy & Evil Troy fits the bill nicely.

How this expansion stacks up against other Zombicide expansions

Compared to other two-hero expansions for Zombicide Black Plague, Troy & Evil Troy is well-balanced, leaning more toward narrative flavor than raw power. You will feel the difference when you add them to a game that has previously relied on generic survivors and standard equipment. If you enjoy bite-sized upgrades that feel thematic and add strategic choices without upending the core rules, this expansion is a safe bet. If, however, you crave the strongest possible meta-game optimization, you might find some of the Evil Troy risk mechanics a touch on the swingy side but still within the spirit of the game’s design. In short: Troy & Evil Troy is a solid, kind-of-glorious addition for players who want more character, more decisions, and a dash of mythic chaos.

Final verdict and the Geeknite take-away

If you enjoy Zombicide Black Plague and want to enrich your table with two very distinct hero archetypes that play differently but complement each other, Troy & Evil Troy is worth a close look. It does not reinvent the wheel, but it does provide a handful of clever mechanics that improve the pacing and decision depth of a session. The two heroes offer a narrative hook that keeps players invested in every mission, and the risk-based play provided by Evil Troy adds a delicious layer of tension that your standard survivors rarely achieve. It is also a treat for groups who enjoy discussing the ethics of loot, risk, and glory as part of the global undead buffet.

If you are a completionist who wants every piece of the Black Plague ecosystem, this expansion is a no-brainer. If you are new to Zombicide, you may want to start with the core box and one or two smaller expansions before jumping into Troy & Evil Troy to avoid an overwhelming onboarding experience. Either way, you will find the expansion delivers consistent fun, a handful of memorable moments, and a voice in the box that makes your table feel a little more alive—albeit in the most undead sense possible.
