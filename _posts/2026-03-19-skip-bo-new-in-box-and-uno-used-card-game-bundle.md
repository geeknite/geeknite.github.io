## How to Play: Quick-Start Primer for Both Games
### Skip-Bo: a crash course in queue management
Skip-Bo is a race to empty your stock pile by playing cards in ascending order onto the building piles. Each turn you can play from your hand to the common building piles, or draw a new card to replace one you just played. The wild cards (Skip-Bo) function as vagabond wildcards that can stand in for any number. The tricky part is that you’ll often want to hold onto certain numbers to complete a sequence later, which makes this feel like a delicate balancing act between “play now” and “play smart.” The rulebook is your friend here, but your friends are better; they’ll remind you that your plan has more holes than a Swiss cheese factory.

### Uno: color, numbers, chaos
Uno is simpler to explain but deceptively deep. Match a card in color or number with the top card of the discard pile, and you’re one step closer to emptying your hand. Special cards spice things up: Draw Two, Skip, Reverse, Wild, and Draw Four. The social layer is the real-star here: when someone notices you’re about to win and you flex your “Uno!” with a triumphant smugness, you’ve proven that you can be competitive without being cruel. The used Uno deck may show signs of love, but it still loves back with a well-timed Wild or a strategic Draw Two that breaks someone’s 4-turn strategy.

### Table talk and cooperation tips
- Establish a light meta: “First to three wins” or “Best of five” to keep momentum.
- Define house rules quickly: how strict to enforce Uno’s Draw Four? The most chaotic version is often the most fun, but you’ll want an agreed-upon limit before someone calls it quits.
- Use a timer if needed: a 45-second hot take per turn reduces time-wasting, which is essential when your snack bowl is on the table and your patience is not.
