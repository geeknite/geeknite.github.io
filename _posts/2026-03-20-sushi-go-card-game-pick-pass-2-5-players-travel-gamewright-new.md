## How to play (2–5 players, travel edition)

### Setup

Each player starts with a hand of cards dealt face down. In a four-player game you’ll usually have around 10 cards per deal, though the exact number can vary a bit depending on the edition and house rules you adopt. The deck is split, the first round begins, and your eyes go from “I’m hungry” to “I’m calculating the probability of a sashimi dragon appearing in the next three cards” in roughly seven seconds.

### The draft and pass mechanic

On your turn you pick one card from your hand and pass the rest to the neighbor. The passing direction can flip between rounds, adding a light strategic dice-roll flavor to what would otherwise be a simple tempo game. The table dynamics come into play: do you go for a risky sushi combo that requires you to snipe two rounds in a row, or do you secure a safe but modest scoring option and hope the deck gives you a clean path later? In travel mode, you’ll be navigating small tables, occasional interruptions, and the world’s loudest coffee grinder in the distance. The core page-turning mechanic remains intact: draft, pass, score, repeat.

### Scoring and combos

Most cards score in small increments until you hit the big scoring gambles. Nigiri cards typically score a fixed amount when paired with wasabi cards, maki cards provide points based on how many you collect, sashimi offers a chunky score when you gather a trio, and the dumplings scale in a way that makes you feel like the card-drafting version of a sushi chef counting rice. The travel edition’s scoring keeps all that flavor but trims the complexity to keep rounds snappy. You won’t find yourself buried under a rulebook in the airport lounge; you’ll be glancing at your hand and calculating a plan while your coffee cools to the exact color of your competition’s eyes.

### A round-by-round mini-lesson

Round 1: You’re learning the rhythm. You pick a carby card (or a fishy one) and pass, trying to deduce what your neighbors might be collecting. Round 2: The table starts to talk; someone reveals a cunning plan to stack nigiri while pretending to be innocent as a panda. Round 3: The sashimi reveals itself to be the wild card that could push you from mid-pack to hero status—or doom you to the realm of the “nice try.” The travel edition rewards both speed and cleverness. It rewards quick mental math without punishing you for being a little distracted by a nice view out the window.

### Strategy for 2–5 players

- In smaller games (2–3 players), you’ll notice the deck runs leaner and the chance to complete higher-value combos increases. You can risk bolder plays, because there are fewer opponents to block you. The corners of the table feel more intimate, and the negotiation dynamics become a friendly banter duel rather than a full-blown strategic arm wrestle.
- In larger games (4–5 players), you’ll want to track your neighbors’ visible intentions and try to predict which cards they’re likely going to pass. The beauty here is that you don’t have to memorize dozens of scoring rules; you simply aim for patterns: e.g., “I just need two maki cards and I’m set,” or “If I can grab sashimi three-of-a-kind, I win big.” The travel edition keeps the math nice and digestible while letting you string along a couple of satisfying combo chains.

## On-the-ground practicality: packing, setup, and playtime

### Packing tips for a smooth trip

- Use a protective sleeve for the tin to avoid dents from luggage mishaps.
- Carry a small microfiber cloth to keep card faces visible in dim station lighting.
- Include a compact scorepad or use the in-game track, but if you’re a data hound, a tiny notebook works better for you.
- Consider a lightweight travel chair or a stable surface; a wobbly tray table can ruin your plans faster than a soggy cucumber roll.

### Setup speed and playtime expectations

Expect a typical 2–5 player match to land in the 15–25 minute window, depending on how many players chase high-value combinations and how chatty the table becomes. That window makes it perfect for a mid-travel session and a quick palate-clearing break between activities. If you’re a gamer who likes long, sprawling campaigns, you’ll need to temper your expectations. But if you want a quick, snack-sized victory before the next boarding announcement, this edition is your go-to.

### House rules and friendly variants

Travel games thrive on lightweight variants. You can try a “no-passing-on-round-two” rule to quicken rounds, or a “nerd mode” where players discuss probability for each choice like it’s the difference between making it to the gate or missing a connection. The beauty is that the edition invites flavor without forcing your group into a new moral calculus.

### Strengths
