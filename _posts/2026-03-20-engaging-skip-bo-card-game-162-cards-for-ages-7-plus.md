## What is Skip-Bo? A quick primer
Skip-Bo is a card-building game where players race to empty their personal stock piles by playing cards in numerical order on central build piles. The catch is that the central piles must be built from 1 upward, and players can only play if they have the appropriate card in hand or can draw it from their discard piles. The box claims 162 cards plus a few rules cards, which means enough content to justify a Saturday afternoon with multiple rounds and generous heckling between turns.

Key ideas:
- Build piles in ascending order. The first to exhaust their stock pile wins.
- Discards and hand management matter. If you hoard cards, you might stall your own progress.
- The game blends luck with strategy, so even the best player can be undone by a lucky draw and a daring move from a neighbor.

For those curious about the meta, this is not purely “shuffle until you win.” It’s more like a strategic shuffle where you pretend you are playing solitaire while your kitchen table becomes a tiny, chaotic arena. To the skeptical reader who wonders if a deck with 162 cards is overkill, we say: yes, yes it is, but it is glorious overkill.

## How to Play: a quick primer and a few pro moves
Skip-Bo has rules that are simple to learn but surprisingly hard to master. Here’s the quick run-through:
- Each turn: draw up to your hand limit, then play cards from your hand or discard piles to the central build piles in increasing order starting from 1.
- You can only play from your discard piles if the cards there can be moved in order; this forces you to plan several moves ahead, which is where the fun begins.
- Build piles can only be built in ascending order. You can use wild cards to substitute any number to keep the chain going.
- The first player to empty their stock pile wins. That stock pile is the ultimate pressure point: you need to be efficient, focused, and a little ruthless.

Now, how do you win? The short version is: balance speed with timing. You don’t want to rush your draw if you can’t follow through. You also don’t want to give away your “build” opportunities at the exact wrong second. It’s a game of chess with cards, where the pieces are bright, loud, and occasionally insistent about who gets to go next.

Common beginner mistakes:
- Over-accumulating in your hand and not using the discard piles effectively.
- Forgetting that wilds can save you at critical moments instead of hoarding them for a dramatic endgame.
- Misreading the number order on the central piles because you are too focused on what your neighbor is about to do.

A solid starter strategy includes keeping a flexible hand, using wilds early when you can set up multiple sequences, and paying attention to what your opponents are building toward.
