## Introduction: What this game even is
City of Mist is a tabletop role-playing game that leans into the smoky, neon-lit fantasy of a city that’s as much a character as any detective. Your heroes aren’t caped crusaders with an unpronounceable superpower; they’re ordinary people who are also legends in disguise—their “mythos” is the thing that makes them special, while their “logos” is what makes them human, with flaws that bite as hard as the villains they face. It’s a game about identity, crime, and the way we narrate ourselves into power. Think hardboiled detectives who discover that the new mayor is not just corrupt, but a literal embodiment of a myth you’ve tucked away in your psyche since childhood.

This Starter Set is the gateway drug to that vibe. It’s not the full-flavored, rulebook-hungry experience; it’s a curated bite-sized portion designed to give you the mechanics, the mood, and enough content to run a couple of sessions without needing to loot a library’s worth of PDFs. If you’ve ever whispered, “I want to be a legendary person who still pays taxes,” you’re in the right neighborhood. If you’ve ever shouted, “But what about the proper combat rules?”—well, hold your horses. City of Mist isn’t built on brutal crunch; it’s built on storytelling leverage and camera-ready character hooks.

## What’s in the Starter Set
If you’re the type who sniffs components first and then decides if the game passes the vibe check, you’ll want the inventory rundown. The Starter Set is designed to be compact, but that doesn’t mean stingy. It includes a compact rule guide with the core mechanics, pre-made investigators to drop into your first session, a few mission briefs to spark ideas, and enough cards and tokens to make the table feel alive without turning the kitchen into a boardroom.

- A concise quick-start rulebook that covers the essential mechanics: how you roll, how you escalate, and how you narrate a scene that matters.
- A deck of Power Tags: the game’s emotional power cards that help you define your actions, your enemies, and the way your mythos interacts with the real world. These behave like prompts rather than strict rules, which is perfect for a table that’s more improv than calculus.
- Pre-made investigators: three ready-to-play characters built around noir-ish mythos combos. They’re not ultrapwny superheroes; they’re people who become legends under pressure, which means they feel relatable and risky in equal measure.
- A quick-start city map and scene briefs: a set of sample locations and scenarios to jumpstart your first sessions. They’re built to leg up your storytelling, not to trap you into a single path—though the sessions will try to trap you anyway, in the most dramatic way possible.
- A GM screen (thematic art and quick references): helpful for GMs who want a transparent session flow but don’t want to memorize a wall of numbers.
- Tokens, signs, and setup aids: small physical pieces to help you track status and time-of-day in a game (heavily noir, lightly caffeinated).

In short: you won’t be wading through a spine-stiffing rules bible to host a night of fun. You’ll be able to run a couple of sessions, feel the mythic weight of the city, and still be able to laugh at your own narration when a villain’s monologue goes on too long.

One thing to admire here is the packaging. The Starter Set uses a matte-black aesthetic with a neon-pink accent that screams “city at midnight,” and the rulebook is designed to be held without a breaking back from a full beer-in-hand session. It’s ergonomic and stylish in a way that says, “You’re allowed to be glamorous while solving crimes.” If you’re a content-creation type, you’ll appreciate the ambient mood art that doesn’t over-stretch your brain with busy layouts.

## How the system works (in a nutshell)
City of Mist runs on a narrative-first engine. You’re not grinding for the biggest damage output; you’re parroting the city’s vibe and guiding a fellow player toward a character beat that matters. Mechanics-wise, you’ll use a pool of six-sided dice to resolve tasks, using your Power Tags to evoke your mythos and your Logos—your grounded, human side. The balance is deliberate: you’re rewarded for storytelling and character decisions that feel thematically true even if they’re not the most efficient in a spreadsheet sense. This is not a fatal flaw; it’s a design choice that aligns with the game’s mood of myth emerging from the everyday.

In practice, that means: you tell a story beat; the GM acknowledges the beat’s significance; the players lean into “Will this mythic moment actually work in the real world?” Then you roll and narrate how the city’s powers respond to the moment. It’s a dance between the obvious (you want to punch bad guys) and the subtle (your secret identity complicates the plan). The Starter Set captures this balance with careful, lightweight rules, so you’re not stuck in a long monologue about probabilities. The goal is momentum: you want the table to be moving, the tension to peak, and the mystery to emerge from the choices people make when they stop to think about who they are in the moment.

## The thematic flavor and vibe
City of Mist is unapologetically atmospheric. It asks you to lean into the noir fantasy vibe: rainy nights, hard decisions, and the fearsome idea that every person around you could be more than they appear. The Starter Set nails the mood with art direction that feels cinematic without being overdone. You’ll see posters and graffiti that suggest legends hiding in plain sight. You’ll hear a soundtrack in your head (I hope you’re listening to something moody while you read this). The rules work with that mood, not against it. The mechanics are designed to enable the storytelling focus: if you want to describe a moment where a petty thief reveals a greater truth about the city’s hidden mythos, the system gives you the tools to make that moment land, not a technical glossary that makes you feel like you’re cramming for a test.

The mythos/logos duality is a clever framework; it invites players to examine both their grand ambitions and the messy, sometimes ridiculous realities of daily life. If your group loves power fantasies with a killer twist of identity crisis, City of Mist is your jam. If you prefer raw, crunchy combat and policies of “you must roll higher than your opponent’s AC,” this might feel like a spicy dessert you didn’t order. The Starter Set makes that mood approachable, with mission briefs and character prompts that encourage you to lean into the dramatic tension.

## Artwork, production quality, and how it feels at the table
The physical components are well-made, and the art direction translates well to a table—especially the GM screen and the player aids. The cards are sturdy, the tokens are legible, and the city map feels intentionally designed to be road-tested under a few hours of play. The typography is readable, the color palette pops, and the overall packaging conveys a sense of “you’re about to uncover something bigger.” If you’re the sort who borrows a friend’s fancy coffee before a game and asks for a refill, you’ll appreciate the ambiance this Starter Set delivers even when you’re not actively playing.

The tension here is that the Starter Set might leave you wanting more. It’s intentionally a taste: enough to know if you’re in for the full experience, but not enough to satiate the cravings of a long-running campaign. If you’re a serious GM who wants to orchestrate a sprawling season arc, you’ll want to dive into the core rules and the expansion materials. But if you’re a new group, the Starter Set is a gateway to a city of potential, a playground where your friends can try on legends without the fear of breaking too much (or any). In Geeknite terms: it’s a stylish starter pistol, not a cannon. It gives you a sense of the city’s pulse, without risking a head-on collision with a rulebook police squad.

## How it plays at the table: table dynamics and pacing
In our sessions, the Starter Set games moved along with a steady rhythm: a scene arrives, the players choose a mythos and a Logos beat to push the scene forward, and then someone makes a roll that either confirms the vibe or twists it into something messier and more delicious. The dice pool resolution keeps things accessible; you don’t need to memorize dozens of modifiers. The Power Tags, meanwhile, become a kind of narrative currency. They let players offer a flourish or a risk, and the GM can lean into a consequence that feels satisfying without becoming punitive. The result is something that plays well for a first night and scales neatly as players accumulate more mythos and stories.

One of the most satisfying moments in our sessions was when a character, thinking about their own tragic backstory, used a Power Tag to unlock an off-screen memory that recontextualized an entire case in a single line. The table leaned into the reveal with a chorus of nods and a couple of “oh, snap” reactions. That’s not just good fiction; it’s good RPG design. The Starter Set handles that scenario with a gentle touch, inviting players to lean into the dramatic beat without forcing them to conflate it with the most complicated tactical choices. If you’re new to RPGs, you’ll likely feel empowered to contribute something memorable within a session or two, rather than waiting for the perfect rule-narrow moment that never arrives.

## Pros and Cons (the quick verdict)
Pros:
- Strong mood and theme that blend noir detective vibes with mythic legends.
- Lightweight, approachable rules that emphasize storytelling over math.
- High production quality with mood-enhancing art and components.
- Quick-start content to run sessions in a single evening without a rulebook marathon.
- The Power Tags mechanic is intuitive and encourages player collaboration.

Cons:
- The Starter Set is not a substitute for the full rulebook; if you love the game, you’ll want the core rules to dig deeper.
- Some players who crave crunchy combat options may feel the system leans too narratively and not enough on tactical complexity.
- The two-chapter per session pacing can feel a bit short for groups that like long, multi-night arcs; you’ll likely finish a couple of stories in a single sitting and want more.

If you’re the kind of gamer who wants a fast entry to a world where your character’s myth and your city’s secrets collide, the positives far outweigh the cons. If you’re a table that needs high crunch, there are other games for you—City of Mist isn’t shy about leaning into the story-first approach.

## Quick-start guide (H3)
### 1) Set the mood
Prep a single session in a moody setting: rain, neon reflections, a case that seems too neat to be true. Let players introduce their mythos in a single sentence: what makes you extraordinary, and what personal cost does that power impose?
### 2) Pick your investigators
Use the three pre-made characters as templates, then encourage players to craft their own if you want a longer first arc. The Starter Set gives you enough to show players that mythos is flexible and that legends can be fragile at the same time.
### 3) Run a concise case
Use one of the included briefs as a pilot. Don’t overstructure the arc; have a couple of beats that you want to land and let the players fill in the gaps with their own ideas.
### 4) Lean on Power Tags
Encourage players to describe how their tag interacts with the environment. The more descriptive, the more momentum you’ll gain and the more you’ll reveal about the city itself.
### 5) Debrief and iterate
Ask your players what matters most about the story. What mythos did they lean into? What did they learn about themselves? Use the feedback to guide your next session.
