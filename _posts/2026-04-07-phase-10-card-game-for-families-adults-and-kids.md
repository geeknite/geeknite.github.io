## What is Phase 10 Anyway?

Phase 10 is a fold-and-go card game that uses a special Phase Deck. The deck contains numbers 1 through 12 in four colors, plus wild cards and skip cards. The objective is simple in spirit and surprisingly tricky in practice: complete ten specific phases, one by one, before your friends do. Each phase requires a different arrangement of cards, such as a run of numbers, groups of the same value, or a mix of both. Once a player completes a phase, they advance to the next one and start filling their hand with new cards to prepare for the next target.

The rules are compact and easy to pick up. A typical turn means you draw a card, try to lay down cards that fit your current phase, and discard. If you can lay down your required cards, you move on to the next phase. If you cannot, you keep drawing until you can. It is a little like chasing a moving target while dodging the occasional skip card that tries to send you back to square one. The combination of luck and the occasional strategic choice keeps the game lighthearted yet frequently nail-biting.

## How to Play: The Core Loop

Phase 10 operates on a repeating loop that is elegant in its simplicity and merciless in its pacing. Each player has a hand of cards. The first phase on the board is straightforward, but as you move forward, the requirements get trickier. Meanwhile, the action remains friendly and chaos-friendly, which means you will likely end up winking at the person across the table who somehow pulled off a run of nine while you still have a couple of stray cards that do not belong to any known combination.

### Turn structure
- Draw a card (from the draw pile or the discard pile, depending on the house rules).
- Play cards that match your current phase. You can also add cards to your own laid-down phase or take advantage of wild cards to complete the phase in unconventional ways.
- Discard one card to end your turn.

If you complete your phase, you advance to the next phase and begin building toward it with your new hand. If you finish all ten phases, you win. If you are playing with a tall-tale storyteller, someone might insist that completing phase ten is a personal life achievement you should frame and hang on the wall next to the family photo albums.

### The Phases (a quick primer)
- Phase 1: 2 sets of 3 or more cards of the same value
- Phase 2: 1 set of 3 or more + 1 run of 4 or more
- Phase 3: 1 set of 4 + 1 run of 4
- Phase 4: 1 run of 7
- Phase 5: 1 run of 8
- Phase 6: 1 run of 9
- Phase 7: 2 sets of 4
- Phase 8: 1 set of 4 + 1 run of 5
- Phase 9: 1 set of 5 + 1 set of 2
- Phase 10: 1 set of 5 + 1 set of 5

The exact rules can vary by edition and by family. The important part is that each phase has a different challenge, which means the game rewards flexibility and careful planning more than brute force luck.

## Strategies, Tips, and House Rules

Phase 10 is not just about luck. It rewards players who manage their hands with a blend of foresight and opportunism. Here are a few practical tips to get more wins at your table:

- Plan a couple of phases ahead. If you can anticipate your next target, you can arrange your hand so you can knock it out with a single well-timed discard.
- Use wild cards strategically. They can either complete a phase more efficiently or serve as flexible fillers when you are stuck. Don’t waste them on a marginal improvement; save them for the moments that matter most.
- Don’t forget the discard pile. Sometimes you can deduce what other players need and discard cards that deprive them of the chance to finish their phase easily. This is where games become war stories about who poked whom with a joker at precisely the wrong moment.
- Adjust for kids and adults differently. If you have mixed ages, consider shorter play sessions and simpler house rules for younger players. You can always go back to the full rules later when you are in the mood for a longer cerebral battle.

House rules that can make the game more accessible and more fun:
- Quick hand rule: deal each player 8 cards instead of 10 for a shorter game that still yields plenty of drama.
- Urban lean rule: allow players to draw both from the draw pile and discard pile on a turn, making it easier to complete phases for younger players.
- Skip mercy rule: when a player hits a phase, the other players each take one extra turn before the next phase begins so there is more social interaction. It slows the pace in a good way for bigger crowds.
