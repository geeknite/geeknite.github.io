### Scale and Base Design

The Fortune Row figures approach the typical City of Mist scale with a height in the 28-32 mm range depending on pose and perspective. The bases are standard round bases (25-28 mm) with a few variants to accommodate standing, leaning, and dynamic leaps. The bases have light texture details—cobblestones, grate patterns, and frost-dusted pavement—that read well under a wash and dry brush. The effect is a set that can blend into a mixed-bag of minis from other lines, but still maintain a distinct silhouette that looks right at home in noir settings.

If you prefer display-friendly bases, you can easily convert the pieces to scenic bases featuring alley bricks or rooftop edges. The key is to not over-pin or over-stress the slender feet; the posture is elegant but not overly delicate.

## Material, Casting, and Build Quality

Fortune Row is cast in resin, which means you will encounter the classic resin-work realities: tiny pieces, fine details, and the occasional hairline seam that begs for a hobby knife. The resin quality is consistent, which translates into crisp lines on the leather textures and subtle fabric folds. If you have ever cleaned a resin model and then come away with a smug sense of accomplishment, you know the feeling The Fortune Row set is designed to be hobby-friendly rather than a model-building ordeal.

Casting is generally clean, with a few minor spots where a light break in the casting line might appear along the edge of a scarf or the corner of a brim. A quick file or a careful soak in warm water will take care of any residual mold release. If you paint, you will likely hit the best results by priming with a light grey or white undercoat to catch the fine lines before you apply color. Do not fear the tiny details; they reward careful brush work and a little patience.

One small caveat about resin minis: they are porous, and color priming may reveal potential nibble marks around the edges if you rush the process. The Fortune Row set seems to respond well to a two-coat primer, followed by a gentle airbrush layer for shading. But if you are a spray-only painter who swears by “one coat, done,” you might want to try a sink or soak test to confirm compatibility with your preferred paint brand.

## Painting and Table-Top Playability

If you are already the type of hobbyist who color-codes characters like an Excel spreadsheet on a caffeine binge, Fortune Row will feel like a gift. The level of detail offers plenty of opportunities for different paint schemes, from classic noir black-and-white schemes to neon-tinted, color-splashed looks that scream Night City meets Gotham by way of a rainy evening in a city that never sleeps.

A practical painting plan:

- Basecoat with a neutral shade (neutral greys or browns) to anchor skin tones and fabrics. This gives you a reliable foundation for shading and highlights.
- Build up midtones with slightly more saturated colors to define clothing and accessories.
- Use a dark wash in the recesses to bring out the creases and textures; a bit of a glaze across metallics will help the gleam of a belt buckle or a knife catch the light.
- Highlight raised edges with brighter tones to emphasize the silhouette against the urban backdrop of your scenario. Remember: you are painting characters who are used to slinking through fog and neon—subtle highlights can convey speed and danger without looking garish.

For table-top use, these minis excel in a noir mood. The dynamic poses invite movement; you can stage a chase through a rain-slick alley, a tense negotiation in a backroom, or a dramatic revelation under a flickering streetlamp. The figures pair well with City of Mist’s storytelling approach, which emphasizes the tension between a split reality—masked identities and the real world. You can map each figure to a corresponding aspect of the Player Characters (PCs) or to a set of antagonists with motives that are as ambiguous as the city itself.

To capture the right vibe, you may want to set up a quick board that emphasizes narrative beats: a social interaction stage (for The Oracle), a chase path (for The Runner), and a hide-and-seek corner (for The Gambler). The Fortune Row set shines when used as a catalyst for the session’s dramatic shifts: a single figurine can prompt a flashback or a new clue, turning a routine encounter into a memory that will haunt the players long after the dice are put away.
