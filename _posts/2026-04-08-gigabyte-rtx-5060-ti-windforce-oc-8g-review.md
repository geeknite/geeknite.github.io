## Overclocking Headroom and Practical Tweaks
Since we’re dealing with an OC variant, the card invites a little experimentation in the form of a modest overclock and a few cooling tweaks. Here’s what to expect and how to approach it responsibly:

- Core Clock: The factory OC is a foundation; a light manual offset can push a bit more headroom without pushing power draw into the red. The exact uplift depends on the silicon lottery and thermal headroom in your chassis.
- Memory Clock: Given the 28 Gbps memory speed, an increment here can yield small but tangible improvements in texture-heavy scenes. Don’t overdo it; memory overclock can push VRAM temperatures higher than you want in a compact case.
- Power Target: A modest bump is often sufficient. If your case cooling is excellent, you may push a little further; if not, avoid aggressive voltage targets that can shorten the card’s life and your patience.
- Thermal Performance as a Constraint: The Windforce OC design is capable, but like any real-world OC, you’ll be limited by the case airflow, ambient temperature, and the fan curve you choose. A well-ventilated case with clean cables helps the card breathe, which in turn helps maintain clock stability.

In practice, you’ll likely settle into a comfortable, stable boost around 50–100 MHz above stock in many games, with memory offsets of 50–150 MHz. The exact numbers vary by silicon lottery and driver optimizations, but that’s the gist. If you’re chasing huge overclock results, you’ll want to approach with a benching setup, an external power meter, and a backup plan in case you push a bit too far and see unexpected throttling.
