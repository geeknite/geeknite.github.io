## Hardware Overview: A Time Capsule with a Modern Soft Spot
### CPU and RAM
The star of this show is a dual-core processor that’s had its coffee and decided to keep going anyway. It’s not going to melt your GPU with ray tracing, but it will spit out a perfectly respectable desktop experience for light productivity, web browsing, and the occasional cheese-tandoori-press of doom on Word. With 8GB of RAM, you’ll be able to run a handful of tabs without resorting to heroic multitasking strategies. If you’re a browser tab hoarder, you’ll want to consider an upgrade before you start video conferencing with 12 tabs and a YouTube playlist playing at the same time.

### Storage: 240GB SSD as the Emphatic Heartbeat
The 240GB SSD is the kind of drive that begs you to declutter your life. It’s fast enough to feel snappy for everyday tasks, but the 240GB limit means you’ll want to lean hard on cloud drives or external storage for media, large projects, or your collection of 4.3GB software installers. You can bump around Windows, install a few essential apps, and still have room for a few gigabytes of memes and offline maps of your favorite indie arcade games. 

### Graphics and I/O
Integrated graphics, modest by today’s standards, more than capable for office work, streaming 1080p video, and some light indie gaming with titles that spawn on a 720p canvas. The machine includes a DVD-RW drive—yes, a real optical drive—which will be a blessing for certain retro software installers and for those who still enjoy burning mixed CDs like a time-traveling DJ. The audio out, Ethernet, USB 3.0, and a couple of USB 2.0 ports round out the IO. If you’re chasing USB-C, you’ll need an adapter or a new motherboard, because this throwback is not the era of USB-C fluff just yet.

## Performance Benchmarks: How It Handles Real Life
### General Responsiveness
In daily tasks, the system feels fluid enough for typical office workloads. Start-up time, app launches, and the responsiveness of the taskbar are all well within the expectations for a budget desk PC from this era. If you’re the kind of user who likes to open 12 tabs and then watch a video, you might observe occasional stutters when the browser is perched on memory management. For the price, this is a survivable, friendly performance that won’t send you toward the nearest anti-anxiety supply store.

### Browser and Web Apps
Web browsing is where this machine shines; it’s got the RAM to support multiple tabs, and the SSD ensures snappy page loads once the content is retrieved from the cloud. You’ll find that heavy pages with lots of ads might push the CPU to ~70-90% usage, but it will still respond well enough for most tasks. For people who are into streaming and productivity, this is a decent pairing: 1080p videos play without jank; video calls are acceptable on moderate bandwidth connections. 

### Storage Tests
We ran a handful of cold-boot tests, measured file copying speeds, and tested typical office workloads. The 240GB SSD shines with fast boot times; you can expect 15-20 seconds to the login screen on a cold boot, which is not bad given the era. File copy speeds hover around 450-520 MB/s for sequential reads, while writes may slow down under heavy mixed workloads. It’s not the fastest thing around, but it’s enough to feel modern and gives you breathing room for a moderate software library. 

### Gaming and Media Playback
Do not purchase this PC for modern AAA gaming; that would be hilarious in the worst way. If you want to play retro games or indie titles that aren’t graphically brutal, you might be okay at low settings at 720p. For streaming 1080p content, this machine handles it, with occasional stutter if your browser is also chewing heavy tasks. If you’re a cinephile or a gamer, we recommend using this as a dedicated workstation and pairing it with a modern GPU in a separate budget build for the heavy lifting.
