## Design and Build Quality

The 17MT70 uses a magnesium-aluminum alloy vibe with a light, rigid shell. ### Ports and Cable Management
On the edge you’ll find the USB-C input—your gateway to video, data, and (in many cases) power delivery. A secondary USB-C port can operate as a pass-through for power, meaning you can keep your laptop charged while you work without dragging around a wall socket like a medieval squire. There’s no abundance of ports, but the layout is refreshingly simple: one USB-C for video and power, and a couple of minor slots for future-proofing or optional accessories. The cable channel keeps things tidy; you won’t be wrestling with a spaghetti of cords when you want to stow the monitor away.

## Display Quality and Real-World Visuals
### Panel Type and Resolution
The 17MT70 features a high-resolution IPS panel—likely around 2560x1600—providing a generous workspace for spreadsheets, dashboards, code, and multi-document workflows. The color space coverage is sufficient for everyday tasks; it leans toward a natural look that’s easy on the eyes for long sessions. If you’re a pixel peeper chasing perfect color accuracy for a living, you’ll want a dedicated color-accurate monitor, but for most of us, the 17MT70’s palette lands in a comfortable middle ground.
### Brightness, Contrast, and Viewing Angles
In typical indoor environments, brightness around 300–350 nits keeps the display legible in standard office light. The anti-glare coating helps reduce reflections near windows or in bustling coffee shops. The viewing angles are solid; you won’t experience severe color shifts as you move sideways. The contrast ratio is good enough to separate UI elements and keep text crisp, which matters when you’re reading code or reviewing dense docs.
### Color Accuracy and Calibration
Out of the box, colors look pleasing, with a bias toward natural skin tones and realistic greens and blues. If you’re editing photos or doing UI design that, you know, matters, you’ll likely want a quick calibration pass using your system’s color calibration tool. It’s not a dedicated pro monitor; it’s a portable second screen. For many, that means “good enough for work,” which is exactly the kind of pragmatic verdict we love at Geeknite.

### UI and Desktop Real Estate
Extended real estate is the key win here. Dragging windows across two large canvases feels liberating after months on a single 15–16 inch laptop screen. The 17MT70 effectively doubles your desktop space, letting you place reference materials on the submonitor while you actively type or code on the Gram’s main display. It’s a productivity accelerant, not a novelty.

## Connectivity and Setup Experience
### Setup Essentials
Plugging in is simple: connect the USB-C to your Gram 17 and you’re golden. If your Gram supports power delivery through USB-C, you can power both devices from a single charger, which is a major convenience for travelers and minimalists alike. There are no drivers to install on Windows or macOS; the system detects the extra display automatically, and you’re off to the races in a few seconds.
### Extending, Mirroring, and Multi-Tasking
Windows makes it straightforward to extend or mirror the display. The general experience is smooth with no major lag during drag-and-drop or window resizing. The 60Hz refresh rate is standard for portable displays and is more than enough for productivity, web browsing, and light media tasks. If you’re hoping to game on the side, you’ll want to temper expectations—this is a secondary display first and a gaming companion second.
### Keyboard and Typing Comfort with a Submonitor
Typing while the submonitor is angled on a desk can feel odd at first, but you quickly acclimate. The extra screen space means you can keep your main document uncluttered while you reference a web page or API documentation on the submonitor. The ergonomic result is a less cramped hands-on-laptop experience, which translates into fewer neck twists and more sustained productivity.

- Productivity power-user: You’re editing a large spreadsheet while keeping the financial model on the submonitor. Your code editor sits on the main Gram display, with console output quietly streaming on the second screen. This separation makes complex tasks feel less chaotic and more manageable.

### Practical Limitations to Consider
No device is perfect, and the 17MT70 has its fair share of trade-offs. The color accuracy is not at the level of a calibrated professional panel; it’s a portable device designed for convenience and extended workflow rather than color-critical tasks. There are no built-in speakers, so you’ll rely on headphones or external speakers for audio tasks. It’s also not the cheapest peripheral in its class, so you’re paying for a cohesive Gram look and a polished plug-and-play experience rather than the cheapest price. If you’re a power-user with a strict color workflow, you may want to weigh this against a more robust portable monitor with factory-calibrated color profiles.
