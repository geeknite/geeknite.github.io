## What is Sushi Go?
Sushi Go is a fast, family-friendly card drafting game where players are assembling the best assortment of sushi dishes as they zoom past the table like a tiny, adorable culinary relay race. On each turn, you pick one card from a hand and pass the rest to the next player. You’re not drafting for a grand grand strategy—this is lean, breezy optimization with a timer-ticking sense of chaos that makes it feel like a fast-running meme about balancing your ego and appetite.

The objective is simple: score the most points by collecting specific combinations of cards across several rounds. There are mini-sets for nigiri, sashimi, tempura, maki, and a few other cheeky dishes, each with its own scoring rules. You’ll be chasing a nice mix of immediate points and long-term boons—like using Wasabi to triple the next Nigiri you grab, which is basically the culinary version of “save it for the boss fight.”

For the curious, Sushi Go is a stand-alone card game from a family of sushi-themed titles, and it also branches into Sushi Go Party!, which expands the party-sized buffet with more choices and more ways to customize. If you’re a rules minimalist, Sushi Go provides all the thrills you want in a snack-sized package. If you’re a rules maximalist, Sushi Go Party! is waiting in the wings with even more combos, more Japanese culinary cunning, and more excuses to break out those plastic sushi emojis you’ve been holding onto since 2011.

## How to Play: A Quick Tour
If you’ve played any drafting game before, you’ll feel right at home. If not, fear not—we’ll walk you through the basics without roasting your brain over a sushi knife.

### Setup
- Shuffle the sushi cards and deal a hand to each player. The number of cards in hand depends on player count; in practice, you’ll have enough cards to keep the table lively but not overwhelmed.
- Place a score track in easy reach; you’ll be tallying rounds and later evaluating pudding bonuses, which are the sneaky little cherries on top of this pastry of a game.
- Decide who goes first. In two-player games you’ll still follow the same draft-and-pass mechanic, though the rhythm is a tad more intense since you’re passing to a partner who is only slightly less competent at card counting than you are.

### Turn Structure
- On your turn, you select one card from your hand and place it face-down in front of you, then pass the remaining cards to the player on your left. Your choice is usually about which synergy you’re chasing now and what you want to block your neighbors from getting.
- After all players have chosen, you reveal your chosen cards and move to the next round, repeating until the deck runs out.
- The next mini-round begins with everyone again drafting one card from their new hand.

This simple loop—draft, pass, reveal—drives the entire game and creates moments of glorious miscommunication, strategic misreads, and accidental table-selected spicy tuna that will haunt you for days. The pace is brisk: most matches wrap up in about 15–20 minutes, which makes it a perfect “one more game” filler or a light palate cleanser between heavier titles.

### Scoring (The Snack Map)
Scoring in Sushi Go is where the magic happens and where you learn to balance short-term gains with long-term setups. Here are the core categories you’ll encounter:

- Nigiri: Each nigiri card is worth points—typically 1, 2, or 3 points. The twist: Wasabi is a multiplier for your next Nigiri, so timing matters. If you manage to pair Wasabi with a high-value Nigiri, you can swing a round with a single PANG of delicious math.
- Tempura: A pair of Tempura cards is worth a set number of points (commonly around 5). If you only collect one Tempura, you’ll be disappointed; the two-card set is where the glory hides.
- Sashimi: A trio of Sashimi cards yields a big payoff (usually around 10 points). If you snag two Sashimi, that’s not nothing, but you’ll be quietly nibbling your own disappointment in the corner.
- Maki: The player with the most Maki cards gets a large bonus, with a smaller bonus for the second-most. It’s a risk-reward gambit: if you’re trailing in other points, you might go all-in on Maki to steal the late glory.
- Pudding: Pudding cards score at game end, not during the early rounds. They’re the “season finale”: the player with the most pudding cards can rack up a surprising chunk of points, potentially changing who wins from “solidly ahead” to “uncomfortably close.”
- Chopsticks: A fun utility card. When you play a Chopstick, you’re setting up a future turn where you can pick two cards at once. It’s clever misdirection that can allow you to execute a second, sneaky plan if you’re paying attention.

Scorekeeping can feel like a micro-sudoku puzzle, but the rules are straightforward enough that you’ll forget you ever dreaded math in the first place. The key is to read the room—are you playing with folks who chase the big combos, or those who are happy just collecting cute pieces of edible art? The answer to that question will steer your drafting decisions for the entire night.

## Strategy Tips for the Hungry and the Witty
Let’s cut to the chase with a few practical tips you can actually use on the table, in the spirit of a game night where you’re not trying to out-math your friend, you’re trying to out-laugh them.

- Plan a two-round arc: In a 15–20 minute game, you’ll be drafting in several rounds. It’s smarter to target a small set of cards early and look for complementing cards later rather than chasing a sprawling dream that collapses by round two.
- Don’t ignore Wasabi: It’s not much by itself, but it makes your next Nigiri explode in value. If you can use Wasabi with a high-value Nigiri (3 points) you’ll often lock down more than a single point.
- Tempura and Sashimi require tempo: The Tempura two-card set and Sashimi three-card set reward patience. If you’re behind on those vibes, pick one or two chips toward those sets rather than sprinting for a lone high-value card.
- Read your tablemates: Sushi Go rewards a little social deduction. If someone keeps taking cards you’d want, pivot to a different strategy—sometimes the best move is to deny a rival their preferred path.
- Use Chopsticks wisely: The chopsticks card is the “save for later” button. If you see a future combo you want, prepare to trigger it with one extra card in a later round.
- Endgame pudding shenanigans: If you’re ahead on other points, consider stacking pudding and forcing a close race at the end. If you’re behind, a last-minute pudding surge might be all you need to catch up.
- Two-player nuance: In 2-player games, the pacing tightens. It’s easier to anticipate your opponent’s moves, but the danger is that the rounds feel shorter, so you must exploit every edge while avoiding eye-rolls over perfect plays.

## Variants, Expansions, and How Much World There Is Beyond the Tray
Sushi Go Party! is the big brother to Sushi Go. It expands the food procession with dozens of different card sets and allows for more players and more variety in each game. The base game is lean—and that lean is part of its charm—but the Party edition gives you extra spice, like adding more nigiri types, more maki twists, and more ways to customize your scoring with a not-so-subtle wink toward “your table is your own mini-restaurant.” If you crave re-playability without needing a full-blown board game library, Sushi Go Party! is often the recommended upgrade.

If you’re hunting for a mix of speed and depth, Sushi Go Party! is a solid upgrade while preserving the core vibe. ## Who Should Play Sushi Go?
- Casual gamers who want something fast, light, and instantly satisfying. If you’re the sort of person who loves flash-moveness and cute food cards, this is your jam.
- Families looking for a gentle game that teaches turn-taking, risk assessment, and simple scoring. Sushi Go scales well enough to be a talking point rather than a brain-wrecker.

It’s not a flaw; it’s a feature—like choosing a bite of chocolate mousse instead of a seven-layer cake when you just want something quick, tasty, and not too heavy to digest before the next act of the night.

## Sushi Go vs. Sushi Go Party!: A Quick Comparison
- Player count and customization: Sushi Go Party! shines if you want to host more players and mix-and-match card sets for different nights. The core rules stay the same, but with more optional cards, you can create a hundred micro-experiences without rewriting the rulebook.
- Table presence and value: The base game is ultra-compact, perfect for travel and quick coffee-shop sessions. Sushi Go Party! is a touch larger and friendlier to longer gatherings where everyone has a favorite little sushi card to defend or target.
- Learning curve: Both are approachable, but Party! gives you more cards and more combination possibilities. If you love combos and the idea of spending a long time studying potential synergies, Party! scratches that itch.
