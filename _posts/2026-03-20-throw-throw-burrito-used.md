## What is Throw Throw Burrito, Really?

Throw Throw Burrito is a fast-paced, reaction-based card game designed by confrontationally cheerful people who clearly love both chaos and snacks. Each player gets a hand of cards that represent different burritos, and on the table are two plush burritos that function as the projectiles in the game’s almost-too-simple-yet-perfect combat system. When a player plays a matching card or a specific action card is triggered, players race to grab a burrito and “tag” their opponents, scoring points by either achieving a burrito-based objective or simply being faster on the draw.

In a standard game, players collect sets of three matching burrito cards to score. But in the real world, especially with used copies, you’ll discover that the real scoring often happens in the moments right after a burrito swipes your sleeve, a card shuffles into a new, chaos-inducing pattern, or someone yells something that becomes the caption of the night. The result is a dynamic, loud, and gleefully chaotic experience that rewards quick reflexes and even quicker apologies when you accidentally bonk someone with a burrito that’s now soaked with salsa-flavored tears (figuratively, please).

### Components and their aged personalities

A used copy usually comes with:

- 2 plush burritos (one green, one red, each with a soft, squeaky core and occasional pop from a fold that could be a memory of a foam battle)
- 1 deck of burrito cards (colorful, giggle-inducing art, some corners worn, some cards creased, some with lunch-like fingerprints)
- 1 rulebook (small, surprisingly durable, and now with margins enriched by someone’s doodles from a high school math class) 
- 4 player mats or standees (depending on the edition you’ve picked up and the seller’s sense of spatial awareness)
- 1 mini plastic score tracker (be mindful: used copies may have sticky residue from snacks, or, in a more poetic sense, memories of parties past)

If you’re buying used, the key to happiness is knowing how far the wear goes before it starts to impede play. Plush burritos should be soft enough to squeeze and not so torn that stuffing escapes like a confetti cannon. Cards should be flat and not splattered with unknown substances. Rulebook should be legible, not too dog-eared, and ideally free of doodles that make you question whether the person who drew them had a side quest called “Find the Burrito.”

### Condition checks you actually need to perform

When inspecting a used Throw Throw Burrito copy, you’re basically doing a micro-audit of a very loud life form:

- Plush burritos: Check seams for rips, stuffing integrity, and odor. If a burrito smells like a kitchen experiment, it can be cleaned with care, or you can decide that a slightly questionable aroma is part of the game’s character arc. If the fabric has pilling or a loose seam, that’s a sign your burritos might be about to confess their sins in public.
- Cards: Look for water damage, warping, and sharp corners. Warped cards can still be playable, but you’ll feel the crease in your brain as you try to locate a matching symbol under a 3D burrito tide.
- Rulebook: Ensure all pages are readable and that the rules don’t require a neural implant to parse. A well-bent page is a badge of honor; a torn page is a sign that your crew really leaned into the chaos.
- Score tracker and mats: The plastic pieces should snap into place and not feel like they’ll crack at any moment. A wobbly score tracker is a small catastrophe waiting to happen mid-game.
- General wear: Smudges, coffee rings, and mystery stains tell stories. If those stories include a nightmare of a party where the burritos won, you’re in the right territory for a legendary night.

If all that checks out, you’ve probably found a gem that has seen a few rounds of laughter but still wants to throw down one more time. If not, you can often negotiate with the seller for a discount, or you can decide that the used copy’s flaws give it a certain “character edition” charm that fits your party’s vibe.

## The Gameplay Experience: Quick Refresher (With a Used Lens)

The core loop of Throw Throw Burrito is simple enough to explain to a stoned alphabet: play a card, draw the burritos, and try to grab the burritos before your opponents do when a match triggers. The chaos escalates quickly. A used copy may reveal subtle differences in the card stock, but the chaos remains the same: you’re racing, dodging, shouting, and probably stepping on a burrito at least once per game.

- Setup: Each player gets a hand of burrito cards. In the center, the two plush burritos sit within reach, like a pair of cult leaders whispering about who’s next to declare war on the snack table.
- Turn flow: On your turn, you play a burrito card or action card. Action cards can force someone to swap cards, redraw, or set up a chaotic “dodge” moment where everyone lunges for the burritos. It’s basically a party game version of a chaotic improv scene where you pretend you’re a hero in a burrito-based quest.
- Scoring: When you collect a set of matching burrito cards, you gain points. In a used copy, the scoring tapes sometimes hide under a crust of folded card corners, requiring a bit of scavenger-huntery to tally correctly. The true score, however, is the memory of the group’s laughter—the counts on the score sheet are merely ceremonial.
- End condition: The game ends when someone hits the target score. In practice, a typical session ends when your living room looks like a burrito buffet exploded in slow motion—and everyone is still laughing.

One of the secret joys of a used copy is noticing tiny aging artifacts that add flavor to the session. A slightly sticky card back from a soda spill becomes a badge of honor. A burrito that floats a little when squeezed reminds you of a memory of someone sneaking into the kitchen for a snack during a break in the game. This is not a flaw; it’s a feature that hums with the energy of shared moments.

Either way, the game is designed to handle the accidental chaos that comes with being a party game in the wild.

## Tips for Getting the Best Used Copy

- Inspect the burritos first. If the seams look intact and there’s no obvious odor, you’re in a good zone. A slight odor can be remedied with a careful wash and airing out, but strong smells might be a sign of a long, aromatic life you didn’t sign up for.
- Check the cards for warping and creases. A few bends are okay; tombstone-creased cards that become impossible to shuffle are not. If you’re a purist, you can replace the deck from a fresh set, which is usually easy to source.
- Ensure all components exist. Two burritos, a deck, a rulebook, and the score trackers are a bare minimum. A missing rulebook can be solved with the publisher’s official rules online; a missing burrito, however, is a dramatic loss of the full chaos ecosystem.
- Look for signs of moisture damage. A slightly damp box can harbor mold or mildew that’s dangerously funny in a non-funny way. If you smell it, pass on the deal or politely request a discount to cover dehumidification costs.
- Ask about shipping and condition. A seller who’s honest will reveal the rough edges and expected wear; a seller who overclaims “like new” deserves a dramatic burrito throw in their direction (in a purely theatrical, non-destructive sense).
- Consider the group’s tolerance for chaos. A used copy might come with a lore of past parties; if your group loves chaos, you’re golden. If your group is more of a calm, strategic crowd, perhaps you’ll want a new copy or a sturdier introduction to the chaos with house rules that temper the energy a bit.
