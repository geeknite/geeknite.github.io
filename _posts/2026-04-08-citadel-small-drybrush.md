## What is a small drybrush, exactly? (H2)

A small drybrush is a compact version of the classic drybrush technique. The bristles are shorter, stiffer, and more tightly packed, which translates into better control when you want to emphasize textures on narrow surfaces—think shoulder pads, buckles, chainmail edges, and the tiny highlights along a weapon blade. It is not meant for broad coverage; that job belongs to the bigger sibling in your collection. If you treat it like a magic wand, you will be disappointed; treat it like a precision tool, and you will wonder how you painted past your own limitations for so long.

### Why size matters (H3)
- Precision on fine details: rivets, lens-glass, helmet edges, gauntlet knurling.
- Cleaner edge highlights on tiny surfaces that would smear with a larger brush.
- Less paint per stroke, which means you can build up layers slowly and reduce mistakes.
- Better control around sculpted textures that require a feather-light touch.

### Build quality and ergonomics (H3)
Citadel frequently nails the premium feel, and the Small Drybrush is no exception. The handle is compact but balanced, giving you the sense you control the brush rather than it controlling you. The ferrule is snug, not loose enough to beat a brush into submission, and the bristle cluster is trimmed to a fine point that holds its shape after cleaning sessions, which is basically the adult version of a miracle in this hobby.

## Handling and bristle performance (H2)

The bristles on the Citadel Small Drybrush are synthetic and designed to spring back after gentle pressure. They’re not super-soft like makeup brushes or feathery like some premium handmade sets, but that softness isn’t what you want for drybrushing anyway. You want the bristles to land the pigment on raised surfaces and stay out of the cracks—the bristles mustn’t drag mid-air across the miniature, creating a smeared, muddy look. The key is a light touch and a light load.

### Bristle wear and longevity (H3)
If you take care of this brush, it will survive the back-to-back painting sessions with a squad of Primaris or a regiment of goblins. Rinse after use, reshape the bristles, and avoid aggressive scrubbing on any surface that isn’t made of plastic. Over time you may notice bristles flatten a little or shed a handful of hairs after a rough wash; this is not a planet-ending catastrophe, but it is a cue to either replace the brush or prune the bristles with a gentle trim. The important bit is to avoid prying at the ferrule and to keep the bristles aligned so no one mistakes your brush for a tiny drone-sized broom.

### Loading and paint behavior (H3)
Load with a small amount of paint by dabbing gently; do not dunk or flick the brush as if you are trying to siphon coffee. The small drybrush needs a lean paint load. Too much pigment will smear and settle into crevices you meant to keep clean. The goal is micro-layering: a whisper of color on the raised edges, then another whisper on top for a subtle progression. If you can visibly see a blob of paint on the surface, you loaded too much.

## Drybrushing technique with a small brush (H2)

Drybrushing is about controlled abrasion, minus the sandpaper vibes. Here is a practical approach that yields crisp highlights on small details without turning your model into a tired, dusty potato.

### Step-by-step guide (H3)
1) Prime and basecoat your model as usual. Let the surface dry completely. Color separation helps; you want your highlights to pop, not muddle.
2) Load the brush with a lean amount of paint. Tap off excess on a cloth or paper towel until barely any color is left on the bristles.
3) Gently drag or flick the brush across raised edges. The goal is to leave color on the high points while the recesses stay relatively unpainted.
4) Inspect and adjust. If you see fat edges or blotches, you loaded too much paint or pressed too hard. Lighten your touch and try again.
5) Layer if needed. Small drybrushes excel at adding subtle gradations; you can add another light pass with a slightly different hue to simulate wear or lighting.
6) Clean up. Rinse and reshape after use to keep the tip sharp for next time.

### Common mistakes and how to avoid them (H3)
- Overloading the brush: This is the most common sin. Solution? Lean paint and small taps.
- Pressing too hard: You are not sealing a wound; you are highlighting a shoulder pad. Light, feathery strokes are your friend.
- Brushing across too many textures at once: Focus on one detail at a time for best results.
- Neglecting cleanliness: Old paint sits on bristles and makes every new stroke look muddy. Clean, rinse, repeat.

## Practical painting scenarios (H2)

### Sci-fi infantry with metal armor (H3)

### Fantasy heroes with leather straps (H3)
Leather and buckles demand a delicate touch. Drybrush the strap with a light khaki or tan to catch the raised grain, then wash lightly. The result should look like the leather has worn texture without jumping to attention as a chalky mess.

### Weathered wood and rust (H3)
With careful layering, you can use the small brush to pick out the grain on a wooden shield or the rust on a metal hook. Start with a mid-tone, then a lighter highlight along the grain, and finish with a very faint accent around the raised rough patches to simulate chipped paint.

## Maintenance and care (H2)

- Rinse with lukewarm water after use. If you used solvent-based paints, follow with a gentle soap wash.
- Shape the bristles back to a point while damp and let air dry with bristles pointing downward or upright, depending on your drying rack. Do not leave the brush bristles pressed against the ferrule.
- Store in a protective sleeve or case to avoid bristle splaying. A small ziplock with a dry brush can work, just don’t forget to label the gear.
- Periodic bristle checks are wise; if the tip becomes uneven, trim lightly with a sharp pair of scissors to maintain a crisp edge. This is not a fashion accessory; it’s a professional maintenance habit.

## Tips for building a robust brush toolkit (H2)

- Pair the small drybrush with a mid-size brush for highlights and a large flat brush for base coating.
- Keep a dedicated palette of near-edge colors (silver, gold, pale gray) for edge highlights that pop without becoming garish.
- Practice on a test model before applying to a finished army. A spare head with a similar surface is perfect practice ground.
- Label your brushes with a color-coded system so you instantly know which brush has which job. It saves mental energy for the battle of painting time versus the time you can keep your focus.
