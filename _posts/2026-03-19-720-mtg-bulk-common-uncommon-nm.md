H2: What you get in a bulk lot of commons and uncommons
H3: The broad strokes
In a typical 720-card bundle labeled NM commons and uncommons, you are likely to see a wide distribution of sets and eras. You will encounter a lot of white border and modern sets alongside some older reprints. The exact mix varies by seller and lot age, but the general pattern is predictable: tons of commons, a fair handful of uncommons, and an occasional surprise. The wow factor, if you’re chasing it, is usually short-lived because the bulk nature of the product emphasizes number over rarity.

H3: Quality and condition in bulk
Near mint is a quality descriptor that can be misleading when applied to bulk lots. In thousands of cards, minor imperfections are common. You might see a few cards with subtle edge whitening, micro scratches, a tiny crease from heavy handling, or slightly bent corners. None of these are catastrophic, but they do mean that bulk NM is a spectrum rather than a threshold. If you are a serious collector who wants pristine examples, this is not your quarry. If you are a deck-builder or a trader who can live with small blemishes, bulk NM is your playground.

H3: The optional but common mystery: the occasional rare or foil
Yes, sometimes a bulk lot will include a rare or a foil. It happens, but it is not the goal. Don’t make your purchasing decision on the hope of a rare payoff; treat it as a possible cherry on top rather than the main course. If you do pull a rare, your valuation should reflect market prices, not dreams of fortune. For the rest, you’ll be dealing with a sea of commons and a few uncommons that you can repurpose into decks or trader piles.

H2: Sorting, grading, and the big plan
H3: The step-by-step sorting plan
A practical workflow helps the bulk feel less like a chaotic pile and more like a mosaic of possibilities:
- Step 1: Empty the stack and do a quick visual skim for obvious damage. Separate anything truly worn, water-damaged, or creased.
- Step 2: Separate by color families: white, blue, black, red, green, colorless, and multicolored. Have a big tray or labeled bins for each color group.
- Step 3: Within each color, sort by rarity: common first, then uncommon. This helps you identify duplicates quickly.
- Step 4: Within each rarity, sort by mana cost bands: 1-2, 3-4, 5-6, 7+. This makes it easier to build early sets and draft-oriented decks later.
- Step 5: Scan for set alignment. If you care about set integrity, set-by-set sorting can help you group cards that belong to a single library.
- Step 6: Create a duplicate pile. These are trade bait or fodder for budget decks.
- Step 7: For any card with potential value above bulk, flag it for a closer look and price check. A quick search on Scryfall or MTGGoldfish is useful here.

H3: Practical tools and tips
- A sturdy storage solution with labeled boxes or bags helps a lot. You don’t want to be chasing a loose card around your kitchen floor at 2 am.
- A simple tallying method can help you estimate value quickly. Even rough counts help when you want to decide if you should proceed with the buy.
- A digital notepad or spreadsheet for price checks and card counting can save you headaches later. It also makes it easier to share the results with your group.

H2: Sorting sanity and time management
H3: Realistic expectations
Sorting 720 cards is a long-term project. It is not something you do in a single sitting unless you’re either a card-hoarder or a professional organizer. Plan a schedule, and break the work into bite-sized chunks.
