## What is Sushi Go Card Game?
Sushi Go is a hand-drafting card game from Gamewright that simulates a bustling sushi restaurant on a tiny countertop. Players are chefs trying to assemble the tastiest, most valuable plates by drafting one card at a time from a hand and then passing the rest around to your neighbors. It’s a game about timing, memory, and not overthinking a plate of salmon nigiri. The art is bright, the cards are sturdy, and the pace is brisk enough to turn a casual game night into a friendly sprint where every decision matters but nothing takes longer than a coffee break.

For geeks, it scratches several itches at once: drafting, set collection, and a dash of deduction as you watch the cards go around the table and try to guess what you’ll be handed next. For families, it offers easy teachability, quick rounds, and a scoring system that remains engaging even for younger players without devolving into arithmetic chaos. And for solo players who like to pretend they are a chef competing against a silent crowd of enthusiastic takeout patrons, there are ways to approximate the experience with two to four players playing at once, though the game truly shines with a table full of people who want to debate whether the uni nigiri is the king of the meal or if the sashimi deserves the crown.

## How to play: a quick tour
Sushi Go is designed to be fast to learn and—if you want—quick to memorize. The core loop repeats across three rounds, and each round is basically a sprint of decisions with a gentle ramp in difficulty as you learn the card pool.

### Setup
- Shuffle the entire deck and deal a hand of seven cards to each player (the exact number can vary slightly depending on player count, but seven is the standard for up to five players).
- Each player looks at their hand, picks one card to keep, and places it face-down. The rest of the cards are passed to the left.
- When everyone has chosen, all players reveal their chosen cards and add them to their growing board. Then the passed hand continues to the left, repeating the same pick-and-pass action until all cards in the hand are claimed and the round ends.
- After a round ends, players tally Nina-simian points for the cards collected and set aside any Pit? Not exactly; you collect the cards and prepare for the next round's hand. Yes, it’s that simple. The next two rounds repeat the same flow, with new hands dealt from the same deck structure.

### The core decision: one card, pass the rest
The heart of Sushi Go is the micro-decision with macro consequences. You pick one card from your current hand and pass the rest around. The trick is reading the table, anticipating what others will draft next, and predicting how the final scoring will shake out. It feels almost like a microgame inside a microgame: the real drama arises from what you don’t choose and how you time your Wasabi with your Nigiri.

### Scoring: a small but spicy puzzle
The scoring in Sushi Go combines several simple piles into a satisfying payoff at the end of each round and across the entire game. Here’s the lay of the land:
- Nigiri cards: These are single-card power plays that score points immediately, with values typically 1, 2, 3, or 4 points depending on the exact type. Wasabi interacts with your next Nigiri, boosting its value. The exact numbers are easy to memorize and quick to apply, which is what makes the drafting feel so smooth.
- Maki Roll cards: At the end of a round, the player with the most Maki Roll symbols earns a handful of points, with a second-place payoff that can be just as satisfying. Ties are shared, which means you can hold a tiny grudge or a tiny celebration with equal flair.
- Tempura and Sashimi cards: These come in small-but-glaring bundles. A complete set of Tempura yields points; a complete set of three Sashimi yields a chunk of points as well. Missing one card from a set means you walk away with nothing. It’s the sort of high-risk, high-reward mechanic that teases your memory and rewards careful attention to what others are collecting.
- Pudding cards: Pudding doesn’t pay off until the very end of the game. It creates a gentle tension across rounds because it nudges you to remember to keep, not discard, certain cards. When the final tally arrives, the pudding bounty can tilt the overall winner by a surprising margin.
- Chopsticks: This is a kind of action card that adds an extra layer to the drafting round. In some builds, you can use Chopsticks to grab two cards on a future turn, letting you scheme and plan with a little more freedom. It’s not always a game-changer, but it is a sneaky way to swing momentum when you want to surprise your neighbors.

The scoring rules are deliberately approachable. You can teach a new player in less than five minutes, which is a rare feat in the modern board-game ecosystem. And yet the scoring still offers delightful room for clever play and subtle misdirection, especially when you start to optimize the timing of your Wasabi and the way you chase that elusive set of Sashimi in a late arc of the round.

### The Wasabi conundrum: one card, infinite potential
Wasabi is a deceptively simple card that signals a more dramatic decision later in a hand. The standard read is that the next Nigiri you play will be worth more than its face value when paired with Wasabi. The exact multiplier is less important than learning the rhythm: if you pass Wasabi early, you surrender the possibility of a big score later; if you hold Wasabi, you risk delaying your essential Nigiri turn to the point of missing out on a lot of quick points. The net effect is a neat, crunchy puzzle: do you go for a reliable next Nigiri with moderate points, or do you seed Wasabi hoping to explode on your next turn with a big number? Like most good ice cream flavors, Wasabi should be enjoyed in moderation, but it can be a game-changer when used with surgical precision.

### A how-to guide for beginners
- Start with Saiyan-like patience: The first round is mostly practice for your brain to recognize the flow of cards and the rhythm of passing.
- Don’t fear the pass: Sometimes the best move is to accept a mediocre card now in order to deny a strong card to a table competitor.
- Track what others chase: If you notice a table buddy chasing Maki or Sashimi, you can use your picks to intercept and block, or you can pivot to more consistent Nigiri values.
- Balance risk and reward: Tempura and Sashimi offer big rewards when you assemble the full set, but the odds of completing those sets drop with every passing turn. Decide early what your risk tolerance looks like for each round.
- Don’t forget Pudding: It’s tempting to ignore the pudding tokens, but the later-scoring flexibility can swing overall results in subtle but dramatic ways.

## Strategy: playing the long game in a sprint format
Sushi Go rewards a mix of short-term tactics and long-term planning. The game’s speed can lull you into making snap judgments, but the real winners are those who maintain a mental map of what’s gone out of the deck and what remains in the mix. Here are a few strategy ideas you can actually apply in your first three rounds without needing a notebook:

- Early tempo, late payoff: In the first round, go for consistent Nigiri values and a few safe Makis. The goal is not to clean up the entire board but to establish a baseline you can improve on as the rounds progress.
- The Maki chase as a social contract: If you see the table collectively leaning toward Maki, don’t try to outpace them alone. Use your picks to diversify your scoring potential and then exploit a sudden shift when you can wrangle a high-value Maki card with a bite-sized risk attached.
- The Wasabi gamble is a mood call: If you hold Wasabi, commit to a plan around a high-value Nigiri in the next hand. If it doesn’t come, you haven’t lost too much; the Wasabi can still shape the next round’s choices in subtle ways.
- Pudding as mind-game: Don’t ignore pudding unless your table is stacking it aggressively. The final tallies often hinge on who stayed aware of pudding cards and who let them slip away. A careful pudding chase can sometimes secure a last-minute edge when the table is otherwise evenly matched.
