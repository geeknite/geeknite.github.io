## Practicalities: Stock, Seals, and Storage
### The Seal and What It Means for Value
A sealed two-deck set is a dual threat and a safety net: it preserves the exact moment of purchase and the pristine condition that matters for collectible value. If you plan to resell or swap later, a sealed set typically commands a premium over open decks, assuming the seal is intact. However, be mindful: sealed decks can occasionally harbor subtle moisture or atmospheric changes over long storage durations. If you ever plan to store them away for a decade or more, consider a climate-controlled environment and a negativist attitude toward humidity, because humidity is the stealth villain for card stock. If your set has foggy plastic or an odor that suggests a lab experiment, you should probably set it aside for a while and air it out before you consider it collector-grade.

### Storage Tips for Your Two-Deck Time Capsule
Keep the set away from direct sunlight to prevent color fade of the backs, especially the red deck in the set. A dark shelf or a sealed display box is ideal. If you have a fancy card display, ensure the vibration from a nearby window or door won't rattle the two decks into a state of disarray. A gentle, dust-free environment helps; dust is the silent saboteur of collector decks everywhere. And if you keep your decks near a computer or a coffee machine, you may want to place them in a secure spot that is not a caffeine-scented hazard or a magnet for stray USB drives.
