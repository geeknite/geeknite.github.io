## Chaos Draft Explained
Chaos draft is a format that takes the standard draft concept and adds a twist of randomness and unpredictability. Instead of five cards per pack and a color-specific mana curve, chaos draft might shuffle cards across packs, mix tokens, or include unique chaos cards that wreak minor havoc on the drafting order. The result is a deck-building challenge that rewards adaptability over strict theorycraft and punishes players who rely on a single archetype. The three-pack variant simply stacks your odds: more cards, more color pairings, more potential curve-balls, and more opportunities to draft something that resembles a well-tuned deck before you’ve even discovered your first rare.

## What you get with 3 Pack Chaos Draft
- 3 booster packs: Each purchase yields three boosters, typically from a mix of sets or a single set depending on the retailer.
- Chaos elements: Some organizers include chaos cards, random pack effects, or shared pools to increase the unpredictability.
- A draft environment: You and a group of 7-8 players each open a pack, pick a card, and pass, continuing until every pack is exhausted. Then you build a 40- or 60-card deck depending on local rules and your mood.

## The pros and cons

Pros:

- Opportunity for weird decks: The chaos factor makes offbeat combos viable.
- Social fun: Chaos drafts are a riot with friends and strangers alike.

Cons:
- RNG heavy: The randomness can tilt power balance toward the luckiest opens rather than the most skilled drafting.
- Card availability: If you’re chasing specific staples, chaos drafts can be maddening.
- Time sink: Three packs per player plus passes can stretch a night longer than a rerun of a favorite show.

## How to prepare and how to draft
- Know your meta: In chaos, the concept of meta still matters, but it’s more like the meta that happened in a parallel universe where everything is two mana cheaper and one color is always open.
- Sort early: As you open packs, categorize cards by color and by power level. If you’re in a two-color color pair and see a strong removal spell in the same color as your other potential picks, consider locking in that path.
- Be flexible: The chaos factor will disrupt your original plan; you’ll need to pivot to utilities, versatile cards, and curve-friendly plays.

## A sample draft scenario
Let’s say you’ve got three packs lined up: A, B, and C. Pack A reveals a midrange bomb in green, plus a solid removal spell in blue. Pack B gives you a green ramp card and a red big finisher. Pack C looks like a colorless support pack with mana fixing artifacts, a couple of multi-color spells, and a token-maker in white. The chaos gods smile, your seatmate reveals a legendary creature from a set you’ve never heard of, and suddenly your draft is less “Kubrick-level plan” and more “slapstick comedy with a nid of threat.”

## The drafting process in chaos
- Open pack, pick a card, pass to the left or right? In chaos, the rule might be to pass after picking; a chaotic variant could say “pass to the player who drew the shortest straw.”
- Draft to 40 or 60: Build with a flexible curve; you need to keep options open for the chaos that’s about to happen on the battlefield.

## Deck-building strategies for chaos draft
- Prioritize flexible cards: Cards that can fit multiple archetypes or operate in a two-color or three-color deck.
- Early removal matters: You might not get ideal top-end cards, so having removal to hold the fort is essential.
- Be open to off-beat combos: Chaos may reward you for trying something outside of the standard “best two-color curve.”
- Sideboard: In chaos, a rotating sideboard concept may appear; use it to adapt to what the group is drafting.
