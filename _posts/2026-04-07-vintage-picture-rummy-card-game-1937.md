### Condition grades and what they mean
- Poor to Fair: The cards show heavy wear, with significant creasing, staining, or color loss. The deck can still be legible but is more a conversation piece than a game-dayer. Value is modest.
- Good to Very Good: The majority of cards survive with readable art and legible pips. There may be minor corner wear, but the deck is cohesive and the box (if present) is generally intact.
- Excellent to Near Mint: The deck looks almost untouched, with minimal edge wear and bright, crisp imagery. The original box often helps push value higher, and the set feels ready for display or gentle play on a rainy Sunday.
- Mint: The unicorn of card collecting. Mint editions are rare and often come with pristine boxes, unblemished cards, and evidence of never having left the shelf. Expect the price to reflect the rarity and the exactness of the packaging.

## Authenticity and care for the long haul
If you decide to invest in a vintage 1937 set, proper care matters. Collectibility leans heavily on how well the deck has aged and whether it has been stored in a way that minimizes humidity, heat, and direct sunlight. Here are a few practical care tips:
- Keep the deck in a cool, dry place away from sunlight. UV rays are the arch-nemesis of print, especially in the mid-century palettes.
- If the box is present, store it upright to minimize pressure on the corners. A dusty shelf is a better friend than a coffee table drop.
- Handle with clean hands. Oils from skin can accelerate edge wear and affect color in the long run.
- Consider a protective sleeve or display case if you plan to showcase the cards rather than use them regularly. A little display attention goes a long way toward preserving the art and the memory.

For authenticity buffs, verify the card stock pattern, the typography on the back, and any publisher marks that indicate the edition year. If you can locate a copy with a matching period advertising insert or a small catalog page, you have a stronger case for a true 1937 origin. But remember, part of the charm is the history baked into its wear—the micro-abrasions around the corners tell a story of many hands and late-night rummy battles.
