## Overview: what is Exploding Kittens, exactly?
Exploding Kittens is a small, cheeky card game designed for 2-5 players (though house rules often let you bring more people into the hilarity). It’s a take-that, highly luck-based card game with a simple core mechanic: do not draw the Exploding Kitten card. If you draw it, you’re out—unless you have a Defuse card, in which case you might talk your way back into the game using a defusing move that seems equally ridiculous and delightful.

The original edition, published by The Oatmeal in partnership with critically caffeinated game designers, leaned into a particular blend of humor and quick rounds. The idea, in short, is to agitate your friends into laughter while simultaneously preventing them from claiming victory by sheer luck or reckless bravado. This is important: Exploding Kittens is not about deep strategy; it’s about capturing that sweet spot where you feel clever for about 20 seconds before a Baby Cat or Laser Pointer card erupts into chaos.

## The gameplay: how it feels to play a brand-new copy
Exploding Kittens is fundamentally about risk, probability, and momentary panic. The experience of a brand-new deck is a little like seeing a stand-up comedian try a new five-minute bit: you’re excited, you’re a little confused, and you’re ready to laugh at the chaos when the punchlines land—or miss spectacularly.

### Setup and basic rules
- Setup: Shuffle the deck, deal 7 cards to each player, and place the Exploding Kitten card pile face-down. The Defuse cards are kept in hand, because you’re probably going to need them. The first player starts by drawing a card, and then play passes clockwise. If you draw an Exploding Kitten, you either perish dramatically or successfully defuse and reinsert the kitten back into the pile in a spot that will cause other players to panic later.
- Core mechanics: The actions—Skip, See the Future, Shuffle, Attack, etc.—give you strategic options to avoid drawing the dreaded kitten. The See the Future card lets you peek at the top few cards, which is basically early access to fate. The Shuffle card lets you mix things up when the deck seems to be trailing toward a bad ending. The Attack card can push the termination risk onto the next player, transforming your panic into their panic in a very satisfying way.
- Card draw resolution: You draw one card per turn unless you’ve activated an effect that changes the flow. The tension comes from not knowing when the next kitten draw is coming and whether you’ll be the one to hit it. In a new-packed deck, you often get a few surprises in a row—moments where you think you’ve got a handle on things, and then the deck decides to be cruelly efficient.

### A few quick strategies for the new edition
- The Defuse economy: Defuse cards are your lifelines. In a factory-sealed copy, you’re likely to start with a handful of Defuses in the deck. Use them judiciously; saving too many can lead you to misplay later when you actually need one.
- See the Future: Early glimpses into the deck cost you nothing but a little emotional energy. It’s worth using this card if you’re unsure whether your next draw will be catastrophic or mildly inconvenient.
- Timing the Attack: Attacks can be a masterstroke or a disaster depending on who’s waiting. With a fresh deck, you’ll see a lot of “one more person” moments, where you pass the buck and watch someone else squirm.

## The humor, the tone, and why it sticks (or doesn’t)
One of Exploding Kittens’ signature attractions is its irreverent, meme-friendly humor. The cards come with captions that range from “OMG I can't even” to “This is fine,” often featuring cats in peril or existential crises. The 2015 edition captures this vibe with gusto. If you’re in the mood for a game that’s as much about shared laughs as it is about trying not to explode your friendship, this is the edition to have.

However, humor doesn’t age exactly like fine wine. Some jokes land harder than others, and a few of the cat puns might feel repetitive after a few sessions. That said, the real joy comes from watching your friends rib each other as the deck narrows and the tension rises. The “new factory sealed” factor adds to that magic because everyone knows they’re playing with pristine cards, which feels fancier than it should for a game about exploding felines and defusal cards.

## The value proposition: is this edition worth hunting down?
If you’re a collector, the 2015 original edition holds a certain nostalgic weight.
