## How to Play: Setup, Rounds, and the Beat-The-Parents Flavor
This edition sticks to a straightforward premise that works well for mixed-ages groups: teams answer questions about Disney knowledge, but the twist comes through prompts that celebrate team dynamics, playful taunts, and the occasional “mom-didn’t-hear-the-question?” moment that fuels the laughter.

### Setup
- Split into two teams: Kids (younger players) and Parents (older players). If you’re playing with a large family, you can rotate teams mid-game to keep things fair and spicy.
- Place the board in the center, shuffle the question deck, and lay out the scoring tokens within easy reach.
- Each team picks a color and takes their starting tokens. The turn order is decided by a quick mini-challenge: the fastest person to recite a favorite Disney lyric gets to choose who goes first. Yes, this is the title wave of chaos we’re embracing.
- Determine the number of rounds. The core game design assumes multiple rounds: standard rounds for point-building, wildcard rounds for chaos, and a final “Disney Finale” sprint that tests memory under pressure. You can adapt how many rounds you want depending on your family’s stamina.

### Rounds and Scoring
- Each round features a question card with a category (e.g., Villains, Princesses, Pixar, Disney Channel era nostalgia). Players answer in turn or as a team, depending on your house rules.
- Correct answers earn points. Some questions offer bonus points for extra details: e.g., naming three supporting characters or quoting a movie line. The bonus incentive encourages collaboration and a little showmanship—perfect for family storytelling.
- Wildcards can either multiply points, steal a turn, or swap a question with a “Plot Twist” prompt that forces teams to explain a scene in a minutely ridiculous way. These moments are where the game becomes a performance piece rather than a strict quiz.
- The timer adds a sense of urgency: you have 30 seconds (or 1 minute if you’re feeling generous) to deliver an answer. If you’re stuck, you can pass, but passing costs you strategic points in some tracks, which adds tension without breaking the family mood.

### The 2023 Edition Additions
- Updated and expanded question catalog: more recent films and shows added, so if you’ve committed your life to binge-watching, you’ll feel seen.
- “Character Spotlight” prompts: players must describe a character’s arc in 15 seconds or less, capturing emotional nuance while not spoiling plot twists for younger players. It’s basically a tiny improv exercise that yields big laughs.
- Thematic rounds: special rounds built around Disney parks, soundtracks, and iconic moments that evoke fond memories of family trips and clumsy rollercoaster rides. These rounds tend to be the crowd-pleasers and the source of the most dramatic misrememberings.

## Strategy Tips: How to Tilt the Odds in Your Favor (Without Being That Person)
- Pair up for synergy: Use a quick, couple of-word description of a character or scene to trigger a memory cascade in your teammate. Short, vivid cues help your team recall more efficiently than long-winded explanations.
- Use the wildcards strategically: Don’t fear the chaos—embrace it. If you’re trailing, wildcard rounds can close the gap quickly, especially if you pull a multipliers card when your team is primed to recall a difficult sequence.
- Listen for cues: In family games, people’s reactions often tell you when you’re close or off. A chorus of laughter or a sharp “no!” from the other team can be a hint indicating you’re onto something or completely off-base.
- Build a memory map: Track a few favorite films and their iconic moments. You don’t need to memorize every plot twist, but having “anchor moments” can dramatically improve your performance on a few tricky prompts.
- Adjustable difficulty: If you’re playing with younger kids, designate a “kid-friendly mode” to soften the difficulty, with shorter time windows and the option to earn partial points for partial knowledge. The goal is inclusive fun, not a ruthless high-score grind.
