# Zombicide Black Plague: Adrian Smith Special Guest Edition FR Nuovo & Imballo Originale

If you thought the zombie apocalypse was over, think again. Zombicide Black Plague rolls into the courtyard with more knights, more undead, and a splash of art by the legendary Adrian Smith. This Special Guest Edition from France brings not only a shiny new cover but also a packaging refresh that makes you feel like you are unboxing a treasure chest rather than a board game. In this Geeknite review, we dive into everything you need to know about Adrian Smith's second sit-down with the Black Plague universe, what Nuovo & Imballo Originale (new and original packaging) actually means in practice, and whether this edition is a must-have for collectors, seasoned survivors, or people who just like the smell of cardboard and victory in the morning.

## What makes this edition special

This Special Guest Edition centers on artist Adrian Smith, a name that might ring bells in the science fiction and fantasy art world. His involvement adds a layer of visual storytelling that makes the grim medieval world pop in a way that the standard edition can only dream of after a caffeine-fueled sleepless night. The core gameplay remains familiar to anyone who has played Black Plague, but you get to encounter design touches that feel like they were sketched in a cathedral and then refined by a horde of thirsty zombies with manners and swords—kinda.

### Adrian Smith: the guest star you did not know you needed
Adrian Smith is not just a guest on the cover; his artwork spills into the cards, the boards, and the hero minis. The knights are given a slightly more stylized silhouette; the monsters carry a bit more menace; and the overall color palette leans into a moody, robed vibe that makes you squint at the board to decide if you are looking at a game or a piece of dark fantasy art. If you have ever stared at a Smith painting and thought, this would make a fantastic tabletop game, this edition is basically your fantasy bingo card in three dimensions.

In our time with the set, the art changes the atmosphere more than you might expect. It’s not just about pretty pictures; the aesthetic cues influence your sense of jeopardy and urgency. When you flip a card and see a Smith-painted knight staring back, you are less likely to treat it as a mere token and more as a nemesis that demands your attention. This is a subtle but real shift in how you approach the table, especially when you are in the thick of a wave-mopping siege with two pushy bosses bearing down on your heroes.

### Nuovo & Imballo Originale: packaging that pretends to be a gift
Nuovo & Imballo Originale translates roughly to new and original packaging. What that means in practical terms is a fresh primary box, new sleeve art, and a packaging layout that prioritizes protection while also letting you pretend you are unboxing something from a boutique game store rather than a standard cardboard fortress. In some cases, the new packaging includes updated component trays, improved inner padding, and a few tokens that have swapped colors to shout, Look at me, I am premium cardboard now.

We tested the unboxing experience twice: first with a pristine, untouched copy and then with a slightly manhandled demo unit from a trade show. The first box felt like opening a chest of shiny coins; the second felt like discovering a well-organized dungeon with a map that actually makes sense. The difference in packaging is not just aesthetics; it can affect how quickly you get to the table and how easily you can transport the game to a friend’s house without losing a limb to a loose zombie token rolling under the sofa. If you value presentation and a satisfying unboxing ritual, this edition nails that particular category.

## Contents and components: what you actually get

This edition sticks to the core Black Plague formula but sprinkles in Adrian Smith’s signature flavor across its cards and miniatures. In practical terms, you can expect the usual Zombicide skeleton crew of zombies and knights, plus a handful of promotional or variant pieces that celebrate the guest edition. The exact materials can vary slightly depending on the run, but the unboxing experience is consistent: a sturdy box, well-protected miniatures, and a set of reference sheets that make you feel like a battle boss instead of a guy who forgot how to park a knight on a 4×4 square.

- Hero miniatures: The heroes retain their classic silhouettes, but you may notice subtle re-sculpting or new plastic variants that look sharper on the table. If you have a soft spot for heroic poses, you will enjoy the extra character Smith lends to the figures.
- Zombie and enemy tokens: Expect a few re-colored or repurposed tokens that match the new color palette. The zombies still swarm, but they do so with a different swagger now that the art direction is more cinematic.
- Cards and boards: The art on the cards and the board overlays benefits from the new palette. The edges look crisp, and the font choices make staying alive during a brutal round feel a little more official, as if you are playing in a knightly tournament rather than skirmishing in a tavern.
- Rulebook and quick reference: The rules themselves stay faithful to the original game, but the layout in the quick reference sheets has a readability boost. If you hate flipping through pages, this edition gives you a better chance of remembering where the traps are, which is half the battle won already.

A note on fair play: most changes in a special guest edition are surface-level art and packaging. The gameplay loop remains the same: you spawn horde-control missions, take turns performing actions, and try not to die in a shower of plastic chits. The Adrian Smith edition does not reinvent the wheel; it polishes the wheel so it shines a bit brighter when you roll it across a dungeon floor.

## Artwork and theme: why this is more than a pretty box

Artwork matters. It is the difference between feeling like you are playtesting a serious tactical exercise and feeling like you are playing a dungeon crawl where the dragons are on the cover of the rulebook. Adrian Smith brings a sense of scale and menace to the Black Plague universe that is hard to quantify, but easy to feel. The knights have heavier armor silhouettes, the monsters carry a more intimidating silhouette, and the environment feels loaded with narrative potential.

From a thematic standpoint, the integration of Smith's art into the cards and miniatures adds a layer of storytelling. You can almost hear the clank of steel, the creak of a longbow string, and the distant groan of an encroaching horde as you study the art on each card. It is not just art for art's sake; it creates a mood that enhances the tension of the mission briefings and the thrill of a well-timed tactical play.

If you collect art-driven editions, this one sits well in a shelf with other Cosmic-Dread or Eldritch-Black aesthetic games. It is not the most minimalistic black-and-white vibe you could imagine, but it has a charismatic, slightly baroque charm that fits the Black Plague setting perfectly. And for those who enjoy immersion, the art can spark little narratives at the table: Which knight made a reckless dash to save a comrade? Which zombie lurker has the artful sneer of a villain in a Sunday comic? The answers are often far more entertaining than the dice results alone.

## Gameplay experience: setup, turns, and the feel of the siege

The core gameplay of Zombicide Black Plague remains as you remember it: you assemble a squad of survivors, deploy them on a map, take actions to move and fight, and face a relentless wave of undead and knights who want nothing more than to ruin your day. The Adrian Smith edition does not mutate the mechanics; it tweaks the table experience. You will still be balancing: who leads the charge, who stays back to guard the escape route, and how you manage your limited equipment to maximize survival on a mission that is almost certainly going to end with red dice and a chorus of groans.

Setup time is reasonable. If you have played Black Plague before, you will recognize the flow: set up the map tiles, place the initial spawn, deploy your heroes, and study the encounter cards for the required number of turns. The packaging refresh does help with organization; the tray design means you can pull out a hero kit without losing a finger to a stray axe head rolling under the sofa. The first few missions tend to walk you through the mechanics while offering enough variety to feel like you are starting a real campaign rather than a coffee-stained demo.

In terms of balance and difficulty, the edition maintains the classic Diablous of Zombicide: a careful dance of luck and planning. The dice are unforgiving, but the player who plans ahead and positions the knights to funnel the zombies into chokepoints tends to survive longer. The enemy design remains on-brand: relentless mobs, occasional heavier threats, and a few mission-specific modifiers that keep things interesting without tipping into unfair territory. The Smith art changes the vibe; the numbers on the board still drive the drama—the thrill remains in how you adapt when the odds look like a bad room full of chattering teeth and floating torches.

### The French edition angle: language, layout, and accessibility
The FR edition brings its own idiosyncrasies. The rulebooks and cards are translated with care, and you get the benefit of localized nomenclature that may rhyme better with your escape route or your coffee preferences. For players who prefer original language play, the English components are still present, but the FR adjustments can be a delightful twist if you enjoy bilingual sessions or teaching the game to friends who love the French edition’s packaging and presentation.

## How this edition compares to other Zombicide Black Plague runs

If you own the standard Black Plague, you will likely appreciate the Adams-style polish and Adrian Smith’s signature artwork as a standout upgrade rather than a price-gouge. The core experience remains familiar: you will still be rolling dice to clear corridors, acquiring equipment, and surviving the siege long enough to reach the objective. The difference you notice is less about the mechanical edge and more about the table presence and the sense of adventure.

Compared to earlier expansions, the Adrian Smith Special Guest Edition tends to feel more like a collectors’ item with practical upgrades in packaging and presentation. If you enjoy the experience of presenting the game as a showpiece while actively playing it, this edition offers a flavor that can elevate the mood of a campaign night. For players who prefer minimalism or who already own multiple copies of Black Plague, this edition might feel more like a premium upgrade than a functional necessity. The decision often comes down to how much you value the art and the unboxing ritual versus the sheer quantity of zombies in your kitchen table universe.

## Value for money: is this worth picking up?

Value is subjective—this is the kind of question that depends on your collection, your friends, and your tolerance for premium packaging. If you are a completionist who loves Adrian Smith art and you enjoy a tactile unboxing experience that feels premium, this edition offers a strong case for purchase. The FR language angle adds an extra layer of accessibility for francophone players; the nuov o packaging ensures your shelf looks tidy rather than chaotic after a month of unboxing and reboxing.

On the other hand, if you already own a Black Plague core set and you are not chasing variant art or a fancy packaging, you might want to consider whether the price point aligns with your priorities. The gameplay isn’t reinvented, and while the new art is appealing, it does not change the fundamental experience of mowing down mobs with a squad. As a purchase decision, ask yourself: do you want the table presence, the art, and the packaging, or do you want a stronger impact on long-term gameplay value? The answer will guide your wallet accordingly.
