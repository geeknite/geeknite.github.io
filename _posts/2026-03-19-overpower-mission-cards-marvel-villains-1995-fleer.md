## What are Mission Cards

Mission Cards in OverPower are where the game forgets to be a pure duel and starts acting like a tabletop RPG with a cape obsession. These cards set specific objectives, conditions for victory, or quirky twists that can swing a match from a clean sword fight into a theatrical showdown with a villain monologue. The 1995 Mission Cards pack introduced a dedicated slot where you can slot in a villain as the central figure for the mission, turning a hero battle into a story arc where the villain is trying to stop the good guys from flipping a city block or stealing a priceless artifact guarded by a few heroic egos.

In the Singles concept we are talking about choosing one or two villain figures to lead a mission or control a trap in collaborative play. The 1995 Fleer set had a surprisingly elegant approach: you pick a villain or two, the mission cards give you veiled tasks and conditions, and the rest of the deck plays as normal. The result is a game mode that is less about raw power and more about narrative mischief and deck tuning. You learn quickly that the villain you choose changes the pacing—Doctor Doom leans heavy on schemes and control, while Magneto shouts magnetic mayhem and pushes the board around with a casual shrug.

### The Singles concept in practice

The concept of singles is simple and delightful: you pick one or two villain figures to anchor the mission, and you tailor your hero deck around beating the villain under the mission constraints. This is not a pure coin flip duel; this is strategic storytelling with a card game spine. With the Marvel universe as a playground, you get to combine iconic villains with familiar heroes in ways that feel satisfying and a little over the top. The best part is that you can mix and match across your hero board to chase synergies that make sense in a 90s style of grand plans that are delightful to attempt and funny to watch fail.

## How to Play With Singles in a Modern Tabletop Session

Even though this is a retro product, it can slot into contemporary gaming nights with a touch of nostalgia. Here is a practical way to approach Singles and Mission Cards on a current date night.

### Step 1: Build your villain anchor
Choose one villain that will anchor your mission. Doom provides control and traps, Magneto offers battlefield manipulation, Loki brings subterfuge. Pick the tone you want for the night and decide whether you want a victory through brute force, fate manipulation, or sickly sweet misdirection.

### Step 2: Prepare the hero deck
Assemble a hero deck that complements the villain you chose. If you pick Doom, a deck with disruption and tempo control works well. If you pick Magneto, you want mobility and fast removal to keep the battlefield from tilting too far in the villain’s favor. The hero deck is your play space to respond to the mission conditions and adapt as the villain pushes the narrative forward.

### Step 3: Set the mission conditions
Read the Mission Card text aloud and let the players set up the board according to the described mission. The fun comes from interpreting the objective and figuring out a plan that feels heroic but also satisfies the mission twist. If the mission mentions a city block being endangered, imagine the heroes rushing to seal the breach while Doom cackles from a pedestal in the corner.

### Step 4: Play with the energy of the era
Embrace the late 90s vibe. Talk like a cartoon villain from a Saturday morning show. Echo the dramatic pauses. The silliness is not a villain; it is part of the charm. The more you wink at the camera and lean into the melodrama, the more the room goes from showroom to stage, and that is where the OverPower experience shines.

### Step 5: Debrief with a grin
After the match, talk about the single best moment of the mission. Did a hero miraculously dodge a doom-laden trap? Did the villain misplay a trap and cause a spectacular chain reaction that folded the entire plan like a cheap lawn chair? Either way, celebrate the improbable and the dazzling. The retro card game gods love a good story as much as they love a good punchline.

## Strategy Tips for Singles and Mission Cards

- Favor flexibility over raw power. Mission Cards give you a dramatic edge when you can adapt to changing conditions rather than rely on an overpowered set of cards.
- Pair hero synergies with villain traits for emotional resonance. If you pick Loki, aim for traps that reward misdirection and misreadings of the board state.
- Don’t underestimate positioning. On a small play area, a single misstep by a hero can cascade into a villain triumph. Use the mission twist to force that cascade at the right moment.
- Think narratively. The fun of these objects is the story you tell around the cards. Treat the table as a stage and your friends as co-authors.
- Preserve the theme in your play style. If you research Doom and magnetism, that sense of who these characters are will tier your play experience up from good to memorable.
