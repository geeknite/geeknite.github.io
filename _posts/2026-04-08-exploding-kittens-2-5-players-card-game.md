## Overview
Exploding Kittens is the kind of game that starts with a cute catastrophe and ends with you negotiating with Fate over whether a GIF of a cat should ruin your entire evening. It remains fast, silly, and surprisingly strategic, a deck that somehow fits on a coffee table while also threatening to ruin a dinner party if you misplay a card. The base game is designed for 2 to 5 players, which makes it ideal for intimate game nights, chaotic family gatherings, or a casual podcast titled “We Swear This Is Strategic.” The Oatmeal’s art style is instantly recognizable, a perfect blend of sarcasm and cat-adjacent chaos that makes you feel clever for laughing at a card depicting a smug feline with a laser pointer labeled Do Not Touch. The goal is brutally simple: avoid drawing an exploding kitten while deploying a toolbox of mischief, misfortune, and ridiculous combos that feel like a printer working overtime under the pressure of internet humor.

## How to play: a quick guide
### Setup steps
1. Shuffle the deck and deal 7 cards to each player. 2. Add a Defuse card to each player’s hand. 3. Remove a number of Exploding Kitten cards from the deck based on the player count (the exact number varies by edition, but the principle is one fewer kitten than players). 4. Shuffle the rest of the deck and place it face down as the draw pile. 5. Decide who goes first and start the chaotic ride.

### On your turn
- You may play any number of cards from your hand, following the card instructions. The key cards are:
  - See the Future: peek at the top few cards of the draw pile to plan your fate.
  - Skip: end your turn immediately without drawing a card.
  - Attack: force the next player to take two turns unless they counter with a card.
  - Shuffle: shuffle the draw pile to disrupt the next player’s predictions.
  - See the Future and Nope: combining these creates delightful counter-moves as the table debates whether a move is allowed.
- After playing cards, you must draw one card from the draw pile. If you draw an Exploding Kitten, you must play a Defuse card from your hand to survive; if you cannot or choose not to, you are out of the game. If you survive, you place the Exploding Kitten back into the draw pile (where chaos reigns).
- Cat cards come in groups. Typically you need to play two Cat cards to steal a random card from another player, which can derail someone’s carefully laid plan or simply leave you with a better hand as the table’s mood shifts. Different Cat cards have different flavors of mischief, but the rule is simple: two Cat cards equal power, one Cat card is a tease.

### Ending the round
The last player standing after all explosions have cleared and all defuses have been exhausted is the winner. In practice, that means the table will likely be louder, a little more chaotic, and exhausted by the end of a single round, which is precisely the point.

## Card types and interactions: a closer look
### Exploding Kitten
The bomb you do not want to pull. It’s a mood in card form and a test of your risk tolerance. You hope your Defuse cards are ready and your strategy is strong enough to dodge the blast.

### Defuse
Defuse cards are the lifeline to staying in the game. When you draw an Exploding Kitten, you can play a Defuse to save yourself and reinsert the kitten somewhere that will hurt the most. The tension here is delicious: do you use your Defuse now or save it for a later moment when the stakes feel even higher? The tradeoff is real and hilarious.

### Cat cards
Cat cards form the chaotic backbone of the game’s social engine. You pair two Cat cards to steal a card at random from another player, which means you can sabotage a rival’s plans or rescue a card you desperately need. The goofy flavors — Tacocat, Hairy Potato Cat, and others — inject humor into the chaos, ensuring that even a failed strategy can feel entertaining rather than tragic.

### Other action cards
- See the Future lets you peek at upcoming draws, giving you a sense of control in a game that thrives on the illusion of control.
- Skip and Attack adjust the tempo of the round, turning a calm moment into a heated standoff.
- Nope, the crowd favorite, lets you cancel someone else’s action, leading to glorious misdirection and muttered curses about luck and timing.

## Strategy, luck, and how to enjoy both
### The luck factor
Exploding Kittens is a loud reminder that luck matters. The draw pile is a roulette wheel and the table is the audience. Some rounds feel like a masterclass in careful risk-taking; others feel like a chaotic sprint toward an explosion with a gleeful soundtrack in your head. The luck dynamic prevents the game from becoming a pure puzzle. The more Cat cards you collect, the more meaningful the luck becomes as you leverage pairs into steals and disruptions.

### The skill factor
Despite its leaning on luck, there are clear strategic moves that separate the good players from the casual bluffers. Here are a few tips gleaned from countless table debates:
- Hold onto Defuse cards for life-or-death moments. Don’t use them during a smooth round just to prove you can survive a big draw—unless the table demands a dramatic turn.
- Use See the Future when the top of the draw pile looks dangerous. A quick peek can save your neck and set you up for a safer long-term plan.
- Cat card management matters. Building a toolkit of Cat cards to control the table’s tempo matters more than hoarding a single lucky pair.
- Be mindful of table dynamics. The more you antagonize others with clever plays, the more likely someone will target you next turn. This is not just a game of cards; it is a social experiment in group psychology under pressure.

### When to lean into chaos
There are moments when a bold move pays off and others when the room laughs in your face. The beauty of Exploding Kittens lies in embracing ridiculous outcomes. If you have a strong hand, you might push for a sequence that forces others to react, trading a predictable end for a legendary moment of chaos. If you’re behind, doubling down on defuse confidence and sheer luck can tilt the odds in your favor.
