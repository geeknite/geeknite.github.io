Open Box time. The Gloom 1st Edition Base Set—Atlas Games, designed by Keith Baker—arrives with the grace of a funeral procession and the whimsy of a circus. If you like your tabletop experiences with a pinch of melodrama, a dash of dark humor, and a storytelling engine that literally lets you stack misfortune, this is the box for you. Today we crack open the plastic sleeve, peel back the glossy belly of the base set, and see what happens when sorrow meets a family recipe book and a stack of translucent plastic cards.

Introduction: the vibe and why this box still feels fresh
Gloom is a game that wears its theme on the sleeve and then buries the sleeve under a pile of punch cards. The first edition base set is where it all began: a system built around telling miserable little stories about gloomy families and their unfortunate fates. The art direction stays cheeky rather than grim, which is ideal for those of us who like our tragedy with a wink rather than a grimace. The box art leans into the spectral mischief, the typography is playful, and the whole presentation telegraphs: you are about to tell macabre, comic mini-dramas with your friends.

What is in the box? a tour through components first
The unboxing ritual in Gloom is part of the spectacle. The base set comes with a handful of card types that you will see repeated across plays, plus a few novelty touches that make it obvious this is not your average card game. Here’s the core assortment you’ll encounter upon first opening:

- A deck of character cards, each one a member of a family with a backstory and a future you will be narrating toward. They’re the protagonists of your gloomy little tragedies.
- An array of “gloom” cards that introduce misfortune, typically played to worsen a character’s life. The artwork leans skewed comic, which makes the wrongness feel safe and funny rather than alarming.
- A pile of “scene” or event cards that you layer onto the characters to define the circumstances that will lead to their inevitable demises. The layering is where the magic happens in Gloom—you are literally building a story by stacking translucent layers.
- Transparent overlay cards that let you visually stack misfortune. These are the signature gimmick: you can see through the cards to the misery beneath, which is somehow both morbid and delightful.
- A concise rules pamphlet that tells you how to build your family tree, how to play cards, and how the scoring works when characters die in the most dramatic fashion.
- A small rule reference and a couple of token pieces that help you keep track of happiness, sadness, and the pallor of doom that has settled on your family’s long-form tragedy.

The box itself is sturdy enough to survive a few accidental shelf dives, which is important in the wild world of game nights where dice hit tables with the enthusiasm of a marching band. The insert is tidy, which matters when you want to actually play the game instead of fishing a lost card out of a cranny while your friends wait with crossed arms and raised eyebrows.

Design, art, and the tactile personality of the base set
The design language of the Gloom base set is consistent with Atlas Games’ indie vibe: clever, a bit quirky, and unabashedly character-driven. The card back art is dark but readable, and the front-facing illustrations lean into melodramatic whimsy rather than horror. It’s an aesthetic that invites storytelling: you want to read the faces of the characters, you want to smile at the misfortune that’s about to befall them, and you want to narrate a life arc that ends in a blaze of dramatic irony.

Tactility matters here. The translucent overlay cards—while a little more fragile than your average poker chip of cardstock—reward careful handling. If you’ve ever tried to stack waterlogged transparencies in a science experiment, you’ll appreciate the durability of the Gloom cards and the clean interface between layers. The production quality says: we’re serious about the mood, but not so serious that you can’t laugh at the chaos you’re about to unleash.

How to play at a high level (the quick tour)
Gloom’s core mechanic is a storytelling engine connected to a simple scoring system. The goal is to have the happiest score card show at your own family’s funeral and the saddest score card at your opponents’ family funerals. In practice, you want to minimize your own family’s happiness while maximizing the misery of your rivals’ families. The catch: your opponent does the same thing to you. It’s a reverse-greed, cooperative-sardonic vibe—funny until deadline day when you realize you’re rooting for a fictional character’s tragic misfortune.

Turn order is traditional: draw, play, discard, and reveal when you’re ready to end a family member’s life (in the game sense, anyway). The twist is that most cards you play on a character are modifiers—the gloom cards—that bend the story toward gloomier outcomes. You can also play positive cards to buff up a character’s happiness, but the fastest path to victory (or, more often, to losing the game in the most melodramatic way) is to let misfortune accumulate.

The role of the audience: why the box invites storytelling rather than pure luck
Gloom isn’t about who draws the best card; it’s about who can weave a story around the cards while maintaining a sense of dramatic pacing. Your group is the chorus to a little theater of misfortune. The emotional payoff comes from watching the narrative unfold as you reveal cards, layer scenes, and discover which character dies last (or first, if you’re feeling particularly cruel or emotionally honest about your friends’ tastes).

From open box to table: a typical setup experience
Setting up Gloom for a table of friends who love a good meme in the middle of a serious game night is straightforward. Shuffle the gloom deck, the character deck, and the scene deck separately. Deal a character card to each player, and you’re ready to begin layering misfortune. The first few rounds are a gentle warm-up—an opportunity to establish your family’s tone and decide which players are going to be the masters of tragedy and which should be the masters of restraint (which, ironically, makes the other players mis each other’s misfortune with comedic timing).

A language of doom: storytelling mechanics you’ll actually use
The beauty of Gloom is in its narrative vocabulary. You’re not just playing cards; you’re composing a tableau. Each card is a sentence in the family’s life story. The gloom cards are adjectives that color the subject’s life with woe; the happy cards are attempts to soften the blow of the narrative. The resulting story becomes a kind of satirical obituary for each character, but the fun is in how dramatically you can phrase the tragedy while still keeping the group laughing.

Character design: consistent, bizarre, and very geeky
Keith Baker’s design sensibility shows through in the character selections. Each family member has a vibe—some are Victorian tweed detectives, others are quirky forest-dwellers, and some are tragically earnest pen-pushers who somehow make the worst life decisions with the best intentions. The variance in archetypes keeps the storytelling fresh card after card, night after night. If you’re worried about repetitive stories, fear not: the base set has enough personalities and scenes to sustain dozens of different narratives before you’ve seen it all.

Thematic bite with a smile: humor as a design constraint
Gloom’s humor isn’t for everyone. It’s that delicious edge where tragedy and comedy intersect. The worst things you can imagine for your characters are the kind of punchlines that would be uncomfortable if said aloud in real life, but in a tabletop context, they land as witty, satirical, and surprisingly wholesome—because the aim remains to entertain as much as to grimace. The base set leans into that balance, never tipping too far into sourness, always letting you laugh at the inevitability of doom even as you orchestrate it.

How long does a Gloom session last, and is it replayable?
A standard session with a few players tends to last somewhere in the 30 to 60 minute range, depending on how quickly you want to appropriate the preachy narrator voice and how many times you want to retell the same couple of lines about Aunt Gertrude’s unfortunate cutlery collection. Replayability hinges on the mix of characters, scenarios, and the group’s appetite for grim whimsy. Because the deck isn’t infinitely explored in one sitting, you’ll come back to the base set with fresh eyes after a few nights of chasing different outcomes. And if you want even more variety, the broader Gloom ecosystem includes expansions that tweak the mythos while keeping the same core engine intact.

Visuals and table presence: how it looks when it’s playing
The table presence of Gloom is strong. The translucent, layered cards create a window into a micro-drama as players reveal new elements of the story. The aesthetic of light and shadow in the art adds a sense of cinematic drama, which is perfect for streaming or for a long tabletop session where the visual payoff matters as much as the gameplay. If you’re into photo-friendly components, Gloom’s setup offers some genuinely memorable table shots—perfect for a post-night-out tuck-in with friends who owe you a decent story for once.

Design notes: what makes the 1st Edition Base Set feel special today
There’s a certain magic to a 1st edition, especially when the production and packaging were still flexible enough to feel handmade in a good way. The base set has a scratch of nostalgia in its box that newer reprints lack—partly because of the printer’s quirks, partly because of the early adopters who made every card flip a little ritual. If you’re the sort of gamer who collects editions the way some people collect vinyl, the 1st Edition Base Set is a nice centerpiece for a bookshelf of mood-driven games. It signals a specific moment in the hobby: a time when storytelling mechanics began to dominate the card game space, and a designer thought, what if we built a crooked little stage and invited players to act like melodramatic bards within a doom-soaked family saga?

Component quality and durability in real-world use
In actual play, the cards hold up well to repeated shuffles and storytelling riffs. The translucent layers persist through a few games without showing coaxing wear, and the box tolerates enthusiastic table flips without the whole thing turning into confetti. If you spill a drink near the table, you’ll want to protect the cards with sleeves anyway—this is the kind of game that invites a little dramatic flourish, not a furious tear when one card slips from the story. Overall, the production feels deliberate, with enough personality to justify a long shelf life next to other mid-weight storytelling games.

Who should buy this base set, and when does it shine the brightest?
Gloom shines best when the group embraces a storytelling frame and can laugh together at the terrible things they’ve put on the table. It’s a good pick for circles that enjoy collaborative theatre-style games, lampooning family dynamics, or simply a night you want to commit to a theme and lean into it with gusto. Play it with friends who enjoy a mix of wit, horror-light humor, and a dash of theatrical performance. It’s also a fine entry point for newcomers to narrative-driven card games who want something lighter than heavy RPGs but richer than a straightforward push-your-luck title.

Closing notes: why this base set still matters
Even in an era of more elaborate storytelling engines and digital assistants that track your saga, Gloom’s particular charm remains its tactile storytelling engine. The 1st Edition Base Set captures a moment when a small company, a bold designer, and a cadre of players formed a culture around the notion that tragedy can be delightful when framed as a playful vignette. The unboxing ritual, the durable but characterful components, and the simple-but-satisfying scoring system all come together to offer a game night that feels like a quirky family gathering rather than a tense strategic gauntlet. If you’re seeking a social experience that rewards witty narration and collaborative fabrication of a family lore, you owe it to yourself to give Gloom a spin in its native edition.
