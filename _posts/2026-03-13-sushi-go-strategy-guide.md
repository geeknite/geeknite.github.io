## Sushi Go! Strategy Guide: Geeknite's Quick, Crunchy Card Drafting Adventure

Sushi Go! is not just a game; it's a miniature sushi buffet you can play on a coffee table, in a board game cafe, or on a bus if you have the balance of a master chef in a windstorm. In Geeknite style, we treat this light, snack-sized card-drafting gem with the reverence of a chef sharpening a knife for the first time. ## Overview

Sushi Go! is a fast-paced drafting game designed by Phil Walker-Harding. Each round, players pick one card from a hand and pass the rest to the next player. When all cards are chosen, you reveal your picks and score for that round. After three rounds, tally the pudding cards for the grand finale. The result? A little victory dance, a lot of food puns, and enough strategic depth to keep your brain nibbling for hours.

## How the game works

In the base game, you’ll encounter a merry medley of cards: Nigiri, Sashimi, Maki Rolls, Tempura, Dumplings, Pudding, Wasabi, and Chopsticks. The art is adorable enough to make you forget you’re calculating turn order like a crypto trader during a market crash. The rounds flow quickly: deal a hand, pick one card, pass the rest, repeat until all cards are exhausted. Then reveal and score. Three rounds means three opportunities to outsmart, outpace, and out-sushi your friends.

Two quick notes before we dive deep:
- This is a light drafting game. Don’t expect a brain-crunching engine-builder; expect a delightful puzzle you can solve with friends who just want to eat something cute and win something small.
- The table will erupt with puns. You’ll thank us later for embracing the chaos, because the puns are half the fun.

### Card-by-card overview
Now we venture into the core components and the strategy they invite. This is where you’ll learn to balance risk, timing, and a dash of social manipulation without becoming a card-counting villain.

#### Nigiri
Nigiri cards are your bread and butter, the classic payoff. They have fixed point values, typically ranging from 1 to 5 in most sets. The real kicker: Wasabi. If you have Wasabi and then snag a high-value Nigiri, its value can triple in a flash, dramatically changing the end-of-round math. The practical approach is simple: secure high-value Nigiri when you can, but don’t chase them so hard you neglect the other tracks that score points more consistently. Wasabi timing is everything. If you grab Wasabi too early and then miss the next big nigiri, you’ve set yourself up for a letdown. If you time it perfectly, you can watch your score soar as you snap up a 5-point Nigiri for an 8-point swing when Wasabi doubles it.

#### Maki Rolls
Maki scoring is tidy and a little sneaky. At the end of a round, the player with the most Maki earns two points per Maki, while the second-most earns one point per Maki. It’s possible to swing a round with a clutch Maki haul, but the problem is you often have to balance Maki with other scoring paths. The core tactic here is to pick Maki when you’re not blocking a bigger, more exciting run for yourself; you want to be the one who capitalizes on a Maki pile that the table isn’t actively contesting. If you see a track where several players are likely to fight for Maki, resist the urge to hoard and instead invest in a secondary plan to maximize your total score.

#### Sashimi
This is the classic “go big or go home” card line. Sashimi pays off if you collect three cards, awarding 10 points in most rule sets. The risk is real: if you only collect one or two Sashimi cards, you get nothing for that round. The sweet spot: aim for three if the opportunity presents itself early, but don’t chase Sashimi at the expense of already-strong combos like Dumplings or Tempura. A common tactic is to secure Sashimi cards as a multi-round threat—grab one early and hold off on pulling the trigger until you see at least two more in the same or following hands.

#### Tempura
Tempura scores in pairs: two Tempura cards yield 5 points. The trick is to avoid chasing Tempura for its own sake. If you can pick up two Tempura without sacrificing a larger scoring path, you’re golden. Practically, you’ll want to time your two Tempura cards with rounds that allow you to snap both, while not giving away the chance for others to complete their own combos. Tempura is the “I’m building a safety net” card—you want to be confident you can secure the second piece before you lock in this route.

#### Dumplings
Dumplings scale nonlinearly: 1 Dumpling yields 1 point, 2 yields 3, 3 yields 6, 4 yields 10, and so on. The sweet math here is that if you can chain a stack of Dumplings, your score climbs fast. The downside is that Dumplings are easily forgone for flashier cards like Sashimi or Nigiri. The best approach is to start with a couple of Dumplings so the stack grows in your favor, then switch to other scoring tracks when the mood of the table shifts away from Dumplings. The key is to avoid leaving too many Dumplings on the table for others to pick up later on.

#### Pudding
Pudding is the silent assassin of Sushi Go!. It’s not scored until the end of the entire game, but it can swing the final tally dramatically. The trick is to balance Pudding collection with round-by-round success. If you’re behind, you may opt to grab a Pudding to close the gap. If you’re ahead, you may still want a few Pudding cards to secure a cushion against late-stage surprises from opponents. Remember: Pudding is the long-view investment. It rewards patients who plan across three rounds rather than three turns.

#### Wasabi
Wasabi acts as a multiplier for your next Nigiri. The best use of Wasabi is to wait for a high-value Nigiri to appear in your next picks and ride a high-scoring wave. If you pick Wasabi and then fail to secure a high-value Nigiri on the next turn, you’ve wasted a strong engine. The safe play is to use Wasabi when you’re reasonably confident you’ll attract a high-value Nigiri within the same round—often when you’ve picked Wasabi and see a 3-5 value Nigiri about to come into view.

#### Chopsticks
Chopsticks are the sneaky utility card. When you play them, you can choose to draw two cards on your next turn instead of one. This is the ultimate “Oh, you didn’t see that coming” tool. Use Chopsticks to set up a late-round double-dip on two valuable cards, or to block an opponent by forcing them to discard a strong option you want for yourself. The caution: if you waste Chopsticks by not leveraging both cards effectively, you’ll feel the sand slip through your fingers. The best strategy is to save a Chopsticks for a round where you can confidently pull off two strong cards in the following pass.

### Basic drafting tips

- Read the table and count what’s left. If several players chase Sashimi in early rounds, you can safely pivot to Dumplings or Pudding to maximize your endgame without competing for a single set. If it’s a free-for-all on Sashimi, you’ll need to be disciplined and pick other value cards that complement your endgame plan.
- Plan Wasabi and Nigiri in harmony. If you can secure Wasabi early and then a high-value Nigiri in the same round, your score can jump dramatically. But if you mis-timed Wasabi and lose the next high-value Nigiri, you’ll regret the card choice for the rest of the round.
- Don’t chase a single combo to the detriment of a well-rounded score. A balanced approach—some Nigiri with Wasabi, some Dumplings, some Pudding—often yields the most consistent success across three rounds.
- Block smartly. If a known table shark is chasing a specific combo, consider blocking with a card that denies them a critical piece, while you pivot to your own scoring track. It’s a game of information and psychology as much as a game of cards.

## Strategy by player count

Sushi Go! behaves a little differently depending on how many players are at the table. Here are practical notes for common counts:

- Two players: The draft becomes a tight, interesting duel. You’ll notice patterns quickly and need to anticipate your opponent’s picks a fraction of a turn ahead. It’s more about forcing the table into bad choices and capitalizing on the tiny windows of opportunity than brute force.
- Three players: A sweet spot for reliability. You can often secure a strong set while still denying others. The round pacing remains brisk and you’ll feel the tug-of-war on each pass.
- Four to five players: Chaotic and charming. The more players, the more unpredictable the flow of cards. It’s a test of your ability to adapt on the fly and to chase multiple scoring tracks in parallel without getting lost in the sauce.
- Six players (if you’re playing a party edition): This is the social stage—there’s more talk, more blocking, and more opportunities for everyone to shine if you’re comfortable with the extra noise and extra card rotations.

## Play-by-play mini-scenario

Consider a typical round where you are balancing Dumplings and Sashimi with occasional Nigiri and Maki. The hand arrives:
- One Dumpling
- Two Sashimi cards
- A 5-point Nigiri
- A Wasabi card
- A Maki Roll
- A Tempura

Decide to grab the 5-point Nigiri with Wasabi, ensuring you’ll have a high-value Nigiri if you can land a second high-value card in the same round. Pass the rest. Your opponent grabs Sashimi, hoping for the three-card payoff, while another player takes Dumplings to start stacking. On the next pass, you consider taking Tempura or Maki to diversify your scoring, depending on how many Sashimi cards you think will hit the table. The round closes, you reveal your picks, and you tally the points. The Wasabi-Nigiri connection—if successful—might give you a meaningful swing that keeps you in the lead even after the Maki roll’s rounding and the Sashimi’s drama.

## Two quick game-alike tips

- If you’re new, go easy on the high-risk combos. The quick rounds are perfect for learning the rhythm of draft and pass without getting overwhelmed by a huge scoring potential.
- If you’re a veteran, look for the “glue” cards that tie multiple scoring tracks together (for example, a Dumpling that fits with an emerging Pudding plan, or Wasabi that helps a high-value Nigiri). These synergy cards often swing the final outcome more than chasing a single big payoff.

## Extensions: variants and house rules

If you want to spice things up, you can adopt a few light house rules. Some geek-friendly variants include:
- Quick-fire rounds: speed up the game to make it more frantic and energetic. This is perfect for games with a lot of players who like a brisk tempo.
- Team play: In a 4-player game, pair up with a teammate and share pool points (but you still can’t coordinate actions in real-time to cheat).
- Pudding emphasis: Add extra Pudding cards or alter their end-game scoring so Pudding becomes a more central strategic thread across rounds.

## Post-game reflections and meta-strategy

After a few plays, you’ll notice that the best players are not just the ones who chase the most valuable card each turn but those who optimize across rounds. The meta shifts with table composition and player experience. Some players become excellent at reading the “flow” of the game. You’ll know a table is ready for sophisticated manipulation when you see them diversifying their picks and confidently executing multi-round plans that optimize Dumplings and Pudding together. The goosebump moment happens when Wasabi, Nigiri, and Dumplings collide into a perfect score chain—like a sushi chef revolutionizing a buffet with just a napkin and a gleam in their eye.
