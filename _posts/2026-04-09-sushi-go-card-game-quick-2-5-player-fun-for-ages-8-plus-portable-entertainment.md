## What is Sushi Go! Card Game?
Sushi Go! Card Game is a fast, light drafting game where players pick one card from a hand and then pass the rest to the next player. This continues until all cards are taken, at which point you reveal your choices and tally up points. The gimmick is elegant in its simplicity: build the best Sushi Go party by collecting sets of cards that score in specific ways, with timing and risk management playing out across multiple rounds. The art is cute, the themes are food-adjacent, and the pacing is so breezy that you’ll swear you were just told a joke about tuna nigiri and somehow consented to a board game version of a sushi buffet.

### The core idea
At its heart, Sushi Go! is a card-drafting engine that rewards foresight and pattern recognition rather than heavy math or long-winded rules explanations. Each type of sushi has its own scoring rule, which means there are several mini-strategies you can chase in any given round. You might try to grab as many Tempuras to score a big 5-point chunk, or you might chase Sashimi sets and hope your opponents slip up and leave you with extra cards after the last pass. The balance between short-term gains and long-term sets makes it feel like a light strategic workout with zero dull moments.

## How to Play in 5 Minutes (or Less)
Here’s the ultra-condensed run-down. If you’ve ever played a drafting game, you’ll be sipping through this like a hot cup of tea in a cafe with a friendly robot barista.

1) Setup: Determine the number of players and deal a hand of cards accordingly. For 2 players, deal 10 cards per person. For 3-5 players, deal 9 cards per person. Place the rest of the deck face-down as the draw pile. Pudding cards go aside for endgame scoring.

2) Drafting round: Each player looks at their hand, selects one card, and places it face-down in front of them. Then everyone reveals their chosen card, places it into their personal Sushi Go! collection, and passes the remaining hand to the left. Repeat until all cards are taken. This is the heart of the game: you must decide whether to chase a short-term point chip or risk waiting for a larger set later.

3) Scoring the round: After the hand is exhausted, you score the cards you collected for that round according to the card types. Nigiri cards score points directly based on their type; Maki rolls score by number of rolled mats, with the player who has the most getting extra points; Sashimi pays out in triples; Tempura requires pairs; Wasabi multiplies the value of your next Nigiri; Puddings are set aside until the end and can swing the final score in big ways.

4) End of round: Shuffle the deck and begin the next round. The game ends after the final round when all Pudding cards have been counted. The winner is the person with the highest total score.

5) Quick tips: Don’t sleep on Wasabi; you can activate Wasabi on a Nigiri and dramatically increase its value. Also, keep an eye on the Pudding cards as the game nears its end; they’re the subtle sneaky things that make you realize you should have chased them earlier.

A small but crucial note: the game scales wonderfully with the player count. For 2 players, the pacing is punchy and snappy; for 5 players, you get a lively set of mini-snap-shots on the table as everyone tries to out-draft each other while still keeping an eye on their own plate. It’s the difference between a stampede and a party bus, depending on how many folks you’ve invited to your sushi-soaked shenanigans.

### Quick scoring example
Let’s say you end a round with a small but satisfying collection: 2 Tempura, 3 Nigiri (two Tamago and one Ebi), and 1 Sashimi. The Tempura scores in pairs (two cards = 5 points in a typical rule set), Nigiri values depend on the type (for example, Tamago is worth 1 point, Salmon 2, Squid is 3, and Uni tops out at 5), and Sashimi rewards a 10-point bonus for triples (we’ll assume you got three Sashimi across the rounds). The Wasabi card, if used on the next Nigiri, can more than double the score of that single Nigiri. This means your total for the round can swing dramatically with smart card timing, which is the whole game’s vibe in a nutshell: timing, pattern recognition, and a little bit of bluffing about which hidden card you’re about to reveal.

## Strategy Basics for Beginners (and those who forgot their math in middle school)
Sushi Go! rewards pattern building more than raw number crunching. Here are some core ideas to help you get started without turning your brain into a human sushi roll.

- Chase the big combos, but don’t let the small gains slip away. Tempura and Sashimi are classic power plays, but you’ll feel the sting if you miss out on 1 or 2 Nigiri trips that would have added up by the end of the game.
- Be mindful of what you pass. The card you pass away might be exactly the card your neighbor needs to complete a desirable set. This is the social part of the game in a nutshell: you’re drafting not just for yourself but to influence the table balance.
- Wasabi is a strategic accelerator. You want to pair Wasabi with a strong Nigiri, but don’t forget that saving Wasabi for a late Nigiri can sometimes turn a modest gain into a crushing victory.
- Pudding is the wild card that shows up at the end. If you neglect Pudding early, you could be surprised by a late surge or face a quiet fall. It’s like the surprise twist ending to a food-based heist film.
- Adapt to player count. With more players, the pool of cards becomes more varied, which makes it harder to plan a single, clean path. Embrace flexibility; the joy of Sushi Go! is in how often you catch a lucky break or a partner’s blunder.

### Common mistakes and how to avoid them
- Overreaching on Nigiri without backing support: It’s tempting to chase that glow of Uni, but you’ll waste rounds if you ignore the balance of other sets. Mix your Nigiri with Maki and Sashimi as the board demands.
- Ignoring Wasabi: people underestimate Wasabi until the end of the game when a single well-timed Nigiri multiplies the score. Don’t let Wasabi be the wallflower of your strategy.
- Forgetting Pudding: If you ignore Pudding, you might wake up with an empty bag of points at the end. Reserve a plan for the endgame where Pudding can flip the scoreboard in dramatic fashion.
