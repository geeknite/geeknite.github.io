## Introduction

### What is this edition all about?

Herd Mentality Moovies and Moosic places you in a room with players who must guess the most likely answer to prompts like “Name a movie you would watch on a hopelessly rainy Saturday” or “Pick a song that has the word ‘love’ in it.” The twist is that you’re not aiming for your own good idea; you’re aiming for what the group thinks is the most popular choice. In this edition, the questions tilt toward cinema favorites and music milestones, which means you’ll get goofy genre mashups, guilty pleasures, and a chorus of “Is that even a real song?” from your relatives—especially the aunt who insists that every 80s track deserves a place in the hall of fame.

Big Potato leans into their signature quick rounds and accessible components, so even if your household has zero interest in pop culture trivia, Moovies and Moosic still sings. The packaging, the card art, and the inside jokes are all designed to be visually approachable and instantly playable. It’s a party game that wants you to engage, not to study an entire filmography just to pretend you know your directors. That said, the game does reward players who genuinely love pop culture, and that’s where the real fun kicks in.

## How to Play: The Core Loop

### Setup time and player order

Each round, one player is the host. The host draws a Moovies or Moosic prompt and reads it aloud without revealing the intended answer. The rest of the players privately write down an answer they think would be the most popular. Then, everyone presents their choice or, depending on the house rules, the host reads the winding list of options and players choose where their answer might fit. The heart of the mechanic remains the same: you’re trying to predict the crowd’s top pick, not your own personal favorite.

### The voting process

After the prompts are revealed, players vote by placing their chips face-down on the space that corresponds to their chosen answer. The host then reveals the majority (or, in some variants, the “Herd” decide in a more chaotic fashion). The twist here is the social dynamic: sometimes the wildest, most obviously wrong option becomes the crowd favorite simply because it’s the loudest or the silliest. And that’s the beauty of Moovies and Moosic: the more outrageous the response, the more players get swept into the hilarity of the moment.

### Scoring and rounds

Scores are awarded based on how many players chose the same answer as the majority. A perfectly aligned herd earns maximum points, while lone, quirky choices can still fetch points if they align with a vec- e.g., “the most surprising crowd-pleaser.” It’s not the game to chase a perfect trivia score; it’s a social experiment in guessing what everyone will yell out when asked about a specific movie or song. The result is a session that can swing wildly from round to round, which keeps energy levels high and debates delightfully ridiculous.

## Variants, Modes, and House Rules

### The standard Herd mode revisited

In the base Herd mode, players align with the crowd’s likely pick. The laughter often comes from watching a quiet member of the group become a stabilizing force who predicts the crowd with uncanny accuracy or a notoriously stubborn player who refuses to join the consensus and ends up as the fun exception that anchors a whole round.

### A light party-mode for larger crews

If you’ve got a room full of people (think family reunion, birthday party, or a game night at a local cafe), you can turn Moovies and Moosic into a party mode that scales by rotating the host more quickly and encouraging more rapid-fire prompts. The pacing picks up, the energy rises, and the chance of someone toppling into a fit of giggles increases exponentially.

### Creative rules and optional twists

Seasoned players can drop additional constraints: only allow two word answers, require a pun in the response, or ban the most popular picks for a round to force riskier choices. These variants extend the lifespan of the game beyond a single night and invite players to explore different social dynamics with the same prompt deck.
