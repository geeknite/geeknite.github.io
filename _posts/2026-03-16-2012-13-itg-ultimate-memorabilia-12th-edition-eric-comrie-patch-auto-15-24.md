### Grading and authenticity considerations
For grading, autograph integrity and patch stability matter most. Patches are evaluated for cloth integrity and seamless integration with the card surface; autographs are judged for legibility, ink consistency, and signing location. In ITG’s era, grading templates were not universal, yet many collectors pursue third-party evaluation for patches and autographs. If you intend to pursue a grade, request high-resolution photos and consider a reputable service that specializes in patch autos.

## Design and display tips
This card wants to be seen. A display with a dark, neutral background helps the patch colors pop; soft lighting that reveals ink without glare is ideal. A top loader plus semi-rigid sleeve provides basic protection, and a slab or magnetic case gives extra security for long-term display. Pair this card with a few related items to tell a story—perhaps another Comrie card from a similar era or a goalie-themed mini-collection that shows his career arc. Narrative curation adds value beyond the numbers.

### Practical care and preservation
Preservation is essential. Direct sunlight is a villain, but so is a volatile environment that can warp patches and fade ink. Aim for a stable climate; store in a cool, dry place; and handle with clean hands or gloves to minimize oils. Archival-quality storage materials help keep everything pristine; avoid cheap plastic sleeves that can crease or warp over time. Periodic checks can catch creeping issues before they become stories you don’t want to tell.

## Buying tips and red flags
- Verify edition and numbering: 15/24 should be visible; if not, ask for clarity.
- Inspect autograph legibility: A crisp signature is a good sign; a smeared one is a potential red flag, though not a death knell if the patch remains compelling.
- Check patch alignment: The patch should sit squarely within the card’s borders; misalignment can indicate manufacturing issues.
- Seek clear seller photos: Multiple angles, macro shots of stitching, and patch texture matter.
- Ask about provenance: Previous grading, encapsulation, and handling history can help you assess value and risk.
