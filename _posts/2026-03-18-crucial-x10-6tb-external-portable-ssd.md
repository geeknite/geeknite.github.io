Performance in the Real World

We ran a series of practical tests designed to mimic real-world creative workflows. The tests included large media file transfers, mixed-file workloads, and a few sneaky background tasks that would typically interfere with a background copy on a slower drive. In short, we tried to provide results that would actually matter to a creator, photographer, or gamer, not just a number shown on a spec sheet.

- Large-file transfers: Copying a 50 GB video project folder with hundreds of raw clips showed sustained write speeds in the 900–980 MB/s range on a capable USB-C port. Reads hovered in the 950–1050 MB/s range depending on file fragmentation and Drive Letter assignments on Windows. In other words, this is not a sloth; it is a turbocharged mule when you need to move big chunks of data quickly.
- Mixed workloads: Copying thousands of small files (textures, project assets, a few stock photo sets) typically yields lower speeds, with overheads that bring writes into the 400–600 MB/s zone. This is the world we live in; random IO matters for media libraries and game assets and the X10 handles it more gracefully than many budget drives.
- Random IO and boot times: If you use the X10 as a scratch disk for video editing software or game development assets, you will notice snappier edits and faster asset loads compared to older USB 3.0 drives. It isn’t a full blown NVMe internal drive, but the external experience feels surprisingly responsive given the portable form factor.

The drive does get warm under heavy workloads, which is perfectly normal for a compact metal body doing the heavy lifting. A light thermal pad or album of sticky notes is not required to fix anything; the chassis itself is doing the heat sharing work. If you are editing 4K video on a long flight, consider a short break every now and then to avoid any potential thermal throttling, though in our tests we didn t observe dramatic throttling during sustained transfers.
