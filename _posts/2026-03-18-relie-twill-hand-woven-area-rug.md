## Design and Materials

### Weave and texture

The Relie Twill is, as the name suggests, woven in a twill pattern. Twill weave is characterized by its diagonal rib lines that run across the fabric. This construction gives the rug a certain richness of texture that you can feel with your toes and see with your eyes from across the room. The diagonal lines are not overly pronounced, which means the rug reads clean and contemporary up close but rewards closer inspection with subtle geometry. In practical terms, twill is resilient because the weave distributes wear along the diagonal more evenly than plain weave options. It may not be the softest rug in the world, but it is not a sandpaper either—it sits somewhere in the comfortable middle, like a reliable cartridge in your favorite gadget.

### Materials used

Relie claims a hand woven approach with natural fibers. The most common iteration you will encounter uses a wool-based blend, sometimes with cotton for the warp or weft. Wool provides resilience, natural spring, and a degree of stain resistance, while cotton adds a bit of flexibility to the loom and makes certain colorways pop without becoming overpowering. The texture of wool allows feet to respect the rug as a surface but not as a grippy opponent; it slides on smoother floors, but remains stable under typical living room traffic. In a sunlit room, the rug will show a gentle variation in hue as natural fibers react to light, which is a fancy way of saying the rug gets a little more lively in daylight and calms down under lamps.

## Comfort, Feel, and Everyday Use

### Underfoot sensation

Hand woven wool rugs have a comfort profile that sits somewhere between a soft carpet and a firm, structured surface. The Relie Twill provides a cushy underfoot feel without being overly plush. If you are longing for a meditation cushion under your desk, this rug is not the same as a shag, but it offers enough softness to make computer chair rolls a little more forgiving. For standing tasks like cooking in front of an island or prepping plants in a sunlit corner, you’ll appreciate the balance between support and texture that twill weaves bring to the floor.

### Noise and warmth

In apartments and open-plan homes, a rug can help dampen footfalls and reduce echo by adding a layer of fiber and density underfoot. Wool has natural insulating properties, so in a drafty living room it can help keep the floor from feeling cold on chilly mornings. It won’t turn your space into a sauna, but it does contribute to a cozier atmosphere when paired with a warm color palette and soft lighting.

### Maintenance mindset

The big question with any wool rug is maintenance. Wool fibers resist stains better than synthetics in many cases, but they are not invincible. The key lies in early intervention. Blot spills with a clean white cloth, avoid rubbing, and treat stains with a wool-safe cleaner recommended by the manufacturer. For routine refreshment, vacuum with a suction-only attachment, avoiding the beater bar on delicate fibers. A gentle rotation every 6 to 12 months helps even wear and hue exposure from sunlight, preventing one side from looking perpetually sun-kissed while the other side pale.

## Care and Cleaning Guide

### Routine care

- Vacuum regularly with a suction-only attachment. 
- Rotate the rug seasonally to promote even wear and fading. 
- Use a rug pad underneath to reduce slipping and extend life. 

### Spill and stain protocol

- Blot liquids immediately with a white cloth or paper towel. Don’t rub. 
- For stains, follow the cleaner instructions on wool fabrics. Test on a small area first. 
- If the stain persists, consider professional cleaning. Wool rugs usually respond well to gentle, wool-safe cleaning methods. 

### Long-term preservation

- Avoid direct, prolonged sun exposure to minimize color fading. If you can, use window treatments to moderate UV. 
- Periodic professional cleaning every couple of years can refresh the fibers and preserve the weave integrity.
