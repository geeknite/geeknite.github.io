## Assembly and installation

Let us be honest: a 10-person workstation is not a one-person morning project. It is a multi-cart, multi-hour, possibly multi-beer event. The box count is substantial; plan for a couple of strong humans or a small team with a trolley. The estate of the furniture pieces is modular, which should reduce the complexity compared to a full custom build, but that does not mean there is zero complexity. 

### Prepping your space

- Confirm door width, hallway clearance, and any staircase turns. This unit will require careful handling and may need two people to guide it through tight spaces.
- Determine access to power and data outlets. If you are in a building with modest electrical capacity, you may want to consult a professional electrician to ensure you have adequate load for 10 workstations and any peripherals you intend to deploy.
- Decide on a layout. There are multiple configurations possible: single spine with ten stations, two five-station blocks, or an L-shaped arrangement that creates a natural meeting nook. Use painter tape to map out the footprint on the floor before you start moving components in.
- Prepare cable management gear. Zip ties, cable raceways, and standard grommets will be your friends here. Mind the heat and ensure adequate ventilation around the central trunk.

### Step by step assembly gist

- Start with the spine frame on a clean surface. Attach the two rows to form the backbone of the system. Check alignment with a level and adjust until the spine sits square.
- Attach the desk tops along the spine. The exact attachment method will vary by model iteration; ensure screws are snug but not overly tight as you want to avoid warping.
- Install cable management components. Route the main trunk along the underside and secure it at fixed points. Add side channels and any optional dividers according to your chosen configuration.
- Connect power distribution if included and route data conduits to the central point. If you have a shared power strip, consider mounting it under the spine end for easy reach and cleaner lines.
- Add accessories such as monitor arms, CPU holders, and document trays if you have them. Do not go crazy with aftermarket gadgets; you want functionality, not a portal to the thrice-daily cable apocalypse. 
- Perform a final safety check. Tighten all bolts, verify stability, and test with a light load before putting the team on board. 

If you have never assembled a large modular desk before, give yourself extra time and a few extra hands. The satisfaction of a completed setup is huge, but the journey there includes several moments of puzzlement and a surprising amount of foam packing and tiny screws that vanish into the couch of the universe.

### Practical tips during assembly

- Use a magnetic screwdriver to keep screws from rolling away, especially during a tight corner turn.
- Keep a spare power strip within reach of the central trunk to facilitate quick top-ups for laptops and chargers.
- Label cables as you lay them down. You will thank future you when you rearrange a workstation or troubleshoot a connectivity issue.
- Document your layout with a quick sketch or a simple diagram. When the team relocates for a surprise standup, you will be glad you did.

The beauty of a no-screen system is that you can tailor the final look to your team; the downside is that management has to approve a consistent display policy that aligns with your security and data governance requirements.

## Maintenance and longevity

The hardware is designed to stand up to a busy office. Regular cleaning is recommended to maintain the matte black finish and to prevent dust build-up in cable channels. A quick wipe with a damp microfiber cloth will keep the surface looking sharp, and a less frequent, deeper clean with a mild cleaner will prevent grime from building up in corners. Under normal use, you should expect minimal wear and tear on the frame, provided you avoid extreme velocity when moving components or dragging chairs across the surface. The key to longevity with any large modular system is consistent maintenance: revisit cable management every quarter, check fasteners every six months, and re-check alignment after a major internal office reconfiguration. 

If you are moving offices, document your new floor plan and reuse the same layout by following your existing assembly instructions. Keeping a detailed parts list and a schematic of your central trunk will save you a lot of headaches when you reassemble in a new location.
