## How Skip-Bo Plays: Rules for the Nostalgic
Skip-Bo has a reputation for being approachable to novices but cunning enough to challenge players who take board games seriously in a positive way. The core mechanic is to build up the foundation piles in numerical order from 1 to 12. The stock piles are your private goal; you must work through your stock pile by playing cards onto the foundation piles in ascending order. The deck includes wild cards that can fill any numeral gap, which adds drama and last minute, heart-stopping moments that make you rethink every decision you made during your last turn.

### Basic Turn Structure
- Draw cards to a hand up to a certain limit (usually five or six, depending on your table rules).
- On your turn, you can play top cards from your stock piles onto your building piles in the right order.
- You can also play cards from your hand onto the building piles as long as they fit the sequence, starting at 1.
- Wild cards can fill any missing number, which can turn a stagnant game into a sprint toward victory.
- If you manage to play all cards from your stock piles, you win the round. The first player to win a set number of rounds claims victory overall.

The rules are kept intentionally lean, allowing players to improvise and argue about a misread ruling with good humor. And if your memory is a bit fuzzy, you can always refer back to the simple sheet that ships with the set. The game thrives on quick, clean turns and a bit of bluffing when you pretend that you do not actually need your last Skip-Bo card to climb to the finish line.

### Strategy Notes for the Vintage Player
- Early tempo matters. Getting a few cards into your building piles early opens up the possibility to chain plays later in the round.
- Card management is key. If you can hold onto wilds for the late game, you can disrupt opponents by forcing them to draw through their own stock piles faster than they planned.
- Recognize the risk of over-optimizing. Like many classic card games, Overthinking can lead to delays that give your opponents an edge when you stare at a hand for five minutes trying to decide if there is a better sequence.
