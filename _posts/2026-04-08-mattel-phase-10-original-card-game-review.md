## What is Phase 10?

Phase 10 is a card game about completing ten phases (tasks) as you pass around a track of color, numbers, and cunning. The Mattel Original Card Game version uses a standard deck modified with color-coded numbers, plus special cards like Wild and Skip. The goal is to be the first player to complete all ten phases and get rid of your hand. The gameplay is simple enough for kids to grasp, but there is enough strategy to keep adults engaged for multiple rounds.

### How to play (the quick version)

On your turn you draw a card and discard one card. If you can lay down a phase on your turn, you do so. Laying down means you meet the phase’s requirements, such as two sets of three, or a run of four, etc., and you place those cards on the table in front of you. Each round ends when a player goes out by discarding their last card. That player wins the round and scores based on the cards left in other players' hands. The first player to complete all ten phases wins the game.

- Use Wild cards to stand in for any card, but be mindful of keeping them available for your own needs.
- Use Skip cards strategically to slow down the person who is closest to finishing.
- When you draw a Wild or a Skip, consider whether it helps you more to hold onto it and use it later, or to dump it now to disrupt someone else’s plan.

## The phases (an overview)

Chapter of the phases: There are ten different phases you must complete throughout the game. They escalate in complexity, and you’ll find your strategy changing as you move through them. As a general idea, you’ll encounter a mix of sets and runs, sometimes combining them in ways that require careful planning. In practice, this means you’ll be building a plan: [I want to finish Phase 3 first because it’s easier to get to the top if I do], only to be forced to reconfigure after the draw of the night.

## Strategy and tips for winning

- Be flexible with your plan. If a run becomes untenable, switch to sets. If your early plan is derailed by discards and draws, adjust quickly.
- Track opponents’ discards to infer what they’re chasing. If you see a flood of a certain color or number, there’s a good chance someone is close to a phase that requires that.
- Wild cards are powerful but finite. Use them to fill the most critical gaps, but don’t be afraid to let others suspect that you’re playing a different game altogether.
- The discard pile is a weapon if used properly. If there is a card you know you don’t want, consider discarding it strategically to influence the game.
- End-game management matters. The moment someone is about to go out, you may want to alter your plans to reduce the risk of giving away a high point hand.

## Variants and house rules

Phase 10 is a very flexible game. Some groups play with shorter thresholds (play to the first 5-6 phases) to speed things up. Others combine multiple Phase 10 rounds to create a longer epic session. House rules can adjust the scoring or the way Skip cards impact turns, and some players like to add “point bonuses” for extra ambitious finishes. If you’re feeling experimental, consider trying a variant where players can swap cards with the discard pile under certain conditions or where there is team-based play to share the burden of completing phases.

## The experience: anecdotes and practical notes

From the demo table to the final hand, Phase 10 is a study in social dynamics. It’s a game where you’ll celebrate small wins—like completing a tricky phase with an improbable Wild card—and lament losses in the most dramatic fashion possible. It’s a game that emphasizes group dynamics as much as card math: there’s ambivalence over whether to maldify the other players’ actions or to simply accept the randomness of the draw. The Jekyll-and-Hyde feel of the game is charming: sometimes you’re calm, collected, and strategic; other times you’re shouting like a classic gamer in a mid-2000s LAN party.

A few practical notes:

- The score pad matters. Keep it accessible and legible, or you’ll forget who’s ahead and who’s behind.
- The tempo matters. Don’t drag after someone finishes. The social energy will sag, and you’ll lose the momentum that makes Phase 10 so addictive.
- The table matters. Phase 10 is much more fun with a group of people who enjoy playful banter and light-hearted trash talk. If you’re playing with people who take it too seriously, you may want to swap games; this is not the game for a panel discussion on probability.
