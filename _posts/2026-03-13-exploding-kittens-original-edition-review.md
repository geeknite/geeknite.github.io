## What is Exploding Kittens Original Edition?

Exploding Kittens is a card game designed by Elan Lee, Matthew Inman (the Oatmeal), and Shane Small. The premise is ridiculous in the best possible way: players take turns drawing cards from a single deck. Some cards help you survive; some cards make other players cry a little inside; and a handful of cards threaten to blow up the entire table with a dramatic flourish. The core mechanic is simple enough for a younger crowd to grasp, while still offering enough mischief to keep adults entertained for multiple rounds.

The Original Edition is the baseline version of the game. It comes with a deck of cards that includes the exploding kitten cards, defuse cards, and a selection of action cards that influence the risk and reward of each draw. It is the kind of game that looks approachable on the surface but reveals layers of tension and hilarity once you get a few rounds under your belt. It also kickstarted a wave of memes, merch, and a handful of spin-offs that attempted to better reflect the internet’s appetite for adorable chaos.

If you want a quick read on the official background, the Kickstarter page offers a tale of how a quirky idea became a global party staple. ## How to Play: Setup and Turn Flow

The core flow of Exploding Kittens is deceptively straightforward. To start, the number of players can vary, but the general approach remains the same: players draw cards, play cards to influence the draw, and try not to explode. The last player standing wins. Here is a more detailed look at how it unfolds in a typical round.

### Setup
1. Remove all Exploding Kitten cards from the deck if you want a more forgiving turn count, or include all of them for a game that’s willing to risk dramatic endings. In the Original Edition, the standard approach is to remove enough Exploding Kitten cards so that there are exactly one fewer kitten cards than players. This guarantees a dramatic finale, often with a surprising number of defuses in hand.
2. Shuffle the deck and deal a starting hand to each player. A typical starting hand includes a couple of Defuse cards and a few action cards. You want enough tools to react, but not so many that the board becomes a math problem rather than a social heckling festival.
3. Place the remaining deck in the center, face down. You will draw from this pile on your turn.

### Turn Structure
A player’s turn can be simple or chaotic depending on the cards in play. A basic turn looks like this:
- Draw one card from the deck at the end of your turn (or at a different point if you’ve played certain action cards).
- If you draw an Exploding Kitten and you do not have a Defuse card, you’re out of the round. If you do have a Defuse, you defuse the kitten and place the Exploding Kitten back into the deck in a position of your choosing (some players like to bury it deeper for a moment of suspense).
- End of turn. If you played an action card, its effects take place according to the card’s text. If not, you simply pass to the next player.

### Card Types in Focus
- Exploding Kitten: The dreaded card that ends your turn with a pop and a puff of dramatic smoke (also known as the card that makes you cry when you’re unprepared).
- Defuse: Your life insurance policy. It lets you reinsert the Exploding Kitten into the deck after defusing it. It’s a precious resource, and you’ll use it on someone else if you can.
- See the Future: A knowledge card that lets you peek at the top three cards of the deck. It’s information, which is power, which can be used to avoid disaster or to torment your friends with spoilers.
- Attack: Forces the next player to take two turns in a row. It’s basically saying, Here, have double trouble while I watch your panic.
- Skip: Skip your draw. Sometimes you need a breather, or you want to pass the risk to someone else.
- Shuffle: When the deck becomes too predictable, shuffle it and watch the chaos erupt again.
- Nope: A counter-card that cancels any action (except for the Exploding Kitten itself). This little card is the chaos multiplier that can derail your neat plans in the most glorious fashion.
- See the Future: See the Future is a staple goodie that gives you foresight; the more players you have, the more fun it becomes to abuse the early knowledge for maximum dramatic impact.

The interplay of these cards creates a dance. Some rounds are quiet, a strategic waltz of who defuses which kitten, who sees the future and who simply refuses to draw. Other rounds resemble a chaotic mosh pit of counter-cards and dramatic reveals. Either way, the pace stays brisk, and there is a real satisfaction in pulling off a well-timed defuse under pressure.

## Why It Works as a Party Game (and When It Doesn’t)

Exploding Kittens hits the sweet spot for many party-night situations. It requires minimal setup, quick rounds, and a mood that tolerates a little chaos. It scales up nicely from a small group to a larger crowd, which makes it a versatile choice for gatherings where you might be rotating players. But there are caveats. For players who want heavy strategy and long, methodical play, Exploding Kittens can feel shallow and repetitive after a handful of rounds. The luck element — drawing from a single deck with explosives waiting to go off — is real, and sometimes there isn't much you can do to influence the outcome beyond hoping for a defuse card or a perfectly timed counter. In other words, it’s a social experience more than a tactical conquest. Some players adore that; others crave a game where decisions matter more than a few lucky draws.

If you’re playing with younger players, or you want to keep the high-energy vibe, the game shines. If you’re trying to host a more serious game night with a focus on strategy or long-term planning, you might want to pair it with another game or choose a more deeply tactical option as the lead title for the evening. The key is balancing expectations: Exploding Kittens is a comedic, fast, and chaotic experience rather than a grand game of chess dressed in cat ears.

## Strategy and House Rules: Getting More from Your Rounds

There is room to inject some house rules to keep the game fresh across sessions. Below are some practical ideas that keep the laughter high while sharpening the play:
- Limit the number of turns per round: If the table is large, you can cap the round length by setting a maximum number of draws or by banning overly long standoffs in later stages.
- Draft-defuse variant: Allow players to choose their starting Defuse cards during setup, but with a cap on how many you can hold. This prevents early hoarding and creates a more diverse tactic from the first turn.
- Nope power balance: If you want more chaos, reduce or remove Nope cards from the deck. If you want more counterplay, increase their count. The choice depends on whether you want a more aggressive or a more protective social dynamic.
- See the Future with consequences: Make See the Future reveal a specific action that would be most beneficial to the viewing player. For instance, you could require the seeing player to pass plus one card to the next person, adding a small puzzle element to the reveal.

These house rules can stretch the play time, so make sure your group is on board with longer rounds before you start folding them into your rotation.

## The Replay Value Equation

Replay value for Exploding Kittens is a function of three things: the group, the energy level, and the mood you want for the night. In practice, the game rewards familiarity but not obsession. You get better as you recognize patterns, count the likelihood of a defuse, and learn which players are more likely to bluff while counting cards. The more players you have, the more dramatic the misdirection becomes. If you want to maximize replayability, consider mixing in expansions, rotate who leads the rules, and keep a loose policy about what counts as a friendly, good-spirited trash talk session.
