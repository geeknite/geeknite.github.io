## Specifications and what they mean
### Core specs
The Archer T4U is a USB 3.0 dual band adapter. It supports 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz networks with a theoretical ceiling that commonly lands just over 1 Gbps when you do the math across both bands. In real terms, expect upper 300 Mbps on 2.4 GHz and around 867 Mbps on 5 GHz under ideal conditions. This is the sweet spot for online gaming, high-definition streams, and snappy file transfers without needing to upgrade your router or your PC’s network stack.

Key practical points to remember:
- USB 3.0 is the gateway to decent speeds. If you plug this into a USB 2.0 port, you are likely to feel the lag of a dial up dinosaur wearing roller skates.
- The 2.4 GHz band is more forgiving, but more congested. The 5 GHz band is faster but requires a clear line of sight to your router or a fair amount of adaptive pathfinding from the devices around you.
- External antennas improve range and reliability in tricky rooms with concrete walls or a dog who keeps stepping on your router’s cooling vent.

## Troubleshooting tips and common issues
### Drops and disconnects
If you experience random disconnects, start with basic checks: ensure the USB port is not experiencing power issues, try a different USB port, and verify router firmware. Sometimes a simple driver update or a quick change of channel can restore stability. If you notice persistent drops, check for interference sources like microwaves or Bluetooth devices near the router; repositioning either the router or the dongle can yield dramatic improvements. 

### Performance quirks
A few users report that enabling or disabling certain power management features in the OS can influence performance. If you notice inconsistent speeds, experiment with power settings and see if a conservative approach yields steadier results. In many cases, a clean driver install resolves such quirks once and for all. 

### Compatibility concerns
If you run into compatibility issues on macOS or Linux, confirm kernel support for the chipset and review any available TP-Link resources or widely used community drivers. The Linux space tends to be very community-driven, which means helpful guidance is often available but may require a bit of detective work.
