## Condition and grading: how to assess your No. 262 gem
Condition matters more in Carddas than in a casual sports-card pile, especially for a Very Rare card. Here are practical tips to assess quality without losing sleep:
- Corners: Look for crisp, square corners with minimal whitening. In Japanese printing, some edge wear is common, but severe corner damage reduces value more than minor surface scuffs.
- Surface: A clean surface with minimal scratches or gloss issues is ideal. A soft scratch can be cosmetic, but multiple scratches can compound in photos or vending-machine lighting.
- Centering: Some Carddas cards aren’t perfectly centered by design, a nod to their vintage machine-driven production. However, extreme off-centering can affect display values in grading.
- Color and print clarity: Compare your card to known-good references. Faded color or ink bleed can indicate exposure to heat or moisture—but a lightly sun-faded edge sometimes adds “character.”

Grading services specific to Carddas are not as universally standardized as sports cards, but many Japanese card graders understand the niche. For international collectors, consider keeping a card in a protective sleeve with a rigid top loader and avoiding excessive handling. If you’re thinking about sending a No. 262 to a grader, be prepared for a long turnaround and a potential sticker shock, but also the possibility of a higher final value if the card is exceptionally preserved.

## Care, storage, and display: keeping the magic alive
A lot of Carddas collecting boils down to how you protect your cards from the slow, relentless march of time.
- Sleeves and top loaders: Use acid-free sleeves and rigid top loaders. Carddas cards are smaller than typical American-style cards, so ensure your sleeves fit. A snug fit prevents edge wear from card-to-card contact.
- Binders: Choose a binder with proper thickness pages—ideally with anti-glare sleeves to keep light from washing out the colors.
- Environment: Keep cards away from direct sunlight, humidity, and temperature extremes. DBZ characters deserve a stable climate, not a sunburn or a war against rust on the binder rings.
- Handling: Minimize handling. Use clean, dry hands or wear cotton gloves if you’re in pro collector mode. Keep a microfiber cloth handy for quick polish avoidance—watermarks can ruin the image panel more than a minor corner ding.

Display ideas: a glass-front display case with LED strip lighting can make No. 262 pop. If you’re into DIY, you can build a small shadow box framed around the card with a monograph of the Dragon Ball fusion mythology on the back wall. It’s nerd-chic, it’s geeky, and it’s absolutely you.

## Authenticity, verification, and where to buy
If you’re building a No. 262 collection, you’ll likely encounter a mix of resale marketplaces, auction sites, and local hobby shops. Here are practical steps to verify authenticity and avoid being burned by a counterfeit:
- Compare to reference images: Use multiple sources of reference images to confirm the card’s color balance, line sharpness, and text alignment.
- Check the packaging: Some prints were offered with protective sleeves or small display booklets. If your card arrives with a suspiciously modern slip or a non-standard packaging, you might be looking at a counterfeit or a later reprint.
- Ask for provenance: If a seller can provide a story—where and when they acquired the card, any trades, or photos of the card in its original packaging—that adds credibility.
- Seek professional opinions: When in doubt, turn to community experts. There are passionate DBZ Carddas communities that can help you assess a No. 262’s legitimacy.
