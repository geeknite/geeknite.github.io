## Core Mechanics
### Turn order and actions
Turns flow with the grace of a caffeinated octopus. On your turn you typically do:
1) Do one major action (build, recruit, move, or upgrade),
2) Take a secondary action (draw a card, gain income, steal a neighbor’s resources),
3) Pass or prepare for the next round.
The trick is memory: you must remember what you did last round because your opponent will forget what you did and pretend it never happened.

### Resource management
Resources come in flavors: wood, stone, gold, influence, and synergy. The trick is to know when to hoard and when to sprint. Hoarding is for vampires and dragons; you, friend, are a person who must also pay rent.

### Combat (if applicable)
If your The Game includes combat, you know the drill: dice, dice modifiers, and the creeping suspicion that your unit is better at building a tank than fighting a siege engine. The payoff is that even a minor victory can tilt the game in your favor if timed correctly. The important moral: you don’t need to win every battle; you need to win the last one.

## Strategy Fundamentals
The bread-and-butter of any strategy game is not simply the best unit or the best build; it’s the ability to adapt.

### The meta and the map
Maps matter. The layout of starting positions can decide the early rhythm of the game. Some maps reward early expansion; others reward careful development of a strong mid-game economy. The meta—what players expect to do in typical games—changes as people learn from losses and triumphs. This is not destiny; it’s a reflection of how humans adapt when the timer is ticking.

### Positioning and tempo
Tempo is the measure of how quickly you are able to do your actions relative to your opponent. The general rule: early tempo matters more when you can snowball into a lead; later tempo matters when you have to survive the final rounds. Good players know when to press and when to hold.

### Economy vs. military
If The Game leans into resource collection plus board control, you must balance economy and military. If you lean too far in either direction, you may blow through your resources too quickly or fail to mitigate threats at the map edges.

## Openers and Build Orders
This is where the bravest players emerge from their basements and realize that a well-timed opening can set the table for victory.

### Beginner opener
- Focus on steady resource collection.
- Build a flexible unit or base early to avoid bottlenecks.
- Plan your next three turns with contingencies for common misplays.

### Intermediate build path
- Scout early to gain information.
- Invest in a stable mid-game engine (a “board backbone” that supports multiple options).
- Start controlling choke points.

### Advanced tempo sequences
- Learn the timing: when to press for expansion; when to retreat to defend.
- Use decoys and misdirection to confuse opponents and draw them into traps.
- Your best tool is the ability to adapt to what your opponent does.
