### What is Herd Mentality Family Edition?

In the family edition, prompts skew toward universal experiences, silly hypotheticals, or nostalgic prompts that spark shared memories, rather than edgy content. ### The Table Setup and Flow

To set up, designate a moderator for the round (the person who will read prompts and keep time). Everyone else gets a pencil and paper to write down their answer privately. The moderator reads the prompt aloud, and the clock starts. Players write down an answer that they think is likely to be chosen by the majority. When time’s up, all answers are revealed and shuffled so no one can immediately identify who wrote what. Then everyone uses the voting discs to choose which answer they think represents the majority response. If your chosen answer is indeed part of the majority, you score points. If your own answer is part of the majority, you also score—because sometimes the best strategy is to think like… well, the herd.

In the family edition, the emphasis is on inclusivity and laughter more than cutthroat competition. It’s about how many people can signal a shared experience without stepping on anyone’s toes. That makes it a great vehicle for teaching kids about social dynamics in a fun, low-stakes way, while adults get to relive those embarrassing early-2000s fashion choices in the most wholesome context possible.

> Quick note: the “majority” concept can be trickier with a big crowd, and that’s part of the charm. If your group is split 50/50, you’ll see the classic dilemma of who screams “I’m in the majority!” loudest. In real life, that’s basically every family reunion condensed into a single table.

### Round Structure: A Sample Pass-Through

1) Prompt is revealed: “Name a food that should always be served at family gatherings.”
2) Players privately write an answer: “potatoes,” “ham,” “mac and cheese,” etc.
3) Answers are revealed and shuffled: no one knows who submitted what yet.
4) Players cast votes for the answer they think the majority would choose.
5) Points are tallied: those who picked the majority answer score, and the author of the most common answer also secures a point if their answer was chosen by a majority.
6) A new round begins with the next player acting as moderator.

The cadence is fast, the laughter is frequent, and the occasional groan is completely normal—after all, groans are basically the oldest running joke in family entertainment.

### Variants and House Rules
If you want to spice things up beyond the standard flow, here are a few family-friendly variants you can try:
- Team Herd: Split into two or three teams and compare majority choices across teams. The team with the most correct matches wins extra points.
- Lightning Mode: Shorten the timer to 30 seconds to increase tempo and chaos in a good way. It’s like speed dating, but for answers.
- Silly Penalty: For the funniest or most outrageous answer, award a “golden rubber chicken” token that’s purely decorative but incredibly satisfying to claim.
- Nostalgia Nights: Create prompts that focus on shared memories from different decades. It’s amazing how a simple prompt can uncover a repressed memory about 90s cartoons or early-2000s tech.

### Hosting Tips: Getting the Most Laughter per Minute
- Set the tone early: a brief, goofy intro helps everyone loosen up. A quick montage of “the Herd” in your house can be a joke on the living room dynamics.
- Make a “house style of prompts”: encourage players to customize prompts with inside jokes that won’t single anyone out. You’ll get more authentic answers that way.
- Use the timer as a mini-game: the person who speeds through their answers should get a bonus point for confidence. The person who overthinks might get a penalty or a gentle roast.
- Rotate moderators: keeping the moderator rotation quick ensures no single player dominates the table and gives everyone a chance to shape the round.
- Have snacks and water ready: a thirsty crowd is a grumpy crowd, and a grumpy crowd is not your friend when trying to achieve a heartwarming majority.
