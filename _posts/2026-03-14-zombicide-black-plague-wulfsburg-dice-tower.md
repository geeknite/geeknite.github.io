## What is Zombicide Black Plague, and what does Wulfsburg bring?

Zombicide: Black Plague is a cooperative, dungeon-crawly zombie game that plays in waves. Players take on the roles of survivors who have to scavenge, fight, and sometimes bargain with the undead to make it across a mission board and survive until the end. It’s chaotic, it’s tactile, and it’s surprisingly satisfying when you manage to push a savage zombie off a balcony with a lucky weapon combo.

Wulfsburg is an expansion that nudges everything toward a fortress siege vibe. It adds a new district board that feels like a castle suburb fell into a modern war, plus a handful of new minis, new cards, and a few new objective cards that tempt you to chase side quests even when the main objective is screaming at you to hurry up. The fortress look is part of the expansion’s charm: you’re not just fighting a mob of undead; you’re trying to secure a keep, seal a gate, and prevent the last workers from retreating into a neighboring bakery while you sprint after the loot you just glimpsed around a corner.

If you’re wondering about tone, Wulfsburg does not shy away from a dramatic filter. The new components look grand on the table, and the new enemies hit with more cinematic flair than your average zombie horde. Expect boss mechanics that feel like a miniature boss fight from a fantasy RPG, with a dash of “I have a plan,” followed by “No you don’t,” as the plan collapses in a glorious flourish of panicked dice and improvised strategy.


### Components and production values

- A new fortress board with a mix of streets, courtyards, and gateway chokepoints. It’s not just a repaint; the layout enforces new tactical decisions and makes you think twice about movement routing.
- New survivors delta: more varied starting abilities, which means you can build a creative party that matches your playstyle (or your friend’s stubborn insistence on being the “melee tank who can also pick a lock with one hand tied behind their back”).
- New weapons, armor, and items: enough kit to turn a hopeless charge into a hair-raising, last-stand victory if luck and timing align.
- New zombie types plus a handful of boss-level adversaries: these are the kind of threats that demand you coordinate with your squad rather than sprinting solo with a lucky last-shot.
- New scenarios and objectives: the fortress setting is conducive to hooking together multiple mission threads, which means your campaign nights can feel a little more like a TV arc and a little less like a single-tactical-tile “land two hits, survive” loop.

The visual design stays in line with the Black Plague aesthetic—bright, chunky minis and a color palette that makes it easy to spot weapons and their effects on the board. If you like a game that looks as bold as it plays, Wulfsburg delivers with confidence, even if you’re a little overwhelmed at first by the sheer number of elements on the table.


## The Fortress Board: why it matters

The fortress board is the real star here. It introduces the sense of “zones” and “line of sight” that the base game occasionally lacks. You’ll have more opportunities to block a doorway, force a zombie into a bottleneck, or create a death corridor that sees a line of survivors juggling a handful of crucial turns while the boss paces at the far end of the map.

The map’s design invites careful planning. It rewards the kind of thinking you’ll recognize from real-time strategy games: hold this corridor, control this stairwell, and use the gate to funnel the undead into an inevitable, cinematic trap. It’s a style of play that feels very rewarding once you pull off a well-coordinated multi-turn plan where every teammate’s action creates a chain reaction leading to a dramatic finish.


### Bosses and special encounters

Wulfsburg brings a handful of boss designs that are more than just “big health bars.” They’re integrated into the mission flow in a way that changes your endgame pacing. Boss mechanics often require careful timing, a read of the board state, and a bit of risk-taking on your parts. If you enjoy boss battles in video games or RPGs, you’ll find the Zombicide take on bosses to be satisfying: it feels like you’re fighting a villain from a fantasy epic rather than just another wave of walkers.

One of the delightful things about these encounters is the way they turn the board into a stage. When the boss is active, the board becomes a choreography of attacks and countermoves. It’s not just a “kill the boss and take the loot” moment; it’s a mini-act within your campaign where players must react adaptively to changing threats and timing windows.


## Play flow: setup, turns, and pacing

Setup is longer than base Zombicide—there’s more board to lay, more cards to shuffle, and more variables to consider. This is the kind of expansion where you want to assign a “rule explainer” for first few sessions, just to speed up the early game and reduce the “rule-checking intermission” that breaks the flow.

Turn flow remains familiar: you activate your survivor, take movement actions, attack, and possibly draw loot. But with Wulfsburg, you’ll want to think about door control, line of sight, and the timing of board transitions. A good player will be able to create a plan that uses two or three turns in advance, while a great player can read the board and pivot when a boss suddenly changes the threat level mid-turn.

If you’re a storyteller at heart, the fortress and its scenarios invite you to weave mini-narratives: a heroic door-crossing, a last-minute reload of a crossbow as the undead flood the courtyard, a deliberate sacrifice to save a teammate who can still turn the tide. You’ll find yourself weaving silly banter and epic quotes into turn summaries, which is half the fun of co-op gaming in the first place.


### Solo play and replayability

The solo mode in Zombicide remains a bonafide thing, but Wulfsburg shines when you have a group. For solo players, the expansion adds more material to generate longer “one-person campaigns,” which can be fun if you’re into self-imposed challenges. For groups, the enhanced map, more objectives, and additional boss encounters mean the session can scale into a multi-hour epic that tests memory, planning, and your ability to stay calm under the siege of cardboard monsters.

Replayability comes from multiple angles: the fortress branching paths, the variety of survivors, and the different boss configurations you can choose to include in a mission. If you’re someone who enjoys returning to a game night with a different strategic question each time, Wulfsburg provides ample raw material for that kind of play.

The miniatures are large enough to spark a little fangirl/fanboy squeal around the table, and the loot cards provide moments of gleeful chaos whenever someone draws a weapon that looks ridiculous but ends up being the perfect fit for that turn.

You’ll feel like you’re playing a heroic chapter from a fantasy saga, complete with close calls, dramatic saves, and a healthy dose of luck that keeps everyone honest. The humor—mostly self-deprecating banter about your teammates’ dice luck and the occasional mock-heroic line—lands well in Geeknite’s voice, which specializes in taking tabletop moments and turning them into little stories you’ll retell at future game nights.

## Strategy tips, house rules, and “what to do in the moment” advice

- Map awareness is everything: use the fortress layout to pressure zones and funnel zombies into kill corridors. The first rule of Wulfsburg is not to forget that doors are not just walls; they’re strategic assets you can lock, open, or use as a temporary shield.
- The “plan three steps ahead” approach works better than “solve one problem now.” In Wulfsburg, the boss can change the chessboard on a whim, so having a few contingency plans ready is extremely valuable.
- Loot management matters: not all loot cards are immediately useful. Some items are situational, so think about how you can convert a questionable card into a long-term advantage down the road.
- Don’t underestimate the power of a well-timed retreat. Sometimes the best action is to pull back, regroup, and reframe the objective rather than throwing bodies at a fortress wall until the clock runs out.


## Verdict and rating

- Strengths: Adds a new narrative flavor, increases tactical depth, expands the playlist of scenarios, and gives bosses a satisfying sense of grandeur. The fortress map makes positioning feel meaningful in a way the base game occasionally lacks.
-Weaknesses: Setup takes longer; you’ll want a dedicated pre-game organization ritual if you’re hosting overcrowded sessions. For new players, the learning curve is steeper than a single-box game rotation.

Our takeaway: Wulfsburg is a strong, thematically rich expansion that elevates Zombicide: Black Plague from “great group game” to “epic-co-op siege night.” It’s not essential for every group, but for players who crave more fortress vibes, bigger boss moments, and a little more ornate chaos, it’s a worthy purchase.


## Final recommendation

If you’re into long, story-driven co-ops with a fortress siege aesthetic, Wulfsburg nails the vibe and delivers a robust set of new tactical possibilities. If your table is already juggling a dozen expansions or your sessions try to stay under 90 minutes, you may want to approach with a bit more planning—or keep it as a monthly “big night” treat. Either way, it’s a strong addition that will spark a lot of memorable moments, a few dramatic near-misses, and more than a few “I can’t believe that just happened” posts among your gaming group.
