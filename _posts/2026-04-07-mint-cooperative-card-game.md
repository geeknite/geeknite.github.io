What is Mint Cooperative Card Game?
Mint is a cooperative card game built around the idea of running a tiny, bustling mint—the place that stamps out fresh coins with the enthusiasm of a synchronized swimming team. Each player takes on a role with a unique ability, and together you must complete a sequence of production and distribution tasks before the clock strikes “deflation,” or whatever the game decides is the end of the economy as you know it. If you’re a fan of games that reward collaboration more than backstabbing and you like the aroma of victory that smells faintly of peppermint and victory-laundering, Mint might be your next obsession.

Components
- 1 rulebook that is surprisingly readable
- 60 Mint cards that are colorful in the way a bag of M&Ms is colorful
- 8 Action cards that actually feel like they matter
- 20 Coin tokens (the kind you don’t want to flip too quickly in real life)
- 4 Player mats that are sturdy enough to survive kitchen-table warfare
- 1 Market board that looks suspiciously like a tiny stock exchange for herbs
- 1 Clock tracker (because every good coop needs a strict time limit)

Setup
1. Shuffle the Mint deck thoroughly. If you’re the kind of person who hums while shuffling, you belong here.
2. Each player chooses a role and takes the corresponding mat.
3. Place the Clock tracker to the starting time; typically “12” means deep breath, not midnight.
4. Reveal the first few Market cards to form the early-round market.
5. Sort the coin tokens by denomination and keep them within reach, like a coin-flavored talisman.

How to Play
Turn structure
- On your turn you typically take two actions. Actions include Playing a Card, Trading with the Market, or activating a special ability on your Role card.
- You’ll be building a supply chain in real-time. If your chain breaks or your mint’s quality control fails, you’ll need to improvise with a few quick-play cards that can salvage the situation—assuming you’ve managed your resources properly.
- The goal is to complete a sequence of production steps before the Clock reaches the end; this can involve creating a certain set of mint types, fulfilling demand on the Market, and ensuring all players survive the stress of the mint’s daily grind.

A sample turn
- You draw up to your hand size (which varies with role) and perform two actions.
- You can Play a Card to add resources or upgrade capabilities.
- You can Trade with the Market to acquire different types of coins or resources.
- If you trigger a Market event, you may need to adapt quickly; think of it as a dramatic interruption, but with a color palette that belongs in a candy shop.
- End your turn by resolving any Market effects and passing to the next player.

Strategy and Roles
Mint’s joy comes from the interplay between roles. Each role has a unique ability that encourages cooperative synergy rather than solo heroics. For example:
- The Alchemist elevates resource conversion; you won’t just mint coins—you’ll craft compound currency that can unlock new actions.
- The Architect improves production lines; think of it as upgrading your factory with better logistics rather than mere cosmetic upgrades.
- The Keeper ensures you don’t waste coins; this role has a knack for preserving resources when the clock is ticking.

Tips
- Balance short-term gains with long-term production. Mint runs on memory and momentum; you’ll need to recall what cards you’ve discarded and what you still can pull from the deck.
- Communicate clearly. The table is your moral compass; if you keep secrets, the Market will punish you with a stray token or an odd negative effect.
- Use Market events to your advantage. Some players fear the Market; those are the moments you’ll want to lean into the chaos—within reason, of course.

Artwork and Theme
The art style leans into a modern, clean aesthetic with bold color blocks and playful fonts. It feels like a high-end coffee shop that decided to start minting currency as a hobby. The minting process is portrayed with a sense of whimsy that fits the cooperative mood perfectly, while still keeping the theme of production and distribution plausible enough to avoid immersion-breaking.

Mood and Humor
- The flavor text on cards leans into mint puns. You’ll read lines like “Freshly minted. Don’t lick it; you’ll ruin the economy” and you’ll smile, because the world needed more jokes about currency and breath-freshening herbs.
- The game doesn’t take itself too seriously, which is good, because the reality of running a mint under a time limit would be terrifying, and a little levity is exactly what you want at the table.

Replayability and Difficulty
Mint includes several difficulty levels and multiple end-game conditions. The base game offers a solid challenge that scales as players improve in coordination. If you’re chasing a longer, heavier experience, try a few variant rules that add more Market cards or alternate end conditions. The variety keeps Mint feeling fresh across sessions.

Solo and Duo Modes
If you’re a lone-wolf gamer or you have a partner who occasionally ghosts your group messages, Mint has a viable solo mode. It’s not just “one-handed solitaire with a timer”; it’s a carefully tuned puzzle that challenges you to optimize your actions and route planning. Duo mode, where two players team up as a double-role synergy, can be surprisingly satisfying and surprisingly exhausting (in a good way).

Comparison to Other Cooperative Games
Mint sits somewhere between the warmth of a family-friendly co-op and the brain-burning tension of a well-tuned puzzle game. If you’ve loved Pandemic for its collaborative problem-solving but wanted something lighter on fiddly rules, Mint hits the sweet spot. If you’ve enjoyed The Crew for its card-driven co-op and press-your-luck elements, Mint adds a tangible theme and a market/production twist that spices the gameplay.

Final Verdict and Recommendation
Mint Cooperative Card Game is a successful blend of cooperative pacing, strategic planning, and playful theming. It rewards communication and careful resource management without requiring a spreadsheet to enjoy. The game shines in groups of 3-4 players where the dynamics are lively but not chaotic; with a pair you’ll still have plenty of synergy, but there’s a bit more pressure to cover gaps in the plan. The production quality stands out, with crisp card stock, good artwork, and a sturdy board that won’t buckle under the enthusiastic table slap you reserve for a truly good turn. The solo mode provides a surprisingly satisfying challenge that can stretch your mental muscles in a good way, while still remaining approachable for new players.

Pros

A note on variant rules
Mint shines when you agree to mix in a few optional rules. A few recommended variants:
- Market Shuffle: After every two turns, reshuffle the Market deck to ensure that no one gets a stale set of events.
- Role Roulette: At the start of the game, each player draws a temporary role card with a slightly different power; this creates unexpected synergy and fun chaos.
- Quick-Start: Remove a handful of lower-value Mint cards to speed up the game for very new players.
