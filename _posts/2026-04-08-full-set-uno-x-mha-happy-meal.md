## What is the UNO x Anime My Hero Academia collab all about?

At first glance, it’s a deceptively simple idea: slap My Hero Academia artwork on UNO cards and toss in ten Happy Meal figurines that double as collectible tokens for the UNO game. But there’s more. The set leans into the Plus Ultra energy—the show’s signature rallying cry—by giving each character a distinct UNO-themed ability motif. Think of it as a tiny, edible spark of cross-brand synergy: kid-friendly packaging, collectible chibi figures, and a UNO variant that supposedly rewards you for understanding who fights with the best quirk combos.

The packaging leans heavily into MHA color schemes—bright greens, fiery reds, cool blues—and includes a small blurb about each character’s hero trait. The design team clearly did their homework: you can tell Izuku’s card is all about resilience, Todoroki’s about balance, and Bakugo’s screams are, well, Bakugo. We’ll break down each figure below, but the big picture is this: McDonald’s wanted to offer something more than just a plastic toy and a few boomerang-shaped stickers. They wanted a mini crossover event that could ignite a friendly family competition in the car, the living room, or the unfortunate corner of a grocery store where the only other people with taste are the folks buying extra ketchup.

## The full set: meet the 10 heroes (and one villain they made friends with)

Below is a curated tour through each piece in the set. Each entry lists the character, the UNO-related flavor, and a quick verdict on collectibility and display. ### 1) Izuku Midoriya — Deku, the Green Ace

- Card motif: Green hero aura with a subtle Plus Ultra glow
- Quirk-inspired UNO ability: Resilience Mode — when active, you may redraw once as if you had a “deflect” card, representing Deku’s durability in battle.
- Build and display: The figure has a flexible pose, and the art on the card looks crisp under bright desk lamps. It’s a good starter piece for your shelf.
- Collectibility note: High, if you’re building a Deku shrine. Medium if you’re chasing all 10 and only want “epic” characters.

### 2) Katsuki Bakugo — The Explosive Blaster

## Design, packaging, and build quality: does it stand up to the hype?

Design-wise, the set leans into the modern anime aesthetic without losing the wholesome vibe that Happy Meal toys are known for. The figures are solid plastic with a glossy finish; they aren’t cheap but they aren’t alarmingly fragile either. If you’ve ever snapped a toy arm off a random action figure, you’ll appreciate that these have a surprisingly sturdy feel for their size. The collaboration art on the UNO cards strikes a great balance between being kid-friendly and collectible for older fans who aren’t afraid to admit their inner completionist.

Packaging is clean and friendly, with a clear window to show off the ten figures and two UNO card decks inside the Happy Meal box. The design team did well to avoid an overbearing hero-verse vibe; instead, they created a cohesive color palette that resembles the MHA hero lineup while still feeling like a McDonald’s product. It’s not trying to be a manga panel — it’s trying to be a family game night that your kids won’t mind playing with you, even if you’re sneaking in a quick strategy lecture about when to use a skip card.

From a display perspective, these items shine when arranged in a neat row. A top-down photo of the set on a coffee table makes for excellent social media material, especially since the characters align with the color-coded UNO suits (green for Deku, red for Bakugo, blue for Todoroki, etc.). The cards themselves carry decent readability, though if you’re planning to actually play the UNO variant with kids, you’ll want to separate the card artwork from the standard UNO deck to avoid confusion.

## How to play with the set: UNO meets heroics

The marketing team has done something interesting here: they proponed a lightweight variant of UNO that uses hero-themed actions as flavor text for special moves. While it isn’t a full-blown new UNO edition, it provides enough spice to justify a few test games on a lazy Saturday afternoon. The rule set is fairly approachable:

- Each player draws five cards from the standard UNO deck. The hero cards act as wild mood-setters that alter standard Uno outcomes.
- When you play a hero card, you invoke the associated ability. Some abilities trigger immediately (like Resilience Mode), while others modify the discard rules (like Gravity Shield).
- The Shigaraki card, as a villain-side piece, introduces a chaotic flip that can reshuffle the draw pile in a pinch.
- The game recommends a “Plus Ultra” rule variant where a mini-challenge is added for kids to earn extra turns. It’s silly, but it adds a nice family game-night energy.

Is this variant a serious competitor to your standard UNO? No. Is it a fun, flavorful supplement for a party game with a My Hero Academia vibe? Absolutely yes. It encourages players to discuss hero traits, strategize around the quirks, and, most importantly, have a lot of joy while munching on French fries—because, in the end, that’s what Happy Meal nights are really about.

## The shelf appeal: where does the set shine most?

Display is where this set really earns its stripes. The 10 figures line up nicely, with each character occupying a unique color space that minimizes overlap. The mix of hero and villain (Shigaraki) adds a dash of drama to the shelf, and the card artwork provides enough visual variety to keep your eyes entertained for long browsing sessions. If you’re building a dedicated MHA display, these cards complement other merch you might own: posters of the season arcs, figures from other toy lines, and even a stray prop from a cosplay stall.

If you’re unsure about how to stage your collection, a simple tip: group by color families (green, red, blue, purple, etc.). Then place the cards on an adjacent shelf or a coffee table with the UNO board so you can switch between display and gameplay quickly. It’s not NASA-grade organization, but it works.
