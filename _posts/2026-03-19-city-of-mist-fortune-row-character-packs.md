## Overview: what are Character Packs, really?

Fortune Row, the latest neighborhood-of-mythic-masquerade expansion, has a knack for turning a coffee shop into a crime scene and a single card into a city-wide conspiracy. The Character Packs are a series of mini-boxes designed to slide into your Fortune Row game with minimal setup. Each pack spotlights a specific character archetype—think: a street-talking cunning ghost, a tech-wizard with a glitchy AI sidekick, or a benevolent-turned-bizarre ally who can pivot between help and hijinks depending on the roll of a die and a moral compass that’s been through the wash.

What you get in each pack varies a little, but most packs include:
- A beautifully illustrated character card detailing the mask, backstory, and a few signature powers tailored to City of Mist’ s mythos framework.
- A character sheet with backstory hooks, social ties, and a few “Mythos”-adjacent moves that players can weave into scenes.
- A handful of background cards or “triple-taps” of flavor text that can be used to seed scenes, especially if you’re playing with a GM who loves a good noir monologue.
- If you’re lucky, a small map or token that makes tracking this character at the table a little less chaotic and a lot more cinematic.

The idea is simple and elegant: give players more masks, more routes to involvement in the city’s mysteries, and more ways to disrupt (or deepen) a scene. In practice, that translates into a few unequivocal strengths and a few design decisions you’ll want to consider before you drop the equivalent of a latte budget on a rainy Tuesday.


## What’s inside a Fortune Row Character Pack?

Each pack has a curated set of components designed to feel cohesive with the Fortune Row aesthetic: moody lighting, a noir poem on the back of the character card, and a crisp layout that jokes about the “hero’s journey” while still insisting that everyone signs a release form. The specific contents vary from pack to pack, but here’s a typical breakdown you can expect:

- Character Card: A single, sturdy card with the character’s mask, real name, a signature move, and a few quick-start prompts for GMs.
- Background & Hooks Sheet: A compact sheet that helps players slot the character into existing plots or provide a jump-off point for new scenes.
- Mythos Moves: A small number of tailored moves that play into the model of City of Mist where mythos isn’t just a costume; it’s a way of seeing the world.
- Tokens or Small Props: A dozen or so tokens that help track status, influence, or a “mythic advantage” in a single scene.
- Art Print or Postcard: A nod to the game’s aesthetic—edgy, stylish, and just a hair dangerous to frame in your apartment.

If you’re the kind of player who loves to organize a desk for an hour so your dice don’t roll off into a parallel dimension, you’ll appreciate the physical packaging and the care put into the tactile feel. If you’re the kind of player who exclusively uses digital tooling, you’ll still benefit from the clarity the card design brings to the table when you’re trying to figure out which mask has access to which mythic ability.


### Design and artwork: does it hold up?

Fortune Row has always leaned into a polished noir-tinged aesthetic with bold typography, moody photography, and a color palette that says, “we know you’re wearing a trench coat in your living room.” The Character Packs continue this tradition. The character art tends to lean into silhouetted figures, rain-slick streets, and eyes that look like they’re wallpapering over a heist. It’s not hyper-realistic, but it doesn’t pretend to be. It’s a visual language that says: your city is full of secrets, and every pack is a small key.

From a production standpoint, the packs are sturdy enough to withstand a dedicated table’s abuse. The cards are thick, the printing is crisp, and the finish on the art print is the kind of glossy that makes you want to frame it and pretend you’re a real game influencer for about five seconds. If you’re picky about iconography, you’ll notice a set of recurring motifs across packs that gives a sense of world-building continuity—very handy when you want to evoke a shared feel without turning every game night into a design seminar.


## How to integrate Character Packs into a Fortune Row game

If you’re familiar with City of Mist, you know the game’s flow: masks, myths, and a city where every street corner could be a mystery or a trap. Character Packs slot in as ready-to-play options that expand this dynamic at a table that’s already comfortable with improvisation. Here are a few practical ways to fold them into your session planning:

- Quick-start sessions: Use a character pack to generate two or three scene hooks and a single “major move” that the table can leverage in the first act. This is perfect for one-shot sessions or a short arc where you want momentum without a six-hour preamble.
- Long arcs: Choose two or three packs that complement each other’s masks or offer conflicting objectives. The irony is delicious when you realize two “ally” characters might both be pursuing the same outcome for different reasons, which invites organic character drama.
- Thematic threads: Because Fortune Row emphasizes a modern mythos, you can tie character moves to city-wide events (a gala gone wrong, a blackout in a specific district, a public figure with hidden loyalties). Build threads that run through multiple chapters, letting players reference each character’s hooks as the story evolves.
- GM references: The Background & Hooks Sheets are perfect for GMs who want quick prompts for scene framing. If you’re a GM who loves meticulous session notes, you’ll want to staple these to your notebook so you can glance at a page and say, “Right—this pack wants X to happen by act two.”

A note on balance: in City of Mist, the best plans are often the ones that don’t survive contact with reality—your players. These packs are most effective when you treat them as spice rather than the main course. Don’t force a character to fit a plot; adapt the pack’s hooks to a plot that your players actually care about. If a pack’s hook doesn’t resonate, you can swap it with a different character’s soil to cultivate custom drama rather than forcing a square peg into a round myth.


### Narrative hooks: sample micro-scenarios
To give you a sense of what a single character pack can spark, here are three micro-scenarios you can drop into a Fortune Row game:

- The Street-Side Oracle: A pack that centers on a character who can glimpse possible futures in the reflections of shop windows. The hook invites players to chase a singular vision of a heist that may never happen—unless the mythic future is altered by a choice in the present. Great for a tense urban chase scene with a strong moral dimension.
- The Glass-Soft Hacker: A tech-savvy mask whose past in a non-profit organization leaks into the present as an underground data-sculptor. This is ideal for a data-heist session set in a museum or tech startup, where every digit can be a doorway and every voicemail is a trap.
- The Velvet-Contractor: An ally whose ethics are ambiguous but the payoff is undeniably sweet. This hook is perfect for a political intrigue arc where you want a “friend” whose loyalty shifts with the moon—and with the roll of a die. It’s dramatic deliciousness if your group loves negotiating with the mythos itself.


## The mechanics angle: how these packs interact with City of Mist rules

City of Mist uses a dual-mask system: the real-world identity and the mythic persona. The character packs respect that symmetry by giving you concrete masks and mythic hooks that feel integrated, not tacked on. A few notes on mechanics and setup:
- Moves are character-driven: Each pack’s moves are designed to be invoked by narrative beats rather than tedious rule checks. Expect prompts that encourage your players to emphasize their character’s unique angle on a scene.
- Thresholds and beat structure: The packs typically align with the game’s beat structure—investigation, escalation, and confrontation—so you won’t be resetting the clock on your session by introducing the pack mid-scene.
- Mythos synergy: If your table loves cross-cutting alliances or rival factions, these packs provide your player characters with direct ties to those groups, creating symphonic tension rather than random, one-off encounters.
- Balancing for groups: A cautionary note—some packs may feel powerful in the hands of a player who pulls off a clever stunt. Balance in practice means you’ll need to watch for a single character’s dominance early on and adjust scene emphasis if needed.


## Artwork, components, and value for money

From a pure production standpoint, Fortune Row Character Packs deliver a premium feel without the premium price tag you’d expect from a niche product line. The cards are sturdy, the art is striking, and the additional sheets are clean to read and quick to deploy. The token art is legible at a glance, which is more than you can say for some other RPG expansions where tokens look like abstract pollution rather than helpful cues.

Value-wise, you’re paying for hooks, aesthetics, and a modular way to inject new life into an ongoing campaign. If you’re a tuck-or-dundle gamer who loves a well-curated table vibe, you’ll see the value in the presentation alone. For players who prefer digital tools or who already have an abundance of printed content, you’ll need to weigh the tactile joy against your budget and gaming style. A well-considered purchase is one where the packs unlock scenes you’d otherwise skip and give your group new angles to explore the city.


## Strengths, weaknesses, and who should buy this

Strengths:
- Strong thematic cohesion with Fortune Row’s noir-mythic vibe.
- Quick-start hooks that save GM prep time and accelerate session flow.
- High production values: art, layout, and physical durability.
- Flexible enough to slot into both one-shots and longer arcs.

Weaknesses:
- Some packs lean into familiar archetypes; if your table wants radically new masks, you may want to mix in other sources.
- The value proposition hinges on playtime: if you’re gaming sporadically, the packs may sit on a shelf gathering mood-lit dust.
- For groups that primarily play digitally, the physical components offer less incremental utility than a purely virtual toolkit.

Who should buy:
- Groups that love rich noir atmosphere and character-driven scenes.
- GMs who want ready-to-run anchors to kick off sessions without hours of prep.
- Players who crave a bit more personality in their Masks-and-Mythos toolkit.
- Collectors who appreciate the craft of RPG packaging and the joy of opening a new character to read aloud in a dramatic whisper.

Who should skip:
- Players who primarily use pure digital resources or who don’t want to manage more physical components.
- Groups that already own similar archetypes across other Fortune Row packs and don’t want repeats.


## A quick comparison: Fortune Row packs vs. base game and other expansions

Compared to the core City of Mist experience, Fortune Row’s Character Packs are less about changing the rules and more about enriching the storytelling surface. They don’t reinvent the wheel; they give you better wheels, fancier hubcaps, and a spare tire that’s also a small, stylish weapon of narrative persuasion. If you enjoyed the base game’s flexibility and you’re hungry for more masks and more relationships, these packs deliver a focused, thematically cohesive upgrade.

When stacked against other Fortune Row expansions, these packs excel in consistency. You’ll notice a deliberate approach to how each character integrates into the city’s power structures, rather than a hodgepodge of one-off adds. This helps keep a long campaign feeling coherent rather than episodic. If you’re a GM who values continuity and a group that thrives on long-form arcs, you’ll likely appreciate the design decisions here.

## Final verdict and recommendation

If you’re already invested in Fortune Row and you’re craving new masks that feel like “the city talking back to you,” these Character Packs are worth picking up. They deliver on the core promise of the line: more masks, more personal stakes, and more dramatic potential with minimal prep. The mechanical footprint is light enough not to overwhelm new players but substantial enough to spark meaningful roleplay for veteran crews. The packs don’t pretend to be a complete game in a box; instead, they act as a curated extension to the story you’re already telling in your campaign. If you want a quick lift that adds texture to your city without forcing you into a new system or a heavy rule set, Fortune Row Character Packs are a smart buy.

That said, they’re best enjoyed with a group that can lean into the drama and allow the new masks to drive scenes rather than serve as a “one more thing” obstacle. If your table thrives on improvisation and character-driven plots, you’ll likely end up using these more than you thought you would. If, conversely, your group leans toward crunch-heavy mechanics and you prefer campaigns with tight, pre-defined beats, you might use one or two packs and treat the rest as optional flavor.


## Final recommendation score: 4.5 out of 5 masks

A strong addition to Fortune Row, with gorgeous art, thoughtful hooks, and play-ready content that respects the game’s tone. It’s not a replacement for good GMing, but it is an excellent toolbox for creating scenes that feel like a movie you’re all watching together—and then quietly rewriting as you go.


## Concluding thoughts: the city calls, and the masks answer

If you’ve ever wished your City of Mist table could lean a little more into the noir, a little more into the myth, and a little more into the character drama that makes you stay up late arguing about who the villain actually is, Fortune Row’s Character Packs deliver. They’re not a magic bullet that will fix every campaign problem, but they’re a reliable source of inspiration that can spark a session that lands with a satisfying thud of noir-y truth.

So, should you buy them? If you want richer backstories, cleaner scene hooks, and a touch more cinematic flair, yes. If you’re on a shoestring budget or your table prefers purely digital tools, you might skip for now and instead wait for a sale or a bundle that includes multiple packs. Either way, the city is waiting, the masks are sharpening their lines, and Fortune Row’s character packs just invited themselves to the party—whether you asked them or not. Accept the invitation, and let the streetlights illuminate the drama you’re about to spin.
