## Keyboard Feel: Touch, Feel, and Virtual Weightlessness
The keyboard has 88 keys—fully standard in range, though not full-size graded hammer action. It’s a modern digital piano action with lighter touch than a stage-weighted keyboard, which is ideal for quick practice lines, scales, and the occasional arcade synth line. The keys respond predictably to velocity, so you’ll get quiet expressions for softer passages and a snappy response when you hit a fortissimo chord (or at least a convincing one-handed cluster while pretending you’re in a late-night synthpop jam).

Polyphony on paper is generous for a foldable toy-like instrument: enough to handle most practice sessions, some complex arpeggios, and a casual pad layer when you want to fake a soundstorm on the hotel room desk. In real-world use, you won’t max out polyphony during a routine, but if you try to stack multiple layers with heavy sustain and long decays, you’ll eventually reach the limit. The takeaway: don’t expect a full-blown studio rig; expect a friendly portable piano that plays well with minor layering and live practice.
