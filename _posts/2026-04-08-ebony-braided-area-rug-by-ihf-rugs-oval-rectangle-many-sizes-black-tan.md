### How to Pick the Right Size
Rule of thumb: leave about 18 inches to 24 inches of bare floor around the rug in living spaces. That spacing helps define the seating area and prevents the rug from feeling like a floating island. In dining rooms, ensure the rug can accommodate the table with chairs pulled out; a rectangle rug that’s too small can make the room feel chopped. Oval shapes work beautifully under curved coffee tables or in rooms with soft, flowing lines; rectangles better anchor sofas and media walls.

## Durability, Cleaning, and Care
### Everyday Wear and Tear
Braided rugs are known for resilience. The tight braid construction distributes stress across the fibers, which helps in high-traffic zones like living rooms and entryways. Expect excellent foot traffic tolerance, but also be mindful that darker colors can show dust less prominently than lighter hues; regular vacuuming is still essential to keep fibers from compressing and to maintain the rug’s loft.

### Cleaning Basics
- Vacuum regularly with a standard beater bar on a low setting. Overly aggressive agitation can fray loose fibers at the braid seams.
- For spills, blot immediately with a clean cloth. If a stain sets, a mild mixture of water and a gentle detergent can help; test in an inconspicuous corner first.
- Avoid saturating the rug; braided rugs are not usually meant for soaking. Excess moisture can lead to backing loosening or color bleeding.
- Rotate every few months to promote even wear and fading patterns.

### Stain-Resistant Claims and Reality
While a black base helps hide minor stains, no rug is truly stain-proof. Spills from red wine, coffee, or pet accidents require prompt action. Consider using a stain treatment compatible with synthetic fibers, and always test a small patch before going full castle-defense on your floor. If you have kids or pets, be prepared for giggles, messes, and occasional mystery stains that only a laundry machine can solve later.

### Rug Pads and Underlay
A quality rug pad is a wise investment here. It reduces slipping on hard floors, adds a touch of cushioning, and can extend the rug’s life by absorbing some of the friction from foot traffic. For wooden or laminate floors, a felt or natural rubber pad is a solid choice. For carpeted floors, a thinner pad helps prevent bunching. The pad also makes shoving the coffee table across the rug a tad less dramatic when you re-arrange furniture for a party playlist.
