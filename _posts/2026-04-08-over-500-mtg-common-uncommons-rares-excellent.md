## What makes a card excellent+?
Excellent+ isn’t a single stat or a single mechanic. It’s a blend of reliability, versatility, and the potential to scale across formats. Here’s the mental model we use when scanning 500+ cards and deciding who gets the badge:

- Efficacy for the mana curve: a common or uncommon that remains useful on turns 1 through 5 or helps you accelerate into your midgame while still bringing value later.
- Synergy with archetypes: does the card play nicely with popular combos or standard tribal themes without requiring a dozen other specific pieces?
- Format flexibility: can this card shine in Commander, Modern, or Cube, or does it stay that much more valuable in a casual kitchen-table setting?
- Longevity: will this card still feel relevant in a year or two, or does it become a nostalgia pick that’s fun but fragile?
- Safety of color and mana base: does the card help fix mana or smooth your color identities, or does it demand a fragile mana base that breaks when you’re trying to run three colors?

In a sea of rares that can feel like glitter, the excellent+ cards are the ones you’ll reach for again and again. They’re the kinds of cards that become core pieces of your grindy midrange decks, or your go-to build-around cards in Commander. They aren’t a guarantee of victory, but they’re a sturdy bet that you’ll get a lot of value from in many different settings.

## Methodology: how we measured excellence+
To keep this review practical, we used a simple, repeatable checklist that could be applied to a large pool of cards without getting lost in the weeds. Here’s what we looked for:

- Playability across formats: does the card work in more than one format, or is it situational to a single niche?
- Efficiency: how many relevant effects does the card provide for its mana cost? Is it a one-trick pony or a reliable multi-tool?
- Redundancy: does this card fill a role that’s already covered by multiple other cards in the pool, or does it provide a unique angle?
- Resilience: is the card robust to disruption (counterspells, removal, and artifact removal), or is it an issue when the board goes sideways?
- Revisions and reprints: we favor cards that have seen multiple printings, as these tend to retain value and broad usability across sets and formats.

This approach doesn’t pretend to be a universal law. MTG is a living game; format rules shift, new mechanics emerge, and card text changes can redefine a card’s place in a deck. Still, the methodology offers a practical framework for evaluating a massive pool of cards without getting lost in every last edge case.

## Common gems: the underappreciated workhorses
Common cards are the bread-and-butter of most budget decks, and a well-chosen handful can tilt games in meaningful ways. In this collection, a handful of common picks rise to the top by delivering reliable value across several archetypes. Here are the kinds of common picks that earned the excellent+ badge this time around:

### Common picks that accelerate and stabilize
- Fast ramp that doesn’t over-commit the mana base: one-drop accelerants that fix two colors or more while leaving you flexible later in the game.
- Efficient early blockers and career-long evasive threats: early bodies that survive long enough to enable your plan while pressuring the opponent.

### Common picks for card advantage and filtering
- Cards that draw or filter for two or three cards across the game and don’t crash your curve if drawn late.
- Effects that replace themselves or draw with a discount when you have a plan in motion.

### Common picks with resilient value
- Cards that survive removal or that provide friction against opposing plans, helping you stretch your resources across turns.

In practice, these commons don’t just fill a seat at the table—they steady the ship and let your midgame plan come online reliably. They’re the quiet workhorses that, when paired with the right Uncommons and Rares, become the backbone of a durable strategy.

## Uncommons that hold their own in every format
Uncommons are where the jolt of power often lives. They’re not splashy mythics, but they’re the cards you splash into a deck because they add a level of consistency, flexibility, or raw tempo that changes a lane into a highway. Here are the uncommons that stood out in the 500+ card review:

### Uncommons that redefine the tempo
- Cards that alter the course of a single turn or two with a clean, efficient effect and low mana cost.
- Effects that turn a block into a sequence of favorable exchanges, letting you push through damage or disrupt opponents more effectively.

### Uncommons with deep synergy potential
- Interactions that weave nicely with a wide range of archetypes, creating cross-deck compatibility.
- Cards that enable offbeat combos or empower smaller creatures to punch above their weight class.

### Uncommons for late-game inevitability
- Persisting card draw, recursion, or repeatable effects that keep your engines alive when you’re down on tempo.

These high-value uncommons often form the backbone of hybrid strategies and are the glue that holds oddball combos together. They make it possible to implement a plan without needing a heroic card draw every game.

### Rares that enable aggressive and midrange plans

A well-chosen Rare is not just a big threat; it is a versatile tool that improves the quality of almost any deck in your collection. In a 500+ card pool, rares that check multiple boxes stand out as the obvious anchors for both casual and competitive play.

## Deck-building implications: using 500+ cards wisely
If you’re building a cube, a budget Commander collection, or just card-slinging for fun, this pool provides a few practical principles:

- Don’t chase power in isolation: ensure every card has a purpose in your current deck or a clear path to a future one. If a card sits in your pool unused for months, it’s probably not excellent+ material for you.
- Look for cross-format relevance: cards that work in multiple formats save you money and draft time at the same time. These are the true keepers.
- Prioritize consistency first, power second: you’d rather a reliable two-for-one card than a flashy single-game finisher that loses more often than not when your mana is off.
- Culture your mana base alongside your cards: a deck’s true efficiency is often bound to how smoothly it can deploy cards on curve. Fixing mana improves every other card in your pool.

In other words, the excellent+ pool isn’t about stacking the biggest spells. It’s about stacking the most reliable spells that scale well, play nicely with others, and keep your deck from becoming a hot mess of mismatched ambitions.

## Practical picks and play-testing tips
As you start to experiment with your 500+ pool, here are a few practical exercises you can run to get the most out of your collection:

- Build three 60-card decks from different color combos and test how many Common and Uncommon cards you end up using in each. If you’re drafting from a 500+ pool, you’ll want consistent early to midgame plays that scale.
- Run a mini-pilot with your top Rare picks and compare how often they carry the game vs when you rely on the pack of uncommons. If your Rare is consistently underperforming relative to your Uncommons, you may be overvaluing big splashy effects.
- Consider a cube approach: pick a core of around 120 cards that you think are reliable. Then add in 80 to 100 highly-flexible cards that can slot into many lists. The rest can be reserved for theme drafts, if that’s your vibe.
