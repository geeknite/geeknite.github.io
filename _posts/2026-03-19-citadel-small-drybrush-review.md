### What is a drybrush, and why does size matter?

Drybrushing is painting's version of whispering; you apply a tiny amount of paint to a brush, remove most of it, and then drag the brush over raised surfaces to catch highlights. The result is a grit-free, textured edge that shouts “relic armor” rather than “mud Pie.” The size refers to the brush's ferrule-to-handle length and the bristle width; a smaller brush gives you greater precision on tight corners, chisel-edged details on armor plates, and chips in the paint without turning your model into a lesson on abstract expressionism.

Citadel's Small Drybrush is designed for control. If you have the steady hand of a caffeinated sloth, this brush will help you keep those gritty highlights away from the face of your miniature. The bristles are stiff enough to drag across ridges without curling into the troughs, yet forgiving enough to blend on metal and stone. In short: it’s a scalpel in a kitchen knife’s sheath—a tool you can rely on for tiny, careful touches.

### Drybrushing in practice: a step-by-step guide

Here’s how to use the Citadel Small Drybrush for reliable results, even if you’re not Jack Sparrow with a paint palette:

1) Prep your palette. Squeeze a tiny amount of paint onto your palette. Don’t go crazy; this is a drybrush, not a paint cannon. You want a chalky concentration that can cling to raised textures. If you’re painting a chainmail, gold trim, or leather highlights, choose a shade slightly lighter than your base coat.
2) Load lightly, then wipe away. Dab the brush on the lid and wipe most of the paint off on a scrap tissue or paper towel. You want the bristles almost dry. This is the essence of “dry” in drybrushing.
3) Find a raised edge. Lightly touch the brush to the edge of the raised features—think ridges of armor, edges of a cloak, or the edge of a rock formation. Use quick, short strokes that skim across the surface, not long dragging lines that smear everything under the brush’s weight.
4) Build texture slowly. You can repeat the process several times, but gradually. It’s easier to layer highlights than to erase mistakes. If the highlight is too bright, wash over with a slightly darker shade to mute it back down.
5) Inspect and adjust. Step back twenty centimeters and look for flat surfaces that have picked up too much light. If you see bright patches where you don’t want them, gently dull the highlight with a clean, dry brush and brush away the excess.

This is where the Small Drybrush earns its keep: tiny, precise increments of highlight that won’t bleed into neighboring details. The trick is to treat it like a laser pointer on a tiny stage, not as a sledgehammer on a wall of texture.

### Keeping it clean: brush care that actually works

A lot of drybrush consternation comes from two places: dried paint on the bristles and bristles losing their stiffness. Here’s the Geeknite-approved routine:
- Immediately between colors: wipe, wipe, wipe. When you switch colors, wipe the brush clean on the towel.
- After you’re done: rinse under lukewarm water and gently use a soft brush to remove paint from the ferrule-area. Avoid hot water; it can damage bristles.
- Soap matters: gentle brush soap or a dedicated brush cleaner is your friend. Work the bristles in the palm of your hand to release trapped pigment, then rinse again.
- Dry properly: lay the brush flat to dry or hang bristle-downwards. A damp brush near a heat source is a recipe for kinked bristles.

If the bristles start splaying, you may have used too much pressure, or you’ve heated them with a fast-drying solvent. Cue the minor panic, then remember: patience and a little soap go a long way. The Small Drybrush is robust, but it isn’t invincible. Treat it well and it will still be with you at the end of a long campaign.

### How it stacks up against its siblings: a quick size comparison

- Small Drybrush: precision, control, perfect for tight corners, edges, and subtle highlights.
- Regular Drybrush: a bit bulkier, useful for broader texture coverage, mid-range texturing across terrain and larger surfaces.
- Large Drybrush: for fast coverage on terrain and non-detailed surfaces, not ideal for tiny models with crisp edges.

The key is to know when to pick which. The Citadel Small Drybrush is your go-to when you want to avoid smearing your hard-won details. It’s not for shading; that’s a different technique altogether. Think of it as the difference between whispering and shouting—both convey information, but in our hobby, silence can be more elegant.

### Maintenance and longevity: how long will this last, and how to keep it happy?

Given proper care, the Citadel Small Drybrush will outlive several models. Here’s the upkeep plan:
- Store it with bristles clean and dry. Moisture retention lets bacteria thrive and bristles go soft.
- Avoid leaving it soaking in water. Water under the ferrule is not your friend.
- Periodically condition the bristles with a light going-over using brush soap, then rinse and dry.
- If you notice bristles starting to split or bend permanently, replace the brush. Drybrushing on a hairline fracture will only ruin future projects and create a brush-split end to your painting career.
