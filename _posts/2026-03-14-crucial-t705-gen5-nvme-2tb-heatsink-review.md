## Installation and Compatibility: a quick practical guide
Installing the T705 is a straightforward affair for most builders who have installed NVMe drives before. The integrated heatsink simplifies things for a clean, all-in-one approach, but you’ll want to keep a few caveats in mind:

- Check your motherboard’s M.2 slot clearance. If you’re packing a dual-slot GPU and a chunky CPU cooler in a compact case, the heatsink might block adjacent slots or cables. Measure before you buy.
- Confirm your BIOS supports Gen5 NVMe. Some older motherboards can bottleneck Gen5 speeds by default unless you enable specific settings. A quick BIOS update and a glance at the NVMe settings is worth the effort.
- Cooling airflow matters. In a well-ventilated case, the heatsink does its job with minimal fan noise. In a cramped chassis with poor airflow, you’ll want to add a fan or two to the case—because nothing ruins a vibe like a warm glass of soda in the same chassis as your SSD.
- Firmware updates matter. Crucial ships firmware updates that optimize performance and fix potential compatibility quirks. Don’t skip these; they’re the social etiquette of SSD ownership—necessary and usually beneficial.

To install, simply insert the card into an M.2 slot, secure with a screw, and boot. If you’re mounting both a GPU and a motherboard that uses a large PCIe slot blocker, consider a different slot arrangement or a low-profile heatsink alternative. It’s not rocket science, but it does require a mindful approach to space management in your case.

## Endurance, Reliability, and the TLC Question
TLC NAND is the energy drink of the SSD world: it provides great capacity and speed at a lower cost but can wear a bit faster than SLC or MLC counterparts. The T705 uses wear leveling and error correction to maximize longevity, and the integrated heatsink helps keep performance stable under load, which can be beneficial to endurance in sustained workloads.

When evaluating TLC NAND drives, you’ll see TBW (terabytes written) or MTBF (mean time between failures) numbers. These are useful guidelines, but you should also consider your own workloads. If you write terabytes of data every day for years on end, you’ll want to factor in the TBW and your backup strategy. If you’re a light user, you’ll probably never reach the endurance ceiling, and the purchase will feel like a fast, future-proofed upgrade that’s more about peace of mind than a race to the bottom on price.
