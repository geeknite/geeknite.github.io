This is Expansion 2, so if you’ve already got Expansion 1, you’ll know what you’re in for: two new survivors, some flavor cards, and a fresh dose of Miguel Coimbra’s art directing the apocalypse with swagger.

## Box contents and presentation

The box arrives sealed, which is a blessing and a curse. Blessing because sealed product usually means you’re not paying for someone else’s lunchtime; curse because CMON often packs the content to the rafters with tiny blister packs and a little more suspense than a horror film trailer. Inside, you’ll find:

- Two new survivor miniatures, styled with Coimbra’s signature line work and a dash of cinematic grit.
- A set of two survivor character cards, each with unique abilities and flavor text that looks great on a sleeve and might make you choose a different tactic mid-scenario.
- A handful of gear and item cards designed to fit the two survivors’ playstyles. These are the lifeblood of the expansion, giving you new ways to hack your way through the undead chaos.
- A small pile of tokens and reference cards that integrate with the core game’s rules. If you’ve got the standard Zombicide setup, these slot into the existing workflow without forcing a home improvement project on your dining room table.
- A cinematic art sheet and, of course, the official product description that nods to Miguel Coimbra’s style as if to say, “Yes, you’ll want these on your shelf next to your hero posters.”

If you’ve opened any of the other Special Guest packs, you’ll recognize the rhythm here: compact, thematic, and a little less shy about leaning into the collector vibe. The quality of the miniatures is typically of CMON’s standard: sturdy, with clean mold lines that clean up nicely under a little hobby glue and a pot of patience.

The components feel designed for both hot-seat players who want to swap quick strategies and long-haul fans who intend to weave this into a campaign arc. And yes, the plastic is crisp enough to feel premium without being a luxury tax on your gaming budget.

## The two survivors: design, flavor, and playstyle

Expansion 2 brings two new survivors who are a perfect fit for Coimbra’s aesthetic and the mood of Zombicide. Here’s the gist of what you’ll experience with each:

### Survivor A: The Artist in the Ruins
This character feels like you’ve handed your turn-key hero a canvas and a paintbrush soaked in adrenaline. The playstyle skews toward creative mitigation of danger; you’re rewarded for clever positioning, card synergy, and making the most out of a single action by turning it into multiple small advantages. The entire identity of this survivor is about turning a tight space into a kill-box with style. If you’ve ever looked at a room full of zombies and thought, “If I angle right, I can chain two or three kills in one move,” this survivor is basically built for that line of play.

Mechanically, expect a nod toward action economy: one of the unique abilities encourages you to reuse an action or to squeeze extra value out of your turn when you pull off the right sequence. It’s satisfying, but not game-breaking. The risk, of course, is that you’ll overextend to set up a big play, only to realize you left yourself exposed for the next wave. It’s a classic painter’s trap: glamorous on the surface, precarious in execution—but that balance is part of the fun.

### Survivor B: The Neon Scout
The second survivor carries a different tempo: a faster, more forward-thinking style that rewards aggression and map control. Think “flash of movement, then control the zone.” This character is the one you pick when you want to push forward, claim a corner, and press the zombie horde into a dice-rolling trap of your own design. Their gear and cards tend to push you toward tighter turns and better use of line of sight to maximize the effectiveness of every shot.

Together, Survivor A and Survivor B offer complementary approaches: one excels at turning a clever placement into a multi-zombie takedown, the other thrives on speed and territory denial. It’s a yin-yang pairing that encourages players to coordinate, talk through turns aloud, and occasionally give your teammate a gentle nudge when their plan sounds like a bad scifi plot twist.

If you’re curious about how their mechanics actually look on the table, you can skip ahead to the official cards and see the exact text on the expansion deck. But the spirit, the tempo, and the mood are what you’re paying for—and Coimbra nails that.

## Mechanisms, balance, and how it plays with the base game

Zombicide’s core mechanics are pretty sturdy: you move, you shoot or attack, you spawn zombies, and you try not to flip the table in frustration. This expansion doesn’t reinvent the wheel; instead, it adds two new spokes that help the wheel feel a little more stylish while not destabilizing the ride.

### Abilities and synergy
The two survivors are designed to reward a mix of careful planning and bold execution. Their abilities encourage you to think about the ladder of actions—what you do now, what you do next turn, and whether you can trigger a combo with gear cards you’ve already drawn. The art touches matter too; it’s easier to get into a rhythm when you’re staring at a character who looks like they could solve your neighbor’s HOA problem and also fend off a zombie apocalypse with a paintbrush and a can-do grin.

In practice, the synergy with base game cards is solid but not overpowering. You won’t suddenly steamroll every scenario with this expansion alone, but you’ll find new, satisfying ways to chain turns and set up “zona control” moments that feel cinematic rather than spreadsheet-y. That balance is crucial in a cooperative game where you want to feel clever without feeling forced into a single “optimal” path.

### Difficulty and pacing
A lot of Zombicide expansions flirt with the idea of “more is better” and sometimes swing toward inflated board state complexity. Expansion 2 keeps a steady pace. It gives you more tools, more flavor, and more potential for clutch plays, without turning every scenario into a brain-burner. It’s approachable for new players who have the base game down and still offers enough nuance for veterans who want a fresh object lesson in tabletop strategy.

If you’re into campaign play, these new survivors play nicely with the campaign rules in the base game and expansions. They provide hooks for narrative moments and theatrical finales without corrupting the core XP progression or the balance of the enemy deck. You’ll still want to keep the handy action-queue card on hand, just to remind yourself which order your new abilities go in when you’re juggling two boards and a coffee cup that’s getting dangerously close to tipping over.

## Art, components, and presentation

Miguel Coimbra’s design language comes through loud and clear. The artwork is moody but readable, a rare combination for a game that requires rapid decision-making during chaotic rounds. The survivors’ silhouette styling and the color choices are expressive, making it easy to tell at a glance who’s who on the board, even if you’re wearing your “I only speak dice” face.

The miniatures themselves are solid. They stand up to the usual tabletop abuse—no wobbly bases, no bent plastic, nothing that makes you want to swap them out for metal because you’re dramatic about your hobby. The cards are sturdy and legible, with clear iconography so you don’t have to pause mid-turn to re-interpret a rule that’s longer than your last text message. The artwork on the cards themselves is a small “gallery tour” of Coimbra’s aesthetic—dark, slightly angular lines, neon lighting accents, and that sense of a story about to erupt from a panel.

If you enjoy the packaging as a visual treat, you’ll appreciate how the box art ties in with the other Special Guest releases. It’s a cohesive line that looks right on a display shelf, which, let’s be honest, is half the battle when you’re trying to justify buying more plastic people with faces painted on them.

The unboxing experience is satisfying but not a wild ride. If you’re a pragmatic player who wants to play right out of the box, you’ll find the contents enough to deliver a handful of memorable sessions without needing to raid your card binder for extra sleeves and tokens.

## How it fits with the Special Guest line

The Miguel Coimbra line is designed to be a little bit of a storyteller’s dream in a tactical hobby. It gives you two primary benefits: aesthetic immersion and a distinct playstyle that stands apart from the base game. Expansion 2 continues that trend, offering a new pair of survivors with a different rhythm from Expansion 1. Together, they create a mini-archetype of “artist as survivor” and “neon scout as survivor” that pairs nicely with a variety of scenario types.

If you’re collecting the whole line, Expansion 2 is almost a no-brainer. It helps you build out a more diverse pool of survivor options, which in turn lets you rotate your party dynamics more freely and stage more varied story beats across your sessions. It also provides a touchpoint for discussing Miguel Coimbra’s portfolio with your gaming group—if you’re the kind of person who loves inspecting art style while you roll dice, this is a satisfyingly thematically cohesive add-on.

## How it compares to other expansions

Compared to other CMON releases in the Zombicide ecosystem, this expansion sits in a comfortable mid-range for value and complexity. It adds two survivors and some cards, which is not massive in terms of new rules, but it’s heavy on flavor. The difference between Expansion 1 and Expansion 2 is stylistic rather than mechanical; both aim to provide fresh objectives and new storytelling hooks without destabilizing the core game’s balance.

If you’re deciding between this expansion and a different line or a different game altogether, ask yourself: do you want more narrative-driven survivors with a distinct art vibe, or do you want more tactical depth and a higher swing in combat outcomes? Expansion 2 leans into the mood and role-playing flavor while still keeping the action crunchy enough to feel like a Zombicide session rather than a calendar-planning exercise.

## Is it worth it? Final thoughts and recommendations

Short answer: yes, for the right players. If you already love the base game and the Special Guest aura, Expansion 2 is a solid add that enhances the experience without overshadowing the core rules. It’s also a good entry point for players curious about Miguel Coimbra’s artwork but wary of stepping into a full campaign upgrade. The two survivors offer different playstyles that encourage cooperation and tactical discussion, which is precisely what you want in a cooperative game.

If you’re more of a pure tactics person who wants a hard rulebook with numeric tables to memorize, this expansion will feel a little lighter on that front. But if you crave theme, artful character design, and a fresh narrative beat to your sessions, Expansion 2 delivers in spades. It’s not a jaw-dropping leap forward for the system, but it’s a meaningful, well-produced addition that respects the source material while giving you something new to show off at the table.
