## What the Extreme Outdoor Edition changes

This edition is not a complete rewrite of the core game; it’s more like a costume change that still keeps the same heart beating. The Extreme Outdoor Edition adds a few outdoor-specific twists that make you adjust your expectations (and your throwing arc):

- Wind and distance dynamics: The wind card is no joke. It adds a factor of real-world physics into the laugh track. A gentle breeze can turn a near-perfect throw into a sparkly disaster as the burrito sails toward the neighbor’s yard or bounces off a garden gnome. The far more dramatic gusts that sometimes appear in the middle of a July picnic become a cooperative hazard that you learn to embrace with a grin.

- Terrain-aware rules: The lawn is a stage and sometimes the stage is a hazard. The edition includes cards that reference grass blades, uneven pavement, and that one sprinkler you never notice until your burrito makes a heroic arc toward the mist.

- Weather check: A light, comical card that asks whether the players are okay with continuing given the current conditions. This is less about weather forecasting and more about acknowledging that you’re playing a physical game outdoors in a real world with real wind and real sunburn potential.

The core idea remains intact: you draw and match sets by throwing burritos, but the outdoor version injects a splash of real-world chaos to generate laughter rather than friction. It’s the same game at heart, just with more excuses to declare science when a burrito curve ends up in your hat.

## How to play outdoors

The mechanics remain fairly approachable. You’ll draw a hand of cards and attempt to complete sets by throwing burritos at other players to force them to take those sets; the action is interrupted by the occasional burrito tossed moment where you have to quickly dodge and react. The outdoor edition adds a zero-to-sixty vibe, where players are encouraged to drive the energy by shouting, laughing, and improvising around the burrito’s arc.

Setting up your outdoor game is straightforward:

- Choose a safe, open space with a soft ground surface. Grass is ideal; if you’re on concrete, bring a mat or a picnic blanket to cushion the inevitable tumble.

- Establish boundaries so you don’t accidentally wander into the street or the neighbor’s pool. The game loves a dramatic finish, but not at the expense of your HOA or your dignity.

- Assign a “ref” or a cooperative rulebook reader so everyone can stay on track with the rules while you’re chasing a wobbling burrito.

The gameplay loop is still about memory and speed, but now you’re also juggling wind gusts, sun glare, and the occasional adjacency to a sprinkler. The humor comes from the sheer chaos of trying to form a set while avoiding a burrito that’s been carried off by the breeze. It’s fast, it’s loud, and if you’re doing it right, it’s a little chaotic in a very charming way.

## Practical tips and setup checklist

- Make sure you have a soft surface. The outdoors can be a blast, but you don’t want burritos bouncing off hard surfaces and creating a hazard.

- Declutter the play area. Objects, pets, and plants can all become unintended targets for burrito throws; you’ll want to minimize accidental chaos.

- Hydration is important. You’ll laugh, you’ll shout, you’ll probably forget to drink water; keep a bottle handy to avoid the heat.

- Create a light burrito safety rule so everyone understands how to throw kindly and avoid injuring wrists or eyes.
