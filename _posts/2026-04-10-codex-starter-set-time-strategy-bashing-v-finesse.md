## Unboxing: what’s inside a starter set that hasn’t seen sunlit surface in years

Let’s start with the obvious. An opened-but-never-played Codex Starter Set is, in some cosmic sense, a guarantee of two things: pristine card borders and opinions formed through rumor and YouTube thumbnails rather than actual gameplay. The box includes a curated pool of cards designed to teach players the three archetypes. In practice, the three archetypes are often summarized as follows:

- Time Strategy: The control and tempo game, where your goal is to outlast your opponent, counting the ticks until you can finally drop the ultimate “awkward victory” moment.
- Bashing: Aggro, direct pressure, the “hit you in the face with a card and then ask for forgiveness later” approach.
- Finesse: The trickery archetype—bluffs, sneaky combos, and deck thinning that makes your opponent question the laws of probability and your life choices.

In a box that’s been opened but not played, you’ll find:

- A compact deck collection with a few dozen unique cards—enough to form some rudimentary idea of archetypes, but not enough to establish a tight, reliable strategy without a lot of house-rule flavor.
- A rather nice mini-rulebook that looks like it was translated by a very enthusiastic but caffeinated robot.
- A handful of dice, tokens, or counters that say, in their own understated way, “We could be useful, but you’ll overthink us anyway.”
- A player aid that reads like a cross between a who-dunnit and a flowchart for a parking ticket.

The physicality is not the issue here. ## Time Strategy: counting the seconds until card draw glory

Time Strategy in Codex is pitched as the “long game,” a concept that feels both grand and terrifying when your opponents look your way with that practiced poker face you’ve seen on internet memes. Time Strategy is not simply chasing the late game; it’s about crafting a tempo where each turn feels inevitable, like watching a well-edited montage where your own hero is the protagonist and the villain is your own impatience.

In a box that has never been played, Time Strategy cards exist as potential. Theoretical synergies would include: card draw engines that ramp your inevitability, early control options that don’t derail your own plan, and win conditions that require you to say, with exactitude, “Now is the moment.” The irony of an unopened starter kit is that this supposed clockwork of tempo has almost no chance to function without actual playtesting.

What you can reasonably expect from Time Strategy components in Codex starter form:

- A couple of draw or filter cards that promise you a clean top-deck in your long-term plan. In practice, your top-deck might be a blank card that looks exciting under a desk lamp.
- A few tempo screws that you can tighten in actual play, but inside an unopened box you’re mostly reading about potential synergy rather than witnessing it in motion.
- A set of victory conditions that require you to survive early aggression, which is always a fun theoretical exercise until you actually get blitzed on turn two.

Practical tips for testing Time Strategy once you finally crack that plastic:

- Start with a small, focused draw engine and avoid overloading with too many “card draw” cards. The sweetness of tempo is speed; you don’t want to be the car stuck in the driveway because your trunk is full of ectoplasmic tokens.
- Practice with a friend who has a similar temperament: calm, patient, and not the type to sigh dramatically when a draw order is not quite optimal.
- Use a rule-explanation walk-through to keep everyone on the same page; nothing kills tempo like a rules debate that lasts longer than the actual game.

## Bashing: the art of the cheerful all-caps aggression

Bashing in Codex Starter Set is the archetype that wears the loudest t-shirt and eats the loudest snack. It’s the “I am in your face and I brought snacks” approach, the kind of deck that makes your opponent rethink whether they should have stayed a little later at the table. The premise is simple: leverage fast, direct pressure to overwhelm opponents before they can set up their grand plans.

The unopened starter set’s Bashing cards promise a straightforward strategy: you hit early, you hit often, and you try not to trip over your own enthusiasm. In practice, Bashing decks demand a few things: reliable early aggression, enough resilience to weather a counter-punch, and the mental fortitude to resist pivoting to a more nuanced plan after the first two turns of chaos.

Common Bashing observations from an unopened box perspective:

- Early-game plays are flashy, but without a proper engine you might stall out if your opponent has the right answer. The recipe here is “fast, but not reckless.”
- You’ll want some survivability or board presence that doesn’t crumble under a well-timed removal spell. Otherwise you’ll be staring at a single chump blocker and a lot of regret.
- The color and flavor of Bashing cards tend to lean into the joy of “I slam you with a big number and grin while you pull a face like you’ve just found out there’s no dessert.”

### A practical approach to Bashing in real games

To maximize Bashing’s potential, you’ll want to schedule your turns so your opponent spends most of theirs looking at you. That means economy on your side and punishment on theirs. If the starter set’s Bashing cards land late in the game, you’ve either misread the metagame or have had a conversation with the rulebook that went too long and forgot to stop. The trick is to time your hit with the momentum of your deck’s earlier threats, not simply lunge for the biggest number available.

In real play, Bashing tends to reward players who can maintain pressure while gradually building a broader board presence. ## Finesse: misdirection, mischief, and the art of not telling your opponent everything

Finesse is the house of subtext: not what your cards say, but what they imply. Finesse decks tend to be the ones you’d pick if you wanted to feel clever while your opponent mutters their life total into a stern, futuristic calculator. It’s about trickery, subtle plays, and the satisfaction of a perfectly timed bluff that makes your opponent question every decision they’ve ever made about deck-building.

Within the Codex Starter Set, Finesse cards promise opportunities for deception and spark-blur combos. In an opened-but-unplayed scenario, the cards look like gleaming invitations to “try this at home,” but the risk is that you’ll blunder into your own trap while trying to write the rules in fancy calligraphy. The sensibility of Finesse is: think twice, then think again, then maybe think in a different color for flavor.

Practical notes on Finesse from a box that hasn’t yet seen a table:

- Your fragile illusions require careful setup. If you rush a bluff, you’ll give away your plan faster than a magician who forgot his wave-spotter.
- The payoff is the moment your opponent misreads your intention and you reveal the actual trick just in time to clinch victory. It’s the brain teaser you didn’t know you wanted until your friend smiles and you realize you’ve been outplayed by your own misdirection.
- Finesse hinges on information control: knowing when to reveal a piece of your plan and when to withhold it for maximum effect.

## Meta, matchups, and the open box truth

In the world of card games, matchups are the stories you tell yourself to stay awake during a long night of play. The Codex Starter Set, with its three archetypes, invites players to explore a pseudo-meta where each deck type has a mirror opposite and a few crossover possibilities. The box’s unplayed state means you’ll need to write those stories from scratch. Here are some starter-set realities you should consider before you declare a winner:

- Time Strategy vs Bashing: The classic test of patience against pressure. Will your tempo survive the early onslaught, or will your opponent’s aggression force you into a corner where you question every choice you’ve ever made? The answer is usually a blend of good openings and the ability to pivot when a reliable plan remains just out of reach.
- Time Strategy vs Finesse: The brain chess game. When both players try to out-think each other, you’ll get a rhythm that feels like a waltz with a trapdoor. The trick is to avoid tipping your hand too early and hope your opponent’s bluff becomes a breadcrumb trail you can follow to victory.
- Bashing vs Finesse: A clash that rewards reaction and misdirection. Your opponent may lean heavy on a direct assault, but if you can bait them into overextending, you’ll be ready with the exact counterplay.

## Deck-building pragmatics in a never-played starter set

The act of turning an unopened Codex Starter Set into a playable skin on a toothpick is a rite of passage. You need to extract a coherent 40- to 60-card shell from a mix of archetypes, decide what you want to do with your curve, and decide how to handle the inevitable board state that arrives when you forget to bring your calculator to a friendly match. The starter set does not come with a single, perfect build; it offers the seed of a few paths, plus some spare goats to choose your path by.

Key tiplets for building from a starter set:

- Start lean. Pick a base of 20–25 cards that emphasize your chosen archetype’s core engine.
- Prioritize synergy cards that enable core interactions. If you’re chasing Time, you want draw and tempo locks; if you’re chasing Bashing, you want efficient versus command-the-table cards; if you’re chasing Finesse, you’ll need disruption and misdirection.
- Include a handful of flexible cards that can serve multiple roles depending on matchups. This helps you avoid getting stuck with a one-trick pony when the meta shows up wearing a different hat.
