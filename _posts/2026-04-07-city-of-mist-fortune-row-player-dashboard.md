## What is Fortune Row and why a Player Dashboard?
Fortune Row appears in spirit as a collection of micro-footholds in a city that refuses to stay quiet about its legends. It is a playground for a certain kind of detective work where the line between myth and memo becomes blurrier the longer you stay in the city. The Player Dashboard aspect is meant to centralize a few essential bits: character status, relic inventory, faction cues, and a running thread of active mythic crises. The idea is simple on the surface: give players a cockpit where they can see their narrative dials in real time rather than flipping through index cards and post-it notes. On the page, Fortune Row asks: can the players keep track of the citys pulse without turning every session into a scavenger hunt for the GM screen?

From a design perspective, this is a love letter to habit forming. The dashboard is not about overshadowing the narrative with pure data; it is about shaping the rhythm of play. When you can glance at a panel and tell at a glance whether your Hero has a Mythic Reservoir that could spark a new lead, you are already ahead of the game. The dashboard becomes a living motif of the citys heartbeat. The more it aligns with the players actual play tempo, the less we chase after the rulebook and the more we chase after the moment. It is DMs who will appreciate the dashboards sense of top-level overview, and players who crave clarity about where their legends stand in the moment to moment chaos.

## Core design choices in the dashboard
### Modularity and quick toggles
Fortune Row embraces modularity. The dashboard comes with a handful of panels that can be rearranged or swapped depending on the table style. Want to focus on a single player? Move their panel to the center. If the table runs with a call-and-response cadence, set up a rapid glance bar where the party can ping a status or a crisis with a single click. The modular approach reminds me of the early 3D strategy games where you assemble your war room before a mission. The benefit here is pragmatic: you can tailor the dashboard to the players you actually have instead of the players you wish you had.

### Aesthetic that carries weight
The art direction of the Fortune Row dashboard leans into noirish neon and mythic glyphs. It is part street-level detective turf, part mythic underworld. The interface uses contrasting color blocks to indicate who is in the spotlight, which crises demand attention, and what relics may tilt the next roll. If you enjoy the flavor of City of Mist, you will appreciate how the dashboard looks like a tech-scorched urban map and a prophecy board all at once. This is not just style; it is a cue system that tells you what to look at during a tense scene.

### The data model under the hood
Under the hood, the dashboard tracks a few essential primitives: character tags, mythic assets, ongoing crises, factions alignment, and a running log of notable outcomes. It does not pretend to be a full blown character sheet; instead, it abstracts to what the table needs most often: who has what power, what is currently happening in Fortune Row, and what lever can be pulled to shift the momentum of the scene. This abstraction helps keep things fast and cinematic but can be disconcerting for players who want to micro-manage every last detail. The dashboard clearly favors story over bookkeeping, which is an intentional design philosophy that aligns with a certain era of City of Mist play style.

## A tour of the main panels
### The Hero Spotlight panel
This is where the players show up as the protagonists. It highlights the current status of each hero, including active Masks, recent success or failure, and any special conditions tied to the current scene. For players who like to track their own narrative progress, this panel is a small miracle. It reduces the cognitive load of following a sprawling plot by presenting essential context in a single glance. The downside is that if you rely on it heavily, you might lose some of the micro-narrative texture that comes from physically flipping through a binder. The dashboard is not trying to replace the GM's storytelling, but it does pump up the players' sense of agency with a crisp, readable snapshot.

### The Crises board
Crises are the heartbeat of a City of Mist table, and the dashboard translates this into a rolling ticker. You can mark active crises, set their threat level, and trigger narrative twists with a couple of taps. The visual language here is important: red for urgent, amber for brewing, green for resolved or in good standing. It is a simple macro that communicates the macro story in seconds—a huge win for sessions that want pace. The risk, of course, is overuse. When everything is flagged as urgent, nothing feels urgent at all. The designers clearly anticipated this tension and built in a cooldown mechanism to remind players that not every problem screams for attention at once.

### The Relic and Power inventory
Relics are one of the most flavorful parts of City of Mist, and Fortune Row treats them like a small museum that travels with the party. The dashboard keeps a running catalog of relics owned by each hero, including mood-coded tags like arcane, tech, or street-level. It also links relics to potential story hooks, which is a clever way to nudge play toward the mythic threads the table wants to explore. While a dedicated physical relic sheet can be tactile and fun, the dashboard offers the advantage of cross-session continuity. If a relic gains a new experiential effect in session five, the dashboard can reflect it automatically in session six, so there is less back-and-forth and more mythic momentum.

### The Faction and Allegiance section
City of Mist thrives on alliances and rivalries, and the dashboard makes the abstract tangible. Factions are tracked, with quick toggles for standing, influence, and current goals. This helps the GM orchestrate faction-driven scenes without micro-managing each NPC in the moment. Players can also see when they have leverage with a faction or when a betrayal could swing the next scene. The risk here is letting the dashboard turn into a spreadsheet of intrigue, but the design intent keeps the data readable and the tension narrative-driven rather than numeric.

## Usability and play experience
### The onboarding curve
If you are new to City of Mist, Fortune Row might feel a bit fussy at first. There is a lot happening in the UI, and a portion of that is narrative scaffolding rather than raw data. The best way to approach it is to start small: pick one or two panels, get comfortable with toggling between them, and gradually expand to include more of the dashboard as your table breathes. The good news is that the dashboard is resilient to this approach; you can onboard gradually without breaking immersion. Veteran players will appreciate the speed boost and the streamlined cross-scene continuity, while newcomers get a gentle ramp into the systems the game uses to build mythic drama.

### Accessibility and readability
For long sessions, readability matters. Fortune Row uses high-contrast color cues, legible typography, and scalable panels. The layout supports both tablet and laptop play, which is great for online sessions and in-person tables alike. There is an option to print a condensed player view, which is a nice nod to analog play and ensures the dashboard can be used without a screen in front of every player. That said, the advantage of a rich UI can become a distraction if you let it. The designers provide a sensible default mode, and you can switch to a leaner view when you want to focus on dialogue and character moments.

### Reliability and performance
In my sessions, the Fortune Row dashboard loaded quickly and remained responsive through a variety of scenes. The data sync between players and GM remained stable for the most part, with occasional minor lag during heavier scenes that involve multiple simultaneous crises. This is not a deal breaker; it is a reminder that you are playing a live, social game backed by software. The performance caveat is more about your network (or cell signal) than the dashboard itself. If you need rock solid reliability for a convention game, consider running a local networked session or offline install of the dashboard to minimize latency.

## Practical setup and table integration
### Getting started with your session zero
A good way to adopt Fortune Row is to run a session zero with the dashboard as a live prop. Let players create their dashboards for their main character, connect their relics to a few story ideas, and set up the initial crises as a shared table artifact. This initial alignment reduces friction later, when players want to pull threads and escalate the stakes. A zero session also helps your table decide how much autonomy you want to grant to players in managing the dashboard. Some groups prefer to let the players push their own status bar updates, while others want the GM to curate the flows to maintain tension.

### Online use and cross table sharing
If your table plays online, the Fortune Row dashboard plays well with common video conference tools and shared whiteboards. It supports multi-user access, which means you can have one player keep an eye on a particular crisis while another tracks relics and masks. For cross table campaigns, the dashboard can be a shared memory of events, creating a sense of ongoing mythic continuity that players can latch onto across sessions.

### In-person play and tactile flavor
For in-person tables, the dashboard translates well to a projected screen or a large monitor. You can pair it with printed handouts for players who prefer physical artifacts. The tactile feel of flipping between panels, tapping a crisis to trigger a scene, or sliding a relic into a new position on the board still matters. Fortune Row respects that tactile truth while offering the convenience of digital organization. It is not a replacement for a good GM or a good group; it is a tool to enhance both by reducing the friction of logistics.

## Pitfalls and places to watch out for
### The risk of overstructure
One common pitfall is turning Fortune Row into a rigid cockpit that stifles improvisation. The dashboard should be treated as a dynamic companion rather than a hard constraint. If the table starts to feel as if it is navigating a prewritten script, it defeats the spirit of City of Mist, which thrives on the interplay of myth and mundane. The best sessions use the dashboard as a springboard for ideas, not a script that must be followed to the letter.

### Information overload for new players
If your table has players who are new to the system, the dashboard can become a flood of labels and statuses. The recommended approach is to phase in capabilities gradually, start with essential panels, and then layer on extras as players gain familiarity. You want your dashboard to reveal its secrets, not bury them under a pile of blinking indicators.

### Data hygiene is a team sport
The value of the dashboard is tied to how well the data is maintained. If players forget to update their relics or misclassify a crisis, the dashboard becomes unreliable. Establish a simple cadence for updates—perhaps at the end of each session—and you will keep the data trustworthy and the narrative smooth.
