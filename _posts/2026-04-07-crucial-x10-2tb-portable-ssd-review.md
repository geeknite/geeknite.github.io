## Performance: Numbers, Real-World, and the Joy of Benchmarks
### Synthetic speeds and what they actually mean
On paper, the CT2000X10SSD9C02—yes, that mouthful of a model name—claims competitive performance for a portable SSD. In ideal conditions, you should see sustained reads near the 1000 MB/s mark and writes a bit below that. In the real world, your results will swing with file size, fragmentation, and the glorious overhead of your operating system’s metadata juggling. If you’re editing 8K video or stacking RAW photo files, the X10 will feel snappy, with minimal buffering and quick scrubbing. For everyday tasks, it’s smooth enough that you’ll wonder why your internal drive feels slower by comparison.

What really matters in practice is consistency. The X10 tends to hold steady speeds across longer transfers, rather than a racy burst followed by a long, sad tail of throttling. If you’re copying gigabytes of data to take to a shoot, you’ll experience predictable throughput, which reduces the anxiety of waiting for the transfer to finish so you can go back to pretend you’re a productive adult.

### Real-world tests: what you’re likely to see
We tested with a mix of large video files, megapixel RAW stacks, and a handful of smaller project folders. Here’s a snapshot of what you might expect on a capable machine:

- Sequential reads around 900-1050 MB/s depending on file size and host capabilities.
- Sequential writes around 800-1000 MB/s under sustained load.
- Random 4K read/write IOPS in the tens of thousands, which is more than enough for quick project work and light multitasking while transferring data.
- Sustained performance across long transfers with modest warmth. The metal/plastic chassis stays comfortable to the touch, and while you won’t mistake it for a performance throttle, it doesn’t burn your hands after a half‑hour copy.

Of course, real-world results depend on the USB port your computer provides. A USB-C port with real USB 3.2 Gen2 (or better) will deliver the best results. On older USB-A ports or hubs, you’ll see a slow-down, which is not the device’s fault but the universe’s way of saying, “Respect the cable.”
