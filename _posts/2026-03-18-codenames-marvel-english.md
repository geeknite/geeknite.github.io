## How to play (the Marvel twist)
If you’ve played Codenames before, Marvel Edition will feel familiar, but with two notable enhancements: a larger pool of theme-specific cues and a few clever rule tweaks to emphasize storytelling and pop-culture vibes. If you’re new to Codenames, fear not—the learning curve is still gentle, with enough humor to make the rules feel approachable.

### Setup and team roles
- The board is set with 25 word cards arranged in a 5x5 grid.
- Players are divided into two teams: red and blue. Each team designates a Spymaster who gives one-word clues followed by a number, like “Stark 3” or “Shield 2.” The rest are Field Operatives who try to guess the word cards that connect to the clue.
- The Marvel edition adds recurring Marvel-flavored themes or characters to the clue-giving vocabulary. A few extra cues are introduced to help tie the clues into the Marvel universe without forcing players to memorize a long list of names.

### Clue-giving and guessing (the core loop)
- The Spymaster gives a one-word clue with a number. The clue must connect to as many of their own team’s word cards as possible while avoiding the opposing team’s words and the assassin word.
- The Field Operatives discuss, deduce, and point to cards they think match the clue. The more accurate the guess, the more control you have over the turn.
- If a guess is correct (one of your team’s words), you continue guessing up to the number plus one optional extra guess if you think another card might fit. If you hit an opponent’s word, their turn ends. If you reveal the assassin word, immediately end the round and possibly bring the other team a victory or dramatic shift in momentum.

The Marvel edge injects thematic cues that can be a blessing or a trap. For instance, a clue like “Stark 3” might lead to Mr. Stark’s neural arc, but it could also be misinterpreted as something from the broader tech world. The key is balance: Spymasters should aim for the Marvel-leaning connections while avoiding crossovers that could mislead both teams. It’s a fine line between clever and cheeky, and Marvel Edition loves that spot where clever hints collide with pop culture overload in the best way.

## Strategy, tips, and best practices
A robust set of strategies exists for Codenames in general, and Marvel Edition is no exception. Below are practical tips to up your game, whether you’re new or returning to the Spymaster’s chair.

### For beginners: building a safe foundation
- Start with safe, obvious connections: pick clues that unambiguously link a few of your own words without pulling in too many potential traps.
- Use Marvel tropes and cinematic shorthand: references to tech, teams, or widely known events can be effective as long as they don’t circle into ambiguous territory.
- Read your teammates: the same clue may click for one group and fall flat for another. Consider your Tag Team dynamic and adjust your clues to the shared knowledge of your squad.
- Manage risk: if you’re uncertain, guess only a couple of cards in a turn to avoid accidentally hitting the assassin or the opposing team’s words.

### For veterans: weaving complexity with clarity
- Create multi-target clues that guide multiple of your own words at once, but don’t corner yourself into a trap. For example, “Avengers 3” could connect to three of your words while risking a misstep if the grid aligns poorly.
- Use cross-category cues sparingly. Marvel Edition can benefit from clever cross-links (like “New York” or “S.H.I.E.L.D.”) that grab several words at once, but you’ll want to ensure those words are likely to be claimed by your team.
- Exploit the assassin carefully. If you sense a high-stakes clue could swing the turn in your favor, weigh the potential payoff against the risk of a sudden game-ending mistake.
- Balance theme with play pace. A well-chosen Marvel cue can keep everyone engaged, but overusing lore-heavy references can grind the game to a halt for non-fans.

### Common missteps and how to avoid them
- Too-specific references: If your clue is tied too tightly to a single character, you risk alienating teammates who don’t share the same breadth of Marvel knowledge.
- Over-reliance on pop culture knowledge: The game should be accessible; if players get bogged down in guessing, rotate to safer cues and let the fun return.
- Ignoring the grid: The spatial pattern of the 5x5 board is a powerful tool. Don’t ignore the way words cluster on the table—often a well-placed spatial cue can guide multiple teammates in one turn.
