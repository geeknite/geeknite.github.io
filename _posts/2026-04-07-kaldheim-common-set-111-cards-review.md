## About Kaldheim Common Set
Kaldheim’s common set is the backbone of the set’s draft and sealed experience. It’s where you’ll find efficient bodies, reliable removal, early pressure, and the occasional late-game stabilizer that can swing a match in your favor even if you reveal your plan only after the board has already toppled into a four-turn skirmish. The 111-card pool is designed to be generous enough for under-cost strategies but tight enough to reward thoughtful drafting rather than “slam a large bomb and hope for the best.” The color balance is deliberate, ensuring that each color has a coherent role in the ecosystem.

From a playability perspective, commons in Kaldheim often reward staying on plan: if you present early pressure and maintain tempo with timely removal or blockers, you’ll often win games before your opponent can pivot to one perfect top-deck. The foretell mechanic—an extra wrinkle that lets you stash cards for cheaper plays later—creates interesting decisions for players who enjoy planning ahead, while snow synergies (though not as dominant as in dedicated snow-themed sets) provide an additional flavor layer that can lead to satisfying combos.

If you’d like to learn more about the official setting and flavor, Wizards’ site is a good starting point. For those who want a broader sense of how this type of 111-card commons pool can fit into casual formats, keep reading and imagine how these pieces come together in a kitchen-table meta.

## Color-by-Color Breakdown

### White
White commons in Kaldheim are your tempo enablers, your lifelink protectors, and your efficient blockers all rolled into a neat little package. You’ll find early drop creatures that trade evenly or win the race when combined with a well-timed removal suite. If you’re pursuing a white-based plan, you’ll want to pick up cards that help you maintain pressure while keeping your life total stable. Anthems and static buffs are nice to have in larger sets, but in the commons environment, you’ll be grateful for any card that helps you punch above your weight early.

White’s power in a commons-only deck comes from clean two-drops and reliable one-drops that hit the board on turn 1 or turn 2. In a well-constructed 60-card build, white cards can form the backbone of a midrange-to-aggro strategy that looks to end the game before your opponent can assemble an answer.

### Blue
Blue’s role is less “blow up the opponent” and more “neutralize their game plan while you draw into your own gas.” Expect a handful of cheap cantrips and some cost-efficient tempo plays. Blue commons aren’t always the loudest, but they’re the ones that keep your deck from stalling. If you enjoy controlling the pace of the game, blue can be a satisfying lane to explore within a 111-card pool.

The blue commons that shine in a budget list are the ones that let you filter through your deck, find your plan, and keep your opponents from executing their best plays. A couple of defensive or evasive creatures also add resilience to your early game, letting you survive to the midgame where your plan can come online.

### Black
Black commons tend to be efficient and sharp: cheap removal, two-for-ones where possible, and some combat tricks that make your opponent’s life miserable. If you’re building around a black strategy, aim to pair disruption with a few solid threats that can finish the job once you’ve reduced the opponent’s options. The beauty of black isn’t in bombs; it’s in the rhythm of trading up, pressuring the opponent’s life total, and forcing your opponent into awkward blocks.

The best black commons in a fixed pool are those that combine cost-effective removal with resilient threats. When you can remove a blocker and present a threat at the same time, you’ve found the heartbeat of a good black deck.

### Red
Red is the color of speed, aggression, and a little bit of chaos. Red commons in Kaldheim often come with early pressure, direct damage that helps you close the game, and bite-sized threats that demonstrate how a simple creature can still win games when backed by a loud tempo. If you’re drafting red, you want a consistent tempo between your early and mid-game plays that keeps your opponent on their back foot and never quite prepared for your next bit of pressure.

Red’s role in a commons-only build is to press when you’re ahead and to punish over-extensions when you’re behind. A few cheap two-drops and some direct damage can swing the board back in your favor, turning precarious positions into your next victory.

### Green
Green is the big-game hunter in the commons pool: efficient bodies, solid ramp, and a few “get bigger fast” cards that let you overcome the opponent’s defenses. A green-focused plan is about stacking advantage through bigger bodies, helping you snatch wins with early board presence and solid midgame pressure.

In practice, green is a reliable option for players who enjoy straightforward, powerful plays on a budget. It’s also a good color to pair with others when you want to maintain a strong presence on the board while you assemble your plan.

### Archtypes and Synergies
The 111-card common set supports a few flexible archetypes:
- Aggro/midrange: A lean, efficient curve with a mix of early threats and removal
- Tempo-control: A pair of removal spells and evasive creatures to tilt the board
- Foretell-based strategies: Exile cards you can cast cheaply later to out-value your opponent
- Snow synergy: A few cards that reward playing snow permanents

These archetypes aren’t mutually exclusive; if your pool supports it, you can craft a hybrid approach that leverages your best pieces.

## Draft and Sealed Considerations
Drafting from a 111-card commons pool requires a different mental model than opening a bundle of rare and mythic bombs. With only commons, you’re solving a puzzle: which two colors do you want to lean on, and how do you survive until your plan comes together? The common set is forgiving in that you’re not risking a heartbreak with a “draw empty” moment; you’re rendering value from every card you pick with a careful balance.

For sealed formats, a 111-card pool packs enough density to deliver consistent gameplay. You’ll often find that your decisions early on matter more than rare picks; a few well-timed removal spells can swing a match, and a single early threat can lock a game if you’ve got the right follow-up.

When building around Foretell and snow synergies, you might want to set aside a couple of compensating cards that help you realize your plan in the midgame, whether that means paying for a cheap surge of creatures or playing a safe defense while you set up your finisher.

## Deckbuilding Tips and Tricks (Expanded)
- Plan your curve: A smooth curve—two drops, three drops, a couple of four-drops—will help you keep tempo without stalling.
- Budget plan: Use only commons to build a 60-card deck and test your strategies in friendly games; the process is educational and fun.
- Foretell synergy: If you’re excited by Foretell, pick cards that enable you to exile under-cost spells and then cast them cheaply to accelerate your plan.
- Removal economy: A single, well-placed removal spell is often more valuable than two marginal ones in this context; keep the best removal for critical moments.
- Evasion matters: A few evasive commons can help you bypass blockers and close out games that stall on the board.

A note on the playtesting: The best way to learn how well a commons-only deck runs is to play a few games with the same pool. You’ll quickly learn which cards are the engine and which cards are filler. If you find yourself comfortable with a plan, you’re probably ready to upgrade your deck using a mix of uncommon and rare picks from your collection.

## Draft and Sealed Example Deck Ideas
If you want a tangible starting point, here are two archetype templates you can adapt from the 111 commons. Remember: you’ll adjust the counts as you open your pool and learn what you’ve got access to.

- Aggro/Tempo (White-Red): A lean early curve, with 1- and 2-mana threats and cheap removal. Your aim is to push aims on turn 3-4, then finish with a couple of on-theme pressure blockers.
- Midrange/Control (Blue-White): A slower plan with card draw and removal, using the commons to keep tempo while you ramp into your late-game plan.

60-card Budget Deck Example (Commons-only, two-color focus)
- White: 8-10 early threats, 3-4 removal options
- Blue: 6-8 cantrips and draw spells; 2-3 removal options
- Red: 6-8 aggressive threats that pressure the opponent
- Green: 4-6 bodies with reasonable power at low mana cost
- Mana base: 16-18 lands (adjust to your mana requirements)
- Utility/side: 2-4 filler cards for flexibility

If you’re a fan of mono-color builds, you can easily tailor this toward a single color with a similar distribution, focusing on the primary strength of that color. The point is not to chase perfection but to create a coherent, repeatable plan you can play and enjoy.

### 60-card Budget Deck Example (One-Color Focus)
- White mono: fast creatures, removal, and a few lifelink bodies to stabilize
- Blue mono: draw, filtering, and evasive defense
- Red mono: fast and relentless pressure with a dash of direct damage
- Green mono: efficient bodies and early ramp where available

These are scaffolds rather than final lists; you are encouraged to modify and test with your friends to learn what works best for your group.

## Playstyle and Value
One of the chief joys of the Kaldheim common set is that every card has a role in a deck that feels complete. It’s easy to underestimate the power of a well-timed removal spell or a tiny two-drop that trades above its weight. This is the beauty of commons: it rewards precision, not just raw shock value. You’ll discover that building around a few core cards and letting 8-10 supportive pieces do the lifting can yield a surprisingly strong board state. And if you’re the kind of player who loves the “aha” moment of a well-timed foretell or a clever combat trick, this pool offers more than enough nuance to keep you engaged.

The monetary value of 111 commons is not the point here; it’s the practice of deck-building discipline, which can translate into better play in more expensive formats. You’ll learn when to pull the trigger on a removal spell, how to sequence your threats in compressed turns, and how to maximize your tempo to outpace opponents who might come in with flashier cards. The satisfaction of a well-assembled, commons-only deck is as real as the satisfaction you get from finding a hidden path through a maze of ordinary-looking cards.
