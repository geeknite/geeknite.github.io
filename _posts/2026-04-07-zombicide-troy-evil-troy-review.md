Zombicide fans, assemble your holy grail of chaos and carnage. If you thought the base Black Plague set was enough to ruin your apartment with tiny plastic corpses, wait until you add Troy and Evil Troy to the mix. Guillotine Games team up with CMON to deliver a duo of characters who can flip the table of fate with a single card draw. In this review we dive into what the Troy and Evil Troy expansion brings to the Black Plague universe, how it feels at the table, and whether your party should invest in two heroic but probably morally questionable brothers in arms.

Overview
Troy and Evil Troy are two unique heroes designed to slot into the Black Plague campaign. Their rules are not gimmicks, they are a shift in how you approach combat and movement. Troy is the classic fearless frontline bruiser, a walking meat shield with a surprisingly nimble backstory, while Evil Troy is the chaotic foil whose abilities lean into fast lane play, risk taking, and turning the board into a candy store of bad ideas. The dynamic between the two feels like a buddy cop movie where someone forgot the budget and decided to lean into the undead.

What is included
The box content focuses on two new hero miniatures, their character cards, and the usual assortment of tokens and a few kitbash scenario tweaks. You will get two new hero boards, two mini figures, two hero backstories that feel like they could star in a limited edition spinoff, plus a small pile of gear cards that support and sometimes hinder both heroes depending on the draw. The production quality is typical CMON: sturdy plastic, crisp art, and a color palette that pops on the table even when your colleagues lean toward the pale undead vibe.

Twin chaos inside a single expansion
One of the clever design moves here is to present Troy and Evil Troy as two halves of a moral coin. The synergy comes mainly from the way their unique abilities interact with zombie spawn patterns and objective based play. Troy can tank a horde and absorb the brunt of the challenges, while Evil Troy pushes the envelope with risky moves that could pay off in spades or land everyone in a thick soup of rot and bone. This dynamic encourages players to coordinate and plan around both heroes rather than treat them as separate options. The net effect is a campaign flavor that adds drama without requiring you to break the game into a dozen new rules.

Balance and difficulty
Zombicide is known for balancing bite sized chaos with a cooperative groove. The Troy expansion fits into that rhythm by giving players options that reward smart positioning and route planning. On a normal board the two heroes can swing the tide, but the undead still move with menacing efficiency. The risk element introduced by Evil Troy can push games into a frenzied crescendo, especially when you combine his risk taking with cards that modify loot and equipment. The challenge remains accessible for new players while offering deeper tactical layers for veterans who want to squeeze every drop of efficiency from their two preferred fighters.

Mechanics and design
Abilities
Troy is the frontline tank in the duo. He carries higher survivability on the front line, can absorb damage, and his movement rules emphasize close quarters combat. His weapon choices are tuned for melee commitment, enabling him to slice through groups of zombies with a style that feels almost cinematic, if cinematic fights involved tiny plastic limbs and a lot of dice. Evil Troy, on the other hand, leans into speed, cunning, and opportunism. His skills reward aggressive choices, backdoor paths, and bold gambits. The synergy between the two is what gives this expansion its staying power on the table.

Strategy tips
For experienced players the secret is not just to let Troy soak the damage but to set up the board so that Evil Troy can strike at the perfect moment. Think of it as a dance where Troy pins the horde in place while Evil Troy darts in to grab critical objectives or slice off the heads of the stronger enemies. The gear cards that come with the expansion are best used to support this tempo, offering options like extra movement or temporary buffs that can keep Evil Troy safe while he attempts a high value mission. In practice this means planning two or three rounds ahead and paying attention to zombie spawn rules that could suddenly flood your space with a new wave.

Gameplay and replayability
The two hero play styles push players toward new meta approaches. Co op teams that thrive on a single go to the left plan will appreciate having a sole hero who can hold ground, while the other player can act as the wild card. Solo play remains a fun challenge for those nights when you want to test a strategy by yourself, albeit with fewer humans to blame for the board becoming a walking catastrophe. Replayability comes from the different spawn patterns, gear combinations, and the unpredictable nature of Evil Troy who can swing the tide with a single gamble.

Theme and aesthetics
Miniatures and cards carry the same level of care that fans have come to expect from this line. The Troy and Evil Troy figures are dynamic, with elongated poses that capture the moment of a lethal strike or a well timed dodge. The art on the hero cards tends toward the grim humor that Zombicide fans adore: a mix of gothic fantasy and over the top action that makes every zombie feel like a possibly appropriate audition for a low budget action film.

Playability and comfort at the table
The setup is straightforward if you are already deep into Black Plague. The expansion slots neatly into the campaign and the new leaders do not require a complete rework of existing rules. The weight of the new content is moderate; it adds depth and a fresh dynamic without dragging down the turn clock. In many two player or three player games the two heroes become focal points that drive the entire strategy and keep the game feeling lively rather than monotonous.

Value for money
In terms of build quality the Troy expansion matches its siblings: solid minis, well printed cards, and a component count that is manageable for a typical pickup session. The price point is reasonable for what you get: two unique heroes, a handful of new gear cards, and a rewatchable cinematic vibe on every staging of the board. If you are a dedicated Zombicide collector this is a no brainer; if you are on the fence this expansion offers enough flavor to justify a gamble.

Tips and house rules
To maximize your run with Troy and Evil Troy consider the following:
- Use Troy to anchor a corridor while Evil Troy seizes objective tokens from a distance
- Save morale boosting gear for the tougher rounds because a single misstep can cascade into a zombie pile up
- Swap in house rules that allow Evil Troy to take a card from the top of the deck if a critical objective is near
- Balance the board by adjusting spawn timers when you want a longer or shorter cycle of action
- Use terrain elements to create chokepoints that favor Troy’s effectiveness

Design philosophy
Two heroes, one board
The concept behind this expansion is to create two linked characters whose choices feed off each other. Troy acts as a magnet for attention and enemies while Evil Troy thrives on misdirection and speed. This pairing encourages players to think about the board as two intertwined lanes rather than a single path to victory.

Mood and storytelling
The tone stays true to the franchise, balancing grim vibes with occasional flashes of over the top humor. On table you will find moments where the horror of a rampaging horde collides with a punch line from a heroic figure, and that contrast is what keeps the game from tipping into grim misery.

Playing with different table sizes
Two players and three or four players will feel different with Troy and Evil Troy. In a two player game the dynamics are more intense as the two heroes often become the entire engine for success. In larger groups the supporting cast can absorb some of the attention and let the two main heroes do the dramatic work.

Accessibility and learning curve
The two hero system introduces a moderate learning curve for new players but offers a gentle slope for veterans. The rules interplay is not overly complex; it is the interpretation of the two hero ramps that matters. The cards are clear and the artwork helps quick recognition on the table.

Modular integration with base game and other expansions
Troy and Evil Troy blend with the base Black Plague expansion and with other Zombicide add ons in a way that feels cohesive. You will not need to relearn the wheel to fit two new heroes into a scenario. The expansion also scales well with different campaign lengths so you can tailor the experience to a weekend session or a longer narrative arc.

Final thoughts

It does not overwhelm the core game, instead it nudges the boundaries and invites you to push further. If your table is craving a mix of heroism and a dash of chaos, Troy and Evil Troy deliver.
