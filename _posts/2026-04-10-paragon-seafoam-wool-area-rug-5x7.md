### Foot traffic and wear patterns

In high-traffic zones, natural wool will show some wear over time, but that’s part of the rug’s character. A little patina is a sign of life, not laziness. If you’re worried about visible wear in the early months, rotate the rug every few months to encourage even use. This simple habit can help maintain a balanced look across the surface, especially in rooms with a lot of sunlight that tends to fade portions of textiles slower than others.

### Pets and spill mitigation

Pets will appreciate the softness, and spills are manageable with prompt blotting. Wool fibers repel water slightly better than cheap synthetics, and the natural lanolin provides a modest barrier to minor staining. For stubborn spills (red wine, tomato sauce, you know the drill), treat as you would with any wool product: blot, don’t rub, and consider a wool-safe cleaner in small quantities. Always test any cleaning product in an inconspicuous corner first.

## Maintenance and Care

### Cleaning routine

Vacuuming weekly with a low-suction setting and a non-beater vacuum head is recommended to preserve the pile height and minimize shedding. If your vacuum has a beater bar, switch it off or use a gentle setting. Rotate the rug every few months to promote even wear and color retention. Spin-fingertip tests (aka you touching the carpet) will tell you if you’re due for a light fluffing; wool loves a good air-out session in sunlight, but avoid prolonged direct sun to prevent color fading.

### Stain management

For everyday spills, blot with a clean cloth as soon as possible. Avoid rubbing, which can push stain fibers deeper. If needed, mix a small amount of wool-friendly cleaner with water and dab gently. Always follow with a clear water blot to remove any cleaning residue. For tougher stains, it’s often best to consult a specialist and avoid over-wetting the fabric. Wool is durable but not invincible; treat it with the same respect you’d offer to a beloved retro game console sitting in a fragile display case.

### Sun exposure and fading

Direct sun can slowly fade color over time. If your rug sits under a window, consider a window treatment that filters UV rays or rotate your furniture to ensure even exposure. Fading is cosmetic rather than structural; it simply adds a little character, much like the way a retro cartridge adds character to a vintage console.
