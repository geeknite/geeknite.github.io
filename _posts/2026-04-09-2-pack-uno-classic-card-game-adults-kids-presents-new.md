## How to Play (A Light, Not-So-Serious Overview)
UNO hasn’t changed much since its ’70s inception, and that’s a virtue rather than a limitation. The objective is simple: be the first to run out of cards. You do this by playing cards that match either the color or the number of the card on the discard pile. Action cards throw a little chaos into the mix—Skip cards make the next player miss a turn, Reverse flips the direction, Draw Two dumps two cards into the next player’s hands, and Wild cards let you choose a color to continue the chaos. The Wild Draw Four is the apex of strategic mischief, but remember: the earth remains full of players who discover loopholes in the rules every time you’re about to win.

The 2-pack setup shines when you have a mixed-age audience. Kids can rush to slap down a number card with the energy of a caffeinated squirrel, while adults can scheme through a gentle game of “one more round” until the sun comes up and you all regret your decision to include the neighbor’s cousin in the mix. The rulebook is intentionally short and sweet, which is fantastic for a party game where people want to start playing immediately rather than launching into a legalistic rules discussion. The quick-start rules are easy to memorize, and the two decks mean you can escalate to higher-stakes versions of UNO (like playing with two stacks of color-coded chaos) without needing to buy extra expansions.

## House-Rules to Try (Safe, Friendly, and Fun)
- Stacking Draw Twos: If a player puts down a Draw Two, the next player can stack another Draw Two instead of drawing. This can escalate quickly; ensure everyone knows the boundary between “fun chaos” and “game-ending chaos.”
- Color Lock: If someone calls a color and succeeds in sticking to it for three turns in a row, they gain the “Color Assassin” badge for the evening (a purely ceremonial title, but it feels epic).
- UNO Cut: If a player has less than three cards and shouts “UNO,” the group skips a round to celebrate a strategic play. It’s silly, but it reduces table-police tension dramatically.
