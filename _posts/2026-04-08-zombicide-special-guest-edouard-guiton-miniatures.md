## A closer look at the sculpts and the Edouard Guiton signature

The first thing that jumps out when you open the box is the level of gesture in the figures. Edouard Guiton has a knack for capturing motion in a still character, which translates beautifully on a tabletop. There is a sense of motion that makes kneecaps and ribcages feel like they are catching air as if the survivors are stepping between panels in a comic book. The zombies carry a swagger that, frankly, is a little terrifying when you imagine them shuffling toward you while you debate whether to risk a brush stroke on a exposed bone or simply blast them with a firecracker of a gun. This is not your average undead; this is undead with personality and posture.

The sculpts feature strong silhouettes. Edouard favors elongated limbs, dramatic angles, and sometimes a hint of haute couture in the apparel. You will notice long coats that seem to billow even when there is no wind on the board, and hats that tilt just enough to say I am stylish even as the jawbone wants your brain. The weapon designs range from classic blades to improvised tools that feel ripped from a postapocalyptic catalog. These choices do impact the table visually and add a layer of storytelling to each encounter. The cards you draw will often reference the mood of the mini you are fighting as well as the tactical dynamics, because Edouard’s minis were designed with both aesthetics and function in mind.

From a sculpting perspective, the work is crisp. The undercuts are clean enough for injection molding, and the hands are posed in ways that make it plausible to attach pretty much any weapon without too much drilling or filing. If you are a painter who loves to highlight facial expressions, this collection provides a treasure trove of micro-expressions to practice shading on. The faces can be a little dramatic, which is a feature, not a flaw, because it makes the characters easy to read at a gaming distance and gives you something to talk about during a long round of skirmish combat.

In terms of scale, the minis align with the standard Zombicide bases. If you want to mix them with older sets, there should be no shocking misalignment or base-size surprises. They sit, stand, and pose the same way as the rest of the gang, which means you can drop them into your next campaign and watch them hold their own in both appearance and gameplay. The only caveat here is that if you use them as a focal point for cinematic shots, you might find yourself trying to craft a story around why the zombie horde looks so dapper this time around. For those curious about materials, Zombicide miniatures are generally plastic and easy to assemble. There is no need to wield a Dremel to fix an ankle, and the gluing process is relatively forgiving. The Edouard Guiton line sticks to that tradition, with clean mold lines and a little extra care given to the hands and faces to preserve expression after priming and painting. ## Painting tips and practical hobby notes

- Start with a solid black primer. The darker base makes the highlights pop and gives you a strong foundation for the facial tones that Edouard’s minis shine with.
- Use a mid-tone color for the coats and clothes, then layer lighter edge highlights on raised details. The result looks cinematic without being too fiddly for a one-night painting session.
- Wash or shade deep recesses to bring out texture on the coats and faces. A brown wash on leather and a blue wash on denim can make the clothing look worn without overwhelming the sculpt.
- For metallics, dry brush silver on raised areas and seal with a matte coat. You can add a subtle rust wash for a weathered look on the improvised weapons.
- Basing is where you can really tell a story. Consider resin rubble, broken boards, and a touch of static grass to evoke a derelict setting. A tiny touch of red tufts can hint at recent action without looking cartoonish.
