## Character deep dives: eight archetypes in practice

#### The Fortune Teller
This figure embodies fate as a living force rather than a passive concept. The Fortune Teller is the go-to for guiding the party toward a choice that seems inevitable. In play, use them to foreshadow a twist that players can either embrace or resist. The figure makes a strong visual anchor for a scene in a crowded club or a back-alley meeting where fortunes are exchanged as easily as secrets. In terms of narrative juice, the Fortune Teller shines when a player bets on a destiny and learns that sometimes destiny needs a strong counter-story to keep the city honest.

#### The Clockmaker
A master of time and logistics, the Clockmaker is perfect for plotlines that rely on sequence and cause-and-effect. Their presence can help your players track down a deadline or reconstruct a timeline that reveals a villainous plan. If you want to push a tense moment, have the Clockmaker trigger a countdown or reveal a critical, time-based clue that requires a split-second decision from the party. The aesthetics of gears, cogs, and a tailored pocket watch can spark visual storytelling that makes time feel like another character in the room.

#### The Velvet Ghost
The Velvet Ghost is all about memory and presence without being fully corporeal. They lend a spectral luxury to a scene, perhaps appearing in a smoky club as a reminder of a long-past heist or a betrayal that still haunts the city. Using the Velvet Ghost as a memory anchor can create a multi-layered scene where players must decide whether a memory is a truth worth clinging to or a weapon others can use against them.

#### The Streetlamp Widow
This figure embodies guardianship and vigilance. The Streetlamp Widow can serve as a moral compass for your party, a person who knows the citys quiet corners and the costs of keeping secrets. In a session, position this figure at the edge of a confrontation to highlight who deserves protection and who is willing to pay the price for a hidden truth to remain buried beneath streetlight glow.

#### The Iron Locket
A relic with a secret, the Iron Locket is perfect for a mystery that depends on a single artifact. Its history can intersect with the party members own stories, allowing you to explore themes of memory, possession, and the weight of objects that carry histories heavier than their weight would imply. Use the locket to connect distant plot threads and force players to decide whether to unlock a door that might unleash more than they bargained for.

#### The Paper King
A strategist who writes fate on napkins and flyers, the Paper King translates information into leverage. Their presence on the board is a reminder that information is power and that the citys rumor mill is a weapon as sharp as a blade. In play, let the Paper King shape the flow of a plan by manipulating what information the party receives, sowing distrust, or highlighting a hidden ally who could tip the scales in a crucial moment.

#### The Gardener
The Gardener cultivates truth and rumor alike. They are excellent at turning a mundane street into a microcosm of the citys psyche. Use the Gardener to grow a subplot about what your party is chasing and why; their manipulation of soil-and-seed metaphors can yield a memorable scene in which the party must prune a dangerous conspiracy without destroying the citys fragile balance.

#### The Shadow-Mage
A master of perception and obscurity, the Shadow-Mage thrives in the moment when players realize that not all is as it seems. They can cast doubt on a key clue, forcing the party to examine every assumption they have made. In game terms, use the Shadow-Mage to introduce a false lead that challenges the party to prove a theory through roleplay, not just dice.
