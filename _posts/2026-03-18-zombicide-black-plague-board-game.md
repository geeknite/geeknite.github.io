## How to Play in a Nutshell (The Long Version You Won’t Regret Reading)
This is a cooperative affair where the group shares a common goal: survive long enough to complete a scenario. The game runs in turns alternating between players and the zombie horde. Here is the fuller flow, with a few extra bits to keep you honest:

- Setup: Build the map using the scenario’s tile layout. Place zombie spawn points and doom tokens as indicated. Each player selects a hero, grabs a weapon and equipment cards, and readies for doom, which is not the calendar app you forgot to update.
- Action phase: On your turn you have a pool of actions. Move, search for loot, pick up items, attack zombies, open doors, or perform special actions from your gear. Four actions per turn is the baseline, which means you are either a mastermind tactician or a walking chaos cannon depending on your dice luck.
- Zombie phase: After everyone has acted, the zombie monsters activate. They move toward the nearest noise or survivor and attack when they can. They do not have infinite patience with stealthy heroes; they want brains now, please. When they attack, you roll a number of six-sided dice; the results determine bites, blocks, and the occasional heroic shrug from a hero who forgot to bring an extra weapon. The dice feel ordinary, but the stakes feel epic enough to justify a dramatic dance of near misses.
- End of round: Rinse and repeat until the scenario objective is achieved or everyone dies in spectacular fashion. The undead may win by sheer momentum, but the table wins when a ragtag group of friends pulls off a last stand that would make a tavern brawl look like a silent meditation.

The rulebook is concise and friendly, a good balance of clarity and tactile chaos. It is not lightweight; it rewards players who track spawn points, lines of sight, and the occasional card that modulates enemy behavior. It rewards a learning curve that pays off with coordinated team plays and moments when your squad actually pulls off a plan that resembles strategy more than pure luck. Expect a few misplays early on, but celebrate the moments when you finally sync your actions to sling a zombie into a trap or push a survivor through a doorway just before a doom card detonates the alley in a dramatic explosion of green mist.

For players who want to push it up a notch, there are expansions and variants that add new heroes, monsters, and objectives. They can complicate the decision space in delightful ways and reduce the repetition that settles into long campaigns. If you are a completionist or just hungry for more chaos hours, these options are a gift that keeps on giving … until it doesn’t, which is usually the moment you realize you desperately need to recalibrate your dice strategy.

## Setup Time, Pacing, and Keeping Your Sanity Intact
You always wonder how long from box to first turn. In our experience, a standard four‑player match takes roughly 15 to 30 minutes to set up, depending on how familiar everyone is with the rule book and how aggressively you want to shuffle loot. The actual playtime per scenario typically lands around 60 to 120 minutes, though a late-night chaos session can stretch longer. If you’re teaching new players, budget extra time for a few learning rounds and a healthy debate about whether to rescue a lone survivor or secure loot from a doom track surge.

The pacing is excellent once the group learns a few tactical phrases and starts planning two turns ahead instead of one. Expect dramatic swings; that is part of the charm and also the reason your nerves end up jangling like a keyboard left on a pizza scented desk. A few pro tips: pre-sort loot into labeled bins, pre-assemble terrain where possible, and appoint a loot goblin to manage the cards so players can focus on the on-table action. A small tray for zombie miniatures helps too; nothing kills momentum like a shoelace of figures on the floor when the horde surges into your tavern.

If you want to baby-step future sessions toward maximum momentum, try a little habit discipline: keep a consistent turn order, build a shared vocabulary for line of sight and range, and never forget to laugh when a zombie lurches into a hero who just rolled three blanks in a row. The table can handle it; the zombies can’t.
