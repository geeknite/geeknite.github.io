The 650 W class is not a full-on bone saw for heavy-duty industrial use, but it handles frequent bone cuts in small batches with grace and a hint of defiance.

## Maintenance and Longevity
### Blade swaps and spare parts
Stock a couple of extra blades with varying tooth geometry. If you cut through bone regularly, consider blades rated for bone and tougher tasks. The blade tensioning system should be adjusted according to the blade width; too tight can cause guide wear, too loose leads to wandering cuts. Keep a small toolkit handy for on-site blade swaps and a spare motor fuse if your area experiences power fluctuations.

### Cleaning routine
Always unplug before cleaning. Use water and mild detergent, wipe dry, and allow to air dry. Avoid abrasive cleaners that can scratch the surface. For the drive belt and bearings, apply a light food-grade lubricant per the manufacturer’s guidelines. Keeping a routine will extend the machine’s life and keep your cuts clean and consistent. A thorough monthly breakdown for inspection of bearings, seals, and tension springs is a good practice in busy environments.

### Long-term storage and off-season tips
If you store the machine for a period, apply a light coat of food-grade oil to exposed metal parts to prevent rust. Cover the machine to protect it from dust and humidity. Periodically reinstall parts and rotate the blade to ensure uniform wear. A simple snowball of routines prevents unexpected surprises when you bring the machine back into service.

### Common issues and troubleshooting
If you notice jerky motion, check the blade alignment and carriage guides. If the motor is overheating, verify that the vent is unobstructed and that the power supply is delivering consistent voltage. A tripped safety switch means there was a moment of overstrain; reset safely and check the feed. Unusual noises can indicate misalignment, worn bearings, or a loose blade; stop immediately and assess before continuing to avoid damage.
