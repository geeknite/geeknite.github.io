## What is Codenames? A Quick Rewind for the Overwhelmed

For the uninitiated, Codenames is a word game that uses a grid of secret words. Two rival teams, each guided by a spymaster, try to deduce their agents by giving one-word clues that tie together multiple words on the table. The catch? The assassin word sits among them, and revealing it ends the game poorly for your team. The objective is to identify all your agents before the other team does. Simple concept, complex execution, and an astonishingly broad vocabulary test wrapped in a social hour that can devolve into a debate about whether pineapple belongs on pizza or in clue servers.

## Gameplay Overview: The Quick Playthrough

Codenames revolves around a grid of 25 word cards. Each card bears a word that may evoke all sorts of associations—a bank, a piano, a dragon, a marshmallow. The red and blue teams compete to guess their words, guided by a spymaster who knows which words belong to which team. The spymaster provides clues in the form of a single word and a number, which indicates how many words on the board connect to that clue. For example: “Animal, 2” could connect to Dog and Cat, assuming those words are on the board and belong to your team.

The catch? Not all words are friendly to your goals. There’s the assassin word, which ends the game in a loss if guessed, and there are neutral words that don’t help either side. The blue team might have to guess their words while the red team tries to steer them away from danger. The tension is palpable because a single misstep can swing the entire board and create a cascade of “why did you say that?” moments that last for the rest of the session.

### Turn Flow
- Spymaster examines the board and selects a clue word and a number that best ties together their team’s words while avoiding the other team’s words and the assassin.
- The field operatives (guessers) interpret the clue and select cards, one at a time, from the 25 on the board.
- If a guess is correct, the card is covered by the team’s color; if wrong, it can give advantage to the other team or end the turn depending on the card color.
- The round ends when either side has collected all their agents, or when a misstep reveals the assassin word (instant loss for the team that chose it).

The experience is a careful blend of linguistic acrobatics, social deduction, and the occasional lived-in-grammar moment where someone compounds a clue into a hilariously confusing sentence. When played with a group that enjoys puns and pop culture references, Codenames becomes a theatrical performance where everyone is improvising a story around 25 words. And because the game scales with the number of players, you can easily fit it into a party of five or stretch it to a larger group with rotating spymasters and fresh clues.

## Strategy Tips for First-Time Playthroughs

- Start with a simple two-word clue when introducing the game to new players. You don’t want to overwhelm them with a 4-3 clue that sounds like a cryptic crossword clue.
- Choose clues that link words thematically rather than trying to force an elaborate narrative. A well-chosen single word, like “Music, 3,” may be more effective than a trailing sentence that leaves everyone staring at you in confusion.
- Pay attention to tone and pacing. The best clues come from a spymaster who can time their reveal so that the group remains engaged, not paralyzed by analysis paralysis.
- Watch for cognitive biases. Your teammates will default to words they personally associate with, which means your clue might be interpreted through a personal lens rather than the board’s material reality.
- Don’t worry about perfect accuracy. The best moments come from the collective misdirection that makes everyone giggle and misinterpret the clue, ultimately learning a new vernacular of wordplay in the process.
