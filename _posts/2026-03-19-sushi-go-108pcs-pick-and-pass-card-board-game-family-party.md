## Overview
Sushi Go is a card-drafting game that plays in short order and scales nicely from two to five players (six with the expansion). The goal is to assemble the best possible platter of sushi combinations by drafting cards as they “go” around the table. Each round feels like speed-dating with a conveyor belt—everyone tries to pick the sweet combos while the rest of the table swerves to pick the spicy options before you can blink.

The 108 cards are divided into several sushi types: nigiri (miso majesty), maki rolls (stackable bragging rights), tempura (the crunchy bragging rights), sashimi (the high-risk, high-reward), and the ever-elusive pudding (the tiebreaker in late-game drama). There are also chopsticks and special cards that spice up the flow. The game does away with dice and heavy setup; you deal out a hand, everyone looks at their cards, then you pick one and pass the rest to your left (or right, depending on the round). Rinse and repeat for a few rounds, tally scores, and boom—dine-and-draft victory.

This 108-card edition is the “full” experience without the need for multiple expansions. It’s designed to be compact enough to slip into a backpack, or a grocery-store tote (you know that tote that has seen more adventures than a caffeinated raccoon). The theme is cute, the mechanics accessible, and the humor is gentle enough to avoid an emergency parental briefing about how to pronounce nigiri without sounding like a cartoon chef.

The inclusion of special cards, like chopsticks, introduces tactical choices—do you reserve the chopsticks for a future turn to maximize your card count, or use it for a quick sabotage of someone else’s plan? It’s a delicate dance of misdirection, memory, and occasional glee when someone misreads your hand and bonuses.

## How to Play: A Quick-Start Guide
1) Deal the entire deck (or a pre-determined portion for short rounds) into hands equal to the number of players. The game is designed to be fast, so don’t overthink the distribution.
2) Each player looks at their hand, selects one card to keep, and passes the rest to the neighbor. The goal is to assemble the most valuable sushi set combinations by the end of the round.
3) You reveal chosen cards and keep them face-up in front of you. As rounds progress, you’ll be gathering sets, such as a bundle of maki rolls for the big points or a collection of nigiri for consistent scoring. The exact scoring rules are printed on the cards and are easy to track, which is essential when you’re trying to keep a cheerful vibe rather than a math conference vibe.
4) After all cards have been taken, tally points. Some rounds are punctuated by pudding—if your pudding count is not enough, you may be in for a bitter, pudding-less realization. The pudding cards are the quirky late-game tiebreakers and often swing the final scores by a handful of points.
5) Repeat for three rounds (or more, if you’re chasing a longer session). The rounds bleed into one another in a satisfying rhythm.

If you’re curious about the precise scoring, you can check official rules and diagrams on the official site and BoardGameGeek’s community pages. But the gist is simple: collect sets that maximize your point yield, claim the occasional risk-reward thrills, and avoid leaving too many pudding cards in your wake.

## Mechanics: Why the Pick and Pass Ring Works
The core mechanic is drafting with passing. You pick one card from your hand, then pass the rest to the left. Simplicity is the engine: players learn quickly, but the decision space remains interesting because you must anticipate what your neighbors are likely to do. That’s the sweet-spot of social tabletop design: enough information to be strategic, but not so much you need a spreadsheet and a chair for your calculator.

The Chopsticks card adds a layer of planning. It’s a built-in “save for later” mechanic that makes people consider the timing of their drafts. Do you bite on a risky but high-scoring piece now, hoping to snag a better combo later with the two-card save you’ll draw on your next turn? These micro-decisions give the game its tension without introducing heavy rule burden.

The 108-card count ensures variety across plays. You’ll see the same card types, but never the exact same distribution, which helps prevent the “we always go for the same two sets” problem that can crop up in lighter games. It’s a small miracle of packaging: a lot of content in a pocket-friendly box.

## Strategy Notes: Getting the Most Out of Your 108 Cards
- Early game focus: Build a flexible plan. Don’t commit to a single sushi type too early. The first few rounds are your chance to feel out what others are collecting and adjust.
- Pudding dynamics: In many games, pudding is a late-round swing. If you’re closing in on the end, weigh whether grabbing a couple of surefire points now is worth sacrificing potential pudding booster values later. The pudding cards can be the difference between a modest win and a small avalanche of dramatic points.
- Pattern recognition: Track what you see passing around the table. If you notice that several players are chasing maki rolls, you might pivot to nigiri or tempura to diversify your scoring. The beauty of Sushi Go lies in its rapid inference opportunities—your brain gets to do a fun little mental math dance while you pretend to be innocent about your plan.
- Endgame pressure: In the last rounds, a well-timed chopsticks play can break open a victory. If you’re holding Chopsticks, consider saving it for a moment when you can snag two high-value cards instead of one mediocre one. It’s like a strategic punt—save your energy for the moment that matters most.
