## Layout and Space Planning: 10 Desks, 1 Clear Path to Sanity

### The classic 2x5 grid vs. alternative configurations

The two-row, five-column grid is the default hero layout. It offers an even distribution of desk-length across a shared visual axis, which makes it easier for the team to communicate while maintaining a degree of personal space. But the beauty of the WTE Black system is its adaptability. If your floorplan is constrained by beams, pillars, or oddly shaped rooms, you can reconfigure into a U-shape for collaborative sessions or a straight line for a more corporate, formal vibe.

If you’re aiming for warm collaboration with semi-private pods, you can drop a meditation zone or a small whiteboard island in the middle, surrounded by a few “quiet zone” desks. The key is to maintain consistent desk heights and monitor alignment, so no one’s neck does that awkward Twitch™ when they switch from code to design in a single glance.

### Ergonomic alignment: monitor height and chair symmetry

A good setup places the monitor tops roughly at eye level, about 2–3 inches below eye height for most folks, with the monitors spaced to avoid glare from overhead lighting. The WTE frames accommodate monitor arms and stands that let you fine-tune tilt and height without requiring a degree in rocket science.

Chair choice matters more than the desk itself in terms of comfort. When you scale to 10 seats, cheap chairs become a recurring cost—both in dollars and in the daily morale of your team. We recommend a breathable mesh back with adjustable lumbar support and a seat depth that accommodates your team’s range of leg lengths. Pair with a proper footrest for those who discover, mid-quarter, that standing 8 hours is a fantasy their knees won’t share.

### Lighting and screens: avoiding the glare trap

Good lighting should be a mix of ambient and task lighting. In the WTE setup, you’ll want under-desk or cable-integrated LED strips along the outer edge, and a few adjustable desk lamps for the far ends of the row. This helps with color-critical tasks and reduces the eyestrain that arises when someone on the far side of the table is staring into a glossy monitor while someone else is debugging at 3am.

You’ll also want to manage screen glare. If your office has big glass walls, consider anti-glare monitor films and blinds that can be controlled in the same breath as the desk height adjustments. The better you handle lighting, the less you’ll hear about “eye fatigue” becoming the new office buzzword.


## Cable Management and Power: The Hidden Hero

### Pathways that actually work

A 10-person desk setup can turn chaos into a symphony when you implement a robust cable-tray strategy. The WTE system typically ships with integrated paths and port channels that let you run power, data, and auxiliary cables with minimal cross-over. The real magic is in the easy-to-access grommets that let you route cables to a central hub without needing a forklift license to reposition a desk.

Cable anchors should be placed near each bandwidth-heavy device: laptops, docking stations, printers, and conference cameras. If you’re running a video conference in the same room, you’ll want a dedicated, centralized power block with surge protection and a UPS option to protect sessions from a micro-outage.

### Power distribution and safety

Most setups include a centralized power strip or a row of them that feed through the cable trays. The safety angle here is important: avoid daisy-chaining power strips across multiple desks. The WTE philosophy leans toward modular, ceiling-mounted or wall-mounted power sources so you don’t have to chase a tangle of cords under every chair. If your client insists on under-desk power, ensure you have anti-pinch cable channels and keep human traffic paths clear to prevent accidents.

### Data connectivity and network layout

A reliable network backbone is essential for a 10-seat configuration. This means high-quality Ethernet drops or Wi-Fi access points optimized for dense environments. The WTE kit isn’t a network switch, but it does provide clean routes for data cables to meet a central switch or a small server room. If your workspace relies on conference calls, make sure you pre-wire a conference A/V line to two or three central desks to reduce voice echo and jitter during meetings.


## Ergonomics and Comfort: The Human Factor

### Chairs that ship with dignity

Chairs are where many office setups either pay for comfort or lose the plot entirely. The WTE system pairs well with chairs that offer adjustable lumbar support, seat height, seat depth, and armrest width. For a 10-person office, you’re talking a mix of body types and preferences; the best approach is to provide a chair with a wide adjustment range and a default position that favors neutral posture.

### Monitor arms: range and reliability

You’ll want monitor arms that support at least 180 degrees of horizontal reach and 15–25 inches of vertical adjustment. The more people at the table, the more you’ll appreciate arms with quick-release mechanisms for team exercises or reconfigurations. Steel arms with a damped tilt feel like a luxury in a busy office but make a real difference when people switch between coding, designing, and video calls.

### Keyboard and mouse: aligning for comfort

Consider split keyboards or tented grips for those who experience wrist strain. A shared keyboard tray can reduce surface clutter while ensuring that the keyboard remains perpendicular to the monitor. The same applies to mice—opt for ergonomic, multi-button options with easy thumb rest. The goal is to minimize pronation angle and keep wrists neutral.


## Installation Process: From Box to Boardroom

### Planning stage

Before unpacking the boxes, map out your layout. Mark the “power drop zones,” “data line routes,” and the “quiet corners” that will host team members who need to concentrate without the nation-wide ping of Slack. A good plan includes a floor plan with scale, a bill of materials, and a roll-out timeline that doesn’t turn the project into a sitcom about contractor delays.

### Assembly steps

1. Unbox the modules and sort them by type (desks, legs, cable trays, panels).
2. Assemble the main desk frames in a controlled area. Tighten bolts to manufacturer torque specs and avoid overtightening, which is a fancy way to strip threads and ruin a Friday afternoon.
3. Install cable trays and grommets. Route power and data as you go; don’t wait until the tables are stacked with monitors to realize you forgot a trunk line.
4. Place desks according to your layout plan. Ensure legs are level and that the rows align. Measure diagonals to confirm there isn’t a stealth misalignment that causes one row to seem like it’s sloping toward the door in a “we’re not sure” sort of way.
5. Install monitor arms, keyboards, chairs, and any whiteboards or collaboration pods in the center. Calibrate monitor height and alignment per user height.
6. Test-run a day in the life: printers jam, calls happen, people stand up in the middle of the meeting to stretch—make sure your layout accommodates the workflow, not just the furniture showroom vibe.

### Post-install checks

Do a walk-through to ensure there are no loose cables, no pinch points in the leg areas, and that all monitors show up correctly in display settings. A quick test user session can reveal glare hotspots and poor lighting zones that you didn’t anticipate.
