## Introduction

Long before Mega Evolutions and GX attacks, there was a time when the Pokemon Trading Card Game was all about basic strategy, pristine art, and the satisfying crunch of opening a fresh pack that smelled like cardboard dreams. The Jungle expansion arrived as a curious sibling to Base Set, bringing a splash of rainforest color to the original card mosaic and a batch of critters that would become memory lane fixtures for players who grew up with a plastic binder full of wonder. Among these, Gloom stands out not just for its droopy charm but for the way it echoes the early days of the game: simple mechanics, big personality, and a dash of botanical drama.

In this English Jungle edition, you might see Gloom labeled as card number 37 out of 64 in some printings or as the grand finale of the set at 64/64 in others. The exact numbering can vary by printing, but the character remains the same: a basic Grass-type Pokemon that invites you to think in terms of stages, bench management, and the classic synergy with its evolution step, Vileplume. This review will take you on a journey through the card's design, its gameplay role in Jungle-era decks, its art and flavor, and why this particular Gloom is more than just a nostalgic fetch in your binder—it’s a window into how the early days of the TCG taught players to think like strategists and collectors alike.

## Card Details and First Impressions

In the Jungle edition, Gloom takes the familiar form of a Stage 1 Grass-type Pokemon. It evolves from Oddish, delivering a classic evolutionary arc that players learned to respect in the pre-GX era. The art, as with many Jungle cards, leans toward bold color, a rustic font, and a background that looks like it was torn from the edge of a vivid rainforest postcard. The silhouette of Gloom is instantly recognizable: droopy purple head, a tiny tuft of leaves, and that telltale flower crown that signals the paradox of a Pokemon whose fragrance is both alluring and, well, a little poisonous if you squint your eyes just right.

From a gameplay perspective, Jungle Gloom operates at a weight class you’d expect from a 1999-2000 card: simple, reliable, and fond of pairing with a larger, more intimidating final boss—in this case, Vileplume. Gloom’s energy requirements are straightforward: you’ll typically be building up Grass energy to power Gloom’s attacks and to prepare for the Vileplume transition that would turn your bench into a flower-powered fortress in the right deck list. The economics of the card revolve around two core concepts that every vintage player appreciates: tempo and danger. Tempo, because you want to get Gloom up and running quickly to start pressuring your opponent and building towards the big payoff on the evolution, and danger because field control at that time hinged on few tricks—mostly pure card advantage and the occasional stall tactic.

Gloom is part of a larger ecosystem that rewards planning and bench management. You don’t want to rely on Gloom as your end game finisher; you want to use it as a stepping stone—your stepping stone to Vileplume, and from there to the more monstrous cards that roared into the deck-building scene in later sets. The card’s strength lies not in dramatic one-turn KO potential but in its reliability and the creative ways you can weave it into a deck that aims to outlast opponents who rely on brute force alone.

### The Artwork and Flavor

Art in the Jungle set is a fantastic snapshot of the era’s aesthetic: bold, punchy colors, sturdy silhouettes, and a sense of motion that says, This is a card you can play with gusto. Gloom’s art, in particular, captures the juxtaposition that the character embodies in the games—a plant-like Pokemon that survives by exuding a potent fragrance. That fragrance doubles as a metaphor for the TCG itself: a fragrance that attracts the curious collector and lures them into a chase for the perfect print, a chase that sometimes ends with a binder full of near-miss cards and the warm glow of memories.

In terms of layout, Gloom sits comfortably with the bulk of Jungle’s cards. It’s easy to read, the typeface remains legible even when a memory-laden binder is pressed against it, and the illustration fills a good amount of the card face without overwhelming the essential information the card carries. The old-school energy of the border, the pale text box, and the faint dotted pattern are all signs of a time when the TCG’s visual language was still discovering itself—the clumsy yet charming infancy that made the hobby feel like a treasure hunt rather than a structured sport.

## Print Run Realities: 37/64 vs 64/64

One of the most common questions about Jungle Gloom is about card numbering. Jungle’s printing history is a little tangled, because early print runs in English used both 37/64 and 64/64 for different reasons. In some print runs you’ll find Gloom labeled as 37/64, which places it roughly in the middle of the set’s lineup, while other printing variants treat Gloom as the closing 64/64. The effect is more than a trivia footnote: it changes how you value and categorize your cards when you’re building a collection or evaluating a set’s completeness.

If you’re chasing the complete Jungle set, here are a few practical takeaways:
- Check the bottom-right corner for the number fraction. 37/64 indicates the card’s position in that particular run; 64/64 is the final card in the set for some printings. Don’t rely solely on the number—have a look at the holo patterns and the set symbol to confirm.
- The holo pattern varies by printer. Some Jungle sets feature holo variants in the same way that Base Set did, though the Jungle line often prioritized the non holo print for many common cards. If you’re after rare variants, you’ll want to verify the holo version and the card’s set symbol carefully.
- Condition is king. In older sets, edge wear, whitening, and centering were the biggest culprits to a card’s price, often more so than the difference between 37/64 and 64/64. A pristine Gloom is a dream in a binder and a talk of the town among a vintage community that still exchanges stories about centering disasters and glossy smiles.

For those who like to nerd out about details, I strongly recommend visiting your preferred database and searching for Jungle Gloom with both numbers to see every printing’s subtle differences. If you want to see an image of the card as printed, check the Jungle card gallery—one of the best ways to visually compare the different printings is to line up the images and study the set symbol, energy cost, and the card’s text box.

Industry tip: if you’re building a nostalgic deck around Gloom and Vileplume, consider using a 37/64 Gloom for a mid-set feel and a 64/64 Gloom as a collector’s centerpiece. The two can coexist in a collection, and the difference in numbers often makes for a fun conversation with fellow collectors who enjoy the little quirks of vintage printing.

### Holo vs Non-Holo: The Aesthetic Debate

While Jungle did not make every card holo, the holo versions are the ones that catch the eye in a binder under a desk lamp. A holo Gloom in good condition can be great to look at and adds a little sparkle to an already charming card. The non-holo Gloom is still elegant, with its own understated charm. For many collectors, the holo print is a signpost of investment potential, but the non-holo prints have their own appeal—their rarity is often defined by print runs rather than by flashy holo finishes. Either way, Gloom remains a reliable piece of the Jungle puzzle: a card that’s easy to understand, easy to appreciate, and a staple in a well-rounded nostalgia deck.

## Gameplay and Strategy: How Gloom Fits into the Jungle Era

Gloom’s core identity is simple: evolve from Oddish, provide steady Grass-energy-based power, and set you up for greater things—namely Vileplume. In practice, that means you’ll be thinking about the bench more than most players in modern decks with their dozens of evolving lines and niche techs. In the Jungle era, you were often playing with fewer tools and fewer options, so the decision about when to bring Gloom into the active position was crucial. You wanted to avoid getting stuck with a “dead” Gloom in your hand and you wanted to maximize your deck’s tempo by ensuring you could hit your evolution on a predictable schedule.

Two things to consider when building around Gloom:
- Early pressure and mid-game transformation. You want to bring Gloom into play at a point where you can accelerate into a stronger stage 2 with as little downtime as possible. The goal is to keep your opponent from stabilizing their own board, while you prepare your own evolution line.
- Bench management and head-to-head matchup dynamics. Because Jungle decks typically have fewer options, you’ll want to maximize your bench’s utility. Gloom acts as a stepping stone, allowing you to hold onto other basic Pokemon or to set the stage for a larger strategy that relies on your evolving arsenal.

In practice, a Gloom-focused deck might emphasize cycling through a few key trainers and energies to ensure that you can hit your evolution curve consistently. You’ll also want to consider the ways in which your opponent’s deck tries to disrupt you—discard effects, energy denial, and the occasional paralysis move. Gumption is the name of the game here; with Gloom, you’re not looking for a one-turn knockout, you’re looking for a dependable plan that outlasts your opponent’s tempo.

### The Big Picture: Gloom and the Jungle Universe

Gloom is not the star player in most Jungle decks; that role belongs to the larger evolutionary chain, especially Vileplume. But Gloom’s contribution is crucial: it provides the energy you need to power future plays, gives you a steady presence on the bench, and offers a sense of forward momentum. The card is a reminder that in the earliest days of the TCG, a well-timed evolution could change the entire tempo of a match, and that the journey from Oddish to Gloom to Vileplume was as much about strategy as it was about collecting the right pieces.

## Collectibility, Value, and Print Run Realities

From a collector’s eye, Gloom in Jungle is a reliable staple. It’s not the rarest gem in the coin-op world, but its position in the Jungle lineup—whether you’re looking at 37/64 or 64/64—offers a nice balance between accessibility and nostalgia. The value is driven by several factors:
- Condition: The difference between a near-mint and a heavily played Gloom matters a lot. The early print lines show wear around the corners and edges with some whitening; if you’re chasing a pristine example, you’ll pay a premium for mint centering and sharp corners.
- Print variant: holo vs non-holo, 37/64 vs 64/64, and even minor font differences can create a little delta in price. Some collectors chase these small variances with a feverish joy that would make a scientist proud.
- Popularity: Nostalgia for Jungle cards is real; as the 1990s TCG memory becomes a shared story among older and younger players, Jungle Gloom becomes part of a broader conversation about where the game started and how far it has come.

If you’re new to the hobby or returning after a long time, a Gloom card can be a gateway to a broader Jungle collection. The market for vintage cards fluctuates with the seasons, with grading results often turning a card from casual buy to showpiece. It’s not uncommon to find a well-kept 37/64 Gloom priced lower than a higher-numbered holo variant of a different card, simply due to supply and demand dynamics that are as human as the hobby itself.

### Rarity and Grading Realities

Grading can be a game-changer for a Jungle Gloom. A PSA 8 or 9 can elevate a card from casual meme to showpiece status. A rounded corner, a crisp edge, and the absence of heavy scratches in the holo pattern can transform a card into a collectible you’re proud to display. The grading journey is rarely a straight line, but when you get that 9.5, you’ll know why the hobby is so passionate about the smallest details. If you plan to invest in such a card, do your due diligence: verify centering, confirm there are no heavy scratches in the holo pattern, and ensure you’re dealing with a reputable seller.

### How to Identify Variants and Hunt for Completeness

For collectors chasing the Jungle edition, it’s helpful to have a map of the variants. The presence of 37/64 versus 64/64 is a relatively simple clue, but you’ll want to cross-check with the set symbol and the year imprint to ensure you’ve got the right print. Some databases maintain side-by-side comparisons and images that make this process straightforward. You may also consider opening a small checklist in your binder: one column for 37/64, another for 64/64, and a third for holo vs non-holo. That way you can quickly glance at your Gloom card and know exactly where it sits in the Jungle lineage.
