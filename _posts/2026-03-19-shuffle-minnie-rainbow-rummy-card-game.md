## How to Play: The Rainbow Rummy Routine
### Setup and Components
Two to four players can enjoy Minnie’s rainbow shenanigans, though the manufacturer nudges you toward three or four to maximize interaction. The box includes a full deck of rainbow-coded cards, a handful of scoring markers (tiny discs or chits), and a quick reference card. The quick reference is a godsend for beginners, offering a crisp outline of the turn flow: draw, meld, discard. The presence of a reliable cheat sheet reduces the barrier to entry that often stops families mid-round with endless debates over “is this move allowed?” 

### Turn Structure: A Light, Snappy Rhythm
A turn plays out like this: draw one card, optionally lay down a set or run (if you’ve got the right color-coded combination), then discard one card to end your turn. If you can’t or don’t want to meld, you simply draw and discard as normal. The rainbow mechanic adds a subtle puzzle: the way colors align across your hand can unlock more strategic melds, so you’re constantly evaluating not just what you can lay down but what you’re leaving for the next player. The pace is brisk, though not breakneck; there’s enough downtime to plan a cunning move and enough pace to prevent the game from turning into a long, silent stalemate.

### Runs, Sets, and Rainbow Economics
In classic rummy, you aim to form sets (same rank, different suits) or runs (consecutive cards of the same suit). Minnie Rainbow Rummy reinterprets this with two primary color families that behave like suits and a rainbow wildcard that occasionally rotates, adding a surprising element of luck without tipping into chaos. The color-coded approach helps new players quickly grasp the concept of melds, while experienced players can exploit color adjacency to squeeze out extra points. 

A notable design choice is the scoring economy. Instead of counting every die-and-drill like you would in a heavier game, this version uses a straightforward point tally for unmelded cards at the end of each round. This keeps the game lean and accessible, ensuring that even if a round doesn’t go your way, the loss is relatively small and quickly recoverable. It’s a clever balancing act between rewarding smart play and avoiding punishment theater that can plague lighter games.

### The Rainbow Run: A Thematic Twist
One clever mechanic is the rainbow run, a special meld that can only be formed if you have a complete rainbow sequence in a given color family. Completing a rainbow run grants a minor bonus, encouraging players to chase longer, more challenging melds. The payoff isn’t overbearing, but it adds a satisfying crescendo to late-game decisions. The psychology here is simple: it nudges players toward action rather than perpetual waiting, which is ideal for groups that drift toward analysis paralysis when there are more than two choices on the table.

### Endgame and Flow: When the Rainbow Fades
As the deck thins, tensions rise with the same arc as a good sitcom finale. Players scramble to reduce their hand, the pressure to meld intensifies, and the table becomes a chorus of strategic whispers and friendly banter. The game typically ends when a player goes out or when the draw pile is exhausted, whichever comes first. In our playthrough, the pacing proved resilient across player counts; the interaction remained lively, and the variety of melds kept every round feeling distinct rather than repetitive.
