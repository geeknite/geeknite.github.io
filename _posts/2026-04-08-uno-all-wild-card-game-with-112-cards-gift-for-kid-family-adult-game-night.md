## How to Play: Core Rules and the All Wild Twist

UNO remains the anchor of casual gaming: match colors, play numbers in sequence, and use special cards to alter the flow. The All Wild Card edition expands that world with a few notable twists:

- Wild cards: The standard Wild and Wild Draw Four remain, but you’ll encounter additional Wild variants. Some let you declare color and also impose a penalty on opponents; others allow a “color-lock” for a single turn, which means you can set a color and force others to react.
- All Wild cards: A key addition. When a player plays an All Wild, they can switch to any color with a single card. It’s a dramatic moment—perfect for a social media-worthy declaration and a chance to recover from a rough stretch.
- Wild plus rule cards: A new category that combines color changes with a special action. You may find yourself drawing extra cards, skipping an opponent, or re-shifting the game’s stats in unpredictable ways.
- Draw mechanics: Expect more moments where players draw a few extra cards—not just from the opponent’s hand, but from the entire deck’s mood. The bigger deck adds more room for strategic gambits and, frankly, more opportunities to misplay if you haven’t got your wits about you.

If you are teaching kids or new players, you’ll likely want to establish a simple house rule: clarify how many Wild cards can be used in a single turn, and whether players must declare color before playing a Wild. It’s common sense to set expectations (and to save the group from a heated debate about “Is this really legal?” mid-game).

Hint: The All Wild Card mechanic shines in two-player and small-group games when you want to flip the tide in one decisive move. In larger groups, the chaos multiplies, which is perfect for a lively party but can slow down the pace if you’re chasing a tight clock.
