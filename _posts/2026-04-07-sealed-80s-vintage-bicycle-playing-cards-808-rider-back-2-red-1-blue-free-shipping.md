## What makes the 808 Rider Back special?

The 808 Rider Back is a classic Bicycle design that collectors swear by. The Rider Back refers to a particular more ornate back design that appeared in mid-20th century Bicycle cards and persisted into the 1980s. The 808 variant is one of those widely distributed releases that fans identify by the digits stamped on packaging or the court cards—though, in some lines, those digits stand for nothing more than an internal SKU. The key thing: the Rider Back has a symmetrical, scrolling pattern that looks fancy while you’re returning to your second coffee and your third failed bluff.

### Card stock and finish

In the 1980s, Bicycle used a stock that felt thicker and stiffer compared to today’s more flexible, air-cushioned finishes. The 808 decks typically had a smooth but slightly waxy feel, a result of old-school varnish and shellac layers. The result? A deck that will shuffle with a satisfying snap, then politely refuse to fan in a dramatic, modern way. If you’re a cardist or a magician who loves pocketful-of-gifts flourishes, the stiffness will reward you with structure—though you might need to adapt your fans and maybe your entire technique.

- Pros: durable stock, nostalgic heft, strong color fidelity on red and blue faces.

## Unsealing: when you break the seal, you awaken the past

A sealed deck is a time capsule. A vintage seal may be made of foil, wax, or just the standard factory wrap that survived the continuous onslaught of humidity in attics and basements. When you break that seal, you release more than a deck of cards; you release a whiff of old cardboard, a memory of a magician’s late-night practice, and possibly a thread of static electricity that travels to the tips of your fingers.

Tips for opening without breaking the aesthetics:

- Use a clean, dry blade or a corner knife designed for packaging. A dull blade can ruin the wax seal and create a tear that will haunt you later.
- Do not slice into the face of the card; the seal should give without revealing the cards. You want to avoid early creases; the deck is old, not invincible.
- After opening, place the deck under a gentle weight for a few hours to help flatten any potential warp caused by humidity.

Once opened, inspect: are the cards slightly rigid, or do they bend easily? Is the color still vibrant? Does the blue back design show a cool, almost Arctic bite? All these small checks become a story you tell your friends when they ask where you found the “sacred artifact.”

## Caring for your vintage deck: longevity over flashiness

Care is the quiet art here. Vintage decks thrive on stable humidity, steady temperature, and minimal direct light. Here are a few tips to maximize shelf-life and usability:

- Storage: keep away from direct sunlight; the colors on the face cards may fade with prolonged exposure. A dark cabinet or closed shelf is ideal.
- Handling: wash hands, dry them, and avoid sweaty fingers; oils can degrade the finish over time and cause a slickness you didn’t bargain for.
- Cleaning: don’t wipe the faces with solvent or cleaners; a gentle dusting with a microfiber cloth is enough to keep them readable.
- Display: a glass-front case with a small LED light can show off the back design without heating up the deck.

Here’s the true-world verdict: sealed vintage decks can be a good investment if you are patient, selective, and sure you’re buying from a reputable seller who guaranteed seal integrity and age. The 808 Rider Back’s value will largely depend on:

A good rule of thumb: compare the print quality, look for consistent back pattern, and examine the tuck box edges for the tell-tale signs of mass-produced 'reissues' rather than time-tested original runs.

## Where to buy and what to look for

If you’re considering a purchase, here are practical steps:

- Verify the seller’s reputation and return policy. Vintage decks can be widely faked or misrepresented, so a seller with a clear return policy is a good sign.
- Check the photos closely: seek clear images of both the tuck box and the back of the cards. Look for color consistency and any signs of wear around the edges.
