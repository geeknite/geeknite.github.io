## Sculpt quality and base design

The Orc Zombie’s sculpt is a study in “functional menace.” The facial features, while simplified to accommodate the zombie aesthetic, still convey a sense of personality. ## Painting and tabletop experience

### Primer and first colors
Prime the Orc Zombie with a neutral gray or black primer. The goal is a surface that grabs the first layers of green with bite. I find that a light gray primer helps avoid the “muddy goblin” problem that sometimes plagues undead figs. Then, lay down a base coat of a desaturated olive or army green. The key here is to avoid neon fantasy greens; undead orcs benefit from a more earthy palette that reads as “putrid, but still alive enough to swing a weapon.”

### Colorwork: greens, purples, and rotting highlights
The miniature responds well to a classic three-layer approach:
- Base green: something like Mournfang Brown mixed with Steel Leg or a similar muted green. You want depth, not a neon lime. 
- Mid-tones: introduce a slightly brighter green on raised surfaces such as biceps and ridges to create contrast. This helps maintain readability on the table when the figure is moving in and out of cover.
- Shadows and rot: a purplish or brown wash adds the “rotting” texture. A dark wash in the recesses is essential for reading depth, especially on a tabletop with multiple lighting angles.

Highlight with a pale green or taupe-green on raised edges, focusing on the brow, knuckles, and the edge of the jaw. The eyes—a minuscule but crucial detail—should catch light with a dot of white or pale yellow. It gives the impression of a living corpse with a spark of malice rather than pure mindless hunger.

### Weapon and wound details
If you’ve chosen a club or a sword, use slightly different tones to differentiate weapon metal from flesh. For metal, a cool steel color with a light wash keeps things grounded and realistic. For wound edges, a touch of pink or bone tone can be used to suggest damage that’s recent enough to bleed but not so fresh that the whole model looks wet. The aim is texture, not gore saturating your gaming table. You want players to identify the zombie as a threat, not a blood-slick hazard that hides under the dice cup.

### Tabletop play: D&D vs Zombicide compatibility
In a standard D&D encounter, this Orc Zombie can function as a classic undead foe—low-to-mid hit points, a straightforward melee attack, and a motivation to wander toward the nearest non-zombie target. However, the Zombicide: Black Plague ruleset treats zombies as a mass threat where one model is worth less than its combined impact. The Orc Zombie is a perfect bridge: it can be used as a named orc zombie hero for a D&D one-shot or bulk undead in a Zombicide scenario. If you want to use it in D&D as a “boss minion,” flavor the encounter with a whispered prophecy and a terrain hazard like a crumbling statue to enforce a dramatic edge. In Zombicide, consider it a “special” zombie that can be a slingshot to trigger mission objectives—an obstacle with personality rather than a mere stat block.
