## Introduction
City of Mist: The Gilded Age is not your typical tabletop expansion. It doesn’t come with a handful of new stats you’ll forget before the first pull of the dice; instead, it drags you into a gilded, grease-slicked underworld where the heroes wear masks so the public can pretend they are just ghosts in a bustle of brass and back-alley whispers. If the core game asked you to suspend disbelief and flirt with myth, The Gilded Age dares you to flirt with myth while pretending to be respectable gentlemen and ladies at a club that might explode from a single misplaced secret. It’s a delicious mashup of late-19th-century opulence and pulp-noir punch—think skyscrapers wearing top hats, and every elevator ride might be a moral test you could fail with style.

This review wants to be your no-nonsense-but-still-sarcastic guide through what the Gilded Age adds to the City of Mist toolbox. We’ll tour the setting, the new mechanics and masks, how it feels at the table, and whether you should drop your coin purse on this expansion or instead invest in a different flavor of myth-and-crime. As with all Geeknite content, expect a wink and a nudge toward the ridiculous while giving you concrete advice you can actually use at the table.

## What’s in The Gilded Age box (or PDF) and how it feels on the table

### Artwork and vibe
The Gilded Age doubles down on opulence: velvet, marble, mercury mirrors, and a skyline that gleams with both opportunity and threat. The art direction leans into the era’s glamour while reminding you that glamour is often a trap you walk into wearing the right mask. It’s a balance that City of Mist handles well: you’re never forced into a single take on the era. You can lean into the rich decadence of the 1890s, or you can subvert it with a punky, modern twist that would make you respectable in a speakeasy—if speakeasies existed yet in the game's mythic-late-1800s. The end result is a setting that feels alive, busy, and just a little bit ready to misbehave in the best possible ways.

There’s more to appreciate here than surface-level aesthetics. The artists clearly had a game plan: evoke the era’s social stratifications—the rags-to-robins arc, the socialites who drown in champagne and secrets, the officials who are just as compromised as the criminals—and then layer mythic motifs over the top. If you want to stage scenes where a gilded lobby becomes a tribunal for a god of commerce, you’re in the right neighborhood. The mood is not merely “mystery noir” but “mythic-noir with a skin of haute fashion.” It’s deliciously specific, yet flexible enough to let players bring in their own mythic inspirations.

### Mechanics overview (Power Tags, Logos, Mythos, Masks)
If you’ve played City of Mist before, you’ll feel right at home with the core conceits here. The Gilded Age expands the system by deepening the intersection between your Mask (your secret identity), Mythos (the legendary power you channel), and Logos (the rational, unified explanation you present to the world). The result is a more textured dance between what your character believes they are and what the city believes they are capable of. In practical terms, you’ll notice:

- New Masks and Logos tailored to the era. Expect archetypes that feel like they could star in a long-running stage adaptation of a classic penny dreadful—but with modern, quirky twists. The Masks aren’t just “the hero with a gimmick”; they’re vehicles for moral ambiguity, flamboyant dressing, and the occasional moral wobble when the plot twists demand it.
- Mythos that scale up with the setting. The Gilded Age provides mythic forces tied to capitalism, empire, and the social machinery that keeps a city in motion. You’ll be able to pull strings in ways that feel both grand and intimately personal. This isn’t big brawl myth; it’s big room full of doors you can open if you’re cunning enough to spot the right creak in the hinge.
- Logos that ground the fantastic in the gritty. While the Mythos might conjure carousels of marvel, the Logos keeps the PCs anchored in believable consequences. It’s a balancing act—mythic action that feels consequential rather than glitzy for the sake of it. The end result is a game where heroic deeds have real-world texture and weight, which is exactly the kind of thing we crave in a noir fantasy setting.

For the player experience, the mechanics stay approachable. You’ll still narrate through a mosaic of tags, benefit from the dice pool to set up outcomes, and watch as your masked persona interacts with a city that is both generous and dangerous. The expansions don’t throw away the core feel; they deepen it, giving players more narrative levers to pull when the night grows thick with secrets.

### The Gilded Age setting: 1890s New York as a living stage
The Gilded Age in City of Mist is not just a scenic backdrop; it’s a character with a long shadow. This is a city of capricious fortunes and brutal inequalities, of salons where the conversation is a weapon, and of back-alley deals that can topple a stock market in a single whispered rumor. The book does a fine job balancing realism and myth. Real-world historical flavor is threaded in—telegraph lines, steam-powered transit, the clamor of new money and old secrets—without turning the setting into a dry documentary. The result is a city that feels lived-in: a place where you can chase a myth through a theater district, a bank, and a rooftop garden where the champagne never stops flowing—except when the night gets too uncomfortable and someone vanishes into the fog.

From a GM perspective, the gilded age offers abundant hooks for investigations with moral complexity. Who counts as a hero when the city’s system rewards your every risk? Which masks fit best for a character who wants to do good but must pretend to be ordinary to survive? And how do you stage moments where a hero’s power could ruin a rival’s life as easily as it could save a victim’s day? The setting invites classic noir dilemmas—trust, betrayal, and the dangerous romance of power—while letting players stretch their mythic muscles across a sprawling, dangerous metropolis.

### Character creation and example masks
Character creation in The Gilded Age leans into the Masks-as-identity core of City of Mist, but with a twist suited to the era’s flavor. You’ll draft a Mask—your public persona—and align it with a Mythos and a Logos, but the era’s social texture adds fresh dimensions to every choice. Expect Masks that emphasize social maneuvering, public spectacle, and the era’s distinctive power structures (industry barons, reformers, performers, and con artists). The designers are careful to give you both the glamorous option (the Mask who glitters while plotting) and the grounded option (the Mask who blends into the crowd but still exerts influence from behind the scenes).

A few sample Mask ideas that you might encounter or want to adapt: a social reformer who channels a myth of renewal, a stage star whose performances expose the city’s deepest lies, a banker who somehow commands a Mythos of prosperity and ruin, or a reporter who uses evidence and rumor as twin blades. Each Mask gives you an angle on how you approach investigation, conflict, and personal drama. It’s not just about how flashy your power is; it’s about how you wield your truth in a city designed to reward the clever liar and punish the kind hearted sucker who tells the truth at the wrong moment.

### Adventures and micro-scenarios
The Gilded Age comes with a collection of ready-to-run threads that feel both cinematic and scalable. Whether you want a long arc about the collapse of a financial empire or a string of one-offs that reveal the underpinnings of a city’s power structure, there’s something to grab onto. Expect scenarios that center around: corrupt philanthropy, the meaning of “public good” in a city that monetizes every human moment, art-culture intrigue, and the ghastly beauty of a mythic creature that thrives in wallpaper gilding and backroom politics.

One of the book’s strengths is how it blends investigative pacing with mythic escalation. You can begin with a seemingly ordinary mystery—someone is stealing a rare artifact, a property dispute reveals a bigger conspiracy, a theater premiere hides a supernatural occurrence—and then watch the threads weave into a tapestry that reveals the city’s secret history. The Gilded Age doesn’t shy away from the violence of its era, but it reframes it through the lens of mythic power and human misdirection, which makes even brutal moments feel thematically meaningful rather than gratuitous.

### Design choices and the GM’s toolbox
For GMs, the expansion offers more than new masks and mythic hooks. It provides a toolbox of narrative devices to help pace sessions and escalate tension. You’ll find modular beats you can drop into any ongoing arc, designed to fit the era’s mood without forcing a particular path. The guidance emphasizes the city as a character and encourages you to craft scenes that reveal the tension between spectacle and consequence. It’s not just about a hero stopping a villain; it’s about deciding what civilization you’re willing to tolerate in exchange for protection, prestige, or a moment of personal glory.

Humor has its place here, too. The Gilded Age doesn’t demand grimness 24/7; it invites players to play with the contrast between polished surfaces and the ugliness lurking beneath. A well-timed quip can puncture a gilded façade and turn a tense revelation into a communal laugh that still lands with impact. It’s a sign of good design when you can balance tragedy and levity in a single scene, and this expansion mostly nails that balance without feeling disingenuous or out of place.

### Visuals, layout, and accessibility
The physical book (or PDF) is enjoyable to handle. Layouts are clean, with well-marked sections that let players find what they need quickly, a boon during a tense chase through a dark alley or a crowded club where the wrong rumor could be fatal. The artwork isn’t flashy for its own sake; it’s curated to support the moment: the right character, the right location, the right line of dialogue that could shift a scene from a standoff to a confession. If you’re a GM who likes to narrate with a strong sense of environment, you’ll appreciate the way the art guides your descriptions without stealing focus from your players.

In terms of accessibility, the core approach of City of Mist remains friendly: the rules are approachable, the concept is scalable, and you can tailor sessions to your group’s experience level. The Gilded Age adds depth, not a new learning cliff. It’s possible to run it with a light touch for newcomers or to run it as a dense, multi-session arc for veterans who want richer social manipulation and mythic spectacle. As always, the key is to communicate the tone you want for your table and adjust the level of mythic wonder accordingly.

## How The Gilded Age plays at the table

### Tone and pacing
The tone is crucial here. The Gilded Age invites melodrama while rewarding restraint. If your group leans into over-the-top dialogue and flamboyant gestures, you’ll feel right at home; if you prefer a quieter, investigative approach that foregrounds character choices over flash, you’ll still have plenty to work with. The pacing support is well-tuned: you can run tight 2-3 session micro-arcs to establish Masks and Mythos, then gradually layer in longer arcs that reveal the city’s hidden architecture. The line between “public” and “private” becomes a literal stage: what the city presents to the crowd versus what the Mask can reveal in a private moment is where most of the drama happens. It’s a design win when you can stage a revelation that forces a character to confront a truth they’ve maintained for years, and then watch the table react with a mixture of awe and comedic discomfort.

### Player experience and narrative control
Players get to wield power with a sense of theatricality, but the storytelling remains collaborative. The city’s myths aren’t just “things you do.” They’re social contracts that require players to negotiate with the city’s power brokers, friends, enemies, and their own inner contradictions. The expansion does a good job of giving you a spectrum of narrative choices: you can be a mover and shaker, a behind-the-scenes manipulator, or a charismatic face who pulls the strings while maintaining a veneer of respectability. The masks serve as windows into distinct narrative modes; you can switch the lens in moments, turning a potential hostage situation into a moral conversation about what the city owes its citizens.

### GM tips: running The Gilded Age smoothly
- Start with a strong initial hook that leverages both glamor and risk: a gala, a theater premiere, a bank shakedown, or a political fundraiser where a mythic force begins to reveal itself.
- Use the city as a character by weaving environmental clues that reflect mood shifts: fog creeping in around a corner, lights blinking in a way that seems almost prophetic, or a statue that seems to be watching as the plot unfolds.
- Pace your reveals so that the players always have a choice that matters: saving a city official who is actually a corrupt mask or letting a subordinate suffer the consequences. The city’s power structures reward moral choices that feel meaningful, not merely clever.
- Mind the balance between Mythos spectacle and Logos consequence. A dramatic stunt should lead to a meaningful outcome that affects future scenes, not just a one-off moment of flash.
- Lean into social conflicts. The era’s emphasis on appearances makes social maneuvering an art form; use it to create tension between characters who all want the same thing but for different reasons.

### Myths, morals, and mischief
The Gilded Age plays with the ethics of power, the lure of wealth, and the ways secrets can become weapons. It’s easy to drift into melodrama, but the best moments arise when someone’s Mask collides with a truth that’s corrosive or transformative. The expansion’s best scenes feel like a chess game where every move has a backstory that can be exploited. If your group enjoys that kind of layered storytelling, you’ll likely end up shelving the dice a lot less and letting dialogue and choice carry the day—though there will still be moments when a carefully aimed power tag saves the day with style.

## Format and accessibility notes

While this review emphasizes the mood and narrative potential, there are practical things to consider before you buy:
- It’s not a standalone ruleset. Expect to rely on the core City of Mist system as your backbone. The Gilded Age expands that system—not revolutionizes it.
- If you’re new to the game, you’ll want the core rulebook nearby for reference on tag interactions, dice mechanics, and the core concept of Masks/Mythos/Logos. The expansion guides you, but you’ll still want to understand the base vocabulary.
- The material is adaptable to different group sizes, but the recommended approach thrives with a table of 3-5 players. Too many Masks can muddy the narrative, while too few can flatten the city’s bustling energy.
- The art and layout support accessibility. Clear section headers, readable type, and purposeful illustration choices make it easier for players to parse what’s happening in a scene. If you’re someone who values tactile design as part of immersion, you’ll appreciate the care here.

External note: If you’re seeking a quick primer about the Gilded Age setting beyond the Mythos, a shallow skim through reputable sources on late 19th-century New York can give you color without heavy-handed history lessons. The point is to spark mood and motivation, not to teach a class on urban development from a gilded perspective.

## Who should buy City of Mist: The Gilded Age?

- Groups who love noir, myth, and stagecraft. If you enjoy stories where performance matters as much as prowess, you’ll feel right at home.
- Players who want more narrative hooks and scenes that emphasize character choice rather than brute force. The Gilded Age rewards storytelling over brute power, with plenty of dramatic capes and dramatic entrances.
- GMs who like setting-driven modules that still leave room for improvisation. The scaffolding is sturdy, but it’s designed to flex with your table’s vibe.
- Collectors of atmospheric RPG materials. The aesthetic is strong, and if you enjoy the “world-building as fashion show” vibe, you’ll get a lot of joy from this expansion.

If you’re still undecided, consider your table’s taste: do you want a tightly plotted, city-sized conspiracy with a tragic core, or a looser, character-driven arc where the city’s secrets feed the drama as much as the players? The Gilded Age can do either, with a little planning and a willingness to embrace the mythic oddities that define City of Mist.

## Final recommendation

City of Mist: The Gilded Age is a solid expansion for fans of both noir and mythic storytelling. It doesn’t reinvent the wheel, but it upgrades the ride with richer era flavor, more nuanced masks, and a design that delights in the interplay between mask, myth, and the moral compromises of a city that never sleeps. If you’re ready for a session that looks good on the surface and feels even better when you scratch at the underbelly, this is a worthy addition to your City of Mist library. It rewards players who lean into the theatrics of identity while challenging them to consider what kind of myth they want to become in a city that could swallow them whole in a single, glittering night.
