### Quick-reference cheat sheet (core loop)
- At the start of each round, deal a specific number of cards to each player depending on table size.
- On your turn, you choose one card from your hand to keep and pass the remaining cards to the left.
- Repeat this process until all cards have been drafted by everyone.
- Score the round according to the card types collected; after three rounds, tally total scores and declare a winner.
- Some cards interact in ways that multiply or modify points, particularly Nigiri with Wasabi and certain card sets.

### Card types in a nutshell
- Nigiri: A point card that usually yields a fixed value, sometimes multiplied by Wasabi in the same turn.
- Sashimi: Collect three to gain a big bonus; otherwise, the bonus is lost for that set.
- Dumplings: Early rounds can be used to build up a large balloon of points later; the more you collect, the bigger the payoff.
- Maki: The player with the most Maki markers gets a bonus; second place gets a smaller bonus.
- Wasabi: A multiplier that interacts with the next Nigiri you play in a given turn; timing matters.
- Temaki and Puddings: Be mindful of these two; they count toward end-game scoring in specific ways and can influence risk decisions during rounds.

If you want more depth on any card type, we provide mini-scenarios later in this guide that show how these cards interact in practice.

## Core rules recap with worked examples
The booklet provides a concise framework, but sometimes the numbers and timing need a do-over. Here are expanded examples that make the rules tangible, especially for new players.

### Example 1: Wasabi timing with Nigiri
- In round 1, you receive a Wasabi card and a Nigiri card in your hand. You decide to play Wasabi first and place it face up on the table. On your next turn, you pick a Nigiri from your new hand and place it on top of the Wasabi stack. The Nigiri’s points are tripled as part of this interaction in the round that you played Wasabi. If you do not play a Nigiri that same turn, Wasabi does not carry to another card in that round.
- Takeaway: Wasabi is a one-time multiplier that requires immediate pairing with Nigiri in the same turn to activate. The timing rules are strict, but the payoff can be worth planning around with a simple plan each round: Wasabi early, Nigiri to follow.

### Example 2: Sashimi set bonus arithmetic
- If you collect all three Sashimi cards in a round, you earn a round bonus. If you miss one, you earn zero from Sashimi for that round. The numbers are not complex, but the edge case is real: two players can be vying for a Sashimi-heavy strategy, whereas another player might pivot to Dumplings or Nigiri for balance.
- Takeaway: The Sashimi bonus rewards consistent collection, but you must commit to the set to realize the benefit. This is a classic case of the trade-off between risk and reward in drafting games.

### Example 3: Dumplings scaling and late-round leverage
- Dumplings scale nonlinearly — the fourth Dumpling yields more points than the third, and so on. Early in the game, aiming for Dumplings can be a strategic misstep if you are behind because the late rounds can’t fully compensate for a poor start. Conversely, if you are already in the lead, a Dumpling stack can cement dominance as other players chase other cards.
- Takeaway: Dumplings reward timing and risk assessment; late-round focus can be a game-decider if you calculate your position accurately.

These worked examples are the kinds of mental anchors that make the 2014 rules feel logical again when text alone seems evasive.

## Edge cases and how this guide resolves them
Edge cases are where players tend to talk past each other and the table becomes a courtroom of opinions. Here we tackle several common concerns with crisp, play-tested resolutions.

### Edge case A: Tie for the Maki race
- If two players tie for the most Maki markers at round end, the 2014 booklet doesn’t always spell out a clear tiebreaker. Our replacement guide suggests a simple tie-break where you look at the total number of Maki markers individually and then use the next-best-scoring card in that round as a secondary criterion. This keeps the contest exciting without dragging the game into a rules debate.

### Edge case B: Timing of end-of-round scoring
- In some setups, players can be unsure when to stop drafting and start scoring. The guide clarifies that end-of-round scoring occurs after the last card has been chosen and all players have finished their drafts for that round. If you still find disputes, rely on the card text summaries in the cheat sheet for a quick decision.

### Edge case C: Interactions between card types that look similar
- The difference between Maki bonuses and Wasabi multipliers can be subtle. The guide provides a mini-key with side-by-side comparisons of similar-looking interactions, so players can disambiguate at a glance without a full rule check.
