## Ports in detail: what you can connect and what to expect

- DisplayPort 1.4: 4K at 60 Hz is a comfortable expectation, provided your GPU and laptop driver support it. DisplayPort also supports high color depth, which matters if you’re into photo editing or color-critical tasks.
- HDMI 2.0b: Another reliable 4K60 option. If you’re using a second screen that’s an older HDMI panel or you want to run a TV as a second display while keeping a primary monitor over DP, this is a good path.
- USB-C PD passthrough: The 120W PD is designed to power most laptops which are TB4-capable. The actual delivered power can vary based on the laptop’s needs and the TB4 driver negotiation, but you’ll typically see a smooth pass-through for day-to-day workloads and some light to medium-lift tasks.
- USB-A ports: Great for peripherals like a mouse, keyboard, or USB SSDs. It handles typical workloads with ease, though if you’re pushing multiple external drives at once you might consider distributing the load via a separate dock or hub.
- USB-C port: Perfect for newer laptops with high-speed data demands or for charging when your TB4 chain is not fully utilized. It’s flexible enough to stream data to external drives, charge peripherals, or mirror displays through compatible devices.
- Ethernet: A wired network connection is still the king of stable speed. If you’re gaming online, or doing high-bandwidth workflows like large file transfers, the Ethernet port will keep you honest and predictable.
- Audio: The line-out is sufficient for headsets or external speakers. If you’re stitching together a quick podcast around your new desk, you’ll be able to route audio effectively without additional adapters.

In daily use, the port layout and performance feel natural. You don’t have to hunt for cables and adapters every two minutes, and you’ll be able to power a compact docking scenario with a few monitors without feeling like you’re wrestling with a transformer toy.

## Setup, compatibility, and the big picture

Compatibility is where docking stations shine or falter. The HP Thunderbolt Dock G4 is designed to work with TB4-enabled laptops across Windows and macOS ecosystems with reasonable expectations. In practice:

- Windows laptops with TB4 typically see straightforward driver negotiation. You plug in and you’re ready to roll. Display outputs are recognized quickly, and the PD pass-through works as advertised.
- macOS devices on TB4 usually require you to confirm the display arrangement in System Preferences, but the physical connections themselves are robust and stable. If you run a multi-monitor macOS setup, the DP and HDMI outputs behave reliably.
- Linux support for docking stations is a mixed bag across vendors. If you’re a Linux enthusiast, you’ll want to test your specific kernel and driver stack to ensure the DP/HDMI output and USB-C charging behave as expected. In many cases, the HP G4 performs well, but there may be edge cases depending on your hardware and software stack.

A note on compatibility with older laptops: The TB4 standard is explicitly designed to be backward-compatible with TB3 devices, but your experience will vary depending on the exact laptop model and firmware. If you’re pairing with a TB3 or USB-C-only device, you may still get video out and charging through alternate paths, but the full TB4 feature set may not be apparent. If you are reading this and planning a multi-OS workspace, the HP G4 is a flexible ally that’s usually more forgiving than you’d expect.


## Setup tips and best practices

- Place the dock within arm’s reach of your primary display and keyboard, avoiding a chase for cables every two minutes. The compact footprint helps, but a clean cable run is still worth planning.
- Use the DP port for your primary 4K display and HDMI for the secondary if you want the simplest two-monitor configuration with the least negotiation headaches.
- Invest in a short HDMI or DP cable to minimize clutter. A tidy cable run keeps signals stable and reduces the chance of accidental disconnections when you reach across the desk.
- Consider a powered USB-C hub for extra peripherals if you’re running a high-IO workstation. The HP dock handles a decent number of devices, but a separate hub can help you preserve free ports for on-the-road travel or expansion.
- Check firmware updates from HP. Docking stations sometimes gain stability and feature refinements with firmware releases. A quick update can be worth it if you run into odd display hiccups or switching issues.
