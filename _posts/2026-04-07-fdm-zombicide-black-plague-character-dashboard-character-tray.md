## What is the FDM Zombicide Black Plague Character Dashboard?

This is a purpose-built, FDM-printed tray that consolidates a handful of essentials for each survivor: the character board, health counters, item/weapon tokens, and the action cards you will inevitably flip during a tense zompocalypse. The goal is simple: keep everything in reach, clearly labeled, and visually aligned so you can glance at your board and know, at a single breath, whether you have enough actions left, which items you’re wielding, and who is about to become zombie chow if you forget about line of sight.

In practice, a dashboard tray for Zombicide Black Plague means a multi-compartment base with a central character dial space, a row for gear tokens, a pocket for loot cards, and a route for dice and damage counters. Some designs also offer a card-mounted slot for quick reference on the survivor’s unique abilities. The exact geometry varies by design, but the principle remains: give each player a compact, modular mini-desk in front of their miniature, rather than a chaotic pile of chits that would make any librarian weep.

## Design Goals and Constraints

The best dashboards are: 
- Modular: fits different character sheets and different campaigns; 
- Stable: won’t tip over when you reach for a dice; 
- Accessible: all pieces visible and readable without moving clay-like zombie arms around; 
- Printable: friendly to common FDM printers with typical 0.2 mm layers; 
- Durable: resistant to table-top slapstick when a dice rolls off the board.

The constraints come from the nature of Zombicide Black Plague: sometimes you have longer character sheets, sometimes you have small, portable tokens. The trays need to accommodate expansions, with the potential to reprint or adapt for the “boss” zombies or special items that show up in later chapters of the campaign. If you’re printing in PLA, you’ll want to consider longevity and heat resistance (for those sweaty summer sessions). If you choose PETG, you gain durability with a touch more stringing potential—but hey, that’s what post-processing is for.

## What’s in the Tray?

A typical character dashboard for Zombicide Black Plague includes:
- Character board slot: a tidy zone for the survivor’s profile and abilities.
- Health and stamina track: counters or sliders to track life, possibly wounds or fatigue.
- Item/Weapon tokens: spaces to drop in gear you’re carrying (and possibly those you’ve dropped for the round).
- Action card/skill slot: a small place to rest the hero’s available actions so you don’t have to hunt for the current turn’s options.
- Zombie threat indicator: a quick glance light that shows the current threat level if your design includes it.

The real magic happens when the tray aligns with the dimensions of your playmat and the character sheets. A good design keeps the tokens flush with the board edge and ensures that you aren’t constantly repositioning pieces during critical moments. It’s not about aesthetics alone; it’s about reducing cognitive load so you can stay in the moment as the horde approaches.

## Printing and Materials: The Practical Side

If you’re reading this, you’re probably comfortable with the idea of spending 5–12 hours on a single print, then sanding and painting for two nights. Here are practical considerations to help you optimize the process:

### Material Choice: PLA vs PETG

- PLA: Easy to print, cheap, and comes with a nice surface finish. It’s great for a first or second iteration when you want to test geometry without fighting with warping. The downside? It can become a little brittle under heavy table-play stress or elevated heat.
- PETG: A tougher animal. It resists impact better and handles table-snack-slip life a bit more gracefully. It’s still not bulletproof, but it’s more Baymax than a soft marshmallow.
- ABS and other exotic filaments: Not recommended for most home environments unless you’re chasing a very specific finish or heat resistance. Also, the smell is, well, memorable in a not-so-great way.

For most hobbyists, PETG represents a sweet spot between ease of printing and durability. If you plan to paint or post-process, PLA’s surface will sometimes offer a cleaner base. If you’re going full-blown display piece, PETG with a proper primer and lacquer gives a sturdier, long-lasting finish.

### Print Settings and Tuning

- Layer height: 0.2 mm is the default and comfortable; if you’re chasing speed, you can push to 0.28–0.24 mm, but you might lose a bit of fine detail.
- Infill: 15–20% is enough for a tray that needs to stay rigid without ballooning into a brick. For more durability, 25–30% is reasonable, but you’ll pay with longer print times.
- Perimeters: 3–4 perimeters gives a nice balance of strength and weight. You don’t want the thing to slice in half when you drop a dice.
- Bed adhesion: a brim or raft might be overkill for many printers, but it helps with a warping-prone build. A good brim is your friend for the first few layers.
- Post-processing: light sanding, a thin coat of primer, and a couple of layers of flat spray can transform a matte print into a collector-grade look. If you have access to filler primer, you can hide layer lines better and achieve a more museum-grade finish.

### Geometry Considerations

A dashboard that asserts itself on the tabletop should balance minimalism with function. Remember: space is not free. A good design keeps your miniatures within easy reach without forcing everyone to do the awkward PhD-level contortion to pass a weapon card. The less you fight the tray during the round, the more you fight the zombies at the proper moment. It is a simple equation, really: fewer table juggling moments means better chances of not waking the entire neighborhood when your table dice roll.

## Design Features and Ergonomics

### Layout and Fit

The best dashboards align with the standard character sheet footprint used in Black Plague. The tray should accommodate both the base sheet and any expansion overlays without obstructing creature bases or the action deck. A practical approach is to design a central character dial space with a surrounding ring of token slots. The central dial can be used for HP or a wound tracker; the surrounding slots hold weapons, items, and armor. Independent modules can snap in or out depending on the party size. This modularity is how you scale from a casual 2-player game to epic 6-8 player sessions without needing a second set of trays.

### Stability and Durability

If your tray is a flimsy, thin-walled dish, your dice will eventually become projectiles and your survivors will weep for mercy. Durability is achieved by a combination of wall thickness, ribbing, and a solid base. If you’re using PETG, you’ll notice that a properly tuned design can take a few accidental bumps and still hold its shape. The trick is to avoid overly aggressive overhangs without adequate support, which can lead to sagging corners and a sad face on your hero board.

### Aesthetics and Thematic Fidelity

A Zombicide-themed tray benefits from a subtle, grimy aesthetic: a dark, metallic tone with a touch of rust-like texture painted into crevices. It’s not strictly necessary for play, but it adds a sense of immersion you can appreciate after the tenth round when you’re still alive and the table hasn’t collapsed under the weight of zombie tokens. If you’re not into weathering, a clean monochrome can still read very well with a simple base coat and dry-brushing to highlight edges.

## Assembly, Finishing, and Customization

### Assembly Tips

- If your tray is modular, test-fit all modules before painting. Dry-fit first to ensure there is no binding between compartments.
- For a multi-part design, consider gluing in a faux-screw detail or a small LED-like effect to indicate which character is active. It’s a silly detail, but it adds to the experience.
- Use a light primer to help paint grip and to reveal any print imperfections that you want to fix before you coat with flat varnish.

### Finishing Techniques

- Priming: a basic gray or black primer helps the colors pop without heavy coats.
- Base coat: your color of choice, ideally in thin layers to avoid obscuring detail.
- Dry-brushing: highlights raised edges and the outer rims of token slots for a tidier look.
- Topcoat: a matte or satin finish to reduce glare during bright play sessions.

### Customization Ideas

If you’re a sucker for customization, the dashboard is a perfect canvas:
- Add character-specific modules that reflect class abilities; if a survivor has a special power that changes the way tokens look, you can create space to reflect that change.
- Create color-coded slots for different item types (weapons, armor, consumables) to speed up card management.
- Add label slots for each survivor’s name and a short tagline; this makes the table feel more personal and dramatically immersive.
- Paint a faux metal look or rusting effect to fit the Black Plague aesthetic, but don’t go heavy-handed — subtlety reads much better during a tense round.

## Real-World Field Test: Campaign Log

We printed a handful of dashboard trays and tested them across a four-session mini-campaign. The first thing we noticed was the difference in table flow: players could locate their items in a fraction of the time, and the middle of action felt smoother because fewer tokens slid away or got jammed under a miniature base. By session two, players stopped shuffling cards and started discussing strategies, which is exactly what you want in a cooperative game: more planning, less rummaging.

During those sessions, we tested prints in PLA and PETG. PLA prints held up fine in a standard living room environment, but a few light bumps produced micro-scratches along the edges. The PETG versions, while slightly more cumbersome to print (depending on your printer’s temperature stability), endured more aggressive handling and occasional table nudges without showing significant wear. The practical takeaway: if you expect rough play or frequent table movement, PETG is a safe bet; if you’re aiming for a quick, cheap, low-effort build, PLA is perfectly acceptable for a home-game vibe.

We also compared the dashboard to a resin-cast alternative in terms of durability and finishing. Resin trays tend to look premium, and they can hold fine details exceedingly well. However, resin prints require post-curing and more delicate handling, which translates into longer lead times and more meticulous post-processing. If you’re impatient and want a solid, serviceable tray that you can print and put into play the same day, an FDM design remains the pragmatic choice. Resin counters to this point include more precise geometry and a superior surface finish, but for most home game tables, the FDM version is more than sufficient to elevate play.
