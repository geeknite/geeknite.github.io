## Card Design and Mechanics

### Card Types and Mechanics

The deck leans into a familiar triad: resource cards, action cards, and cheat sheets of rules. The Altered Edition adds a fourth dimension: the Altered Card. These cards flip things on their head—steal a tile you didn’t know you wanted, force an opponent to draw, or re-roll a dice that didn’t exist in your version of the game. The balance remains reasonable if you’re paying attention; the risk is that Altered Cards can snowball quickly if you don’t contain them with counters or smart timing.

The resource system is intuitive. You’ll collect chips, coins, or mana-like tokens that power actions and builds. The synergy between resources and actions is where the deck really shines. You’ll start drafting early—round two, in fact—where you either go heavy on card draw to fuel your hand, or pivot to tempo, shaping the board so you can win on the next turn before your opponents realize what hit them.

### The Altered Edition Twists

The Altered Edition is the game’s wild card—see what I did there? It introduces two core ideas:
- Altered Effects: Cards that flip outcomes, bend rules, or provide alternate win conditions. These cards encourage improvisation and push players to reevaluate what winning even means in the moment.
- Frame-changing Rules: Each round can add or remove fundamental rules, creating an evolving play space. This is where the deck becomes a test bench for your brain: can you adapt quickly without losing your mind?

The design philosophy here is clever: the deck rewards bold plays while keeping the structure recognizable enough to avoid total chaos. It’s a careful balancing act between delightful chaos and unfair chaos—the kind you often walk when your party agrees to a house rule that turns into a strategic masterpiece halfway through the second round.

## Play Tips and House Rules

- Start with a lighter Altered Card set for your first few games to grasp the rhythm. Ramp up to full chaos as players demonstrate mastery.
- Establish a quick check-in rule: if an Altered Card would clearly break the game for everyone, pause, re-read, and decide together how to handle it. It preserves fun and fairness.
- Use a timer for turns to keep energy high and prevent analysis paralysis. The deck rewards decisive moves more than you’d expect.
- Rotate the Chaos Card draw so everyone experiences the turbulence—mutual chaos is better than a single chaos tyrant dominating every session.
- Create a “chaos budget” for your table: allocate a fixed number of Altered Card effects per session to prevent burnout. It’s like debt relief for your sanity.
