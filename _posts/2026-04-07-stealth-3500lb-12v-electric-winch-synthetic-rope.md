## Performance in the field
Recovery is always a performance of inputs: vehicle weight, surface friction, anchor quality, and a dash of luck. In our tests, Stealth handled up to its rated capacity with ease when the anchor was solid and the surface was snow-free. On steep grades, the winch slowed down, as expected, but kept consistent control and moderate heat.

Key takeaways from field use:
- Smooth rope payout with synthetic rope; reduced risk of whipback compared to steel lines.
- The winch maintains speed on moderate loads; on heavier pulls, expect a hum and a heat rise, but not a dramatic spike in noise.
- Wireless remote range is sufficient for most off-road scenarios; keep a backup wired remote near your bumper for edge-case retrievals.

## Installation and compatibility
- Mounting pattern: 10 x 4.5 inch bolt pattern; use 3/8 inch bolts or larger depending on bumper tolerance.
- Electrical: 12V power supply; recommended to install a dedicated fuse or circuit breaker rated for startup surges (150-175A).
- Cable management: keep the line away from sharp edges; use a protective sleeve if necessary.
- Bumper clearance: ensure the winch does not collide with the frame or the tires on full steering lock.
- Control options: wired remote for proximity work; wireless remote for flexible operation.

### Step-by-step installation (at-a-glance)
1) Remove the bumper or access the mounting area according to your vehicle’s manual. 2) Align the winch mounting plate with the bumper’s bolt pattern. 3) Tighten the bolts in a star pattern to ensure even loading. 4) Route the power cable with a heavy-gauge wire and install a fuse block. 5) Connect the remote control receiver and test before you button up the housing. 6) Spool out a foot of rope, then re-wrap and test the pull with a safe load. 7) Add the tree saver strap, winch damper, and a spare hook.

### Electrical requirements (in more detail)
A winch draws a surge of current, especially under load. If you’re wiring to a stock battery, expect around 150-175A startup current. If you’re planning long-recovery sessions, run a dedicated heavy-gauge cable (2 AWG or 1/0) with an inline fuse or a circuit breaker. If you have a dual-battery setup on your 4x4, a second battery can significantly improve performance and reduce strain on your alternator. Don’t forget proper grounding and a robust switch to prevent accidental engagement while you fetch the coffee.

## Safety and use guidelines (deeper dive)
Winches are powerful tools that demand careful handling. Here are practical tips to maximize safety and minimize drama:
- Always establish a three-point safety plan before you engage the line (anchor, line, observer).
- Wear gloves with a non-slip grip; the synthetic line is easier on the hands than steel, but it still hurts if you grab the wrong spot.
- Use a dampener to reduce potential line recoil effects.
- Inspect the entire assembly after each use; look for frayed rope, damaged hooks, or loose mounting bolts.
- Keep bystanders clear of the line path during operation.
- If you’re not sure about load and anchor quality, test with a smaller weight first.

## Minor myths and reality checks
- Myth: Synthetic rope is indestructible. Reality: It’s strong and safer to handle, but it can still wear from heat, UV exposure, and abrasion. Monitor rope quality and replace when needed.
- Myth: You can winch anything with more horsepower. Reality: Winching is physics, not magic. You still need proper anchors, proper technique, and a plan.
- Myth: The remote is optional. Reality: The remote reduces risk by letting you stand far enough away to avoid dangerous line tension, so it’s not optional in practice.
