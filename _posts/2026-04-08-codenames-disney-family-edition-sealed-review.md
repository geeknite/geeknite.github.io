## How to play in a nut shell

### Core mechanic recap

Codenames is a guessing game where teams compete to find their agents on a grid of unrelated words. A teammate acts as the clue giver, who provides a one word clue followed by a number indicating how many words on the grid relate to the clue. The opposing team then tries to guess which words the clue refers to, potentially uncovering your own team’s agents, neutral agents, or the dreaded assassin. The Disney Family Edition keeps this exact skeleton but wraps it in Disney-themed words and family friendly phrasing.

### Disney twist on the rules

The words on the grid are themed to Disney franchises and characters. If you draw a word card that says Simba, for example, you might be able to connect it with a clue like Pride which could perfectly align with the circle of characters from the Lion King and its broader story. This twist makes cultural references and associations easier for kids and older fans alike because a lot of Disney baggage is shared across generations.

### Example round in practice

Imagine a grid with words like Mickey, Simba, Olaf, and Sword, among others. The clue giver might say Disney 2, signaling that two of the words on the grid connect to Disney in a single, cohesive concept. Maybe the clue relates to a movie or a trait common to multiple characters. The team then debates the possible matches and attempts to pick the cards that best fit the clue without veering into red or blue territory. It is a test of vocabulary, memory of Disney lore, and the meta strategy of risk vs reward.

## Depth of play and strategy for mixed groups

### Where the strategy shows up

In Disney Family Edition, the big strategic lever remains the same as in standard Codenames: the distance between an optimal clue and the grid of words. The flagbearer moves from raw word knowledge to a more nuanced association game. Teams benefit from:

- Thinking in groups about shared Disney knowledge and overlap between characters and concepts; what do two or three words have in common that also ties into a widely known Disney property?
- Evaluating risk of spillover. One well chosen clue can claim multiple relevant words, but you risk dragging in a trap word that helps the opponent more than you.
- Reading the board for groups. Even with Disney words, some cards are easier to pair with a single, strong clue while others are ambiguous and might cause confusion.

### Clue giving as a social task

For families, clue giving is as much about social negotiation as it is about the word choice. The clue giver must manage the room, keep an eye on safety in terms of age appropriateness, and ensure the clue does not obviously reveal too much about the other team. In practice this fosters collaborative thinking and a little light leadership from the more experienced players, without devolving into a power trip.

### Tactics for kids and adults

- Start with broad Disney categories: classic animated films, Pixar, or star heroes and sidekicks. This helps frame your clue without venturing into obscure references that only the older players know.
- When in doubt, pick the more obvious connections first. It reduces confusion and accelerates the game pace for everyone.
- Use team dynamics to your advantage. Let kids claim a couple of words as long as the clue giver is confident the risk is manageable.
