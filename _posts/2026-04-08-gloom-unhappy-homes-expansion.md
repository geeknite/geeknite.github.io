Gloom is the kind of game that makes you feel seen and judged at the same time, which is the perfect mood for a Sunday when you realize your life is a black-and-white comic with very little sunshine. The Unhappy Homes expansion for Gloom leans into that vibe with the confidence of a neighbor who’s been hiding a haunted piano in the attic since the 1920s. If you came here for sunshine and cupcakes, you’re in the wrong house. If you came for a clever, pun-filled mechanism to make your gloom feel personal, you’re in the right parlor.

## What’s in the Unhappy Homes box?

This expansion doesn’t pretend to be a new game; it’s a clever add-on that nudges the existing mood engine in a dozen new directions. It feels like the designers looked at the base game’s vibe and said, “Yes, but how can we complicate the melodrama in a way that still feels somehow playful?” The box contains a mix of new cards and a small handful of components designed to be integrated with ease into a typical session.

### Card types and what they do

- Neighborhood cards: These are the big wildcard additions. They subtly shift scoring, morph the way gloom accrues, or introduce new drafting tactics that encourage you to think about your family as a little neighborhood orchestra of misfortune.

- Family cards: The heart of Gloom is your cast of characters. The Unhappy Homes expansion broadens the roster with new traits, new synergies, and some familiars that look like they lost a bet with fate.

- Event and mood cards: A handful of new events that can swing a turn in unexpected directions. They’re the kind of cards that produce twin moments—one slow-burn memory and one gut-laughter moment when someone draws the wrong card at the wrong moment.

- Tokens and markers: The mood tokens that help you track curses, motives, and the occasional mood swing—handy for keeping the table from dissolving into chaos in the wrong way.

- Reminder sheets and a small player aid: These help you keep track of the new rules and ensure you don’t forget how the new neighborhood effects interact with the base game.

### How it changes the flow of a session

In a word: momentum. The base game is a study in patient misfortune; the expansion injects tempo into the proceedings. You’ll find yourself juggling more variables, which can feel chaotic in a delightful way. The new Neighborhoods create opportunities to plan multi-turn strategies, which means you’ll often set up a situation where one player’s tragic memory affects several others in the chain. This is the kind of social mechanic that makes room for bold bluffing and gleeful misdirection—two activities that geeks do almost as well as pigeonhole-sorting their own regrets.

## Theme, humor, and the narrative spine

Gloom’s core appeal is not just the morose vibe; it’s the humor that threads through the tragedy. Unhappy Homes leans into that with a wink. The new art maintains the same charmingly macabre style, with characters that look like they wandered out of a Victorian postcard and into an aggressively modern family reunion. The tone remains a loving parody of domestic life, with enough bite to satisfy players who enjoy dark humor without tipping over into actual despair.

### Art direction and production values

The artwork continues to be a highlight. The color palette is moody without being dreary; the linework is crisp, and the expressions on the characters’ faces are pure “why did we invite the neighbor over again?” The production quality matches the core game—cards are sturdy, with a satisfying weight, and the finishes feel like something you would proudly display, except for the occasional card that makes you squint and murmur, “Yes, I am the problem and that’s okay.”

## Interactions with other expansions and scalability

One of the real strengths of Gloom is how well its expansions slot into an existing table dynamic. Unhappy Homes plays nicely with other additions like Uninvited Guests, Family Matters, or even experimental custom house rules. The expansions don’t fight for space on the table; they converse with one another in a way that top-tier co-op and party games often fail to achieve. The result is a session that can deepen your strategic options while giving you more ridiculous anecdotes to tell later at the grocery store.

### Multiplicity and player counts
- Two players: You can enjoy the small-scale drama, but the new cards are best played when there’s a little more chaos on the table. The Neighborhood cards shine in a 2-player duel where each side tries to out-maledict the other.

- Three to four players: This is where the expansion shines. The new cards create opportunities for alliances, backstabbing, and the occasional “I’m not skull-fond enough to do that” moment that makes your friends question their life choices.

- Five to seven players (with a larger base game or added modules): The increased tempo can push the table into a glorious buffet of misfortune. The new cards rarely feel overbearing because there are enough spouses, siblings, and spectral neighbors to keep the table in a lively chorus of groans.

## Strategies and practical tips

Here are a few rules of thumb that we’ve tested in the field of gloom and doom (aka, our weekly game night where coffee is the lifeblood and sarcasm is the currency):

- Start with a plan but be ready to improvise: The Neighborhoods encourage planning, but misfortune loves improvisation. Don’t overcommit to one strategy; let the cards twist you into new shapes.

- Build around a “core character,” not a single card: The Family cards reward synergy, and the Neighborhoods can magnify those relationships. If you can anchor a couple of characters with clear interdependencies, you’ll feel the room lean into your narrative.

- Manage tempo with events: The new Event cards can reset or accelerate the pace. Use them to punctuate the table’s energy. If people are losing track of the moment, drop in a well-timed event to re-center the mood.

- Remember the mood tokens: They’re not just fancy adornments. They can influence scoring, influence drafting, and even alter who’s “in charge” of planning the next round. Don’t forget to look at them as a resource—not a complication.

- Adapt to your group’s vibe: If your table loves heavy strategy, dive into the deeper Neighborhood synergies. If your group is more about storytelling, lean into the narrative prompts that the expansion provides and let the drama flow.

- Accessibility and learning curve: The expansion adds depth, but it’s not an impenetrable gate. New players can get involved fairly quickly if you introduce cards gradually and use simple house rules for the first session.

## Design decisions behind Unhappy Homes

There’s a subtle elegance in how Unhappy Homes threads its new content into the base game’s fabric. The designers didn’t dump more gloom into the deck and call it a day; they added relationships, consequences, and timing that give you a sense of cause-and-effect across multiple turns. The Neighborhood cards lean into a theme that rewards planning, but they also reward lying well and telling an entertaining tale when you inevitably fail your own plan. It’s meta-gaming at its finest, and it’s way more entertaining than it has a right to be.

## Longevity and replayability

One of the best measures of an expansion is how often you find yourself playing with it a second and third time. Unhappy Homes checks that box nicely. The new cards create enough permutations to keep sessions feeling fresh across repeated plays, without turning the game into a kitchen-sink meta where every turn requires 20 minutes of debate over card sequencing. It straddles the line between “easy to teach” and “satisfyingly complex” in a way that is rare for a small-box expansion.

## Accessibility and component quality

The physical quality matches the base game: sturdy cards, good weight, readable typography, and an art direction that makes gloom feel cozy rather than claustrophobic. The learning curve, while non-trivial, is approachable. You can teach a group of first-timers in 15-20 minutes, then dive into the expansion’s richer interactions without hesitation. It’s the kind of product that you want to bring out on a regular basis, not just for “special occasions.”

## Final rating and recommendation

If you like the base Gloom experience and you crave more interaction, more storytelling, and more opportunities to flop dramatic cards onto the table in a way that makes everyone laugh and groan, Unhappy Homes is a strong addition. It does not dramatically alter the game's core structure, but it does offer enough variety and clever design to justify a purchase if you want to extend those cozy nights of misfortune. The new content is thematically coherent, mechanically thoughtful, and more playful than you might expect given the morose premise.
