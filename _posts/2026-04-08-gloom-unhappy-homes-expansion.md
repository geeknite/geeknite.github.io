## Introduction
Gloom is the kind of game that makes you feel seen and judged at the same time, which is exactly the mood you want for a Sunday when you realize your life is basically a black and white comic strip with extra rain. The Unhappy Homes expansion for Gloom leans into that vibe with the confidence of a neighbor who has been hiding a haunted piano in the attic since the 1920s. If you came here for sunshine and cupcakes, you are in the wrong house. If you came for a clever, punny mechanism that makes your gloom feel personal, you’re in the right parlor.

Unhappy Homes doesn’t rewrite the rules so much as it adds a new layer of soap opera to the existing melodrama. It gives your gloom a new neighborhood to lurk in and a fresh cast of characters who are somehow more dramatic than the last batch of misfit souls. The result is a session that still feels unmistakably Gloom but with enough fresh hooks to justify another box on the shelf. Think of it as upgrading your haunted suburban cul-de-sac from a single candle to a chandelier that sighs when you roll a die.

This review aims to be thorough, but also hopeful in the sense that hope is a mood you can subject to precision scoring. We’ll look at what’s inside the box, how it shifts the flow of a session, how it plays with other expansions, and whether the new content actually improves the overall Gloom experience. Spoiler: it does. Also there will be jokes because gloom loves a good punchline almost as much as it loves a bad memory.

## What’s in the Unhappy Homes box?
This expansion does not pretend to be a brand-new game; it is a clever add-on that nudges the base engine in several directions at once. The box is compact, which is one of the reasons Gloom remains an excellent party game despite its morose temperament. Inside you’ll find a curated mix of new card types and a handful of components designed to slot into a typical session without requiring a factory reset of your emotional life.

### Card types and what they do
- Neighborhood cards: These are the big wildcard additions. They subtly shift scoring, morph the way gloom accrues, or introduce new drafting tactics that encourage you to think about your family as a little neighborhood orchestra of misfortune. They are the sort of cards that make you mutter under your breath about the HOA and then realize you are the HOA.

- Family cards: The heart of Gloom is your cast of characters. The Unhappy Homes expansion broadens the roster with new traits, new synergies, and some familiars that look like they lost a bet with fate. Expect odd little helpers who suddenly gain dramatic entrance music every time they arrive on the sheet.

- Event and mood cards: A handful of new events that can swing a turn in unexpected directions. They’re the kind of cards that produce twin moments—one slow-burn memory and one gut-laughter moment when someone draws the wrong card at the wrong moment. Yes, that is how misfortune learns timing.

- Tokens and markers: Mood tokens that help you track curses, motives, and the occasional mood swing—handy for keeping the table from dissolving into chaos in the wrong way. They also provide a therapeutic outlet for when you want to pretend you are running a tiny municipal budget and not a mortal family falling apart.

- Reminder sheets and a small player aid: These help you keep track of the new rules and ensure you don’t forget how the new neighborhood effects interact with the base game. They are the grown-ups at the table, gently saying either you remember or you get a reminder card to nudge you back on track.

### How it changes the flow of a session
In a word: momentum. The base game is a study in patient misfortune; the expansion injects tempo into the proceedings. You’ll find yourself juggling more variables, which can feel chaotic in a delightful way. The Neighborhood cards create opportunities to plan multi-turn strategies, which means you’ll often set up a situation where one player’s tragic memory affects several others in the chain. This is the kind of social mechanic that makes room for bold bluffing and gleeful misdirection—two activities that geeks do almost as well as pigeonhole-sorting their own regrets.

To get a sense of the tempo: you might draft a Neighborhood card that triggers a temporary misalignment of gloom penalties. Then you watch as the table recalibrates, with players forming temporary alliances, only to break them the moment a reminder token points in someone else’s direction. It’s not chaos for chaos’s sake; it’s a carefully choreographed catastrophe where you are both the conductor and the audience.

### Theme, humor, and the narrative spine
Gloom’s core appeal is not just the morose vibe; it’s the humor that threads through the tragedy. Unhappy Homes leans into that with a wink. The new art maintains the same charmingly macabre style, with characters that look like they wandered out of a Victorian postcard and wandered into a family reunion that forgot to wash the blood off the cutlery. The tone remains a loving parody of domestic life, with enough bite to satisfy players who enjoy dark humor without tipping over into actual despair.

If you have ever told a joke that only makes sense to the cruelly observant, you will recognize the rhythm here. The expansion understands that gloom and jokes share a metabolism: both rely on reverse expectations, timing, and the willingness to watch a plan derail in the most charming way possible.

### Art direction and production values
The artwork continues to be a highlight. The color palette is moody without being dreary; the linework remains crisp; and the expressions on the characters’ faces communicate more than the text ever could. The production quality mirrors the base game: sturdy cards, a satisfying heft, and finishes that look like a trophy you would proudly display on a shelf that is also a bookshelf for more stories of bad luck. The occasional card that makes you squint and murmur that you are the problem is not a flaw; it is a feature that reminds you exactly what you bought into.

## Interactions with other expansions and scalability
One of the real strengths of Gloom is how well its expansions slot into an existing table dynamic. Unhappy Homes plays nicely with other additions like Uninvited Guests, Family Matters, or experimental custom house rules. The expansions don’t fight for space on the table; they converse with one another in a way that top-tier co op and party games often fail to achieve. The result is a session that can deepen your strategic options while giving you more ridiculous anecdotes to tell later at the grocery store.

### Multiplicity and player counts
- Two players: You can enjoy the small-scale drama, but the new cards are best played when there’s a little more chaos on the table. The Neighborhood cards shine in a 2 player duel where each side tries to out-maledict the other. Expect clever callbacks and shorter cycles that lean into the competitive edge rather than the storytelling vibe.

- Three to four players: This is where the expansion shines. The new cards create opportunities for alliances, backstabbing, and the occasional I am not skull-fond enough to do that moment that makes your friends question their life choices. The room tends to move faster, and the storytelling becomes a shared chorus rather than a solo act of doom.

- Five to seven players (with a larger base game or added modules): The increased tempo can push the table into a glorious buffet of misfortune. The number of personalities at play increases the potential for dramatic narratives, while the Neighborhoods give you multiple pressure points to juggle. It can get a bit chaotic, but that is exactly the point if your group thrives on theatrical misery.

## Strategies and practical tips
Here are some rules of thumb that we’ve tested in the field of gloom and doom (aka our weekly game night where coffee is the lifeblood and sarcasm is the currency):

- Start with a plan but be ready to improvise: The Neighborhoods encourage planning, but misfortune loves improvisation. Don’t overcommit to one strategy; let the cards twist you into new shapes and make you snort with laughter at the resulting chaos.

- Build around a core character, not a single card: The Family cards reward synergy, and the Neighborhoods can magnify those relationships. If you can anchor a couple of characters with clear interdependencies, you’ll feel the room lean into your narrative like a polite audience that suddenly decides to heckle with love.

- Manage tempo with events: The new Event cards can reset or accelerate the pace. Use them to punctuate the table’s energy. If people are losing track of the moment, drop in a well-timed event to re-center the mood and remind everyone that fate loves a good beat drop.

- Remember the mood tokens: They are not just fancy adornments. They can influence scoring, influence drafting, and even alter who’s in charge of planning the next round. Don’t forget to look at them as a resource—not a complication. Treat tokens like tiny plot devices that can swing the narrative to someone else’s advantage if used cleverly.

- Adapt to your group’s vibe: If your table loves heavy strategy, dive into the deeper Neighborhood synergies. If your group is more about storytelling, lean into the narrative prompts that the expansion provides and let the drama flow. The beauty is in the flexibility; you can tilt toward either approach without breaking the mood.

- Accessibility and learning curve: The expansion adds depth, but it is not an impenetrable gate. New players can get involved fairly quickly if you introduce cards gradually and use simple house rules for the first session. A quick teach followed by a light first round is often the best way to invite people into gloom rather than scaring them away.

## Design decisions behind Unhappy Homes
There is a subtle elegance in how Unhappy Homes threads its new content into the base game’s fabric. The designers didn’t dump more gloom into the deck and call it a day; they added relationships, consequences, and timing that give you a sense of cause and effect across multiple turns. The Neighborhood cards lean into a theme that rewards planning, but they also reward lying well and telling an entertaining tale when you inevitably fail your own plan. It is meta gaming at its finest, and it is way more entertaining than it has a right to be.

The balance work here deserves a nod. The new components are not just there for show; they reframe risk and reward in a way that encourages players to trade information, bluff, and choreograph dramatic moments without tipping into chaos. In short, this expansion respects your table time and your desire to finish a session with a story rather than a spreadsheet of gloom math.

## Longevity and replayability
One of the best measures of an expansion is how often you find yourself playing with it again. Unhappy Homes checks that box nicely. The new cards create enough permutations to keep sessions feeling fresh across repeated plays, without turning the game into a kitchen-sink meta where every turn requires 20 minutes of debate over card sequencing. It straddles the line between easy to teach and satisfyingly complex in a way that is rare for a small-box expansion.

If you enjoy the base game but crave more texture in the relationships between characters, you will likely reach for Unhappy Homes repeatedly. The Neighborhoods offer fresh ways to connect stories and misfortunes, and the new moods give you reasons to recalibrate your tactics as the table dynamics shift with every memory that surfaces.

## Accessibility and component quality
The physical quality matches the base game: sturdy cards, good weight, readable typography, and an art direction that makes gloom feel cozy rather than claustrophobic. The learning curve, while non-trivial, is approachable. You can teach a group of first-timers in 15–20 minutes, then dive into the expansion’s richer interactions without hesitation. It is the kind of product you want to bring out on a regular basis, not just for special occasions. The components fit neatly into the existing storage setup, and the tokens are pleasant to handle, which matters when you are likely to thump them onto the table in dramatic fashion.
