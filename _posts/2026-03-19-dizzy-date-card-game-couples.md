## Quick Setup and Playtime Expectations

Dizzy Date is designed to be a plug-and-play experience. You can be up and running in under 5 minutes, which is perfect when you’re late for date-night and your couch has clearly become your best friend. Here’s how it typically plays out:

- Players: 2-6 (with optional expansions for larger groups).
- Playtime: 20-40 minutes for a single, vibrant session; 60+ minutes if you lean into party-mode chaos with a full table.
- Objective: Have fun, build shared stories, and earn bragging rights for the night’s most memorable moments. There’s no heavy scoring; the emphasis is on laughter and connection.

Setup steps:
1) Shuffle the prompt deck and place it face-down.
2) Decide your host for the first round (rotation is recommended to keep things fresh).
3) Distribute any Dizzy action tokens as per the edition’s guidelines.
4) Pick a mode. While the base game favors romance and goofy dares, expansions unlock party-night insanity.
5) Establish what “skip” means in your circle (the general Geeknite approach is: consent first, skip later—don’t force a moment that makes someone uncomfortable).

House-rule tip: set a “no-token-left-behind” rule to ensure everyone uses tokens if you’re playing in a larger group. It prevents token hoarding and keeps the energy high.

## Core Gameplay: How the Prompts Work

At its heart, Dizzy Date is a prompt-based system that asks players to respond with stories, performances, or team efforts. It’s about turning a deck of prompts into shared memory rather than a mere card-reading exercise. The prompts swing between:
- Romantic challenges that encourage tenderness and closeness.
- Silly dares that prompt goofy behaviors, ridiculous voices, or spontaneous skits.
- Story prompts that invite you to recall a memory, create a collaborative tale with a twist, or explain something you’ve always wondered about your partner.
- Dizzy twists that suddenly change the rules mid-round, forcing you to improvise on the spot.

In practice, a typical round might go like this: the host draws a card, reads it aloud, and the other players contribute in whatever style the prompt calls for. If the prompt asks for a romantic confession, you might tell a story about your first date in a playful, non-awkward way. If it’s a silly challenge, you’ll perform something ridiculous—think goofy dance, fake accents, or a dramatic reading of your grocery list. If a Dizzy twist card is in effect, you might have to swap partners for a moment, or complete the task while wearing a silly accessory or while one hand is tied behind your back (in a safe, consensual, and non-damaging way, of course).

The rules for scoring are delightfully loose by design. In many sessions, the host or the table awards points for humor, creativity, delivery, and how well you embody the spirit of the prompt. The result is less about “winning” and more about the memories you create together. The same night you’ll have couples telling stories they’ll forget to tell again, and friends learning that you two are secretly great improv partners.
