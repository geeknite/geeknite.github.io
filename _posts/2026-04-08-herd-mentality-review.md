## How to set up: quick, painless, and primed for chaos

Setting up Herd Mentality is the kind of experience where you want to minimize the “dead air” between players. The box tells you what to do, and you’ll probably do it in roughly five to seven minutes, depending on your group size and how loudly someone can shout “obvious” without judgment. Here’s a fast setup checklist:

- Shuffle the prompt cards and place the deck within reach of all players. 
- Each player draws an answer sheet (or uses a provided pad) and a voting chip set. 
- Designate a timer. Your host can use a phone, a kitchen timer, or a loud old clock that sounds like a broken submarine. 
- Decide on the rotation order for players so there’s a natural flow, but don’t overthink it—chaos loves a loose structure.

In short, there’s not much “pre-game” ritual required. The goal is to start playing quickly so you can get to the glory of seeing a room full of adults (and possibly kids sneaking in the back) lock onto a single, obvious-but-incorrect answer and then be hilariously wrong together. 

## How to play: the core concept explained (brief and glorious)

The core mechanic of Herd Mentality is deceptively simple: a prompt card appears. Every player writes down what they think is the most obvious answer to that prompt. After everyone’s written greedily in pen, the answers are revealed one by one (or all at once, depending on your house rules), and players vote on which answer they think is the “Obvious” answer. The kicker is that you don’t vote for your own answer; you vote for the one that matches what you think the herd would say. If your answer aligns with the majority, you score; if your answer is the herd’s pick but the herd as a whole picks someone else’s answer, you still score but with a different twist. The exact scoring varies by edition, but the gist remains: misalignment with the herd feels like a comedic victory rather than a failure. 

In practical terms, rounds unfold like a social experiment conducted in a living room. You watch people squint at “obvious” prompts and then vehemently defend the most ridiculous responses as if defending a thesis in a college cafeteria. The fun isn’t in being “clever” so much as it is in being perceptive about how your friends will react under social pressure. If you’ve ever suspected that your group contains a secret hive-mind with a flair for theatricality, Herd Mentality is the test you didn’t know you needed. 

### Round flow and decision moments
- Prompt reveal: everyone studies the prompt and quickly jots down their answer. 
- Answer reveal: all answers are shown (except for the ones you’ve shielded with your poker face). 
- Discussion window: a short-burst debate may occur where players defend their answers and attempt to persuade others. 
- Voting: players vote on the answer they believe best represents “the herd.” 
- Scoring: points are awarded for alignment with what the herd picked, plus any bonus points for guessing what other players might think, depending on the edition. 
- Reset and repeat: new prompts, new chaos, new memes. 

If you’re playing with younger folks or folks who aren’t native to social heuristics, you’ll see the narration of ideas shift from “I know what you’ll say” to “We’re all in on the joke,” which is precisely the kind of social glue that makes a party game memorable. The rules are deliberately light and easy to parse, which means you’ll be playing in minutes rather than having to memorize a sprawling rulebook. 

## The host perspective: running the chaos smoothly

If you’re the one holding the deck, Herd Mentality is a party in a box that requires just enough leadership to prevent total chaos from extinguishing the fun. A few host-friendly tips:

- Keep the tempo up. The game shines when you’re not letting silences linger long enough for someone to pull up a dating profile on their phone. A gentle timer helps. 
- Normalize wild answers. Part of the humor is in the ridiculousness of some of the “obvious” responses. If someone writes something off-the-wall, celebrate it—don’t scold it. 
- Create environmental cues. A quick mic check, a clap every time someone says a “yep, obvious” line, or a goofy nickname for the “herd” helps keep energy high. 
- Be mindful of sensitive prompts. The fun is in the chaos, not at someone’s expense. If a prompt touches a nerve, skip it or reframe it. A good host keeps the room alive without letting anyone feel boxed in or embarrassed. 

The host role is deceptively simple: you’re the conductor of a social orchestra. You don’t need to know all the notes; you just need to keep the tempo, the laughter, and the chips from disappearing into a black hole under the couch. The best hosts are the ones who laugh with the players, not at them, and who remind everyone that the point is shared amusement, not the loudest brag. 

## Player dynamics: who benefits most from Herd Mentality?

Herd Mentality is at its strongest when you have a mix of bold funny people and quiet observers. The bold players tend to drive the energy, while the quieter participants provide the “watch this” moment that makes the loud people’ s antics even funnier. The game rewards the perceptive players who can deliver answers that are obvious to the herd yet surprising when you realize the herd’s thinking process. In large groups, the social dynamic shifts towards collective identity, where the thrill is the moment the room unexpectedly converges on a single answer or devolves into a delightful disagreement about whether that answer is truly “obvious.” 

In practice, you’ll see scenarios like: someone writes a very straightforward, obvious answer; another player’s response is a subtly clever misdirection that sounds obvious but isn’t the “popular” pick; and a third player provides a completely absurd answer that becomes the loudest laugh of the night. The beauty here is that “clever” is not a guaranteed win; sometimes the most obvious choice wins because everyone else mentally copies the herd’s expectations. It’s a social psychology experiment disguised as a party game, and the twist is that the experiment is socially enjoyable rather than scientifically rigorous. 

## Strategy: can you game Herd Mentality effectively?

Yes, but only if you redefine “strategy” beyond a scoreboard and into the realm of social awareness. Here are some practical tips for maximizing your giggles and your score:

- Read the room. If the crowd loves obvious, you want to present an answer that feels like “the obvious” to the collective mood in the room, not just to you personally. 
- Lean into the crowd’s vibe. If you notice a group leaning into a certain theme (nostalgia, pop culture, a meme of the week), tailor your answer to fit that vibe so it looks like you’re in sync with the herd. 
- Balance risk and reward. Some prompts invite safe, obvious responses; others reward the risk of a bold but plausible misdirection. Use both as the night unfolds. 
- Support your teammates. In large groups, you’ll often get better results if you coordinate with a few friends to create a shared “obvious” narrative—without appearing to collude. The best moments come when players harmonize without looking like they harmonized. 
- Keep it inclusive. The strongest moments come from shared laughter, not from punching down or shoving someone into the spotlight. If you can get a room to giggle at a collective misunderstanding, you’ve mastered Herd Mentality. 

For players who love meta games and predicting social behavior, Herd Mentality is a playground. For players who prefer strict strategy and deterministic outcomes, it may feel a little chaotic. That’s not a flaw; that’s the core appeal: the unpredictability of a social organism at play. 

## Variants and house rules: tailoring the chaos to your crowd

Big Potato often encourages social experiments beyond the core rules, and Herd Mentality is no exception. Here are a few fun tweaks you can try to tailor the experience to different groups:

- Team mode: Split into teams of 3–5 and have each team discuss their answer privately before writing it down. The herd voting can be done as a team, which introduces some light strategic collaboration while preserving the laughter. 
- Silent round: For a challenge, skip the discussion window and require players to write their answers in silence and then reveal. This spotlights the awkward silence of social guessing and can be hilarious in its own right. 
- Speed round: Set the timer to a shorter window (e.g., 15–20 seconds) to force faster thinking and ruder but funnier decisions. The energy spike from rapid-fire rounds is unmistakable. 
- Family edition: If kids are involved, keep prompts gentle and ensure there are more “cute obvious” options than punchlines that could make things awkward. The point is to keep the room laughing with, not at, each other. 
- Thematic nights: Run a theme for the prompts—80s nostalgia, sci-fi, or internet culture—so inside jokes land more easily and the room feels cohesive. 

House rules are where the real memory-making happens. It’s your call how to bend the game to your charisma, your apartment layout, and your ability to tolerate a little noise. The beauty is you can experiment and still keep a robust scoring system that rewards participation and collaborative mayhem.

## Comparisons: Herd Mentality vs. the competition

- Say Anything: Both games celebrate open-ended thinking and crowd reception, but Say Anything leans more toward writing witty responses and pitching them to a judge.
