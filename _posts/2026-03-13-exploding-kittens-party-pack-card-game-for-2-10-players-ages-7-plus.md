## Setup and first impressions

Getting ready to rumble with Exploding Kittens Party Pack is pleasantly straightforward:

- Set the table, fan out chairs, and make sure there’s at least one snack that won’t ruin your card play if somebody throws it across the room in mock frustration.
- Remove the number of Exploding Kitten cards according to the player count (the base rules apply here; in general, you adjust the deck so there’s a balanced risk). Place the rest of the deck facedown in the center.
- Shuffle thoroughly. This is important; chaos likes a good shuffle. If you have a friend who insists on shuffling in a “gentle, ceremonial” manner, this is the time to let them do it—joyful noise is part of the game’s charm.
- Deal seven cards to each player (the standard starting hand for many Exploding Kittens variants). You’ll need to choose a dealer or rotate the deal clockwise.
- The top card of the deck becomes the first draw card; you can flip or reveal the top few cards with See the Future if you’ve got such a card in your hand.
- Everyone is ready to roll, which in this case means you’ll be laughing while your plan crumbles under the next “Attack” or “See the Future” reveal.

## How to play: the quick-flow overview

Exploding Kittens is a card-collection, hand-management, dodge-the-bomb game with a heavy emphasis on social interaction. The Party Pack adapts that premise with more players, and thus more moments to watch someone miscalculate a draw.

- Objective: Survive longer than your opponents by avoiding drawing an Exploding Kitten without having a Defuse card. If you draw one and can Defuse, you get to keep playing; if not, you’re out of the round.
- Turn structure: On your turn, you may play any number of action cards from your hand before drawing a card from the deck at the end of your turn. The action cards let you alter the deck, peek into it, steal a card from an opponent, or force them to draw. The exact mix of action cards varies by the Party Pack version, but the core mechanics remain intuitive: you’re trying to navigate risk while poking at your friends’ plans.
- Core action cards you’ll recognize include: See the Future (peek at the top cards), Nope (negate the previous action), Attack (pass extra draws to the next player), Shuffle (reshuffle the deck), and Favor (steal a card from another player). The Party Pack may introduce a handful of new tactical options to keep the late-game tense and the early game breezy.
- Defuse vs Exploding Kitten: Defuse cards are your safety valve. They give you a chance to survive an exploding draw, but you’ll have to rely on rerouting the deck and careful planning to keep that buffer in play.
- Endgame and elimination: The round ends when all but one player has drawn an Exploding Kitten (and failed to Defuse). Depending on your group, you’ll either run multiple short rounds or a longer single-elimination style depending on how sweet the chaos tastes.

In short: the Party Pack keeps the rhythm of the original game but adds a social slope that scales well with a bigger crowd. The more players you have, the more surprising moments you’ll witness—like a player you thought you could trust flipping a card that makes the entire table groan in delicious disbelief.

In a two-player game, the dynamics shift a bit toward tension and rapid decision-making. The two-player setup tends to reward careful use of See the Future and a cautious, read-your-opponent strategy. You’ll likely see longer stretches of turns where each player tests the deck’s luck, then doubles down with a bold play when it’s least expected. The Party Pack still keeps that signature charm—the cat cards wink and nod, while the other player tries to outwit you with a well-timed Nope or an unexpected Attack that turns the table in a heartbeat.

### 3-5 players and beyond
As you add players, the game’s tempo becomes more unpredictable. A single Attack card can tilt four players’ turns at once, creating a domino effect of misreads and near-misses. The social layer grows: more chances for misdirection, more opportunities to bluff, and more sides to cheer (or groan) as the deck’s fate unfolds. The Party Pack handles the scale with lighter rules for big groups and a few new card interactions that help keep track of the chaos without tipping into “endless loop” territory. You’ll also notice more frequent use of See the Future to peek the top of the deck and judge whether to press your luck or reset the stage with a Shuffle.

## Strategy and tips

While Exploding Kittens is often framed as a luck-based party game, there’s a surprising amount of strategy if you lean into it:

- Save Defuse cards for the high-stakes moments. You don’t want to waste a Defuse on a harmless draw when the next card could be your doom. Timing matters more than you’d expect, especially in large groups where someone else is likely to draw in your place.
- Use See the Future to read the room. If you can anticipate a dangerous run, you can push the deck toward safer territory or force your enemies into riskier draws. Even in a big group, this mental accounting pays off.
- The Nope card is your best friend. It’s not just about negating a move you don’t like; it’s about delaying the moment when someone else’s turn turns into a disaster for you. A well-timed Nope can turn a bad draw into a glorious, meme-worthy save.
- Attacks are weaponized social tools. They don’t just force someone else to draw; they shift the turn order and give you a few seconds of psychological control over whose fate gets sealed by the next draw. In groups, the Attack card becomes a workshop for petty-but-epic mind games.
- Read the room. In family groups, humor wins over edge, while in gamer circles, sharp thinking and a quick playstyle tend to win rounds. The Party Pack rewards players who adjust their strategy to fit their audience, not the other way around.
