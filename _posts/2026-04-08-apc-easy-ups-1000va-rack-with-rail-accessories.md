## Setup guide and best practices
The setup process is straightforward but not a magic trick. You want to avoid forcing the rails into a crowded rack and you should take your time to align the rails, especially if you are working above your head in a rack with a low ceiling. Start by disconnecting any devices and laying out a clear path for cables. Attach the rail brackets to the sides of the UPS using the included hardware. Then mount the rails to the rack at the height where the UPS will slot. Slide the UPS onto the rails and fasten it with screws. Before powering up check that the outlets are not blocked by heavy cables and that there is at least a little clearance for ventilation. Once powered you will see the display come to life showing the current status.

A word of caution: do not try to overload the outlets with high wattage devices. Remember the 600 W real power limit. For a small lab that means a couple of small switches, a NAS, and a few USB hubs. If you plan to attach high energy devices like printers or large servers, you will need a bigger UPS.

If you are using this UPS for a NAS or network switch, consider shifting the most critical devices onto the battery backed outlets to ensure you can gracefully shut them down in the event of a power outage. The rest of the gear can ride out a brief outage if necessary. ## Battery life and maintenance
The battery life is the usual mystery box in consumer grade UPS units. In practice you get a few minutes of runtime at small loads and perhaps longer if you are running only a single device. It is always wise to estimate your runtime not by the number on the display alone but by actually testing with the devices you plan to protect. When the time comes to replace the battery you will need to follow the model numbers in the user manual. Replacing the battery is not too tricky if you have the right screwdriver and you are comfortable with basic electronics. Keep a spare battery on hand if uptime is mission critical. The battery replacement interval will depend on usage and environment but in most lab environments you will be exchanging batteries every 3 to 5 years.
