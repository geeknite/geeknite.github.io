## Overclocking and Tuning Tips
If you’re the type who likes to push hardware a bit further, DDR4 overclocking is still very much a thing in 2026. Here are practical tips for squeezing a bit more performance from the Ripjaws V 32 GB kit without turning your PC into a hot tub:

- Start with the XMP/DOCP profile
  Most boards will recognize the 3600 CL16 profile and apply it cleanly. If you enable it and hit stability issues, try relaxing to CL18 or CL19 and see if you gain stability while preserving most of the performance uplift.

- Don’t skimp on voltages
  DDR4-3600 CL16 timings typically run at 1.35 V. If your board refuses to post at those settings, a small voltage bump to 1.37–1.40 V (in small increments) can salvage stability. Always monitor temps and avoid excessive voltages that reduce lifespans.

- Mind the memory controller and platform
  Some motherboards have quirks when pushing memory to high frequencies. If you encounter boot loops or POST issues, try reseating modules, updating BIOS, or testing in a different memory slot configuration (e.g., dual-rank modules in primary slots). The goal is not to force a miracle but to find a stable, repeatable profile for your configuration.

- Consider a modest latency trade-off for higher clocks
  If you want to push beyond 3600 MT/s, you’ll usually need looser timings and potentially different voltages. A 3600 CL16 is often the sweet spot for the widest compatibility and best real-world gains without chasing phantom performance.

- Do benchmarks after changes
  Always re-run your favorite tests after any BIOS changes. Small changes can ripple into larger differences in bandwidth and latency. Consistency matters when you’re benchmarking your own machine rather than chasing headlines.
