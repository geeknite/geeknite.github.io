### Sub-hilt, grip, and ergonomics
Here’s where the Honshu earns some points for showmanship and practical grip engineering. The sub-hilt is designed to act as a finger guard and an additional contact point for your hand. The idea is to give you unparalleled control during heavy cuts and baton-style actions, reducing the chance of your hand sliding forward onto the blade in a moment of enthusiastic excess. The sub-hilt doesn’t just exist for drama; it serves as a mechanical assist for grip security when you’re thrusting, drawing the blade through tough fibers, or performing controlled chopping on a windy day when your balance decides to take the day off.

The handle uses TPR, or thermoplastic rubber, a material known for its grip-ability in wet or slick conditions. In practical terms, you’ll notice better traction than a plain G-10 or hardwood grip when your hands are damp, sweaty, or wearing gloves. The trade-off is that some purists would rather have a harder handle material for longevity under heavy punishment; TPR is forgiving and comfortable but can show wear marks faster than a high-end micarta or G-10 surface. If you’re chasing pure ruggedness, you might also consider a quick regrip or handle upgrade after a season of use. Still, for value and comfort, the TPR choice is a solid middle ground that won’t punish you with hand fatigue during longer sessions.

## Maintenance and care
A stainless blade is more forgiving than carbon steel, but “forgiving” does not mean “maintenance-free.” The following routine will help you keep this Honshu looking mean and performing clean:

- Wipe the blade after use to remove moisture, especially after outdoor use. 
- Light oil on the blade surface to inhibit rust and corrosion. 
- Dry the blade before re-sheathing to avoid moisture being trapped in the sheath ecosystem.
- Inspect the sheath and hardware for wear; replace any loose screws or frayed straps as needed.
- Sharpen at a steady pace with a compatible stone or guided system designed for thicker blades; avoid aggressive angles on the bevel if you’re not used to it. 

Maintenance is not glamor, but it pays off in reliability. Stainless steel reduces the frequency of rust-related issues, but it does not forget to remind you that it’s a blade and wants proper care. If you’re building a multi-tool kit, this knife can be a centerpiece, but remember to pair it with a maintenance ritual that includes polishing, rust prevention, and blade-specific sharpening routines.
