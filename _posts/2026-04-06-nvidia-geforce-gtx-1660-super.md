## Overview

The GTX 1660 Super sits in Nvidia’s GeForce 16-series lineup, a quirky family that trades real-time ray tracing for solid traditional raster performance. It’s built on the Turing-era TU116 GPU, but unlike the RTX cards, it doesn’t bring RTX cores or Tensor cores to the party. That means no hardware-accelerated ray tracing or DLSS shenanigans; instead, you get dependable 1080p performance with efficient power usage.

In plain terms, the 1660 Super is a budget-friendly upgrade over the original 1660, mainly thanks to beefier memory bandwidth: 6GB of GDDR6 clocked at 14 Gbps with a 192-bit bus yielding roughly 336 GB/s of memory bandwidth. That’s a nice bump over the GDDR5 of some older equations and helps keep texture-heavy titles paddling along at 1080p without your frame rate turning into a discouraged snowflake.

## Specs and Architecture

### Core Specs at a Glance
- GPU: TU116-125 (same family as the 1660 Super’s siblings, but with its own flavor of CUDA cores)
- CUDA cores: 1408
- Base clock: ~1530 MHz
- Boost clock: ~1785 MHz
- Memory: 6GB GDDR6
- Memory speed: 14 Gbps
- Memory interface: 192-bit
- Memory bandwidth: ~336 GB/s
- TDP: 120–125W (depending on single- or dual-fan designs and your motherboard’s patience)
- Outputs: typically 1x HDMI 2.0b, 1x DisplayPort 1.4, sometimes multiple options depending on the board partner

This is not a “future-proof” gadget by any stretch. It’s a card that laughs at 1080p like a cartoon villain, but politely declines 4K like a fence-sitting politician. The 6GB of GDDR6 memory plus the memory bandwidth are the real stars here, letting you push higher texture qualities while maintaining playable frame rates in popular titles.

### Architecture Notes
The 1660 Super is built on Nvidia’s Turing-era approach (without RTX/dedicated RT cores). It focuses on traditional rasterization efficiency and solid driver support. You won’t be relying on fancy upscaling tricks like DLSS to save you from reality; you’ll be sitting in your chair, memorizing FPS curves, and pretending you understand hardware marketing buzzwords. If you want DLSS-like magic, you’ll need to look at RTX cards or performance-focused upscalers—though the 1660 Super’s bandwidth does a nice job smoothing textures in lots of games at 1080p.

## Performance and Gaming Benchmarks (Real-World-ish Numbers)

Don’t expect miracles—this is a card designed for 1080p, not for 4K pixel-porn. With that in mind, the GTX 1660 Super provides robust, dependable performance in a huge swath of games, especially at high to very high settings at 1080p. Here’s what you can typically expect:

- In esports titles (Fortnite, CS:GO, Valorant), you’ll usually see well over 120 FPS on high to max settings, provided you’re not pairing the card with a potato of a CPU. The 1660 Super is a great companion for a 144Hz monitor, letting you ride that sweet frame-rate wagon. 
- In mainstream AAA titles from a few years ago (Shadow of the Tomb Raider, Assassins Creed Odyssey), you’re often in the 60–100 FPS range at 1080p on high. When you dial down a texture setting or two, you’ll have a comfortable envelope to chase 144 Hz if the rest of your system cooperates.
- In more demanding modern releases (Cyberpunk 2077, Metro Exodus, Red Dead Redemption 2), expect roughly 30–60 FPS at 1080p on high with some texture concessions. The card isn’t a miracle worker, but it’s a pragmatic one, offering smooth experiences with a touch of discipline.

To keep things practical, here are some qualitative takeaways:
- 1080p gaming: excellent for the vast majority of titles. You’ll be able to run most modern games at high settings with frame rates that feel snappy and responsive.
- 1440p gaming: possible in several titles, but you’ll be trading some eye candy for steady frame rates. It shines when you keep the resolution at 1080p and use the 1660 Super as a stable cockpit for your esports fantasies.
- Ray tracing: don’t count on it. If you want global illumination and fancy reflections, you’ll be disappointed unless you step up to an RTX-class card or rely on software-based tricks and good old-fashioned optimization.

## Power, Thermals, and Noise

Power draw is modest, typically in the 120–125W ballpark. This means you can pair it with a mid-range CPU without fearing a solar flare in your power supply. Thermals depend a lot on the board partner and the cooler design, but generally you’ll find single-fan cards spinning quietly enough for most living rooms, and dual-fan designs providing a bit more headroom for sustained loads.

- Noise: quiet under light gaming, noticeable but not annoying under heavy loads; plenty of cooler designs exist to keep fans from turning into hummingbird-level whisperers.
- Temperatures: expect 60–75C under typical gaming loads with adequate airflow. In a cramped mini-ITX build with poor case ventilation, you might see the temps creep higher—so ensure you have good airflow if you’re pushing limits.

## Overclocking Potential

Overclocking the 1660 Super isn’t a magic wand. You can probably push a modest 5–15% performance uplift with careful voltage control and tuning, but the cultural truth remains: the biggest gains come from memory bandwidth and driver optimizations rather than sheer core frequency. If you enjoy chasing every last frame, you might squeeze a touch more out of a moderate overclock; if you’re chasing high numbers, you’ll likely hit diminishing returns quickly.

A note from the lab: do not pair your overclock with a laptop's throttling mentality. Desktops only, kids. Also, keep an eye on temps—the more you clock, the hotter it gets. And nobody likes a hot gaming session that ends with a throttling scare.

### Practical Buying Notes
- Look for a card with a solid cooler and reasonable noise levels. If you’re in a hot case or a compact build, consider a dual-fan design rather than a small, single-fan card.
- Check the memory speed and bandwidth. The 14 Gbps GDDR6 makes a big difference here; a 1660 with slower memory can feel noticeably less zippy in texture-heavy scenes.
- Consider your PSU. A 450W to 550W unit is plenty for a 1660 Super with a mid-range CPU, but always verify your power needs with your entire system before you pull the trigger.
