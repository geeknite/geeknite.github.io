## What is bike outer casing cable housing
Cable housing is a pretty straightforward bit of tubing that sits over the steel or braided wire that actually moves on your bike. On the inside there are various lining materials designed to reduce friction and to limit compression. The outer diameter of the housing is what matters for compatibility with the frame guides, the cable ferrules, and the shifters or brakes you have installed. The two common diameters you will run into are 4 mm and 5 mm, with the 4 mm typically serving derailleur housings and the 5 mm serving brake housings. In some setups you will encounter slightly different variants such as compressionless housing or housing with a steel braid reinforcement. These can alter the effective diameter, the stiffness, and the market price, but in practice most people will be choosing between 4 and 5 mm for a standard street or trail bike.

In the sacred texts of wheels and gears, the right diameter makes a difference in how smoothly your lever action translates to wheel movement. If you choose the wrong diameter, your shifts might feel like a sewer pipe pushing a mouthful of marbles. If you choose the right diameter, your cables move with the grace of a ninja cat sliding along a rail, and you swear you could hear your chain whispering, thank you, kind rider. We do not want melodrama here, but we do want a product that performs, lasts, and feels like it was picked by someone who has spent more than a casual lunch break thinking about housing diameters.

### 4 mm vs 5 mm: sizes explained
The 4 mm housing is typically made to house derailleur cables and internal routing in many cross bikes and road bikes. The 4 mm diameter tends to be more flexible, easier to route through tight guides, and cheaper to replace. The 5 mm housing is the standard for brake systems on most bikes because brakes have to handle higher loads and more compression resistance. A 5 mm housing resists squashing under tension when you pull hard on the brake lever. The tradeoff is that the 5 mm housing is stiffer, less flexible in tight corners, and can be a little heavier and pricier per meter than 4 mm. If you are upgrading a road bike with new cable routing that involves long straight runs, 5 mm brake housing might feel like a direct performative upgrade. If your build is light and you want snappy shifts, a 4 mm derailleur housing could be your friend. In some modern bikes with fiber internal frames or internal cable routing, you may still encounter a 5 mm outer diameter even for derivative tasks, simply because the frame guides are built around that size.

### Material and construction variations
Not all 4 and 5 mm housings are created equal. Basic non coated, non compressionless housing typically uses a plastic or polymer lining and a flexible outer jacket. Premium versions may include compressionless cores, which minimize flex and improve shift feel, especially on longer cable runs. Some housings include a stainless steel braid on the outside for abrasion resistance, which can be beneficial for downhill or frequent cross country use where the housing might rub against rough surfaces in the frame or fork. There are also armored options that include additional layers to resist damage from potential impacts. These variants can influence both weight and price per meter, so if you are chasing a bargain, the simplest non compressionless 5 mm brake housing or 4 mm derailleur housing will usually suffice for most casual riders.

## Price per meter: what to expect
If you are browsing in a bike shop or browsing online, the price per meter for outer housing can be surprisingly variable. Here is a practical range to help you budget for a normal maintenance or upgrade project without turning your wallet into a flat tire:

- Basic 4 mm derailleur housing: roughly 0.50 to 1.50 per meter. This is the bread and butter option for casual riders and new setups with straightforward routing. If you are buying a bundle or a length that will cover multiple sections, you can often get a tiny discount on per meter price.
- Basic 5 mm brake housing: roughly 1.00 to 2.50 per meter. This range covers the most common standard housings from well known brands. Expect better performance, a nicer finish and slightly lower compression in premium lines within this price range.
- Compressionless and premium 5 mm or 4 mm options: roughly 2.50 to 6.00 per meter. Compressionless housing is designed to minimize stretch under load, which improves shifting accuracy on longer runs or on bikes with high tension in the shift system. Armor or braided sheathing can push the price higher, but for riders who ride aggressively or who route a lot of housing through rough frame sections, the extra cost can be worth it.
- Budget and clearance lines: sometimes you will encounter generic or store brand 4 and 5 mm options priced lower than the mainstream brands. The quality difference may be small for a casual ride but if you ride in wet or muddy conditions you might notice more fraying or stiffness after a season.

A practical approach to budgeting is to estimate your total run length in meters, multiply by the price per meter, and then add a little buffer for waste, miscuts and future maintenance. If you are unsure about the exact amount you need, it is safer to buy a longer length and cut it to size rather than buying a few short pieces. The idea is to avoid a situation where you run out of housing mid project and must wait for a new shipment to finish the job. Also remember that the price per meter often drops a little when you buy by the spool or by multi meter length, so a slightly larger purchase with a clear plan can save a few dollars.

## How to choose the right diameter for your setup
Choosing between 4 mm and 5 mm is mostly about the job the housing is doing. Here is a simple decision guide you can use in a real shop or a quick online cart hunting session:

- If you are replacing brake housing, go with 5 mm. This diameter is designed to handle the greater compression and higher forces that brakes generate.
- If you are replacing derailleur housing or doing internal routing for the derailleurs, 4 mm is usually sufficient and offers more flexibility in tight routing spaces.
- If you have a modern frame with internal routing and you are swapping to a smoother inner cable, you might find either diameter feasible depending on the internal cable guides. In some frames with small internal cavities, you may run into a conflict if you choose the larger 5 mm. In such cases, check the frame manufacturer specs or consult a local bike shop technician for guidance.
- If you care deeply about ride feel and tension, consider a compressionless housing. It helps maintain consistent shifting and reduces the feel of cable stretch during long rides. It does cost more, but it can be a good investment if you are chasing performance.
- If you live in a region with a lot of moisture or road salt, consider a housing option with better abrasion resistance and a coil that resists rust. The extra cost here is not just about price per meter but about the long-term life of the material.

### Practical decision making for a real project
Let us imagine you are upgrading both brake and derailleur housing on a mid range mountain bike. You decide to run a 4 mm derailleur housing for the rear derailleur along with a 5 mm brake housing for the front. The frame has a couple of tight routing sections that require careful bending. In this case you would want a slightly more flexible 4 mm for the derailleur and a 5 mm for the brakes to ensure you can thread the housing through the guides without snagging. You might then opt for a compressionless option on the derailleur for better shift feel in the long runs and perhaps a standard non compressionless 5 mm for the brakes if you want the cost to stay within a reasonable budget. In practice, the decision is about the path the housing takes and the amount of force that the system will apply to the housing. Given a typical MTB ride, the 5 mm brake housing will feel stiffer but more robust, while the 4 mm derailleur housing will feel more forgiving in tight spots. The most important thing is to route the housing properly and to use ferrules that lock in the ends to keep things from sliding in the frame.

### How to measure and cut housing correctly
To avoid waste and ensure clean cable runs, you should cut the housing with a dedicated tubing cutter or a very sharp utility knife. It is important to cut squarely and to avoid crushing the ends of the housing. In most cases you will want to use ferrules at both ends of the runs to ensure a tight seal and to prevent the housing from sliding out of the guide. If you are routing through a frame with internal guides, you may need to use a small pull cord to thread the housing through the internal guides. This is a common trick that saves time and reduces the risk of damaging the frame. After installing the housing, test the system by applying a gentle pressure on the lever and observing how the derailleur or brake behaves under load. A good setup will feel smooth and responsive with little play; a sloppy setup will feel spongy, and you may hear a little squeak or rattle when you apply the brakes.


## Durability and performance tests
We all want gear that lasts longer than our old college textbooks and performs better than a caffeine fueled sprint. When evaluating housing in a real world context consider the following tests and experiences:

- Friction test: Run a length of housing along a guide and compare the movement of the inner cable with and without lubrication. A well lubricated and correctly sized housing will show less resistance and smoother movement. If you notice heavy friction, it may indicate that the inner cable is not well matched to the housing or that the lining is worn out.
- Compression test: On a bicycle, the housing will compress as you apply load to the cable. A high quality compressionless housing will show far less elongation under load, which translates into more precise shifts and consistent braking power. This matters especially on longer rides where constant pressure is applied for long periods.
- Abrasion resistance: Expose the housing to grit, salt, or mud and observe how the outer sheath holds up over time. A robust housing will resist abrasion and not crack, corrode, or separate from the ferrules. In more rugged environments, the extra cost of armored housing may pay off in the long run.
- Temperature and humidity tolerance: Some materials degrade or stiffen when exposed to extreme environmental conditions. In a snowy mountain pass or a hot desert, you want a housing that does not become brittle or overly stiff. The premium options are typically tested across a broader range of conditions, making them more reliable in adverse climates.

### Common mistakes to avoid
- Forgetting ferrules at the ends of the housing runs. Ferrules keep the ends from fraying and prevent the housing from being crushed at routing points.
- Cutting housing with a dull knife. This can crush the end and make it difficult to insert the inner cable. Always use a proper housing cutter or a sharp tool.
- Mixing diameters in long runs without checking compatibility. A sudden change in diameter where you cannot route the housing cleanly may cause binding or poor movement.
- Not factoring in the frame design. Some frames have specific requirements for the type and size of housing, particularly if they use integrated cable routing or unique ferrule geometry.

### A note on compatibility and future upgrades
When you upgrade your housing, you may find that jerking your brakes or rough shifts are not the only symptom. Sometimes the lever pull will feel different if the new housing changes the cable tension due to a different friction coefficient in the lining. In such cases, you might be forced to re-tweak the entire shifting system, including the cable length and the barrel adjuster. If you are planning a big upgrade, write down a rough plan of the steps you will take, the parts you will replace, and the order in which you will do things. A plan helps you avoid a scenario where you buy a length of 5 mm brake housing that does not fit due to internal frame routing or a miscalculated length.
