### How to approach solving: practical strategy for a 1000-piece journey
If you’re reading this while contemplating whether to start or to return to an unfinished board, here are real-world strategies that work well for this particular Disney piece:

1) Prep and edge first: Separate edge pieces from the interior, then assemble the border. This creates your frame, both literally and metaphorically, and gives you a sense of progress that’s essential for motivation.

2) Color block scouting: Sort pieces by color families. You’ll be surprised how quickly you can assemble a teacup blue section or a pink-hued card-splash area. This speeds up the process when you’re staring down a field of purple mushrooms and wondering if you should bring a color wheel to your dining table.

3) The face and wardrobe anchor: The characters’ distinctive outfits act as semi-mottos for piece shapes. The Hatter’s hat, Alice’s dress, and the Cheshire Cat’s grin provide recognizable landmarks that help you anchor a few key pieces more quickly.

4) Use the reference image strategically: Keep it handy, but don’t rely on it too slavishly. The joy of a puzzle is the discovery—the moment you realize a piece belongs to the teacup’s handle rather than its saucer is oddly gratifying.

5) Work in short bursts: Don’t marathon it in one go unless you’re a machine. Short sessions with interval breaks keep your eyes fresh and your jaw from cramping from the ‘Ah-ha!’ mouth-wide grin you’ll inevitably adopt when you find that one elusive piece.
