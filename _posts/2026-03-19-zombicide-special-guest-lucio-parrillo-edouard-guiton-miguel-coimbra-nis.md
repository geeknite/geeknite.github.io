## The Artists’ Styles: Parrillo, Guiton, Coimbra
To truly appreciate this Special Guest Lot, you need to know the artists behind the figures. Each brings a distinct flavor that will be instantly recognizable to fans and newcomers alike.

### Lucio Parrillo: Dark fantasy with a surgical line
Parrillo’s work is all about bold silhouettes, dramatic lighting, and creatures that feel like they could leap off the page and bite your lunch. In this Zombicide batch, his influence shows in zombie silhouettes that have a hint of noir fashion—think trench coats flipping in a windy alley while the undead lurch forward with a predator’s intent. If you like your zombies with capes that billow like the opening scene of a rainy fantasy epic, Parrillo’s style is going to scratch that itch. The survivors under his spell carry a swagger; they aren’t just ready to shoot or stab, they look like they’ve studied bad decisions and come out ahead of them.

### Edouard Guiton: Industrial grunge meets cyberpunk grit
Guiton’s design language is all about texture: rugged armor, scratched metal, weathered leather, and a taste for the post-apocalyptic chic. The Guiton-influenced figures carry a weight that says they’ve survived multiple rounds of zombie onslaughts and still remembered to bring a flashlight that actually works. The skin tones, fabrics, and gear show a painter’s obsession with tiny details—the strips of tape on a backpack, the dent on a helmet, the subtle tension in a scarred leg sleeve. His sculpts feel like they’re ready to jump into a painterly diorama and hold a mini-festival of doom.

### Miguel Coimbra: Gothic elegance with a feral edge
Coimbra brings a lean, almost primal aesthetic to the table. The survivors in his range feel lit from within, as if their courage glows with a kind of morbid poetry. The zombie hordes under Coimbra’s influence lean into the grotesque with a surprising grace; their sinews ripple with life as you tilt the figure under the lamp. If you enjoy painting minis that look like they walked straight out of a dark romance novel, Coimbra’s work hits that sweet spot perfectly.

In short: Parrillo’s creatures punch you in the eyes with bold silhouettes, Guiton adds texture and weathered grit, and Coimbra gives you that gothic, high-contrast drama. Put together, the Special Guest Lot isn’t merely a set of miniatures; it’s a curated stage where three artists collaborated without colliding into the usual end-of-run color choices. The result is a box that has you thinking in paint names and color theories instead of just “red” and “brown.”

## Component Quality and Painting Potential
Let’s talk shop: the real test of a limited edition like this is not just how it looks on a shelf, but how much it invites you to paint, customize, and game with it. The kit plays nicely with the core Zombicide rules, but it also carries the aura of a “collectible premium” line. The plastics are crisp, the torsos and limbs fit reasonably well, and the faces have enough micro-texture to keep you busy with tiny highlights without losing the overall silhouette in the lighting of your hobby corner.

### Sculpt integrity and assembly
CMON’s sculpts typically respect a standard of clean mold lines and well-placed peg points. In this lot, you’ll notice the sprues have a few extra support aids for the dynamic poses—these are helpful for beginners and fun for the more patient painter who wants to preserve every nuance of the expression on the character’s face. Some pieces will reward hobbyists with a slightly fiddly assembly style, forcing you to plan your color sequence and basing early. The good news: there are enough alternate posing options to give your table a different vibe from the usual single-pose zombie wave.

### Painting tips tailored for the trio
- Parrillo’s zombies love high-contrast shadows. Start with a midtone base, apply a dark wash, and bring back highlights with dry brushing on the edges. The silhouette benefits from black-lining around the eyes and mouth—just enough to give a cinematic effect without turning the mini into a cartoon.
- Guiton’s textures respond well to a weathered palette. Use a base of olive drab or brown leather for gear, then add salt-washed metallics for armor accents. A light rust pigment on metal edges sells the industrial vibe.
- Coimbra’s figures thrive under a dramatic lighting plan. Consider a cool blue or violet secondary light on armor and fabrics to create that moody gothic look. Skin tones should lean towards slightly cooler pinks to pop against the darker clothing.

If you’re new to painting, this set is both a stress test and a reward. You’ll count to five, realize you’ve already spent two hours shading leather, and still not regret a single moment. If you’re a veteran, you’ll appreciate the range of textures and the subtle details that reward careful blending and edge highlighting.
