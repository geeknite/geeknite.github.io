## How the Game Works: A Playthrough Microguide
Exploding Kittens is deceptively simple. The objective is to avoid drawing an Exploding Kitten card. You do this by playing various action cards that disrupt the draw sequence, attack the turn of others, or help you peek into future draws. The Defuse card is the lifeline. If you draw an Exploding Kitten and you have a Defuse, you can play it to defuse the bomb and place the Exploding Kitten back into the deck secretly (or in some variants, you shuffle it back into the deck with strategy). The round ends when all but one player has exploded their kitten and is eliminated.

The pace of play is the real charm. It moves in quick bursts: a See the Future reveals a glimpse of your fate; a Shuffle disrupts the upcoming cards; a Skip or Attack can force someone else to draw. The humor lands most in the timing—the moment when a seemingly safe draw is followed by a rousing chorus of gasps and laughter as someone pulls the dreaded card. The sealed edition’s quality helps maintain that crisp, fresh feel as you shuffle. You’ll probably argue about who shuffled best, who misread the future, and which player’s cat-skin hoodie is technically acceptable in public spaces.

### Turn Structure (A Rough Template)
- Draw a card at the start of your turn.
- You may play one or more action cards to affect the deck or other players.
- If you didn’t draw the Exploding Kitten and survive, your turn ends and the next player continues.
- If you draw an Exploding Kitten, you must have a Defuse to survive; otherwise, you’re out and the deck reshuffles with your dramatic exit noted in the group chat later.

The balance between luck and mischief is what makes Exploding Kittens memorable. It’s not about calculating probabilities; it’s about reading the room, reacting to the group’s vibe, and letting your inner chaos chef cook up a deliciously ridiculous turn of events. In a new-sealed box, you’re guaranteed a pristine starting point, which matters when your group wants to jump right into chaos without any pre-existing deck wear or odd card curls from a previous owner’s coffee spill.
