If you have ever wanted to feel like a treasure hunter, but the treasure is a stack of cardboard that smells vaguely of crushed dreams and bubblegum, you have cracked open the world of Dragon Ball Super Card Game Masters mixed lots. ## What is a Masters Mixed Lot

A Masters mixed lot is exactly what it sounds like: a grab bag of cards pulled from the Dragon Ball Super Card Game Masters line, but not curated. You will find common cards, uncommons, and rarities that are non holo. The holo versions may be present, but the focus here is on the everyday heroes of the collection the non holo rares and their less glamorous cousins. These lots are popular for beginners who want to dip a toe into the DBS TCG pool without breaking the bank, collectors looking to complete playsets on a budget, or investors hoping that one or two underpriced non holo rares explode in value with the release of a new set or a rotate of the meta.

In practice, what you get can vary significantly. Some batches are a gold mine of draft rares that never saw a tournament table. Others feel like someone sorted cards by color but forgot to include the red one that actually matters. The charm is that you never know what you will pull, which is part of the dopamine that keeps our hobby addictively fun while we pretend our life hinges on a common card with a cool art pose from a past era.

> Quick tip: if you are new to this, manage expectations. You are not building a tournament-worthy deck from a non holo rare pile alone, unless you get unbelievably lucky or have an absurd amount of patience and a time machine to foil your own fate.

## The Anatomy of a Masters Mixed Lot

### Commons: bread, butter, and sometimes salad

Commons are the backbone of any mixed lot. They are plentiful, inexpensive, and they teach you what the set is about without requiring a mortgage on your soul. Expect a lot of filler from the base set, a few classic character shoutouts, and maybe a meme or two that somehow survived to the modern era of limited print runs. The benefit of commons is that you can round out a playset without feeling like you robbed a local game shop in broad daylight. The downside is that many commons do not appreciate much in price, which means your flipping strategy will rely on bulk value rather than standout rare sightings.

To maximize value from commons, sort them by set, then by reprint status. Some commons from early blocks reappear in reprint sets with subtle art changes. If you manage to assemble a nice stack of high-demand commons that saw limited print runs, you could fetch a surprising price on platforms that love to brag about every dollar small cards can generate.

### Uncommons: the undercover heroes

Uncommons are the sweet spot between cheap bulk and potential value. They often feature better artwork, more iconic characters, and mechanics that actually see play in some decks. In a Masters mixed lot, you will find enough uncommons to justify sorting the pile with a coffee in hand and a smug grin on your face. The right uncommons can unlock specific synergy in older decks, or be the missing pieces to a budget-friendly modern build. The trick with uncommons is knowing which ones pop up repeatedly across multiple sets and which are only good for a one-off combo.

When evaluating uncommons, look beyond the card name. Consider ability keywords, mana costs, and the deck archetypes they support. Some uncommons become surprisingly valuable because they enable a staple combo, while others become underappreciated because the art is not as catchy as the holo versions. If you are serious about turning a mixed lot into a respectable collection, you should catalog each uncommon by set, ability, and potential synergy, then chase buyers who love specific archetypes.

### Non holo rares: the sometimes shiny disappointment with hidden value

Non holo rares are the stars of the show for some collectors and the bane for others. They carry rarer print runs than commons and uncommons, yet their non holo status means they lack the sparkle that makes holo foils craveable. The non holo rares, however, often come with more robust collectability than their holo counterparts in certain print runs because they were produced in larger quantities but still have a place in deck strategies.

Key things to look for in non holo rares include: set relevance, signature characters, and any reprints that gained new popularity due to changed meta. A non holo rare that is a cross-set reprint of a popular character can be surprisingly valuable, especially if the card plays well in competitive formats or emerges as a favorite for casual players who love the character. The value curve for non holo rares can be volatile; do not be surprised if one week a non holo rare is a couple of dollars and the next week a few copies fetch a surprising sum due to a deck archetype resurfacing or a local tournament feed posting a new list.

## How to Sort and Grade a Masters Mixed Lot

Sorting a mixed lot is not about heroics; it is about order, discipline, and a little bit of spreadsheet sorcery. Here is a practical workflow you can adopt:

1. Clear a surface and lay out the lot. Put a light source above your workspace so you can actually see the print quality on each card. If you have a magnifying glass, now is time to use it for the ultra-chill vibes.
2. Separate cards by rarity: common, uncommon, rare non holo. If you happen to find holo or parallel versions, set those aside for a separate evaluation. You can laugh at your luck later when you realize holo market value surged after a weekend tournament.
3. Within each rarity, sort by set. This will help you quickly identify reprints and potential value players love to chase. Also, it makes listing on selling platforms easier.
4. Check card condition. In bulk lots, expecting near mint is unrealistic, but you can do a quick readability check. Bents in corners, whitening on edges, and heavy creases will dramatically affect price. If you are selling, be transparent about condition; if you are buying, use a gentle approach and offer a fair price that reflects wear.
5. Price as you go. Use price guides as a rough guide, but adjust based on market trends. If a lot of cards in one set have a sudden spike due to a new deck archetype, you can reflect that in your pricing. Do not chase every price wave; the goal is to move the lot, not to become a full-time price shark.

Jekyll tip: keep a running tally in a small notebook or a one-off spreadsheet. It helps you stay sane when you realize you own more non holo rares than you thought, which is a mild existential crisis that comes with the territory of card collecting.

## Pricing and Value: What to Expect from Non Holo Rares

The value of non holo rares is highly contextual. In practice, you should expect a wide range of outcomes:

- Common non holo rares may be worth a few cents to a couple of dollars depending on their set and how badly people want specific characters in non holo form for deckbuilding or binder completeness.
- Uncommons can range from a few dollars to tens of dollars for sought-after print runs, especially if they support popular archetypes or come with unique art variants that collectors appreciate.
- Rare non holo cards can sometimes surprise you with a price spike if they unlock a strong synergy or if a new deck concept uses them as core pieces. Do not be shocked if a non holo rare roars to the top of a price list during meta shifts, even if it looked unassuming in the box.

This is where a good price guide becomes a friend rather than a foe. You do not need to live in the price wars; you just need to know enough to price your lot fairly and to identify any hidden gems. A pragmatic approach is to value the lot as a combination of a bulk buy with a few potential highlight cards. You want to avoid overpricing on a pile of ordinary cards and you also want to avoid underpricing if there are a handful of non holo rares that could command more attention.

External resources that can help you calibrate price without relying on memory alone include the official DBS Card Game site and price guides such as TCGPlayer. While you should not treat price guides as gospel, they can serve as a reference point for what the market is currently comfortable with. Remember that local markets matter too; a lot that is underpriced in a big city might still go for a modest sum in a smaller town because supply and buyer pools differ.

## Practical Tips for Maximizing Your Mixed Lot Experience

- Sort and bundle for bulk sale. If you have large stacks of commons, consider selling them in bulk to buyers who are building playsets. Bulk listings are incredibly popular for players on a budget or for teachers running classroom events where students need a starter pool.
- Create value-added bundles. Pair a selection of non holo rares with a couple of uncommons that complement a popular deck archetype. This simple combo can be enough to entice buyers who want ready-to-play options.
- Use high-quality photos. A clear shot of the cards with lighting that shows the card fronts is essential. Shiny sleeves, modest backgrounds, and a tidy workspace can go a long way toward convincing buyers that you are not a shady card stealer from the Dark Realm.
- Be honest about condition. If you have a small amount of whitening or corner wear, state it up front. Buyers will appreciate honesty and you will avoid negative feedback that can ruin your next sale before it starts.
- Consider local options. Local Facebook groups, Reddit communities, and local game shops may offer better terms for bulk lots than national marketplaces. Local sales can be fast and avoid shipping damage concerns entirely.

## A Note on Display and Storage

Non holo rares deserve a dignified life as part of a binder, a portfolio, or a storage box that does not involve ziploc bags that smell vaguely of gym socks and ceremonial regret. For best results, sleeve non holo rares individually and label them with set information and rarity. Keep your sleeves away from magnets and extreme heat; you want your cards to stay flat, legible, and prevent the dreaded curling that makes a card look like it did a bad impression of a ramp in a low-budget sci fi film.
