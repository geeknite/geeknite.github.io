## Blade and tang

### Blade geometry
The blade is designed for looks rather than power. It commonly uses 1065 carbon steel or a similar alloy, with a dark finish or simple polish that shows a toothy edge rather than a razor edge. The geometry emphasizes a graceful arc and a convincing silhouette over lab-grade performance. The bevel is functional enough to hold an edge for display and light practicing but not intended for cutting through dense targets. The curvature is historically accurate enough to give you that cinematic line when you swing.

### Full tang
A hallmark feature for this line is the “full tang” claim. In practice, you’ll notice the blade’s metal runs into the handle, giving more confidence in everyday handling. Don’t treat this as an Olympic-level training blade, but it’s a step above a decorative piece and enough to feel sturdy when you pose with dramatic tension.

## Maintenance and care

Maintenance for this blade is straightforward. The key is moisture control and routine oiling. Wipe after contact with skin to remove oils; apply a light coat of mineral oil to reduce rust risk; avoid soaking the blade or exposing it to humidity for extended periods. If you’re going to display it in a cold, dry room, you’re good. If your house is humidity-challenged, consider a small display case with a dehumidifier.

If you have other display pieces, a simple care routine helps unify the look. Keep the blade clean, the wrap intact, and your display space dust-free.

## Safety and training

Even though it’s a budget piece, treat it like a real sword during demonstrations. Keep the blade away from eyes and faces, wear safety goggles when demonstrating martial moves, and stay in a controlled space. Use a practice blade for training if you intend to perform choreographies or forms; the Honshu Wakizashi is not designed for aggressive training or repeated impact on hard targets.
