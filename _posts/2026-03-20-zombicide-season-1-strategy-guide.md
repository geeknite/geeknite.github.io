## What is Zombicide Season 1 anyway?

Zombicide Season 1 is the original invitation card to the world of cooperative zombie relief work. You take on the role of survivors in a city overrun by the undead. Your objective varies by mission, but the core idea stays the same: work together, manage your weapons, keep the Felicity of the group intact, and do not let a zombie chain plan you out of your turn. The rules are simple but the challenges scale up like a dramatic sunburn: slowly, painfully, and with more zombies than you remember you could handle.

## Core mechanics refresher

Zombicide Season 1 uses a shelf of familiar mechanics: you control a team of survivors, each with limited actions per turn, and you use search actions to scavenge weapons and gear. The zombies chase you with relentless zeal; the only thing more certain than a zombie biting you is your next action being a dice roll that might fail. The key is to balance movement, range, and the number of weapons you carry. In this season the zombie spawn mechanism is mean, but predictable enough that you can learn to use corners as your friend. Terrain is both a friend and a trap—great for escaping a tight pocket of walkers, terrible when you realize you’re blocked in by your own team.

### The survivors: roles and tools

In Season 1 you pick a squad of survivors, each with a distinct skill path. The cards give you options: soldiers who can shoot better, scientists who can search for tech, and civilians who can hold doors like a hero on a sitcom. The trick is to build a roster that complements each other; if you have four people who love to shoot but hate to search, you will be soon eating brains in a room full of tape measures and missed opportunities. The key is to mix roles so one person can hold the line while another fetches weapons or opens new routes.

- Scout types: the fast mover with a knack for escaping danger. They can explore quickly and set up safe zones.
- Tanks: the heavy hitters who can soak up damage and create space for teammates to operate.
- Medics: essential to keep the squad breathing; their ability to remove wounds or heal teammates becomes critical in longer campaigns.
- Builders: heavy on utility, who can deploy barricades, control zones, and shore up a bad situation with clever positioning.

### Weapons and equipment: what actually matters

Season 1 grants you a wide array of weapons, from knives and blunt instruments to pistols and the occasional shotgun. The order of operations is simple: determine the risk, decide on a weapon to use, apply the damage, and pray to the dice gods. Artefacts and equipment cards can turn the tide of a mission, but scarcity makes every pick precious. The rule of thumb is that rarer items do not just deal more damage; they are the tools that turn a bad turn into a salvage mission. Two tips: carry a ranged weapon if you’re the other side of a wall, and always transfer an ambidextrous weapon to a teammate who will actually use it.

### Zombies and spawn logic: staying ahead of the bite

Zombies in Season 1 aren’t mindless chaos; they operate under a simple, cruel rhythm. They spawn in waves, crowding the map in ways that make you want to start a confessional. The trick is to use line-of-sight and choke points to funnel the undead into predictable patterns. The more you play, the more you realize that the most dangerous units aren’t the biggest zombies but the ones that come in groups that break your formation. Keep your rotation tight, don’t overextend, and never leave a teammate to draw a line for the entire horde on their own.

### Board layout and map control: why corners matter

Your map is a playground of secrets and hazards. The Season 1 board is filled with doors, rooms, and loot stashes that can be secured to keep the team flush with resources. You want to secure choke points, set up defensive perimeters, and carve safe routes to the rescue squares. Do not chase loot in exposed areas; there’s a reason why the center of the board looks like a bad idea. If you can lock down a corner and hold it until reinforcements arrive, you’ve already won half the battle. The other half is getting out alive.

## Strategy pillars: how to win without losing your mind

### Pillar 1: board control and zone ownership

In Season 1, the map is a set of rooms connected by doors. The safest route to survival is to claim a handful of rooms as your own; you can use these rooms as staging grounds for safe zones, resource caches, and fallback positions. The rule of thumb is simple: if you can force your zombies into a single doorway, you can kill them one by one without inviting more to the party. When in doubt, adopt zone ownership. If you own a room and your teammates own the connecting doors, you can retreat safely and plan a counter-ambush on the next turn.

### Pillar 2: management of actions and turns

Every turn, your survivors have a fixed pool of actions. You want to optimize every action for the maximum effect: a search here, a move there, a shot with a reliable weapon. Don’t waste actions on reckless heroics that get you bitten and murdered by a closet full of screaming zombies. The real power is in sequencing: pick an action that unlocks more options next turn. If you search a room for a key item and then wait for reinforcements, you can leave the early rounds feeling like you’re slowly building a gear library rather than a panic attack. 

### Pillar 3: coordination and roles

A good team works because each member has a unique role and a plan for how to help the others. Cultivate a rotation: a scout that opens doors, a medic who patches the wounds, a tank that strafes the horde, and a builder who creates choke points. When you have a plan for two or three turns ahead, you feel less like a lost cat and more like a well-oiled zombie-slaying machine. We recommend a 2–3 turn plan whenever possible; it’s a lot easier to survive when you know where your teammates will be and what they will do.

### Pillar 4: risk assessment and resource economy

Zombies aren’t the only threat. The scarcity of weapons and gear is an invisible antagonist that will crush your morale faster than a day-old asteroid pizza. Plan your scavenges to minimize risk: if you can grab a weak item and still survive, do it; if you might lose a specialist for a few extra bullets, discuss with your team first. The economy of gear means that you should not hoard everything for personal glory; you will need to share and trade items to ensure the group’s overall survival. The moment you decide to keep a weapon to yourself is the moment you realize that you’re not playing co-op anymore — you’re playing solo with bad teammates.

> Pro-tip: If you’re stuck in a bad position, consider sacrificing a weak weapon to draw the horde away from a critical area. Your teammates will thank you when they’re not staring at a wall of undead and thinking about what they could have done differently.

## Early game strategies: setting the foundation

The first few rounds are the most important, because this is where you either secure a foothold or realize you’ll be leaving a few survivors behind to become part of the decor. Here are the big ideas to keep in mind during the early stages:

- Establish a safe zone early on. Choose one or two rooms that you can defend with minimal risk. The exact location will depend on the map, but aim for a spot with easy access to loot and a line-of-sight path to your teammates. If you can seal off a corner with a barricade or two, you’ll create a staging area for the rest of the mission.
- Prioritize searching for gear. While it can be tempting to run and grab every zombie if you’re feeling heroic, your gear is what will carry you through the tough rounds. If you can find a ranged weapon or a tool that improves your team’s odds, do it.
- Manage your risk exposures. Do not put a scout in a one-shot zone if you can avoid it. The goal is to minimize the number of deaths in early rounds while still acquiring essential items. If you must, accept the risk in exchange for a big payoff later on.
- Learn from the misses: if you fail a mission, analyze what could have been done differently. Debrief like a gamer who forgot to save the world last night but learned something valuable in the morning.

### A sample early-turn plan
On the first turn, if you have a light and fast scout, place them near a door that leads to a stash. On turn two, search the stash and hope for a weapon upgrade. If you get a weapon upgrade, assign an extra shooter to cover the other doors while the medic heals. In a worst-case scenario, if you find yourself with few options, concentrate your group into a safe zone and wait for the next spawn wave; sometimes the best action is to not act at all and let the board’s AI do the heavy lifting for you. This approach prevents you from bleeding survivors and keeps you in the running for the longer game.

## Mid to late game: when the horde grows up

The mid game is where you test your reliability as a team. The horde doesn’t get nicer; it gets bigger and more determined. A well-executed plan in the mid game hinges on four elements: synergy, resource distribution, environment control, and timing. You should aim to pass critical weapons to the survivors who will use them most effectively in the next wave. For example, a shotgun is excellent for a frontline fighter while a pistol is perfect for a scout who has to move quickly and stay alive. The main challenge is to avoid waffling between two bad options and pick something that benefits the team as a whole. A good rule of thumb is to convert every potential loot into a shared resource when possible; the team will thank you later when the last door opens and the horde pours in.

### Endgame tactics: the last stand

As the board begins to tilt in favor of the walkers, your goal is to survive until the final turn. The best strategy is to create a safe apex: a corner that the zeds can’t flood too quickly, with enough weapons to hold off the closing horde. If you can perform a last-minute scavenging run that supplies a critical weapon, you’ll turn the tide. If you can coordinate a trap with a barricade and a few fire weapons, you may just squeeze out a victory in the final moments.

## Scenario archetypes in Season 1 (and how to approach them)

Season 1 includes several mission archetypes with traditional goals: rescue survivors, retrieve an artifact, or clear an area of zombies. Here are a few archetypes and how to approach them with a co-op mindset:

- The Rescue Run: your team must extract a survivor from a dangerous zone. Plan ahead by clearing a path and placing the medic at the extraction point. If you fail, the rescued survivor becomes part of the horde in your next session, which is a great reminder of why you should save first and loot later.
- The Artifact Hunt: you search for an artifact that has a unique effect on the zombie populace. Prioritize the artifact that yields long-term benefits for your team. Keep an eye on the zombie spawn card that asks you to bring the artifact back to a safe zone; this is a great moment to set up a multi-step plan.
- The Area Control: you must defend a zone for multiple turns to ensure the mission’s success. The best approach is to position your defenders along the most effective lines of attack while your more mobile teammates search and gather resources in adjacent rooms.

> Pro-tip: use your search actions to create a solid pool of equipment. The more weapons and items you have, the better your odds of surviving the final encounter.

## House rules and variants you can try

If you’re playing with a regular group for weeks on end, you might want to experiment with optional rules to freshen things up. Some players add a “berserk mode” where a zombie horde spawns faster if a player has not found a weapon within the first three rounds. Others propose a “limited inventory” rule that forces players to discard items after a certain threshold. Of course, you can also invent your own crazy house rules: extra dice for critical moments, a hero die that changes outcomes, or a time-limited mission that requires players to finish within an hour. House rules encourage creativity and will almost certainly produce more memes than actual wins, but that’s fine—the fun is in the process.

## Common pitfalls to avoid

- Overloading a single survivor with too many powerful weapons. It makes them a magnet for the zombie horde and ruins the teamwork dynamic.
- Ignoring the map’s layout. It is easy to get tunnel vision and forget the rest of the board, which leads to a slow collapse and a dramatic failure montage.
- Skipping scouting opportunities. If you avoid scanning the map for loot, you will miss the chance to strengthen your team and position yourself for the next wave.
- Not coordinating with teammates. A mission is not a solo sprint; it is a team-building exercise with mind-blowing stakes and some horrific hair-pulling moments.

## Why Season 1 still matters in a world full of new skins

Season 1 was the seed from which many of the modern Zombicide expansions grew. It introduced the core mechanism of scavenging, teamwork, and dice-charged risk that continues to define the series. Even with newer seasons and expansions, Season 1 retains a nostalgia factor that makes players smile when they recall the first time they made a risky move that somehow paid off. It’s a great introduction to the franchise to see how the concept has evolved, and you’ll likely notice that some of the design decisions from Season 1 still echo in later editions. If you’re a completedist collector, Season 1 will always hold a special place in your gaming cabinet.

## Final recommendations and verdict

- If you crave chaotic, brain-mick-rolling co-op sessions with just enough clever planning to feel like a chess match against a cereal box of zombies, Zombicide Season 1 remains a gold standard of how to do cooperative board games right. It nails the balance between luck and skill, gives you a reason to work together rather than just mash the dice, and still leaves room for ridiculous stories that you’ll tell at parties for the next decade.
- It rewards players who think in sequences, map the board like a small-scale tactical operation, and learn to share resources without turning into a barrel of chaos.
- If you’re new to Zombicide, Season 1 is a friendly introduction that will teach you the language of co-op play and set you up for success in later seasons.

## Quick tips to remember

- Pick a diverse team with a good mix of offensive and support abilities.
- Favor zone control and safe routes over reckless direct assaults.
- Keep your gear organized and distribute items for the team’s benefit.
- Practice, debrief, and adjust your strategy turn by turn.
