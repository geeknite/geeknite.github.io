## Thermal and Power Considerations
Thermal behavior matters more than you might think when you’re pushing a 4TB drive to the red line for extended periods. The T705 is designed with a thermal regime that’s friendly to most cases with adequate airflow. In a mid-tower with a decent intake and exhaust, you’ll see robust sustained performance without the drive hitting a thermal throttle. In cramped laptops or compact SFF builds, you’ll want to ensure you’ve got some passive cooling or a small heatsink if available. The last thing you want is throttle-induced stutters during a long render or a critical game session.

Power draw is reasonable for a Gen4 NVMe drive. It’s not a “gluttonous puck of photons” like some high-power PCIe devices, but it’s also not a stone-cold minimalist. Under light usage, it sips power; under heavy loads, it performs, with the understanding that sustained bursts come with a power cost. In short: plan for adequate cooling and airflow, and you’ll reap the benefits in performance consistency.

## Compatibility and Installation
The T705 is an M.2 2280 form factor, compatible with most modern desktops and laptops that support PCIe Gen4 NVMe drives. If you’re building a new system, you’ll benefit from the drive’s full performance when paired with a Gen4 motherboard. If you’re on Gen3, you’ll still see a noticeable uplift over SATA-based storage, just not the peak numbers you’d see on a Gen4 platform.

Installation tips:
- Ensure you have an available M.2 slot and the PCIe lane configuration to maximize speed.
- Use a standard OS clone tool if you want to migrate your existing OS and data; or set up a fresh OS install and move data later.
- Consider enabling NVMe optimization in the BIOS for a smoother experience.
- Cable management and airflow matter: a tidy case with good airflow reduces the risk of thermal throttling and helps maintain steady performance.

### A Note on Thermal Throttling in the Real World
In sustained workloads (think long renders or marathon game sessions), you’ll observe gradual throttling if your case doesn’t offer decent airflow. That’s not a fault of the drive; that’s thermodynamics reminding you that even the quietest engine can overheat if you seal it inside a shoebox. The mitigation is simple: improve airflow, consider a small heatsink on the M.2, and avoid stacking high-power devices directly above the SSD if you can help it.
