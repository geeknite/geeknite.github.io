## Upgrades, Maintenance, and Long-Term Prospects
One of the selling points of open box units is the possibility of simple upgrades without breaking the bank. Here are practical upgrade paths:

- RAM: Jump from 16 GB to 32 GB if you intend to run virtualization, heavy multitasking, or modern multi-game streams. The motherboard style in this class usually supports at least 32 GB or more, depending on the model.
- Storage: If your unit ships with a single NVMe drive, add a second SSD for faster boot times and snappier game installs. You can also swap a small SSD into the M.2 slot for the OS and a larger SATA drive for mass storage if you prefer traditional hard drives.
- GPU: A GTX 1660 Super is not a bottleneck for 1080p, but you can upgrade to a stronger mid-range GPU if you plan to chase higher frame rates in newer titles. Be mindful of power supply ratings and clearance in the case when swapping GPUs.
- CPU: The CPU upgrade path will depend on the motherboard and socket. If you are eyeing a more modern core count and higher clocks, you might need a new motherboard and PSUs, which is a larger investment but worthwhile for a long-term build.

Regular maintenance includes cleaning dust from fans and heat sinks, updating BIOS and driver software, and keeping the system free from clutter in the OS. The open box status means you should verify that all drivers and essential software were properly installed by the seller and reinstalled after you perform any major hardware changes.

## Open Box Caveats and Buyer Tips
