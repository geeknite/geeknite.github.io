## How to play in a nutshell

Codenames is a two team word guessing game with a spymaster on each side. The spymaster gives a one word clue followed by a number, indicating how many words on the table relate to that clue. The clue words must help their team identify the red or blue word cards that match the clue. The opposing team is listening intently for trickery and hoping to snipe the other team with the wrong guess. The grid on the table shows 25 words arranged in five by five. The key card shows which words belong to red, which are blue, which are neutral, and which word is the assassin.

The game starts with a random key card that designates which words are red, which are blue, which are neutral, and which word is the assassin. The starting team is indicated by the color on the key card. The spymaster takes the first turn and tries to steer their team toward as many correct words as possible with a single clue. A clue can link several words, but you can never give a clue that points to the assassin or to words that belong to the other team. During play, team members guess the tied words in turn, saying the word aloud and revealing the corresponding card. If a team guesses a word that belongs to them, that card is turned over. If they guess a word that belongs to the other team, play passes to that team and the guessed card is turned over with the appropriate color. If a neutral word is chosen, play ends that turn and the other team gets the chance to guess.

And then there is the assassin. If your team accidentally guesses the assassin word, you instantly lose the game. This is where the tension and the thrill come from, and where the spymaster earns their stripes or eats their snacks in defeat. The game continues until one team has correctly identified all its words, wins the game, and celebrates with an overdramatic high-five.

Tip: a helpful starting approach is to cluster words that might share obvious categories like animals, foods, or everyday objects. But be careful not to rely on silly pun clues or famous names that could be mis interpreted by teammates who moved one too many cups of coffee before the game started.

An optional variant is to play with more than two players by rotating spymasters and keeping the same grid. The dynamic becomes a little more chaotic as you rotate the lead, but it can be a lot of fun for large gatherings where the clacking of tokens and the chorus of guesses becomes part of the evening soundtrack.

> Pro tip: avoid giving multiword clues that rely on obscure cross references. One word, one number tends to be enough to confuse and delight everyone at once.

## Expansions and alternate flavors worth noting

Codenames has several spin offs and variants that keep the concept fresh. The most popular are Codenames Pictures, where the word cards are replaced with a grid of pictures that require different kinds of associations. The Pictures version often adds a new layer of mind bending as players interpret images in context of their clues. Then there is Codenames Duet, a cooperative variant designed for two players or two teams that share a single grid of words. Duet flips the dynamic: both players cooperate against the clock to identify all words using limited clues. There are also more adult oriented variants like Deep Undercover which adds naughtier humor for specific crowds and a more risqué tone that may not be everyone's cup of tea.

If you are collecting CGE games or you like the original word game concept, these variants offer a familiar core with a twist in theme or play style.
