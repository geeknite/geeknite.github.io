## Setup and Basic Play

You don’t buy Codenames for the manual; you buy it for the moment a good clue collapses the entire table into cheers or groans. The New In Box CGE edition keeps the classic two-team structure, which is the essence of the game:

- Two teams, red and blue. Each team has a spymaster who can see the entire board, and field operatives who can only read the clue words and try to guess the associated cards.
- A grid of 5x5 (classic) word cards lays out on the table. Each card has a single word on the front.
- Spymasters take turns giving one-word clues and a number. The clue must connect as many of their team’s words as possible while avoiding the other team’s words, neutral words, and the infamous Assassin word.
- The opposing team tries to decode the clue with their own guesses. The first team to reveal all of their words wins. If you accidentally reveal the Assassin word, you end your team's eventful turn with a dramatic and sobering silence. It’s not just a game; it’s a life lesson about consequences.

If you’re new, the learning curve is pleasantly gentle: you can start with the simplest approach—spymaster gives a one-word clue plus a number, like “Ocean 2” to connect two blue-team words (e.g., “Whale,” “Harbor”). The rest of the team sifts through the board, attempting to make the connection. If they’re lucky, they hit two blue words; if not, they may hit a red word (hand wave, “oops”). The pace picks up as players develop a vocabulary strategy and a knack for deciphering each other’s thought processes. It’s a bit like a team sport of semantic agility, and you’ll learn more about your friends’ brains than any group photo can reveal.

## Gameplay Nuances: Strategy Without a Lab Coat

Codenames is famous for turning wordplay into a high-stakes team exercise. The CGE edition doesn’t drastically alter the core mechanics, but it does offer a few practical touches that affect the strategy:

- Clue-length discipline matters. A one-word clue is classic, but you’ll quickly realize that longer clues aren’t a free pass to cover every word. You still risk hitting an opponent word or the assassin. The challenge and elegance lie in finding a clever but safe thread that connects several of your words without stepping onto a minefield.
- The number in the clue is your permission to reveal multiple words. Sometimes you want to play it safe with “2” or “3,” and sometimes you gamble with a bigger number because your team’s momentum is built on a single, glorious chain. The CGE edition’s card clarity helps you evaluate risk more cleanly, which is nice when you’re under pressure to perform like a spy who isn’t sure which key card will unlock the next clue.
- The assassination risk is real. This is where the spymaster’s skill gets tested. A good clue can cause a wave of confident guesses, while a mis-specified clue can trigger a cascade of red words and a dramatic table shake. You’ll learn more about your group’s communication style than you will from most co-op games.

In terms of replayability, CGE’s layout lends itself to easy resets between rounds, which is essential for a social game. The rulebook is clear enough to handle a late-night rematch, but the real replay value comes from the player dynamics: which teammates can read you, which words on the board are traps, and how well you can adapt your clues to players who overanalyze every word while others blurt out a guess before finishing your clue sentence.
