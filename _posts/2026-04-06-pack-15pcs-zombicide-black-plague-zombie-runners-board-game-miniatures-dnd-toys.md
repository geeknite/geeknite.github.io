## Design, Details, and Aesthetics
### The Look and Feel
The runners feature skeletal frames stretched into apex predator poses. You’ll see ripped fabrics, torn leather, and the occasional belt buckle that has seen better decades. It’s not hyper-realistic gore; it’s stylized enough to fit the game’s pulpy vibe while remaining suitable for a broad table of players. The contrast between dark clothes and pale undead flesh helps keep the silhouettes clear on the board, which is a boon when you’re juggling multiple figs and trying to keep track of scuttling threats.

### Base and Stability
The runners sit on standard zombie bases; nothing fancy here, but it’s practical. The bases are wide enough to prevent tipping when you nudge the table in a moment of dramatic storytelling, and they’re solid enough to survive being knocked by the inevitable elbow of a nearby dice roll. If you enjoy posing miniatures on improvised terrain, you’ll appreciate how the runner silhouettes read clearly from a distance, which is essential when you’re trying to coordinate a five-figure horde with a single glance.

In most cases, these runners arrive as ready-to-play castings—no glue required. If you do decide to customize or magnetize bases for a more modular setup, you’ll find the process straightforward. ## In-Game Utility: How They Function on the Table
### Movement and Tactics
Zombie runners are, by design, the sprinting menace. They’re the ones that threaten to engulf survivors by moving swiftly across the board and jockeying for alternate routes to the party’s flank. In Black Plague, the runners typically have movement that makes them an aggressive but predictable threat; you can use them to force survivors into tough decisions—shoot or sprint, hold ground or fall back, risk a chance to break through the pack or stage a calculated retreat.

For game masters who enjoy the pacing of an escalating siege, a pack of 15 runners can create a compelling corridor of danger. They present a shared challenge: you can’t ignore them because they’ll flood your space, but you don’t want to invest everything into one single unit when multiple runners can overwhelm your survivors in a couple of rounds.

### Interaction with Survivors
These minis pair nicely with bigger zombies and special infected types. The aesthetic of runners serves as the frontline pressure in most scenarios, setting the tempo for the encounter. Because they are fast, you’ll want to coordinate objectives that require survivors to move quickly or to rely on environmental tricks to outpace the threat. The result is a dynamic clash where players must balance raw DPS with mobility and positioning.

### DND and RPG Cross-overs
If you’re using Zombicide figures as DND toys, these runners can function as quick-hitting undead scouts or dash-in threats in a homebrew encounter. Their visual design also helps players quickly categorize threats at a glance—great for table-wide narrative clarity during tense moments.

## Painting, Customization, and Hobby Tips
### Quick Painting Roadmap
- Prime with a light gray or off-white to bring out details.
- Basecoat the clothes in muted plums, browns, or dark greens for a plague-era vibe.
- Flesh tones should be pale with a touch of sickly yellow and blue in the shadows to emphasize the undead look.
- Dry brush with a lighter gray or bone color to highlight raised edges.
- Add gore with a red wash and a dab of bright red or magenta for a splashy, cinematic effect.
- Finish with matte varnish to protect against tabletop wear.

### Non-Combat Modding Ideas
If you want to tilt the table toward narrative flavor, consider giving a few runners small, thematic belongings—tattered banners, rusted tools, or oozing plague artifacts. These little touches make each figure feel unique, which helps when players start naming their adversaries and muttering about the creeping dread of the plague apocalypse.

### Storage and Handling
Because these are 15 slightly delicate pieces, store them in a padded tray or a dedicated mini box. Avoid stacking heavy items on top of them, especially if you’ve added basing elements like tufts or small scenery pieces. A simple clamshell with foam inserts keeps them organized, easy to grab mid-game, and less likely to become a casualty of the dreaded dice-rolling avalanche.

## Tabletop Setup and Thematic Tricks
### Board Etiquette with 15 Runners
A dense board setup with multiple runners creates a cinematic feel, particularly when paired with survivors who must decide between running through a tight corridor or turning to face the mob. Use lighting to your advantage: a dim room with a single overhead light can cast ominous shadows that make runners look more menacing as they surge forward. In a long corridor, line the runners up in a way that invites the players to think about bottlenecks and choke points.

### Terrain Interactions
Mix them with terrain that offers speed-based obstacles, like narrow stairwells, broken staircases, and shadowy alcoves. The runners will add a sense of urgency as they press forward, while your survivors maneuver around hazards. If you have modular dungeon tiles, place the runners on the far side of a chokepoint so players must choose between a risky sprint and a more measured approach—perfect for dramatic storytelling.
