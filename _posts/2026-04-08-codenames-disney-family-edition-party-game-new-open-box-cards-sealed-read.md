How to Play, in Brief

If you know the baseline Codenames structure, you’ll be right at home. A 5x5 grid of 25 cards sits in the center. Each card bears a Disney character. Two teams, red and blue, compete to uncover all of their agents. A spymaster on each team provides one-word clues that tie several of their team’s characters together without pointing to the opposing team’s agents or the assassin. A correct guess reveals an agent; an incorrect guess ends that team’s turn or, if they hit a danger card, ends the game for that team depending on the edition rules.

In the Disney Family Edition, the clues tend to revolve around familiar Disney lore: family relationships, shared traits, or cross-franchise connections. This makes the game friendlier for players who aren’t the kind of person who memorizes obscure film trivia. The one-word clue system rewards lateral thinking and a little bit of crowd-sourced strategy, as your teammates interpret whether the clue maps to a handful of characters you’ve covered in conversations during past game nights.

To run through a typical round: the spymaster studies the grid and chooses a one-word clue that links several of their team’s characters, followed by a number indicating how many cards on the grid are tied to that clue. The teammates then discuss and select cards they think match the clue. If they hit their own team’s agents, the cards are revealed and stay on the table. If they hit the opponent’s agents, those cards flip as well and the turn passes to the other team. If they hit a neutral card, nothing too dramatic happens, but the turn passes to the other team. The dreaded assassin card ends the round immediately—unless you treat it as a dramatic twist in your campaign of winning by cunning and not chaos.

Strategies and House Rules

If you are looking to up your game night, here are a few practical strategies that work well with this edition:
- Start with broad family ties: initial clues that group several characters by family roles or movie eras tend to produce multiple hits. For example, a clue about siblings across films can link a surprising number of characters from different franchises.
- Use cross-links sparingly: one word that loosely touches on multiple characters can be powerful, but be mindful of giving away the other team’s potential targets in the same breath. Balance risk and reward with your team’s confidence level.
- Read the board aloud with teammates: a little vocal collaboration helps you test the plausibility of your clue before you commit, especially when a character is less well-known to certain players.
- Don’t be afraid to adjust on the fly: if your clues land on everything but your team’s characters, step back and pivot to a safer theme that ties more clearly to the cards left on the grid.
- Rule variants for younger players: consider allowing a two word clue for families with kids who are still learning to connect ideas, as long as your table agrees. The goal is inclusive fun, not oppressive rigor.

Playtime Tips for Different Group Sizes

- Small groups (4 players): designate one spymaster per team and rotate the rest as guessers. This setup encourages discussion while keeping the turns brisk.
- Large groups (6 to 8 players): you can run a multi-team format or have two teams with two spymasters each. The dynamic changes, as rival riddles emerge and the table buzzes with independent clashing theories.
- Mixed ages: lean into characters that are widely recognizable. If a character is new to some players, a quick primer before the game can save time during the first round.
