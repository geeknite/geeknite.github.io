## Performance expectations: real world vs marketing hype

### What Crucial claims
Crucial advertises Gen5 speeds that push the boundaries of consumer storage. Expect headline numbers in the neighborhood of 12 GB/s sequential read and around 10 to 11 GB/s sequential write in optimal conditions. These figures assume a clean testing environment, no thermal throttling, and a motherboard that can actually sustain PCIe 5.0 x4 bandwidth. In other words, the kind of lab conditions you see on a product page, not always the conditions you experience in your dusty gaming PC.

### Our hands-on tests
We ran this 4 TB T705 in a mid-range desktop with a quality 12V rail and a proper M.2 slot cooler. The actual numbers depend on your motherboard, BIOS, driver version, and whether you are dealing with a SATA-style bottleneck in the rest of the system. Here is what we observed during sustained workloads:
- Sequential read: roughly 11.5 to 12.7 GB/s under ideal temperatures, with a small drop under longer sustained writes when the cooling hits a plateau
- Sequential write: roughly 9.8 to 11.5 GB/s in sustained tests, with some real-world variability depending on the data pattern and the presence of mixed workloads
- Random 4K IOPS: around 1.1 to 1.6 million read IOPS and 1.0 to 1.4 million write IOPS under queue depths that mimic desktop workloads

These results align with Gen5 expectations, showing that the T705 is no slouch when you have a fast CPU, a clean bus, and some heat management in place. If you push the drive in a laptop with thermal throttling or in a prebuilt small form factor PC with poor airflow, you’ll likely see numbers more in the lower end of that spectrum. If you have a beefy desktop with a proper M.2 cooler, you could approach the higher end.

## Thermals, power, and endurance: what the numbers feel like

Gen5 technology is a double-edged sword: you get higher raw speed, but you can also pump more heat into the environment. In our tests, the T705 behaved predictably enough, with temps that hovered in the safe zone when the case had decent airflow. If your chassis is compact or your GPU fans are revving hard, you may notice the drive warming up. A compact heatsink or a passive copper pad can help keep those temps in the sweet spot, preserving performance and reducing the risk of thermal throttling.

Power consumption is a bit higher than Gen4 drives under load, which makes sense given the higher speeds. In typical desktop use, you’re talking an incremental watt increase rather than a night-and-day jump. Don’t expect it to turn your rig into a carbon-drawn furnace, but do plan for better airflow if you push the drive with long, sustained heavy IO tasks.

Endurance is a hot topic with any long-term SSD review. The 4 TB class tends to offer a robust warranty and TBW ratings that keep a healthy margin for consumer and prosumer workloads. Always double-check the warranty terms for your region, but a multi-year warranty is a common expectation for Crucial drives in this class. The practical takeaway is simple: expect years of service, with the usual caveats about write-heavy workloads and steady thermal environments.

## Practical installation tips and pitfalls to avoid

- Ensure your motherboard supports PCIe Gen5 x4 and that the NVMe slot is not sharing bandwidth with a PCIe 3.0 device in a way that would throttle it. Some consumer boards reserve lanes for other devices, which can inadvertently cap performance.
- Pair with an appropriate heatsink if you plan long sustained IO sessions. Gen5 can be expressive; letting it breathe is good for longevity.
- Update BIOS and install the latest NVMe drivers. Sometimes a simple driver update can unlock additional performance headroom or fix a minor incompatibility.
- Back up important data before migrating OS or large workloads onto the drive. No one enjoys discovering an unexpected data migration hiccup halfway through a project.

### Installation quick guide
1) Power down and unplug your PC. 2) Open the case and locate an available M.2 slot. 3) Remove the screw, insert the drive into the slot at a slight angle, and press it flat. 4) Secure with the screw, apply a heatsink if you have one, and reassemble. 5) Boot, enter BIOS, set the drive as preferred OS or data drive, and format. 6) Install your preferred OS and apps, then run a few benchmarks to check temperatures and stability.
