## Condition and preservation
Because the deck is sealed, the current player not only gains a pristine surface but also a sense of suspense. The cards inside should be mint by default, assuming the wrapper has not suffered any major abuse. Humidity and temperature are the sworn enemies of collectible card stock; a sealed deck in a climate-controlled display case is the ideal scenario for long term storage.

The major caveat with sealed decks is the risk of airdrops of condensation over time. If you are considering breaking the seal for inspection, weigh the potential joy of unsealing against the risk of sacrificing the value of the mint condition. Some collectors prefer to keep the ship sealed and display the packaging while still having a separate opened deck for actual use. It is a compromise that preserves the value while keeping the player entertained for a while.

## How to authenticate and avoid fakes
Authenticating a sealed 1980s Bicycle deck is a mixture of detective work and trust in the packaging. Check the following: the wrap integrity, the printing quality on the tuck box, the shading of colors on the artwork which should reflect the 1980s printing standards, and the interior stock, which should exhibit the typical punchiness that Bicycle decks from that era possessed.

If the catalog number is present, compare it with known records from the manufacturer. The presence of a cryogenically stable seal that shows no sign of reseal is a good indicator. A reversed seal or a twist in the wrap might suggest a rewrap, which often reduces the value to serious collectors. It is not always a deal breaker, but it is a red flag that deserves a careful look.
