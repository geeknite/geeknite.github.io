## Overview
Phase 10 is a race to complete ten predefined phases. Each phase is a specific hand (like 2 sets of 3, or a run of 7), and you must complete them in order from Phase 1 to Phase 10. The first player to finish Phase 10 wins the round; the game continues until someone wins three rounds. The 2012 Mattel edition keeps the core loop intact but polishes the art, improves the rulebook clarity, and makes setup a touch quicker.

## How to Play in a Nutshell
- Everyone starts with 10 cards. On your turn you draw one card and discard one card.
- You can only lay down your phase when you have exactly the cards described by that phase.
- After laying down a phase you can add more cards to your laid-down cards.
- Wild cards can substitute for other cards in most phases; skip cards complicate things by skipping the next player.
- The first player to complete all ten phases wins the round; the game continues until someone wins three rounds.

## The Card Makeup
Phase 10 uses a deck of cards in four colors with numbers 1 to 12, plus wilds and skip cards. The core mechanic is classic Rummy-ish: you assemble sets (same number, different colors) and runs (consecutive numbers in the same color). The 2012 Mattel edition sticks to this formula but swaps in a few tasteful touches that help keep the game readable at the table.

## Phases in Detail (1 through 10)
Phase 1: 2 sets of 3
Phase 2: 1 set of 3 plus 1 run of 4
Phase 3: 1 set of 4 plus 1 run of 4
Phase 4: 1 run of 7
Phase 5: 1 run of 8
Phase 6: 1 run of 9
Phase 7: 2 sets of 4
Phase 8: 1 run of 4 plus 1 run of 4
Phase 9: 1 set of 5 plus 1 set of 2
Phase 10: 1 set of 5 plus 1 set of 3

These phase requirements give the game a natural arc: early rounds emphasize quick card matching, mid rounds reward broader planning and card-cycling, and late rounds reward longer runs and tighter resource management. It’s a simple skeleton, but it lends itself to lively table talk and the occasional dramatic comeback.

## The Strategies and Tactics
- Look for a fallback plan. If you’re missing a critical card to complete a phase, map out three possible sub-goals that would allow you to finish in two turns anyway. The ability to pivot is the difference between in control and head in a spin.
- Wild cards are powerful. Use them to fill in gaps that will grant you your single completed phase in a round, but don’t squander them on small improvements that may not advance you to Phase 10.
- Board awareness matters. Watching what others collect can inform your discards. If you notice someone chasing a color or a set, consider discarding a card that would block them.
- The discard pile is a resource, not a menace. Don’t be afraid to pick from the discard pile early if it helps you complete a phase, even if you reveal your plan to the table.
- Running the table. If you’re lucky enough to complete a phase, you can continue to add cards to it in the same hand, accumulating points for speed and maximizing your chance to win in a single round.
- House rules for speed. If you want to accelerate, replace the standard draw with a two-card draw on a player’s first turn. If a player completes a phase on a single turn, grant a bonus to the next turn.

## The Sealed Twist: Myth or Marketing Gag?
The packaging of Phase 10 2012 asserts that cards are sealed, a line that inspires a certain romanticism about the game. But in a practical sense, the sealed aspect is mostly a marketing flourish. There isn’t a core sealed mechanic in the standard ruleset that changes how you build phases. Instead, consider sealed as a metaphor for the sense of anticipation when you flip your first card, and the awkward joy when your plan unravels because someone else snags the wildcard you desperately needed.

That said, if you want to experiment with a fun home rule, here is a sealed twist you can try:
- Seal the top three discarded cards face-down in a small envelope. A player can flip one of these sealed cards once per round to replace a card they discard. This introduces a sense of secret planning and a choose-your-own-adventure vibe, while still keeping the game accessible.
- Or, add a small sealed deck of one-off phase modifiers that can be drawn at the start of your turn. The modifiers can be used to alter one phase for you or one opponent — for example, a wildcard that lasts only for the current round.

Whether you keep it truly sealed or use it as a springboard for your own variants, that sealed vibe can lead to funny stories. For example, the time you thought you finally had the perfect hand only to realize your sealed card was the wrong color or the wrong number.
