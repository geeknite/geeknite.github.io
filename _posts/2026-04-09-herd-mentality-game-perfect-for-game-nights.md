## The concept in a paragraph
Herd Mentality asks players to predict not what they themselves would answer, but what the majority of the group would answer. Each round starts with a prompt, and players write down their own answer to that prompt. Then, in the key second phase, players guess which answer will be considered the majority by the table. If your guess lines up with the actual majority, you score points. If your own answer happens to be the majority, you also snag points. The exact scoring can vary slightly by edition or house rules, but the core mechanic remains the same: think like the herd, then revel in the herd's inevitable quirks.

This creates two things that are magic for game nights: social pressure in a friendly form, and a perpetual stream of surprising, funny, or sometimes borderline ridiculous responses. The humor comes not from clever writing alone but from the social dynamics—who optimizes for pure humor, who tries to be the voice of reason, and who simply wants to be the center of attention for five minutes while everyone nods and pretends to judge their life choices by the strength of their answer.

## How to play (step by step)
To respect both newcomers and veteran party gamers, we’ll outline the flow in a way that makes sense regardless of edition. If you’ve played party games before, you’ll recognize the rhythm immediately.

### 1) Setup the round
- The host draws a prompt card and reads it aloud.
- Each player writes down one answer on their personal sheet. There’s no need to share yet; the fun is in the secretness of your predictably unpredictable brain.
- A few rounds of setup take less than a minute, which is ideal when your group is still debating snacks or whether the neighbor’s dog is judging your gaming prowess.

### 2) Reveal and guess the herd
- After everyone has written an answer, all players reveal their responses simultaneously.
- Now comes the twist: each player secretly guesses which answer will be the majority. You’re not guessing your own answer; you’re guessing the table-wide consensus.
- The majority is determined by the real-world distribution of answers. In practice, this means you’re hoping your prediction matches the most common response that appears on the board.

### 3) Scoring the madness
- If your guess matches the actual majority, you receive points. This is the heart of the game: thinking like a herd and catching what the herd will do next.
- If your own answer turns out to be the majority, you also score points. It’s a double dip: you can be both right about the herd and have written the popular answer yourself.
- Most editions use a simple point mechanic, often 1 point for a correct guess, plus an extra point or two when your answer is what the majority chose. Some house rules add a bonus for rare or clever answers; others keep it brutally simple. The key is that rounds are fast, so even misfires become memorable without dragging the night down.

### 4) Rotate and repeat
- The next player acts as host, reads a new prompt, and the ritual repeats. A full game typically comprises several rounds across multiple prompts, balancing humor with occasional moments of surprising depth about your friends and family.

## The mechanics decoded
Let’s break down what makes the game tick from a design perspective, because understanding the craft helps you host with confidence.

- The majority concept is a delightful mirror: People aren’t just answering for themselves; they’re trying to read the room. This is why you get those shared jokes and those glorious misreads when the room goes off-script.
- The scoring rewards social acuity and witty writing: The better your sense of what others will say, the more points you rack up. Your personal boldness matters—the daring answer that ends up being the crowd’s favorite can pay off handsomely.
- The prompts are the engine: A strong deck keeps the game from stalling. If you ever draw a dull prompt, the round flops. Smart prompts—spiky, relatable, or absurd—spark conversation and contrast with the more thoughtful questions.

### Round flow: a quick example
Imagine a prompt like: What would be the most overrated gadget of all time? Players write their answers: “Smart toaster,” “Self-cleaning vacuum,” “Voice-controlled lamp.” Everyone reveals, then everyone secretly guesses which answer will be the majority. If “Smart toaster” is the crowd favorite, those who guessed it get points, and anyone who wrote “Smart toaster” also gets points if their answer happens to be the majority. The host tallies, and you’re already onto the next round before the pizza deliveries have finished their first lap around the block.

## Variants and house rules worth trying
Every group evolves its own style, and Herd Mentality invites a few twists that can extend the life of the deck:

- Team play: Pair up players, so each duo collaborates on an answer and a guess. This reduces social friction for shy players and can yield surprisingly cohesive strategies. 
- Speed rounds: Cut the time for writing down answers to 15 seconds. It cranks up adrenaline and forces briefer, sharper responses.
- The double-dare round: Introduce a rule where the majority can be determined by a vote among the first three answers. If there’s a tie, the host selects the majority. It creates a suspenseful cliffhanger mid-game.
- The educator variant: Use prompts with a learning twist, such as bizarre historical “facts” or science-y what-ifs, to spark curiosity and conversation beyond the laugh-out-loud moments.

## Tips for the host
A great host can turn a decent night into a legendary one. Here are practical tips to maximize the fun:
- Prep a few prompt cards in advance that align with your group’s humor. Personal touches make rounds feel tailor-made and more meaningful.
- Keep a light tally: A small scoreboard keeps energy up without turning the game into a tournament about bragging rights.
- Embrace the chaos: Don’t scold players for odd or offbeat answers. In fact, reward the weird. Laughter is the currency here.
- Use thematic snacks as pacing devices: If a round is dragging, announce a snack break or a coffee station to reset energy levels.
- Be mindful of sensitivities: Some prompts can touch on topics that feel uncomfortable for some players. If you notice someone is squirming, pivot to a lighter prompt and keep the mood inclusive.

## The Herd Mentality vs other party games
Of course, you’ll likely compare Herd Mentality to other social games in your shelf rotation. Here’s a quick comparison to set expectations:

- Wavelength: Wavelength rewards cooperative guessing and shared brainpower. Herd Mentality trades a shared predictive approach for the thrill of outsmarting the room with humor and timing.
- Telestrations: Visual miscommunication is the star here; Herd Mentality relies on verbal wit and social reading. Both are excellent for big groups, but the latter is faster and often louder.
- Werewords or Codenames party variants: These rely more on deduction and wordplay. Herd Mentality is more about social rhythm and group psychology than pure deduction, which is refreshing when you want something lighter on rules and heavier on smiles.

- Round 1: Prompt: If your toaster could talk, what would it complain about most? Answers: a) burned bread stacks, b) too loud mechanical clanks, c) mysterious butter leaks.
