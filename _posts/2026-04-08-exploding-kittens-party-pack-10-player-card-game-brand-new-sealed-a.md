## Setup and First Impressions
Setting up for ten players is a different beast than the standard 2–4 player games we usually review. The goal is to get everyone into the rhythm quickly without losing your mental grip on who is currently wielding the Defuse card like a ceremonial shofar of doom.

- Shuffle the deck thoroughly. Yes, even that one friend who insists on “just one more shuffle for luck.”
- Deal seven cards to each player (rules may vary slightly by edition; check your pack). The rest forms a draw pile.
- Each player receives a Defuse card or two based on your house rules. The rest of the Defuse cards go into the draw pile, because chaos loves company.
- Place Exploding Kitten cards into the deck according to the number of players. In a ten-player game, you’ll have enough to keep your heartbeat in check while someone mutters, “I swear I shuffled.”
- Decide how many turns you’ll allow before you declare a winner, or just go until someone finally yells, “I survived the box and all of you.”

The vibe here is “party game that is serious about fun.” There’s a playful energy: a room full of adults acting like cats while trying to manage a deck of explosive tokens. If you have ever attempted a group project with friends who have wildly different interpretations of “teamwork,” you’ll feel right at home. The setup time is a small investment for a session that can last anywhere from 20 minutes to an hour, depending on your group’s tolerance for dramatic table talk and dramatic risk taking.

## Gameplay: The Core Loop for 10 Players
Exploding Kittens is a push-your-luck game with a lot of interruptions. The core loop is simple enough to remember: draw a card, play action cards to influence others, and avoid drawing an Exploding Kitten unless you have a Defuse card that can bail you out. The party version streamlines access to everyone, but when you have ten players, you’re basically running a small social experiment in probability and bluffing under the weight of your average cat pun.

- On your turn, you draw one card from the deck. The deck is stacked with Defuse cards and a handful of Exploding Kitten cards. Your goal is to avoid drawing an Exploding Kitten unless you’re prepared to negate it with a Defuse.
- You can play action cards to alter the flow: Attack, See the Future, Skip, and Nope cards (to cancel others’ effects). The exact set of cards can vary by edition, but the spirit remains: disrupt, delay, and outplay your friends.
- See the Future lets you peek at the top cards of the deck. This is the shallow victory of the game: knowledge that may or may not help you avoid doom.
- Defuse cards can neutralize an Exploding Kitten. If you’re the one who holds a Defuse and you flip an Exploding Kitten, you can discard it and continue the game—provided you’re not immediately exploited by the next turn order.
- Attack cards force the next player to take multiple turns. In a 10-player game, that means someone else’s fate is now tied to your mischief for a longer duration. It’s a great way to create suspense and social tension—two essential ingredients for a party game win.

With ten players, rounds unfold at a frenetic pace. You’ll have moments where the table erupts in chaotic laughter as someone deploys a perfect “Nope, I don’t think so” to cancel a game-changing move, only to have another player counter with a better card, and suddenly the entire table shifts alliances like a late-night multiplayer match where everyone is trying to feel like they’re in control of the narrative. The chaos can feel overwhelming at times, but it’s precisely this unpredictability that makes Exploding Kittens Party Pack addictive in social settings. If your group thrives on energy and bold decisions, this box will sing. If your group values quiet, strategic minimization of risk, you might need to adapt expectations—or your liquor cabinet.

## Strategy and Chaos: How to Approach It All
There’s not exactly a deep strategic algorithm here, but there are several patterns you’ll observe when a crowd embraces Exploding Kittens Party Pack with enthusiasm rather than fear.

- Defuse Card Management: In larger groups, Defuse cards become almost currency. If you can hold onto at least one Defuse card for a risky draw, you’ll feel a sense of security while everyone else scrambles to avoid doom.
- Card Counting-ish: It’s not a perfect system, but in a 10-player game, you’ll start to spot patterns: who tends to play See the Future at the most opportune moment? Who shouts “Nope!” the loudest when someone announces a game-changing move? You can leverage this social information to predict how others will react to future plays.
- Play the Crowd: This is a party game; sometimes the best move is not the strongest technical play but the move that elicits the best reaction. If your group thrives on theatrics, you should lean into those dramatic moments and let the tension fuel the laughter.
- Patience vs. Impulse: The larger the table, the more tempting it is to force a rash move. The hero of the night tends to be the person who can wait for the right moment to deploy an Attack card or to throw a See the Future just when the table looks ready to explode with anticipation.

### Practical tips for maximizing the experience
- Establish house rules before you begin and write them on a whiteboard or sticky note at the table. Common variations include how many Defuse cards you start with or whether you can barter Defuse cards mid-game.
