# Gloom Second Edition — Brand New Sealed: A Geeknite Review

Gloom, the game that teaches you to smile while your characters bleed out emotionally, is back with a second edition and a brand-new sealed smell that screams, in a delightfully perfumed way, “you should have bought the insurance plan.” If you’ve wandered into the world of Keith Baker’s Gloom with its famously dour family tree and thought, “Sure, I’ve played lighter tabletop fare… like a family reunion with a sitcom budget,” you’re not alone. The second edition arrives with all the moody flavor you remember, plus a few polish upgrades that make it easier to learn, play, and—dare I say it—enjoy the misery.

In Geeknite fashion, we’ll unbox, dissect, and then proceed to ruthlessly judge the emotional integrity of each character’s misfortunes. Spoiler: there will be laughter, possibly tears, and definitely more than one moment where you question your life choices as a gamer and as a person. Let’s dive into what this sealed box has to offer and whether it’s worth breaking the seal for your next game night.

## What's in the Box (Second Edition Specifics)

First impressions matter, especially when the scent of brand-new cardboard hits you like a tiny autumn breeze of doom. The box art in the second edition leans into the same melancholic whimsy, but with crisper lines and a few extra flourishes that say, “We learned how to print slowly and well.” Inside, you’ll find a deck of Character cards, a deck of Story cards, and a pile of Condition and Condition-Adjacency cards that somehow manage to look elegant while implying your family is a serial-wannabe-misfortune. The card stock feels thicker than a normal playing card and, yes, the edges are beveled enough to survive the clumsy hands of folks who shuffle like a caffeinated raccoon.

There are also the classic Tales of Woe cards—these are the spark that starts your narrative engine. In this edition, the art has a cleaner, more cohesive direction; it doesn’t scream at you from across the table, but it will wink and say, “Yes, we know the humor is dark, and that’s the point.” The new rulebook is a welcome update: it’s not a cookbook for chaos, but it does tell you how to build a mood arc that won’t require a psychology degree to interpret.

For the table stakes folks, the board is replaced by a compact “gloom track” that runs along the edge of the table, letting you track how miserable a family’s life has become with a quick glance. The tokens feel satisfyingly weighty, and the whole package seems built to survive both enthusiastic dice rolling and the occasional table flip—though please don’t test that last one; your neighbor’s cat will judge you silently from the sofa.

If you’re curious about the nuts and bolts beyond the general vibe, you’ll see that the second edition keeps the same core loop: you play a card to reveal a negative condition on a sibling, and you attempt to push as much sorrow as you can onto your opponents’ families while keeping your own a notch above purgatory. It’s a delicate dance of storytelling and card management, with the occasional “gotcha” card that can flip the entire round in a hilarious and heartbreaking fashion. The art is stylized, slightly gothic, and always witty; it’s modern enough to feel fresh, but old enough to feel like a fondly remembered campy horror movie from the 60s. In short, the contents are a well-executed upgrade without betraying the game’s soul.

## A Quick Tour of the Mechanics (If You Need a Refresher)

Gloom’s second edition sticks to its signature mechanism: you attach negative cards to your family members, and you reveal them to reveal just how tragically, humorously, catastrophically unfortunate your characters can become. The rules aren’t overbearing; they’re built to be learned in a single sitting, then discussed in a later sitting when you realize you’ve been lying to yourself about your ability to stay emotionally detached during a “choose-your-own-path-to-ruin” scenario.

- Each player controls a family: a handful of members with their own traits. The goal is to make your own family as miserable as possible while keeping your rivals’ families a step more miserable than yours. It’s competitive misery, a sport you might call “misfortune dexterity.”
- You use Story cards to drive the narrative. A Story card isn’t just a sentence or two; it offers a mini-scenario that you narrate, and it rewards you for weaving a coherent, entertaining, and ruthlessly inventive tragedy around your cards. The second edition clarifies some phrasing and improves storytelling prompts so folks aren’t left staring at a paragraph of terms that sounds like it was translated by a warlock with a keyboard.
- Negative cards (Condition cards) attach to characters as the round progresses. You can rearrange and stack them with a certain flow to maximize thematic impact, but the card piles honor a simple rule: you can’t murder the mood of a color you don’t own. The color coding is neat, and it makes scanning the table for looming doom quick and satisfying.
- End-game scoring: the more gloom you’ve accumulated on your own family, the better when you reveal. The catch is that you must reveal last, which creates dramatic tension and a few “no you didn’t” moments at the table. If you’re playing with newcomers, you’ll probably drink in the drama as they realize their careful plans can be undone by a well-timed misfortune.

Although the edition has some tweaks, the core experience remains delightfully consistent: a light-to-middling learning curve, a high ceiling for storytelling, and a deck that rewards both clever narration and sharp card management. If you’ve played the first edition before, you’ll notice the improvements in production quality, readability, and the emotional weight of the card art. If you’re brand new, you’ll be quickly looped into a world where your own family’s tragedy becomes the evening’s entertainment rather than a long, awkward confession after a game ends.

## Thematic Flavor, Humor, and Production Quality

Gloom is famously darkly funny. The humor isn’t about mocking real misfortune; it’s about the absurdity of trying to orchestrate a grand tragedy using nothing more than a few cards and a willing table. The second edition amplifies this by letting the artwork lean into the melodramatic without tipping into campy parody. It’s still silly in the right places, but the mood feels more cohesive—like a well-assembled gothic novella you’d read aloud at a tabletop book club that also happens to serve coffee in tiny skull-shaped mugs. The prose on the cards is punchy, and the margins between tragedy and whimsy are carefully balanced so that the joke lands without feeling like you’re punching down at your own fun.

From a production standpoint, the sleeved cards slide smoothly in and out of the deck, which matters when you’re shuffling after a rival has just turbo-charged a story arc with a sugared-lament twist. The stock is sturdy enough to handle a handful of coffee rings and the occasional table-tap with the zeal of someone who just rolled a critical fail. The box insert is sensible, not fussy; it keeps the cards in place so that even the most enthusiastic players won’t spill out a pile of drama along with their chips and pretzels.

If you care about the aesthetics, you’ll appreciate the way the second edition’s color palette harmonizes with the gloom tokens. It’s not loud; it’s deliberately moody, giving you the vibe of a Victorian parlor where the chandeliers are a little too bright for the mood. The art direction also makes it easier to parse what’s on a card at a quick glance, which is important if you’re trying to tell a compelling story before the “reveal” moment becomes a chorus of groans.

For those who enjoy a good critical appraisal of components, I’ll note a small improvement: the tokens are easier to read and the negative condition names are less punishing to parse when you’re deep into a round. This minor polish matters when your brain is already juggling three different misfortune arcs and a coffee cup that’s teetering toward the edge of the table.

## Play Experience: A Sample Session

To give you a feel for the rhythm, here’s a condensed mock session that captures the flavor of a typical Geeknite night with Gloom Second Edition:

- Setup: Four players, four families, and a timer that is suspiciously helpful for forcing dramatic turns. The box smells brand-new, and the room has that rare combination of focus and improv vibes.
- Round 1: Player A announces a Story card that frames a family feud about a garden gnome that supposedly blesses the neighbor’s cat. Cards are attached. Laughter erupts as the first misfortune card pulls on a string that ties two characters’ fates together. The tension is palpable, not because anyone fears losing, but because the story has already turned into a delightful little soap opera in under five minutes.
- Round 2: Player B doubles down with a set of condition cards targeting Player C’s character. The mood shifts from lighthearted mockery to bittersweet melodrama as their romance arc spirals into a tragedy involving a broken music box and a pet tortoise that refuses to grow up. The table’s energy becomes a cocktail of sympathy, mischief, and the pre-emptive defense of whose narrative is the most entertaining to witness.
- Round 3: A well-timed Story card flips the focus onto Player D, who now has to narrate a twist where the gloomy family’s heirloom is revealed to be… a uselessly small teacup that somehow amplifies sorrow when it’s broken. The audience roars with a mix of pity and amusement, and a new plan forms: how to maximize your own misfortune without tipping off others to your strategy.
- Endgame: After the last reveal, the table tallies up gloom points. The winner is the one whose family has endured the most charming despair, and yet somehow managed to make the night feel like a shared, ridiculous catharsis rather than a funeral for a game. In my test run, the second edition proved adept at balancing humor and heartbreak, giving every player a chance to steer the story toward a satisfying, if morally questionable, conclusion.

In practice, the game’s flow remains intuitive: narrate, attach, reveal, score, and repeat. It rewards quick thinking and clever wordplay, but it never punishes you for a moment of over-ambition. The storytelling aspect is where Gloom truly shines, and this edition makes it easier for newcomers to find their voice without feeling like they’ve just been dropped into an alien language of gloom and doom.

## House Rules and Customary Tweaks

As with any strong party game, there are always ways to tailor Gloom Second Edition to your group. Here are a few suggestions that keep the spirit intact while letting you tailor the difficulty and pace:

- The Narrator’s Guild Rule: designate one player as the “Narrator” for a round. That person gets to choose a Story card and propose a hook for the round’s misfortune—no backseating from the other players allowed. This accelerates storytelling and can prevent the table from devolving into argument-based chaos.
- Timebox the Tales: set a 90-second timer for each Story round. It keeps things moving, encourages punchy narration, and makes the endgame feel more like a dramatic series finale than a long-running TV soap.
- The Sincere Mourner: for players who fear that their jokes are getting too mean, award a small bonus to the player who includes at least one genuinely heartfelt moment in their story. It balances the mood and keeps everyone connected to the narrative rather than spiraling into pure misfortune for misfortune’s sake.
- No-Topple Variant: some groups worry about table-stabbing (metaphorically, of course) when the gloom tokens pile up. Use a variant where you can only attach a maximum of two new negative cards to a single character per round. It lengthens the game slightly but can improve the storytelling by preventing runaway misery.

These tweaks acknowledge that you’re here to have a good time, not to stage a tragedy so epic that the dice start crying. Gloom is a social experience first and a card game second; the quality of the night comes from the people around the table, not the cards in your hand.

## Who Should Play Gloom Second Edition?

- People who enjoy storytelling as a social activity with a dash of competition. If you like sharing ridiculous tales about tragic family members and turning a simple prompt into a mini soap opera, Gloom is your jam.
- Groups that like easy-to-teach rules and quick setups. The second edition is friendlier to newcomers than the original at times, thanks to clarified wording and improved components.
- Players who want a game night that can pivot between “seriously silly” and “dramatically macabre” without requiring a big investment in time or learning. A single session tends to be satisfying, and you can easily stretch it across multiple rounds if you’re enjoying the mood.
- People who appreciate exteriors that look polished on a shelf. The box and cards themselves are not just functional; they’re a mood piece that invites you to peek inside and immediately start weaving gloom into your stories.

Who should probably pass? If you’re the sort of player who hates storytelling games, or you prefer games that are purely mechanical and don’t hinge on narrative improvisation, Gloom Second Edition might not scratch your itch. If you’re allergic to dark humor, or you have family members who would rather not role-play misfortune, this isn’t necessarily the best choice for you. But if you’re curious about how stories, strategy, and a little theatrical gloom can combine, you’ll likely find something to enjoy here.

## Pros, Cons, and Verdict

Pros:

Cons:

Verdict: Gloom Second Edition remains one of the more charming anchors of modern storytelling tabletop gaming. If you crave a game night that rewards clever narrative construction as much as you reward a well-timed card play, this box is worth opening—even if you’re only doing it to smell that conclusive brand-new-card vibe and to tell yourself you’ll actually finish one of those stories before bedtime.
