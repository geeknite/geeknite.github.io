## Overview and first impressions
The Gang is a cooperative card game designed by Thames & Kosmos that centers around forming poker hands as part of your team’s mission. Each round represents a set of actions you must complete to advance the gang’s plan, all while balancing personal objectives and the ever-present risk of failure. The core gimmick is simple to grasp but deliciously hard to master: your success is public (the team’s poker hand is visible) and your failure is private (secret objectives and personal twists can wreck the plan if you reveal too much too soon).

Whether you are playing with two people or a full table, the vibe stays relentlessly tense in a good way. The art direction is noir-ish but clean, the components feel sturdy, and the rules strike a careful balance between flexibility and structure. The game rewards good communication without turning into a shouting match, and it punishes sloppiness with a shared cost (which you will pay with chips, tension meters, or a heavy sigh). If you and your crew enjoy heist simulations, poker-inspired puzzles, or games that make you talk through moral choices under time pressure, you will likely have a very good time here.

### What I wish I knew before playing
- Do not expect a puzzle box of pure logic; expect a living room jailbreak with a deck of cards and three cups of coffee.

- The personal objectives are not merely flavor; they shape how you should communicate and when you should betray your own strategy for the good of the group.
- The setup is quick but the learning curve is real. Give it a couple of practice rounds if you are not used to co-op games that hinge on negotiation and bluffing disguised as planning.

## Components, setup, and first setup thoughts
The Gang comes with a deck of custom poker cards, a set of role cards, a handful of mission cards, and a small handful of tokens and a tension track. The quality of the cards is solid; the art is crisp, with a clean noir aesthetic that keeps the vibe consistent across different missions. The tokens feel tangible enough to remind you there are consequences to every decision. The rulebook is compact but not mean; it provides enough examples and scenarios to make you feel safe to experiment in your first two rounds.

Setup is straightforward: assign roles, deal out starting hands to each player, reveal the first mission card, and place the tension dial at its starting position. The roles grant unique abilities or triggers that can tilt the odds in your favor—at the cost of revealing more information about your personal hidden objective. The mission cards set the group’s goals for that round (or multi-round arc), adding variety to the same underlying poker mechanic.

One neat thing about setup is that it primes the table for discussion. You will talk about which hand to push, what to discard, who should be the decoy, and who should take the risk to draw a line between “we can do this” and “we should take a safe pass.” The game rewards that conversation without requiring you to turn every session into a debate club about ethics in the underworld. In short: you are not just playing cards; you are negotiating who deserves a larger slice of the loot and who deserves to be the decoy.

## How to play: flow, decisions, and the poker engine
The core loop of The Gang is a shared one: you will form poker hands using cards from your hand, attempt a mission or lane of play, and then resolve outcomes that affect the team’s objective and the tension track. The exact rules are easier to grasp than they are to optimize, which is exactly what makes the game satisfying after the first couple of rounds.

- Each round, players secretly select one or more cards from their hand to contribute to a shared poker hand. The number of cards you can contribute and how you combine them depends on the active mission and your role’s special abilities.
- You reveal your chosen cards simultaneously, forming a hand with a rank similar to standard poker (pair, two pair, three of a kind, straight, flush, full house, four of a kind, straight flush, etc.). The strength of the hand determines how well you advance toward the mission’s objective.
- The team uses the result to progress a plan card or resolve an event. If you achieve the target hand quality or better, you push the plan forward. If not, you suffer consequences that increase the tension on the track.
- The tension track acts as a life bar for the table’s survival. It increases when you fail to meet the mission's criteria or when you overstep boundaries in the story. It can cause negative events, additional complexities, or even a late-game escalation of stakes.
- Personal objectives add flavor and strategy. They can reward clever bluffing or misdirection, but they also risk tearing the team apart if you reveal too much. The best players manage their personal score without tipping off others about their hidden agenda.

The turn structure is deliberately circular, encouraging dialogue and planning before the cards hit the table. The designer’s intention is for players to feel like masterful negotiators playing a high-stakes poker game—without the actual risk of losing your house (we hope).

### The role system and how it changes the dynamic
Roles are the spice that give The Gang its flavor. Each player receives a role card that grants a unique ability, such as drawing extra cards, swapping hands, altering a single card’s suit, or peeking at other players’ hands under certain conditions. The catch is that these abilities are not free; they typically come with a cost or a risk. Maybe using your ability increases tension for the round or reveals a small hint about your personal objective. This is where the game shifts from a mere puzzle to a social dance. You are not just calculating odds; you are reading the table and deciding who can be trusted with the critical move.

Try to pair complementary roles: one player prepares a broad hand, another patient fetches crucial cards, and a third handles the misdirection that stops others from sabotaging your plan. The synergy emerges from real-time negotiation and the occasional strategic sacrifice. The Gang wants you to be nimble on your feet, to misdirect when needed, and to know when to fold without pouting like a child who dropped their ice cream.

The overall presentation invites you to lean into the chaos, which is the exact charisma you want from a cooperative title that uses poker as its language.

## Solo play and group dynamics
The Gang scales with player count fairly well. With two players, it becomes a tense, almost intimate planning exercise—two minds trying to anticipate the other’s needs, a healthy test of trust and reading the other’s tells. As you add players, the conversation becomes the real engine of the game. You get a larger pool of roles, a wider array of possible strategies, and more moments when a team member’s bluff feels like the perfect misdirection at the exact right moment.

There are solo rules included, which is a treat for those nights when your table is taken by the cat, your neighbor’s dog, or your own procrastination. The solo mode is a proof of patience: you simulate multiple “gang members” by controlling their roles and letting the game’s tension track act as your sole opponent. It can be satisfying to wrestle with the scenario on your own, though it lacks the social catharsis of roping in a few friends to argue about the best way to form a straight flush while still saving the crew from a bad outcome.

Replayability comes from mission variety and the interplay of roles. Each mission card introduces a new objective, requiring you to rethink your hand selection, your communication, and your risk budgeting. After a handful of sessions, you’ll start noticing which missions are more forgiving, which require sharper negotiation, and which require you to dare the table to do something truly reckless and brilliant at the same time.

## Tips, tricks, and strategies for success
- Communicate early, communicate often: The best runs come when players describe their intended cards and ask for permission to push a big hand. Do not rely on subtle signals alone; talk through your plan and confirm that you understand each other’s risk tolerance for that round.
- Use roles to create tempo: If your team can stage a safe first round to build momentum, you will avoid the early, brutal escalation of tension that ruins later rounds. Use roles to set up a predictable rhythm that buys you time to plan.
- Read the table: If someone is hinting at a critical objective you have not uncovered, be mindful of that potential reveal. The moment you know a player’s personal objective, you can adjust your approach to minimize the risk and maximize the reward.
- Don’t be afraid to stall: If the hand you’re about to throw would be a disaster, consider delaying a round by using a role that allows a temporary draw or discard. Better to stall than to push a doomed mission and lose face with the crew.
- Learn the edges: Some hands are inherently risky. Identify which hands you can turn into a safe pass and which hands have to be risked for a big upside. This is the real risk-reward calculus the game asks of you.

## Verdict and recommendations
The Gang hits its stride when the crew is ready to talk, bluff, and occasionally brazenly risk it all for the greater good. It thrives on social interaction and the constant negotiation that comes with a shared goal and individual motives. It is not the easiest slot on your shelf to explain to newcomers, but it is one of the most satisfying when a group clicks and manages to thread a tough needle together. The poker mechanic is clever and accessible, providing a familiar framework that players can lean on even as the situation in front of them grows more complex.

Pros:
- Tight, clever use of poker mechanics to drive cooperative play
- Strong theme and aesthetic that reinforces tension and teamwork
- High replayability through mission variety and role synergies
- Solo play option that still feels like a proper game rather than a pared-down version

Cons:
- Learning curve can be steep for players unfamiliar with poker hand rankings
- Some rounds feel swingy; you can go from hero to zero in a couple of cards
- Solo mode doesn’t quite capture the social dynamics of a table full of players

Suggested player count and setting: 2-5 players, 30-60 minutes per session, best with a group that enjoys lively discussion and a little strategic risk-taking.

If you are shopping for a game night that blends strategy, bluffing, and cooperative puzzle-solving with a punchy theme, The Gang is a strong pick. It rewards you for planning, punishes you for miscommunications, and gives you enough neat engine twists to keep you coming back for another round of constitutional mischief with your most cunning friends.
