## Setup and rules in minutes

Saboteur thrives on its simple setup and fast rulebook. The complete English edition keeps this spirit. Each round starts with role distribution. Some players are designated as Saboteurs, others as Prospects. The role distribution is done secretly; players should not reveal their allegiance until the end. The objective is straightforward for the Prospector side: connect a path from the mine entrance to a treasure card, using path cards to build corridors. For Saboteurs, the objective is to derail that path and push the treasure to a location that the Saboteurs can reach before the Prospector team does.

### Turn structure and core actions

A typical round advances in quick steps. A player can play a path card to extend the tunnel layout, place a reward card, or trigger a sabotage action that helps their team or hinders opponents. You draw a card to replenish your hand at the end of your turn or when your action ends. The game emphasizes speed and misdirection more than heavy calculation. It is not a brain-burner, but a sharp poke in your social armor. The complete English edition shines here because the printed icons and minimal language requirements allow players of different language backgrounds to jump in with minimal friction.

## Saboteur strategy and mind games

As Saboteur, you are less about building the most efficient tunnel and more about convincing others you are not the saboteur. Subtle misplays and well-timed sabotage cards can create a narrative that leads others to suspect the wrong person. The better you hide your tells, the more misdirection you can sow. As Prospector, you are a hunter of patterns; you watch for tells such as when someone avoids certain paths or when they push the group to commit early to a risky route. You can use action cards to speed things up, but just as often your best move is to quietly pick your timing and let a few rounds of misdirection do the heavy lifting.

## Mechanics and why it works

The core mechanic rests on tile-like path building while managing hidden roles. The path cards create a network toward the treasure by chaining adjacency. A single blocked link or a misdirection card can alter the entire trajectory, forcing players to adapt their plans on the fly. Because players have limited information about roles, guessing becomes part of the game. This is a classic design choice: you give players enough scaffolding to build a plan that feels plausible, and then you strip away the certainty to force improvisation.

### Variety, replayability, and how long the fun lasts
