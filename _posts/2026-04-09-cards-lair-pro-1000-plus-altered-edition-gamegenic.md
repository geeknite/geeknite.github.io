### Build Quality and Sleeves Compatibility
Let’s talk durability. ## Gameplay and Mechanics
### Setup and Core Loop
Setup is substantial but surprisingly smooth for a box this size. You lay out the market or deck pool, distribute player boards, and hand out starting resources. The Altered Edition adds optional setup variants that affect the initial distribution of cards, which can feel refreshing after a handful of sessions where you always end up with the same opening draw. The core loop blends card drafting, engine-building mechanics, and light area control. It’s not a pure deck-builder in the sense of tearing down your own deck from scratch; rather, you’re sculpting your path as you go, capitalizing on combos, and adjusting to the evolving table state.

Turns are structured to minimize downtime. You’ll typically perform actions that trigger card effects, draw replacements, and maybe trigger an event or two that reshapes your strategy on the fly. The pacing tends to be brisk but with enough tent-pole moments—where a single perfect synergy can turn the game around—to keep things exciting for the full duration of a play session.

### Variant Rules and Altered Edition Features
The Altered Edition shines in its modularity. You can add optional rules to tilt the balance toward a more aggressive or more strategic style, vary the victory conditions to create new endgames, or even implement a draft phase that wasn’t part of the original ruleset. These features unlock higher climbability to a broad audience—from meticulous tactical players to social players who treat games like a mood board rather than a strict schedule of outcomes. The added complexity is tangible, but it’s not a barrier to entry for veterans of the genre; it’s a doorway that invites experimentation. The rules read clearly, and the documentation is designed to minimize rule-lawyering time—even when your table is craving a spicy change of pace.

### Solo and Group Play
Solo play shines as a test of memory and foresight, with the deck acting as a mischievous counterpart that can surprise you with tricksy plays. It’s the kind of solo experience that compounds the energy you invest in setup and learning, rewarding repeated plays with a better mental map of card synergies. In group play, the variability of the Altered Edition cards ensures that no two sessions feel the same, barring the fact that you’ll still have the same players, but with different strategies. The risk here is potential analysis paralysis in highly competitive groups; thankfully, the game provides clear thresholds for allies vs. opponents in a manner that keeps the game moving without sacrificing tension.
