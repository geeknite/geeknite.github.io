## Mechanics and design philosophy
Fortune Row doubles down on the core City of Mist idea: the character’s power isn’t raw subtraction of a villain’s health bar; it’s a narrative catalyst. The Special Characters in this set are built around tags such as Fate, Coin, Echo, and Crowd. Each tag acts as a lever: Fate invites you to reframe outcomes, Coin leans into probability and luck, Echo makes history loop back or reverberate through scenes, and Crowd introduces social momentum and the crowd’s mood as a driving force.

This isn’t about rolling harder; it’s about storytelling leverage. When a player taps into Fate, for instance, a failed attempt may not be a setback, but a pivot that reframes the scene’s stakes. The design intention is clear: misfortune should function as a “story engine” rather than a roadblock. It’s a tonal gamble that pays off if your table buys into mythic storytelling where consequences matter as much as actions.

If you’re curious how Fortune Row interacts with the City of Mist core system, you’ll recognize the familiar undercurrent of tags, Mysterious Auras, and Mythos cues, but with a sharper emphasis on narrative turn-taking. For fans of other City of Mist expansions, Fortune Row’s approach to fate-driven storytelling offers a refreshing reminder that the game isn’t a crunchy tactical battle, but a stage for mythic improvisation.

## New Special Characters
The four new Special Characters anchor Fortune Row’s thematic backbone, each offering a distinct flavor of fate-driven storytelling. They’re designed to be played solo or integrated into ongoing City of Mist campaigns without requiring a complete table-wide retooling.

### The Whisper of Coin
The Whisper of Coin is all about probability and the sweet spot where luck shines or falters at the exact moment a choice is about to be made. The character’s mechanics encourage players to offer two paths, with one path seemingly favorable and the other a deliberate misdirection that becomes the group’s turning point. Story prompts often revolve around a choice that seems trivial but carries a cascade of consequences when a single coin flip goes the wrong way. This character is ideal for sessions that emphasize decision fatigue in a noir environment—what could go wrong, and what does that wrongness unlock later on?

Story hooks for The Whisper of Coin lean into gambling dens, backroom deals, and the idea that the city’s luck is a shared resource, sometimes siphoned off by a single ambitious decision. GMs can weave in a recurring motif where the coin’s face changes with each use, hinting at a larger fate at play.

### The Alley Oracle
The Alley Oracle is a seer of street-level whispers—the kind of oracle who receives messages from the graffiti gods and from the last train’s echo. Its power tags revolve around perception and social cues, translating overheard conversations into clues that pivot the party toward or away from danger. The charm of this character lies in social dynamism: NPCs respond differently depending on how the Oracle interprets a line or a rumor. The GM can play with the idea that the Alley Oracle’s visions aren’t always accurate, but they’re always morally instructive in a city that thrives on ambiguous loyalties.

Session seeds for the Alley Oracle often involve overheard statements that the party must interpret quickly. The tension comes from the crowd’s evolving mood and the danger of misinterpretation—an elegant conduit for broadcast-style scenes that escalate into public spectacles.

### The Clockmaker of Dust
The Clockmaker of Dust tethers time itself to the city’s gritty textures. Expect power tags that bend tempo, allow rewinds of brief moments, or create countdowns with unpredictable results. The Clockmaker is perfect for heists, chase sequences, and capers where timing is everything. The character’s philosophy is poetic: even in a realm where fate rules, timing remains the most stubborn ally of a plan, and the most ruthless antagonist of chaos.

Hooks for clockwork plots involve orchestrated delays, misdirection timed to the second, and the city’s pace being a character in its own right. The GM can pull in ticking clocks that appear both physically and metaphorically—things that must move at a certain rhythm or else trigger a dramatic reversal.

### The One Who Keeps Secrets
Secrets are stock in trade in City of Mist, but The One Who Keeps Secrets elevates disclosure into a weapon and a shield. This character thrives on silences, evasions, and the delicate art of choosing what not to reveal. Their power tags revolve around control of information flow, selective truth-telling, and the pressure of unspoken consequences catching up with the group at the least convenient moment.

This Special Character offers a fantastic springboard for long-form mystery arcs. The GM can develop recurring scenes where a withheld truth becomes a looming shadow over the party, forcing players to weigh the cost of keeping secrets versus the need to reveal something that could shift the city’s balance of power.

### Notes on balance and playstyle
Each character emphasizes narrative levers over brute force. They’re designed to invite misdirection, philosophical debates about fate, and moments of dramatic irony rather than pure statistical dominance. That’s not a flaw—it's the essence of City of Mist. If your table loves “moments that feel earned” through choice and consequence, Fortune Row’s quartet is a strong fit. If you prefer hard counters and straight-up skill checks to drive the session, you’ll need to lean into GM creativity to stage scenes that let these characters shine without overshadowing the rest of the table.

## Character creation experience
Fortune Row doesn’t demand a total rework of your character creation flow. You already have a baseline system for Fate and Mythos, and Fortune Row slots into that with a handful of prompts and subtler thematic hooks. Expect prompts that nudge players toward themes of luck, perception, and the city’s capricious mood. The prompts are designed to be quick but evocative: a memory attached to a coin, a rumor that never quite resolves, a clock’s faint heartbeat in a warehouse.

What does this mean in practice? You can build a character that slots into an ongoing arc or teleport into a brand-new arc with minimal friction. The prompts guide you toward meaningful choices that make sense in a noir-tinged, mask-wearing world where everyone has a double life. The result is a character that feels like a fragment of a larger tapestry rather than a stand-alone hero who’s simply new to the table.

To speed things up, we suggest a three-phase approach: (1) pick a core fate tag and a backbone motivation, (2) choose two supporting prompts that tie into potential story threads, and (3) map how other players’ Fate and Mythos elements intersect with your character’s journey. You’ll end up with a character who feels like a living rumor rather than a static stat sheet.

## How to run a session with Fortune Row
Running Fortune Row is about embracing the city’s kinetic energy rather than attempting bulletproof, flawless heists. Here’s a practical approach to weaving these characters into a one-shot or a longer arc:

1) Set the mood with a neon-lit setting: rain-slick streets, flickering signs, and whispered stakes. Establish that fortune is a currency that can run dry or flood the party with sudden opportunities.
2) Introduce a seed encounter: a seemingly trivial situation—someone’s missing item, a rumor about a key, a coded message in a mural—that requires players to interpret clues, not just smash through obstacles.
3) Hand players two or three prompts per scene that push them to lean into Fate, Coin, Echo, or Crowd. Encourage players to lean into misfortune as a storytelling catalyst, not a hindrance.
4) Use a soft escalation: let the scene look like it’s spiraling toward catastrophe, then reveal how the city’s underlying truth shifts the odds in the party’s favor—when they least expect it.
5) End with a twist that reframes the group’s perception of who the heroes are. Fortune Row loves stories where the “solution” reveals a new kind of moral complexity.

In terms of table setup, Fortune Row plays best with a few unobtrusive props or visual aids: a deck of fate cards, a small prop “clock” to mark time-sensitive scenes, and a place where players can tuck short prompts for quick reference. If you run a longer campaign, consider weaving Fortune Row’s stories into a city-wide arc that gradually unravels a larger conspiracy or mythic truth about the city itself.

If you enjoy cinematic vibes with moral ambiguity and a willingness to let fate tilt the table’s expectations, you’ll find Fortune Row’s pacing harmonious with your table’s tempo. The risk is overloading the session with too many fate-driven twists at once—guardrails here include giving players space to interpret, reflect, and react, rather than narrating every outcome into a megadramatic turn.
