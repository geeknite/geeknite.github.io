## The Identity: No. 044 Gloom in Japanese Pocket Monster Line

No. 044 in the Japanese Base Set (Pocket Monsters) is a Gloom card that has endured as a symbol of early Pokémon card culture. The Japanese text reads ポケットモンスター, which fans abbreviate around the table as “Pokémon” in a more stylized tone. The Gloom card itself is a single-stage (with the option to evolve into Bellossom later in the series) and sits in the long lineage that connects Kanto to the broader trading card game world. The connection to Bellossom—the alternate evolution that came much later—adds a neat sense of progression that players love to discuss when they’re sorting by No. 044.

From a collector’s perspective, the No. 044 stamp is a minimal yet meaningful marker: it anchors a specific print run, a specific era, and a specific art direction by the original illustrators. For many fans, that “No. 044” tag carries as much weight as the character’s lore itself. And when you pair the No. 044 designation with a Japanese base-set design, you get a snapshot of what early Pokémon card aesthetics looked like: crisp line work, bold color, and a layout that says, “We’re here to make cards that you’ll carry to tournaments you’ll probably never win, but you’ll love anyway.”

### The Artwork and Its Place in Pokémon Lore
Gloom’s artwork—typically anchored by Mitsuhiro Arita’s early-Pokémon style—is a study in botanical whimsy and Pokémon-y melancholy. The petals around Gloom’s face dangle like a bouquet that’s perpetually late to a festival. In Japanese prints from this era, the color balance, the typography, and the little card-back detailing all contribute to a feel that’s simultaneously wholesome and a little haunted by the future of cards—like a selfie that ends up in a binder instead of a social feed.

The Japanese variant also marks an important distinction: while English printings often appear with the familiar “BASE” set name and a standard layout, the Japanese Pocket Monsters line tends to polish the artwork in a way that might feel more intimate, more print-perfect, and sometimes with subtle centering and border differences. For LP Gloom fans, those subtle differences—on the edge of a card, or the tint of the petals—become little speedbumps of delight whenever you slot this card into a binder spread.

## The Practicalities: Grading, Collectibility, and Value Drivers

Grading is a curious thing, especially for older Japanese base-set cards. Here’s what affects an LP Gloom No. 044’s value and appeal:

- Centering: The two most valuable prints in this era are those with surprisingly good centering. A slightly left-tilted Gloom can still look great, but it won’t fetch the same premium as a perfectly centered card. If you’re aiming for display impact, you should weigh centering heavily in your evaluation.
- Edges and Corners: LP cards often show minor whitening along the corners or faint edge wear. This is almost expected for a card of this age, and some collectors will accept minor blemishes if the card presents well in person or in a binder spread.
- Surface: Scratches and micro-blemishes on the surface are common in LP prints. Because Gloom’s art is fairly intricate, you’ll notice the surface texture and tiny scuffs in certain lighting conditions. If you’re photographing for sale or display, you’ll want to capture these details, or your buyer may ask for a grading report.
- Print Variants: Some LP Gloom No. 044 cards come from slightly different print runs—Japanese Base Set had its own printing quirks, and if you’re a completionist, you’ll want to verify if your copy is from a particular batch. These variants can influence value in subtle ways for the right collector.
- Rarity and Demand: The No. 044 designation already places Gloom in a special category within the Base Set. In Japanese markets, where pocket monsters and early 1990s–2000s collectibles hold a revered status, LP copies can be particularly appealing to fans who prize “the original look” of the era.

A note on condition jargon: LP (Lightly Played) is what you’ll often see in listings where the card has been gently used in casual play or kept in protective sleeves with modest wear. If you’re chasing minty copies, you’ll want to hunt for NM (Near Mint) or MT (Mint) examples, each with their own premium. And yes, a pristine holo version would command a premium in any condition category—though in this particular No. 044 scenario, holo variants aren’t the standard for the Japanese pocket monster run in question.

## How to Care for an LP Gloom No. 044 (Practical Tips)

If you’re lucky enough to own or bid on an LP Gloom No. 044, here are practical tips to keep it looking good for years to come:

- Sleeves and Binders: Use archival-safe sleeves and a binder with enough space to avoid rubbing against the card edges. A white inner sleeve can help prevent border discoloration from exposure to light.
- Storage: Store away from direct sunlight, humidity, and heat. The petal art looks best in bright-but-controlled lighting, but your card will thank you for avoiding sunburn-like fading.
- Handling: Handle with clean hands or cotton gloves if you’re prepping photos for sale or display. The goal is to minimize fingerprints and oils on the surface, which can be more visible on non-glossy or lightly glossy cards.
- Lighting for Display: Use soft, even lighting to highlight the edge wear or centering without washing out the colors. A small LED light that doesn’t emit heat is ideal for ongoing display.
- Grading Considerations: If you ever decide to get a formal grade, enroll in a reputable service that has experience with older Japanese base-set cards. The grader will consider centering, surface, edges, and corners—and will compare against the set’s known print runs to determine if your LP copy qualifies as authentic for its given grade.
