This bundle is a neat triad: one party-drinking game, one zany expansion for a fan-favorite, and one fast-paced social-deduction-ish party game. ### Sip or Spill

Sip or Spill is the self-styled “drinking game with personality.” It’s built around dares, questions, and the occasional truth that emerges when you’re sipping a beverage and staring at a stack of bold-faced prompts. Setup is minimal: shuffle the card deck, place the dare tray within easy reach, choose a host, and determine your preferred safety lines (we’ll get to safety in a moment—don’t worry, we’ll keep it civilized). The core mechanic is as straightforward as a hyperlink: draw a card, perform the prompt, and either keep it classy or risk a spill, a spill, a spill.

This game thrives in groups that enjoy quick-fire banter and don’t mind swapping uncomfortable truths for a laugh. It’s particularly friendly to newcomers because there’s no huge rulebook to master; you learn by playing, which in Geeknite terms equals: “We’ll explain it as we go, and if the group misses a nuance, we’ll guess the intent and call it ‘creative interpretation.’” The recommended player count is flexible, which makes Sip or Spill a great icebreaker for gatherings with mixed familiarity—e.g., a first time game-night crew meeting up with longtime friends who still claim they’re “not competitive.”

In terms of components, this one leans into a big, color-saturated deck and a small tray or mat that holds the prompts. The visuals are punchy, cartoonish, and intentionally cheeky—perfect for memes and awkwardly hilarious reactions when someone hits a “spill” card. The art direction signals both the tone and the level of sass you should expect for the night. It’s not aiming to be a strategy powerhouse; it’s aiming to be the kind of thing you can teach in two minutes and then unleash with reckless enthusiasm.

Pro tip for Sip or Spill nights: customize your “safety dial” before you start. Unlike a dimly lit bar, you can tailor what’s off-limits: mild embarrassments, bodily-awkward topics, or anything that might push a guest from “funny” into “uncomfortable.” Keeping a few boundaries in place helps ensure the laughter stays in bounds and the night doesn’t end with someone naming a barstool a witness. For extra flavor, convert a few prompts into group challenges—like “everyone votes ‘best dramatic reading’ for this prompt”—to keep momentum up without forcing anyone to reveal the family constitution.

### Bad People

Bad People is a social-deduction-ish party game that thrives on late-night honesty, playful suspicion, and the dramatic moment when someone realizes everyone at the table just called them a “bad person” in the most affectionate way possible. The gist: players are confronted with questions or prompts that encourage guessing which player fits a given negative descriptor. The fun (and the chaos) comes from how people guess, how they defend their choices, and how quickly the group can come to a consensus that’s as ridiculous as it is surprisingly accurate—contextually speaking, of course.

The core appeal here lies in the banter: you’re nudging friends to reveal who they think is the biggest risk-taker, the most likely to spill secrets, or the player most likely to bail you out of a sticky situation—only to find out that the group’s impression is hilariously wrong. Round times are brisk, which makes it excellent for a rotation night: you get two or three complete rounds per hour, plenty of time for snacks, and enough momentum to justify another round without feeling exhausted.

Setup is minimal: you’ll need a few scoring tokens or marks, a timer (even a sand timer works), and a host to keep the prompts flowing. The game scales well from small groups to larger ones by dividing into teams or letting individuals play and justify their answers in real-time. The social dynamic is the star here: it’s not about who’s “correct” but about who can deliver the most entertaining justification while staying within the boundaries of good-natured ribbing.

For mixed sociabilities, Bad People is a balm and a spark. It invites shy players to contribute in short bursts while giving extroverts a platform for bold, humorous takes. If you’ve got a crew that loves memes, pop-culture references, and inside jokes, Bad People is your go-to anchor for a night that wants to be loud, fast, and endearing rather than calculative.

## The Bundle Experience: How the Games Play Together

Pairing Sip or Spill, Zombie Kittens, and Bad People creates a rhythm that can feel like a well-curated roller coaster of moods. The pacing is the first thing you’ll notice: you’ll start with light dares and quick quips, escalate into a few cat-attack moments with Zombie Kittens, and then close with a round of “who’s the bad person” that encourages everyone to lean into the humor of the moment.

- Flow and pacing: Start with Sip or Spill to loosen up the crowd. Move to Zombie Kittens to ignite competition and friendly rivalry, then cap with Bad People to bring out the social flavor—the honest truth you never knew you needed until someone blurted it out with a grin.
- Player count considerations: Sip or Spill shines with 4–8 players; Zombie Kittens scales well with 2–6 (to keep the rounds snappy); Bad People shines with 4–10. When you combine the trio, aim for something like 6–9 players to keep the energy high without the table feeling crowded.
- Host tips: designate a “tone steward” who makes sure jokes land in fun territory and no one walks away feeling singled out or embarrassed for the wrong reasons. Use a visual timer (sand timer or smartphone) to remind folks to keep things moving. For safety, you can adopt a simple house rule: if a prompt veers into someone’s personal boundary, they can tap out and you’ll replace that card with a lighter alternative.
