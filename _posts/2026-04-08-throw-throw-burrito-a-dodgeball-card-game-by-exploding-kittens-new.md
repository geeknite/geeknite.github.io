## What is Throw Throw Burrito?

Throw Throw Burrito is a two-to-six-player party card game that doubles as a dodgeball battleground for your dining room. The players are given a deck of cards with various effects that trigger when two or more players align in a certain way. The core objective is simple on paper: collect sets of matching cards, avoid being hit by burrito-projectiles, and orchestrate the most dramatic, ridiculous streaks of luck and misfortune.

The gimmick is not merely the burritos themselves—it’s the concept of “dodge, duck, dive, and dish” all happening within a sixty-minute window (give or take a few dramatic pauses for crying-laughs). The burritos are soft, squishy, and oddly satisfying to collide with. The design makes each game feel like a miniature sport, a chaotic comedy skit, and a strategic bluffing match all rolled into one soggy package.

This isn’t a game you play quietly while sipping chamomile tea. This is a game you play when you want to relive the glory of dodgeball without the gym, the bruises, or the P.E. teacher who knows your last name better than your own. It’s loud, it’s messy, and it’s absolutely worth the cleanup—the piles of plush burritos, the giggles, the occasional shouts of triumph, and the expressions of pure, unfiltered chaos.

## How to Play: A Quick Setup and Runthrough

The setup is intentionally quick, because the goal is to get you throwing burritos before your guests start discussing the moral implications of violence in memes. Layout the burritos at the far end of the play area, shuffle the deck, deal a hand to each player, and decide who will be the first referee for the round. The first player picks up two cards and reads the prompt aloud. Then, the crowd goes wild (in your own living-room-academic-assembly way) as players attempt to match cards or call out a “swat” to interrupt a rival’s move.

The core mechanic revolves around three actions: toss, dodge, and burrito. Each turn, a player can discard or play a card to influence the current round state. When two players reveal a matching pair—or a set of specific trigger cards—the burritos are tossed. The toss is the moment that sends a ripple through the room: dodge with your movement, or get tagged by a soft burrito. If you’re hit, you may suffer a penalty or have to draw more cards, which increases the stakes until someone emerges as the ultimate burrito-behemoth.

### The Rules, in Layman’s Terms
- You want to collect sets of matching action cards. Those sets grant you advantages or force opponents to do ridiculous things.
- When a two-card or higher match is completed, burritos fly from the burrito spawn points toward the playing area. The precise target is determined by the rules, the referee, and your friends’ current mood.
- Dodge cards and swat cards exist to mitigate or redirect the burrito onslaught. Think of them as the human shield and the referee whistle rolled into one.
- If you are hit by a burrito, you draw penalty cards or lose points—depending on how over‑the‑top your table wants to go that night.
- The game ends when a player reaches a light-but-still-specified victory condition, often tallied by points from sets plus the successful completion of a few objectives.

We won’t pretend this is a precision-strategy engine. It isn’t. The joy of Throw Throw Burrito rests on the chaos calculus: you will predict a move, your opponent will predict yours, and you will be wrong in spectacular, fluffy fashion. The beauty is that even a catastrophic failure can feel like a strategic masterstroke after the fact, because you can retroactively claim you were “testing the unpredictability of the universe.” And honestly, in a room full of friends, that is a perfectly valid reel to run.

It’s the exact same arc you’d expect from a live-action improv night with a dodgeball badge and a bag of plush projectiles.

## Strategy and Tactics: Do You Break the Rules or Bend Them?

If you’re coming at Throw Throw Burrito as a traditional card game, you’ll quickly realize that the “strategy” is mostly about social engineering and timing. The cards give you tools, but the real leverage is in your ability to read your group and manipulate the moment. Here are some practical angles to consider:

- Reading the room: The best players aren’t just throwing burritos; they’re diagnosing morale. If your group is riding a high on laughter, you can lean into rapid-fire plays that maximize chaos. If the table is tense, slower, safer plays might keep the peace long enough for a few memorable moments.
- The power of misdirection: Use dodge and swat strategically. A well-timed dodge can shift the momentum from a near-catastrophe to a safer, more controlled outcome. The audience appreciates a dodge that makes the burrito sail past everyone and land in someone’s beverage, creating a new kind of hazard for the rest of the night.
- Bluffing and callouts: Some cards encourage you to bluff your way through a round, pretending you’ve got a better plan than you actually do. The room loves it when someone pretends to be in complete control and then monologues about the geometry of the burrito before being frankly demolished by a random card draw.
- Coordinate with teammates: In multi-player rounds, forming temporary alliances can be fun and chaotic. The trick is to maintain plausible deniability about that alliance so that it remains humorous rather than a tournament of divided loyalties.

The meta-game emerges: players start to anticipate the exact doom-chuckles that come from certain setups. You will learn to weigh risk and reward not by mathematical probability but by the shared experience you’re crafting together. And that, dear reader, is the heart of the game: you are not just playing cards; you’re curating a spectacle where the rules bend to the gravity of laughter.

## Theme, Humor, and Accessibility

Throw Throw Burrito leans hard into humor, and it dances a fine line between silly and smart. The burritos themselves are cute and goofy rather than menacing, which makes the game approachable for kids and adults alike. The art direction leans into pop culture references while avoiding mean-spirited punchlines, per the modern party game playbook. That balance helps reduce the “my dad would hate this” vibe and creates a shared language for players who may not be die-hard gamers but show up for a good time.

Accessibility is a mixed bag, as with many action-heavy party games. The physicality of the burrito toss means that players with mobility limitations may find the game less comfortable. If you’re hosting, you may want to tailor the display and the throwing distance to fit the space and the participants. The deck is straightforward enough for younger players to grasp, but the fast-paced camaraderie can feel overwhelming for complete newbies. The best approach is to let new players observe a quick demonstration round before jumping in—think of it as a live audition for your living room’s acting chops.

Art and production quality are worth highlighting. The color palette, typography, and character design feel cohesive with the Exploding Kittens universe, while still offering enough originality to stand on its own. The cardboard components feel sturdy, the plush burritos are comforting to squeeze, and the packaging is light enough to carry to a friend’s house without requiring a van loan. In short: it’s a party game that wears its personality on the sleeve of its rubber burritos, and that personality is unmistakably geeky and gleefully chaotic.
