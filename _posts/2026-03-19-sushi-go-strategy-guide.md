## What is Sushi Go! and Why It Works
Sushi Go! is a multiround card-drafting game for 2–5 players that captures the feel of a bustling sushi train with a handful of cards on the table. Its core loop is elegant: draw a single card from your hand, play it face up, then pass the rest to the neighbor. Repeat until all cards are played. The scoring components, while compact, create emergent strategy: you’re balancing short-term scoring with endgame potential, you’re trying to collect not just what’s valuable on its own but what benefits your current set, and you’re constantly reading the table to guess what others might chase next.

The genius of Sushi Go! lies in its delightful asymmetry: some cards synergize with each other (Tempura needs two to score; Sashimi wants three), some reward you for predicting others’ behavior (Maki), and some are levers that can massively swing a round (Wasabi paired with Nigiri). The pace is relentlessly brisk, which makes it an excellent palate cleanser between heavier titles or a satisfying party snack game when you’ve got five hungry friends (or roommates who will not stop stealing your sushi-shaped swag). It’s a tiny engine that can be built in moments and optimized over many games—perfect for Geeknite’s ethos of cheerful optimization and glee.

- 108 cards divided into stacks of Nigiri, Maki, Tempura, Sashimi, Wasabi, Puddings, and Chopsticks.

## How to Play: The Flow in Five Minutes or Less
1. Each player gets a hand of cards (the exact count depends on the number of players; in a typical 2–5 player game, you’ll be dealing out hands of 9 to start, then gradually decreasing as cards are played).
2. Everyone picks one card from their hand and places it face up in front of them. Then everyone passes the remaining cards to the left (the direction can reverse if you flip a rule card, but that’s not in the base game—yet a fun house rule).
3. You repeat the pick-and-pass until all cards are played.
4. Score the round. Then deal the next hand and repeat until the agreed number of rounds (usually three).
5. End of game: You tally up the endgame pudding points in addition to the round-by-round points.

The quickness is not just a gimmick—it creates a “just one more round” loop that is perfect for a quick coffee-break session or a long train ride. The real trick is not always the best single card, but the best card you can pass to an opponent who will penalize themselves next turn by taking it. It’s a social puzzle with a delicious garnish of risk assessment.

## Core Mechanics: Card Drafting and Set Collection
At its heart, Sushi Go! is about two ideas: draft and collect. Drafting is where you grab a single card from your current hand and pass the rest; set collection means you’re trying to assemble a portfolio that scores as high as possible given the rules for each card type.

### The Card Types—What to Watch For
- Nigiri: The core scoring cards. Each Nigiri has a base value (commonly 1 for Egg Nigiri, 2 for Salmon, and 3 for Squid). Wasabi doesn’t add points by itself but enhances the value of the next Nigiri you play on a Wasabi card by tripling its value. So a Nigiri under Wasabi can be worth 3x its base value. This is where timing and memory collide: you want to pair Wasabi with a high-value Nigiri, but not at the cost of missing other scoring opportunities.
- Tempura: A two-card combo worth a fixed amount when you collect both. If you only get one Tempura, that card is basically a dessert garnish: helpful, but not scoring on its own.
- Sashimi: A three-card combo worth a nice chunk of points, but you’ll need three of them. It presses you to think about corners of the deck where you can chase all three without overcommitting too early.
- Makis: The point race. Each round, the player who collects the most Makis gains a fixed number of points, with the second-most receiving half as many. Ties are interesting; they can turn a potential lead into a shared victory and complicate endgame bragging rights.
- Puddings: A late-game scoring twist. Puddings are not in the brief window of “now scoring” during each round; they accumulate in front of you and are tallied at game end. The most Puddings get a big payoff, the second-most a smaller one, and others walk away empty-handed. These cards encourage players to deviate from pure round-based thinking and plan for the final tally.
- Chopsticks: A unique utility card. When you play a Chopsticks card, you prepare to take two cards on a future turn. It’s a little weird at first, but you’ll quickly learn to stack them with two strong picks or smartly dump a bad draft you don’t want to risk keeping.
- Wasabi: The multiplier hook. Wasabi can feel like a trap when you take it too late or too early, but when you align it with a high-value Nigiri, it’s a perfect payoff.

### A Round-by-Round Strategy Sketch
- Early rounds: prioritize safe, high-probability scoring cards. Tempura and Sashimi require more cards to payoff, so you’ll often secure small, steady points with a few Nigiri and some Maki-distribution play.
- Mid-rounds: start reading the table more aggressively. If you notice your neighbors gravitating toward Tempura or Sashimi, you can pivot toward Maki and Pudding to sway endgame points.
- Late rounds: pudding, wasabi, and the last opportunities to grab those last Nigiri combos matter. If you’re behind, high-risk, high-reward plays might be necessary; if you’re ahead, you can tighten your strategy and minimize risk.

## Score Sheets and Card-by-Card Guidance
A practical cheat sheet helps a lot when you’re learning the rhythm of the game. Here’s a concise reference you can memorize between rounds.

- Nigiri (Egg 1, Salmon 2, Squid 3): Basic revenue. Always consider Wasabi pairing; the triple-value potential can swing a round by several points.
- Wasabi: Pair with one high-value Nigiri when possible; otherwise stash it with a plan to pair in the future. Don’t waste Wasabi on low-value Nigiri if you’re already chasing a Tempura/Sashimi combo elsewhere.
- Tempura: Two copies yield points; one copy is a wash. If you’re not seeing a second Tempura, don’t force it.
- Sashimi: Aim for three; if you’re not likely to hit three, don’t chase a third at the expense of other good options.
- Maki: Round-by-round battle. If you’re in a position to win, commit to Maki; otherwise, try to snag a second-place for a comfortable bump.
- Pudding: Endgame magic. If you’re close to the top in puddings, you might alter a few mid-round picks to lock the top place. Don’t overplay it—puddings are a gravity well for behavior, not a universal key to victory.
- Chopsticks: Use them to set up a future two-card take. They are a tool, not a victory condition—don’t get too attached to Chopsticks in round one if you’re not prepared to leverage the next opportunity.

### Simple 2-Player Variant (For Those Who Must Play the Duo)
In two-player games, the dynamics tilt toward dueling and memory, since you’re effectively drafting against a single opponent and you’ll see more cards from a given pool. A recommended tweak is to reduce the Pudding emphasis, as the endgame points are lessened with fewer players, and to slightly increase the importance of Tempura and Sashimi by adjusting the draw count so you still feel the tension of chasing combos. If you’re feeling cheeky, you can introduce a “hidden hand” mechanic where both players secretly draft two cards per turn and reveal one later, creating delightful bluffing opportunities.

## Variants and House Rules Worth Trying
- Sushi Go! Party!: An expanded card pool that introduces new character sushi cards with unique effects. It’s a great upgrade if you want more variety and longer play sessions without sacrificing speed.
- No-Rush Rounds: Remove the timer-ish pressure by allowing a short pause between rounds. It gives players more time to discuss table talk and trade banter, which is half the fun in group gaming.
- Thematic Variants: Swap in pop-culture or sci-fi sushi cards for a themed night. The core mechanics stay intact, but bragging rights become more entertaining with flavor text.
- Pudding Tournaments: If you’re hosting a mini tournament, assign pudding points as a ladder with a few twists (e.g., tie-breakers based on most Tempura or most Wasabi used) to create a microcosm of competitive play.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid (So You Don’t Look Silly on the Internet)
- Over-prioritizing one combo: It’s easy to chase three Sashimi or two Tempura and ignore Maki or Pudging potential. Balance is the name of the game.
- Ignoring Wasabi: Wasabi is tempting, but misusing it can burn through potential points. If you’re not sure what to do with Wasabi, skip it and grab a safe scoring card instead.
- Forgetting endgame: In a hurry, players often forget Puddings exist until the last round. Don’t sleep on pudding! It’s the difference between “meh” and “legendary” at the end of the table.
- The two-player trap: In 2-player, you’ll notice that the round-to-round fluctuations are bigger; watch the passing rhythm, not just the cards in your own hand.

## Practical Tactics: From Opening Moves to Final Brags
- Opening gambit: In the first couple of rounds, you’ll want to secure a small baseline of points with a couple of Nigiri cards and maybe a safe Maki. If you’re lucky and you see Tempura or Sashimi early, you can pivot to a more aggressive combo strategy.
- Mid-game pivot: This is where table-read comes into play. If your neighbors have loaded up on Maki, you might choose to diversify with Nigiri and Wasabi to ensure you don’t fall behind when the Maki points are tallied.
- Endgame flourish: If you’re in the pudding lead, protect it by prioritizing Pudding cards or preventing others from grabbing a critical Pudding. If you’re behind, you may chase a late Sashimi set or push a Wasabi-Nigiri combo that could swing a few key points in your direction.
