## Introduction
In the neon fog of the city, fortunes are not just coins tossed to the wind; they are sticky with myth, memory, and maniacs wearing fedoras. Fortune Row is a scenario pack for City of Mist that leans into street level magic and crime, a delicious blend of noir mood and mythic flavor. This review dives into what you actually get when you crack open Fortune Row, how the three acts hang together, and whether it will survive a table full of players who insist on turning every clue into a podcast audition. If you crave a compact, mood heavy arc that respects player agency while giving the GM a sturdy scaffold, Fortune Row should be on your radar.

## Fortune Row concept
Fortune Row takes the City of Mist premise and narrows the focus to a single district of the city where luck is a currency and the fate of blocks rises and falls with the tides of probability. The pack treats luck as a social asset as well as a narrative engine, letting players gamble with outcomes and coax consequences out of the city itself. Expect a vibe that mixes film noir grit with art deco shine and a touch of carnival magic. This is not a sprawling metropolis mega-arc; it is a micro arc designed to fit into a single evening or stretch neatly into a two session mini campaign.

The package also leans into the tactile pleasures of tabletop gaming: print friendly maps that look good on a kitchen table, handouts that get passed around like tax forms at a chaotic club, and an art direction that feels cohesive with City of Mist but distinctly tuned to Fortune Row. You get enough components to run things smoothly without feeling like you have to hire a cartographer and a sound designer to pull off a one shot.

## How Fortune Row plays
City of Mist players operate as masked investigators who embody myths and legends in the modern world. Fortune Row leans into that by foregrounding luck as both a complicating factor and a narrative lever. The GM kit is designed for versatility: use it as a standalone one shot to deliver a complete experience, or expand it into a two or three session arc within a broader campaign. The three acts provide a clock that ticks with escalating stakes, while the acts themselves are built to accommodate player-driven detours without breaking the overall tempo.

The three acts are structured around a core motif: Fortune Row is not merely about solving a crime; it is about negotiating the citys luck around you. When a clue leads to a roll of the dice, when a ally or foe hints at the price of a granted wish, players feel the sense that their choices matter in a city that is both alive and adversarial. The system stays true to City of Mist principles: it favors storytelling and character over heavy tactical crunch, but Fortune Row provides enough scaffolding to support intentional GM pacing and a sense of forward momentum.

## The acts and key scenes
Act One introduces the district, its cast, and the central conceit: a string of seemingly random events are orchestrated by forces that understand luck better than the average gambler. The opening scenes set tone, stakes, and relationships, offering a range of entry points for players with different priorities—some investigators who want social intrigue, others who want action or mystery. The first act plants seeds that will germinate across the rest of the pack.

Act Two deepens the mystery and widens the horizon. A betrayal surfaces and a twist reveals that what looked like a simple con may be levers in a larger machine. The pack guides you to intensify the tension while still giving players space to improvise. This is where the luck motif starts to feel personal for the characters. The choices they make in Act Two begin to influence Act Three, creating a sense of causality rather than a random coincidence.

Act Three culminates in a climactic decision about the fate of a district and the role of fortune in everyday life. Players weigh personal risk against communal payoff, and the GM has a few dramatic options for endings that feel earned rather than handed to them by a preordained script.

Some sample scene ideas you can pull from Fortune Row include:
- The Market of Mirrored Fortunes where you bargain with quivering prophecies and double dealing merchants
- The Alley of Whispering Clocks that stores time in small pockets and can reveal the precise moment a choice becomes a turning point
- The Fortune Row Casino Subplot where luck itself becomes a playable resource and the edges of reality blur as players chase a coveted reward

## GM tools and play experience
Fortune Row shines when it comes to GM empowerment. The GM guide includes ready to run encounter scripts and modular cue points that help you keep scenes tight without feeling scripted. The mood boards give you quick visual anchors for personas and locations, a handy trick when you want a table to instantly picture a setting without a long descriptive monologue.

A few practical tips for running Fortune Row smoothly:
- Start with a one sentence hook to re-anchor the table on the districts focus and the luck motif.
- Keep a small list of villainy options in your pocket for improvisation, especially in Act Two when player choices may nudge you toward an alternative twist.
- Use ambient audio or a playlist to cue mood shifts as fortunes rise and fall. A good cue often happens at a scene boundary, signaling a shift in stakes or tone.
- Have a couple of player-centric mysteries ready so they feel their choices matter, not merely the story moving around them.

For players, Fortune Row offers a balance between character growth and narrative payoff. You get opportunities to leverage your masks as you navigate a city that is actively trying to bend your fate. The pack respects player agency, rewarding clever dialogue and creative problem solving with meaningful outcomes rather than forced plot beats.

## Thematic touchstones and tone
Fortune Row leans into a vibe that blends noir grit with mythic whimsy. The world feels lived in: a neon soaked downtown, a hidden arcade that stores the memories of elders, a casino where luck itself is a weapon. The writing tends toward evocative imagery without tipping into purple prose, which makes it accessible for groups who want atmosphere without getting bogged down in prose poetry courtship. If your group enjoys the idea of gods who lost their maps and ended up selling them in a back alley, Fortune Row delivers.

### Replayability and campaign integration
The three acts structure invites replay after a single table run. Changes to NPC loyalties and different factions can create a new flavor for a subsequent run. The modular approach also makes it easy to weave Fortune Row into an ongoing campaign by slotting the acts as a side quest or a mid arc pivot when the table needs a quick reset point.
