## Overview

Throw Throw Burrito is a fast-paced party game that thrives on chaos, physical comedy, and the occasional accidental trip hazard. The premise is simple: players collect burrito cards by hitting other players with soft foam burritos and by snapping up taco cards while dodging flying fluffy projectiles of questionable softness. It is what would happen if a family game night collided with a dodgeball championship and someone whispered that cards needed a bit more spicy action.

For the uninitiated, this is not a deep strategy game. The joy comes from the slapstick energy, the laughter, and the occasional mild bruises that remind you that you are alive. If you want a quiet, contemplative session with a nice cup of tea, you probably should look at something like Tokaido. If you want a memory you will tell your future grandchildren about, you are in the right place—or at least in the right neighbor’s living room during a party.


## What you get in a used copy

A used Throw Throw Burrito copy will typically include:

- 2 foam burritos (the soft, squeezable kind you can actually throw without causing real harm)
- A deck of burrito and taco cards
- A set of blingy goal cards that instruct you to collect specific taco combos
- A small rule book
- A player pawns stand that doubles as a quick reference guide
- Possibly a couple of stickers or promo cards that were included in a special edition

In reality, your mileage may vary. The burritos may have lost their fluffiness, the cards could exhibit light wear, and the rule book might smell faintly like a vintage arcade. But the beauty of a used copy is that its previous owner has already broken it in for you. They have wrestled with the burritos, learned the rhythm of the game, and you get to witness the afterglow in the form of a bargain that might not scream “mint condition,” but definitely whispers “character.”


## The used market: pros, cons, and etiquette

Pros:

- Price: Used copies often undercut new prices by a decent margin, which means more money for snacks and extra burritos for the chaos to come.
- Story potential: A used copy has a history. There might be doodles in the margins, a sticker that tells you the history of a party, or a tiny scuff that becomes a conversation starter.
- Preparedness: If the previous owner already played through the learning curve, you get to skip some of the awkward setup jitters and dive straight into chaos.

Cons:

- Wear and tear: Worn foam burritos can feel less than fluffy; cards may have creases; you might need a quick wash or re-fluffing of the burritos to get back to peak performance.
- Odor: Some used copies may carry a faint scent. Common culprits: pizza, soda, gaming nights that ran late, or that one mysterious spill that created a legend.
- Missing bits: It’s not unheard of for a used copy to come with a missing burrito or a miscounted deck. Always verify before you bid farewell to your hard-earned cash.

Etiquette tip: if you are flipping a used copy, message politely and ask for photographs of the burritos, cards, and any signs of wear. This is not a retro gaming market where you swing fists for entitlements; a friendly approach yields better results and less buyer remorse.

## How to play: a quick refresher (for the used copy veteran)

Throw Throw Burrito is built around a simple loop that people frequently mistake for chaos and discover as the core joy:

- Draw cards and match burrito attacks with card effects.
- When you hit someone with a burrito, you earn a chance to collect a taco card or a burrito token.
- The first person to collect a certain combo of taco cards and burrito cards wins, or the person who clears the most points from the deck may claim victory depending on house rules.
- The action is interrupted by laughter, shouts of glee, and the occasional shriek when the burrito whiffs your hair and you realize you almost became a burrito casualty.

The beauty of the used copy is that you can lean into the chaos without worrying about preserving pristine cards. You can play with friends who have never played a party game before and watch as they realize that timing, not pure strategy, wins the day. The rules, while straightforward, invite improvisation and a culture of goofiness. And that, dear reader, is exactly what makes Throw Throw Burrito a staple in many a geek circle.


## The chaos factor: safety first, fun always

Do not let the term chaos fool you into thinking you should throw burritos at full speed. These are soft foam burritos. They are designed to be safe, but you still want a friendly approach. A good rule of thumb: no aiming for faces, be mindful of pets, and keep the throw height at chest level or below. The last thing you want is a broken lamp, a dented wall, or a neighbor who calls your city’s HOA on you for creating a “public nuisance of pastry-like projectiles.” See, the game can be a hazard, but with common sense and consent among players, it becomes a memory you will laugh about for years.

For those who plan to play with younger audiences, adjust with a lighter hand and set some ground rules up front. The point is to create shared humor, not to injure a family heirloom or smash a glass of milk. If you are using a used copy, you can also inspect the space you play in and designate a no-break policy for fragile items. The result is a night of silly chaos with a safety net that feels almost like a warm hug from a game night that understands your limits.


## Strategy vs. chaos: is there room for tactics?

If you want the short answer: there is not much room for deep strategy. The charm of Throw Throw Burrito lies in its spontaneity. Still, there are small tactical elements you can lean on that make the experience less random chaos and more cheeky collaboration:

- Card timing: hold onto certain taco cards until the risk of attack increases for your rivals. It creates a cat-and-mouse moment that makes the group howl with laughter.
- Placement: use your burritos to disrupt the flow of the play area in a way that slows a dominant player’s momentum without risking your own safety.
- Reading the room: if you know your friends, you can predict when they will throw a burrito and thus dodge, counter, or bait them into a near-miss that doubles the laughter.

In a used copy, the tactile feel of the burritos adds a little extra spice. The fluff may be compressed in places, but that doesn’t necessarily ruin the experience. It may even feel more intimate; the burritos have become a ritual of your group’s identity, the same way a long-running campaign in a tabletop RPG becomes part of your social DNA.
