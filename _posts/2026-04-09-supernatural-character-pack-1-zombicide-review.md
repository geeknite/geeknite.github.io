### Characters and abilities
The heart of any Zombicide expansion is its character roster, and this pack is no exception. We get four survivors, each with a distinct theme and a handful of new action cards that let them lean into that vibe without turning the game into a magical murder mystery in a library.

#### The Seer
- Theme: Vision and foresight. This character seems to know something the other survivors don't—usually that the dice hate them.
- Core mechanic: Once per round, The Seer may reveal the next zombie activation sequence by discarding a specific card, letting the table plan a beat ahead.
- Starting item: a talisman that adds a small defensive boost when you're cornered.
- Playstyle: Think intuitive buff support with a touch of risk management. The Seer excels when you pair them with aggressive teammates who can capitalize on the information sweep that comes from foresight.

#### The Wraith
- Theme: Ghostly mobility and silent takedowns. The Wraith does not need to breathe, and occasionally that matters.
- Core mechanic: May ignore walls for a limited number of steps per turn and can pass through zombies without triggering crowd-control penalties.
- Starting item: a small lantern that reveals hidden threat icons on the board, albeit with a minus to action economy while lit.
- Playstyle: A kinetic repositioner who folds space, a tactical weaver rather than a bruiser. The Wraith rewards careful planning and risk assessment—sometimes that means skirting around a cluster of zombies instead of diving into the melee.

#### The Hexer
- Theme: Curses and hex-based manipulation. Hexes are not friendly to the undead—usually.
- Core mechanic: Can place a Curse marker on a zombie to weaken its defense or slow its movement, with stacking effects if you hit multiple targets.
- Starting item: a ritual dagger that provides a small damage boost against undead.
- Playstyle: The Hexer is a control specialist who punishes poor positioning. If you enjoy turning the tide with status effects rather than brute force, The Hexer becomes a spell-caster with a very physical overlay.

#### The Revenant
- Theme: Comeback from the dead—literally. The Revenant is built to be a durable presence on the board.
- Core mechanic: If knocked out, The Revenant returns to the field at a reduced health value after one round, with a temporary vulnerability that you must manage.
- Starting item: regenerative charm that grants a one-time heal after your first activation if you're still alive.
- Playstyle: A resilient anchor that can soak damage and keep the team's plan alive when the table is about to flip. The Revenant thrives in objective-heavy scenarios where you need someone to stay alive long enough to trigger late-game complications.

These four characters are designed to be flexible and thematic, not gimmicky. The designers have balanced their power curves so that you can mix them into a standard mission without having to restructure your entire campaign. If you've ever wanted your zombies to zig while your survivors zag, this pack delivers that misdirection in spades.

### Cards, rules, and how they fit with the base game
The Supernatural Character Pack 1 doesn't just hand you four new miniatures and call it a day. It threads a few new rules and mechanics into the existing Zombicide fabric, creating opportunities for synergy and strategy that feel natural rather than tacked on.

- New action cards: Each survivor brings a small deck of action cards designed to be played in conjunction with the core action cards. They don't replace the base kits; they augment them, giving you a reason to draft differently when you know you'll be facing a haunted district with a mix of zombies who don't play fair.
- Curse and counter mechanics: The Hexer's Curse interacts with a counter system that sits on the zombie model. This adds a little extra bookkeeping, but it pays off with satisfying chain reactions when you curse multiple targets and chain the effects across the board.
- Objective integration: Some of the new objective cards require players to leverage their supernatural abilities to succeed. This is where the pack shines: it nudges you toward creative objectives, like escort the Seer to the warded citadel or hold a location while the Revenant breathes life back into a fallen ally. It's not a game-changer, but it's certainly a game-softener in the right doses.
- Balance and scaling: The designers clearly tested the pack against several base-game missions with a variety of zombie types. The new abilities don't break the core experience; they tilt it in small, flavorful ways that reward micro-optimizations rather than macro-dolds. If you're a power gamer, you'll appreciate the tiny edges The Seer or The Hexer provides in the late game; if you're more of a thematic player, you'll enjoy the vibe and narrative coherence the pack adds.

You can read more about the core game's approach to expansion balance in the official rules document linked earlier. The takeaway is that Supernatural Character Pack 1 aims to enhance variety without overshadowing the baseline crew you've already fallen in love with.

### Art direction and production quality
The box art leans into moody greens and cold blues, with a glow that made me wish I owned a UV flashlight to photograph it correctly. The miniatures themselves are cleanly sculpted, with good detail on the face plates and cloaks, enough to justify the risk of losing a limb or two while decapitating a goblin with a lucky dice roll.

- Sculpt quality: The sculpts are crisp enough for tabletop photography, with sturdy bases that don't wobble on the average 2D board.
- Card quality: The card stock is thick enough to survive regular use; the foil stamping on character art isn't overdone, which is a relief if you're tired of the shiny stuff drawing attention away from the actual gameplay.
- Component clarity: The pips and symbols are readable, and the color-coding makes it easy to differentiate The Seer's foresight cards from The Hexer's curses without squinting.

If you're a hobbyist who values presentation as a tie-breaker for purchases, the pack holds up in the box, even when you've got a pile of zombie bones at your feet and a tangle of dice at your side.

### Value for money and replayability
Let's talk numbers, because all geeks do math with their spreadsheet eyes when it's time to drop cash on a shiny thing. The Supernatural Character Pack 1 sits in the mid-range expansion tier price-wise. On a per-character basis, you're looking at a similar cost to other small expansion packs, but you're also getting a handful of pretty-quality components and a strong thematic lift that can stretch your campaigns without forcing you to overhaul your entire playstyle.

- Replayability: The four new characters each play very differently from the core survivors. Combined with the new objective cards, you've got a handful of fresh mission ideas to spice up sessions that feel a little stale after a dozen plays.
- Longevity: The pack works well as a narrative spine for a handful of sessions; it's not a replacement for a full campaign arc, but it gives you the arc-lite you sometimes crave when you're not ready to commit to a full new box.
- Accessibility: The learning curve remains friendly for newcomers; you don't need to memorize a sprawling rulebook just to enjoy the new characters. You can integrate one character at a time if your group wants to test the waters before committing to a full party.

All told, the value lies not in raw power but in the flavor and campaign potential. If you're a person who collects every morsel of lore in a zombie apocalypse, this pack pays you back with story hooks and memorable moments more than it pays you in raw damage.

### Playing tips and ready-made scenarios
To help you get started, here are a few practical tips and tiny scenarios you can run in your next session:
- Use The Seer's foresight to pre-plan two turns ahead during crowded zombie spawns; this makes a huge difference when you have a doorway you need to defend.
- Let The Wraith act as a mobile flanker, drawing zombies away from a fragile ally who has the Revenant's big moment on cooldown.
- Build a mini-campaign arc around the Hexer's curses: stage two to three missions where you must cancel a potent curse by performing a ritual, which itself becomes a mission objective.
- In solo play, treat each transformation mechanic as a limited-run power that you unlock after meeting specific conditions. It keeps the feel of a living, breathing world—without requiring four pages of solo rules.

If you want to extend your quick-start ideas, consider designing a tiny scenario: the haunted metro station. The Seer knows a critical exit's location, The Wraith slips through a tunnel, The Hexer curses a zombie boss to slow it down, and The Revenant fights to escort a dying ally to safety. It’s a compact narrative that feels bigger than the sum of its parts.

### The verdict: who should buy this, and why
If you're chasing thematic depth and a dash of supernatural chaos, this pack is a solid buy. It won't turn your base game into a completely different beast, but it will provoke you to think differently about how you approach each turn. If you're the kind of Zombicide player who treats every mission as a story beat rather than just a puzzle to optimize, you'll likely fall for The Seer’s ambush, The Wraith's repositioning flares, The Hexer's curse-cascade, and The Revenant's stubborn resilience. If you're a completionist who wants every variant of every character, you'll probably snag this pack anyway—because the rulebook for life insists that more options = more fun, even if the options are a little supernatural and a lot stylish.

But if you're tight on budget, or if your group's table time is a precious resource and you'd rather a bigger expansion that adds mechanics you'll actually use every game night, you might want to wait for a sale or skip this pack until the vibe calls to your inner storyteller a little louder.

### How it stacks up against other expansions
Compared to some of the other small-box expansions, Supernatural Character Pack 1 shines in its focus on character-driven play rather than pure number-crunching. It's not the biggest expansion in terms of content, but its strongest asset is the way it nudges players toward narrative play. It's less about who hits harder and more about who sets the board's mood, which is exactly what the supernatural theme should feel like on a cozy gaming night.

For players who adore the base game's rhythm and want a gentle nudge toward improvisation, this pack is a good match. For players who want a heavy rules overhaul or a pile of new mechanics to parse between turns, you might feel that you've picked up a few nice cards, not a revolution.

### Final thoughts and a recommendation
If you're a Zombicide enthusiast who loves a touch of the uncanny and a nudge toward more narrative opportunities, you'll likely be very pleased with the Supernatural Character Pack 1. The characters feel distinct, the art and components are up to par, and the new cards give you actionable ways to influence the flow of a session without turning the game into a math lab. It's not a must-have for every group, but it's a warm, flavorful addition that will likely live on your table for many nights of one more mission energy.

If you're deciding right now, ask yourself: do I crave a little more story, a dash more chaos, and a handful of strategies that reward clever positioning more than brute force? If yes, pull the trigger. If no, wait for the next big box and hope it includes your favorite supernatural tropes in larger quantities.
