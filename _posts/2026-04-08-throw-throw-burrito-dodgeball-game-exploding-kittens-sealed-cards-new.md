## What is Throw Throw Burrito, exactly?
Throw Throw Burrito is a party/ding-dong of a game that straddles the line between a card game and an active, physical game. It was created by Elan Lee and Matthew Inman of Exploding Kittens fame, the people who brought you the famous online card mischief and a series of board games that make your inner chaos gremlin feel seen. In this game, players collect three matching burrito cards to score points, all while two—yes, two—soft foam burritos roam the table like hyperactive squirrels raiding a picnic.

The core gimmick is that when you draw or reveal certain action cards, you must physically throw one of the burrito plushies at another player to “hit” them. Miss, and you’ll regret not investing in a foam gym mat; hit, and you’ll be the center of everyone’s laughter and perhaps a minor concussion of pride. The sealed cards variant simply means you’re getting a fresh, un-touched deck with all the explosive kitten energy intact. If you’re into chaotic dexterity games with a strong social deduction vibe (because sometimes you can’t help but question whether your friend is lying about their burrito collection), this one is for you.

## How to play: a crash course for Chaos 101
For our purposes, we’ll keep this section high-level—enough to get you into the fray without requiring a law degree in burrito jurisprudence. The sealed cards variant doesn’t drastically change core mechanics; it simply freshens the card pool and ensures you’re not playing with the same combinations your group used last time.

- Setup: Shuffle the burrito cards and deal them to players according to the rulebook. Place the remaining deck in the center. Put the two foam burritos within easy reach of everyone. Decide on a play order, which, in Geeknite terms, means “who can sprint to the table the fastest without knocking over the lava lamp.”
- Goal: Collect three matching burrito cards to score. Each trio is a mini-victory lap. The moment you’ve got a set, you can declare it and gain points while your table mates gnash their teeth in mock envy.
- Turns: On your turn, you typically draw a card, play an action card, or attempt to make a set. The action cards drive the chaos: you might be forced to swap cards with someone, steal a card from someone, or trigger a dodgeball moment that sends a burrito toward a chosen target.
- Dodgeball moments: Here’s where the game earns its street cred. When an action card mentions a dodgeball moment, you aim (carefully, safely) to throw a foam burrito at an opponent. The non-damaging nature of the burritos keeps the experience silly rather than dangerous. It’s a theatrical, cartoonish moment that makes your party feel more like a comedy improv than a serious strategy session.
- Endgame: The game ends when someone hits a predetermined score cap or when the deck runs out. The fun persists well beyond the last card because of the stories you’ll tell about that one time you almost launched a burrito into a ceiling fan (please, do not do this in real life; we like you in one piece).

## Components in detail: quality vs chaos
- Cards: The sealed cards deliver satisfying shuffles and crisp art. The color coding makes it easier to identify sets at a glance, which is essential when you’re also balancing a foam burrito and maintaining eye contact with your rival.
- Foam burritos: The real star of the show. They’re soft enough to be safe in casual play but sturdy enough to survive a few too-enthusiastic throws. If you’re a nervous host, keep a napkin or two handy for the inevitable burrito spill incident.

## Strategy, luck, and how to look cool when you’re not
Let’s be real: this game thrives on luck and chaos. If you’re hoping to win by careful counting and micro-optimizations, you’re in the wrong arena. But that doesn’t mean you should abandon all strategy. Here are a few tips to maximize your moments (without becoming a jerk about it):

- Read the room, not just the rulebook: If you can anticipate when the table is more likely to react to a dodgeball moment, you can time your card plays to maximize fun and minimize injury (to your relationship with your furniture, at least).
- Stack the dice in your favor with smart timing: When you have a set pending, you may want to hold off on playing an aggressive card until you’re confident you won’t be targeted too early. It’s not about cheating the system; it’s about managing social momentum.
- Don’t be a burrito monopolist: The game is fun when everyone gets their moment. If you find yourself hoarding burritos to deny others, you’ll probably hear more groans than cheers. The spirit is cooperative mischief as much as it is competition.
- Use the two burritos as a strategic resource: They’re not just props; you can leverage them to create dramatic opportunities. For example, you might trigger a dodgeball moment and simultaneously steal a crucial card, turning a near-defeat into a last-second win. It requires timing and a bit of theatrical flair.
