## The Miniatures: Sculpt, Pose, and Painting Potential

Batman’s figure lives in a world where every belt buckle carries the weight of a thousand film scenes. If Batman is the quiet night, Batman-Who-Laughs is the carnival of chaos. ### Robins SW (New): The Reforged Wing
The Robins are the wildcard here. SW (New) suggests an update that brings them into a late-game viability as support artillery or fast-hit units. The sculpts present improved articulation in the legs for dynamic poses, and the armor is modernized with modular bits. Painting them is a treat: you can emphasize the Robin color palette without it looking childish, by using weathered gold trims, serious metallics for gear, and muted red for capes. The team’s synergy with Batman is less “you hit them, and I finish” and more “we hit them together, with style.”

## Painting & Assembly: Time, Tools, and Tips
If you’re a hobbyist who likes the slow burn of a long painting session, Pack 1 is friendly. Most faces are clearly defined enough that you can pick a color style and stay consistent across all three figs. Here are some practical tips:
- Use a thin black wash to accent recesses on Batman’s cape and the Batman-Who-Laughs’ armor plates. It sells the depth without requiring heavy dry-brushing on every notch.
- For the Robins SW (New), pick a base color for armor and a separate color for the cloth—try a dark steel for armor, maroon for cloth, and a bright gold for insignias to draw the eye.
- Metallics can be tricky on resin parts if you’re not careful with primer. A good brass or gunmetal base coat with a blue or green tint wash tends to look cinematic without flashy overkill.
- Base dioramas: CMON’s scenario cards sometimes hint at board terrain pieces. Consider a rooftop or alleyway diorama to set the scene. A few bits of cork, rock texture paste, and dry brush will do wonders.

If you’re a painter who loves to get into the nitty-gritty, the Robins SW (New) provide a nice canvas due to their framing—great for edge highlights and small, careful freehand on insignias. If you’re a color-first hobbyist, Batman and Batman-Who-Laughs are the perfect test beds for dual-tone effects, where you can keep the cape shading consistent with the cowl’s darker, glossy finish.

## Gameplay Mechanics: How They Play on a Tabletop
CMON’s approach typically mixes skirmish-level combat with card-driven mechanics. You’ll get a sense of rhythm when you build a small squad and run through a mission. Here’s what Pack 1 generally offers, in broad strokes:

- Robins SW (New) as support and flanks: They’re likely to have faster movement and targeted abilities that allow you to harass or pin the opponent’s heavier hitters. In practice, you’ll want to weave them into your plan so Batman can safely advance with minimal risk.
- Scenario variability: The mission cards introduce different victory conditions—capture points, escort objectives, and timed threats. The mix of three distinct units fosters multiple playstyles: defensive, offensive, and support-heavy combos.

Balance-wise, Pack 1 leans slightly toward thematic play: Batman and Batman-Who-Laughs define two ends of the spectrum—order vs. chaos—with Robins SW (New) acting as a versatile middle ground. It’s not a pure “one perfect build” situation; there are enough options to encourage players to try unorthodox routes, such as Batman-Who-Laughs leading a rapid strike path with Robins flanking for rear coverage.
