## How to play: Rules, twists, and theater magic
Uno Wicked doesn’t reinvent the wheel; it re-tunes it with a Wicked mallet. The core UNO mechanics remain the same: you match color or number, you draw if you can’t, you play a Wild card to switch colors, etc. The novelty lies in the special cards that echo Wicked lore and the new variants that add a little extra sparkle to the chaos.

### Core rules refresher
- Each player starts with seven cards. The goal is to be the first to shed all your cards.
- You can match either by number or by color. When you can’t, you draw one card.
- Special cards include Draw Two, Skip, Reverse, Wild, and Wild Draw Four, just like classic UNO.
- The game ends when a player plays their last card and calls Uno. If they forget, rivals can remind them with a playful glare and a penalty card—because this is a Witch-Town theater production, not a library.

### Wicked-enhanced twists
- Wicked Card: A new card type that triggers a mini-challenge. When drawn, the player chooses between performing a quick mood-lighting action (e.g., strike a dramatic pose, sing a line from a Wicked song, or do a dramatic bow) or drawing extra cards. The crowd-pleaser is that the other players decide whether the action counts as “mood lighting” or “dive-bomb chaos.”
- Glinda Card: If you play this card, you get temporary immunity from being targeted by Skips or Draw Twos for one round. It’s perfect when your color palette is leaning toward pink, and you want to channel a little Good Witch energy.
- Elphaba Card: This is your risk-reward card. When you play Elphaba, you must either give away one card to the player on your left or draw two yourself—your choice, but the room will judge you with Broadway-level glares.

### Strategy and player interaction
UNO Wicked shines in teams and party-game settings where players care more about timing and theater than strict arithmetic. It’s a social game at heart: you’ll watch people bluff for color changes, stage whispers about who’s really behind the temporary advantage, and then a chorus of groans when someone nails a perfect “Uno!” moment just as the table erupts.

If you’re a pure strategist who loves to optimize odds, you’ll still enjoy it—these new cards create micro-dramas that are hard to model: Will I risk Elphaba’s penalty this turn or wait for the next color flip? The unpredictability is the entire point; it pushes players to recalibrate plans on the fly the way a good musical plot twist does.
