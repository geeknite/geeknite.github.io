## Overview: what is Yourself and who is it for?

Yourself is a card-driven party game that thrives on personal prompts and hilarious raw honesty. The core idea is simple: each round, players pick prompts that force someone to reveal something about themselves in a way that fits the prompt, then everyone tries to respond with the most entertaining or revealing card in their hand. It’s not a deep strategy game; it’s a social experiment with jokes as the primary currency. If you’ve ever played Cards Against Humanity and thought, “I wish this was more personal and less clinical,” you’re in the right zip code.

This game targets groups of friends, couples, housemates, or coworkers who can handle a little spicy humor and enjoy a good roast—without it turning into a sword-fighting tournament about who’s most morally offended. The recommended player count is 3 to 10; at the lower end, you’ll get quicker rounds and punchier humor; at the high end, you’ll unlock chaotic energy, inside jokes, and debates about what counts as “the most relatable answer.” It’s ideal for game nights, parties, or that awkward Thanksgiving where your cousin insists on bringing a “family-friendly” game that clearly isn’t family-friendly at all.

## The mechanics: how Yourself actually plays

### Card types and core ideas

Yourself uses two main card families: Prompt Cards and Response Cards. Prompt Cards set the scene, often asking the active player to reveal something about themselves or choose a context in which their self-identity is the punchline. Response Cards are the meat—the wittiest, most outrageous, or most on-brand replies players can conjure (or rather, can couch in a few words to avoid immediate hospitalization by their mother-in-law).

In some variants, you’ll also encounter Action Cards that shake up the standard flow with wild twists—things like “Swap Hands” or “Everyone reveals one fact about themselves” that can turn a sleepy round into a high-energy improv show. The key twist (and why it’s called Yourself) is that many prompts explicitly reference the players’ own experiences or traits, nudging folks toward content that’s personal rather than generic. If you’re worried about crossing lines, the game typically includes boundaries or a safety note to keep things fun and consensual.

### Setup and quickstart: what you need to know

Here’s the in-a-nutshell version:

1) Shuffle the Prompt Cards and the Response Cards into their respective decks. If you’re using expansions, you can separate or mix them based on how chaotic you want the night.
2) Each player draws a starting hand of Response Cards (usually around 7-8 cards).
3) Pick a round host (or rotate hosting duties). The host draws a Prompt Card and reads it aloud.
4) Every other player (or all players, depending on your house rules) selects one Response Card from their hand that best fits the prompt; they submit it face-down to the host.
5) The host reads the chosen responses aloud and then selects the winner for that round—usually the funniest, boldest, or most accurate fit—depending on your agreed-upon criteria.
6) The winner draws a new Prompt Card, and the round continues with the next host.

A note on pacing: do not overthink the timing. The magic of Yourself lives in the reactions—the quick quips, the confident brag, the awkward pause after a particularly brutal truth. If your group enjoys improv and roasting each other with good-natured vibe checks, you’re going to have a blast. If you crave stricter strategy or deeper world-building, this is probably not your destiny—but you’ll still laugh a lot.

## How to set up for success: quickstart tips

- Pre-sort expansions by group compatibility. If you’re playing with a mixed crowd, you might want to skip the most extreme prompts until you know your audience.
- Establish a house rules quick-start: what’s the minimum for a “win,” how do you handle ties, and what constitutes a “roast that goes too far”? It’s easiest to write these down in the rulebook margins or in a shared chat before the first round.
- Create a “no-judgment” moment at the table. People should feel safe enough to reveal something about themselves without feeling attacked or too exposed. You want laughs, not therapy bills.
- Mix in the Action Cards with the standard round cadence to keep the game lively and unpredictable.
- Keep a running scoreboard that resembles a ridiculous trophy, not a medical chart. The goal is fun, not soul-searching.

## Turn structure: a play-by-play outline

1) Host draws a Prompt Card and reads it aloud.
2) Each player selects a Response Card from their hand and submits it face-down.
3) The host shuffles and reads the responses aloud, mounted to the prompt.
4) The host selects a winner and awards a point—plus, optionally, a silly side-challenge consequence for the loser.
5) Draw back up to your hand size and pass host duties clockwise.
6) Repeat for a predetermined number of rounds or until someone hits a target score.

Turn pacing is the trickiest part. If you let players drone on for long, the table’s energy drops faster than a discount battery on a camping trip. The host should keep things moving with gentle timing prompts and a sense of rhythm. If you feel the energy slipping, drop a quick Action Card that makes everyone swap a card with someone else or reveal a fact about themselves that they’d prefer to keep private. It’s a house rule, after all.

## The humor factor: what makes it funny (or not)

Humor in Yourself comes from the collision of personal truths with outrageous card text. The best moments arrive when a real-life detail collides with a ridiculous prompt, producing a moment of shared recognition and immediate laughter. A few keys to maximizing laughs:

- Embrace boldness, but stay on the safe side of the line your group has drawn. Quick boundaries discussion beforehand is worth the time.
- Valor in vulnerability pays off. Players who take a risk offering a truthful or oddly specific answer often win the room, even if the answer isn’t technically the “funniest.” People love authenticity—within the boundaries.
- Timing beats content. A well-timed, perfectly delivered line can outshine a longer, more elaborate setup. If you pause at the right moment, you’ll squeeze a few extra laughs out of the prompt.
- The roast factor matters, but keep it playful. People love to jab at each other when it’s clearly in jest, not when it’s mean-spirited or crossing a visible boundary.

For many groups, the funniest moments come from a prompt that asks players to reveal something about themselves that’s both relatable and unexpectedly quirky. The little details—a childhood habit, an embarrassing fashion choice, a secret talent nobody expects—are gold when you pair them with a witty or absurd response.

## Safety, boundaries, and inclusive fun

A good party game acknowledges that humor can veer into sensitive territories. Self-conducted boundaries are essential. I recommend:

- Start with a quick consent check: “What topics are off-limits tonight?”
- Use a simple opt-out rule: if someone doesn’t want to answer a prompt, they can substitute a harmless, non-revealing response card without penalty.
- Avoid prompts that rely on deeply traumatic or painfully private experiences unless the group explicitly agrees to explore those topics in a safe, supportive way.
- Consider a “mood meter” on your game-night board. If energy dips, you pause and switch to a lighter rotation or a shorter game.

Humor should elevate the mood, not deflate it. A table full of people who feel safe to be silly together is the real win here.

## Strategy tips: maximizing laughs and staying competitive

- Know your hand: cultivate a small list of guaranteed-strong responses for common prompt archetypes. Having a handful of “best-in-class” lines can save you when time is tight.
- Read the room: the same prompt can elicit wildly different responses depending on who’s listening. A line that kills at a rowdy, late-night session might flop in a family-friendly family BBQ setting.
- Play the long game: if you can control the narrative of your own personal truth (without lying, of course), you can shape how the room interprets your answers. Subtle foreshadowing tailored to the prompt often pays off.
- Use pairing: some prompts pair best with specific card types. Save your strongest unicorn cards for prompts that will truly highlight your unique persona.
- Don’t overdo it: one outrageous answer per round is plenty. If you blow your load too early, the rest of the game loses steam.

## Variants, expansions, and how to mix it up

If you want to stretch Yourself beyond a single game night, consider adding a few variants:

- The Truth and Dare Mix: combine Truth prompts with physical or goofy dares for a party-stirring twist.
- The Roast Router: each round, after a winner is declared, remaining players deliver a one-liner roast about the winner—optional, but often hilarious.
- The All-In Challenge: include “group prompts” that require everyone to contribute a line, creating a collaborative story that ends in a ridiculous punchline.

Expansions can multiply the comedy, but they can also dilute the personal edge. Carefully choose expansions that maintain the core vibe: personal prompts, clever responses, and a healthy dose of mischief.
