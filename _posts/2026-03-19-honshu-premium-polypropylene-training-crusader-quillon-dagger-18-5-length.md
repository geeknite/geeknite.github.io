### Safety Considerations
Safety is the name of the game here. Polypropylene is a forgiving material for training, but there are a few caveats: you should always wear protective gear during drills, avoid hitting people in fragile areas (like the solar plexus and your friend’s sense of dignity), and inspect the blade for stress marks after heavy use. The surface is not indestructible; it can develop small cracks if misused or bent excessively. The manufacturer typically recommends not bending the blade beyond its design. In cosplay terms: treat it as a prop, not a crowbar. It’s the difference between a dramatic poses and a dramatic hospital bill.

For safety, keep the blade clearly marked as a training prop, and if you’re using it for sparring, do it with closed masks and compliant safety gear. If you’re teaching a class, this is a fantastic tool for teaching footwork, disengagements, and safe spacing—without the risk of a real blade injuring someone. ## Practical Advice: Maintenance and Longevity
Polypropylene is famously easy to care for. A quick wipe with a damp cloth to remove sweat, dust, or the existential dread of a long rehearsal, and you’re good to go. Avoid exposing it to harsh solvents or direct flame—unless your cosplay scenario calls for a dramatic meltdown scene, in which case you might want to think about allergy safety first. If you store it in a dry place, away from direct sunlight for prolonged periods, you’ll preserve color integrity and prevent any brittleness that can occur with prolonged sun or heat exposure.

For storage, consider a dedicated prop case or a padded shelf. Do not stuff it into a backpack that also contains your real tools; that would be silly and dangerous. If you want to keep the blade pristine for convention photo shoots, a light dusting and occasional conditioning with a mild plastic-safe conditioner is sufficient. Yes, you can even pretend you’re a museum curator preserving a ceremonial artifact—just with a better sense of humor.
