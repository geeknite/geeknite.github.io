Geeks, assemble your dice and check your sanity at the door. Tonight we dive into Zombicide Black Plague, CMON's medieval-mob of the undead that somehow makes a cathedral look like a war zone with a sense of humor. The edition under the microscope is the FR edition titled Adrian Smith 2 Special Guest, billed as Nuovo & Imballo Originale which translates to yes, we are as fresh as a newly minted corpse and came in the original packaging. But let’s be honest: the real selling point is the two special guest miniatures designed by Adrian Smith, not your grandmother’s rulebook. ## A quick primer on Zombicide Black Plague
### What you get in the base game
Zombicide Black Plague is the cream of the undead-turned-dungeon-crawl crop. It’s a cooperative game where players control survivors, kill zombies with dozens of dice, level up, loot, and try not to turn into a casualty list. The medieval setting adds crossbows, swords, plague tokens, and a grim sense of style that fits nicely next to a lava lamp and a snack plate of skull cookies. The base game includes a large number of zombie miniatures (runners, walkers, fliers—there’s a taxonomy for undead fashion), scenario maps, encounter cards, and a stack of crisp, heavy-duty cards that feel like you could use them as blunt weapons in a pinch.

### Where the Adrian Smith 2 Special Guest edition fits in
This FR edition slots into the lineup as a deluxe variant, with two guest miniatures (Adrian Smith’s signature style) and two new scenarios designed to highlight the guests’ abilities. The packaging proclaims Nuovo and Imballo Originale with fanfare, which is a nice touch for collectors who love that “never-opened” vibe—and yes, you can still pretend you’re a proper game museum curator if someone asks what those dents are on the corner.

## Unboxing FR Edition
### Packaging
The box opens with a satisfying thud that says, We know you’ll be playing this for years, not weeks. The FR language branding and the Deluxe label glare at you, which is either a hint of premium quality or a bold attempt to resell your soul. The two guest miniatures are carefully packed in protective trays, separate from the standard survivors and zombies, making it easy to locate them without the “pitchfork-and-paw” shuffle we all do while rummaging for the holy grail of dice.

### Components
The component list is long and loud: a couple of large boss miniatures, a handful of standard-zombie packs, two guest miniatures, new item cards, two new scenario tiles, and a rules insert with clarifications for the guests. The miniatures themselves are crisp; the base plastic feels sturdy in the hand and the guest figures have that extra bit of sculpting detail that Adrian Smith is known for. The art on the cards is upgraded in key places, and the board art remains robust with the medieval cathedral vibe, making your table feel like a battlefield of the un-dead.

## The table experience: gameplay and pacing
Zombicide Black Plague uses action points and card-driven events to create a rhythm. You move a hero, attack with a weapon, search for gear, or trigger a special ability that may end up being wildly beneficial or catastrophically misjudged. The two new guests alter this rhythm in interesting ways: one acts as a frontline anchor capable of soaking hits and teeing up devastating combos; the other wields artifacts that can alter a dice result at a price. The price? A consequence card that triggers when you use the artifact’s power, ensuring you stay on your toes.

The pacing is strong. You’ll feel the tension rising as you’re whittling down the last wave of zombies, only to realize you’re three steps away from the mission’s fail-state. The ambient experience is enhanced by the sound of dice rolling, which in this scenario takes on the role of a percussion instrument in your zombie symphony.

## The two special guest scenarios: details and balance
The two scenarios are not mere “specials” to pad the content. They integrate the two Adrian Smith guest characters into the map design, requiring players to make strategic choices about how to position their survivors and how to coordinate with the guest abilities. One scenario emphasizes fortress defense with a dynamic, moving threat; the other is a subterranean relic-hunt where the relic’s power is as much a liability as a boon.

Balancing-wise, the scenarios feel like a natural extension of the base game. The guest abilities do not overshadow the core mechanics; rather, they offer new pathways to approach the problem of survival. It’s a nice example of how to expand a well-worn formula without turning it into a power fantasy for a single player.

## The art direction: Adrian Smith’s signature vibe
We come for the looks, we stay for the vibe. Adrian Smith’s art gives these two guest minis a moment at the table. The color palette is slightly more dramatic than the base line, giving the impression of a living painting. The backstory you tell about the guests becomes easier to invest in when the miniatures look like they belong in a graphic novel rather than a board game box. If you’re the sort who takes photos of your game nights for social media, you’ll be thrilled with the scope and presence of the new figures.

## FR edition packaging: language, quality, and value
The FR edition’s language integration is good news for French-speaking players; the translation quality is generally high, with a few phrases that may require a consult with a French-speaking friend. The packaging quality matches the rest of the Black Plague line: thick cardstock, sturdy plastic, and a nice seal on the main box. The fact that it ships with original packaging is a nice touch for purists and collectors who want a new edition that feels like it came straight from the factory.

## Replayability, value, and how it sits on your shelf
From a replayability perspective, the two scenarios and guest minis inject enough novelty to justify a second run or two, especially if your group rotates players. If you are the kind of gamer who regrets ever choosing the easy mode in a scenario and then having to redo it, you’ll be grateful for the new flavor and balance. If you’re not starved for novelty, your mileage may vary: you could enjoy the art and the thematic elements but become less enthusiastic about repurchasing for the same game.

## Pros and cons summary
- Pros
  - The two Adrian Smith guest miniatures breathe new life into the table
  - Two well-constructed scenarios designed around the guests
  - Premium packaging and high-quality components
  - Clear FR language packaging that is accessible if you speak French
  - Strong table presence and thematic storytelling

- Cons
  - Price-sensitive players may skip if they already own a base game
  - It’s not a full new mechanical expansion; you won’t get a dozen new rulesets
  - Only relevant to those who want a more cinematic presentation and extra scenarios

## Final verdict and recommendation
Bottom line: if you’re a Zombicide fan who loves Black Plague, this FR edition with Adrian Smith guest miniatures is a delightful addition. It doesn’t transform the game but enhances it in meaningful ways: more cinematic art, two guest players, and two new scenarios that reward coordination and planning. For collectors and roleplay-minded players, this edition is a lighthouse in the fog of zombie chaos.

If you’re new to Zombicide or only casually interested in art, you may want to pick up the base game first and see if the two extra guests and the FR packaging offer enough value to justify the price tag. For those who crave a more immersive experience with a strong narrative flavor, the Adrian Smith FR edition is a good match.
