Exploding Kittens has a knack for turning simple ideas into chaotic party perfection. Throw Throw Burrito takes that chaotic DNA and punches it in the face with a soft plush burrito and a timer. The result is a game that feels like a chaotic improv sketch played out with your friends, a pair of inflatable headphones, and a bathtub full of dodgeballs — in other words, it is loud, messy, and somehow endlessly replayable.

In this review, we dive into why Throw Throw Burrito lands as both a silly social ice breaker and a surprisingly strategic micro game. If you love the original Exploding Kittens, you will recognize the humor; if you hate loud chaos and questionable decisions, you might want to skip the burrito and just watch from afar. ## Overview and first impressions

Throw Throw Burrito is a fast paced party game published by Exploding Kittens. The premise: you and your friends chase glory by collecting burrito cards while trying not to get clobbered by a couple of soft plush burritos that you can throw at each other. It is a kind of hedge maze for your reflexes where the spoils are three burrito cards of the same color, a collection you can hold up like a trophy tape measure when the round ends.

The rule set is deliberately light. There are no complex board states, no resource management to speak of, and no long rulebook to sift through. The game thrives on its real time pressure and the silly tension created when someone yells dodge and a burrito sails through the air toward an unsuspecting head. The tone is unmistakably goofy: bright colors, silly sound effects, and a soundtrack of laughter that grows louder the more burritos fly. It is the kind of game that makes you think, I should have borrowed my neighbor's air horn for this night, or at least brought a pair of earmuffs.

## Components and setup

### The stuff you actually handle

- 2 plush burritos (soft, safe, and surprisingly camera friendly) that are used to retaliate and lovingly beat back the burrito chaos.
- A deck of cards, filled with burrito cards, dodge and catch cards, and the occasional wild card that flips the mood of the round.
- A timer that ensures rounds don’t stretch into a philosophical debate about whether burritos are ethically sourced in the cosmos. The timer is a tiny but mighty force; it is your timer, your coach, and your worst enemy all at once.
- A starter booklet that is honestly mostly humor and art direction, because who needs complicated setup when you have two burritos, am I right?

The components feel purpose built for mischief. The plush burritos have a satisfying squish factor that makes you want to squeeze them all night, which is exactly the kind of impulse the game wants you to give in to. The deck cards are sturdy and glossy enough to survive repeated shuffles, though you’ll inevitably find one or two that are bent from a night of chaotic play and sticky fingers. In short, it looks good on the table and even better in a social media post, if you’re into that sort of thing.

### Setup notes

Drop the burritos in the middle, shuffle the deck, deal each player a handful of cards, and decide a starting timer. The first few plays feel a little awkward as people figure out who is willing to risk a thrown burrito for a bigger payoff. That awkwardness is deliberate; the game grows into confidence as friends learn how to read the room and what to sacrifice for a chance to grab those perfect three of a kind. If you’ve played party games before, you’ll breeze through setup; if you haven’t, you’ll still figure it out within a couple of turns and a lot of laughter.

## How to play: the bones of the beast

### Basic turn structure

The core loop is simple and chaotic: draw, play, and react. On your turn you draw a card and play a card. The cards grant you various effects, from forcing someone to pick up cards to prompting a dodge that lets you avoid a burrito strike. You can also trigger a burrito duel that has everyone grabbing the plush and aiming it at someone else, which is as ridiculous as it sounds and somehow more entertaining than half of most video game boss fights.

The most common win condition is collecting three burrito cards of the same color. When you achieve this, you reveal your hand with enough pomp to rile up everyone in the room and declare yourself burrito royalty for at least the next five to ten minutes, which is more status than most party games ever give you.

### The real time chaos: dodging and throwing

Two plush burritos are the star of the show. They are meant to be thrown, not to become permanent fixtures on the table, but they do a great job of staying in play long enough to add tension to the next card draw. When a burrito is thrown, players need to dodge or catch it, triggering a cascade of reactions. This is where the game shines: the physicality. It’s not just about collecting cards; it’s about managing a chaotic physical component without letting the burritos escape into a neighbor's drink. There is a knack to timing your dodge, telling yourself not to flinch when that soft projectile lands a bit too close to your sensibilities, and using the moment of chaos to your strategic advantage.

### Strategy and mischief

Despite the goofy premise, there is a surprising amount of mind games here. You learn to read faces, gauge when a player is about to win, and decide whether the risk of a burrito toss is worth the potential reward. The best players stay calm during the burrito storms, making calculated plays while others are busy shrieking at the burrito crossing their line of eye sight. It is a test of composure under ridiculous pressure, which is a surprisingly effective antidote to the typical fear of socializing in a crowded room.

If you want the tactical short version: watch who is behind on burrito cards, time your dodges to create window moments on players who are about to complete a set, and always remember that the plums of victory taste sweetest when you have the sense to catch a burrito just as a wild card lands in your hand, turning luck into a three card triumph.

## Components, design, and humor

### Visuals and art direction

Throw Throw Burrito follows the same bright, cheeky aesthetic that fans of Exploding Kittens know and love. The cards sport oversized illustrations, punchy colors, and a sense of chaos that mirrors the game's tempo. The burritos themselves are cute, huggable chaos incarnate, and they invite players to treat the game as a physical comedy routine rather than a dry card game. The art remains approachable for casual fans while still delivering enough quirk for hobbyists to appreciate the micro jokes scattered across the card backs.

### Theme integration

The burrito theme is silly but surprisingly cohesive. You’re not just tossing a random object; you are whether to trust a burrito you want to use as a weapon to secure a win. The humor is an essential engine: it keeps players from taking themselves too seriously and invites a healthy dose of goofiness, which is often what you want in a party game on a Friday night. The attempts to weave in meta jokes about the Exploding Kittens universe land well enough to earn smiles without feeling forced.

### Accessibility and learning curve

The rules are short enough to be explained in a few minutes, which makes it accessible to players of all ages. The real challenge is in the micro-skill of dodging and the social dynamics that come with it. You don’t need perfect hand management to enjoy Throw Throw Burrito; you’ll still have a good time even if you spend the first round accidentally tossing the burritos toward the ceiling. The simplicity is a strength; it means you can bring folks who don’t play many games into a session without a steep learning curve while still offering enough chaos for the more experienced players to explore roles and timing.

The game scales well from around 4 players up to about 6 or 7 in most editions, with the action staying quick and hectic. Round times vary but typically hover around 15 to 20 minutes, with the option to run multiple rounds in a single sitting if people want to test endurance. The sessions feel cinematic: a short burst of chaos followed by a brief lull where someone repositions and asks for a do-over, only to plunge back into the next wave of mischief.

### Longevity and expansion options

Expansions are a mixed bag with Throw Throw Burrito. Some players relish new card types and color sets; others fear that extra cards could overcomplicate a game that thrives on simplicity and speed. The standard set is perfect for beginners and for those who want a pure, fast experience. If you want more color sets and wilder moments, the expansions can deliver that addictive extra level. In Geeknite terms, it is the kind of add-on that can either be a perfect complement or a distracting detour, depending on your group’s vibe.

## Comparisons: Throw Throw Burrito vs Exploding Kittens and other party games

### The lineage you know

If you have enjoyed Exploding Kittens, you already know the brand of absurd humor and cheeky card design. Throw Throw Burrito scratches a different itch: the kinetic energy of real world chaos. Where Exploding Kittens has intense hand management, Throw Throw Burrito makes you choose between protection and offense in a split second, with your body as a part of the game. It’s less about risk management and more about social improvisation under pressure. It is a natural sibling to other physical party games like Pie Face or the chaos of Get Bit!, but with a more modern, card driven twist.

### Against other party games

Compared to light party games such as Taco Cat Goat Cheese Pizza, Throw Throw Burrito leans heavier on the physical interaction and has less downtime between interactions. If you like a constant ping of social response and a camera ready moment, Burrito has your back. If you want more strategy and longer decision trees, you might prefer something slower like Codenames or a solid gateway strategy title. In Geeknite terms, Burrito sits in a sweet sweet middle ground: not pure dexterity, not pure deduction, but a happy blend that makes your group feel like you are on a chaotic reality show rather than at a board game night.

## Why the game lands as a party essential

The genius of Throw Throw Burrito is the simplicity that invites social chaos. The rules aren’t heavy, but the moment to moment play is heavy with person-to-person interaction. The result is that even players who aren’t “gamers” can contribute to the fun and create moments that become stories long after the burritos are put away.

## Tips for maximizing your Throw Throw Burrito night

- Insist on a dedicated space and clear the floor. Your future self will thank you when there are fewer burrito casualties and more epic photo moments.
- Use the timer wisely. It creates the heart pounding pace that makes the experience memorable. If you skip the timer, you’ll still have a good time, but it will feel more like a chatty board game night and less like a ridiculous gameshow.
- Assign a burrito referee. A sober observer can help resolve disputes without turning the table into a boisterous battlefield. This helps keep the mood light and ensures everyone gets a fair turn at the chaos.
- Capture the chaos. A quick phone video after a round is often the best way to document the night and share with friends who could not attend. The footage practically writes your social campaign for you, with your group as the star cast and burrito thrown as the closing scene.
- Rotate who holds the camera. You don’t want to miss your own epic moment while you are trying to capture someone else’s; rotate the role so everyone ends up on the highlight reel.
