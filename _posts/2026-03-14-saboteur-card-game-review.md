Saboteur, a game about dwarves, tunnels, glittering gold, and the unshakable urge to sabotage your fellow miners, is one of those charming little miracles that manages to feel classic even though it was released in the last decade. It’s one of those games that doesn’t pretend to be epic strategy at 4 AM after three sleepless nights; instead it leans into a streamlined, fast-paced microcosm of human behavior. And yes, it gets loud. It gets messy. It gets gold-dusted and suspicious in the exact same breath.

In this review, we’ll mine into the guts of Saboteur: why it works, when it doesn’t, how to maximize your misdirection without making enemies cry into their pickaxes, and how the game scales from a cozy family night to an all-out table-top election of chaos. If you’re here because you heard that dwarves are secretly terrible at sharing, you’re in the right cavern. If you’re here because you want a fresh take on a compact, deception-infused card game, you’re in the even better cavern.

## Overview: what Saboteur even is
Saboteur is a push-your-luck, hidden-role mining adventure in which players take on the personas of dwarves digging for treasure in a network of tunnels. The catch? Some players are honest miners, while others are saboteurs bent on stopping the crew from finding the gold. The objective depends on your role: miners aim to build a viable path from the start to a treasure card, while saboteurs attempt to derail that path or force the miners to chase dead ends. The game is easy to teach, quick to play, and spectacularly sociable—the exact trifecta for a party-style game that still feels like a game rather than a conversation about wood and stone.

Saboteur’s components are deceptively simple. Card stock for tunnel segments (straight, turn, crossroads, and the ever-favored dead end), gold cards, action cards that siphon loot or bend the tunnel, and role cards that decide who’s who. There’s no complex board to memorize. The map is created on the table as you lay down tunnel cards, one card at a time, drawing everyone into a shared moment of suspense as the path winds toward the treasure or a toppled plan. The art is cute, whimsical, and a bit cheeky—miners with beards that look like they’ve gone hunting for gold and found a disco ball instead, saboteurs wearing suspiciously innocent grins, and a treasure glittering just out of reach, as if it winked and whispered, “Good luck, chum.”

If you’re shopping for a game that plays quickly but feels bigger than its box, Saboteur is a top-tier candidate. It scales from 3 players to 10, and while it shines with a larger group, it remains approachable for smaller crews who want that “everybody at the table knows a secret” vibe without needing a black belt in social deduction. It’s also one of those games that benefits from “house rules” flavor—light tweaks to keep things spicy without turning the whole experience into a boardroom of suspicion.

Hidden roles in Saboteur are the heart and the humor. When the map begins to reveal itself, you as the miner might think you’re about to strike gold, only to discover that the saboteur next to you has just laid a dead end in your path and kept a tunnel open that leads nowhere. The tension comes not from heavy mechanics but from the social energy of “I know you’re probably sabotaging, but I’m not sure enough to call you out.” That ambiguity is deliciously sour and funny, in a kind of cheeky dwarf way.

Total game length is pleasantly short, usually around 20-40 minutes depending on player count and how determined everyone is to reach the gold. That compresses a lot of tension into a little space, making Saboteur a reliable after-work sweat-gland stimulator—perfect for a quick round before winding down with a second game or a pile of snacks that somehow always ends up sitting next to a map of tunnels that people keep trying to plan IRL.

## How to play: the rough-and-tumble setup
### Setup
To start, shuffle the tunnel cards and deal a few to each player. The exact number depends on player count, but the baseline is simple: a start card, several tunnel cards forming a chalk outline of a mine, and a set of goal cards at the far end that may or may not be accessible depending on whether someone laid a path that reaches them. Each player receives a role card face-down: a miner or a saboteur. The distribution favors proportionally more miners than saboteurs, but you’ll be surprised at how quickly alliances shift once the map becomes a little more labyrinth than tunnel.

The treasure cards are placed facedown near the end, and the group agrees on a first player who will lay the initial card. The game then proceeds in turns, with players either laying a tunnel card, playing an action card, or picking up a card from the deck to either extend their own options or sabotage someone else’s plan. The range of action cards includes things like “block” (pinches the path temporarily; a bit of a gamer’s equivalent of a halting spell), “rockfall” (causes a dead end in a suspicious way), and “bridge” (a small lifeline that can connect two previously separated tunnels). The board blank strips of cardboard behave like a living, breathing map—once you reveal a hole in the plan, you learn who’s been paying attention and who’s been daydreaming about ale and gold dust.

### Roles and hidden identity
Miners want to connect the start with a treasure card by building a complete path. Saboteurs want to obstruct, mislead, and occasionally pretend to be helpful miners while quietly ensuring that no safe route can exist. The tension comes from uncertainty: are the person helping you truly on your side, or are they funhouse-mirror scheming behind your back? There’s something delicious about calling a bluff when you’re sure you know who the saboteur is, only to realize you were wrong and now you’re the one being followed by suspicion through a tunnel that abruptly ends in a dead end.

Because the game uses hand management and a finite deck, you’ll find yourself making hard choices about which tunnel cards to discard, which action cards to use early, and when you’re comfortable letting the map chase its own complicated destiny. The pace is mercifully quick, which is ideal for social interactions that become increasingly lively as the tunnel grows longer and more labyrinth-like.

### The card deck: paths, tools, and cunning
Saboteur’s deck is a compact toolkit. You have path cards in a few shapes to lay down the tunnel, tools that can help you peek at goal cards or rearrange the existing path, and a handful of sabotage cards to derail others. The balance between “progress” and “disruption” is key: too much disruption early on can turn a promising round into an exercise in futility, while too little disruption makes the miners’ victory feel inevitable and dull.

One of the game’s subtle strengths lies in how players learn to forecast. When a saboteur uses a rockfall to block a corridor, you’ll notice who quickly recalibrates their plan and who lingers, considering the ethics of building a secret route that might never exist. The mental arithmetic of risk versus reward is on display throughout, but never in a way that makes you feel like you’ve just solved a calculus problem. Saboteur keeps the math friendly and the drama high.

## Strategy and tactics: the micro-mind games at the table
### For miners
- Communicate through acts of collaboration: even though you don’t know who is a saboteur, you can still coordinate with others to ensure that the path remains open long enough to reach a treasure card. The trick is to be inclusive enough to avoid tipping the saboteurs off to your own plan while still pushing toward a common goal.
- Focus on early path development: laying down a contiguous line of tunnel cards creates momentum and signals that your team is serious about progress. If you spread resources too thinly, you’ll get bogged down in dead ends and crumbly bridges.
- Use action cards judiciously: a well-timed “peek” to reveal a goal card, or a “bridge” to connect two tunnels that would otherwise require a risky detour, can change the entire mood of the game. The key is to not waste these tools on vanity plays.
- Read the room: the social aspect is a huge part of Saboteur. If someone looks too eager to help, are they a saboteur playing a long con, or a miner with chaotic generosity? Observing body language—though obviously not a guarantee—adds a layer of fun that math cannot provide.

### For saboteurs
- Seed doubt early, but not too early: you want people to question each other without revealing yourself too soon. The moment you commit too hard to a particular lie, you become a walking neon sign in a dark mine—hard to miss and easy to ignore.
- Create fake progress signals: small, visible signs that you’re on the miners’ side can mislead others about who’s steering the map. This could be as simple as placing a seemingly helpful tunnel card that actually serves a hidden plan to derail later.
- Field your misdirection with tempo: Saboteur cards often turn the game on a dime. The best saboteurs learn to be patient, letting a few rounds drift by before striking—so that the miners feel the heat of inevitability, then watch it collapse under a single well-timed move.
- Embrace the dramatic finish: when the map finally reveals a plausible path that leads to a dead end, you’ve crafted a moment that will be replayed in stories for weeks. The best saboteurs don’t just win; they become legends at the table, the kind of players you invite back because you remember the mischief they brewed.

### Bluffing, misdirection, and the social glue
A large portion of Saboteur’s charm comes from its bluffing potential. You don’t need to be the loudest voice in the room to influence outcomes; a well-placed grin, a faux sigh, or a conveniently misplaced tunnel card can reshape the entire round. The deception is light enough that even casual players can enjoy it, but dense enough for veteran social-deduction fans to appreciate the theater. It’s not about losing faith in your friends; it’s about letting them borrow your faith for a moment and then watching it crumble with a giggle as a tunnel collapses in a puff of cartoon dust.

### Endgame considerations
As the last few cards are in play, Saboteur ramps up. The miners scramble to connect to a treasure card, while saboteurs tighten their grip on the board with a few dramatic reveals. The moment of truth—whether the treasure card is actually reachable—depends on a flurry of decisions: who lays which card, who uses which action, and whether an unexpected bridge makes a risky, glorious path appear. The end can be glorious, anticlimactic, or hilariously chaotic in the best possible way. And that’s what Saboteur does best: it gives you control over the chaos just enough to feel clever, then tosses you a handful of surprises to keep you rooting for the team you just argued with.

Saboteur has a few official expansions that gently nudge the table into new territories without turning the core game into a sprawling behemoth. Some expansions introduce new roles, new tunnel shapes, and even additional treasure cards that complicate the decision tree in satisfying ways. The best expansions maintain the game’s tempo and social tension, rather than dragging players into a tangle of rules that require a whiteboard and a diagram of the universe. If you’re the kind of table where people love “one more expansion,” Saboteur’s kit is flexible enough to accommodate that energy without losing its bite.

## Art, design, and accessibility: the look of a good dungeon
The art on Saboteur is charming and friendly rather than grim and merciless. The dwarves have a cartoonish quality that keeps the game from feeling as brutal as it might be if the theme were treated with a harsher aesthetic. This isn’t a game about gore or grim realism; it’s a game about social play, misdirection, and playful competition. The tactile feel of the tunnel cards is solid; they shuffle well, lay flat, and don’t feel flimsy after multiple rounds. The compact size and straightforward setup mean you can bring Saboteur almost anywhere without specialized gear or a dedicated gaming corner. The accessibility is further enhanced by the fact that most players can grasp the core mechanics after a single round, making it a fantastic entry point for new players who aren’t sure if they want to dive into heavier hidden-role games.

## Real-world play dynamics: what makes it sing at the table
- The faster pace keeps it energetic: a typical round edges toward resolution within half an hour, which is perfect for a mixed group with varying attention spans.
- The social energy is the star: the real currency in Saboteur isn’t the coal or the gold; it’s the stories you’ll tell about the round. Some players become the “Dwarven Director,” orchestrating the map with a theater kid’s flair; others become the quiet conspirators who let others lead, only to pivot at the last moment when the pressure mounts.
- It scales nicely: more players introduce more misdirection and more possibilities for alliances that feel ephemeral and funny. With 5-7 players, the room is electric; with 9-10 players, you’re basically running a dwarf city council with talent for comedy sabotage.

## How Saboteur compares to other quick deception games
If you’ve played other deception games like Blood on the Clocktower, One Night Ultimate Werewolf, or The Resistance, Saboteur offers a different flavor. It shuns the larger-than-life conspiracies and instead celebrates the micro-conflicts of a small group, where a tunnel card can pivot a night from comedic to tense. It’s not about who’s lying the loudest; it’s about who’s lying with the smallest tell, and who’s observant enough to notice it before the round collapses into a chorus of laughter and groans.

The game’s tempo is its biggest strength. It doesn’t overstay its welcome; it doesn’t require a score sheet or a long memorization process. It’s a neighborhood bar of deception where everyone knows a trick or two, and the real charm is how often that trick is used to make others feel both clever and foolish in equal measure.
