## What is Just One, in a Nutshell (and Why It Matters)
Just One is a word-guessing cooperative game for 3–7 players (though you can fudge a few more if you’re feeling radical and have a giant table). Each round features a designated guesser who tries to identify a secret word based on one-word clues provided by the other players. The kicker: if two or more players give the same clue, that clue is erased from the guesser’s sight. The result? A single, clean clue turned into a chain of humorous and occasionally questionable interpretations.

In the best-case scenario, the guesser sniffs out the word with two perfectly distinct clues and a chorus of nodding heads; in the worst-case scenario, everyone writes “word” or something equally tragic and you all collectively shake your heads at the legalistic complexity of human language. ## How to Play: A Friendly, Sometimes Silly Flow
Just One’s core loop is simple enough to teach in under five minutes, but the real magic hides in the timing and the acceptance of glorious mistakes.

- Setup: The deck is shuffled; each round, one player is the guesser. The rest of the players draw a card with a secret word on it. They each write a one-word clue on the provided cards, attempting to be helpful without being too explicit. Nobody knows what others have written, and that is crucial.
- Clue Stage: Each non-guessing player writes one word on their clue card. If two or more players write the same word, those duplicates will be erased from the clue pool before the guesser sees them. This means you need to think fast, but not too fast; you want to be unique without screaming, “I am obviously clever!”
- Reveal & Guess: The guesser reads the remaining clues aloud and tries to guess the secret word. If they guess correctly, you all celebrate; if not, the round ends and you shrug dramatically at the power of miscommunication.
- Scoring: A successful guess earns a point for the team. Missing a guess yields zero, but the real reward is the humor you harvested along the way. In this NEW edition, there are a few optional scoring variants that let you tailor risk versus reward, including one where slightly longer clues earn a tiny bonus—because in our era of content banners, even the word “tiny” can feel like a heroic achievement.

A typical round lasts about five minutes, but with a mischievous group and a questionable typing speed, you’ll probably go a little longer. The humor grows as the clues become more abstract or bizarre, offering a shared sense of “we all missed the obvious, but wow, we got something odd and delightful anyway.”
