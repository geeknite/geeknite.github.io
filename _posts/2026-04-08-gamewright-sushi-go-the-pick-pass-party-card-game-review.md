## What is SUSHI GO Pick & Pass all about?
In this compact party experience, players are assembling a belt of sushi-inspired cards to score points at the end of multiple rounds. The Pick & Pass variant adds a little runway to the standard Sushi Go formula by intensifying the drafting tension and mixing in a few new token types that spice up scoring. The core idea remains delightfully simple: you choose a card, you pass the rest to your neighbor, you reveal, you collect, and you keep the momentum moving like a revolving sushi bar—the faster you play, the more you lean into luck and strategy at the same time.

If you have played Sushi Go before, think of this as Sushi Go with a party hat on. If you have not, think of it as a disease-free gateway into the world of set collection where the only thing you collect more than points is laughter. The Pick & Pass mechanic is designed for 2 to 6 players and is typically played in under 20 minutes, which makes it a fantastic candidate for game nights, classroom showdowns, or weird coworker bonding experiences where no one knows the proper etiquette for talking with chopsticks.

## What's in the box and setup details
- A deck of compact sushi cards with charming, cartoonish art. Temaki, Nigiri, Maki, Sashimi, and a couple of new, playful additions that tickle the fancy of both kids and grown-ups.
- Score tokens or a simple score-tracking method (because we all know spreadsheets are the true rival here).

Setup is mercifully quick. Shuffle the deck, deal a fixed number of cards to each player depending on the player count, and lay out any necessary scoring tokens. The rest becomes a draw pile, and the table is now a tiny omakase counter for the evening. Each round proceeds with players selecting a card from their hand, revealing it, and passing the left or right depending on the round. The twist with Pick & Pass is that the passing direction can change and there are a couple of scoring gadgets that the cards unlock as you collect a set. It is simple: choose wisely, pass strategically, and hope that your neighbor doesn’t nab the exact combo you were building in secret between sips of soda.

## How to play in a nutshell (rule overview)
- Each round, players simultaneously pick one card from their hand and place it face down in front of them. Then everyone reveals their chosen card.
- The remaining cards are passed to the neighbor. Depending on the round, you pass left or right. The direction may flip to keep players guessing, which is where the fun really begins.
- When a player completes a set or collects a specific combo, they take the corresponding scoring tile or token and add it to their personal score track. The round ends when all cards have been drafted and scored.
- The game usually runs for a handful of rounds (the exact number depends on the version and player count). You tally up points at the end, declare the culinary victor, and pretend your sushi prowess was forged by a dragon or some such culinary metaphor.

What makes Pick & Pass sing in this package is how approachable it remains while still offering meaningful decisions. You are not wrestling with an intricate grammar of rules; you are making a quick, high-leverage choice that will ripple through the rest of the round. The cards themselves are bright, whimsical, and immediate. You can pick up the rhythm of the game within a couple of rounds, and then you slip into a confident groove where your brain is juggling sets and your hands are juggling napkins like a caffeinated clown.

### The scoring narrative
The scoring in SUSHI GO Pick & Pass centers on set collection and balancing risk. Basic rounds reward you for completing specific combos—like getting a sashimi card or stacking up maki cards for a big point haul. The more ambitious combos reward big points, but come with the risk that your neighbor might siphon off the precise cards you need in the next pass. The result is a delightful push-pull between prioritizing sure bets and gambling on a spicy, high-impact card that could swing the entire table in your favor.

## Strategy notes and house rules
- Leverage the passing phase. The core tension comes from what you pass away and what you hope the neighbor will discard or keep. Watching patterns in your table can give you predictive power, especially in longer sessions.
- Balance immediate scoring with long-term bets. Some rounds reward fast, early scoring; others require you to hold out for bigger combos later. The best players juggle both risk and timing, playing aggressively when you have a lead or playing conservatively when you need to catch up.
- Adapt to player count. In smaller groups, you’ll see the same few combos repeatedly, which can be exploited to a degree. In larger groups, more chaos and surprises naturally emerge as cards swirl around the table.
- Use the new flavor tokens strategically. They add a fun wrinkle without overcomplicating scoring. They can be the difference between a narrow victory and a hollow runner-up finish, depending on how you place them on your track.
