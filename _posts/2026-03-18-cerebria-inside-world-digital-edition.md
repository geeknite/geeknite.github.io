## Gameplay core mechanics: what you actually do in a match
Cerebria is a tactical skirmish game with heavy emphasis on area control and resource management. The digital edition preserves the core mechanics from the board game and adds a handful of quality-of-life improvements: better AI, online matchmaking, and a robust tutorial that manages to avoid feeling like a chalkboard in a crowded classroom.

The core loop is surprisingly accessible but not simplistic. You draft or select your mood units, you place them on the map, and you start making decisions about when to push forward, defend a key region, or retreat to heal. The digital edition handles the turn flow automatically in most cases, which makes it easier to focus on strategy rather than counting initiative tracks.

### The mood system and how it drives decisions
A central mechanic is how each mood influences the board. Each mood—think of it as an archetype of emotion with its own strengths and weaknesses—has a deck of actions. Some mood effects are straightforward: push the line, heal a region, strengthen adjacent units. Others are more nuanced, such as control-based actions that require you to read your opponent's intentions and anticipate their next move.

In a two-player match, the tension is palpable; in a multiplayer game, it becomes a delightful chaos where alliances form by the hour and then are broken by the minute. The digital edition smooths over some of the fiddly bits, such as calculating range and line of sight, with crisp on-screen indicators and tooltips that pop up gently when you hover over a unit or a region. The result is a game that feels strategic without becoming a museum piece.

### Combat mechanics and resolution in the digital era
The combat resolution in Cerebria is where the system really sings when played on PC. You will roll, draw, or flip chips from decks depending on your mood and the situation. The digital edition handles this with a clean animation suite that keeps you informed about probability and outcomes without making you squint at a tiny icon. The randomness is balanced in such a way that you rarely feel cheated by a luck spike; instead you feel rewarded for clever positioning and good card management.

One of the neat things about the digital version is the built-in AI that scales with difficulty.
