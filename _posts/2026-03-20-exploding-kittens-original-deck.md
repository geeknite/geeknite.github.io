## Exploding Kittens: Original Deck – A Geeknite Review

![Exploding Kittens Original Deck]( /assets/images/exploding-kittens-original-deck.jpg )

Ah, Exploding Kittens. The game that proves you can turn a cat circus into a tactical nerve-wracking sprint to not die of kitten-related explosions. The Original Deck, released after a wildly successful Kickstarter by The Oatmeal crew (Elan Lee, Shane Small, and Matthew Inman), is the version that started a tiny revolution in party gaming: short, chaotic rounds, quick setup, and enough chaos to derail any polite dinner conversation. In this review, we’re going to peel back the fur and look at what makes the Original Deck tick, what it does well, what it doesn’t, and how it fits into a geeky night of games without requiring a safety harness or a PhD in probability.

### What is Exploding Kittens: Original Deck?

Exploding Kittens is a card game designed for 2–5 players (with expansions bumping that number) built around a simple premise: don’t draw the Exploding Kitten card. If you draw one and you don’t have a Defuse card, you’re out of the round. The game is a speed-run of risk assessment, bluffing, and yes, cat-themed chaos. The Original Deck comes with a specific mix of Exploding Kitten cards, Defuse cards, and a handful of action cards that let you peek at the future, shuffle the deck, skip turns, force other players to take more draws, or cancel someone else’s move with the magical power of the Nope card.

Technically, the original deck ships with 56 cards: 4 Exploding Kitten cards, a handful of Defuse cards (the number being generous for dramatic survivals), and a variety of action and counter cards. The math game that emerges is not about deep strategy; it’s about probabilistic theater. You keep track of how many Defuse cards remain, how many Exploding Kitten cards have likely been played, and which players would best withstand a risky draw versus who should be targeted with a See the Future or an Attack to disrupt the table’s order. It’s half math, half improv, and all chaos.

### How to play the core loop

The core loop is elegantly simple, which is exactly why it works in social environments. Here’s a quick walkthrough to jog your memory or help you explain the game to that friend who claims they only play “strategy games” and hasn’t learned how to count to five since the 90s.

1) Setup: Each player starts with a hand of Defuse cards and a small number of other action cards. The deck is shuffled, the Exploding Kitten cards are placed within, and the first player is chosen. The objective is to avoid drawing an Exploding Kitten unless you have a Defuse, in which case you can survive one kitteh encounter (for now).
2) Turn order: On your turn, you may play any number of action cards before drawing. Actions include See the Future (peek at the top cards), Shuffle (reorder the top portion of the deck), Skip (skip your draw), Attack (pass the draw burden to the next player), and more. Some actions are defensive (deflecting others’ plays with Nope) and some are aggressive (forcing someone else to take two draws).
3) The draw: After you’ve finished playing cards, you must draw one card from the deck. If it’s a normal card, you add it to your hand and stay alive (for the moment). If it’s an Exploding Kitten and you don’t have a Defuse, you’re out. If you have a Defuse, you can play it to defuse the kitten, place the Exploding Kitten back in the deck somewhere (perhaps in an anxiety-inducing top position), and continue playing.
4) End of round: The last player standing wins the round. In the spirit of party chaos, rounds are quick—usually 10 to 20 minutes depending on how many players and how much time you spend debating whether to trust the “See the Future” top three cards or to bluff about your own card counts.

The dynamic is what makes Exploding Kittens addictive: a single decision can swing the table from “we’re all going to survive” to “we’re two cards away from a pile of disaster.” This tension is what fuels the humor and the fast-paced laughter that end up being the soundtrack of your game night. The Original Deck is designed to be easy to pick up; the rules are straightforward enough for casual gamers while still offering enough micro-decision points for people who like to pretend they understand probability and human psychology. It’s not a heavy strategy game, but it’s a heavy-hitting social game in terms of energy and entertainment value.

### Strategy: reading the room and counting Defuses

If there’s a core skill in Exploding Kittens, it’s not “outplay your opponent with a clever combo.” It’s reading the room. The asset that professionals rarely admit is how many Defuse cards are left and who has them; that information informs whether you push for a risky draw or you lay back and let chaos accumulate elsewhere. The legal moves aren’t complex; the real trick is timing your See the Future play to reveal the right portion of the deck and then using a Shuffle or a See the Future again to reconfigure probability to your advantage.

A practical tip: early Defuse cards are valuable, but hoarding them for a guaranteed moment can backfire if you’re too obvious about your intent. The table likes unpredictability. If you can project confidence while concealing your Defuse count, you’ll often force others into poor decisions—like drawing too early or letting a powerful Attack card go unblocked when you could simply agree to a safer option. The balance between defusing and risking is a constant negotiation that makes each round feel slightly unique, even if the deck is the same old cat-loving chaos it’s always been.

### Play experience across player counts

- 2 players: It’s a brisk duel of nerve and memory. You lean on the few Defuses and a couple of strategic plays to outlast a single opponent in a high-stakes showdown.
- 3–4 players: This is the sweet spot for the Original Deck. A lot of the tension comes from watching who blinks first when a heavy Attack hits the table and you’re staring down a top-of-deck See the Future.
- 5 players: Chaos erupts in a good way. With more players, you get more misdirection, more opportunities to bluff, and a greater chance of someone getting a “you’ve got to draw now” moment that spirals into a spectacular meltdown of giggles and groans.
- Expansions and variants: If you crave more chaos, there are expansions and additional packs that increase the player count and add new cards, which can significantly alter the game’s tempo and strategy. We’ll discuss these in a separate section below.
