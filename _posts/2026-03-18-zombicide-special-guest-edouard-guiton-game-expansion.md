## Overview

The premise of the Special Guest edition is delightfully simple and utterly chaotic: you invite a guest designer into the zany zombie-collecting party, and you see what happens when a renowned board game illustrator actually gets to tweak the rules, paint a few new miniatures, and insist on a particular shade of neon green that makes the undead look “more photogenic” on camera. The box promises a handful of new survivor cards, a handful of new zombie types, and a new “guest challenge” track that can tilt the game balance in surprising (and sometimes terrifying) ways.

If you’ve played base Zombicide or any of the other expansions, you’ll know what to expect: cooperative chaos, a ticking clock, and an ever-increasing pile of zombie models that threaten to engulf your carefully laid plans. This expansion leans into Edouard Guiton’s aesthetic and design sensibilities—think bold visuals, witty card flavor text, and a mechanic that could only make sense if the designer had once written a graphic novel about a rescue mission that doubles as an art installation.

> Pro tip: Edouard’s influence isn’t just cosmetic. The cadence of the scenarios nudges you toward riskier plays with a sly wink, as if the game itself is leaning over and whispering, “Trust the artist.”

## Unpacking the Special Guest: Edouard Guiton

Opening the lid is a moment of calm before the storm. The components feel solid, with that signature Zombicide heft and a few surprises that nod at Edouard’s profile as an illustrator and game designer. Inside you’ll find:

- 1 double-sided game board variant tailored to the guest scenarios
- 4 new survivor cards, including the titular Edouard character with a signature ability
- 2 new zombie types that introduce interesting decision points
- 1 guest-event deck, designed to disrupt plans in flavorful and thematic ways
- 3 scenarios that weave Edouard’s flavor into the base game’s cadence
- 1 decorative standee that bears a silhouette of the guest
- Rule tweak sheet that clarifies how the new mechanics interact with existing systems

Here is where the art shines. The miniatures are crisp, the cards have punchy typography, and the flavor text occasionally lands like a velvet rope — it invites you to lean in, but you still have to push past the undead prima donnas who want their moment in the spotlight.

The box art and the insert art live up to the “guest designer” branding. If you’re a collector who buys expansions for the packaging alone, you’ll be happy here. For the rest of us, the real question is how much the guest changes the core experience without turning Zombicide into a cosplay fair with a blood stain budget. Spoiler: Edouard’s impact is real, but not overpowering.

## Mechanics and Theme

Edouard Guiton’s footprint on this expansion is most apparent in three places: the guest survivor’s special ability, the guest-event deck, and the new scenario objectives that reward aggressive, high-risk plays. The survivor, let’s call him “Édouard” for flavor, comes with a signature ability coded as Sketch and Sabotage. In practical terms, Sketch and Sabotage lets Édouard mark a zone with a temporary marker that reveals a hidden objective or blocks a zombie path for a turn—think of it as a diva’s way of saying, I need to be center stage, and also, you cannot cross this line unless you’re willing to roll a die with a tragic backstory.

Thematic integration is well done. The guest events are not random flavor text; they reflect the guest designer’s sensibilities. One event card could cause a sudden art-crash: all players draw a random color card, and if you don’t have a match, your unit models become temporarily shy and retreat. The tension this creates is deliciously cinematic, especially when you’re trying to herd a few misfit survivors toward an objective while crimson-lit walkers shuffle into the room like rowdy theater patrons.

If you’re a player who likes puzzle-like turns where you balance turning the tide with careful resource management, you’ll enjoy how Edouard’s mechanics add both room for clever planning and the occasional “nonsense moment” that keeps the session memorable. The combination of a new survivor with a signature ability and a deck designed to shake up early rounds ensures the expansion isn’t a sleepy add-on; it’s a throttle on your adrenaline and a reminder that even a perfectly planned path can be smeared with paint and terror.

## Components and Quality

We’ve come to expect quality from Zombicide, and the Special Guest edition mostly delivers. The new miniatures are crisp and compliant with the standard WYSIWYG approach you’re used to in this line. The two additional zombie types bring some variety without flooding the table with legions of identical figures. The scenario book is well-structured, with clear deployment maps, start objectives, and a narrative that invites you to role-play your way through the session.

One small gripe: the guest-event deck can feel a bit swingy in longer campaigns. There’s a taste of “this is going to be wild” and that taste becomes a bit bitter if you’re in a tight scenario where a couple of bad draws wipe out your best plan. Still, the flavor is worth it. The art on the guest cards is vibrant and the typography is readable even when your kitchen light has the ambience of a neon sign in an alley that smells like coffee and rain.

For players who care about insert quality, the box’s internal layout remains clever, and the new boards fit flush with the base game’s boards, ensuring no misalignment headaches on the table. If you appreciate a nice “click” when you slide components into their trays, you’ll get your fix here.

## The Experience: Solo, Co-op, and Variants

This expansion shines in cooperative play. The new mechanics can alter the team dynamics in interesting ways, especially in smaller player counts where each decision feels heavier. The Édouard survivor’s ability encourages you to consider a few “weight-lift” turns: do you commit resources to draw a Line of Sight that opens up a safer route, or do you press on, hoping your teammates’ momentum carries you through?

In solo play, the challenge scales with an adaptive AI-like deck, and the guest events provide enough chaos to keep you from slipping into autopilot. The key for solo players is to respect the pacing: this isn’t a “set up and ignore” expansion. The guest mechanic invites you to improvise, which is perfect for nights when you feel like playing a game while doing a stand-up routine about your own failed plans.

Co-op groups with strong tactical chemistry will revel in the synergy opportunities, especially when the new characters can set up chain reactions or protect a vulnerable ally long enough to claim a crucial objective. The difficulty curve remains consistent with the base game, but those sharp edge moments—the kind where timing, range, and loot count converge—feel a little glossier thanks to Edouard’s design language.

## Scenarios and Campaign Progression

The expansion includes three scenarios that integrate Edouard’s guest flavor into the overarching Zombicide flow. Each scenario has its own set of objectives, map layouts, and a recommended difficulty curve. The scenarios are designed to be played in sequence or individually, which is ideal for both one-night sessions and longer campaigns where you want a “season arc” rather than a single mission.

The campaign progression features a narrative arc that references the guest’s fictional work within the world of the game. It’s not a full-blown story campaign, but a few connective threads are woven through the objectives and flavor text. If you’re the sort of player who riffs on the setting and crafts your own in-game lore, you’ll appreciate the space this expansion provides for role-playing moments, character banter, and improvised “artistic interventions” on the board.

One of the more entertaining design choices is a special “guest challenge track” that appears if you complete certain objectives within a set of rounds. Completing the track yields minor to moderate advantages in later rounds, but it also increases the stakes. It’s a clever way to celebrate the guest designer’s presence without letting the expansion become a one-trick pony.

## Strategy Tips and Tactics

- Learn Édouard’s ability early, and plan a route that minimizes risk while maximizing payoff. Sketch and Sabotage rewards risk-taking, but it also creates moments of vulnerability if you overextend.
- Balance the guest-event deck shuffles with your own resource management. Don’t let the chaos overshadow your objective-oriented play; use the events to create turf advantages rather than letting them control you.
- Position survivors to leverage line of sight and block zombies where possible. The new zombie types demand a careful approach to reach objectives without getting overwhelmed.
- Don’t ignore the art cues. Some scenarios reward you for recognizing color-coded hints in the flavor text, which means the game can reward careful reading—and a little bit of trivia night-level knowledge about the designer’s inspirations.

## Artwork, Theme, and Flavor Notes

This expansion leans into a bold, comic-book-esque aesthetic that suits Zombicide’s chaotic vibe while giving the guest a chance to shine. The character art is expressive, the zombie designs are varied enough to prevent sameness, and the flavor text on cards lands with a wink or a nudge depending on your table’s humor. If you enjoy a game that lets you feel like you’re participating in a live illustrated panel, Edouard’s edition scratches that itch without compromising the game’s playability.

Thematically, the “guest” concept translates well into the table: a designer’s signature approach is visible in how objectives are set, how the new survivors interact with the base game’s mechanics, and how the deck’s randomization paces the session. You won’t mistake this for a completely new game, but you will feel the air of a collaboration that respects both the franchise and the creator’s personal style.

## Comparisons with Other Zombicide Expansions

If you’ve played other Zombicide expansions, you’ll notice this one sits somewhere between a thematic add-on and a rule-modifying pack. It doesn’t overhaul the entire system the way some big-box expansions do with new core mechanics; instead, it introduces a handful of well-integrated touches that feel like they could have been part of a major release. The guest-focused approach is reminiscent of other designer-led expansions in the genre, but Edouard’s spin makes it feel fresh rather than derivative.

In terms of component quality and game-time impact, this expansion sits comfortably with the middle of the pack. It’s not a time sink, but it’s enough of a shake-up to justify bringing it to the table on evenings when you want a little extra drama without reconfiguring every rule.

## Final Verdict

If you’re a Zombicide fan who enjoys seeing a new designer leave a signature stamp on the game, this expansion is a no-brainer. It respects the base game’s rhythm while injecting enough personality to feel like a distinct, worthwhile addition rather than a cosmetic upgrade. Édouard’s presence is felt through a handful of mechanics that are easy to teach, quick to learn, and satisfying to execute. The scenarios are well-constructed, offering varied experiences that are still accessible for newcomers who already know how to shoot walkers in the head with style.

That said, if your table already wrestles with expansion fatigue or you prefer a pure, straight-ahead zombie-slaying experience, you might want to pace this one with other games or skip it for a while. The guest theme adds character and flavor, but you’ll spend a bit more time negotiating the narrative threads than if you’d simply rolled dice and punched cards. It’s a fun detour rather than the main highway.

## For Whom Is This Expansion?

- Players who love thematic tie-ins and designer-driven experiences.
- Groups seeking a new challenge that can be slotted into existing campaigns without heavy retooling.
- Collectors who enjoy the guest designer angle and the distinct artwork that accompanies the expansion.

## Who Should Skip?

- Purists who want a strictly mechanical upgrade with no extra narrative flavor.
- Players who dislike added variability that can potentially derail a well-practiced plan.
- Groups that prefer compact sessions with a straightforward staircase of difficulty rather than a narrative arc.

## Final Recommendation

Overall, Zombicide Special Guest: Edouard Guiton is a welcome addition for the right table. It strikes a good balance between flavor and function, giving you a few fresh tools to manage the undead without letting the game spiral into an overlong campaign mishmash. If you’re in the mood for something that feels like a collaboration between a graphic novel and a zombie-shooter, this expansion delivers with style and wit.

If you’re ready to add a little gallery-curated chaos to your next Zombicide night, you know what to do. Grab the expansion, gather your crew, and prepare to outsmart the walking dead while being guided by a guest designer who clearly loves both the art and the chaos.
