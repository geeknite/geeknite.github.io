### Painting difficulty and accessibility
If you’re a first-time painter, the Kopinski set is a mixed bag in the best possible way. The crisp outlines help you keep painting within lines, which is a boon for beginners who might otherwise panic at a figure that looks like it was drawn by a very confident toddler. The trick, of course, is balance. Kopinski’s style invites heroics, but you don’t want to drown the sculpts in a single metal wash. A light dry brush on the textures to bring up the raised edges, followed by a controlled wash on the recessed lines, tends to deliver a result that feels lift-off ready for the table without requiring the patience of a saint.

## Painting tips and practical guidance
### Getting the most out of Kopinski’s style on your models
- Start with a bold base color for the skin tones, then work in midtone shadows along the recesses. Kopinski’s figures reward strong light-to-dark contrast, so avoid over-blending in the early stages.
- Use a mid-tone wash to add depth, but don’t go overboard. A little wash goes a long way when you’re respecting the sculpt’s lines.
- Dry brush high points to highlight edges and textures, especially on cloaks and armor. The trick is to apply just enough to catch the raised surfaces without turning the entire model into a metallic frosted statue.
- For cloth and leather, experiment with subtle color shifts—blue-gray for metallics, olive or burgundy for fabrics. Kopinski’s work thrives on color interplay, so don’t be afraid to push a bold accent color here and there.
- Seal lightly to protect your work, but avoid a heavy gloss that kills the matte look you’re aiming for. A satin finish usually hits the right balance between realism and pop.
- If you’re new to painting, consider focusing on a small squad of survivors first. It’s better to finish a few striking models to display on a shelf than to have a dozen half-painted figurines that accumulate dust and guilt.

### Tabletop integration tips
- Place survivors in distinct poses so you can easily identify who is who during complex turns. A quick color-coding on bases can help a lot in the heat of the moment.
- Use objective markers that match the box’s visual theme. This keeps the gameplay cohesive and reduces the cognitive load when you’re juggling multiple tokens.
- Don’t rush to paint every piece perfectly. Zombicide is a social game at heart; you’ll have more fun if your table talk is as strong as your paint jobs. Focus on personal storytelling as much as accuracy—your campaign will feel more alive when you can narrate a scene where Kopinski’s survivors defy the odds with a heroic stance and a witty quip.
