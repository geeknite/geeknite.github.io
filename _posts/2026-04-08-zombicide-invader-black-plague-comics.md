Zombicide Invader and Black Plague comic books take the board game IP into the printed page, and you better believe the undead have filed their papers with a flourish. This review dives into how well the two lines translate their core vibes to sequential art, how the tone shifts from tabletop chaos to page turning suspense, and whether the crossover between space zombies and medieval necromancers actually works. If you thought the only thing these two games share is a love for breaking doors with glorious derelict swagger, you are about to be politely amazed and slightly annoyed at your own nerdy optimism.

## Quick primer on Zombicide Invader
Invader is the spacebound cousin of the Zombicide family. The undead here crash land in starships, orbiting stations, and asteroid outposts, all while wearing enough neon and chrome to make a synthwave playlist jealous. The survivors are less about pinched nerves and more about tactical reactions, jetpacks, and enough blaster fire to keep a hive of macro-brains guessing. In comic form, Invader leans into bright color palettes, high-tech hazards, and a sense of cosmic scale that often tempts fate with a wink. The art team tends to push electric blues, hot pinks, and lime greens against a backdrop of stark space-black, a combination that screams both danger and party at the same time.

A good Invader comic will balance outlandish zombie physics with human moments: a survivor checking a data tablet while a wave of skittering limbs creeps up behind them, a captain barking orders as a hull breaches in a panel that somehow still looks like a diorama you could hold in your hands. The overarching tone is a cocktail of sci-fi dread and popcorn fun, with enough gallows humor to remind you these are people who have learned the value of a well-timed sprint.

## Quick primer on Zombicide Black Plague
Black Plague is the medieval cousin with a taste for bone-white moonlight and plague‑carrying knights who would rather chew their own armor than admit defeat. In comic form, this arc explores plague magic, necromancy, and a society of survivors forced to improvise in a world where every coffin could open to reveal the next wave. Expect grimy textures, earth tones that feel like they’ve been dragged through a dungeon, and a storytelling cadence that loves to slow down for character beats before it hurtsle through a rapid-fire wave event.

In Black Plague comics, the survivors often face moral choices that board games brush past with a grin and a dice roll, which makes for some of the most satisfying sequences. The art frequently leans into stencils and scratchy textures to evoke old world fear, while the color work—rust, terracotta, ink-black shadows—reminds you that the threat feels tangible, not just cinematic. It’s a mood piece as much as a monster piece: a reminder that fear can be as much about setting as it is about the monsters in the room.

## Visual language and layout: art that fights back
A recurring strength of both Invader and Black Plague in comics is how well the pages communicate action with a minimum of sermonizing. In Invader, the panel choreography favors kinetic angles—the camera swoops and spirals through corridors, mimicking the erratic flight paths of both survivors and zombies. The use of negative space is deliberate; the silence between panels often carries more dread than a splash page full of moaning undead. The colorist choices push a cinematic brightness into a space arena where danger lurks behind every lit console.

Black Plague trades that brightness for atmosphere. It leans into texture and atmospheric grit. The shading is denser, the line work more tactile, and the panels feel rippled with dust and damp stone. The difference in palette between the two series is more than stylistic: it signals a shift in tempo. Invader wants you to feel the adrenaline spike as you sprint to the escape pod; Black Plague wants you to feel the weight of whispers in a crypt, the shudder of a torch on damp stone, and the way fear leaks through a village like fog through a keyhole.

If you are a fan of Jekyll-and-Hyde transitions in comic storytelling, both books nail the pivot between staccato action and character-driven quiet moments. Invader often pairs a high-energy action beat with a throat-tight decision by a lead, a rhythm that reads like a chase sequence in a sci-fi blockbuster. Black Plague tends to slow down just enough for a confession or a grim realization, then snaps into a brutal wave of dread that demands tactical thinking and a stubborn refusal to quit.

## Narrative structure: single-volume thrills vs multi-arc tension
Invader stories in comic form frequently frame a contained arc that feels like an episode in a larger show. You get a clear setup, a series of escalating standards of danger, and a satisfying if slightly messy resolution that hints at the next mission. The joy here is in the propulsion: you can binge a handful of issues and feel like you’ve been on a sprint through a cosmic obstacle course. There is room for side characters to emerge—technicians with a knack for improvisation, a pilot with more stormy bravado than sense—and the stakes are consistently high enough to justify the rapid pacing.

Black Plague stories lean toward longer arcs with richer world-building. The knights, necromancers, and clergy carry heavier moral weight, and the writers often use the pages to explore the cost of survival when faith, loyalty, and fear collide. This is not a universe where every problem is solved by a clever grenade toss; sometimes the most powerful tool is the ability to resist despair and keep moving forward when the candles burn out. The multi-issue arc approach pays off with deepened lore and a sense that you are watching a larger epic unfold rather than a single heroic moment.

In both lines, the storytelling respects the source material while letting the medium breathe. The horizontal breath of a comic page—the way a single two-page spread can deliver a strategic tension or a character revelation—feels perfect for a universe built on waves of enemies and the relentless need to survive the next round. If you crave a continuous thread, you’ll likely enjoy the longer Black Plague arcs; if you want quick payoffs with the occasional nerd-out moment, Invader scratches that itch with gleeful gusto.

## Characters: survivors, zealots, and the undead chorus
In Invader, you’ll meet survivors who feel like they could be your neighbors if your neighborhood had a spaceship fueled by panic. The human cast often features a mix of technicians, field officers, and a pilot who believes coffee is a war crime. The zombies in Invader feel almost like a chorus of different voices—some are brutal brutes, others are strange gliding shapes that navigate the ship’s corridors with uncanny persistence. The best Invader comics give you a sense of how a crew would improvise in space when their hull creaks and alarms scream in unison.

Black Plague introduces a cast that resembles a medieval drama more than a sci-fi thriller. The survivors here are defined by loyalties tested under siege, a sense that faith and grit have to carry equal weight with weaponry and wit. The undead, with their plague‑scarred silhouettes, feel both timeless and terrifyingly practical. These aren’t mindless hordes; they are a ritualistic engine of terror that makes you respect the cleverness of their designers. Across both lines, character work lands when the humans show fear, doubt, and stubborn courage in equal measure, often with a humor that lands just enough to remind you these are human beings dealing with extraordinary stress—and a sense that they may not all live to see tomorrow.

## Thematic threads: survival, cooperation, and the joke that keeps you going
One of the joys of Zombicide comics is how they balance the grim realities of a zombie outbreak with the human need to keep going. Invader leans into the adrenaline of spacefaring survival: the ship is your home, your weapon, and your hazard. The joke often lands as characters rib each other about their bad luck or their questionable navigational choices, only to be dragged back into a life-or-death moment moments later. It’s the genre-savvy humor that makes you root for the crew even when their plan seems built on a stack of unlikely coincidences.

Black Plague, by contrast, treats survival as a moral problem as much as a tactical one. You see people arguing about whether it’s right to unleash a necromantic solution in a world where death itself has become the most common ally of both sides. The humor here is darker, a nod to classic gothic storytelling. Yet the writers never forget that the characters’ humanity—bravery, doubt, and stubborn resolve—are what make the terrifying moments land with resonance. If Invader is a fast-paced chase through a neon space corridor, Black Plague is a candlelit negotiation that ends in a scream. Both modes feel essential to the franchise and demonstrate why a comic book is a great vessel for these ideas.

## Accessibility and reading order: do you need the board game to enjoy the books?
Short answer: no, you do not need to own the Zombicide board games to enjoy these comics. The stories are designed to stand on their own, with enough world-building to spark curiosity yet enough standalone moments to satisfy a reader who is just here for the vibes. If you want the full flavor, there are easter eggs and nods to the games that will delight fans who know the expansions, but these are not gatekeepers. The comics do a nice job of translating the core hooks—teamwork under pressure, wave-based threats, and the interplay between survival and sacrifice—into a narrative language that anyone can appreciate.

Reading order is typically guided by story arcs rather than a single definitive sequence. Invader arcs tend to be shorter and more self-contained, which makes them perfect for dipping in during lunch breaks or long bus rides. Black Plague arcs feel like longer weekend marathons, each issue stacking onto the last until you realize you’ve spent a week dreaming about plague gnats and clever traps in a knight’s hall. If you want an ideal pairing, start with Invader to warm up your sci‑fi nerves, then dive into Black Plague for a deeper dive into the world’s history and the moral weight carried by its people. ## Artwork, layout, and production value: is this a coffee-table read or a wallbook? (Yes, both)
If you are the kind of reader who judges a comic by its cover, you will be delighted here. The Invader covers tend to pop with glossy, high-contrast artwork, sometimes echoing sci-fi movie posters with a hint of retro arcade. The inside pages carry the same energy, with crisp linework that holds up even during the most frantic action sequences. The colorist approach is a treat: the blues feel electric, the greens feel radioactive, and the reds feel like a siren call that you know will be answered by a squad’s brave sprint into the unknown.

Black Plague, on the other hand, leans into mood more than splash. The art often uses heavy textures, with a palette that emphasizes stone, iron, and the subtle glow of magical fires. The character portraits have just enough grit to feel real while the action spreads out into panel arrangements that convey the weight of siege warfare and the slow burn of necromantic discovery. It is not a pretty world; it is a meticulously crafted one, where every shard of light in a candle chamber is a dare to move forward.

If you are a collector, you will appreciate the variant covers and the occasional print run exclusives that CMON tends to do for major arcs. The production values make these books feel substantial in your hands, almost as if you could squeeze one into your pack and pretend you are a field operative in a world where alarms beep on every panel edge.

## The reading experience: tone, pacing, and the occasional joke
Geeknite readers know that a good zombie story must balance danger with a sense of play. Invader often lands on a tone that is buoyant enough to keep you grinning even as a horde approaches. The humor comes from characters nagging each other about tactics, from a captain who insists the only weapon that matters is a well-timed one-liner, and from the sheer absurdity of trying to outsmart a creature that can reproduce by the thousand and still look stylish while doing it.

Black Plague uses humor more sparsely and more pointedly, often as a relief after a brutal beat or as a way to reveal a character’s inner life. The jokes here feel earned, usually born from the clash between faith and fear, or from a clever trap that a survivor manages to set against a seemingly unstoppable foe. The pacing moves in waves—long, tense standoffs punctuated by quick bursts of violent motion—much like a well-designed boss fight in a campaign. If you enjoy comics that teach you something about human nature even as they show you a great sword doing its own interpretation of physics, Black Plague will be right up your alley.

## Tie-ins and cross-media synergy: why these books matter beyond the page
The two lines work not just as stand-alone comics but as a bridge between different media experiences. For fans of the original board games, the comics offer a way to visualize the factions, the weapons, and the recurring threats in new lights. They provide lore depth without requiring you to memorize a manual, and they give you a sense of the world’s texture that can enrich your board game nights. The interplay between Invader and Black Plague is not heavy-handed; it’s more like a friendly nudge that says, If you like one, you’ll probably enjoy the other, and you’ll enjoy them in a different mood on a different day.

Readers who enjoy other cross-media franchises—films adapted into novels, board games spun into video games—will recognize the appeal: the confidence to expand a world in ways that respect the core resource of the IP while giving fans new angles to enjoy. The comics allow designers and writers to experiment with tone, with paces, and with moral questions in a sandbox where there are more doors than swords to slam open.

## Final thoughts and who should read these
If your shelf is a little light on cross-genre undead content, or if you simply enjoy the frequent conjunction of clever character moments with big, gnarly threats, these two Zombicide lines are worth your time. Invader is the high-energy cousin that loves neon lighting and a chase through a corridor where every step makes you question your life choices. Black Plague is the patient predator, a slow burn that rewards careful reading and careful planning, with a moral edge that sticks with you after you close the issue.

Who should read both? Pretty much everyone who enjoys a good mix of action, humor, and story that respects the user’s time. If you own a gaming table and you want to bring a narrative complement to your campaigns, these books can enrich your table experience by giving you a shared vocabulary—ships, engines, corridors, knights, plague rites—that you can reference during your next session. If you are a collector, the art and cover variants will probably entice you to chase down a couple of limited editions. And if you are a casual reader who likes a brisk, entertaining zombie tale with heart, these books deliver an accessible entry point into a broader zombie-infused universe.

## Final recommendation
If you are looking for a robust, entertaining bridge between a tabletop universe and the world of sequential art, you will likely find both Invader and Black Plague to be worth your attention. Invader is the quick sprint through a high-tech battlefield with enough humor to keep the pace light; Black Plague is the careful, haunted march through a siege with a weight that lingers beyond the last page. Together they provide a well-rounded snapshot of what zombie IPs can do when they step into a different medium and still manage to keep their core essence intact.

And if you only have time to read one, pick Invader for the energy and the visual spectacle, then circle back to Black Plague for the emotional impact and the world-building that makes you want to return for more. Either way, you are in for a goofy yet gripping ride through a universe where humanity keeps showing up, one run-and-gun page at a time.
