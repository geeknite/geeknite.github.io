## Introduction
Gloom started as a clever little trick deck where you tell a story of a family and try to doom them to a grim fate before the other players doom their families even worse. The art is wry and the tone is bleak with a twinkle of black humor. Unhappy Homes adds new families, new story and condition cards, and new ways to stack misery on top of misery. This is not a game for sunshine lovers, nor for people who believe that a little joy should sneak into a card game now and then. If you enjoy storytelling with a dark carnival vibe, this box is your backstage pass.

In this review the tone stays faithful to Gloom wittiness while offering practical notes on setup, balance, and replay value. We will also compare Unhappy Homes to a couple of other Gloom expansions and talk about how to weave it into a full Gloom run without turning your living room into a psychological horror show you will remember for weeks.

## What is in the box and first impressions
Unhappy Homes is a boxed expansion that slots into base Gloom or into any already established Gloom campaign. Inside you will find new family cards that come with their own set of misfortunes, new story cards that intensify the gloom without making it feel repetitive, and a handful of new gloom condition or event cards that jazz up how you assign fate to characters. The card stock looks sturdy, the art remains in the same delightful insult to optimism style, and the new color accents help you quickly identify which card set belongs to which family. The box also feels a bit more evolved than the original white box; there is a sense that Atlas Games wanted to give you a premium unboxing moment without inflating the price unnecessarily.

To kick things off, you will want to take a moment to admire the new box art and the clever design choices that make it easier to raid the box for the exact card type you need during a frantic play session. There is a new set of reference cards that explain the subtle differences between stories and misfortunes in this expansion, which is welcome because the base Gloom catalog is already a mood board of gloom and chaos. The quality of the cards is solid, with clean print and minimal chipping risk for the average player who treats their gaming gear as a blend of art and ammunition for storytelling.

## Box contents and components quality
Unhappy Homes adds a few dozen cards, a small number of tokens that represent new conditions, and a couple of small accessories that make the game flow a touch smoother. The actual card count will vary depending on how you mix it with previous expansions, but you will definitely notice a more complex tapestry of misfortune. The card backs maintain the familiar Gloom silhouette, ensuring that even when you add new families, the deck remains visually cohesive. The newly introduced story cards tend to have longer flavor texts that encourage players to lean into the drama and improvise a short vignette about why the family experiences such calamities within the walls they occupy.

One delightful aspect is how the new families fit into the existing mechanic rather than forcing a new sub-system. You draw a new family card, pick a set of misfortunes, and start weaving a more tangled emotional web. The boxes are both compact and dense with ideas, which means you can grab the Unhappy Homes set and still maintain the light, fast tempo that makes Gloom appealing for a quick game night or an extended session when the housemates finally agree to skip the kitchen TV for a while.

### Card quality and handling
The card stock remains thin enough to shuffle with a flourish, but not so flimsy that you fear a crinkle on the first reuse. While this is not a deluxe heavy-duty affair, it holds up well to the usual house game night chaos. If you are a player who loves sleeving, you will be happy to know that these cards take sleeves without much fuss. The borders are clean, and the death total of the families is not a literal count of souls but a narrative tally that your group will remember for weeks.

### Artwork and thematic consistency
As with base Gloom, the Unhappy Homes box leans on a Victorian gothic aesthetic with a pinch of humor. The new illustrations lean more into domestic settings that turn quickly into suspenseful stages for the family tragedies. There is a recurring motif of creaking floorboards, long hallways, and a bouquet of old things that look like they witnessed more drama than a soap opera. The art aligns with the tone we have come to expect from Atlas Games and maintains the cheeky but morbid humor that fans adore. If you enjoyed the vibe of the core game, you will find the new art to be a natural extension rather than a jarring departure.

## Mechanics and how Unhappy Homes affects play
Gloom is a game about telling a story while ensuring that a narrative of woe unfolds for each family. The primary objective is to minimize your own family’s happiness and maximize your opponents’ misfortune. Unhappy Homes adds more levers to pull without making the entire system feel bloated. Here are the core mechanical changes or additions you should expect:

- New families with unique flavor and burdens. Each family brings its own set of misfortunes that can be combined with those you already know from base Gloom. Some misfortunes are more narrative and cosmetic, while others actively alter how you play certain cards or how you draw from the deck.
- Expanded story cards. The new stories offer longer micro narratives that inspire improvisation. They can tilt the balance of the round by offering goals that encourage hilarious or grim outcomes.
- Additional condition cards. These can apply to a family at various moments in the game to narrate turning points in their misery. They provide a nice mechanic for players who enjoy twisting the plot mid game.
- Minor rules clarifications. Unhappy Homes emphasizes faster setup and smoother transitions between stories. The rule tweaks are not revolutionary, but they do help keep a tense session under control so you spend less time arguing about whether a card interaction is legal and more time arguing about why your family deserves this fate.

In practice, the expansion balances well with base Gloom. It does not turn the game into a heavy euro or a puzzle box. It preserves the lightness of storytelling while providing more dramatic prompts that yield laughably tragic anecdotes. The pace is still quick, which is essential for a game that rewards quick improvisation more than long strategic planning.

## How to integrate with your existing Gloom setup
If you already own a few Gloom expansions, Unhappy Homes slots in relatively cleanly. You can either mix and match families with a standard deck, or you can build a small Alt-Gloom session that uses just the new families to create a focused night of misery. A couple of practical suggestions:

- Start small: introduce two new families and a handful of stories. If you jump in with everything at once, you may overwhelm new players with too many card types to memorize.
- Theme nights: let each session explore a sub-theme like haunted kitchens or cursed attics. This adds a cohesive feel to the stories and helps players improvise connections between misfortunes.
- Document the tales: since Gloom is about storytelling, consider a little notebook or a shared doc where players jot down the most ridiculous lines or the most dramatic outcomes. It preserves the mood for future sessions and creates a running joke you can reference later.

A note on the post game discussion: after the last card is drawn and the glowing firelight in the hall flickers, you will likely want to explain why the family could not possibly continue in that house. This is an essential part of the Gloom experience. Unhappy Homes amplifies those moments with new premises to explore, but it still relies on the players to fill the silence with theatre instead of explaining rules, which is exactly how this game should be played.

## Play styles and player count considerations
Gloom and its expansions shine when you have a group that enjoys narrative banter and a bit of theatrics. Unhappy Homes stands up to 2 to 4 players comfortably, though there are plenty of sessions where 2 players are enough to coax out the darkest details of a family tragedy. If you have a larger group, you can rotate families between rounds or run parallel tables with synchronized storytelling. The compact nature of the box makes it feasible to tote to a gaming cafe or a friend's place, which is a big win for people who travel with a small board game library.

Solo play is not the intended performance mode for Gloom, but it can be interesting as a writing exercise or a way to test out new family ideas before unleashing them on a human audience. If you do try solo, you will rely heavily on the storytelling prompts to keep the flow alive. Unhappy Homes gives you more prompts, which is a small mercy for solo players who crave character-driven drama without the social overhead of a party game night.

## Theme, humor, and moral tone
A recurring question with Gloom expansions is whether the humor remains tasteful or becomes too grim for casual play. Unhappy Homes stays on the light side of dark. The cards lean into misfortune with a wink rather than an axe to the head, and there is a playful mood that persists even as the misery stacks up. If you enjoy macabre humor that invites storytelling rather than outright horror, you will likely appreciate the tone. If you prefer subtlety or more brutal tragedy, you might crave a different flavor of gloom. The expansion lands in a sweet middle ground that lets you tell dramatic tales without turning the table into a therapy session.

## Artwork, flavor text, and accessibility
The flavor text on the new cards is approachable and often witty, which helps with accessibility for players who are new to Gloom. The art supports the mood without being too grim for casual players. The accessibility aspect is important because Gloom can be tricky for players who are not comfortable with improvisation. Unhappy Homes provides enough scaffolding in the form of descriptive prompts and recognizable archetypes that you can jump into a round even if you do not own every expansion imaginable. It is not a beginner game, but it is friendly enough to invite newcomers to try a session without requiring a specialized dictionary of rules.

## Card management, setup time, and play length
Setup is a tad longer than the base game since you are integrating additional families and cards. It should add about 5 to 10 minutes to a standard round, depending on how orderly your table is and how much time you spend poring over a single story card. If you are the sort of group that uses a timer for storytelling rounds, Unhappy Homes plays nicely with that approach because the new cards offer clear prompts that can be addressed quickly or expanded into a longer tale as the mood dictates.

A typical round still resolves in about 20 to 40 minutes, making this expansion ideal for a game night that features multiple games between long conversations about life choices and guilty pleasures. The replayability is solid because the combination of families and misfortunes creates a different story every time. Several players will relish the challenge of crafting a narrative arc where your family somehow survives while the others accrue spectacular calamities.

## Value and price consideration
When evaluating a Gloom expansion, price is a practical measure of perceived value. Unhappy Homes sits in the same neighborhood as other expansions in terms of price, but it delivers a noticeable increase in the storytelling options and the mood variety. If you already own Gloom and a couple of expansions, this box gives you enough new content to justify a purchase and still feels cohesive with the rest of your collection. If you are new to Gloom, you might consider starting with a smaller expansion or a bundle that includes several sets. The value increases as you accumulate more families and stories, since the core mechanic remains elegant and quick, and you will want to revisit your favorite combinations over and over.

## Tips for maximum enjoyment
- Embrace the misfortune. The point of Gloom is to tell a compelling story, not to win by brute force. Lean into the drama and let the table riff off the prompts.
- Keep a running list of the best zingers. Some lines become inside jokes that keep future sessions light even as the boxes fill with gloom.
- Rotate storytellers. Gloom benefits from varied narration; a different player taking the stage every round can keep the energy high and the punchlines fresh.
- Use a timer for intense rounds. If your table tends to slow down while finding a perfect phrase, a gentle 60 second limit helps maintain pace without killing creativity.
- Adapt to your table. If someone is new to roleplay or improv, you can scale back the number of misfortunes or stories to keep the session approachable.

## Comparisons with other Gloom expansions
Compared to other entries in the Gloom lineup, Unhappy Homes emphasizes domestic settings and personal tragedy without stepping into the grotesque or slapstick extremes found in some spin offs. It strikes a balance between the base game and the more elaborate expansions that add heavy mechanical scaffolding. If you enjoy a storytelling vibe with a strong sense of place, this expansion is a natural fit. For players who prefer more direct combat or competitive optimization, you may want to tread cautiously and keep in mind that the fun in Gloom comes from collaborative storytelling rather than winning by a long shot.

## Playtime expectations and long term value
If you are planning a long Gloom night, Unhappy Homes scales well. Use a couple of families for a light night, or pile on several more to create a sprawling narrative that can fill a couple of hours. The long term value is in the replayability and in the way new prompts encourage you to improvise. If you keep a few notes from each session, you can build a mini anthology of terrible houses and the ridiculous reasons they failed to thrive. In short, this is a box that plays well with the idea of a recurring storytelling club or a casual weekly game night with friends who enjoy dark humor.

## Accessibility thoughts
Unhappy Homes does a good job of explaining the gist of new mechanics without burying players in footnotes. The card text tends to be short enough to parse quickly, and the narrative prompts are straightforward enough to use as anchors for storytelling. The expansions are not designed to require a PhD in card interaction, which is a big plus for a game that thrives on social play and creative improvisation. If you have never played Gloom before, you will still be able to participate with a brief rundown and a willingness to contribute to the story rather than to master a rulebook on the first night.

## Final verdict and recommendation
Unhappy Homes adds just the right amount of new material to refresh Gloom without diluting its core concept. The families feel distinct, the stories offer satisfying prompts, and the new cards integrate smoothly with the existing deck. It is a recommended expansion for players who enjoy a narrative timeout in their gaming sessions, for players who love the darkly comic vibe of Gloom, and for groups who want a little more drama without stepping into heavy strategy territory. If you are already buying a lot of expansions for Gloom purely for card count or mechanical complexity, this one might not be your first choice. However, if you want a well crafted set of prompts that will likely yield memorable stories, Unhappy Homes is a strong contender that deserves a place on your shelf.

In the end, Gloom is about telling a story in a room full of friends, and Unhappy Homes gives you more dramatic paint to tell that story with. It is the kind of box that gets passed around the table with an exasperated grin, a sigh, and the agreement that yes, you will tell the next tale even if it costs you a character or two. The family silhouettes may look quaint when you lay them out, but the stories you craft are where the real treasure lies.
