## Sculpt quality and design language
In the world of Zombicide miniatures, sculpt quality matters more than it ever did in more cerebral war games. The Infected Pack-Figures doesn’t attempt to reinvent the wheel; instead, it refines the wheel with sharper details and more daring silhouettes. The skin textures range from chunky boils to sinewy tendons that look almost like they should squirm when you tilt the model toward a light source. The problem with some zombie lines is the risk of all the figures looking like generic, uniform crowds; the GUGKS03 pack avoids that fate with enough eccentricities to keep each model memorable.

The heads and facial features tend to be the standout areas. If you’ve ever painted a zombie with a distinctive facial scar, a broken jaw, or a mouth that looks permanently deranged due to the infection, you’ll know how much personality the sculpt can inject into a tabletop scene. The Infected Pack-Figures follow this philosophy: each sculpt has a focal point—an elongated claw, a toothy grin, or an eye that appears to be permanently dislodged—so painting them becomes less about disguising flaws and more about storytelling through color and shading.

Texture transitions are well executed. The transition from exposed muscle to diseased skin isn’t abrupt; there’s a subtle gradient that suggests growths and mutations without becoming cross-stitch-level grotesque. This balance matters because in a game where you want players to identify threats at a glance, a clear silhouette beats a nightmare masquerade any day of the week. The scale and proportion feel consistent with the base game’s infected zombies, which reduces the risk of subtraction from the narrative when you’re selecting targets during a hectic round.

For painters who want more technical notes: the skin textures respond well to dry brushing and two-tone basing. A mid-tone gray or sickly green base undercoat can help the raised details pop without overpowering the model, and you’ll find that grime washes settle nicely into the recesses to create that “you-didn’t-clean-this-room-in-a-while” look that good zombie figures deserve.


## Painting and hobby considerations
If you’re a painter who enjoys the process as much as the game, the Infected Pack-Figures presents some accessible entry points and some fun challenges. For beginners, you’ll find that the molds’ recessed areas are generous enough to keep you from losing detail on assembly, but there are enough fine lines around the mutated growths to require careful handling with a fine brush. The good news is that you can achieve strong results with common hobby staples: a black primer, a bold base coat, a couple of washes for the wounds, and a dry brush to highlight raised textures.

Pro tips for painting these figures:
- Start with a mid-tone base that complements your palette for the survivors so the infected pop in high-contrast scenes.
- Use a dark wash into the recesses to emphasize creases and to bring out the unnervingly organic textures.
- A last-pass dry brush in a lighter tone will help you capture raised areas like bone or malformed tissue without turning the model into a comic-strip zombie.
- For eyes and exposed teeth, a tiny bit of white with a dot of red or yellow can go a long way toward giving the models personality without making them look cartoonish.

If you’re short on time, consider a speed-paint approach: base coat, wash, dry brush, and quick highlight. You’ll get a respectable result that reads well on table during a heated session. If you’re feeling ambitious, you can layer on a subtle glowing effect with a glaze for “infected glow” around the mutated areas—this light-ups photos well when you’re posting battle reports or painting progress shots on social media.

For those who enjoy a bit of banter during the hobby process, we’ve found these figures photograph exceptionally well in dappled overhead lighting—less glare on glossy miniatures means more dramatic contrasts in your photos. Just be mindful of reflective surfaces when you’re trying to capture a crisp shot of the teeth without astronaut-level glare.
