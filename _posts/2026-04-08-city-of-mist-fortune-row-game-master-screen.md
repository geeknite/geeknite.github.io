## Introduction
In the neon-soaked alleys of City of Mist, where legends wear trench coats and children narrate crimes with streetlamp poetry, the GM is king. The Fortune Row Game Master Screen, blessed by some corporate desk jockey who probably knows too much about roll tables, promises to keep the players' secrets while offering the GM a tidy fortress of quick references. But does it actually help? Or is it just a pretty piece of cardboard with a fancy name and a price tag that makes your wallet cry in a noir monologue?

This is not just a product review; it's a field report from a table where heroes fold into shadows and the GM contorts into a living Wikipedia of rules and reminders. We'll break down the Fortune Row screen by composition, function, and flavor. We'll test how it handles the twisty, mask-wearing world of City of Mist, and whether it stands up to the long nights of city-building, clue-chasing, and coffee-fueled tension.

### Product overview and physicality
The Fortune Row GM Screen is a four-panel affair, roughly the size of your average hardcover dice bag, with a matte finish that feels good under the thumb and resists the inevitable fingerprints from a player who inherits the screen for a single moment of glory. The panels fold neatly, locking into a triptych of GM-friendly geometry. The cover art leans into the noir-sorcery vibe—pale cityscape silhouettes, glowing runes, and a silhouette that might be your own face if you’ve spent the last three sessions shouting at a streetlight.

Inside, the panels hold a mix of quick-reference tables, NPC prompts, and city flavor that tries to be helpful without spoiling the mystery matrix. The technical terms are all present: action economy reminders, entanglement cues, and the all-important “Reveal Phase” cheat sheet. The typography is legible, the icons are distinct, and the color palette is tasteful enough to avoid giving your players a headache—but also spicy enough to remind you that you’re GMing a mythic noir RPG.

### Design, layout, and usability
The layout is the heart of a GM screen: it needs to present relevant information at a glance, without forcing you to juggle four separate cheat sheets mid-scene. Fortune Row nails this by stacking content into four panels with logical groupings:

- Panel A: Scene Setup and Clues
- Panel B: Masks and Motives
- Panel C: Urban Details and City Lore
- Panel D: Quick NPC Prompts and Fate Hooks

There are also small, unobtrusive editorial flourishes—tiny arrows next to page references, a few icons that indicate “combat,” “investigation,” or “social scene.” It’s a design language that says: we care about your pace, not your aesthetic.

### Material quality and durability
The screen is paperboard rather than metal, which is fine by me because it’s easy to stash in a bag, and you can laminate or sleeve it if you want to pretend you own a tiny shard of the city’s spine. It should survive a few table flips and the occasional insistent gesture from a player who insists on the exact location of the next clue. If you’re planning weekly campaigns, you might consider a three-ring binder and a little clip to keep the panels aligned; the last thing you want is a misaligned page causing you to misread a critical clue.

### Content evaluation and accuracy
Now the heart of the matter: is the content useful? The Fortune Row screen promises to deliver at-a-glance references for the City of Mist system—things like Masks, Powers, Clues, and the mystery strands that form the backbone of your city’s legends. In practice, it’s a mixed bag. Some panels feel like a pro-level cheat sheet that would reduce the time you spend searching for a note and more time for dramatic flourishes. Other panels occasionally duplicate content you already keep on your tablet or a sticky note on your laptop, which is less a flaw and more a symptom of a system that rewards efficiency—but sometimes with redundancy.

### Aesthetics and vibe
City of Mist is all about mood: smoky detective vibes, mythic resonance, steam-powered neon. Fortune Row’s art direction leans into that with a mix of noir silhouettes and arcane glyphs. The editor’s choice of fonts and spacing communicates a calm seriousness, which is the exact energy you want when you’re trying to shepherd a crowd of players through a scene where the city itself might be bluffing. If you’re into immersive visuals, you’ll appreciate the stylistic coherence between the screen’s inner panels and your table’s lighting.

### GM screen in play: practical experiences
We tested Fortune Row across three sessions—two one-shots and one longer arc—featuring a rotating cast of PCs who embodied masks such as The Mobster, The Author, The Sculptor, and The Angel of the Neon Alley. The screen performed well in moments of high tension. During a chase sequence through a rain-soaked district, I found the quick reference for Scene Hooks and Clue Clusters extremely valuable; it allowed me to pivot from a chase to a confrontation in seconds, without flipping through a 400-page core rulebook. The NPC prompts came in handy when a PC asked for a counteroffer or a non-player character’s hidden agenda; they provided a spark without dictating the outcome.

On the other hand, the City Lore panels occasionally tempted me to narrate a long-winded street legend instead of handling the immediate needs of the scene. That’s not a complaint about the screen; it’s a reminder that you still bring your own GM style to the table. If you’re a meticulous world-builder who loves weaving in micro-details, Fortune Row will feel like a gentle nudge rather than a blueprint that boxes you in.

### Storytelling implications
Fortune Row’s design implicitly nudges GMs toward three storytelling considerations: pace, mystery texture, and player agency. The quick references reduce mechanical friction, enabling more time for the players to reveal their characters’ motives and to chase those mythic threads across the city. The “Fate Hooks” and “Plot Seeds” prompts are particularly nice because they give you resonant ideas that you can slot into your ongoing central arc or drop into a side mystery to give it some extra punch.

If you like a heavy toolbox, Fortune Row is that sharp steel box: it gives you a variety of tools, not just a single tool. If you prefer a minimalist approach, you may end up ignoring several panels and using only the core cheat sheet you already keep on your tablet. Either way, the screen doesn’t force you into a particular style; it accommodates a range of GM philosophies, which is a rare virtue in product design.

### How Fortune Row compares to other GM screens
If you’ve used City of Mist core rulebooks and supplement sheets, Fortune Row feels familiar in tone and function. It’s not trying to reinvent the wheel; it’s trying to provide a smoother wheel. Compared to standard, no-frills four-panel GM screens, Fortune Row adds a cohesive aesthetic, a few well-chosen prompts, and a more narrative-ready arrangement of content.

Compared to other games’ custom GM screens, Fortune Row sits in the middle: not the most ornate, not the most robust, but consistently reliable for a City of Mist session. It’s easy to recommend to players who want to focus on roleplay rather than rummaging through pages. For GMs who require deep, page-accurate rules references, you’ll still want your PDF or the relevant book on standby, but Fortune Row will keep your eyes on the players rather than on the table edge.

### What players are saying (and what you should listen for)
Now, you might wonder: what do players think? Usually, players notice three things when a GM screen enters the scene: timing, diction, and drama. If your screen helps you stop fumbling with dice, calls for checks, or clarifications, players will feel the tempo improve. If the screen introduces a lot of jargon that confuses players more than it helps the GM, you risk breaking immersion. Fortune Row strikes a balance: the prompts are accessible and the language is consistent with City of Mist’s mythic noir tone.

Visuals and tactile appeal matter if you play with a showy group or a table that's heavy on ambiance. The Fortune Row visuals align with the modern-noir aesthetic, with hints of mythic glyphs that are clearly print-ready for a table of 4-6 players. Aesthetics isn’t a trivial matter for a game where mood can be as actionable as combat; you’ll appreciate a screen that looks as good as it feels to interact with.

### Usage tips for GMs
Here are practical tips to maximize Fortune Row’s value:

- Use the Scene Setup panel to map out the first act and the turning point. Keep it succinct: one sentence per beat is plenty.
- Leverage the Masks and Motives panels to remind players of the thematic pressure their characters’ identities exert on their choices.
- Keep a small, separate notebook for any house rules or player-driven twists that you might want to fold into later acts; the screen’s content is strong, but your creative flow should be even stronger.
- When you are in a tight schedule (which is all sessions, really), rely on the Fate Hooks for a quick pivot if players derail your planned reveal. It’s the GM’s friend in moments of improvisational panic.
- If you’re running a city-wide mystery, flip the City Lore references into a micro-arc: one clue per hero, one location with a star-crossed past, and one NPC with a hidden agenda. Fortune Row gives you the scaffolding to build that kind of architecture.

### Maximizing durability in real-world play
If your table has a coffee habit and a habit of knocking things off the table, consider a small gaming mat as a base; the Fortune Row screen stands well on its own, but a mat can prevent the absolute murder of its spine. You might also consider acquiring a protective sleeve for each panel if you’re rough with your dice or if your group has a tendency to slide a nail across the panel to keep time. In short: treat Fortune Row like a beloved but occasionally disrespectful NPC who deserves a little padding.
