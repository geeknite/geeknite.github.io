## Gameplay Overview
Sushi Go! classic is a drafting game: you pass cards around, trying to gather the right combos for points. The Collectors Tin Edition sticks with that core mechanic but gave the rulebook a light, modern polish and adapted the card set for smoother play in a variety of group sizes.

### Setup
- Shuffle the deck and deal a hand to each player. The size of the hand scales with player count, which helps keep rounds tight and predictable.
- Place the discard piles within reach of all players. A good rule of thumb is to keep the scoring track visible and not hidden behind a stack of cardboard boats.
- Pick a starting player. The first round may be enough to remind your group about how to breathe quietly while contemplating whether to take a nigiri or a maki roll. The answer is usually both, if you’re playing optimally.

### Turn Structure
On your turn, you select one card from your hand to keep and pass the rest to the next player. This continues until all hands are exhausted and each player has a set of cards that creates a tasty, if competitive, assortment of sushi feelings. The game moves quickly; most rounds finish in around 15 to 20 minutes, which keeps hands from cramping and temperatures from rising too high while you debate the superiority of eel over salmon nigiri.

### Card Types and Scoring
- Nigiri: Simple power. Each type yields points based on its value; you want more of these if you’re chasing a clean, high score.
- Maki: Points are awarded for the most complete rows, providing a little chase dynamic that rewards attention to the board state.
- Tempura: A payoff for collecting two cards of the same type. It’s about balance, not speed, which makes it a nice counterpoint to the faster cards.
- Sashimi: Typically rewards with a set of three for maximum points, though you may encounter variant rules that require two or more depending on your house rules.
- Pudding: A late-game scorer that can swing final tallies in surprising ways if you hold onto it until the end.
- Special cards: Depending on the edition, you may see a chopsticks card or other variants that alter how you draft and score, adding a small edge for careful planning.

The scoring in Sushi Go is a small chess game of choices. It rewards memory and prediction: did your neighbor take the most valuable Nigiri last round? Are you confident you can outpace the Maki race this round? The Collectors Tin edition doesn’t radically change the math, but the updated art and tactile components do make the decision process feel more tactile, which many players enjoy as a sensory reward for crisp decision-making.

## How to Play: Step-by-Step Quick Start
1) Unpack the tin, shuffle the deck, and deal a hand to each player. The number of cards per hand depends on the player count. 2) The first player picks one card from their hand and places it face-up on their personal plate. The rest of the hand is passed to the next player. 3) Repeat until all cards have been drafted. 4) Players reveal their plates and score their sushi sets based on the contained combinations. 5) If there are pudding, chomp down on pudding points at the end of the game to swing tallies. 6) Rotate to the next round, starting again with a fresh hand. 7) After a predetermined number of rounds, tally final scores and declare the winner. 
The quick-start feel is intentional. The game is designed to be explained in a couple of minutes and to deliver a satisfying, fast-paced session that doesn’t demand a full afternoon of rules-lawyering. The Collector’s Tin makes the process feel more premium, which adds to the sense of occasion without slowing things down.
