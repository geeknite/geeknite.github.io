# UNO Attack Electronic Card Game (2017) Review

If you ever wanted to test the limits of a living room with a machine gun of cards, the 2017 Mattel UNO Attack edition is your dream. This isn’t your grandma’s quiet game of matching colors; this is a high-energy, unpredictable tournament where the central mechanism is a card shooter that spits out surprises faster than your cat can knock over a lamp. Yes, the box says “No Box” in your product listing, and your inner monologue responds with: Great, a minimalist experience delivered by a machine that doesn’t even care about your sense of order. In this review, we’ll dive into what makes UNO Attack tick, what might annoy your relatives during a Christmas Eve marathon, and whether it deserves a place in your game-night rotation. Buckle up: your living room is about to become a carnival of colors, chaos, and possibly small-scale thunder claps when the shooter misfires.

## Overview: What is UNO Attack (2017) all about?

UNO Attack is a modern reimagining of the classic card game UNO, dressed up in a flashy electronic chassis that can randomly deploy a fresh batch of cards. The shooter is the star: at various moments, the device forces players to grab new cards from a hopper or to deal with unpredictable bursts, all while trying to be the first to empty their hand. The 2017 edition keeps the family-friendly premise, but adds a distinctive device that makes each round feel like a micro-game within a game. If you’ve played traditional UNO, you know the core mechanics: match colors or numbers, play action cards like Skip, Reverse, Draw Two, and Wild, and keep a keen eye on the ever-ticking tension clock. UNO Attack escalates that tension by injecting a mechanical chaos engine into the mix.

## Components: what’s in the box (or what’s left when the box vanishes)

In a perfect world, we’d have a pristine, sealed box with all the bits accounted for. In reality, a lot of households end up with “No Box” or a box that’s seen better days. The 2017 edition includes the following core components:

- A compact card shooter device with a hopper that shoots random cards into the middle of the table. The device hums, buzzes, and occasionally sounds like a small arcade machine that has decided to dislike you personally.
- A deck of UNO Attack cards featuring standard UNO colors, plus special Attack cards that interact with the shooter. The card design remains classic UNO fare—bright primary colors that look excellent on a coffee table or a kid’s birthday party table.
- A set of protective mechanisms for safety and sanity: a little tray, a handful of “Card Penalties” cards, and a few replacement instruction sheets if you’ve misplaced your manual under the couch since 2017. Some folks report that they never found all the pieces after a house move; if you’re missing something, you’re not alone.

The build quality is a mixed bag. The shooter device is sturdy enough to survive the occasional fan-accelerated spin in a living room, but it’s not immune to sticky fingers, curious pets, or the occasional accidental shoulder-nudge during heated gameplay. The cards themselves feel sturdy, with glossy finishes that resist minor spills and the occasional crayon scribble from a kid who insisted on coloring the back of every card for “artistic purposes.” If you’re a neat freak about organization, you’ll probably want to snap a few extra card sleeves or keep a dedicated storage bin. If you’re a chaotic neutral gamer who thrives in mess, you’ll just call it ambiance.

## Setup: how to get this beast ready for chaos

The setup is straightforward but not exactly lightning-fast, especially if you’re balancing a handful of kids or a small crowd with varying levels of impatience. Here’s a crisp, gamer-friendly breakdown:

1) Unfold the table space and place the UNO Attack device in the center. If you’ve lost the tray or the base, improvise with a sturdy surface and a snack plate—just make sure nothing falls into the launcher. 
2) Shuffle the UNO Attack deck and deal the starting hand to each player (usually seven cards, same as traditional UNO). If you’re playing with a larger group, you may want to reduce the starting hand to maintain balance and avoid the device overshadowing the human players.
3) Place the draw pile next to the device, and keep a face-down discard pile within easy reach. Keep a calm face on you—this is not a game that rewards gritted teeth and tense posture.
4) Power on the shooter. Some models require a small switch; others are battery-driven. The device will begin with a whir or a soft chime to announce that it’s ready to devour your fate, one card at a time. 
5) Decide on house rules. Some groups allow the shooter to run a “round of chaos” after a wild, while others insist on normal UNO rules with a single fierce twist: the shooter might go off and drop a couple of cards in the middle, reshaping the field entirely.

Setup time is a bit longer than standard UNO, but the payoff is a dramatic, unpredictable flow that can genuinely shake up slower rounds. The moment the device first spits a handful of cards into the middle, players realize you’re not just playing a game—you’re participating in a spectacle of accidental theater.

## How to play: turning chaos into strategy

UNO Attack follows the same basic match-the-color-or-number rule as classic UNO, but with a schedule of random injections that keeps everyone slightly terrified of their draw pile. Here’s the core flow:

- On your turn, you can play a card that matches either color or number on the top of the discard pile. You can also play Wild or Wild Draw Four in the standard UNO sense, if you have those in hand, to help navigate the chaos.
- The unexpected: when the shooter triggers, it spits out a variable number of cards into the center. This can be as little as one and as many as a whole handful, depending on the device’s mood and the game’s luck. The cards that land in the center can influence the next player’s options, often forcing you to rethink your approach mid-round.
- If you can’t legally play a card, you must draw, either from the discard pile or from the shooter’s output, depending on the rules your group has set. The device rarely accounts for the human brain’s need for a quick, clean move, so expect a moment of awkward math as you calculate whether a wild card is worth beating the device’s random onslaught.
- When a player has one card left, they must shout “UNO!” to avoid penalty. If another player catches you failing to say UNO before the next player completes their turn, you’ll likely be punished by a sudden mini-chaos moment from the shooter—the game has a mischievous sense of humor about human error.

The added layer of strategy comes from anticipating the shooter’s potential impact and combining that with standard UNO tactics. Smart players use color control and card timing to force the device into a favorable (or at least tolerable) sequence, while less-experienced players may simply brace for impact and hope for a good run of color choices. The result is often a mix of cunning planning and popcorn-worthy luck, with players leaning into the chaos rather than trying to suppress it.

### The shooter as a tactical variable

The card shooter isn’t just a gimmick; it’s a variable in your strategic calculus. The unpredictable outputs force you to adapt on the fly, which can be refreshing in a board-game landscape that sometimes feels measured to death. Some players become adept at reading patterns in the device’s behavior (spoiler: there aren’t reliable patterns; this is the realm of chaos theory in a box). The shooter’s energy adds a sensory dimension to play—the hum, the occasional click, and the dramatic flurry of cards that can turn a quiet moment into a mini-ceremony of card-doling despair. Those who enjoy high-energy, fast-paced social games will likely adore this aspect of UNO Attack. Those who crave measured planning and slow, methodical turns may find themselves adjusting to an experience that occasionally feels more carnival than chess.

## Design and components: form meets function (mostly)

UNO Attack’s design leans into bright, kid-friendly aesthetics that align with the long-running UNO brand. The device’s silhouette is boxy, cheerful, and unapologetically loud in color—the kind of device that looks like something a cartoon wizard might conjure in a garage. The cards themselves maintain the classic UNO feel: bold colors, clear symbols, and readable numbers. The overall look communicates “fun and chaos” without apology.

One key design trade-off is tactile familiarity versus electronic flair. If you love the tactile joy of sliding a card from your hand to the discard pile, you’ll still get that satisfying feel, but with an added kinetic twist when the shooter launches. If you’re sensitive to gadget noise, you’ll want to play with a timer-limited session or in a room where background noise can absorb the soundscape. The 2017 edition doesn’t pretend to be a quiet game night; it proudly wears the sound badge like a badge of honor.

Reliability wise, the shooter’s performance is surprisingly consistent over several sessions, provided the hardware is in decent shape. Batteries matter; the device can sip power like a tiny dragon, so keep spare batteries handy if you’re planning a weekend-long family tournament. The cards survive messy hands, juice spills, and the occasional crumb from a snack—though you’ll certainly want to wipe the faces after a long session to keep the colors crisp and legible for the kids. If you’re buying this for a child’s first full game-night experience, you’ll want to supervise the gadget aspect to ensure the shooter doesn’t become a distraction instead of a gameplay engine.

## Family experience: chaos, camaraderie, and a few shrieks

A major value proposition of UNO Attack is its ability to generate shared moments. When the shooter explodes with a cascade of cards, the table erupts into a chorus of laughter, groans, and playful protests. It’s in these moments that UNO Attack earns its keep: it transforms a routine turn into a group event. People who would normally shy away from a more intense game night find themselves leaning in, shouting code phrases such as “UNO!” and “Draw four!” with genuine enthusiasm. For families, that’s gold.

That said, the chaos can overshadow learning. Younger players might struggle to track the ever-shifting state of the card piles, and some players may become frustrated if they’re repeatedly unlucky with the shooter’s outputs. The social energy is very high, which makes it excellent for larger gatherings or kids’ parties but potentially exhausting for players who prefer a calmer pace. If your crew includes a mix of very competitive types and casual players, UNO Attack tends to tilt toward the energetic side of the scale, so plan your game length accordingly.

### Interaction dynamics and etiquette

Because the game invites surprise, etiquette becomes a bit of a living document during play. You’ll get more playful banter, a few friendly taunts about who’s going to “get nuked” by the shooter next, and a quick pivot to a cooperative vibe when the deck becomes a little too chaotic. Some players adopt their own house rules about penalties and the exact timing for shouting UNO to keep the mood light. The key is to maintain a sense of humor: if you’re going to lose a round, lose with style, preferably while everyone else is laughing with you rather than at you.

## Replayability and value: does it wear well over time?

Replayability for UNO Attack hinges on two factors: the sheer variety introduced by the shooter and the social dynamics of your group. Every session typically plays out differently because the weaponized randomness of the card dispenser creates new combinations of cards and scenarios. This means you can return to the game night after month-long gaps and still find the experience fresh, especially if you rotate players or try out different house rules.

The downside is that the game can get repetitive if you’re playing with a small circle of people who know each other’s tactics very well. In such scenarios, you may feel you’ve seen most shooter outcomes and the novelty wears thin a bit quicker than you’d expect. To counter this, you can mix UNO Attack with other party games to keep energy high without burning out the concept. A well-chosen rotation of titles keeps the “Attack” part of the evening as the wild card rather than the centerpiece for every session.

## Value, setup time, and who should buy this edition

Value depends on your circumstances. If your family or group loves chaotic, fast-paced card play, UNO Attack (2017) is a superb pick that can stretch a two-hour session into a festival of chaos. If you’re seeking a calm, strategic, soccer-mat-of-games vibe, this might not be your jam. The upfront time to set up plus the occasional need for battery changes is a small price to pay for the kind of laughter and adrenaline this game can trigger in a crowded room.

For kids aged 7 and up, UNO Attack serves as a fantastic introduction to strategic thinking under pressure, color recognition, and social negotiation (who takes the loss gracefully, who negotiates a second chance, etc.). For adults, it can function as a nostalgic throwback that channels your inner kid without requiring you to dust off your original UNO rules. The 2017 version remains accessible enough that families can introduce newcomers without a lengthy tutorial—a big win when you’re teaching cousins you only see on holidays.

## Design notes: accessibility, balance, and modernization

UNO Attack’s design is intentionally accessible: the rules are straightforward enough that most players can grasp the core concept within the first couple of rounds. The device adds a modern twist without introducing overly complicated rules. The consequence is a game that can be taught quickly and enjoyed by players with varying degrees of board-game experience. The balance between chaos and control is delicate: too much random injection can derail even experienced players, but when tuned to a comfortable rhythm, it creates a dynamic that invites both bold moves and clever risk assessment.

If you’re collecting UNO variants, the 2017 Attack edition should be in the conversation. It’s distinctly different from the original UNO, yet it remains recognizably UNO in its color-coded language and core objective. You’ll likely want to keep a standard UNO deck on hand as a backup, especially for those who crave a slower tempo or a quieter final stretch when players are sitting on a dwindling number of cards.
