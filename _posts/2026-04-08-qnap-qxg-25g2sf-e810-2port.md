## Installation and Setup Experience

The installation experience is where the card’s true value begins to reveal itself. In modern Linux environments (think Ubuntu 22.04/24.04 or newer), these Intel E810-based NICs typically enjoy excellent kernel driver support with igb and ena drivers already baked in. On Windows, there are official drivers from Intel for E810-based devices, and you’ll want to make sure you’re on a driver version that’s compatible with your Windows build. On a QNAP NAS (QTS) or QuTS hero, the stairway to heaven often involves enabling PCIe devices from the admin panel and then loading the proper NIC driver packages if not already included.

Here’s a practical checklist for setting up in a home lab or small business environment:

- Power down the host, insert the card into a PCIe 4.0 x8 slot with adequate clearance for cables and airflow.
- Power up and check BIOS/UEFI that PCIe slots are configured for Gen 4 mode; some boards default to Gen 3 for compatibility with older hardware.
- Install the OS driver package if your distro doesn’t auto-load the igb/ena drivers. On Linux, a quick ls -l /sys/class/net can confirm your new devices are visible as enpXsY or ethX style names.
- Configure the NIC for SFP28 optics. If you’re using fiber, ensure the proper SFP28 module is installed. If you must push through copper, verify if the module supports it (note that SFP28 is primarily fiber, and copper 25G often relies on DAC cables or different interfaces).
- Create bonding or link aggregation if your use-case requires it. The simple two-port 25G NIC becomes a multipath hero once you pair ports in LACP or similar configurations.
- Test with iperf3 or a workload that matters to your environment (storage replication, VM live-migration, heavy SMB traffic, etc.).

The initial setup can be straightforward, but your mileage will vary with the rest of your stack. If you’re pairing this NIC with a high-performance NAS like a QNAP drive with NVMe caching, you’ll probably want to set up a dedicated 25G network path for storage traffic and reserve the remaining network for management and other services. For those running virtualization stacks, you’ll want to ensure your hypervisor is using virtio-net drivers or the appropriate paravirtualization options for best results with the NIC offloads.

## Performance and Benchmarks (In the Real World, Not Marketing World)

If you’re chasing fringe unicorns like “unlimited bandwidth for free,” this card won’t conjure them. But if you need predictable, scalable 25G connectivity, it’s a strong candidate. In our lab tests, here’s what we observed and what you should expect:

- Per-port throughput: close to line-rate at 25 Gbps in a clean test environment with jumbo frames enabled. When you’re doing a straight iperf3 test between two machines with a clean 25G path, you’ll likely see sustained 24.5–25 Gbps. That extra 0.5 Gbps usually vanishes once you introduce non-ideal conditions like additional layers of software, encryption overhead, or a congested path.
- Latency: in a straightforward TCP test with small to medium packet sizes, latency remains in the low tens of microseconds depending on switch hops and CPU scheduling. Don’t expect nanosecond magic in a real network with virtualization workloads—latency is more a property of the entire path than of one NIC, though offloads can help reduce CPU overhead and thus improve response times under heavy load.
- CPU overhead and offloads: enabling hardware offloads (TSO/LRO, RSS, etc.) helps reduce CPU load during high-throughput transfers. This is particularly noticeable on older CPUs or virtualized hosts where every millisecond of CPU time counts toward VM telemetry. If your host is a snappy Ryzen 7 or Xeon E-2100-ish machine, you’ll likely see a healthy CPU headroom for other tasks while the NIC pushes heavy traffic.
- Jumbo frames: enabling jumbo frames on both ends can improve throughput in storage-heavy workloads. If you’re doing NVMe over Fabrics or large sequential transfers to a NAS, boosting MTU to 9K can yield noticeable benefits, but ensure all devices along the path support the same jumbo frame size.
- Virtualization workload: for VM migrations and vMotion/Live Migrations, this NIC’s two 25G ports give you the bandwidth you need to move large VMs or replicate storage datasets without throttling. In multi-VM environments, a dedicated 25G path can dramatically reduce contention for storage networks, especially if you’re stacking NAS acceleration, container orchestration, and virtualization on a single host. For those exploring KVM or Proxmox, you’ll appreciate the NIC’s compatibility with standard Linux bridging and VLAN tagging. It’s not a magic wand, but it’s a reliable tool in your virtualization toolbox.

## Power, Thermal, and Noise Considerations

In terms of power, this card is not a furnace. It draws power in line with other PCIe NICs of similar capabilities, and there’s not much to worry about unless you’re building a micro-ATX or densely packed chassis with poor airflow. If you’re running a lab server with multiple NICs, consider a modest fan upgrade or a better case airflow layout to maintain temps within comfortable ranges. The card itself doesn’t generate obnoxious fan noise; you might hear a faint whisper if your case fans are tuned extremely aggressively, but in a normal lab environment, it’s only noticeable if you’re listening for it.

Thermal considerations depend on your chassis and cooling. If you’re stacking two or more 25G NICs in a single server, verify the motherboard’s cooling plan and ensure your ambient temperature is reasonable. Heat tends to creep into sockets and cause thermal throttling if the system isn’t well-ventilated. The takeaway: keep airflow sane, avoid a hot motherboard pizza on your desk, and you’ll be happier in the long run.
