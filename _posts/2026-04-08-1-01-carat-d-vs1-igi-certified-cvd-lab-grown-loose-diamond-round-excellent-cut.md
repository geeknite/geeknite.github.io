### The lab growth story: CVD vs HPHT
CVD stands for chemical vapor deposition, a process that grows diamonds from carbon-rich gases layer by layer. The CVD method starts with a tiny seed crystal inside a chamber that is heated and filled with hydrogen and methane or other hydrocarbon gases. The carbon atoms rain down, settle on the seed, and build up a crystal lattice that grows into a diamond over time. The exact conditions, including temperature, pressure, and gas mixture, are manipulated by scientists to coax the crystal into the desired size and quality.

HPHT, or high pressure high temperature, is an alternative growth method historically used to create colorless diamonds from scratch. It mimics natural geological processes by applying intense heat and pressure to graphite, turning it into a diamond. HPHT is excellent for improving color and clarity in some cases, but for our 1.01 ct D VS1 example, CVD offers the advantage of consistent traceability and the ability to tailor diamond properties with precision. The bottom line: both methods can yield stunning stones; CVD is the modern workhorse for lab grown diamonds.

From a buyer’s perspective, the growth method matters mostly in three places: availability, price stability, and the certificate’s confidence. A well-documented CVD grown diamond with IGI certification is usually a dependable pick for practical jewelry that wants to look incredible for decades.

## The care and feeding of a lab grown diamond
Caring for a lab grown diamond is straightforward but not identical to caring for a mined stone in all cases. Here are practical tips to keep your 1.01 ct D VS1 gleaming for years:

- Cleaning routine: Soapy water with a mild dish soap does the trick. Gently brush away debris with a soft toothbrush, rinse well, and pat dry with a lint-free cloth. Avoid harsh cleaners that can degrade the metal mounting or the certificate label.
- Avoiding wear and tear: While diamonds are durable, the setting can be susceptible to loose prongs or chips if exposed to heavy impact. Regular checkups with a jeweler can prevent disaster during daily wear.
- Ultrasonic cleaners: Some lab grown stones tolerate ultrasonic cleaning, but not all rings or settings do. If you own a setting with delicate metal or a composite mount, ask your jeweler before blasting your stone with ultrasonic energy.
- Fluorescence: If your particular diamond exhibits fluorescence, the way it appears under ultraviolet light can vary. In many cases, fluorescence is not a major factor in real-world wear, but it is good to know if you plan to attend UV-lit events or display cases.
