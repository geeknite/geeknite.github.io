## What is Fortune Row and why does an art book exist for City of Mist

City of Mist is a tabletop RPG about superheroes hiding in plain sight in a modern city, with a noir vibe and a system that rewards narratives with a shrug and a smirk. Fortune Row is a setting-inspired ensemble of characters, factions, and prop-worthy visuals that capture the mood of a city where every alley has a backstory and every backstory has a backstory. The Deluxe Edition art book is not just a showcase; it is a design manual for GMs and players who want to craft scenes that feel cinematic while still letting engine numbers keep your group honest.

The deluxe edition contains more pages, more color swatches, more concept art, and more sneaky little bits like card art and layout experiments that would have looked cool in a 2012 iPad game, but somehow still looks fresh in 2026. The page count is a delightfully dense mountain you want to climb not to reach the top, but to enjoy every foothold of art and marginalia along the way.

> If you love City of Mist and you collect art books, Fortune Row is the one you keep on the coffee table to prompt conversations about your next city-wide caper rather than a shelf to throw alongside your 5e monster manuals. It is the art book you show your friend who claims they just draw OC demons for fun and then you realize you both have a love for typography and crime-noir mood boards.

## Design and Production: Quality that Feels like a Heist

The Deluxe Edition is a tactile experience. The cover is heavy stock with a soft-touch finish that makes you think the book is willingly caressing your thumbs. The spine lays flat enough to do a deliberate open to a favorite spread, and the endpapers feel like someone sanded the inside of a piano to reveal its soul. It is a design-nerd dream and a GM’s practical toolset all in one.

### Paper, inks, and layout
The artistry is the star here, and the paper stock honors the artwork. Rich blacks, golden highlights, and a printing quality that doesn’t blur the stakes of a character’s mythic identity on the page. The art style leans into painterly, almost cinematic concept work rather than a glossy, sterile illustration. It feels like you are peering into a curated dream of a Fortune Row street—textures, rain-drenched reflections, neon liquor signs, and the subtle fingerprints of the artists who dreamt them into existence.

The layout surprises with daring spreads, splash pages, and blocks of text that never feel out of place. The typography smiles in the margins with small notes that feel like coffee-stained postcards from the designers. The balance between image and text is deft; you can study a single piece for hours and still extract new detail on a second look. This is not a lazy ‘put a picture here’ art book. This is a book designed to be opened and explored, page by page, as if you were walking down Fortune Row yourself.

### How the art book treats myth and city
City of Mist thrives on archetypes—the Fighter with a mask, the Rogue with a rumor, the Sage with a streetwise prophecy. Fortune Row manages to present these archetypes through art that is both literal and metaphorical. You can see a character transform from a smoky silhouette into a fully realized portrait, and the story of that transformation is etched into the composition. The artists do not merely illustrate; they choreograph a mood. The book invites you to imagine what these characters do when the city sleeps, what their badges are, what their glints of power look like, and what stories are told about them in a tavern whose logo is a flickering neon halo.

## Content inside Fortune Row Deluxe Edition

The Deluxe Edition covers new content that expands the universe’s aesthetics and its practical use in gameplay. The sections are organized to support both narrative and mechanical considerations. ### Character art and identity pieces
One of the core strengths of Fortune Row is how it presents characters through recurring motifs. The art often uses color-coded silhouettes to imply a power set or a mythic identity before you read the stat block, which is a clever trick for players to instantly identify a character archetype at the table. It’s the kind of design flourish that makes you want to print out posters for your own walls, then realize you will never take them down because the illusions of the city would crumble if you did. The Deluxe Edition pushes this further by including alternate costume studies and expression sheets—perfect for players who want to customize their NPCs without losing the mood of Fortune Row.

### Locations and micro-settings
Fortune Row also offers a treasure trove of prestige locations—speakeasies that are operated by the moon, ghastly museums that don’t trust the daylight, and rooftops with wind chimes of possible futures. The art book’s locations section is not a mere list; it’s a guided sensory tour. The city’s textures come alive in the pages: rain that glistens on noir pavements, signage that blinks with an unreliable memory, and interiors that feel soaked with secrets. The designers provide a few “how to adapt for your game” notes that are extremely helpful for GMs who want to preserve the art’s mood while adjusting it for their own party’s level and tone.

### The role of color and composition in storytelling
Color is a language in Fortune Row. The palette ranges from the golds and ambers of dawn-lit streets to the cold blues and purples of midnight alleys. The compositions are sometimes cinematic, with panels that feel like dramatic freezes from a film: a silhouette emerging from rain, a figure framed by a neon arch, or a table in the corner where a deal is made and a fate is sealed. The effect on play is real: when you show a player a piece of art that suggests a scenario, your brain conjures a narrative faster than a GM can describe it. In practice, this means you can hand a player a single page and watch them spin a campaign around it with minimal prompting.

## Practical use for players and GMs

The art book does not just sit on a shelf and judge you for not finishing your last campaign. It provides practical ways to integrate Fortune Row into sessions, whether you run a full campaign or a one-shot at a convention table. I tried a few quick-start ideas and found that the art pieces function as anchors for scenes. The mural becomes a clue, the clue becomes a memory, and the memory becomes a session beat. The art book’s organization helps you reconstruct scenes with a few notes and the right illustration exactly when you need them.

### How to use the art book at the table
- Pre-session mood boards: Place select pages on the GM screen or on a shared display. The mood becomes the session’s compass.
- NPC dossiers: Use character art as the basis for quick reference sheets. The face becomes a memory hook for players when they talk to the NPC.
- Location cues: Show a page that captures the feel of a location and let players improvise around the visuals.
- Prop ideas: Fortunes and talismans shown in the art provide tangible props players can physically interact with at the table.

In short, Fortune Row Deluxe Edition is not just a pretty set of images. It’s a practical toolkit for storytelling that respects the aesthetics of City of Mist while giving you concrete implements to evoke mood, not merely to describe it.

## How it compares to the standard edition and other art books

If you already own the standard Fortune Row art book, the Deluxe Edition is a no-brainer on several counts. It provides a more robust collection of concept art, improved print quality, and additional pages that deepen the world-building without straining your resources as a GM. The deluxe package tends to be pricier, obviously, so it’s worth evaluating how often you engage with the art beyond casual flipping. If you run weekly campaigns or participate in long-form campaigns with a heavy emphasis on atmosphere, the Deluxe Edition will likely become an indispensable reference. If your gaming time is sparse or you operate in a rules-light environment where you lean more on narrative than on aesthetic prompts, the standard edition may be enough to scratch that itch.

Comparison to other art books for RPGs like the Power of Perception or Ink and Iron collections shows that Fortune Row hits a sweet spot: it respects the game’s noir heart while giving you art that is both narrative and functional for table use. The mass-market RPG art books sometimes lean too hard on glossy visuals with little narrative payoff; Fortune Row manages to balance spectacle with utility in a way that is teachable, usable, and just plain fun to flip through.

## Reader tips: getting the most from your Fortune Row Deluxe Edition

- Pair art with quick-play prompts: Use a handful of spreads to craft a 2- or 3-scene micro-campaign where players chase clues across Fortune Row. The visuals provide the plot hooks; your narration fills the gaps with character and conflict.
- Create a mood board session: Print out select pages and let players contribute ideas about the city’s mood, which NPCs feel more dangerous, and how a particular location might tie into a player’s backstory.
- Use the color language: Leverage color palettes as a shorthand for faction identity, to speed up player recognition and maintain narrative clarity when you drop a scene into play.
- Build a salon of rumors: A tiny page with a cluster of micro-art motifs can be turned into a rumor deck. Players will chase a thread that seems innocuous in the art but reveals its darker underbelly on the table.
- Don’t fear ambiguity: Some images are intentionally enigmatic. Let players fill in the gaps with narrative inference; this often leads to the most memorable moments of a campaign.
