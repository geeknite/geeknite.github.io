## Dos — Quick Rules and Why It Works Here
### What is Dos?
Dos is a cooperative game where players try to get rid of their personal piles by playing cards in matching numbers with the central layout. The twist: two piles are in the center and there are rules to merge colors and numbers to keep the flow going.

### How to Play the Dos Rulebook in 60 Seconds
- Everyone gets a set number of cards facedown, with the top of each pile turned face up.
- On your turn, you can play a card to one of the centers piles if it matches either the number or color of a face-up card.
- You can also draw cards if you need to. The goal is to empty your personal piles before everyone else returns the favor to the center.

### Dos Strategy Hacks
- Prioritize color pairing: you want to create multiple centers you can work with on your turn.
- Communicate with nonverbal cues: finger taps and eyebrow raises count as “informal signals.”
- Leave a small buffer: avoid zeroing out too early, you want to maintain a few safety cards in hand.

## Skip-Bo — Quick Rules and Why It Plays Like a Duel
### What is Skip-Bo?
Skip-Bo is a race. The deck is a mix of stock cards and wild skip-bo wilds; the goal is to be first to play all your stock cards in sequential order.

### How to Play Skip-Bo in 3 Steps
- Build piles in order, starting from 1 up to 12 or 99 depending on house rules.
- Use Skip-Bo wilds to fill gaps and maintain flow.
- Use discard piles strategically; you’ll love watching your friends fumble through their cards like TGIF at a coffee shop.

### Skip-Bo Strategy Essentials
- The power of tempo: always be mindful of what you can play next turn.
- The bluff of face-down cards: pretending you have a good move is a common tactic to pressure others.

You’ll feel that calm, "we’re all in this together" mood in Dos, and then you’ll be slapped by the adrenaline of Skip-Bo, where timing and memory are the real superpowers.

If you’re new to both games, you’ll have a learning curve but not a steep one. Dos is approachable in one or two rounds; Skip-Bo might require a couple more rounds to optimize your discard strategy, but both games are forgiving enough that a beginner can still enjoy themselves without feeling like they’ve walked into a calculus lecture.

We attempted a quick two-game sprint: one round of Dos to build trust, then a high-energy Skip-Bo race to the finish. The results were chaotic, but nothing broke (despite a dramatic squeal from one participant when a wild draw changed the table’s mood). This is the essence of the combo pack: a cheerful, sometimes chaotic, always entertaining evening.
