## The Cards: Texture, Design, and That Distinctive 1960s Print

### Card Stock and Finish: A World Before Ultra-Cheap Poly Coatings
Skip-Bo cards, in their modern incarnations, are slick and durable. ## Care and Preservation: Keeping the Dream Alive
Here are some practical tips for preserving a vintage Skip-Bo box without killing the romance:
- Keep it out of direct sunlight. UV light loves to ruin color and velvet with the same enthusiasm a dragon has for gold.
- Control humidity. The goal is a stable environment—roughly 40-60% relative humidity—to avoid cardboard warping and velvet compression.
- Avoid handling the contents with greasy fingers. Use cotton gloves if you’re serious about preservation; if not, wash hands and dry them fully.
- Store the set upright in a display-friendly box or cabinet that supports the lid and prevents pressure on the velvet. A little padding helps keep the corners pristine.
- Periodically inspect for signs of aging, and document changes for your own personal archive. It’s fun to track a relic’s “age arc” over time, like a tiny chronicle written in cardboard and ink.

## Authenticity Tests: How to Separate Real from Thematic Reproductions
- Compare the card stock and ink to known production references. A genuine 1967 box will show a certain aging of the stock and a printing style consistent with mid-60s methods.
- Examine the seal: if the tape or adhesive looks suspiciously modern, you may have a reproduction or a later repackage.
- Inspect the interior for matching graphics and typography. Inconsistencies are the red flags of scomers and the bells of a potential mis-match.
- Look for an instruction sheet or rule pamphlet that aligns with the era’s layout and language. A modern reprint might mimic the style but miss the subtle nuance of the original typography and phrasing.
