### Build and assembly: how hard is it?
Let’s be honest: any large-scale benching system requires planning and patience. ### Ergonomics and comfort
Ergonomics are the quiet hero here. The bench height aligns well with standard chairs, and the absence of screens allows you to choose your preferred monitor setup at the user level rather than being dictated by the desk’s integrated layout. For people who like to switch positions, a sit-stand option might be a nice add-on. The key ergonomic considerations you’ll want to watch for include:
- Keyboard and mouse clearance: ensure each workstation has enough room for comfortable forearm support. If your chairs have bulky armrests or you’re using large keyboards, you may want to add a small under-desk tray.
- Monitor positioning: with no built-in screens, each user can tailor their monitor height and distance. Consider monitor arms that fit within the 1530 mm depth so you don’t poke your neighbor with a monitor arm during a quick pivot.
- Lighting: open bench layouts tend to create glare if the lighting is improperly placed. Commission or re-lamp with task lighting to minimize glare on each user’s screen or paper workspace.

Lastly, consider an acoustic moment: with ten people in a single space, the noise floor can escalate quickly. A few well-placed acoustic panels or dividers between bench clusters can reduce noise without destroying the sense of collaboration that the benching arrangement encourages.

## Setup, wiring, and power planning

### Cable management considered
The system’s internal cable channels are designed to keep power and data paths tidy. In practice, you’ll want to map your outlets so that each pair of users has quick access to a power source without running bare wires across the floor. The no-screen variant means there’s less temptation to cluster screens on top of each other, which reduces overhead cable complexity somewhat. Nevertheless, you should plan for:
- A centralized power distribution block or a floor-box approach so you don’t overload any single circuit.
- Sufficient data drops for each workstation, especially if users rely on docking stations for laptops, peripherals, and conference equipment.
- A maintenance plan. Cables will inevitably get tugged; ensure there’s space to re-seat plugs and replace a faulty cable without disassembling the entire bench.

### Tools and tips for quick wiring
A good drill, supplemental screws, zip ties, and a set of cable clips will pay for themselves in saved labor. For the more tech-inclined teams, cable channels with modular dividers help you separate power from data, reducing interference and making the post-install cleanup a breeze. If you’re adding monitors or docking stations, consider a dedicated raceway with enough clearance to avoid pinching or bending cables too aggressively.

### Safety considerations
With ten users sharing the bench, you should implement a standard operating procedure for personal space, chair movement, and cable management. Clear signage about where cables run and how to route them not only reduces trip hazards but also keeps people from “accidentally” unplugging a coworker’s system during a heated brainstorming session. A small reminder wall or digital banner helps keep safety at the top of the daily agenda.
