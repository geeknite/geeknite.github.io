## Rope choice: synthetic vs steel

The 39-foot synthetic rope is a selling point for many buyers who fear rope breaks or kinks. Synthetic rope has a few advantages: it floats, it’s lighter, and it’s generally safer to handle (no steel wire barbs ready to slice your skin). It also makes the overall system lighter, which matters for towing and mounting loads on smaller frames. The downside, of course, is that synthetic rope can degrade with UV exposure, heat, and abrasion. It’s a good match for most consumer-grade winches intended for occasional to mid-range recovery work, but it’s not a unicorn. If you’re counting on pulling heavy loads in extreme environments—think constant, long-duration recovery with rough anchors—steel rope is still king for endurance and heat resistance. However, synthetic rope makes the everyday recovery easier and safer for most trail riders.

In use, the 39-foot length is generous enough for standard ATV/UTV recovery scenarios. If you’re dealing with longer pulls (say, a stubborn truck in a washout at the edge of a cliff), you might need an extension line or longer rope, which is common in the winching world. The rope’s sheath feels tolerant to repeated windings, and the drum seems to handle spooling layouts without excessive line bunching. You’ll want to monitor the rope’s end for any unraveling or fraying after heavy use—this is a basic habit with any synthetic rope winch.

Safety: Synthetic rope, when damaged, can present powder-like fibers that irritate skin or eyes. Wear gloves and eye protection during setup and operation. Avoid dragging the rope over sharp rock faces; use protective sleeves or pads if your anchor points have potential for abrasion.

## Installation and fitment on ATV/UTV

Install-time varies with the vehicle and bumper design. In most cases, you’ll be working with a ready-made winch mount that matches your ATV/UTV’s bumper pattern. The steps, in broad strokes, are:
1) Remove the factory bumper or open the mounting points if necessary. 
2) Align the winch mounting plate with the vehicle’s winch mount standard (the common sizes are 3x4.5 inches or 3x6.5 inches pattern, though yours may differ). 
3) Bolt the winch in with the provided hardware. This is where the “basic hex wrench meets heavy optimism” moment often happens: you’ll probably need a partner to hold pieces in place while you bolt everything together. 
4) Route the synthetic rope onto the drum, ensuring it winds smoothly and leaves a few inches of tail beyond the drum for safe spooling. 
5) Connect the power and the switch, then test with no-load operation to verify the direction and the remote’s responsiveness. 
6) Mount the remote receiver in a reachable spot and test in a safe, clear area. 
7) Re-check all fasteners and cable connections. 

If you’re new to this, take your time. Rushing the process invites cross-threaded bolts and a weekend of scrubbing mud out of your grill. Always consult your ATV/UTV’s manual for mounting patterns and weight limitations. The SucceBuy unit is designed to be friendlier than most pro setups, but it still wants respect and a clean mounting surface.

## Safety and maintenance

Maintenance is not glamorous, but it’s the difference between a long-life tool and a frayed rope sequel. Here are a few practical tips:
- Inspect the synthetic rope before every use for fraying, cuts, or heat-damaged fibers. If you see anything suspicious, retire that rope segment and replace it as needed.
- Check the mounting bolts and ensure the mounting plate remains secure after a few rough pulls. Loose bolts equal dangerous operations and spirited curses.
- Keep the winch free of mud and water as much as possible; wash after heavy use, especially if you’ve been in saltwater environments where corrosion can threaten metal components.
- Use the remote’s safety features responsibly. The “panic” or “stop” function is there for a reason—don’t test its limits on a cliff edge because you forgot to keep your distance.
- Store the synthetic rope neatly when not in use to avoid tangling. A loose coil is a magnet for kinks and a reminder of how creative we can be when procrastinating on rope organization.

Safety notes: never stand in line with the rope during a load. Always stand clear of the line’s direct path, especially if you’re on a slope or near a vulnerable edge. And remember: a pissed-off stump does not forgive, so pick anchors with care and avoid flashy, dangerous setups.
