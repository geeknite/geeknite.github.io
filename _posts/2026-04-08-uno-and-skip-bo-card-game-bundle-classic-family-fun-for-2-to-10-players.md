## How UNO Works

### Core Rules

UNO is about shedding all your cards before anyone else. Each turn you play one card that matches either the color or the number of the top card on the discard pile. If you don’t have a match, you draw a card from the draw pile. Special action cards mix things up: skips, reverses, draw twos, wilds, and wild draw fours that let you call the color after you play them.

A few practical notes from the trenches:
- When you have one card left, you must say UNO. Forget it and you may be forced into a penalty draw unless someone catches you first. The tension is real, especially in a loud living room with dogs and the neighbor's kids cheering you on.
- Jack-of-all-trades wild cards let you choose the color. This is where the strategic drama intensifies and where you can ruin your sibling's day by forcing them into a color they despise.
- Speed matters, but not at the expense of your personal relationships. You will still be friends after a few rounds, even if you yell last card a little too loudly.

### Variant Rules

If you are tired of the same old routine, UNO has a bunch of official variants and house rules that can shake things up:
- Block UNO: A house rule where you stall your opponent by blocking draws with color-swap plays.
- Seven-O: If you manage to swap to the color you dislike last, you gain a tactical advantage. It smells of mischief and opportunity.
- Progressive UNO: Certain action cards stack or chain in unusual ways, turning even the simplest round into a minor enterprise of probability and luck.

Each variant is a chance to discover new tensions and alliances at the table. Remember to set ground rules before the chaos begins so Aunt Judy can still enjoy the night without needing a referee.

## How Skip-Bo Works

### Core Rules

Skip-Bo is a card stacking game with a twist. Each player starts with stock cards, which you can only play in ascending numerical order from 1 to 12. The goal is to empty your stock pile before everyone else by playing cards onto central discard piles in ascending order. The catch is that you can only play from stock piles or don’t forget, you have a hand of face-down and face-up build cards that you can reveal and use strategically.

Key tactical points:
- The game rewards planning and memory. You need to keep track of what numbers are already in play and what numbers are still lurking in players hands.
- Balance risk and reward. Sometimes you choose to play a lower card to unblock bigger plays for future turns; sometimes you gamble and hope your neighbors don’t snatch a needed card from you.
- The interaction level is moderate. You can jabber with friends, trade quips, and still maintain friendly competition—this is not a high-stakes poker table; it is a kitchen table with snacks.

### Strategy Tips

- Watch the discard pile closely. It tells you what numbers are likely to come into play and what others may be holding.
- If you can chain multiple plays in one turn, do it. The additional plays accelerate your progress and showcase your strategic prowess.
- Build a routine of playing from your stock pile early so you can clear space for the build piles later. It keeps the endgame in sight and your tablemates from panicking about the clock.

The pace is slower than UNO, which can feel soothing after a raucous UNO sprint or an energetic round of charades.

## Setup, Playthrough, and Pacing

### Quick Setup

- Separate UNO cards from Skip-Bo piles and lay out the required play areas for each game.
- Determine the number of rounds you want to play, or go until someone reaches a target score. In UNO, score tends to accumulate quickly with draw cards; in Skip-Bo, the vibe is a bit more measured but still full of surprises.
- Decide who starts. The randomness of card draws often dictates this, but you can also go with the classic method of the person who last ate a snack wins the first round as a fair compromise.

### Typical Rounds

In UNO rounds tend to be fast and furious. The winner often emerges in as little as five to ten minutes, sometimes less if someone is on a rampage and the draw pile dries up like a desert. Skip-Bo rounds are a touch longer, allowing for more planning and occasional dramatic comebacks when a player reveals a key stock card at just the right moment.

### Scoring and Endgame

- UNO scoring usually tallies points for cards left in opponents hands. The person with fewer points wins after a designated number of rounds.
- Skip-Bo uses a point system based on a mixture of stock cards left and the speed at which you can finish your own stock piles. The endgame moment when someone declares victory is always dramatic and frequently punctuated by celebratory high fives and the occasional celebratory dance.
