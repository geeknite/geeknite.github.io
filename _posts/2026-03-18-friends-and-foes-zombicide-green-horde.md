Friends and Foes Zombicide Green Horde is CMONs take on the zombie survival co op that asks players to manage a band of survivors through a swamp full of green monsters. The box in front of you is new in box, still sealed in plastic, and every tiny miniature looks smug enough to remind you that you are about to move a plastic warrior across a board and pretend you are saving the world while your dog begs for the snack you secretly pretend not to have. This review aims to give you the full picture in Geeknite style: a healthy mix of admiration for the components, critical eyes on balance, and enough jokes to survive a night of die rolling.

Unboxing and components

What is in Green Horde? The core game comes with a modular board that can be assembled into swampy map chaos, a host of green skinned enemies, a couple dozen survivor miniatures, weapon tokens, equipment tokens, and a thick rulebook that begs you to play and then tortures you with a long paragraph about line of sight. The minis are sturdy plastic with a good amount of detail for the price, and the sculpts capture a wild, feral charm that fits right in with the green skin aesthetic. The cardboard components are sturdy, with die-cut punch boards that feel satisfying to pop out. The production quality is in line with CMONs other Zombicide lines: not the cheapest, not the most fragile, and certainly not afraid of a little glossy finish.

Setup time is not too bad. If you have played Zombicide before, you will be in a good rhythm within 10 to 15 minutes. New players may need a longer stretch while the quick start guide and the scenario book guide you through the basics. One nice touch is the scenario book, which provides bite sized missions that scale with the players and the board configuration. The rules are clear enough for a first session, with a few crisp examples that help you avoid the typical first game gripes, such as mistiming turns or misplacing loot.

Gameplay overview

Zombicide Green Horde sticks to the core rhythm of a Zombicide game: players take turns guiding survivors through actions that aim to clear the board, collect loot, and complete scenario objectives, all while a growing tide of greens and zombies threatens the group. The core mechanics feel familiar if you have played Black Plague or any earlier edition of Zombicide: each survivor has a set of actions per turn, such as move, search, or attack, and you must balance risk and reward as you push across the map.

Turns begin with a setup step that can include spawning new enemies, then the players take their actions, and finally the board is refreshed with new spawns and loot tokens. The spawn system uses a deck of spawn cards and a map specific to the scenario. This keeps players on their toes because you never know which zone will erupt with new threats. The Green Horde introduces enemies that feel both like zombies and like tactical nuisances: orc scouts that rush forward to cut you off, brutes that tank damage and shrug off weak attacks, and agile goblin archers that can ping you from a distance. The mix creates a varied threat profile that rewards careful planning and bold improvisation.

Cooperation and morale

Cooperation is the backbone of any Zombicide game, and Green Horde does not disappoint here. You need to coordinate group movement, share loot, and decide on which objectives to tackle first. The board invites you to split your survivors across multiple fronts at times, which is where the fun and risk collide. The morale track adds a light layer of tension; if your squad takes too many casualties or the objective grows more demanding than your team can handle, you will feel the pinch. The new enemy types in Green Horde push you to adapt; a pack of orcs rushing from the swamp can force you to retreat and regroup rather than push ahead to a reckless glory run.

Thematic vibe and pacing

Green Horde captures a swampy, muddy horror vibe that sits somewhere between a fantasy adventure and a survival horror tale. Orcs are not mindless; they hunt in groups, coordinate ambushes, and use terrain to pressure your survivors. The pacing shifts as you move from early exploration to mid game defense to late game push for a final objective. The scenario design makes you balance exploration with the risk of triggering multiple spawn points, which can snowball quickly if you mismanage your resources. It is the kind of game that rewards players who can stay calm when the dice are screaming and the board looks like a cartoon swamp exploded in your living room.

Length and replayability

A typical Green Horde game stretches from 45 to 90 minutes depending on scenario and player count. The modular board means no two sessions will look the same, and the variety of objectives keeps things fresh across multiple plays. The box includes a selection of scenarios that scale in complexity, from introductory runs for new players to more challenging missions for experienced crews. Because this is a standalone edition, you can jump in without owning other Zombicide sets, but if you already own Black Plague or something similar you will appreciate the small cross references and shared rules that speed up setup and comprehension.

Component quality comparisons

Compared to the most recent Black Plague and second edition Zombicide sets, Green Horde is not a revolution but a refinement. The miniatures are robust enough for lots of table time, and the sculpts carry the fantasy flavor you expect from an orc led undead horde. The color palette is bold and readable, which matters when you have to distinguish survivors from zombies in a chaotic melee. The rulebook is dense but approachable, with the quick start clearly marking out the most important steps for newcomers. If you own multiple Zombicide boxes, you will appreciate the way the game distills the core engine into a leaner experience while still delivering new enemy behaviors and themed gear.

What makes Green Horde different

The Green Horde edition leans into the fantasy vibe of orcs while retaining the fundamental Zombicide model: cooperative play with a push your luck edges as you search for weapons and armor and avoid being overwhelmed by the growing zombie threat. The new enemy types and terrain interactions give this set a distinct flavor that makes it feel more than just a rebrand. It also brings a slightly different risk/reward curve, as players must navigate marshy terrain that slows movement and creates chokepoints. If you like the idea of a zombie apocalypse with a twist of fantasy, Green Horde offers a satisfying flavor upgrade that remains accessible to players who are new to Zombicide.

Value and price

Value depends on how you measure it. If you are primarily concerned with the number of miniatures and the variety of scenarios, Green Horde delivers a solid kit that can provide dozens of sessions of family friendly chaos. The components are durable, and the scenario book is well produced. The price point is reasonable for a fully packaged game of this scope, though you might wonder if you will ever defeat a scenario before your friends decide to blame the dice for their own misfortune. In short, you get a lot of game for the money, and if you are a Zombicide enthusiast, the Green Horde box slots neatly into your collection as a standalone game or as a complementary expansion.

Pros and cons

Pros

- Fresh take on the zombie engine with orc and goblin threats
- Strong cooperative play with clear objectives
- Modular board and scenario variety keep replayability high
- Quick start reduces barrier for new players

Cons

- Dice heavy moments can feel punishing if you want a light experience

Who should buy this

If you enjoy Zombicide in general and want a boxed experience that stands on its own while offering new flavor, Green Horde is a strong pick. It is particularly good for players who crave a fantasy twist and a faster, more accessible session than Black Plague. If you own a lot of CMON Zombicide lines, this edition offers a new way to introduce orcs as villains rather than as heroes, which can be a refreshing narrative change during the later rounds.
