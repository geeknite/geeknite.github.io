## The near-complete state: what’s in, what’s missing, and how that feels
This is the tricky part: the phrase “near complete” isn’t a complaint with a surface-level syntax. It’s a mood. It’s a vibe. It’s the moment you reach for the last few components and realize you own the box, but someone forgot to pack the rest of the map, the directions, or the cake that was promised for the victory screen. In this particular Guillotine batch, there are several categories of items to scrutinize:

### 1) Cards and tokens
A big chunk of Zombicide’s personality comes from its cards—mission cards, level-up action cards, and event cards that drive the pacing. In a near-complete Guillotine box, you’ll likely encounter missing or duplicated card backs, misprinted fronts, or a boneyard of tokens that aren’t labeled in a way that you can quickly decipher mid-slay. In my experience with Rue Morgue Season 3, a surprising portion of the card stock resembles a “watch this space” note stuck to a calendar: the art is fantastic, the flavor text is delightfully grim, but you may find the wrong tokens in the wrong bag or the wrong sized counters hiding in the card sleeves like a zombie wearing a magician’s cape.

### 2) Miniatures and assembly
Miniatures are where you either go from “board game” to “board game that pretends to be a museum statue.” The Rue Morgue release has some stunning sculpts: characters with trench coats that seem to billow in non-existent wind, zombies with ridiculous poses that look like they’re auditioning for a soap opera, and a handful of “special” NPCs whose sculpts tell you that someone had fun with the design budget. Incomplete kits often show up with missing bases, mismatched poses, or miniature sprues that refuse to snap together. In some cases you’ll have to glue a few pieces that aren’t intended to join, which leads to a Frankenstein’s monster of a squad that still somehow fights its way through the campaign. The upside is that you can still paint and customize to your heart’s content, leaning into the “haunted fashion show” look, which is a vibe all by itself.

### 3) Map components and modular barriers
Rue Morgue’s map modules are the stage on which the drama happens. Missing tiles or mismatched modular components can break the rhythm of a scenario, forcing you to improvise with “area control” decision-making that feels more like interpretive dance than strategy. A near-complete set will generally include the core tile sheets and a handful of street sections, with some alleyways missing or a handful of building doors misaligned. The net effect? You’ll end up planning around the gaps, which sometimes yields surprising tactical creativity, and other times yields a game-night nap as you curse the theme’s cruel precision.

### 4) Rulebooks and supplemental guides
Nothing saps the mood like a rulebook with upside-down pages or a supplement that doubles as a cocktail napkin. In a near-complete Guillotine label, there’s a real chance you’ll encounter a rules pamphlet that looks like it was printed on a vintage player-piano’s paper roll: charming, but possibly incomplete. The best-case scenario is a compact reference sheet that distills Rue Morgue’s quirks into something you can memorize after your second coffee. The worst-case is basically a scavenger hunt through a pile of index cards to figure out how “Activation Phase” interacts with “Close-Combat Phase.” On the bright side, the confusion leads to conversations about rule interpretations, which is how house rules are born, and sometimes how masterpieces emerge.

### 5) The “incomplete” factor and the emotional toll
Let’s be real: the emotional impact of owning a near-complete (but not quite there) box is a weird cocktail of nostalgia, frustration, and the stubborn optimism of a gamer who believes the missing pieces exist somewhere in a shoebox under a couch. You’ll scroll your memory for a box you bought at a convention or a friend’s garage sale that might contain the rest. You’ll fantasize about a local game store having a “Guillotine Refill Bin” stocked with the exact tokens you need, like a mythical creature delivering the last piece of the puzzle with a flourish. And then you’ll decide to either patch the gaps yourself, proxy a few components with your own creation, or simply embrace the near-complete charm and run a campaign anyway. The latter is the Geeknite way: adapt, improvise, and pretend the gaps were intentional design choices that encourage improvisation.

If you’re used to a brand-new boxed experience, Guillotine’s “2014 vibe” might feel like stepping into a retro arcade. Still, this is not a deal-breaker; it’s a reminder that this box wasn’t shipped as a pristine museum artifact but as a playable artifact that has seen a few real campaigns.
