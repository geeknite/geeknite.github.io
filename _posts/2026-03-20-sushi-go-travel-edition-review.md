## What is Sushi Go Travel Edition
Sushi Go Travel Edition is the compact kin to the classic Sushi Go, a card drafting game that has delighted table pilots around the globe since it first rolled off the printed conveyor belt. The travel edition keeps the same core idea — draft a single card, pass the rest to your neighbor, collect sets to score points — but trims the fat, tightens the rules, and stuffs it into a travel friendly tin. The result is a game that plays in roughly 15 to 20 minutes, scales from 2 to 5 players, and refuses to pretend that you are on a long vacation while you are actually on a delayed flight with a pretzel substitute for dinner.

If you know Sushi Go already, you know the flavor: a fast, light, snackable drafting mechanic built around cute sushi cards with different scoring rules. If you do not know Sushi Go, think of it as a mini auction, a dash of set collection, and a friendly cardio warmup for your brain. The travel edition keeps the same vibe but swaps the gym membership for a travel tin and a cheat sheet that slides into your pocket like a well-behaved passport. It is not a replacement for a heavier strategy game, but it is absolutely the riff you want when you need a quick airside victory lap.

## How to Play a Quick Game in Real Life Settings
If you have played Sushi Go, the travel edition will feel familiar. If this is your first foray into the world of passing cards around, welcome to a fast education in drafting and micro strategy.

Setup
- Shuffle the deck and deal a hand of eight cards to each player. In a two player game, you may want to extend the rounds by one to keep the tension high. In a five player game, the pace moves quicker and your neighbors become your biggest fans or your most brutal opponents.
- Each player keeps their hand hidden and has a single pass direction based on rounds (left in round one, right in round two, left again in round three).

Turn order
- Each player selects one card from their hand to keep and places it face down. On the count of one, everyone reveals their chosen card simultaneously.
- After revealing, you pass the remainder of your hand to the neighbor in the designated direction.
- This continues until all cards have been drafted and the round ends.

Scoring rounds
- After each round, you tally points for the cards you have collected. The official rules align with the base Sushi Go scoring: sashimi rewards you for three cards, tempura for pairs, nigiri values, maki's ranking by count, wasabi boosting the next nigiri, and pudding as the end of game kicker when included in your edition. The travel edition typically emphasizes a clear and fast scoring checklist to keep the pace from bogging down your gate-side fun.
- Rounds happen three times. After the third round, you tally any pudding or end game bonuses and declare the winner.

Wasabi and other little quirks
Wasabi is a small but mighty mechanic — playing a nigiri under wasabi increases its value on the next nigiri you play. In a travel game where you are juggling seating, timing, and flight announcements, wasabi becomes a tiny gamble that pays off with a crisp payoff if you can steer your own next draw. The travel edition keeps this mechanic intact, only it expects you to be efficient with your memory and your table space, not your legs.

## Strategy What Works Best in a Pocket Sized World
Here are some practical strategies that tend to pay dividends in travel mode:

- Early tempo matters: You want to establish a rhythm that ensures you have at least one strong set each round. If you can punch out a reliable sashimi or a sturdy tempura, your score will stay in the positive zone even when your neighbors go on a run of perfect maki stacks.
- Watch the table: In a two player game, you know exactly what your opponent has passed to you and what they likely hold. In a five player game, you do not, but you can still infer from open cards left in the deck and your neighbor’s actions. Reading patterns in a short session is a skill worth developing without overthinking the math.
- The wasabi gamble: Do not underestimate the power of the next nigiri after a wasabi card. In the travel edition this is often the difference between a tidy 11 points and a clumsy 5 point swing. If you are behind, a well-timed wasabi play can salvage you.
- Pudding patience: In some printings pudding is a catch all tie breaker. If yours includes pudding, this is a small but significant endgame lever. If not, you still have to manage the rest of the scoring. Either way, keep an eye on pudding if you are playing a longer campaign with multiple games in one sitting.
- Keep it simple, soldier: Do not chase every single combination. The travel edition rewards clarity and timing more than heavy multi-card combos that require perfect sequencing. The best plays often come from recognizing the highest value path that minimizes risk and keeps your options open for the next round.

## Variants and House Rules
If you want to spice up your travel game night, here are a few light variants you can try without breaking the bank:

- Two round marathon: Run two games back to back to simulate a longer session while still staying within the travel friendly vibe.
- Draft pressure: In a crowded space, insist that players reveal both their pick and their thought process to speed up the passing and add a little theater to the process. It is surprising how many laughs you can squeeze from a one card explanation.
- House rule for perfect combos: If a player completes a sashimi or tempura combo exactly as per the scoring sheet, award a tiny bonus token that can be redeemed on a future round for a single extra card draft. No math brain required, just bragging rights.
