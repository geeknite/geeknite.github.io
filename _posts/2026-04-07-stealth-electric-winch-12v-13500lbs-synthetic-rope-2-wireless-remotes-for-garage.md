## Installation and mounting considerations

Install is the part where the dream meets the driveway and you realize you should have called a friend with a truck battery that’s actually charged. Here are practical tips to get it right:

- Mounting location: The bumper or frame rail must be robust enough to handle the 13,500-lb rating. If you’re building a wooden fortress for your winch, you might be better off building a small safe fort for your cat instead.
- Electrical: A dedicated power line with an appropriate fuse is mandatory. Don’t slice into hidden wires and pretend you love your car for a week while they patch up your ego.
- Cable routing: Use the rope guides to prevent kinks. Keep the rope away from sharp edges and, if possible, install a protective synthetic sleeve to extend rope life.
- Winch operation clearance: Make sure you have a clear line of pull and an anchor point that won’t shift under load. A bored dog or a curious neighbor’s child is not the ideal anchor, unless you want dramatic chaos in your driveway.

## Safety features, limitations, and common-sense checks

Safety isn’t glamorous, but it’s the reason your project doesn’t become a viral video about “what not to do with a winch.” The Stealth winch includes:

Limitations to remember:
- The rated line pull assumes proper setup. If you’re pulling from a bad anchor or using a compromised mounting, the numbers drop dramatically.

## Maintenance and longevity thoughts

A winch is not a one-and-done investment; it’s a relationship with your vehicle, your tools, and your garage’s sense of humor. Maintenance basics include:
- Regular inspection of the synthetic rope for frays and flat spots. If you see any sign of wear, replace it rather than hoping for the best.
- Cleaning and lubing moving parts per manufacturer guidelines. Avoid over-lubing, which can attract dirt and shorten life.
- Checking electrical connections for corrosion. A cheap spray-on corrosion inhibitor can extend life in humid or salty environments.
- Stored protection: keep the winch and remote components dry and protected from the elements when not in use.

The bottom line: treat the winch like a tool you actually value, not a novelty you mount on the wall to impress guests who never actually visit. A well-maintained winch will be a faithful sidekick for years of garage gambits.
