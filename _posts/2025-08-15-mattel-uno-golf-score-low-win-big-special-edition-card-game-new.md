## How to play (Overview)

If you know UNO, you know most of what you need to start playing. This edition adds a light, tournament-esque corridor with its nine-hole course.

- Setup: Each player starts with a typical seven-card hand, as in classic UNO. The table shows the nine Hole cards, arranged in a row, representing a miniature course. The aim is to complete the nine holes with the lowest cumulative score.
- Turn structure: On your turn, you draw as needed and try to play a card that matches color or number, or you drop a special card to alter the flow of play. If you have no playable card, you draw; if you still can’t play, your turn ends and you note a groan-worthy face.
- The golf twist: When you reach the end of a hole (the exact mechanics defined in the rulebook), your score for that hole is tallied. The score for a hole depends on the values of the cards left in your hand at the moment you finish that hole. It’s a twist that makes you think: do I minimize risk now, or push a risky play that could blow the hole (and possibly the entire game) wide open?
- End of round: After nine holes, whoever has the lowest total score wins. In some variants used by families, players may play multiple rounds and roll up the totals for a grand tally.

The core experience remains the same: match colors and numbers, play special cards to change the flow, and use your wits to avoid racking up a high score. The Golf edition encourages a slower, more deliberate tempo, which can be a boon if your group enjoys longer, story-driven sessions with a light hinge of strategy.

Rulebook highlights to speed up the learning curve:

- The Hole sequence is fixed: nine holes, once a hole starts, you play through the set of nine cards until you’ve reached the end of that hole.
- Scoring is tracked separately by the included pad, so players aren’t left to hold mental math while you’re trying to decide whether to draw two or press your luck.

If you’re the kind of player who loves to argue about rule interpretations, this edition will give you something to riff about. It’s a game about social interplay with a scoring layer that rewards those who can forecast the end of a hole and manage risk across several turns.

UNO Golf doesn’t rush you, which is both blessing and curse. The pace is a little slower than classic UNO, but you gain a social dimension as you discuss hole strategies, tease players who are "in the sand trap" (metaphorically—this is UNO golf, not actual golf), and react to wild cards that suddenly change the hole you’re in.

- For groups that enjoy “hot potato” passing of turns, this edition gives you the room you need to enjoy a few jokes, a little banter, and a few groans.
- For players who want a quick, kinetic session, you’ll want to keep an eye on the nine-hole course and set a clock. It’s easy to adjust to shorter rounds by limiting the number of holes or by setting a maximum number of turns per hole.

The humor is light and family-friendly, with enough thematic cues to keep kids engaged without pushing them away from the table. The result is a product that’s flexible enough for casual game nights but structured enough to enable a few rounds without too much downtime.
