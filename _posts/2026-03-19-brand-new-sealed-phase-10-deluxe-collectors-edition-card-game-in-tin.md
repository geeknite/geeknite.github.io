## How to Play: A Quick Refresh for the Time-crunched Geek
If you’re a seasoned Phase 10 veteran, you’ll know this dance, but it never hurts to re-acquaint yourself with the steps. If you’re new, we’ll keep it brisk and friendly, like a sitcom-friendly tutorial that still respects your time.

### Objective
Be the first player to complete all ten phases (Phase 1 through Phase 10). Each phase requires a unique arrangement of cards (for example, Phase 1 might be two sets of three, Phase 5 a run of seven, etc.). After completing a phase, you move on to the next one. The game ends when a player finishes Phase 10 and goes out, or when you decide you’ve had enough punishment and call it a night.

### Setup
- Shuffle the deck and deal a certain number of cards to each player (the exact number depends on player count and any house rules).
- Place the remaining deck in the draw pile and flip the top card to start the discard pile.
- Each player uses a personal score sheet (or a mental tally if you are the kind who enjoys risk and chaos) to track completed phases.

### Turn Structure
On your turn: draw a card (either from the draw pile or the discard pile), decide whether to lay down cards to complete your current phase, and then discard a card to end your turn. As you complete phases, you progress through the sequence: Phase 1, Phase 2, and so on. The last hand can be a little wild, with players sprinting toward the endgame while still calculating whether their last discard will help them or hand victory to someone else like a friendly ghost around the table.

### Winning
The first player to complete Phase 10 and go out wins the round, and in a limited-series style, you may decide to play multiple rounds or adopt a running score to crown an overarching “Season Champion.”

## Strategy Tips for Glorified Casuals and Ambitious Night Owls
- Learn the phases by heart, especially Phase 1 through Phase 3. A quick memory of the required combos means fewer mistakes when the table’s chatter rises to “Give me luck, gamer!”
- Track the discard pile. If you see a card you need going to the discard discard pile again and again, there’s a good chance someone else is primed to jump on it. You may want to shield your own path by taking a drawn card that doesn’t help anyone—except you in two turns.
- Trade off a phase for future opportunities. In a long game, you can sometimes step back from a current phase to set up a more valuable one later, especially if you’re confident you’ll draw a needed card on the next couple of turns.
