## How to play in a two player duel

Two players, a shared appetite for victory, and a bag of letters that will betray your mood when you draw your seventh vowel. The goal in Bananagrams Duel is to be the first player who uses all of their tiles to form a valid grid of interconnected words. The board is not fixed like a Sudoku; instead, you arrange tiles freely to connect across the main grid. It is a do not cross your own words style of play, which means you get to form a kind of goofy lattice that reveals your love of puns as you go.

### Setup

- Each player grabs a starter set of tiles from the bag. The exact number can vary, but a common approach is to start with 7 tiles each and go from there. You want enough letters to feel challenged but not so many that it becomes a geometry class instead of a word race.
- Place your tiles on a rack in front of you where you can see them clearly. A well lit table helps, because there is nothing more tragic than losing a tile in the abyss of a coffee spill.
- Decide who goes first by a silly method such as a coin toss or who can shout the longest non vowels chain first. The choice is yours, but do not blame the dice if chaos ensues.

### Turn structure and rules of the duel
- On your turn, you place tiles from your rack to form or extend your grid. The grid must be a valid set of connected words, similar to a crossword arrangement. If you feel a wild surge of inspiration, you can rearrange your grid a bit as you add more words, but you cannot simply pick up a tile and jettison it to your opponent without reason.
- If you cannot make a valid word, you draw tiles from the bag to refresh your rack. This is the point where you feel the game starting to tilt toward your competitor as you begrudgingly add consonant after consonant in hopes of the perfect cross.
- The game continues with players alternating turns until one player uses all of their tiles. The other player tallies their remaining tiles to determine a final penalty if needed. The excitement here is that finishing first gives you a small victory dance that almost certainly ends with laughter and mild bragging.

## Strategy and moment-to-moment play

If you like word games, Bananagrams Duel has a few little pockets of depth. It is tempting to go for big, long words early, but the clever players will tell you that the real art is in efficient tile management and cross word optimization. Here are a few strategic notes you may find helpful, whether you are new to Bananagrams or a returning veteran.

- Tempo matters as much as vocabulary. The first few rounds are a warm up. By the time you reach round five or six, you will see patterns emerge in tile distribution that can guide the early decisions.
- Build flexible grids. A grid that can accommodate edge expansions without a major reconfiguration reduces the risk of getting stuck with a wall of unworkable tiles. Think in the direction of modular words that connect across multiple potential cross points.
- Prioritize rack optimization. A clean looking rack makes it easier to see possible words, and a tidy rack is a good sign that you are on track for victory. Do not be afraid to reshuffle to reveal a better option.
- Use cross words to your advantage. When possible, place cross-word letters in a way that creates both existing and potential new words. It helps to visualize a small lattice rather than a single line of text.
- Manage the finish. As you approach the endgame, the temptation to jam a long word to win can be strong, but careful endgame planning can reduce the risk of leaving a cluster of tiles that cannot be used.
