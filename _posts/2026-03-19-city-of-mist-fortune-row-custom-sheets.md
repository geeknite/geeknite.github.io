## Overview of Fortune Row Custom Sheets

Fortune Row brings a set of purpose built character sheets that align with the two core mechanics of City of Mist: the Power tags and the Mythos with the personal cost of being radiant in a city full of shadows. The sheets are designed to be easy to print, quick to fill, and flexible enough to support a range of play styles from fast and hacky street myths to elaborate,-across-the-night arcs where every line of a sheet becomes a breadcrumb trail to a bigger myth.

These sheets are not a replacement for the basic sheet you find in the core book. They supplement it with a sharper visual language, better space for approach angles, and a module style that helps map the player’s power tags to the mythic design of their character. The philosophy behind Fortune Row is simple: help players move from a concept to a playable, trackable engine as quickly as possible without sacrificing flavor.

For those who obsess over layout, you will be pleased to know that Fortune Row emphasizes readability, which is a fancy way to say that you do not need a magnifying glass to decipher your own sheet. Font choices, color cues, and grid lines all work together to create a sheet that feels like the city itself: layered, a little messy, and somehow elegant once you know what each line means. If you want a quick visual reference, the included art direction nods to noir posters, graffiti tags, and the faint glow of a rain-soaked alleyway after midnight. It is not subtle, and that is part of the charm.

## What is Inside the Custom Sheets

### Playbook Alignment and Front Matter
The Fortune Row sheets provide a front page that mirrors the two fakirs of City of Mist — the Mythos and Logos — with space set aside for two things most players forget: backstory timing and conflict hooks. The sheets prompt players to capture two or three particular moments in their character’s life where myth meets mundane reality. This is not a mandatory ritual; it is a friendly nudge toward the kind of narrative leverage that makes the city feel alive. The front matter invites players to note their primary mythic identity, their firearm of choice in terms of narrative use rather than literal guns, and the cosmic cost that comes with leaning into a Power tag.

### Power Tags and Law of Quick Referencing
One of the most annoying things about new sheets is not having quick reference for what the Power tags actually do during a session. Fortune Row solves this by giving a compact reference on the sheet itself. You can see a concise list of your Power tags, the associated dice pool, and the minimum requirements to trigger certain improvements in the story. The layout makes it easier for players to remember their options without flipping back to the core book for page after page of cross references.

### Mythos and Logos Sectioning
Fortune Row emphasizes the dual identity mechanic by offering a dedicated space where players can map their mythic and mundane sides to distinct sections on the sheet. This separation helps prevent chaos when the GM asks for a quick check about a character’s past action under the Mythos umbrella. It also doubles as a visual reminder that every City of Mist character is a walking contradiction: a hero with a shadow, a villain with a dream, and a detective who senses the city’s heartbeat with a rhythm only a myth could understand.

### Conditions, Power Costs, and Strain Trackers
The sheets introduce a clean way to track all the costs of using a Power tag, including the potentially devastating consequences when the city pushes back. A dedicated Strain track sits alongside a Condition box — a practical feature that keeps the game moving and reduces the chance of argument over whether someone is at a -2 to their next roll. The design elegantly reduces micro-decisions during the game, letting you focus on drama instead of arithmetic.

### Gear, Contacts, and Hideouts
Fortune Row does not neglect the crunchy bits either. A dedicated gear box is included, with space to add details that matter in the moment — how a gear interacts with a Power tag, what it looks like in action, and what it costs in story currency. The Contacts and Hideouts sections are thoughtfully scaled to support a neighborhood-level approach to mythic influence. The idea is to give the GM easy ways to weave a character into the social fabric of the city without forcing the table to spin ten new plot threads every session.

### Aesthetic and Artwork Notes
The sheets include a restrained, stylish aesthetic that nods to the retro-futurist vibe of the City of Mist universe. You can see a clean color palette, with lines and icons designed to be legible on both print and digital devices. The art is not loud, but it is not shy either. It hints at the mythic undercurrent of the city while staying practical for a busy table where players want to write names and notes without their eyes taking a beating.

## Design and Play Experience

### Usability: How Do They Feel in Practice?
In practical play, Fortune Row sheets shine when you are in a hurry and still want to look cool doing it. The layout reduces the cognitive load on players who are juggling two roles at once — player and character. It makes it easier for new players to pick up a sheet and have a sense of where to put their stats and tags without needing an expert guide beside them. The improved readability translates into faster character creation and quicker in-game decision making, which is a win for anyone who has sat through a 45 minute character creation only to realize their concept needs a second amendment to function.

### Balance: Are They Too Powerful or Too Permissive?
Fortune Row does not push players into a single playstyle. The sheets are designed to support both tight noir detective vibes and sprawling mythic epics where every decision could tilt the city into a new legend. The balance hinges on how the GM frames the Gear, Contacts, and Hideouts. It is easy to over-index on a single powerful Power tag if the GM does not enforce narrative costs, but the sheet itself is not the culprit here. It simply makes it easier to track what is working and what is not. In short, the risk of a runaway mechanic is not the sheet’s fault; it is the table’s willingness to lean into grand drama.

### Accessibility: Printing vs Digital Use
The creators clearly considered both print and digital play. The print version looks crisp on A4 or letter size paper, with a layout that holds up under rough handling. The digital variant preserves the same structure, letting players fill it out on tablets or laptops. If your table splits the difference and uses a projector to share character sheets, Fortune Row holds up in that scenario too, as long as you keep the font size legible and the projector brightness reasonable. It is not a glossy premium feel, but the design communicates its purpose with aplomb.

## Practical Run Guide: Using Fortune Row Sheets at Your Table

### Quick Start for GMs
1) Print a set for each player or prepare a shared digital copy. 2) Walk players through the Mythos and Logos sections as a quick concept check. 3) Have players populate their Power tags with prompts that tie into the city. 4) Use the Strain and Conditions sections to generate meaningful consequences for failed checks or escalating conflicts. 5) Integrate Gear, Contacts, and Hideouts into the ongoing plot to ensure the sheet carries narrative weight beyond numbers. 6) End session with a quick recap of how each character’s myth has evolved, and set up hooks for the next run.

### Player Tips: Filling Out the Sheets Quickly
- Start with the core tag you want to push this session. It helps you riff on the city’s vibe and makes your Power feel alive immediately.
- Write a single clear clue for your Mythos hand in the back pocket. When the GM asks for a mythic hint, you can pull from that pocket without rifling through pages.
- Don’t overthink the Gear. It is a narrative tool as much as a mechanical one. If your gear helps reveal a story beat, you are doing it right.

### GM Tips: Driving Narrative with Fortune Row
Fortune Row can help you orchestrate a session by providing tangible hooks that players can chase across the city. If a player terms a decision as a mythic break, you can respond with a cost or consequence that reverberates through the session. The sheet’s layout makes it easier to track these consequences, which is particularly valuable when you have a longer arc or a multi-session mystery. In city terms, Fortune Row gives you a way to show players that the city notices their actions and pays attention to the little choices as well as the big ones.
