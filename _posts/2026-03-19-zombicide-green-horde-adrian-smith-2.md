The Green Horde box continues that tradition with a green-tinged twist: a new faction (the Green Horde), a vibe shift toward swampy, mossy dread, and a cadre of adventurers who probably forgot to bring a way to light their torches. The Special Guest Box is, as the name implies, a spin-off treat featuring Adrian Smith’s artwork and a curated set of miniatures, cards, and tokens that feel like they were designed by someone who has doodled a lot on the back of fantasy catalogs while listening to Dungeons & Dragons playlists on repeat.

## The Adrian Smith Factor: Art, Theme, and Narrative Tone
Adrian Smith’s involvement is the big nerdy draw. If you’ve followed CMON’s collaborations, you know Smith’s name attached to a box signals something special: a cohesive visual language, a strong thematic spine, and a sense that you’re not just buying minis—you’re adopting a story arc you can bludgeon your friends with over beers and failed dice rolls.

What Adrian Smith brings to this edition isn’t just pretty pictures; it’s a tonal shift. The Green Horde isn’t just “more zombies,” it’s an ecosystem of green-tinted dread. The survivors are drawn with the kind of character archetypes that you’ll recognize in a dozen fantasy novels, but with enough unique quirks to feel fresh (and to justify those two extra miniatures nobody asked for but everyone secretly loves). Expect swamp-filled backdrops, mossy ruins, and the kind of atmospheric lighting that makes you believe you’re slaying undead in a dank catacomb while your friends argue about loot distribution.

If you’re a fan of Smith’s other CMON projects, you’ll spot recurring motifs: gnarly terrain, dramatic poses, and that sense that every piece of artwork could double as a poster in a nerdy dorm room. The second Adrian Smith edition continues to lean into that aesthetic, while balancing readability for new players who just want to grab a pawful of dice and stab some zombies with a spoon if necessary.

## Building and Components: Quality Check
Let’s slice the “build quality” onion. The Green Horde Special Guest Box doesn’t pretend to be a full game; it’s a curated enhancement that sits nicely on top of the Black Plague experience. Here’s what you’ll actually be assembling and handling:

- Miniatures: Expect a mix of the Green Horde’s iconic fighters and the two heroic survivors. The sculpts carry a lot of character—great weapon poses, detailed armor, and faces that look like they’re actively plotting your doom. The plastic quality is solid, not brittle, which is a nice change from the occasional dry-spring nightmare you get with other games.
- Card deck: New objective cards, new equipment cards, and maybe a few special event cards. The card stock feels sturdy, with clear icons and readable text. You won’t be squinting at a tiny font under a fluorescent kitchen light, which is a small victory worth shouting about between turns.
- Tokens and tiles: Standard Zombicide practice applies—brightly colored tokens, legible icons, and a few fancy border treatments that tell you “this one is important.” The tokens are sturdy enough to survive a few night’s worth of “I only meant to roll once, but the dice kept rolling” moments.
- Rule insert: A compact cheat sheet and clarifications that help you integrate the expansion with minimal book-learning. If you’ve played Black Plague before, you’ll appreciate the absence of a separate, mega-rulebook annex, which is a blessing during a crowded game night.

If you’re into the unboxing ritual, you’ll find this box scratches the itch: good insert organization, a few easter eggs in the artwork, and the sense that you’re holding a well-built product instead of the dystopian ash of a forgotten hobby shop box.

## Gameplay: How It Feels to Fight The Green Horde
The Green Horde Special Guest Box doesn’t reinvent the wheel; it reimagines how you use the wheel in the dark. It brings new mechanics and a more brutal tempo to the board, which can be a blessing or a curse depending on your group’s appetite for stress-testing coordination and memory.

- New survivors and abilities: The two survivors you get in this box bring distinct playstyles to the table. One emphasizes mobility and board control, the other leans into defensive prowess with an emphasis on healing and shielding allies. Together, they create a dance of risk and reward: you push forward on a fragile slug of a plan, and you hope your teammates’ dice rolls cooperate.
- The Green Horde mechanics: The green faction introduces a few twists—faster advances, some creeping reinforcements, and a handful of faction-specific objectives. The pacing can feel relentless, especially if you’re playing on a higher difficulty. It’s a good reminder that zombies aren’t just slow mobs; they can be cunning, too, when an entire horde starts converging across the map like a suspiciously well-coordinated flash mob.
- A new boss encounter: If you’re into boss fights (who isn’t when the snack table has a goldfish-sized bowl of pretzels watching your every move?), the big green boss provides a narrative high point. It’s not just a stats exercise; it’s a thematic beat that lands with a satisfying “this is why we brought extra dice.” The boss fights in this box tend to reward planning and team communication more than sheer luck, which is a welcome change for players who value strategy over slapstick dice luck.
- Solo and two-player modes: If you’re piloting a one-man or two-person crew, the box scales fairly well. Certain objectives adapt to smaller player counts, and the survivors’ abilities pair nicely with a reduced cast. The mental math gets easier as the table shrinks, but the pressure remains—so you still might end up screaming about armor saves in front of your roommates. It’s a weirdly comforting experience when you realize you aren’t alone in your obsession with tactical positioning and fantasy gore.

The overall gameplay experience stays faithful to Black Plague’s core identity: cooperative play with a heavy emphasis on positioning, resource management, and a little element of chaos. The Green Horde spice adds more heroic moments, a few tears of sympathy for your poor survivors, and a lot of “oh no, we forgot to bring a light source” moments that are perfect for late-night gaming sessions.

If you want to compare this to other expansions, the Green Horde is more narrative-driven than some of the more modular “mechanics packs.” It’s not simply “more zombies”—it’s a thematic extension with a defined aesthetic and a storyline that you can attempt to follow (or ignore, which is often the better approach for folks who just want to roll dice and argue about loot).

## Thematic Flavor, Humor, and Social Vibes
One of the best things about Adrian Smith’s involvement is that it adds flavor beyond just new minis and cards. The art direction continues to show up in interesting places: boxes, character sheets, and even the character in-game flavor text. The Green Horde tone feels less like a random assortment of green guys and more like a faction with a distinct culture of its own. You might find yourself narrating a battle with a mock-epic voice, or giving your survivors silly nicknames that actually stick by virtue of you using them every turn.

Humor is still present but is tasteful and well-timed. The designers allow the tension to spike and then give you a tiny breathing room moment, a weird pun in a card title, or a clever artwork gag that makes the table laugh before the next wave of doom arrives. It’s a tricky balance: you want to respect the horror themes while keeping gaming nights accessible to people who come for friends and snacks, not just terrifying, long-winded monologues about the tactical value of a single misstep.

## How It Feels in a Real Session: Tempo, Tension, and Therapy Dice
A good Zombicide expansion should feel like a flow state. You reason about the board, you swap roles, you roll the dice, you get punished when your plan crumbles, and you walk away with that “I could have done better” glow that only comes from a hard-fought victory or a spectacular collapse that ends with a dramatic last-stand sigh.

The Green Horde box excels at pushing your nerve center into a slightly higher gear. The new objectives can force you to split your team, which is always a risky proposition given how easily your survivors can get overwhelmed if you overextend. The new survivors provide different power curves, so you can tailor your group to a more aggressive, “all-out assault” style or a slower, methodical approach that rewards careful planning and tile control.

For those who love to chain-swap loot cards and chain-slap successful trap disarms, you’ll find the new items integrate smoothly with the base equipment pool. The synergy isn’t revolutionary, but it’s satisfying and gives you those satisfying “oh, we actually combo’d our way out of the tight spot” moments.

## A Few Room-Temperature Takes on Value and Price
As with most boxed expansions, the question isn’t just “is it good?” but “is it good enough to justify the price?” The Green Horde Special Guest Box sits in that middle-ground sweet spot: not a full game, but a significant jump from a normal expansion in terms of atmosphere, thematic impact, and the sheer “wow” factor of Adrian Smith’s artwork. If you’re scrolling your credit card with a careful, mechanical calculation of “how many nights of gaming will this add?” then you’ll probably land around a positive ROI.

Keep in mind: the price isn’t just about raw mini counts. It’s the packaging, the art, the narrative threads, and the event-like feel of introducing a fresh faction to your usual group. If you’re a collector, you’ll see this as a must-have. If you’re a casual player who just wants to nerd out for a few sessions, it still provides a rich environment for campaign-style play without demanding a full new game purchase every season.

## The Verdict: How It Stands in the Zombicide Pantheon
Bottom line: the Green Horde Special Guest Box by Adrian Smith 2 is a strong addition to Zombicide: Black Plague. It doesn’t rewrite the rules, but it refines the mood, heightens the tension, and gives you a set of tools that feel purpose-built to create memorable gaming nights. The art is a standout feature; Smith’s contribution helps the box feel like a premium product rather than a filler expansion. The survivors bring fresh synergy; the Green Horde forces you to think about positioning and tempo in new ways; and the boss encounter provides a climactic moment that those late-night sessions crave.

If your gaming group enjoys the heavy-campaign, cooperative puzzle aspect of Zombicide, this box earns a solid recommendation. If you’re new to Black Plague and you’re only curious about the campfire horror vibe, you’ll still find a lot to love, but you may want to pair it with a smaller expansion first to acclimate to the pacing.

### Final Recommendation
- If you want a thematically rich, visually striking, and mechanically engaging addition to your Black Plague games, pick up the Green Horde Special Guest Box. It’s a polished release that respects both veteran players and newcomers.
- If you’re chasing extreme difficulty with a relentless enemy wave, you’ll likely find this box to be a satisfying challenge that rewards teamwork and careful planning.
- If you’re after pure quantity of miniatures, there are other offerings that stack more models in a single package, but the Green Horde’s quality and thematic coherence deliver a more satisfying overall experience.
