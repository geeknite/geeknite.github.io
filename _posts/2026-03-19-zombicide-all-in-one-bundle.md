## Gameplay Mechanics and Flow (in practice)
Zombicide is a dance of cooperation where the table negotiates risk while a swarm of undead presses in from all sides. The All-in-One Bundle preserves the core rhythm and adds enough variety to keep the dance from turning into a simple two-step shuffle.

- Turn structure: On your turn you take a series of actions with your survivor, including moving, searching, trading with teammates, using gear, or engaging a nearby zombie. The number of actions per turn is limited, which forces you to prioritize and coordinate with your teammates. It is a satisfying test of planning and communication because one wrong door choice can escalate quickly into a room full of danger.
- Zombie AI and spawn dynamics: Zombies come alive through a deck-based or round-based system that escalates tension as the game progresses. The All-in-One Bundle integrates multiple zombie archetypes; some are fast and nimble, others are heavily armored, and a few are simply unstoppable if you do not manage the terrain correctly. Adapting your plan on the fly is essential.
- Gear and loot: Collecting equipment expands your tactical options significantly. Some gear gives direct combat advantages, while other items enable more flexible movement or scavenging options. The synergy between gear and actions creates moments where a well-timed search or a carefully placed trap can swing a near-disaster into a narrow victory.
- Objectives and campaigns: Many missions rely on completing specific goals, such as activating a sequence, retrieving a key item, or holding a location for a number of rounds. With the All-in-One Bundle, the objectives can be varied across missions, which helps to minimize repeat play and keeps the narrative feeling fresh.

The experience scales well with player count. With more players, the table benefits from more action economy and more opportunities for teamwork. With fewer players, you will experience more intense turns and a quicker sense of urgency, which can be both thrilling and a touch exhausting. If you are playing with a consistent group, you can develop a rhythm and a shared shorthand that makes even complicated missions feel manageable after a couple of sessions.
