What is the Eternal 10 Person Team Workstation?

In plain terms, this is a modular, multi-user workstation built to accommodate 10 simultaneous users. It is not a single desk with a row of chairs; it is a compact ecosystem where ten humans can do ten different types of work while sharing a single wall of screens, a big central whiteboard, and enough USB-C ports to power a small spaceship. The design philosophy is straightforward: share, segment, and still respect individual work styles. In practice, that translates into a row of linked workstations, a shared monitor wall, ten individual monitor arms, and a central spine that contains power, data, and a dramatic sense of belonging. The result looks like a futuristic classroom that finally grew up and learned to brew coffee that doesn’t taste like burnt circuit boards.

Images are worth a thousand words, so feast your eyes on the hero image above and two additional angles below. The visuals hint at the product’s soul: clean lines, adaptable surfaces, and a cable management system that behaves like a butler with OCD.

Core features and what they actually do

- Modular benches for up to 10 people: A single frame with multiple add-on modules that snap together like legos for grown-ups. Each seat gets its own space without feeling crammed. The modules can be reconfigured in minutes for standups, pair programming, or late-night hack-fests.
- Integrated power spine and USB-C hubs: Every module has access to power without the chaos of random extension cords. The spine consolidates power, ethernet, and data so you aren’t chasing a power strip around the room like a techno ninja.
- Shared monitor wall and private monitor arms: A central wall of screens gives teams a shared context, while each desk still has its own ergonomic monitor setup. No more turning to ask a neighbor to read the tiny font on a remote mockup.
- Built-in whiteboard and acoustic panels: The walls are not just walls; they are writable canvases. Acoustic panels help mute the ambient symphony of collaboration, which, frankly, sometimes sounds like a crowd cheering when you type a semicolon correctly.
- Height-adjustable desks and ergonomic considerations: Standing, sitting, or a glorious hybrid middle ground—your body gets to pick. The height range accommodates your tallest data scientist and your shortest intern with equal dignity.
- Cable management that pretends to be a magical forest: Cables disappear into channels, ducts, and grommets that make the room look tidy enough to present to clients without a disclaimer that you live in a cyclone of adapters.
- Lighting and climate features: Subtle LED lighting and climate management to keep the mood chill and the workstation souvenir-free from sweat and the glow of a neon sign that says you tried.
- Integration with collaboration tools: This is not just furniture; it’s a platform. Integrations with common collaboration workflows help teams stay in sync without a dozen browser tabs sneaking into every shared screen moment.

Tech and compatibility notes

- Power and data spine: Expect universal compatibility with standard office power outlets and most common docking setups. It plays nicely with USB-C hubs and displays spanning multiple resolutions. If your team uses fancy GPUs, you’ll appreciate the bandwidth this spine can deliver to the central monitor wall without turning the room into a power-limited dishwasher.
- Monitor wall options: The wall can be configured with a fixed array or an adjustable grid. If you like to push the envelope, you can add more panels or swap in larger displays for design reviews or spread-out code sessions. The mounting system handles weight gracefully, with a tilt range that saves you from neck pretzels.
- Connectivity: Expect decent Bluetooth/wired peripheral support, with enough ports to satisfy keyboards, mice, drawing tablets, and the occasional VR controller for team standups when the mood hits. You won’t be fighting for ports like you do with a cheap desk from the bargain bin.
- Audio/communication: Built-in, non-cringe conferencing mics and speakers aren’t a given, but there is ample space and provisions for mid-range audio setups. If your team loves open mic sessions or kinetic standups, you’ll want to add a proper mic array; the space is generous enough to do so without turning the room into a feedback festival.

Setup scenarios: who is this for?

- The Standup Brigade: Ten people, ten standups, one wall of screens. This setup is designed to keep daily standups concise, focused, and free of pile-on interruptions. The shared screen wall ensures everyone is aligned without the need for mic cream and sugar to sweeten the meeting.
- The Pair Programming Polis: Strategies that support two-person work at a time while still leaving room for others to pivot into other tasks. You can reconfigure modules quickly to create two-person pods for deep-dives, then snap back to a full ten-person bench for reviews.
- The Design and Review Guild: When the team’s design review needs a broad canvas, the wall of monitors becomes a collaborative canvas for designers, PMs, and developers. The whiteboard walls are perfect for rapid ideation with visual data while peers critique and iterate in real-time.
- The Remote Collective: If some teammates are remote, you can dedicate a portion of the monitor wall to a video conference tile while the rest of the room handles code reviews and live demos. It’s not a perfect telepresence, but it’s a strong upgrade from shouting across a conference table.
