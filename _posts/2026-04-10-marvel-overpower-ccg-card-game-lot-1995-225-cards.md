## Practical Play: House Rules and an Uncomplicated Night Plan
The 1995 OverPower era thrived on a cinematic vibe rather than ruthless balance. That makes it ideal for a laid-back game night with friends who love comics, not calculus. Here’s a simple, low-friction plan to get a session going without a year-long rulebook study:

- Start with a 30- to 40-card deck limit to keep things snappy. This prevents one card from taking over the entire match and keeps luck and synergy in healthy balance.
- Use a common power resource model: players draw cards, gain a limited pool of energy each turn, and spend it on abilities that matter in the moment. Don’t sweat every edge-case text; focus on the feel—dramatic moments, big hits, dramatic art panels in your head.

## Storage, Display, and Preservation Guidance for the Long Haul
If you intend to keep this lot for years to come, a little forethought goes a long way:

- Sleeve the cards in clear standard sleeves to protect edges during play. If you’re shy about sleeves, at least consider a gentle card protector approach for the high-value Special Cards.
- Use a labeled storage box with dividers: separate Heroes, Villains, Special Cards, and Tactics. It makes deck-building nights painless and less like archaeology.
- Keep in a cool, dry place away from sunlight. The mid-90s palette looks best in lower light anyway, and you’ll extend the colors’ lifespan.
- For display: a shallow, glass-front case or a simple wall-mounted shelf with collectors’ acrylic stands lets you present the art without constantly handling the cards. If you’re the type who likes to geek out with themed displays, this is your moment.

The goal is to balance admiration with practical playability. If you can preserve the art and still enjoy it in a casual match, you’ve hit the sweet spot of a retro collection.
