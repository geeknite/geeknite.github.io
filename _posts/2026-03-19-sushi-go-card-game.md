## Rules Snapshot: How the Drafting Works (Extended)
Sushi Go Card Game is built on a few clean, repeatable moves. Here’s a concise blueprint so you can teach the game in under 60 seconds—without sacrificing the jokes. We’ll expand the lens a bit to help you ride the wave of the little decisions that create big smiles.

### Setup and Turn Structure
- Each player starts with a hand of cards (the exact number scales with the number of players, but the core idea remains: you never have to pick the same card twice in a round).
- On your turn, you select one card from your current hand and place it face-down in front of you. Then you pass the remaining cards to the next player. This continues until all cards in the hand have been claimed.
- After everyone has picked, cards for that round are revealed and scored according to the dish-specific scoring rules. Then a new round begins with a fresh hand, and the passing direction flips for the next round. The flip is the mechanic that creates tension—your plan must adapt as the table’s dynamics shift.

### Scoring and Endgame, Revisited
- The scoring system is rooted in a handful of dish types: some award points for collecting multiple copies (like a set of dumplings), some reward specialized combos (nigiri bowls, maki rolls), and some penalize or reward pudding in a way that punishes early triumphs and rewards late-game play.
- The pudding card acts as a late-round strategic lever: it’s worth points at the end, but often tricky to time, because it requires you to balance risk and reward across the rounds.
- The endgame is quick. Most players can complete a match in about 15 to 30 minutes, depending on their familiarity with the cards and the number of players. The short game length makes it ideal for social gatherings where attention spans waver but good vibes persist.

### What Counts as a “Good” Move? Deeper Dive
- Tempo matters a lot. If you’re sprinting toward a set, you may overextend and miss out on more valuable combos later. Conversely, if you’re too cautious, your opponents will assemble the high-point dishes while you’re left with a handful of filler cards.
- Reading others’ intentions is a big part of the game’s fun. You’ll often be sure that one player is chasing a particular eating plan, while another is trying to foil them with a rare card here or there. The challenge isn’t just about picking your own cards; it’s about predicting the table’s evolving appetite.
- There is no hidden information beyond what you see in your own hand; the social aspect is where the spice hides: clever banter, playful threats about next round, and the occasional mock-serious critique of someone’s “fusion roll” choice.

If you want a quick mental checklist you can reference during play, imagine this: Do I have a plan that can adapt if the table pivots? Can I secure at least one strong scoring dish this round? Is there a card that could slightly derail someone else’s plan without harming my own prospects too much? If you can answer yes to two of these three questions, you’re likely in for a good round.

## Practical Strategies for Different Playstyles (Expanded)
Sushi Go Card Game accommodates a spectrum of playing styles—from chaos-loving casuals to methodical optimizers. Here are some practical strategies to help you find your own voice at the table.

### The Treasure Hunter (Aggressive, High-Reward Sets)
- Focus on cards that form big-point sets in larger totals. This often means prioritizing certain dish cards early on and leveraging the round’s passing order to deny others access to those same cards.
- Don’t be afraid to pivot away from a plan if another player signals a more valuable combination is forming. Flexibility is your friend here.
- Use pudding timing to your advantage: if you’re behind on pudding in the early rounds, swing the round’s focus toward a late scoring purchase to boost your score before the pudding cards come into play.

### The Quiet Calculator (Balanced, Subtle Scoring)
- Aim for consistent, moderate-point dishes while keeping an eye on the pudding pile. The power of steady, safe scoring compounds over rounds and rarely triggers dramatic table talk.
- Prioritize diversity over abundance: you don’t want to fill your score ledger with a dozen small gains that are offset by a single large loss. A few well-chosen dish cards can outpace a sea of minor points.
- Be mindful of your passing direction: a lot of the game’s tension comes from the moment you decide to pass a card that could help your neighbor more than you. Learning table rhythm matters as much as card math.

### The Social Optimizer (Low-Conflict, High Vibe)
- The goal is to keep the table smiling while still winning. Choose cards that create satisfying combos for you and encourage playful banter, rather than ones that feel purely punitive.
- Use humor as a resource. A quick laugh or a playful accusation about who has the “best sushi identity” can lighten the mood and actually help your own strategic clarity—when the laughter dies down, you’ll still have your cards.
- Remember: the best strategies often come from watching people adapt to your moves, not from being mercilessly efficient. Sushi Go rewards social flow as much as math.
