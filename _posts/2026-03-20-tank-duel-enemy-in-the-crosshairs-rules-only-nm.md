## What is Tank Duel? The battlefield, the behemoths, and the rules
Tank Duel is a two-player micro-war game played on a square grid. Each player commands a single, stylized tank with a handful of movement actions, attack options, and a dilemma of whether to push forward or to retreat behind the smoke of the cardboard hills. The core promise is simple enough: maneuver to line up a clean shot, resolve damage, and reduce the opponent’s health to zero before they do the same to you. In practice, though, the rulebook reads like a high-stakes treasure map that occasionally leads you to a bookshelf rather than a victory square.

The “Rules Only” label (NM) is a cheeky reminder that this edition leans hard into the mechanical backbone of the game. There’s no lore dump, no cinematic cutscenes, and no dramatic speeches before you roll. It’s just you, the board, and a cascade of numbers telling you when you can yaw, pivot, and fire. If you’re into the poetry of tabletop wargaming, you’ll need to supply your own. If you’re into deterministic, efficient, and occasionally furious combat, you’re in the right place.

## Core rules overview: the bones you won’t forget (unless you drink coffee first)
The rules are built around a few core pillars: movement, line of sight, attack resolution, and damage tracking. Because Tank Duel embraces a “rules first” stance, you’ll find that exceptions and edge cases exist, but they are not there to trip you; they’re there to keep the game from being as simple as a coin toss and as predictable as a toaster.

- Movement: Each tank gets a set number of action points per turn. You can spend these points to move, pivot, or perform a special maneuver. The grid limits your freedom, which is to say you can’t pretend to be Optimus Prime and go from one edge of the board to the other in a single, elegant leap.
- Line of Sight: Fire is only possible if you can see your target. Occlusion from terrain shapes, dunes, and the occasional cardboard bunker creates interesting risk calculations. The mental image is a tank pushing through a field of impossible angles and careful estimations, like moonwalking through a physics diagram.
- Range and Attack: Range bands determine how hard you hit. If you overextend, you risk missing the shot entirely or landing a glancing blow that does minimal damage. The dice add a satisfying friction when you roll a crit, a miss, or a roll that lands somewhere vaguely in the middle: a reminder that probability loves to mess with your best-laid plans.
- Armor and Damage: Tanks have armor ratings that subtract from incoming damage. If you’re lucky, you’ll shrug off a significant portion of the enemy shell. If not, you’ll watch hit markers dance across your sheet like a holographic weather forecast predicting doom.
- End of Turn: Track both the damage and the state of your turret, fuel, and radius. The rulebook emphasizes the importance of careful bookkeeping, which may feel like a party foul in a casual game night, but is a fair trade for the sense of accomplishment when you actually tally all the numbers without a calculator.

As you can tell, the game rewards careful planning and punishes reckless bravado with the same efficiency as a well-placed minefield (metaphorically speaking, of course).

## NM Variant: No Mercy, Just Rules (and maybe some sarcasm)
The “NM” in the title is not just a cute abbreviation; it’s a distinct play mode that emphasizes strict adherence to the rules and minimizes house rules or improvised house-call adjudication. In practice, NM means:

- No narrative fluff: you won’t have a backstory about your tank commander’s tragic past or a grandiose speech before the battle. There are no cinematic monologues. It’s pure, unadulterated semantics and geometry.
- Strict action economy: you’ll be given a fixed pool of actions per turn. The moment you try to bend the system, the game politely reminds you that bending is another word for exploiting, which is frowned upon in NM.
- No mercy on mistakes: if you miscount range, you miscount. If you forget to apply a modifier, you reset the situation, as the rulebook intended. It’s the kind of regime that makes you appreciate the difference between “almost correct” and “correct enough to count.”
- Learning curve, but in a friendly way: you’ll likely stumble, recalibrate, and eventually find a rhythm that looks suspiciously like professional chess—if chess had cannons and the chessboard was a battlefield that smelled faintly of burnt plastic.

In this mode, the rules feel like they were written by a meticulous librarian who also collects model tanks. If you love precision and can handle the occasional eye-twitch when you realize you forgot to rotate your turret by 45 degrees, NM is the regime for you.

## Play experience: pacing, tension, and the tiny squeaks of victory
The pacing is deliberate. The first few turns tend to feel a bit stilted as players map out the opening gambits, but once the board begins to resemble a chessboard with heavier artillery, the tempo accelerates. The tension comes not just from counting damage but from the geometry of the map. You might think you have your opponent cornered, only to discover that a single hex with a hill provides the perfect blind spot for a sneaky counterattack.

If you’re into the drama of micro-decisions, Tank Duel delivers. You’ll pause to recalibrate a line of sight, you’ll debate whether a diagonal move is worth sacrificing an attack, and you’ll curse the moment your opponent rolls a critical hit that invalidates your carefully laid plan. It’s the tabletop equivalent of a tense standoff in a sci-fi western, complete with dust, sparks, and the soft clatter of dice bouncing across the table.

Humor has a seat at the table too. The game doesn’t pretend to be grimdark; it leans into the absurdity of armored squids with big cannons on toy-scale landscapes. That paradox is part of its charm: the higher the stakes, the more ridiculous the board looks. If you’re the sort who laughs when your miniature tank tips over because the terrain card was “bumpy asteroid,” you’ll feel right at home.

## Strategy and tactics: how to tilt the odds in your favor
Two-player strategy in Tank Duel boils down to three core dimensions: positioning, tempo, and resource management. Here are practical tips that will actually help you win more fights while avoiding the “learn-by-emergency-slapstick” vibe:

- Positioning matters more than brute force. A few well-chosen moves can force your opponent into a corner where their options shrink dramatically. Look for terrain features that block line of sight in predictable ways, not just the shortest path to your target.
- Resource discipline wins. Don’t spend moves on fancy maneuvers that don’t meaningfully improve your chances of scoring a hit. In NM, that discipline is even more important because every action must be justified by the rules.
- Manage your damage tokens carefully. If you’re using a version with damage tracks or shields, you’ll want to avoid overextending your defense. A single misstep early on can cost you the entire match.
- Read your opponent. A lot of Tank Duel’s tension comes from bluffing and misdirection. If your opponent projects confidence, they may be parsing your intentions; you can use that to your advantage by feigning weakness or promising a risky feint that actually pays off.
