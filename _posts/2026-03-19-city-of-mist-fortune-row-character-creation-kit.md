## What Fortune Row Is (and Isn’t)
Fortune Row is a curated collection of character-creation prompts, templates, and layout choices designed to speed up how you spawn a new Mist, a new Mask, and a new chance to scream I am Myth, therefore I am inevitable at the tabletop. It’s not a complete, all-in-one game system; City of Mist already has that under its hood with Logos and Mythos and the Masks you love to argue about. Fortune Row, instead, is a toolkit. A well-organized, beautifully designed toolkit with sections that feel like they were blessed by a librarian who also plays D&D on the side.

### What’s inside the kit
- A clean, modular character sheet in printable form and a companion digital version.
- A compact glossary of archetypes and motifs tailored to official CoM vibes.
- Quick-start prompts for a first session (including a ready-to-run fortune moment).
- Relationship webs and clue seeds to weave mystery, not merely to fill a stat block.
- Optional prompts for rotating NPCs you meet along Fortune Row’s city-ward streets.
- A few GM aids, like session hooks, scene prompts, and a couple of memory shards to tie back into a player’s Mill/Mask paradox.

### What it isn’t
- It isn’t a complete campaign or a replacement for the core rulebook.
- It isn’t a magic bullet that guarantees a perfectly balanced party by the time you roll initiative.
- It isn’t littered with filler art you’ll regret later (though you’ll still want the art; it’s pretty).

## How It Fits the City of Mist System
City of Mist runs on the tension between Logos (human-made order) and Mythos (mythic forces). Your Masks—those edgy identities you borrow for a night—sit at the intersection, letting you chase a personal legend while being pulled by the city’s powerful shadows. Fortune Row leans into that dynamic by providing prompts that nudge you toward a Mythos meets Logos moment in every scene. It’s not just about what’s your Mask; it’s about what does this Mask owe to the city, and what does the city owe to this Mask?

### Archetypes and motif families
Fortune Row organizes archetypes into readable families with ready-made stimuli for both player and GM. The prompts encourage players to think about what power means to them and what cost they’re willing to pay. The kit also helps GMs by giving you a library of motifs that feel thematically tight with City of Mist’s vibe: power, secrecy, memory, and what happens when legends forget their human edges. If you’ve ever felt that your party’s Masks are a little too on-the-nose or a little too vague, Fortune Row gives you a sharper blade of direction without squeezing your creativity.

### Mask mechanics in practice
In the core game, Masks unlock a certain range of abilities and social leverage by letting players lean into their hidden identities. Fortune Row emphasizes two practical outcomes: speed and flavor. It strips away some of the dead-end prompts and replaces them with crisp, easily digestible lines like your Mask thrives on secrecy, but the city thrives on your every misstep. It’s not about turning you into a min-max machine; it’s about guiding you toward a story-locked identity with built-in consequences, hooks, and a few delightful questions for future sessions.

## Character Creation Flow: From Concept to City-Claim
If you’ve ever watched a speedrun of character creation and thought, it looks fun but I’m scared I’ll end up with nothing but a specialty roll, Fortune Row has your back. Here’s a typical flow you’ll experience when you open the kit and actually start making a character.

### Step 1: Concept and Archetype pick
You start by choosing a concept that feels like a neon sign in a foggy alley. The kit provides short, punchy archetypes like Haunted Investigator, Velvet Rook, Reality-Anchored Shaman, and more. The objective is to pick something that sparks a story hook right away, not something that guarantees you’re the most powerful character in the room. Bonus points for choosing an archetype that sparks a clash with a known Mythos in your city.

### Step 2: Noun-Verb expansion (identity + power focus)
Fortune Row uses a simple two-column approach: Noun (Identity) and Verb (Power focus). The Noun describes what the Mask clings to in the world (a profession, a secret, a city landmark), while the Verb describes what the Mask does in mythic terms (bends lies, controls weather, steals time). This step is where flavor meets function. A good Noun might be Archivist and a good Verb might be unravel lies. Suddenly your Mask is less about I shoot a thing and more about I untangle the truth of a city’s façade.

### Step 3: Memory and Spark prompts
City of Mist is obsessed with memory—who remembers you, and by extension, who forgets you when the night ends. Fortune Row pushes you to define a Memory that ties your Mask to a moment in the city’s mythic past. A Spark (often a short, idealistic belief or a flawed assumption you’ll have to question) gives you a personal arc that keeps you connected to the plot threads you’ll chase during the campaign. The prompts work like a tiny Mr. Peanutbutter of narrative: sweet, a little chaotic, but unexpectedly essential to the story you’re about to tell.

### Step 4: Relationships and clues network
What really makes City of Mist sing is the web of relationships—who you trust, who hates you, who owes you a favor, and which ancient shadow is whispering in the corner. Fortune Row offers ready-made relationship templates to speed this up, plus clue seeds you can drop into scenes. You’ll end up with a quick map of who matters and what emotional leverage you can pull without needing a complicated spreadsheet and a blackboard that’s too small for your ambition.

### Step 5: Mask fit and playability
The kit checks whether your Mask makes sense within your group’s playstyle. It encourages you to test how your Power Focus and your Memory conflict with your relationships. The result is a Mask that’s cohesive on paper and alive at the table because it breathes through the scenes you actually run.

## Playability: Speed, Balance, and Table Feel
Fortune Row shines in three practical arenas: speed, balance, and table feel. You’ll get up to speed faster than a coffee-fueled spider-moss conspiracy in your brain. The prompts are designed to avoid the classic pitfall of a too-strong Mask that steamrolls the scene or a too-weak Mask that becomes wall decoration.

- Speed: You don’t waste precious minutes debate-drafting your character’s backstory. The kit gives you enough scaffolding to get into the action quickly. If you’ve been in a session where character creation dragged longer than the actual game sessions, Fortune Row feels like a relief valve.
- Balance: It’s not magic-bullet balance, but it helps you avoid egregious power gaps across Masks by nudging players toward narrative-driven design rather than raw dice-pushing. The prompts encourage risk vs. reward thinking and risk-aware decisions that align with the city’s themes.
- Table Feel: The design aesthetics—clear typography, flowing prompts, and tasteful art—make the table feel alive. You’ll want to show off your character sheet and flip through the pages like a well-loved comic. The kit understands that the visual language matters almost as much as the mechanics.

## Artwork, Layout, and Accessibility
Fortune Row isn’t just a tool; it’s a well-crafted experience. The layout is clean, with generous margins and a color scheme that reads as noir neon without causing eye strain. The artwork complements the mood without overshadowing substance. The printable sheets are high-contrast, and the digital version integrates smoothly with common PDF readers and tablet devices.

One notable thing: the design team shaded every option with a little flavor text that hints at deeper lore. It’s not just you are a detective but the night breathes in your ear and asks you to choose: truth or mercy. That voice matters when you’re trying to spark a session that doesn’t spin out in the first five minutes.

## Practical Use: Running Fortune Row in Your Campaign
If you’re a GM already running City of Mist, a Fortune Row session is a delicious side-quest that can seed your main arc or serve as a one-shot breather between bigger stories. Here are practical ideas to maximize its value:

- Use Fortune Row as a pre-campaign character-creation night: Invite players to craft their Masks using the kit, then start the actual campaign with those players stepping into a noir mystery already thick with personal stakes.
- Run Fortune Row as a table-topper: A 2–3 hour session where every player creates a character, and you, as GM, drop a fresh clue that connects to your ongoing Mythos. It’s a great way to accelerate a new group and inject momentum into a campaign that’s stalling.
- Tie-in with existing arcs: Use Fortune Row prompts to reinterpret old characters into new Masks that unlock different facets of the city. Sometimes changing the lens on a familiar face makes the old secrets feel new again.

GM-facing tips:
- Keep an eye on distribution of clues and memory shards. If one player collects too many mythic tokens, don’t be afraid to nudge the table toward collaborative storytelling rather than a duel of wates.
- Embrace ambiguity. Fortune Row isn’t trying to fix every ambiguity in your city; it’s trying to provide enough seeds for you to see new shadows in familiar streets.
- Use relationship webs as ongoing anchors. Revisit them every few sessions to keep the social map alive and dynamic.

## A Quick Playtest Scene (Mini-Exemplar)
To illustrate Fortune Row in action, here’s a compact example of a birth scene for a made-up Mask, The Velvet Archivist.

- Concept: An archivist who can pull memories from documents, but each retrieval costs a personal memory.
- Noun-Verb: Identity: Archivist; Power: Extract truth from proofs and lies.
- Memory: The day a city library burned down and erased a family’s history.
- Spark: If I forget, I forget the city’s pain; if I remember, I bear the weight of it.
- Relationship network: A rival investigator who wants the archives for power, a librarian who guards the last intact memory, a mythic figure who wants to silence the past.

In a 2-hour session using the Fortune Row prompts, you can watch the Mask grapple with a clue that reveals a forgotten truth about the city’s foundation. The tension arises not from a gunfight, but from a memory being tethered to a memory shard that can alter everyone’s understanding of who the city truly is. That is the kind of session Fortune Row aspires to enable: a narrative loop where imagination meets myth and identity.

Now imagine running a full campaign where every chapter starts with a Fortune Row moment: The Spark meets the Memory and reveals a new facet of the city’s mask. It’s not a feat of mechanics; it’s a moment of storytelling that you’ll remember long after you’ve shelved the sheet.

Short answer: yes, if you want faster setup without sacrificing mood. If you’re a GM who’s tired of mentoring new players through the same character-creation pain points or a player who wants a more guided, story-forward approach to Masks, Fortune Row is a strong addition to your toolkit.
