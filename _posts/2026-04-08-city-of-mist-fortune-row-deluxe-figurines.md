## City of Mist: Fortune Row Deluxe Figurines – A Review That Shines Neon and Shadow

City of Mist is a game built on a neon noir heartbeat. The Fortune Row line, a Deluxe Character Figurines set, promises to bring that heartbeat to your table in three dimensions. This review dives into the box, the look, the feel, the potential uses, and whether the price tag translates into lasting value for your games nights.

Unboxing is a ritual in this hobby, and Fortune Row understands the assignment. The box lid opens with a confident click, and the contents settle into a carefully designed foam cradle that feels less like packaging and more like a treasure chest for adults who still pretend to be pirates when the dice are hot. The six core figures come with extra bases, and a slim deck of character cards that reference the Fortune Row lore lines. The cards are slim yet sturdy, the fonts are legible, and the art on the cards mirrors the miniatures themselves so you can instantly match the prompt to the figure without squinting at tiny print under a lamp that’s glare-happy.

Design and Sculpting: The Story Told in Curves and Angles

The sculpting is the true star here. The designers injected a lot of personality into the silhouettes. One figure exudes suave menace with a sly tilt of the head, another channels a charismatic chaos that feels like a punchy line of dialogue before the action starts. The mix of classic noir silhouettes with modern streetwear yields a lineup that reads well from across the table and reveals new details up close. The trench-coat folds, the glint of a high-tech gauntlet, the subtle scuff marks on boots—all of these tell a story without a single spoken line. The occasional minor imperfection is there, but it reads as character rather than flaw. It’s a reminder that these are crafted by humans with pencils and files, not by factory robots who dream of coffee and lunch breaks.

Materials: Weight, Finish, and Handling

The Deluxe Figurines use a premium resin blend that feels substantial in the hand. The weight gives a satisfying heft that makes you feel like you’ve earned the right to narrate a midnight chase, while not so heavy that you fear dropping one during a tabletop monologue. The finish includes a satin coat that reduces glare on bright table lamps and helps the shadows in a figure read as intended. You may notice rare hotspots where light catches a highlight just a beat too much, but nothing that disrupts the overall mood. In a product line that values mood over micro-detail, this is a deliberate choice that pays dividends on the gaming surface.

Paint Jobs and Customization Potential

The stock paint jobs are clean and crisp, with good color separation and tasteful shading. The palette leans into noir: deep blues, shadowy grays, coppery metallics, and the occasional enamel red for emphasis. For hobbyists who love to push the limits, there’s ample room for advanced shading, weathering on leather, and fine freehand insignia on coats. Modders can magnetize weapons or swap out accessory components with standard 25/28 mm bases, though Fortune Row isn’t designed as a modular kit in the sense of swapable limbs. It is, however, friendly enough for your personal touch to shine through—your paintbrush becomes a brush with destiny.

Bases, Stands, and Stability

The bases are compatible with standard 25 mm and 28 mm configurations. They’re flat enough to sit snug on most tables but textured enough to prevent that suspicious, dramatic wobble when someone places a die down with too much swagger. If you want to go full diorama mode, you can mount the figures on a shallow display stand to create a layered cityscape, or pair them with a small backdrop to simulate alleyways. They’re not pre-magnetized, but the community response suggests a quick mod or two will unlock some sweet display options without turning your hobby corner into a workshop.

Display Photography and Lighting Tips

If you’re into photos, Fortune Row photographs beautifully with simple lighting. A modest side light creates dynamic shadows that sculpt the characters’ faces, while a soft top light keeps coats from looking flat. Use a neutral backdrop with a little texture to avoid competing with the paint. For those who want to push the mood, a warm amber glow can evoke a streetlamp scene, while a cool blue wash can transform the figures into silhouettes of a midnight chase. With a tripod and a couple of reflective boards, you’ll be posting table-ready shots in minutes.
