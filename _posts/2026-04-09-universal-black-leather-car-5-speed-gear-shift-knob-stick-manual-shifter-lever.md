## Compatibility and Fitment Notes
Universal does not mean one-size-fits-all perfection, which is a relief for reality-starved gear heads. The main challenge with universal gear shift knobs is thread compatibility and knob diameter. Most manual cars use one of a handful of common thread sizes for the shift knob thread, often M10x1.5 or M12x1.25, though there are many odd ducks in the wild.

What this means in practice:
- Expect adapters to be included that cover a wide range of thread sizes
- You may need to remove the original knob and measure the post to be sure you have the correct adapter
- Some cars with unusual shift patterns or console configurations may not be comfortable with a universal knob

To avoid the dread of a misfit, check the product listing for a compatibility table and the adapter kit contents. If your car is famous for oddball threads, measure, compare, and don’t be shy about asking the seller questions. It is not cheating to ask questions; it’s engineering science in action.

## Installation: A Quick, Not-So-Dirty DIY
Installing a universal gear knob is a two-to-three step affair, depending on how comfortable you are with your own two hands and how many parts the adapter kit contains. Here is a typical workflow:

1) Remove the old knob. This is usually a simple unscrewing process. Some cars require an Allen key or a simple twist to release the knob post. If you feel like you discovered a centuries-old problem when you stripped the retaining nut, you are not alone.
2) Clean the post threads. A quick wipe with isopropyl alcohol ensures the new knob seats properly. It’s not glamorous, but it’s the adult thing to do.
3) Select the right adapter. Use the included adapters to match your thread size. If your car has the oddball post, there is usually at least one adapter that fits. If not, you might be dealing with a non-standard configuration that should be considered a software update, not a hardware one.
4) Thread the new knob on. Do not over-tighten; you want snug, not a stubborn joint that will strip threads. If you hear a squeak or feel resistance, back off and re-check alignment. Misalignment is the single most common reason for wobble and chatter.
5) Test the shifts. A couple of gentle practice runs in a quiet area will reveal any misalignment or binding. If you hear grinding or feel lockout, stop and re-check your adapter and seating.

Helpful tip: keep the car in park and rev the engine a little while testing to ensure there’s no interference with the shifter boot or nearby panels. You do not want to discover a new squeak mid-canyon run because your knob is rubbing against something in the dash.

This process is designed to take under an hour for an average enthusiast. If you’re a perfectionist, you’ll likely spend a bit more time dialing in the torque and the height to get that perfect throw. The key is patience and a little bit of curiosity about how your car was built to work in the first place.

## Pro Tips and Tricks
- Always verify thread size before buying. The universal claim is good, but your car might be a quirky cousin in the family.
- If you’re installing in a car with a tight console, consider temporarily loosening the boot to avoid scuffing. Small steps prevent large regrets.
- Don’t overtighten. The fear of a loose knob is real, but overtightening can strip threads and ruin the upgrade faster than you can say shift.
- Use a soft cloth to wipe the surface regularly. Leather is forgiving, but not invincible; moisture and grime are its worst enemies.
- If your car has a leather-wrapped gear boot, test the knob with the boot on to ensure no rubbing or binding occurs during full throw. A good kit should not require you to rip parts of the interior to fit.
