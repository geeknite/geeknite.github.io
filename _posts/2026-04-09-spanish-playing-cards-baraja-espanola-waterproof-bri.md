## H2: The Anatomy of the Baraja Española
### H3: Suits, numbers, and face cards—what actually lives in the deck
Baraja Española is beloved for its elegant simplicity and distinctive look. Each suit sports cards that have a bold, almost heraldic design: the oros (gold coins) deck tends to be bright and warm, copas bring a splash of cobalt or wine-red hues, espadas carry sword imagery with dramatic edge, and bastos are sturdy, almost practical-looking clubs. The pips and numerals are large, making the cards friendlier to players with aging eyes or those who shuffle with sweaty palms after a heated Brisca round.

The numbered cards, depending on the variant, go from 1 through 7, followed by 10, 11, and 12. The court cards—sota (jack), caballo (knight), and rey (king)—convey a charming mix of medieval and deck-building fantasy vibes, as if a royal court decided to go medieval-meets-gaming-night at the plaza. Unique to the Baraja are the 8s and 9s that appear in some versions but are typically excluded in the 40-card variants used in many Spanish-speaking regions. This is not a “one deck fits all” situation; the deck exists in regional flavors, and your particular set will look and feel slightly different depending on where you bought it.

### H3: How the deck compares to a standard 52-card French deck
If you’ve learned games on a traditional 52-card deck in a Western context, switching to the Baraja Española may feel strange at first.
