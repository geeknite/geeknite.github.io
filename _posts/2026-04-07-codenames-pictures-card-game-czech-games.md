## How to Play: Quick Start (the flow you’ll repeat all night)
Setting up is straightforward. Lay out 25 picture cards in a 5x5 grid. Decide which team goes first and hand the Spymaster role to one member of that team. The other team’s Spymaster will provide clues on alternating turns. The key card designates which color corresponds to which side and where the assassin sits for the current round. The objective remains the same: reveal all of your own agents before the other team does. The pictures version replaces words with images, which is exactly the kind of chaos your brain loves when it sees a bicycle, a cactus, and a violin on the same card and says, Sure, I can tie those together with one word.

On your turn, the Spymaster gives a one-word clue followed by a number, just like in classic Codenames. The clue should relate to multiple picture cards on the table. For example, a clue like beak 2 might point to two bird-themed pictures, while a more surreal clue like Mars bar 1 could reference a card that combines Martian vibes with dessert imagery. Players then discuss and select cards one by one, attempting to find as many of their own team’s agents as possible without stepping on a landmine. Step onto the assassin card, and the round ends immediately in favor of the opposing team.

The Pictures variant leans into visual storytelling. Because each card shows a pair of images, a clue can connect several cards through shared themes like animals doing human things, or improbable crossovers that only exist in a coffee-fueled brainstorming session. It’s not about vocabulary bombs; it’s about pattern recognition, association, and sometimes sheer silliness.

> Tip: The 5x5 grid keeps everyone in the same field of view. It’s easy for a large crowd to participate, which is why Codenames Pictures shines at parties, family gatherings, and that one tabletop group that keeps bringing lunch-and-learn memes to game night.

## How the grid behaves under pressure (and how to read it like a pro)
In most play sessions, the grid becomes a living thing. Teams scramble for the few cards that carry the clue’s weight, while the other team tries to second-guess the spymaster’s intent. The tension ramps up faster than a crowd voting for the best pun, and that’s the secret sauce: rapid-fire decisions fueled by a handful of shared pop-culture associations.

The art direction and card variety play a big role here. Some cards scream out a clear connection; others lean on absurd juxtapositions that require a leap of imagination. The team that thrives under this ambiguity is the team that tolerates a healthy dose of chaos and keeps the conversation inclusive rather than sprinting toward a single “correct” interpretation.

The assassin card sits like the moment you realize your clue might have gone too far. It’s dramatic, it’s spicy, and it makes players consider whether the risk is worth the potential reward. Good players approach the assassin with caution, blending daring intuition with cautious skepticism about what their teammates can interpret from the next few pictures.

## Strategy tips and how to win (or at least not tank the round)
- Build a diverse clue strategy: due to the image-based design, using multi-item clues that rely on visual or thematic connections often pays off more quickly than long, wordy clues. Let the pictures do the heavy lifting whenever possible.
- Use small-number clues to keep control: you don’t always want to burn all your cards on one wild idea. A clue like animal 2 or instrument 1 can yield clean progress without overshooting.
- Respect the assassin: as with standard Codenames, giving a clue that nudges toward the assassin is a risky choice. If you can avoid it, do. If you must, couple it with a safer hint you know you can guide your team to from the previous turn.
- Read the room: the group’s sense of humor and shared references matter. Some tables love pop culture mash-ups, while others prefer cute animals in improbable outfits. Tailor your clues to what your teammates will happily discuss for the next few minutes.
- Don’t fear the weird: some cards will resist your best structured clue. Accept that and pivot. The game rewards creative leaps that still respect the pictures’ content.

### Examples of good and bad clues
- Good: space 3, if three cards depict astronauts, rockets, and aliens. The clue is directionally accurate and not too risky.
- Bad: pet 4, if one of the cards shows a penguin, two cards feature cats, and a third card is a dog wearing sunglasses. It’ll likely mislead more than it helps.

## House rules, variants, and how to tailor the chaos
- Team balance tweak: If you have a very large party, designate two spymasters per team for each round and rotate quickly to keep energy high.
- Hybrid night: Blend Codenames Pictures with a word-based Codenames round for a double feature. This gives players a break from visual clues and then a brain-burner of words.
- Time pressure variant: Use a soft timer for clue rounds to keep the pace brisk. The social contract is important here: everyone should have a turn to chime in before the timer buzzes.
- Family mode: Limit references to non-controversial, kid-friendly images. It keeps the night wholesome and prevents debates that derail the fun.

### Solo play and training (yes, you can practice alone)
While designed as a party game, you can run a mini-training session with one player acting as a coach and the other as a stand-in spymaster. Use a timer, and try to build a few robust clue sets that don’t rely on a shared in-joke. The goal is to internalize patterns of image connections and build a mental library for quick-fire rounds when you’re back with a crew.
