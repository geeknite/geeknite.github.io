## What is Phase 10
Phase 10 is a blend of luck, memory, and light math that somehow became a staple of family game night. Each player draws a card, discards a card, and attempts to complete one of ten specified phases. The phases ramp up in difficulty, from two sets of three to the full-on phase that requires a complete run of ten or more. The core mechanism relies on the shared draw pile and a discard pile, and the occasional skip or wild card to spice things up. The pre-owned 2012 Complete Set gives you the classic rules in a compact box that has aged gracefully, if not with the same glossy finish as a fresh print run. The charm lies in its accessibility: anyone from grandma to your nephew can grasp the concept in just a few minutes, and then the real fun begins as you watch your plans unravel in glorious chaos.

### Phase list overview
- Phase 1: 2 sets of 3
- Phase 2: 1 set of 3 + 1 run of 4
- Phase 3: 1 set of 4 + 1 run of 4
- Phase 4: Run of 5
- Phase 5: 2 sets of 5
- Phase 6: 1 set of 5 + 1 set of 3
- Phase 7: 1 set of 4 + 1 run of 4
- Phase 8: Run of 7
- Phase 9: Run of 8
- Phase 10: One 10-card hand of any valid combination

This is not just a list; it is a roadmap to the tournament of your living room, where every misfit card might become a hero or a zero depending on how you apply it.

### Turn structure and flow
The typical turn sequence includes drawing, discarding, and optionally claiming to be on a new phase. A successful completion of a phase means you move to the next phase; failure means you stay where you are and continue drawing until you can meet the requirement. The tension often centers on whether you should hold out for a Wild or risk a discarded card that might help a rival complete their own forced phase. In a pre-owned box from 2012, you may notice wear on Skip and Wild cards that makes them just a little more satisfying to flip, as you realize you’ve claimed control of a late moment with a well-timed move.

## Strategy and tips
A lot of the fun of Phase 10 comes from not just achieving the phase but in how you get there. Here are some practical tips:
- Early focus on flexible cards. In early phases, quickly building to multiple valid configurations can give you flexibility as cards cycle back.
- Read the room. If your table is full of watchful eyes, don’t telegraph every move. Subtle card exchanges or quiet discards can mislead rivals.
- Save the Wilds for late phases. You’ll often need the Wild to push into your final configuration when the table is hot on your tail.
- Skip with intention. Use Skip to slow someone who is closing in; use it sparingly so you don’t become a target.
- Phase-aware discards. Keep track of what colors tend to come up after a few rounds and use that knowledge to predict the next draws, especially for long runs.

### Quick cheat sheet for Phase 10 newbies
- Phase 1-3: Look for small sets and runs to quickly hit early bonuses.
- Phase 4-5: You’ll be juggling runs more than sets; color management becomes a key skill.
- Phase 6-8: You’ll start to lean into longer runs and more complex configurations.
- Phase 9-10: Final gambits require precise coordination or a lucky Wild card.
