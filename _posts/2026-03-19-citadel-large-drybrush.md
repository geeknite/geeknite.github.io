## What is a drybrush, and why does Citadel sell a Large one?
### The drybrush concept in plain terms
Dry brushing is a painting technique that relies on a brush with most of the paint wiped off, leaving only a trace on the bristles. You then drag that brush across raised details on a model or a piece of terrain. The result: highlights that pop, textures that look like they exist in a slightly dusty universe, and a sense of speed that makes you feel like you’ve hacked reality with a hobby knife. The Large Drybrush embraces the fundamental idea with gusto: fewer bristles for broader coverage, stiffer bristles to lift texture, and a handle that feels substantial enough to unite your inner Tolkien with your inner tech-wonk.

### The design philosophy of Citadel large format brushes
Citadel designed its Large Drybrush to strike a balance between coverage and control. The head is bigger than your standard detail brush, but not so monstrous that you start brushing your entire army with a single stroke. The bristles are stiffer than a coffee schedule after a long convention, which helps pick up paint on the raised edges without burying the details in color sludge. The ferrule—the metal ring that holds bristles in place—feels robust, a tiny piece of metal that promises to survive the occasional drop from your table, which is practically a rite of passage in the hobby. And the handle is short and comfortable, designed for the “grip and go” style of painting rather than the surgeon’s precision.

## How it performs in practice
### Dry brushing technique with the Citadel Large Drybrush
The technique is simple in theory: load the brush with paint, wipe most of it off on a tissue or paper towel, then brush across raised details. The trick is in the wiping step and the pressure you apply when you drag the brush across the surface. Your goal is to leave paint on the raised edges and avoid filling in the recessed lines. With a large brush, you can cover more area per pass, but you must watch your pressure to prevent blotching or smearing across the entire model. The Large Drybrush excels when you want broad, even highlights on textures like rivets, stone, and sculpted armor panel lines. The result can be a stage-ready highlight in fewer passes, which is perfect when you’re balancing painting time with binge-watching your favorite sci-fi show.

### Paint compatibility and color dynamics
Citadel paints (and most other acrylic hobby paints) work well with this brush. It handles mid-to-thick contrast paints and drys fast enough that you won’t learn to build a fully interactive computer game while waiting for it to dry. The brush does well with lighter highlight tones on darker base colors, and it can be used with metallics if you like an intentionally rough, industrial texture. To avoid muddy results, you’ll want to avoid using extremely runny washes with too much water on this particular brush; the goal of a drybrush is not to flood the surface but to lift the topmost details.

### The first wash and second coat test
When you first test the Large Drybrush, consider a small terrain piece or a veterans’ vehicle kit. Apply a drybrush on a raised texture after a base coat. Watch how the brush catches the edges and highlights the raised surfaces. If you’re not getting a crisp edge, you may be applying too much paint or not drying the bristles properly. The key is to be patient and to learn the brush's “personality.” Some days it invites you to sweep; other days it insists you take smaller, controlled passes. Embrace the brush’s rhythm, don’t fight it like a stubborn boss fight. ## Handling, comfort, and maintenance
### Feel and ergonomics during long sessions
The handle on the Large Drybrush is comfortable for long sessions. If you’ve ever spent hours wielding a brush the size of a tiny broom, you’ll appreciate the control this one offers. It’s not so heavy that your wrist falls asleep after a few minutes, and the grip is stable enough that you can do repetitive passes without the brush wandering off into a misaligned crater of paint. It’s the kind of brush that forgives your occasional over-enthusiasm with a sturdy ferrule and a bristle arrangement that resists splaying.

### Cleaning and maintenance tips
- Rinse thoroughly with lukewarm water after use.
- Gently reshape the bristles with your fingers. Do not pull on the bristles to “straighten” them; you’ll just damage the tip.
- Remove as much water as possible and let it air dry, bristles pointing downward to prevent water from seeping into the ferrule.
- If the bristles start to splay, consider a light conditioning with a brush soap and a gentle reshaping session to restore form.
- For stubborn paint, a soft brush cleaner or a dedicated acrylic brush conditioner can help, but avoid harsh solvents that may degrade the bristles.

### Common maintenance pitfalls
- Don’t soak the brush for long periods; the water and paint can creep into the ferrule and loosen the glue.
- Avoid using the Large Drybrush for extremely fine detailing; it defeats the purpose of a large-head brush and may leave you frustrated.
- Don’t use it as a makeshift sponge; it’s not designed to absorb large amounts of water or paint like a mop.

## Contrast with other drybrush options
### Popular alternatives and how they stack up
- Small/Medium drybrushes: Great for tight corners and fine texture highlights; the Large Drybrush complements these by handling bigger areas where speed matters.
- Fan brushes: Useful for textured surfaces and foliage; they produce different highlights and textures that the large brush can’t replicate.
- Specialty cortical brushes: Some brands offer ultra-stiff bristles for heavy-duty dry brushing, which can be more aggressive and less forgiving of mistakes. The Citadel Large Drybrush sits in a sweet spot between aggression and control.

### When not to use it
If you’re aiming for ultra-fine edge highlights on a vehicle’s panel line or detailing on a single rivet, a smaller brush might be a better choice. Also, if your terrain has a lot of recessed detail, you may prefer to use a traditional medium brush or even a brush designed specifically for sculpting tiny highlights. The Large Drybrush shines when speed and broad coverage matter more than absolute micro-precision.

## Common pitfalls and how to avoid them
### Pointers from the field
- Overloading the brush: If you load too much paint, you’ll get blobs and a muddy look. Always wipe off most of the paint before you begin.
- Inconsistent pressure: Maintain consistent pressure across strokes to avoid sudden changes in highlight intensity.
- Not letting dry times happen: If you re-wet too soon, you’ll smear highlights instead of crisping them. Let the surface dry where appropriate.
- Cleaning too late: Paint left in bristles dries and makes the brush stiff and less responsive. Clean promptly.
