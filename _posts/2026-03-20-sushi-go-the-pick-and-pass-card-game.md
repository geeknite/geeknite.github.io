## How to Play (Rules at a Glance)
Sushi Go! is a drafting game built on a single core mechanic: you choose cards, pass the rest to the next player, and then repeat until the round ends. After three rounds, players tally up their points. The rule book is tiny, which makes it a perfect pick for families, classrooms, or a group that has decided that “family game night” needs less setup and more giggles.

### Setup
- Shuffle the deck and deal a hand to each player. The exact number of cards depends on the player count, but you’ll typically start with a growing pool that scales smoothly.
- Place the remaining deck in the center. This is the common pool from which cards will flow to players as hands rotate.
- Decide who will be the first drafter. The rhythm of the table is what makes Sushi Go! tick, and the passing direction often flips with the number of rounds.

### The Draft and Reveal
On your turn, you select one card from your current hand and place it face down in front of you. Then, you pass the remaining cards to the player on your left (or right, depending on the round). Once all players have picked, everyone reveals their chosen cards and starts scoring for that segment of the round. The process repeats as long as there are cards in hands.

### Scoring (the meat and rice)
- Nigiri: Each Nigiri is worth a certain number of points, with the classic singles and doubles forming the baseline. Some Nigiri cards are worth more if combined with Wasabi.
- Maki: The leading Maki cards grant points based on how many players collect the most. It’s a classic “who’s on top this round” moment.
- Sashimi: Set collection rewards a big payoff if you manage to grab three Sashimi cards in a single round.
- Dumplings and Other Bits: Dumplings grow in value the more you collect, offering a nice little ramp if you can snag multiple copies.
- Wasabi: Wasabi cards amplify the value of your next Nigiri, so players will deliberately chase Wasabi in anticipation of bigger points.

- End of three rounds: Tally all points and move to the next round. In most games, you’ll play three rounds total, craftily weaving combos and blocking others with careful card selection.

The rule set is intentionally forgiving, which means beginners can jump in quickly, while wily players can still squeeze out a few extra points by counting card synergies and predicting what others are likely to draft next.


### Quick Teaching Notes
- Emphasize the “pick one, pass the rest” mechanic first. The concept is simple: you’ll draft, you’ll trade, you’ll laugh as someone accidentally passes the Wasabi the wrong way.
- Use clear, quick examples: a single Nigiri by itself is points, but with Wasabi you hope to chain value on a single Nigiri and explode your score.
- Encourage table talk: discuss bluffing and signaling without getting too meta or too competitive. Sushi is inherently social, and Sushi Go! thrives on the extra energy that comes from a chatty round.


## Strategy and Why It Works (or Doesn’t)
If you’re a numbers person, you’ll appreciate the way Sushi Go! sneaks micro-strategies into a tiny framework. The game rewards short-term planning as well as long-term positioning. Here are a few tricks that can elevate your table-talk and your score at the same time:

- Track the flow: Try to remember which cards are likely to make it around to you in the next rotation. If you notice a hand just missed a Wasabi or a set of Sashimi, you might pivot your draft to maximize other combos.
- Weigh risk vs. reward: Some cards are high payoff but high risk if you can’t complete a set. If your table is friendly, you can flirt with these draws; if the table is merciless, you’ll want to hedge with safer picks.
- Don’t chase too hard: In a three-round game, overcommitting to a single strategy can leave you empty-handed when the final rounds pivot. The best players keep a little flexibility—the jar of pickles you didn’t want in the first round may become the perfect pairing in the last.
- Read the room: Part of the fun is the social side. If you sense someone is about to scoop up a critical Maki, pivot to a different finish line and maximize your own path to victory.

This edition shines at casual table speed, where the drama isn’t about who controls the market but who can execute a couple of clean combos while the group shouts in good humor. The pacing is quick, which makes it ideal for a family game night or a short break between longer games.
