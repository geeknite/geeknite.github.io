Gloom is not your typical cozy board game. It’s the kind of game that makes coffee nervous and your in-laws suspicious. Keith Baker’s design for Atlas Games captured a mood: tragic, silly, mordant, and somehow endearing. The Complete Edition released in 2007 bundles together the core game with a wide swath of expansions, so you don’t have to hunt down obscure promo packs at conventions or beg your local game store to special order them with questionable shipping times.

What Gloom wants to do is deliver an experience more like a storytelling game than a strict roll- and move experience. You’re not just playing a set of numbers; you’re orchestrating a melodrama in which every family member has a preposterous tragedy scheduled in their future. And if you’re lucky, you’ll persuade your friends to laugh along with you as you overlay translucent cards and watch gloom creep across the table like a drip of black ink.

## The basic premise
The base idea: each player controls a miserable family. The goal is to have your family members die, the gloomier the better, before your opponents do. The cards include characters with gloom values, as well as story cards that tell the tragedies, events, and conditions that intensify the gloom. The wearing-off of gloom is achieved by overlaying multiple cards to create a stacked gloom score. The lowest gloom wins.

This sets the tone for the entire play: you’re encouraged to weave a tale that’s fun to watch as it grows darker. The humor comes not from cheap jokes but from the sheer audacity of the narratives you conjure on the fly.

### The components and the look
This edition uses transparent cards that stack on top of one another to adjust gloom values. It’s not just about numbers; it’s theater. The art leans toward the tweedy, slightly macabre side of Victorian caricature, which suits both the mood and the silly puns you’ll inevitably deploy when a character delivers yet another melodramatic sob. The box content includes a sizeable deck of character cards, a deck of mood cards, and the overlay components that make the gloom overlay mechanic possible.

### How to set up
Shuffle all the gloom, story, and character cards. Each player chooses a family and receives a starting set of characters with neutral or minimal gloom. The rest of the deck forms the draw pile. The Complete Edition’s extra cards give you more dramatic possibilities, but the rules stay delightfully approachable for first-timers.

### How a turn plays out
On your turn, you draw one card, then you may play a card from your hand. The types of cards you can play include:

- Character cards (the folks you’ll be ruining)
- Story cards (narratives that intensify gloom)
- Condition or event cards (temporary effects that modify gloom on a character)

Once a card is on the table, you may overlay it onto other cards to increase gloom, then pass to the next player. Play continues until all characters on the table are dead. The final gloom totals are tallied, with the aim of having the lowest possible score.

### The card types in detail
Character cards carry an intrinsic gloom value. When a character dies, that gloom value goes to the player who controlled the character. Story cards and conditions can increase or decrease gloom, shift the order of tragedy, or even alter the rules in a playful way. The Complete Edition expands this with more ways to tell your story, beyond the straightforward tragedy, you’ll find hooks for comedy of errors and the occasional melodramatic courtroom flourish.

### Strategy and playstyle
Gloom rewards a storytelling approach. It’s not enough to optimize gloom; you must narrate it with flair. Here are a few guidelines:

- Focus on your own family’s path to doom, but do so with flair. The more color you add, the more your opponents will want to hear the ending.
- Overlay cards to visualize your doom. The translucent cards create a tangible sense of stacked misfortune, which makes the storytelling feel cinematic rather than mathematical.
- Manage tempo. If you flood your own family with devastation too early, you risk ending the game before you get to tell the punchline.
- Read the room. Part of the fun is watching friends groan, groan again, and then laugh when a plot twist lands in the most macabre way possible.

### The Complete Edition vs. the original
The Complete Edition is essentially a one-stop-shop. It contains the base game plus all the expansions and extra decks released up to 2007, so you don’t have to chase down separate packs. This matters because the expansions add color, new death certificates, and cutely punny doom events that feel like they were written by a boardroom of mischievous playwrights.

### The experience at the table
Gloom shines when it’s played around a table with friends who are comfortable with dark humor. It’s not for everyone—if you’re the type who winces at tragic puns, you’ll still enjoy the aesthetic, but the emotional payoff might not land. For groups who love collaborative storytelling, Gloom becomes a theater of micro-dramas. People will argue about whether Aunt Mildred s demise in the Aunt Mildred card is the most grotesque or the most ridiculous. The interactions—overlaying gloom, comparing scores, and narrating the fates of the characters—create a social experience that’s as much about laughter as it is about tragedy.

### Replayability
Because of the broad range of cards and the overlay mechanic, no two games feel exactly the same. The Complete Edition includes different expansions that provide new families, new kinds of gloom, and new ways to tell a story, increasing the number of possible plotlines dramatically. It becomes a yearbook of gloom with an index of jokes and quirks. Each playthrough invites you to try a different narrative approach: a gothic melodrama, a soap-opera farce, or even a tragedy in which everyone’s cat dies, again, but with more dramatic flair.

### Thematic and artistic notes
The art direction leans into a vintage aesthetic. The characters are drawn with that slightly exaggerated Victorian vibe that reads as a public domain press photo meets comic strip. The tone is witty rather than grim for grim’s sake; the humor comes from the ridiculousness of the misfortunes and the way players marshal their plots.
