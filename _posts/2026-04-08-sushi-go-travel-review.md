## How to Play: The Pick and Pass Mechanism
Sushi Go is a fast drafting game. Each round, you look at your hand of cards, pick one to keep, and pass the rest to the left. After all cards are chosen, you reveal and score for the different combinations. The core is simple: pick a card you like, pass the rest, aim for sets that maximize points, and try to read what others are building. The deck includes Nigiri, Maki, Sashimi, Tempura, Wasabi, and Pudding, with the occasional wildcard card depending on the print. When you pick Wasabi, your next Nigiri will be worth more, which is basically a small act of culinary sorcery. The tempo matters: you want to avoid getting stuck with a weak single card at the end of a round. Pour a little strategy into the draft and you suddenly feel like a sushi sommelier with a calculator.

In two to five players, the rhythm scales. With fewer players, you get a quicker cadence and more opportunities to block someone else from completing a pair of Sashimi. With more players, the deck cycles around the table faster, and you find yourself prioritizing single cards that will deny your neighbor a key set. It’s a game about choosing a single card at a time while secretly hoping your neighbors miscount their points. ## Card Types: Zen in a Rice Grain
- Nigiri: simple cards that score by the value shown and are enhanced by Wasabi if you have a Wasabi card first. A single Nigiri can be worth a few points or more in combination.
- Maki: a mini contest round. Who has the most Maki cards when the round ends collects points; this tends to be a high-risk, high-reward feature in larger tables.
- Sashimi: trips score heavy; collecting all three is while fun, not always practical, since someone else might snag the last two.
- Tempura: pairs score; the trick is to collect complements across rounds.
- Wasabi: a multiplier; the next Nigiri you play after Wasabi is worth more.
- Pudding: ends the game; players gain bonus points based on their standing. Pudding is a subtle endgame lever and often a tiebreaker.

The Travel Edition keeps these flavor cards but condenses the deck so you are not wading through 100+ cards at the airport.

## Strategy Guides by Player Count
### 2 Players
In a two-player game, you can afford to take a few riskier cards, because there are only two hands cycling around. You should look for quick sets, but also think about denying your partner their planned sets. A single Mistake could decide the round. If you can time a Sashimi trip or Tempura pair to coincide with your partner's draft, you can swing the round in your favor. The Wasabi trick can be particularly effective here because you have more control of when your next Nigiri hits the table.

### 3 Players
In a three-person game, you’ll see more players chasing similar sets. You can use this to your advantage by aiming for a high-value Nigiri early in a round and letting the other two fight over Sashimi. Because the table is smaller, a single poor pick can blow up your early lead; you want to maintain momentum by choosing cards that progress your own building while subtly blocking others.

### 4-5 Players
With more players, you’ll have more competition for the best sets. Blocking becomes a real tactic; you may pass a card that would otherwise complete someone else’s finished set. You might also choose to focus on Maki or Pudding to steal endgame scoring away from others. The extra four to five players can make it easier to line up a couple of small, consistent point opportunities rather than going all-in on a big set. The key is timing: when to push for a high-value item and when to back off to avoid handing others a perfect finishing card.

## House Rules and Travel Tips
If you take Sushi Go Travel Edition on the road, consider a few simple house rules to keep play fun and quick:
- Shorter rounds: you can remove Pudding cards or skip one scoring mechanic to speed up play for a travel-friendly session.
- Seat rotation: rotate who gets the first pick every game so no one feels shafted by the starting hand.
- Snack balance: keep a small stash of snacks in your carry-on to keep energy high and attention low.

We also appreciate the packaging instructions that accelerate setup. The compact deck means you can reset and begin a new round in under a minute, even with a rowdy group. And if someone complains about the artwork, remind them that you are playing a game about sushi rather than trying to make a heroic dinner decision at midnight.

## Quick Play Summary
- Setup: deal your starting hand according to the rules for your player count; keep your cards secret from the other players.
- Play: choose one card to keep and pass the rest around; reveal and score at the end of each round.

- Time: typically around 15-20 minutes for a standard round, lighter than many heavier gateway games but heavier than pure speed rounds.
