## Thematic Fit and Core Mechanics

Saboteur centers on a dual-layer objective. On the surface, players are dwarves forging a route to a hidden treasure. Underneath, some players are saboteurs, quietly working to derail the route and deny the group their payday. The tension emerges from the interplay between cooperative tunnel-building and covert betrayal. It’s a neat inversion of expectations: you need your neighbors to cooperate to reveal the treasure, but you cannot be sure who is sabotaging you at any given moment.

The core mechanics are straightforward:
- Path cards are played to extend the mine shaft toward the treasure.
- Action cards can disrupt others, repair compromised paths, or force a reshuffle of the board state.
- Roles add a layer of hidden information that makes each round feel like a small social experiment in trust and suspicion.

This combination keeps the game accessible for beginners while still offering rewarding moments for seasoned players who enjoy reading the table and planning around the probabilities of who has which role. ## Setup, Flow, and Teaching the Game

The setup for Saboteur is delightfully quick. You deal a mix of role cards to players (the exact distribution depends on the number of players), lay out the starting path and a few initial cards, then you’re ready to go. The first few rounds function as a gentle primer, allowing players to learn the mechanics through play rather than a wall of written rules. The real teaching happens at the table as players encounter situations like a blocked path card or an abruptly discarded action card that changes the entire dynamic of the next few turns.

In terms of balance, Saboteur benefits from experienced players explaining common misreads and typical strategies. The learning curve is gentle enough that a mixed group can start having a solid run-through after a couple of rounds. ### The flow in practice
1) Draw or pass a card; 2) extend the tunnel with a path card; 3) use an action card to obstruct or assist; 4) keep an eye on your fellow players’ actions and the evolving board state; 5) resolve the round when a path hits the treasure or when a sabotage blocks the key path.

This cycle creates a compelling tempo: there is momentum during the early game as paths branch in multiple directions, followed by a midgame surge of suspicion as players debate who might be sabotaging whom, and finally a closing act where the treasure path is closed or laid open, depending on who has managed to stay one step ahead of the saboteurs.
