## Introduction

Welcome, fellow geeks and hobbyists, to a deep dive into a small but mighty tool: the Citadel Medium Layer Brush. If you have ever stared at a model and thought, I want crisp highlights but not a flash flood of paint, this brush might become your new best friend. The brush is part of Citadel's Layer family, designed for controlled color transitions, gentle glazing, and crisp edge work. In this review we will explore what makes the Medium Layer tick, where it shines, where it falters, and how to coax the most from it on your next mini.

### What is a medium layer brush, and where does it fit in your kit?

In the grand taxonomy of miniature painting brushes, there are base brushes, layer brushes, detail brushes, and dry brushes. The Base brush tends to cover broad areas quickly; the Layer brush aims for smooth transitions between tones, without obliterating the underpainting; the Detail brush tackles tiny features; the Dry brush helps you produce texture and highlights with a dry technique. The Citadel Medium Layer sits in the middle of this ecosystem: it has a pointed tip for precise edging, yet a slightly elongated bristle belly that helps you pull color over surfaces with a soft gradient. It is not the biggest brush in the room, nor the smallest; it is the well-balanced mid-range weapon for your brush rack.

## First impressions and the tactile vibe

When you first hold a Citadel Medium Layer, you notice a couple of things. The handle length is practical: not so long you feel like you are painting in a choir loft, not so short that you pinch your palm in a tiny fist. The weight is just enough to feel like you are wielding a tool rather than a toy. The ferrule is the unsung hero here; it locks the bristles in place with a solid squeeze, and you do not get that wiggly, stray-hair fear before you start your first layer. The bristles themselves look and feel sturdy; not too floppy, not too rigid, with a tip that forms a crisp point when you need to escalate a highlight or define a seam.

### Ergonomics: comfort matters

If you paint for long sessions, you care about grip. The Medium Layer offers a comfortable grip with a mid-range diameter that suits most hands. It is not the most ergonomic design in the world, but it does its job without forcing you into awkward positions. The coating on the handle is smooth, giving you a light friction that helps you maintain control as you glide the brush across your figure. The brush accepts a moderate amount of paint without pooling. It is not a drama queen; you won’t be forced into a fight with your brush every time you want a gradient.

## How it behaves: the painting experience

The heart of the matter is how this brush actually paints. The tip is fine enough for edge work; you can run a crisp line along armor plates and shield borders. The belly of the brush makes it possible to pull color into a soft transition without flooding the area with pigment. The brush is not the fastest for covering a whole torso in a single pass; you will want to plan your layers and do two or three passes to build up a smooth gradient. If you try to push a large block of color in one stroke, you risk pooling at the edge or leaving a visible brush stroke. The trick is to load light, wipe on the palette, and apply with short, controlled strokes.

### The glazing trick

Best used with well-diluated paints, the Medium Layer can perform gentle glazes. Dilute the paint to a very runny consistency; load the brush, and with light pressure drag across the surface to tint the underlying color. The key is to keep the brush moving and the pressure light; you want the glaze to settle into the midtones rather than smear across.

### Edge control versus feathering

The Medium Layer excels at crisp lines along armor plates and seams. When you apply a glaze or shade along the edge of a shoulder pad, you can maintain the crisp boundary with a light touch. Against larger flat surfaces, you can still use the brush to create S-curves and gentle transitions across fabric folds. The balance of tip and belly means you can push into small crevices without losing control of the brush. It is not among the best for extremely fine detail, but it holds up when you want to push a line just enough to separate color zones.

## Comparison to other options in your kit

Let us be real: there are cheaper layer brushes, and there are premium Arena-grade brushes. The Citadel Medium Layer sits somewhere in the mid-range in terms of price; it often delivers a reliable performance that many hobbyists rely on. A comparable brush from a different brand might feel a little stiffer or a little more forgiving, depending on the bristle composition and the tamping of the hair. The ratings and experiences can vary by batch; sometimes you get a brush that feels a touch stiffer, other times it feels more supple. If you enjoy the Citadel grip and you are painting a lot of miniatures with similar color schemes, you will likely appreciate having a couple of these in your collection.

## Care and longevity: keeping your bristles happy

Longer life magic is the result of good care. After you finish painting, run the brush under lukewarm water and comb with your fingers to remove pigment. Avoid hot water as it can weaken the hair and alter the brush’s performance. A proper dry after cleaning is essential. Place the brush on a towel with the bristles hanging over the edge to prevent bending the tip. If you leave it to dry with the bristles pressed against a surface, you risk flattening the tip or forming a crease in the point. A little conditioner or the occasional brush cleanser helps keep the bristle memory intact, particularly after you use paints with heavy pigments or metallics.

### How to use it: practical workflow

- Step 1: Primer and base coat on the miniature. Let the base coat dry completely.  
- Step 2: Load a small amount of paint onto the brush. Wipe excess on the palette.  
- Step 3: Use the tip to lay edge lines and crisp transitions.  
- Step 4: Use the belly to pull a soft gradient across panels.  
- Step 5: If you need to glaze, use a watered-down paint to create a subtle color shift without obscuring details.  
- Step 6: Clean and store.

## The glazing trick (expanded)

When you are layering, glazing is a powerful technique to adjust color relationships after the main layers are in place. With the Medium Layer, you can glaze over a midtone without destroying the highlight on the edge. The trick is to use a brush with a clean tip, apply a very diluted paint, and move the brush in small slow arcs to keep the glaze from pooling. If you have a color you want to push a notch cooler or warmer, a glaze is the way to go. It is not always necessary, but it can save you from repainting large sections when you decide that the color underneath needs a tweak.

## The long view: choosing sizes and variants

Not all Medium Layer brushes are identical. Citadel’s Line may include several variations within the Medium Layer family, including different bristle counts and shapes. If you paint large flat surfaces frequently, you may prefer a wider brush for faster coverage. If you work on tight details, a smaller variation can be helpful to keep your lines crisp. For many hobbyists, a 1 and a 0 size pair will cover most of the typical tasks in a miniature kit. Over time you may collect a couple of variations to tackle different subjects without swapping tools mid-work.

## Where to use it in your mini painting journey

Hobbyists often tell stories about their first brush that clicked. For some, the Medium Layer is that brush: the moment when the layers became more predictable and the transitions smoother. If you like to practice layering and shading on a standard human-scale miniature, this brush is a great partner. If you are into larger dioramas, you can use this brush on the rim details and the base edges rather than requiring a micro-detail brush.

## Final verdict

In truth, the Citadel Medium Layer Brush is a well-rounded tool—the kind you reach for when you want to carve a good gradient, keep edges sharp, and not ruin a good color transition with too much paint. It is not the single best brush for every scenario, but it remains a staple for many hobbyists who want a balance of control, memory of brush feel, and paint retention. If you are building a starter kit and want a single brush that can handle both midtone layers and some edge highlight, the Medium Layer is a wise pick. If you are a pro who needs extreme precision on tiny elements on very small models, you may choose to pair this brush with a smaller detail brush for those micro lines.
