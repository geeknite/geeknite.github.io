## How to Play: Turn by Turn, but with Groovier Moves

If you’ve played UNO before, you’ll be comfortable with the rhythm. If you’re new to UNO Junior Move!, brace yourself for a few kid-friendly improvisations that add energy without drowning the kids in rules.

### Setup

- Each player receives a starting hand of 5 cards. This is a good number for engagement without overwhelming the youngest players.
- Place the remaining deck in the center and flip the top card to start the discard pile. If the first card is a Move card, the game immediately becomes a mini warm-up ceremony, which is weirdly perfect for a chaotic Friday night.
- Pick a starting color based on the first non-Move card if you want to avoid confusion; otherwise, just let the first Move card decide what color chaos you’ll follow.

### How a Turn Works

On your turn, you have two choices: play a card that matches either the color or the number of the top discard card, or play a Move/Wild card if you have one. If you don’t have a playable card, you draw one card from the deck and your turn ends. The music stops when the deck is exhausted—kidding, the game doesn’t have a DJ booth, but it would be pretty impressive if it did.

### Action Cards and the Move Mechanic

Move cards are the heart of UNO Junior Move!. Instead of skimping on action cards, this edition doubles down on movement to keep kids engaged. Examples of Move actions include:

- Jump in place 3 times
- Do 5-second toe touches
- Roll a dice and perform a corresponding silly pose (Yes, we counted dice because dice are still a thing in 2026)
- Spin in a circle with arms stretched out (for the brave)

Wild cards let you pick a color and keep the excitement rolling. If a Move card is played, the current player must perform the action; the next player continues with the new top discard card. The energy level in a living room during a Move action can be measured in giggles per second.

### The Closing Condition

The first player to discard all cards wins. In UNO Junior Move!, victory is a prize—mostly the glory of beating siblings, defeating parents, and the occasional triumphant dance at the end. We’ve seen kids celebrate by inventing a victory lap that zigzags around the coffee table (these traps are not recommended for furniture).
