## Overview

City of Mist Fortune Row is not a video game, but a live action notebook you carry to the table and pretend is a control panel for myths. Ok, maybe that is giving it too much credit. Fortune Row is a player dashboard that aims to unify character fiction, investigator mood, and mechanical bookkeeping into a single, neon-drenched sheet of destiny. If you have ever tried to juggle Stunts, Clues, Masks, and the ever-unreliable dice, you know that dashboards exist for one reason only: to remind you that you are not the mastermind of this chaos, the table is. The Fortune Row dashboard is the sort of tool that arrives at your table wearing sunglasses at night and quietly asks if you would like to borrow its vibe for a little while.

## Design philosophy behind Fortune Row

Fortune Row is built on a philosophy that limits chaos by offering structure without strangling flavor. At the table, you need a way to quickly situate a character within the sprawling mythos. The dashboard is intended to be the instrument that makes that mental map visible. You are not expected to memorize every stat, but you are expected to know where to glance when a GM asks, What is your Mask for this scene? Where does this clue sit on your row? The UI is meant to be tactile enough to feel like a prop you can touch, but flexible enough to accommodate the unexpected leaps of a good noir story.

One of the first choices you notice is the modular layout. Fortune Row presents a central area for core tracks that matter to most investigators: Masks, Fortunes, Clues, and Fate. Surrounding these are auxiliary panels that capture scene-specific data, resources, and a quick action bar for common in-game tasks. The result is a layout that says we know you want speed, but we also know you want theater. Your cursor does not just click; it performs. And you know what that feels like when you are in the middle of a tense scene and your partner yells, I spend a hint to reveal the truth, and the UI beautifully mirrors that moment, rather than delaying it.

The color grammar is clever without being obtrusive. The main interface leans into a midnight blue base with brass or neon accents to indicate critical thresholds. The designers avoid the trap of high-contrast cacophony and instead opt for a design that feels like a city at 2 am — present, moody, and just a little dangerous. Accessibility-wise, the contrast is usually fine for standard lighting, and there are subtle typography cues that help color-blind players parse the essential signals. It is not perfect, but it is a thoughtful balance that makes late-night sessions less likely to degrade into a scribble-fest on a napkin.

The dashboard also introduces micro-interactions that reward familiarity. Hovering over an item reveals a compact tooltip with the relevant rules snippet, and clicking a panel opens a modal with quick actions. It is the dopamine hit you want when the GM rolls a successfully narrative reveal, without the risk of derailing the session while everyone tries to parse a paragraph-long carryover from a previous scene. The micro-interactions feel like the difference between a well-timed jazz flourish and a random elevator ping — they add polish without getting in the way.

The asset library included with Fortune Row leans into the mythic noir vibe. The icons convey a sense of archetypes and mythic motifs rather than technical jargon. Mask icons remind you that a character is wearing a role like a cape, while a Fortunes panel hints at the cosmic weather guiding the day. The design team appears to have considered how a player who wants to lean into performance can flavor their experience, rather than just tick boxes. If you enjoy your dungeon-crawl UI with the aesthetic of a vintage detective novel, Fortune Row is a little treasure map you want to keep in your pocket.

We also tested the dashboard with a group that has a range of tech comfort. Some players are into have-laptop-scan mode, others prefer printouts. Fortune Row scales well in both worlds. The core data is accessible in the digital version, but the option to print or export data means you can keep a physical record for those who still want to scribble notes in the margins. If you are a GM who likes to print a player handout that correlates exactly to the dashboard, you will appreciate the export options. The ability to align what the players see with what you, the GM, want them to see simplifies scene setup and reduces the number of confused stares around the table.

Right away you will notice a few decisions that may feel specific to City of Mist. The Fortune Row dashboard foregrounds concept-driven data rather than strictly mechanical numbers. Fortunes and Clues are not just tallies; they are narrative levers. That means the UI emphasizes storytelling weight and the consequences of choices. It means that as a player you are not only counting software widgets but also counting the story implications of each selection. If you come from a heavy dice-driven system, this approach might feel unfamiliar at first, but it will likely become one of the dashboard strengths as you begin to see the table adapt to the narrative currents that the UI highlights.

The health of any dashboard is measured by how it changes your table dynamics. Fortune Row excels when the group trusts the interface to manage the dark, cinematic energy of the game. That is a design win, especially for groups that want to lean into mythic themes without sinking into a spreadsheet vortex. If you have ever played City of Mist and felt the pinch of juggling narrative cues with dice and tables, Fortune Row offers a path to a calmer, more cinematic flow without sacrificing the essential detail that keeps the city from dissolving into a glorious fog of maybe.

In the context of other dashboards and companions, Fortune Row sits in a space that values mood, vibe, and narrative clarity over brute force data. In short, Fortune Row is a stylish, purpose-built hack that works best when used as the storytelling backbone rather than a robotic data store.
