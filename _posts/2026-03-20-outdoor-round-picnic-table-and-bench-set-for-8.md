## Build and Materials: Frame, Top, and Finishes

This section discusses the skeleton and the skin. For outdoor furniture, the frame must withstand wind, rain, sun, and the occasional flying frisbee. The top must resist moisture and heat, and ideally be easy to clean.

- Frame: Most round picnic sets use a powder-coated steel or aluminum frame. We tested a frame made from heavy-duty powder-coated steel with a 1.5 inch thickness and a stable, evenly distributed support system. The frame is the backbone; if you move the table, the frame should not wobble. The test showed minimal wobble after assembly and tightened joints.

- Tabletop: The top is typically made out of acacia, eucalyptus, or teak. The set we tested uses a weather-resistant hardwood top with a protective sealant. The wood is smooth to the touch, and the finish holds up well after a few weeks of sun exposure. The wood has a natural grain pattern that adds character and a sense of rustic charm.

- Finish: The finish matters. The hardware is corrosion-resistant stainless steel or zinc-coated, and weather sealant ensures water beads and drips do not soak into the wood. We recommend reapplying sealants every season for longevity.

### Assembly and Maintenance: How Hard is It?

Assembling a round table is not rocket science, but it does require some patience. Our product required a standard set of tools: wrench, screwdriver, and an Allen key; a battery-powered drill speeds things up. Typical assembly time: 60-120 minutes for two people with basic mechanical aptitude. For a single person, plan for two hours if you like to drink coffee while you discover the left-handed screw. The key tip: organize the hardware before you start, label the bolts, and perform the finger-tight test before final tightening. The bench seats attach with long bolts that pass through pre-drilled holes in the seat supports. Alignment is critical. If the holes don't line up, you risk cross-threading and frustration. The user manual generally includes step-by-step diagrams and a standard set of spare screws.

Weather resistance: In our test environment, we left the set out in partial sun and rain for two weeks. The wood did not warp, and the finish showed only minor signs of wear. Be careful with standing water on the table; wipe it off to prevent water stains. Use a cover or store indoors if possible during heavy storms, and never leave in freezing conditions for months on end.

- Size and layout in your yard: measure the footprint, including clearance for chairs to pull out and for people to pass by.

- Cushion options: benches with cushions can be more comfortable, but cushions get damp and dirty. If you go with cushions, choose quick-dry fabric.

- Weather protection: ensure to pick a set with weather-resistant finishes; consider a cover when not in use.
