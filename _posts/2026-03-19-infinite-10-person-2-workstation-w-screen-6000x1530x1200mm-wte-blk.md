## Ergonomics and Comfort

When you scale up to ten people, ergonomics becomes less about per-person adjustability and more about shared zones and universal design. The system offers typical adjustability in chair placements, monitor arms, keyboard trays, and the ability to reconfigure the baying into rows, islands, or theater seating for weekly cross-team demos. Here are the main ergonomic highlights:
- Adjustable height zones: Some models offer manual or electric height adjustment across the two desktop benches. The aim is to allow standing tasks or seat-based work without forcing someone into an unpleasant posture for eight hours.
- Monitor placement logic: With a screen wall, you want to ensure viewing angles don’t cause neck strain or “display neck” after the first three hours. The recommended arrangement tends to keep monitors within about 45 degrees of the central axis, which reduces the need to thread your head through the fourth dimension.
- Keyboard and mouse zones: A deeper desk surface reduces the need to “turtle” wrists. The architecture should permit an ergonomic neutral posture, meaning elbows close to the body around a 90-degree angle, wrists flat, and neck not doing push-ups.

Real-world impressions: It’s a bit like coordinating a jazz band where everyone plays their own instrument, but the conductor is the cable management system. If your team is accustomed to chaotic desks with 17 USB cables poking out, you’ll appreciate the clean cable channels and grommets designed to keep the workstation visually calm—and the IT folks a little less stressed.

## Space Planning and Installation

This is where the dream meets reality. A 6-meter long desk needs serious space planning. Consider the following:
- Aisles: You want at least 1.5 meters of clearance on all sides for comfortable chair movement and emergency egress. If you’re in a dense urban office with a lot of traffic, expect to reroute some footpaths and signage.
- Power and data distribution: The two-workstation set-up typically includes integrated channels for power, network, and display cables. You’ll likely need a dedicated wall outlet cluster or a power distribution unit (PDU) at the base. It’s a good time to check the electrical load and avoid tripping circuits during a product launch demo.
- HVAC clearance: The long surface will reflect more heat in the immediate area. The installation plan should consider air flow around the workstation and avoid blocking any vents that could create a tropical climate around the team’s keyboards.

Lead times may vary. The brand often provides an installation guide with step-by-step assembly diagrams, including recommended torque specs, wiring harness routing, and safety checklists. If you are a DIY enthusiast, you’ll enjoy the challenge; if you’re a facilities manager on a tight schedule, you might want to hire professionals who speak “MDF” and “powder coat” with ease.
