## What is Skip-Bo and what comes in this edition
Skip-Bo is a card game built around the concept of building piles in numerical order. Each player starts with a stock pile of cards hidden from other players. On your turn, you draw until you have five cards in your hand, and you attempt to play cards from your hand or discard piles to the table in sequential order. The magical part of Skip-Bo is that the deck contains wild cards, branded Skip-Bo cards, which can substitute any number card to help you forward when your sequencing is stuck like a browser tab in a haunted moment of lag.

The 162 card edition expands on the classic by increasing the total number of cards in play and adding more opportunities for wild play. In this version you will notice more Skip-Bo wild cards, more numbered cards in 1 through 12 sequences, and a few design tweaks that keep the game accessible for younger players while maintaining enough complexity to keep seasoned table strategists from falling asleep between turns.

The game supports roughly 2 to 6 players, depending on how patient your household is with prolonged table talk and enthusiastic cheering. The larger deck is particularly well-suited for bigger groups because it reduces the frequency of repeated card suits and introduces more circulations of the stock piles. It is not unusual to see a three-hourextended game night where multiple poker-faced aunties suddenly unleash their inner tournament host and start setting up unofficial brackets for who can yell the loudest when a wild card is played.

## Gameplay and strategy
### Setup
The setup is simple but there is a little more to it when you are working with the 162 card edition. Shuffle the entire deck, deal each player a stock pile face down, usually consisting of a handful of cards depending on the number of players. Arrange your hand from the draw pile and plan to be reactive. On each turn, you draw up to five cards in hand and start attempting to play onto your own or shared building piles in numerical order starting from 1 up to 12. The first player to empty their stock pile wins. That is the core. Everything else is a tactical comedy of timing and risk assessment.

### Core rules you need to know
- You can play any card in your hand or from your discard piles that fits onto a building pile in ascending order.
- Skip-Bo wild cards substitute any needed card to continue the sequence. The timing of when you use wilds is the difference between victory and a dramatic sigh as someone else splashes down a 12 and mocks your 1.
- You must always draw to five cards after your turns if possible. This keeps the table lively and fuels the next round of misplays.
- If your stock pile empties, you win. But the real drama is usually in the moment right before that, when you are clutching a last card that could spell triumph or ruin the family dinner chorus.

### Strategy bites you will learn to love
- Card counting with a side of memory: In a 162 card deck, you will not know what is left, which means every reveal feels like a reveal in a heist movie. Use memory to anticipate when wild cards may be available and avoid burning them too early.
- Hand management over brute luck: With more cards on the table, your hand should be disciplined. Avoid the panic play of dumping everything in a single loud move; instead, look for opportunities to create safe paths for your stock pile to progress.
- The power of the third option: In many situations you will have the option to place a card on your own build pile, someone else build pile, or to discard. The third option is often the most delicate and the most rewarding if used to set up a future turn or to stall a rival.
- Wild cards as leverage, not gimmicks: Skip-Bo wilds should be saved for moments when they unlock a chain reaction of plays. Do not waste them on trivial plays. The true power lies in sequences that would otherwise stall your progress or create a cascade of favorable draws for future turns.

### Pitfalls and how to dodge them
- Rule fatigue: When you have a big group, players may forget the exact order, which can stall the game. Be ready to gently remind everyone of the right order and make the game feel like a friendly test rather than a grind.
- Deck fatigue: With a larger deck, you can see more repeated patterns. If the group starts getting bored, flip in a light landmark challenge like a free-form rule variant for a round to shake things up.
- The long game risk: The bigger the deck, the longer it might take to finish. If you want a shorter evening, set a timer or a score threshold, so a late night does not turn into a cardio workout for your willpower.
