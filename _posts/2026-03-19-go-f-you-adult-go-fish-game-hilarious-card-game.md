## Setup and Rules: A Practical, Not-So-Boring Guide
If you’ve played traditional Go Fish, you’ll recognize the setup, but you’ll notice the vibe shift instantly. The rules stay mostly familiar, but the tone shifts from “safe and friendly” to “we are all in this together and also we’re going to riff for 30 minutes about your questionable life choices.”

### Setup
1. Shuffle the deck and deal a hand to each player. The exact number of cards per player varies by player count, but the game scales gracefully from a cozy two-player session to a raucous group of eight or more.
2. Place the remaining cards in a draw pile facedown. This is your pool of chaos; draw from it whenever you need a stormy plot twist.
3. Decide who goes first. Best to assign this by a silly ritual, such as the person who can name the most nerdy movie quotes in under 15 seconds. This guarantees a roaring start and a few groans in anticipation.

### Basic rules (abridged)
- On your turn, you ask another player for a specific card rank. The catch: you must phrase your request with a humorous prompt or a witty line. Example: If you’re going for a 7, you might say, I need a 7 to complete my space pirate crew, or I need a 7 to unlock your dragon’s fiery secrets. If the asked player has any of the requested rank, they must give you all of them; you then draw again or pass the turn as your chaos dictates.
- If the asked player does not have the card, they say Go Fish and you draw from the top of the draw pile. If you draw the card you asked for, you get another go—because nothing says strategy like a second chance at a punchline.
- When you collect a set of four of a kind, you reveal it and score points. The fun part is the celebratory cards that pop up when you complete a set, featuring memes or puns that will be instantly referenced by your group for years.
- The game ends when all sets are claimed, or when you realize you have spent more time bantering than actually playing. It’s not cheating if the laughter is loud enough to wake the neighbors; it’s just excellent time management.

### Tips for keeping the pace brisk
- Use a timer for matchmaking prompts to prevent analysis paralysis. A quick 30-second timer creates a sense of urgency and heightens the comedy.
- Assign a “referee” card that can resolve disputes about prompts. This makes the game feel more like a late-night improv session rather than a quiet library visit.
- Keep a running joke sheet. If someone says a killer prompt, write it down; you’ll want to reuse it later for maximum impact.
