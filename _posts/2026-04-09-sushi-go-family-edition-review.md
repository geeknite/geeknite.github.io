### What is Sushi Go! Family Edition?
Sushi Go! Family Edition is a compact, quick-to-play card drafting game designed to be as accessible to kids as it is satisfying to adults who want something snackable after a long day of work. The core mechanic remains drafting and passing, which means you pick a card from your hand, reveal all cards simultaneously, and pass the rest to the next player. The twist here is that the rounds are shorter, the scoring is slightly friendlier to newcomers, and the competition feels more about watching the sushi parade unfold than about constructing a black belt strategy in month two.

You’ll assemble mini-sets of sushi cards across multiple rounds. Each type of sushi has its own scoring rules. The art is bright, cutesy, and intentionally non-threatening, so even if your child’s first instinct is to call the octopus “cute” rather than “dangerous,” you’ll both be having fun. The kit includes a deck of sushi cards, plus kid-friendly components that keep the experience light and breezy. And yes, you’ll still get the famous “instant regret” moment when someone ends up with the most perfect set and you realize you forgot to pay attention to a drearily valuable dessert.

### How to Play: A Quick Walkthrough
The fundamental experience of Sushi Go! Family Edition remains faithful to its drafting core, but the pace is friendlier. Here’s a quick walkthrough to help you feel confident enough to gather around the table without your brain turning into a sushi roll.

1) Deal: Shuffle the deck and deal a hand to each player. The number of cards depends on the player count, but the aim is to have a speed-bumping, minute-long draft per round.
2) Pick and reveal: On your turn, you pick one card from your hand and place it face-down in front of you, then reveal it simultaneously with the other players. The suspense is real—like waiting for the microwave to ding, but with more wasabi and fewer leftovers.
3) Pass: After revealing, you pass the remaining cards to your neighbor. The neighbor picks, passes, and the cycle continues. The passing creates the tug-of-war that keeps the table engaged: you want to hold onto a card that will periodically become a perfect fit for your set, but you never know what the other players are drafting.
4) Score: After all cards are drafted, you tally your points. The scoring rules depend on the card types: some cards reward you for sets, others reward you for a variety of unique combos. The key is to track your progress and not panic as your cousin quietly collects all the dessert cards and declares dessert supremacy.
5) Next round: Start again with a new hand, and perhaps a new strategy. The game typically lasts several rounds, with the rounds getting a little faster each time as players learn the rhythm.

Safety tip: If your kids are new to drafting games, you might want to give them a couple of practice rounds with simpler scoring. The aim is to make the experience fun and approachable, not to turn family dinner into a math test.
