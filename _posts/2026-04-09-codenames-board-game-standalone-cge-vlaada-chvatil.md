## Gameplay mechanics
The game is built around two core activities: clue creation and word deduction. The spymaster begins with a single word clue and a number. The players respond by guessing which word cards on the grid match the clue. The tension ramps as the guesses accumulate, and the risk of hitting the assassin word or an opponent word adds stakes to every single vote.

### Turn structure
The cycle of play is familiar to Codenames veterans:
- The spymaster gives a clue word plus a number
- The team discusses, balancing speed with accuracy
- They select cards one by one
- A correct guess reveals a word that belongs to their team, and the team continues
- A wrong guess ends the turn with some consequences (opponent control, neutral word revealed, or game-ending assassin)
This cadence creates a rhythm: fast thinking, rapid debate, and the occasional loud exhale when someone uses a perfect bracketed clue like “planet 3” or “orbit 2.”

### Clue design dynamics

## Practical tips and tricks
- If your group is split between careful clue-givers and more aggressive risk-takers, rotate the role to give each side a moment in the spotlight. The tension will swing with the rotation, and the group will build shared language around what counts as a smart clue.
- When choosing a clue’s number, consider your confidence and the distribution of words on the grid. A reliable rule of thumb is to aim for as many confident hits as possible without provoking a cascade of wrong guesses.
- Use non-linear clues when possible. Connecting words in a non-linear fashion—like a conceptual bridge rather than a direct category—can create surprising leaps in thinking that yield big results.
- In groups with younger players, consider giving the younger players a lighter role: perhaps allow them to call out a word (if the rules permit) once the spymaster has set the clue. This can keep everyone engaged and reduce the pressure on a single player.
- If you’re stuck on a tough round, take a five-minute break and re-evaluate your approach. A fresh perspective often yields a new route to the same destination.
