## Overview

Gloom, at its core, is a game about making your characters as pathetic as possible while keeping your opponents’ families (the rivals’ characters) as cheerful as possible—because, you know, you want the other players to suffer vicariously through their own doomed descendants. The Complete Collection amps up that concept by including multiple expansions, which introduce new ways to ruthlessly tweak the sorrow balance, new family members with their own tragic backstories, and new ways to pile on the gloom. It’s not a game to play for a quick pick-me-up; it’s a game to play when you want to turn your living room into a small, bleary-eyed theater of melodrama.

### Why this collection matters

- It provides a one-stop-shop for all the gloom you crave, without the need to hunt down rare out-of-print packs.
- The translucent cards are a delight to handle, almost as satisfying as popping a bubble wrap of doom.

## Setup and Learning Curve

Setting up Gloom is a quick ritual more than a task. In the Complete Collection, you’ll find a slightly longer setup due to the extra expansions, but the game remains approachable for casual players and welcoming to new table-top fans who don’t own a PhD in dramatic irony. A typical setup includes:

1. Shuffle the base family cards and distribute a starting character to each player.
2. Shuffle the story deck and set aside a handful for the first round.
3. Place the gloom markers on the “0” track and prepare to stack tentatively tragic fates.

What makes the learning curve friendly is the way the rules reinforce a storytelling rhythm. You play a card with a shorthand snippet of catastrophe, you reveal it, you place the required gloom on a character, and then you pass. It’s collaborative misery with social deductions layered in. The translucent cards are visually delightful and slightly disorienting at first, but once you’ve got the hang of peeking through layers without blinding your own score, you’re in for a pretty smooth ride.

If you’ve played traditional card games that feel like binary choices (play vs. discard), Gloom flips the philosophy: you’re rewarded for making your own people suffer more than your rivals. That’s a deliciously savage turn that also teaches players to balance risk versus strategy, since you want to end with the lowest total gloom possible.

## Gameplay: The Core Loop

### The Turn Structure

Games in this collection typically proceed with a series of turns where players play cards to their own family or to an opponent’s family. Each card adds or subtracts gloom, and you’re constantly weighing how much misfortune to push onto a character before the round ends. The goal is to accumulate gloom on your own family and minimize the gloom on others when you flip to scoring—so you actually want to see your opponents fail to rescue their own hapless heirs.

A core loop looks like this:
- Draw a card, add story elements or misfortunes to a family member, and maybe trigger a special ability.
- Optional: play a “twist” card that folds in new rules for the round, nudging the gloom balance in your favor or riling up the other players.
- End of round: tally gloom and adjust the scoring state. If you’re smart about the timing, you can push an extra misfortune to a rival just as the round ends, turning the table into a dramatic cliffhanger.

### The Role of Storytelling

One of the standout features of the Gloom experience is how it turns into a storytelling exercise. The cards provide prompts, the art provides mood, and players weave a quick, melodramatic tale about each character’s misfortune. The humor lands best when jokes land on timing—if a rival is delivering a heartfelt plea for their character’s “happiness,” you can counter with a ghostly pun that punctures the moment in the most theatrical way.

In the Complete Collection, expansions broaden the storytelling canvas. You’ll meet new family members with quirks, new sorrow-triggering events, and sometimes wacky modifiers that make a “doom twist” feel less like a mechanical penalty and more like a stage cue for an over-the-top performance.

The translucent cards let you layer moods on top of moods, which creates a visually satisfying sense of “the more layers, the gloomer.” The art style doesn’t strive for grim realism; it leans into playful gothic humor that elicits smiles even as you’re ruining a character’s day. Theme-wise, the Complete Collection nudges the humor toward the macabre while staying within the bounds of light-hearted, almost child-friendly storytelling. ## Strategy, Luck, and Table Dynamics

Gloom sits at an interesting crossroads of luck and strategy. There’s a healthy dose of randomness in card draws and event outcomes, but the true skill is in how you order your reveals, how you lean into the mood of a particular round, and how you leverage your opponents’ misfortunes to your advantage.

Smart players track the gloom trajectory across all families, not just their own. If you see a rival about to land a devastating combo on their next turn, you might choose to stage a temporary alliance of convenience—slightly bending the rules of rival gloom for the sake of depriving a more dangerous goose (i.e., your friend) of a win. The social element is where this game shines; the expansions intensify this dynamic by offering more levers to pull when you’re negotiating with others about who gets the last piece of tragic luck.

Memorably, the Complete Collection often leads to moments where a single card flip changes the entire vibe of the table. You’ll hear a chorus of groans, perhaps a cheer if someone manages a clever comeback, and a flurry of whispers while players recalculate doom scores like stockbrokers watching a ticker tape of sadness.

## Accessibility and Player Count

Gloom is friendly to new players, with rules that can be picked up in one or two rounds of play. The Complete Collection scales well from small groups to larger gatherings, though with more players, a session can feel a bit more chaotic as more misfortune cards collide and more stories collide with each other.

- Player counts typically range from 2 to 4 players for a tight experience, up to 5 or 6 in some variants provided by the collection. In practice, 3–4 players is the sweet spot for balance and banter.
- Playtime usually clocks in around 45–60 minutes, depending on the number of expansions in use and how long you linger on each tragic anecdote.
- Setup time is fair; you won’t need a degree in necromantic algebra to get started, though you might need a nap after you realize your character’s fate is going to be a tragedy of epic proportions.

If you love party vibes but want a game that rewards narrative energy and quick wit, Gloom in the Complete Collection is a strong pick. It doesn’t demand long hours of persistence or a gamemaster who can recite the entire Rulebook from memory; it rewards your ability to be dramatic and strategic in small, memorable beats.
