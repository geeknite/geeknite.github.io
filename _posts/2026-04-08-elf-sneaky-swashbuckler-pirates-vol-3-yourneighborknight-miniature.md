## Painting and Color Theory

### Base Coats and First Washes
Start with a cool gray for the skin to mimic moonlight on iron, then shade with a blue-tinged brown for depth. The coat should read deep navy with a hint of emerald, to echo the ocean’s phosphorescence when the deck creaks under night sails. The sash can be a bold crimson so the silhouette pops over the base, while the leather parts stay a rich chocolate to ground the palette. A light dry brush on the hair helps pick out individual strands and gives the look of hair that’s been tousled by sea spray.

### Freehand, Details, and Highlights
The elf’s facial features call for a careful approach; a gentle highlight on the cheekbone can read as cheekbone-glint rather than a chalky wash. The sword has a metallic edge that should glint with a thin silver stroke, hinting at a steel blade that’s been sharpened on a whetstone and in a storm. A pale blue edge highlight on the coat adds dimension to the folds without pushing the color into the throat of the miniature’s silhouette.

### Bases, Terrains, and Thematic Context
A pirate deck is not complete without a splash of sea foam and a hint of wear. Use a few chips of brown and tan across the planks to simulate weathering, then scatter a few tiny shell bits for character. If you’re ambitious, a micro-waves texture with gloss varnish can give the impression of spray from a rogue wave, which is perfect for a figure who a) sneaks, b) fights, and c) blames the wave for any misstep.

## Painting Tips and Tricks

### Skin, Fabric, and Metals
For the skin, a thin glaze of blue over a pale base gives you that moonlit look without making the face appear sickly. The fabric can be layered with mid-tones and a bright edge to catch the eye. For metals, a pre-shade with black, a mid-tone metallic, and a fine metallic edge highlight can create a convincing steel sheen without dulling the color depth of the coat.

### Hair and Accessories
Hair is a narrative device; use a slightly lighter shade on the topmost strands to imply the light catching the crown of a cap. Accessories like belts and buckles should be painted with a cool metallic or a bronze to differentiate from the steel of weapons. Dry-brush gold or brass on belts to hint at weathered brass hardware and give a tactile feel to the piece.

### Freehand and Decals
If you’re a fan of narrative decals, a tiny compass rose or anchor on the sash can tell a story without saying a word. Freehand lines on the sword scabbard or a few runic marks along the edge of the hat can add a layer of lore that your opponents might never bother to read, but you know is there and that’s enough to satisfy your inner loremaster.
