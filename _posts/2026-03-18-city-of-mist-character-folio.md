## What is a Character Folio? (In City of Mist Terms)
In City of Mist, players craft characters as living contradictions: a mortal “Logos” identity who wants to be something bigger, and a Mythos aspect that grants powers and a mythic aura. The folio is the artifact that captures those two facets on a single sheet. It is designed to live at the table as a quick-reference guide to: 
- Identity and backstory fragments that you reveal as you play
- Powers and their weaknesses, tagged as Mythos
- Relationships to other players, factions, and the city’s legends
- The character’s goals, secrets, and what the city wants from them

The folio’s layout encourages players to think of their hero as a rumor with a name, rather than just a sheet with numbers. It is this narrative-first design that makes the folio feel special. The moment you start filling it out, you’re not just calculating modifiers; you’re painting a living poster for your legend to hang behind the game master’s head as you speak.

If you’ve got a quick-start itch for how a folio might look on the page, City of Mist’s official site offers a clean example and, sometimes, a printable sheet that you can print and physically scratch on. You’ll also find the folio’s aesthetic mirrored in the game’s art direction: noir shadows, graffiti textures, and a font that feels like someone whispered a myth in a subway tunnel.

### The Mythos and Logos Dialectic
A folio in CoM is not merely a set of traits; it’s a dialectic between two modes of being. Mythos gives you the power to bend reality in ways that feel mythic, while Logos grounds you in the world with personal stakes and human limitations. The folio helps you articulate both sides with clarity and style so that, when you roll into a scene, you know exactly what your character craves and what they’re willing to do to get it.

In practice, this means you’ll want to fill out: 
- Your Logos identity: name, occupation, core personality, and a few secrets you’re not ready to share with the world. 
- Your Mythos: the power tag(s), a short description of how it manifests, and the price you pay when you push too far. 
- Connections and conflicts: who loves you, who hates you, who owes you a favor, and who you owe a debt to. 
- A short personal quest: a single, concrete objective that threads through the campaign. 

By pairing these elements on a single folio, you create a character that can evolve in interesting ways as the city changes around them. It’s not just about “levels” or “stunts”; it’s about a living myth in the middle of a gritty city landscape.

## Design and Layout: What Works (and What Could Be Better)
The folio’s design is its strongest selling point. City of Mist benefits from a clean, legible layout that doesn’t drown you in icons or opinionated jargon. You get:
- Two primary halves on the same page for Mythos and Logos, making it easy to reference during play without flipping through pages.
- A generous amount of whitespace, which means you can jot notes without feeling like you’re scribbling in a storm drain.
- Thematic typography that keeps the mood intact while still being readable under the game’s typical low-light, high-dramas sessions.
- Jekyll image usage in posts and a consistent visual link to the game’s noir, neon vibe. The folio itself looks like a curated dossier you’d find in a secret city hall basement.

The one possible downside is that the folio can feel a touch compact for characters with unusually long backstories or a sprawling cast of relationships. If you’re the kind of player who loves to dump heavy lore into the sheet, you might want to print an extra page or keep a digital copy handy to expand notes. Fortunately, the folio is designed with modularity in mind. It invites expansion, not constraint.

To illustrate the layout, here’s a compact example folio snippet (summarized):

- Logos: Character Name; Occupation; Core Motif; Secrets; Relationships (with Player Characters and notable NPCs); Personal Quest
- Mythos: One or more Power Tags (with short descriptions); Aspect (how it manifests in the city); Price/Complication (what you lose when you use it); Associated Symbols (images or phrases that anchor the power)
- City Ties: Factions, allies, rivals, and a single city-wide thread they’re entangled with
- Journal/Notes: A small place to scribble a clue that ties into your personal narrative

## Building Your Folio: A Practical Step-by-Step Guide
If you’re ready to craft a folio that feels like a living legend rather than a skeleton of a character, here’s a practical workflow you can use in a session or as a pre-game exercise.

### Step 1: Define the Logos (Your Mortal Identity)
- Start with a one-liner: Who are you beneath the mask? What’s your day job, your reputation, your secret fears? 
- Pick a defining attribute: a trait that makes you memorable to other players. It could be something that grounds you in the real world (a stubborn sense of justice, a fear of water, a habit of saving stray pigeons).
- Sketch a few secrets you’ll only reveal when it matters. Secrets are narrative fuel, and the folio is where you store them for later reveal.
- Map a small web of relationships: who owes you a favor, who wants you to fail, who could be a potential ally when the city is in trouble.

### Step 2: Define the Mythos (Your Super-Powered Angle)
- Pick a Power Tag(s): A concise, evocative statement that captures what you can do and why it matters. Don’t overload; you’ll thank yourself later when you’re routing through scenes." 
- Describe the manifestation: How does your power look, sound, and feel when you use it? Is there a cost, a consequence, or a catch?
- Identify your limitations: The city has rules; you should too. This helps you avoid deus ex machina and creates tension in play.
- Tie the power to your Logos: Your mythic abilities should reflect your mortal identity’s core theme. The best folios create a cognitive loop between who you are and what you can do.

### Step 3: Link, Don’t Snap Your Webs Too Tightly
- Use your relationships to create scenes. Friends can become leverage points; rivals can catalyze your worst nightmares; mentors can deliver plot hooks that feel inevitable.
- Consider faction ties: A mention of a particular faction can instantly pull your character into city-wide quandaries. This is where the folio becomes a playground for conflict.
- Create a personal quest that can evolve: A quest with a reasonable endpoint gives the GM a target and gives you a way to show character growth without re-skinning your entire identity.

### Step 4: Draft a Short Personal Motif (A Mantra You’ll Say on Turn 3)
Humor, gravitas, or a mash of both: your motif is what you riff on when the city shakes. It can be a line of dialogue, a recurring symbol, or a favorite bad pun that somehow captures your character’s charm and flaws. The motif is your sticky brand as a legend in motion.

### Step 5: Refine and Iterate
- Run a mini-session with the folio as your guide: see what’s fun, what’s clunky, and what needs more myth.
- Iterate. The folio should adapt as the story evolves; don’t lock yourself into a rigid arc. The city itself is feedback, after all.

## Play Experience: How the Folio Feels in Action
When the dice start tumbling and the neon rain hits the rooftops, the folio becomes your anchor. The most significant advantage is clarity: you can describe a Logos reveal or a Mythos flourish without rummaging through pages of notes. The other players know the stakes instantly. “This is who I am, and this is what I can do to protect what matters.” That kind of clarity creates smoother turns and a lot more laughter when things go spectacularly sideways.

But the folio also nudges you toward storytelling discipline. You’re encouraged to keep the two halves in balance. If you lean too hard into Mythos, you risk becoming a punchline with glitter; if you lean too hard into Logos, you might feel like a bystander without a city-sized magic system backing you up. The healthiest folio threads both sides together, distributing power and responsibility among the group so that every scene has a sense of personal myth unfolding.

From a GM’s perspective, the folio is a wonderful tool for scaffolding sessions. It gives you quick hooks: “the hero’s secret is about to explode into public knowledge,” or “the Mythos power triggers a response in a sympathetic city ally.” The Table becomes a stage for both character-driven drama and city-wide intrigue—without forcing players to memorize a wall of numbers.

## Practical GM Tips: Using Folio Hooks to Drive Sessions
- Let the Logos drive scenes with a consequence tied to a personal secret. If a character’s secret gets exposed, that’s a narrative jackpot, not a hard setback.
- Use Mythos costs as a recurring torment mechanism. Every time a player uses Mythos, their world becomes a little less safe; the city may react, both positively and negatively, to a power being unleashed.
- Build scene hooks around relationships. A rival’s change in allegiance can spark a mini-arc for several sessions.
- Create a recurring motif or object that appears in the folio and in scene props (e.g., a symbol that returns in graffiti, a specific street name, or a coded message).
- Encourage players to revise the folio after major milestones. The best folios feel used, not archived.
