## Introduction
Saboteur is the kind of game that arrives with a wink and a pickaxe, and a confession that you might be playing a trust exercise with tiny cards. It's the classic gateway drug for groups that want to alternate between bluffing, building, and blaming each other for the collapsing tunnel you somehow caused. If you have ever looked at a deck of cards and thought, I could build a cave with these, Saboteur is probably your destiny.

## What is Saboteur?
Saboteur is a compact, fast-paced card game about dwarves tunneling toward treasure. Some players are secretly sabotagers, others are honest gold diggers. The objective is simple on the surface: lay down a path of tunnel cards from the starting point to one of the gold cards at the far end. The twist? Saboteurs win by preventing a complete path, while the diggers win by guiding a clear tunnel from start to gold.
The game uses a deck of path cards with straight pieces, bent corners, and branching crossroads, plus action cards that alter the tunnel or twist the narrative. The art is light and cartoonish, which helps keep chaos from turning into a permanent grudge. And yes, you will have to lie to your friends with a straight face. That’s part of the appeal.
The setup takes about a minute, the rounds last around 15 to 25 minutes depending on player count, and the laughter tends to outlast your curiosity about who drew which role.

The box usually contains:
- A pile of path cards in several shapes
- A handful of gold cards and a couple of map cards
- Action cards for sabotage and assistance
- A set of role cards telling who is a digger and who is a saboteur
The important bit: the first player to get path to a gold tile scores; the saboteurs score points for their stealthy sabotage. The counterpoint creates tension and humor as players argue about whether there is a real path or a blank card that will become a future legend.

## The components
The cards do the heavy lifting here. Path cards come in artful shapes with edges that click nicely into the current tunnel. The gold cards are bright and easy to spot. There are maps that reveal future moves, and a few tool or action cards that let parochial dwarves steal a turn or cause a small disaster. While the components aren’t premium museum-grade, they are robust and easy to shuffle without losing your mind.

## The roles
The exact distribution depends on player count, but the gist is this: you’ve got a few hidden saboteurs who want to derail the tunnel, and the rest are ore-seeking diggers who just want a path to the gold. The roles are revealed at the end of the round, which means the fun is not just about strategy but also about reading people who are trying to hide their feelings about fellow dwarves.
The roles count is adjustable based on players; more players means more misdirection. That’s where the social aspect shines: you’ll be elbow-deep in suspicion by the time a card is drawn that threatens to ruin the tunnel as you know it.

## How a round unfolds
### Setup
Shuffle the role deck and deal them face down. Each player looks at their own role in secret. Place the starting tile on the table. The gold tiles go at the far end. Each player draws a few path cards to start their hand. The tension begins to rise as players eye their neighbors and wonder who might be the saboteur in disguise.

### Turn structure
On your turn you draw a card (to replenish your hand), then you can place a path card to extend the tunnel from the start place. You must connect to existing edges to keep the tunnel continuous. If you cannot place a card, you draw again. You can also play action cards to sabotage or to boost others, such as forcing a discard, blocking a path, or revealing a hint about an ally.

### End of round
The round ends in one of two dramatic ways: either a digger manages to connect the tunnel all the way to a gold card, or the deck runs out before a path reaches gold. Then everyone reveals their roles; diggers share the gold and saboteurs score points for their stealthy sabotage. Laughter erupts; accusations fly; someone claims to have known all along and is then called on their bluff. The rest of the table nods at the same time nobody wants to give their own secret to the crowd.

### Scoring and replayability
In the base game, points are awarded per player for successful digs; saboteurs earn points by causing the round to fail. The box lends itself to repeat play; you’ll quickly learn when your friends are bluffing, and you’ll master the art of looking innocent while fanning the flames of suspicion.

## Strategy and tips
This is where Saboteur becomes a little more than a party game; the decision-making is fairly intuitive, but the nuance is in guessing and planning around other players' actions.

### For the diggers
- Build with flexibility in mind. Try to leave routes open for future amendments rather than committing to a single path early on.
- Use map cards strategically to reveal the likely path to gold; don’t hoard them for a defeated cause.
- Read the table. People who hoard cards might be saboteurs who want to appear busy while the real path remains closed.

### For the saboteurs
- Sabotage makes a dramatic impact when performed at the right moment; don’t blow your load too early.
- Use rockfall or blocking cards to create choke points; then watch the confusion unfold as others attempt to route around your obstacles.
- Bluff with subtlety; a convincingly sincere patter about a supposed clearly visible path is often enough to draw diggers away from the truth.

### Reading the table
Part of Saboteur's charm is reading others' tells. Some players will always try to act suspicious; others are calm as a glacier, even as a tunnel collapses. The key is to time your reveal—if your role becomes known too soon, you lose the suspense and the game turns into a lecture on trust that nobody asked for.

## Design notes
The art direction leans into whimsy rather than grit, which makes sense given the game's tone. It’s a game about mining gold that doesn’t require a hard hat and a sense of fatalism about cave-ins. The component quality is more than adequate; the cards shuffle well, the icons are readable, and the layout makes it easy to teach new players in under five minutes.

## Accessibility and player counts
Saboteur shines with a handful of players. It scales nicely from small groups to moderately large gatherings. If you only have two players, the game becomes a fast, chaotic duel that’s more about card luck and misdirection than high-stakes strategy; it remains entertaining but is less of a social engine. The key is to know your group: if you can handle playful deceit without things turning personal, Saboteur works beautifully.

## The nostalgia factor
For many gamers, Saboteur is a nostalgic staple. It’s the kind of game you might have played in a dorm, on a weekend at a friend’s house, or at a convention table while people attempt to keep a straight face about a fake gold path that never existed. The sense of history around Saboteur is part of its charm; the older you are, the more of the Oh yeah vibe you get.

## The final call
If you want a quick, fun, and social game that permits a lot of laughter, Saboteur is a strong pick. It’s accessible, quick, and reliably funny. It scales to different numbers and remains fun across sessions. If you prefer heavy strategy, and long sessions, you may prefer something else. If you like deception and the challenge of reading others, this is a no-brainer.

## Final verdict
Saboteur remains a classic because it nails the core appeal: accessible rules, fast rounds, and a social engine that users come back to again and again. The variability among rounds and players means you’ll rarely get bored, and the humor ensures you’ll remember particularly ridiculous moments at the table.

## House rules and improvisations
- Quick-start variant: reduce the number of path cards drawn per turn to speed rounds up to 10-15 minutes each.
- Silent round: one round where players cannot speak about strategy or roles; you’ll hear more bluffing through body language than words.
- Team sabotage: in a lighthearted house rule, saboteurs form a temporary alliance to sabotage a single path now and later split the points; this is great for experimenting with dynamics and reduces one-sided blowouts.
- Role density tuning: adjust the number of saboteurs up or down based on player age, mood, and tolerance for tension; more saboteurs usually increases social complexity and chaos.
- Map-first: allow players to reveal one map card before any digging happens; this helps younger players understand the tunnel logic and creates a shared moment of amusement.

The setup is fast, so you can pivot to this after a heavier game without burning everyone out. A bowl of snacks, some light background music, and a few players who don’t take themselves too seriously is all you need to get a genuinely memorable session going.

## The bottom line
Saboteur is a solid, approachable classic that excels at social interaction, quick rounds, and a vibe that makes people smile even when their plans collapse into a comical heap. It’s not about deep calculus or long-term strategy; it’s about timing, reading people, and delivering a punchline when the tunnel finally reaches gold or spectacularly fails.
