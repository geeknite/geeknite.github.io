## How to play: basic flow and optional twists

The standard play is straightforward but flexible enough to adapt to your mood.

- Setup: Sit close, preferably with a beverage. Decide who will go first (rock-paper-scissors, coin flip, or the age-old “the dryer ate my socks” negotiation). Shuffle the deck and place it within reach.
- Turn structure: On your turn, draw a card and read it aloud. The card contains prompts, dares, or questions. Complete the prompt as described on the card. If you can’t comply, offer a playful alternative—this is your permission slip to improvise.
- Scoring: Dizzy Date isn’t about points. It’s about connection and laughs. Some couples track “moments of mutual sincerity” or “most ridiculous reveal,” but you can ignore scoring altogether if you’d rather not count how many times your partner says “that’s what she said.”
- Endgame: There’s no official end, but the rest of the night should feel earned, not forced. If you’re tired, you can switch to a shorter date mode. The real win is in the conversation, not the completion of a 100-card marathon.

A quick tip for first-timers: if you pull a prompt that sounds terrifying, embrace the ridiculousness. The sillier the prompt, the more likely you’ll both lose your self-consciousness and actually talk about something meaningful later—like your favorite fictional universe or the best Star Trek captain arc you’ve ever seen.

### Quick date mode (15–20 minutes)
If your schedule is tighter than your jeans after pizza night, you can pick a handful of cards, set a timer, and go. No need to chase every prompt to completion; the aim is to spark a short, chaotic, memorable conversation.

### Deep-dive mode (40–60 minutes)
If you’re in the mood for something deeper (and possibly sleep-deprived), commit to answering a set of cards in more detail. You’ll end up with more stories and, if you’re lucky, the kind of candid conversation that makes you feel like you’ve passed an adulthood initiation ritual.
