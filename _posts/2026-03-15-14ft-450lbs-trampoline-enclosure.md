## Assembly and setup (engineering, not magic)

Assembling a trampoline is a multi-step ritual that tests your patience, grip strength, and ability to read diagrams that look like child’s artwork. Here’s a practical breakdown.

- Step 1: Layout and frame assembly. Clear the area and lay out the components. Ensure the ground is level to avoid a wobble that will turn your bounce into a wobbly waltz.
- Step 2: Frame assembly. Attach the base rings, then the legs in a symmetrical pattern. A level is your best friend here; you don’t want a banana-shaped trampoline.
- Step 3: Spring installation. The springs are the heart and drama of this setup. Attach them in a crosswise pattern to ensure uniform tension. A helper can hold the mat in place while you route springs around the perimeter. 
- Step 4: Mat mounting. Place the jumping mat and connect all springs. This is the moment you realize you’re constructing a tiny, portable trampoline; it’s part science, part performance art.
- Step 5: Enclosure net. Install the enclosure poles and drape the net over the frame. The foam padding should be wrapped around each pole to prevent awkward, accidental face-to-pole interactions.
- Step 6: Safety checks and final test. Check that every spring is seated, the net is taut, and the zipper door latches. Do a slow bounce test before inviting your entire family to avoid mid-air drama.

Assembly time and tips
Most folks with a little DIY experience can finish in 60-90 minutes if everything goes smoothly. If you’re new to mechanical mayhem, pencil in 2-3 hours and consider inviting a neighbor to help. A good practice is to complete the frame first, then focus on the mat and net; the more you work in stages, the less cross-threaded your assembly becomes.

The 450-pound rating provides a general guideline for safe loading, but your actual experience depends on weight distribution and how many people are using the trampoline at once. For younger children, ensure there's at least one adult supervising and ready to intervene if necessary.

## Maintenance for longevity (to keep the bounce alive)

- Check every few months for signs of wear on the net, padding, and springs. If you see fraying or loose attachments, address them before use.
- Clean the mat and padding to remove dirt and grime that can degrade materials over time.
- Ensure the frame remains rust-free; wipe down exposed metal and re-tighten bolts if needed.
- Store the trampoline if you don’t plan to use it for extended periods. A dry, cool location helps preserve everything.
