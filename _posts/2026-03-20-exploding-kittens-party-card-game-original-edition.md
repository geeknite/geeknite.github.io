## Rules Overview

### The Core Mechanic

At its heart, Exploding Kittens Party Edition uses a simple loop: players draw cards, use action cards to alter turns, and try not to draw the dreaded exploding kitten unless they have a defuse. If the deck is nuked with an exploding kitten and there is no defuse to save you, you are out for the round. The last remaining player wins the round, and in a party scenario, a handful of rounds often yield consistent laughter, a few surprised gasps, and a surprising number of everyone simultaneously regretting their life choices that led them to this game night.

### Turn Flow

On your turn, you may play any number of card actions allowed by your hand, then draw a card to end your turn. Action cards can skip turns, force draws, steal cards, or otherwise complicate someone else’s night. The defuse cards are one of the most valuable assets, and using them at the right moment can turn a near-disaster into a dramatic save that earns you a chorus of cheers (or at least a few impressed grins).

### The Endgame Angst

Rounds move quickly, and the game ends when only one player remains. In a crowd, this can be a relief for the winner who gets to bask in the glory while the rest groan at their spectacular misplays. The pace is perfect for a party night: light, loud, and short enough that people want to play again instead of bailing out to the couch mid-sentence.

## Strategy Notes

Yes, there is a strategy shape to this chaos, though it is less about calculating odds and more about social calculus. You will quickly learn which players are risk-takers, who panics when the bag of tricks gets emptied, and who may have a calm, cold-face bluff ready for any moment. Use your defuses thoughtfully; running them into early rounds can leave you vulnerable when the late game arrives.

### Card Timing and Reads

Think of the deck as a character with a temper. It loves dramatic entrances and hates predictable acts. Hold onto powerful cards for moments when they will cause the most effect, not just when you draw them. If you can force a big draw on someone who tends to blunder with pressure, you can swing the round more effectively than with a single high-damage move.

### Targeting and Alliances (Temporary)

Alliances in a party game are mostly temporary and built on shared jokes and mutual embarrassment. Use the social bonds you have created to pass the ball around and keep people engaged. A well-timed exchange of favours or a playful accusation can alter the flow of rounds without requiring you to burn your strongest cards.
