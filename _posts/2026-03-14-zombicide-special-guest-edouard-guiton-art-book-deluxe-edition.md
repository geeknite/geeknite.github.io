Zombicide: Special Guest - Edouard Guiton - Art Book (Deluxe Edition) is the sort of art book that makes you rethink your life choices, usually around the moment you realize you own three different editions of the same zombie with slightly different expressions. ## What is this Deluxe Edition, exactly?
Before we get into the art, let’s level-set on what you’re actually getting when you crack open the Deluxe Edition box. This is not just a glossy coffee-table book with some high-res prints tucked inside. It is a curated, co-signed artifact that aims to be the companion you bring to your Zombicide sessions to explain why a certain boss zombie looks suspiciously like your ex-roommate in a chef’s hat.

### The Art Book: a gallery on your shelf
The centerpiece is Edouard Guiton’s art book, a compact tome that radiates with a finish that feels like sanded glass. The pages are thick, the bleed on the color is lush, and the textures have enough tactility to satisfy both thumb and eyeball. The art within breathes life into the zombified universe in ways you didn’t know you needed: moody lighting, dramatic silhouettes, and a surprising amount of character depth for a setting that’s usually all business ends and bitey bits. The book is laid out thematically, moving from early concept sketches to finalizations, and then into the softer, almost cinematic moments where the survivors react to the look of the antagonist. It’s not just “how I drew this” but “how I imagined the world behind the drawing,” and that’s the good kind of nerdy.

### Deluxe components: more than a book
Beyond the hardcover (which sits very well on a display shelf), the Deluxe Edition includes art prints, extra concept sheets, and an expanded foreword by Guiton himself. If you’re the type who frames book pages and puts them on a wall, you’ll be thrilled. If you’re the type who merely stacks things on a shelf and then forgets they exist, you’ll still be able to identify the spine from across the room because the cover screams “collectible.” The distribution of weight in the box is not just functional but pleasing; you get a satisfying heft, a little jingle of cards, and enough internal dividers to keep the aesthetic from turning into a litter box for your curiosity.

### Presentation and display value
The Deluxe Edition is designed with display in mind. The cover art glows in direct light the way a good highlighter glows under a blacklight poster. The inside pages balance white space with panel margins that give your eyes a moment of relief between grand escalations and character portraits. It’s the rare art book that feels as good to brief your friends with as it does to simply flip through when you’re procrastinating from real work.

## Edouard Guiton: the artist in the room
If you’re here for the man behind the linework, you won’t be disappointed. Edouard Guiton’s signature leans heavily into dynamic silhouettes, crisp negative space, and an almost editorial approach to composition. You’ll spot influences from classic pulp illustration, a touch of cinematic lighting, and the kind of ergonomic design sense that makes a character’s weapon feel really, really deliberate on the page.

### Style notes: line, shape, and storytelling
Guiton’s lines are confident without being mechanical. They carry weight and motion, giving even a static pose a sense of impending action. The shapes read cleanly, which matters in a fantasy-souls-like genre where you need to know instantly who is who and what they’re wielding. The color work leans toward saturated primaries with moody neutrals, a choice that makes the undead pop while keeping the survivors grounded. That balance is important—the art isn’t a poster; it’s a guide for the viewer to understand the stakes in a given moment.

### Where the personality shows up
One of the joys of this art book is how Edouard injects personality into otherwise generic silhouettes. A handheld weapon becomes character shorthand. A cloak becomes a narrative device for movement. Even zombie poses aren’t just about menace—they tell you something about their modeling, their gait, the designer’s sense of threat. It’s a reminder that in a zombie-setting, much of the fear comes from what you can deduce about your adversary with a single glance. This is where the art book earns its keep: it gives you a vocabulary for the game’s mood beyond the board text.

## The art book as a companion to Zombicide core
If you’ve played Zombicide, you know the game has two vibes: the tactical planning and the storytelling that emerges at the table. The Art Book doubles down on the latter, enriching your mental catalog of how the world looks and feels. It’s not a replacement for the rulebook or the campaign book, but it is a design dictionary for the game’s aesthetic. When you see a new survivor concept in the deluxe box, you can flip to a matching page in the art book to appreciate the thought process behind the character’s equipment, stance, and silhouette. It’s the kind of synergy that makes the whole product feel like more than a gimmick and more like an actual extension of the game’s universe.

### How the design conversation translates to play
The art direction influences how you imagine the fight scenes in your head. You’ll find yourself mentally narrating “the way the linework pulls your eye toward the threat” as you maneuver a stack of miniatures across a table. That’s not just pretty pictures; that’s cognitive schematics in action. It can make you braver at the table, or it can remind you to slow your roll when a particularly dramatic pose tells a taller-than-life story that the actual game might not support in physical balance. Either way, you’re getting a richer read of the game world, not simply a decorative coffee table token.

## Page quality, print detail, and tactile joy
Let’s get tactile for a moment. The Deluxe Edition pages use a substantial stock that feels rounded to the touch, not like a cheap, scratchy pamphlet. The ink density is high but not oppressive; you get sharp edges on the linework, and color gradients that translate well from concept page to print. The bleeds are generous, which means you can frame full-bleed art without the headache of unpredictable crop zones. It’s a surprisingly practical detail in a book that wants to feel premium. The matte finish helps with glare under bright lighting, making it friendlier for long reading sessions, something you’ll appreciate if you’re one of those “camp out in front of the art house” sort of fans.

### The print run and edition scope
From a collector’s standpoint, the Deluxe Edition is scoped to be a desirable, lasting item rather than a fleeting novelty. It’s not the kind of thing you buy to flip later; it’s the kind of thing you buy because you want to live with it on your shelf, on your desk, in your car’s glove box (okay maybe not your car, but you get the drift). The limited prints included in the package hold up well to framing, and the extra concept sheets provide a tactile trace of the design process that’s often missing in standard art books. If you like the “artist’s studio” vibe, this edition scratches that itch with a rare degree of honesty about the process.

## Layout, accessibility, and reading experience
A beautiful object is only half the battle if it’s hard to read. Fortunately, the layout team did a decent job of balancing imagery with annotation. The concept pages are labeled with succinct notes that don’t overburden the reader, while the final artworks include short captions to connect the pieces to the game’s canon. The typography stays legible, with a respectful nod to the game’s own iconography and terminology. You won’t need a magnifying glass to appreciate the details, but you might want one if you’re a true art nerd who notices every subtle cross-hatching on the armor plates.

## Value proposition: is the Deluxe Edition worth it?
Let’s talk numbers (or at least value). The Deluxe Edition sits at a premium tier. If you’re primarily a gamer who wants a nice accessory to complement your play sessions, you’ll still get solid value from the printed art, the extra concept sheets, and the gallery-like experience. If you’re a pure collector, the edition offers a strong case for purchase, especially if Edouard Guiton’s signature style resonates with you and you want a single curated piece that celebrates the collaboration. If you’re more casual about Zombicide, there’s a risk you might feel the price tag is a little top-heavy relative to the content you actually use during gameplay. However, the overall package does a convincing job of translating “deluxe” into tangible, display-worthy value rather than mere pretension. In short: it’s worth it if you value the art, the artist, and the storytelling vibe, and it might be more of a splurge if you’re more of a “card-and-dice” purist.

### Comparisons to the standard edition
Compared to a standard edition, the Deluxe Edition’s strongest cards are the curated prints, the richer layout, and the personal touch from Edouard Guiton. You’ll likely prefer the deluxe if you’re into the visual world-building that’s at the core of Zombicide. If you’re primarily focused on gameplay efficiency and minimal table clutter, you might find the deluxe’s extra components optional rather than essential. Either way, you’ll emerge with a product that feels more like a collectible coffee-table volume than a simple reference guide, and that intangible value is part of the appeal.
