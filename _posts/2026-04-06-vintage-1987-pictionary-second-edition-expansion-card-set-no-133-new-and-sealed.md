## Packaging, Sealing, and Authenticity

### The Sealed Status: Why It Matters

New and sealed today is not just a sales point; it’s a statement about quality control from a suburban toy company in 1987. When you purchase a sealed expansion card set, you’re hoping for unfaded colors, intact perforations, and cards that haven’t been chewed by a thirsty dog or folded into a small origami crane by an overenthusiastic grandparent. The sealed status also preserves the original box art and the meta experience: the little ritual of breaking the seal, sliding out the inner tray, and letting your imagination run as wild as the label typography.

The challenge in vintage sealed items, of course, is moisture, temperature, and that intangible thing we call “box nostalgia.” If you’re a collector, a sealed No. 133 is a potential centerpiece or a valuable shelf ornament. If you’re a casual nostalgist, it’s a game-night time capsule that will probably still bring smiles when you finally decide to crack it open for a private gaming session with one other willing human.

### Handling and Storage Tips

- Keep it away from direct sunlight; the 80s preferred sunlight to synth-pop in the background, but not on your cards. Bleaching from UV rays can fade the vivid card borders.
- Temperature stability is your friend. A steady room temperature, away from heat vents or damp basements, keeps the card stock from warping.
- Use acid-free sleeves for display if you’re going to pull the cards and flip them, but if you’re a purist who believes in original packaging, you may want to leave them as-is for a true display piece.
- Consider a basic microclimate where the box stays in the dark for most of the day; the 80s vintage aesthetic often goes well with a little noir-lit shelf lighting.

### Authenticity Signals to Watch For
- Tangible box fluting in the corners: early packaging often showed slight bending from storage. A pristine outer corner is a rare delight.
- Card stock texture: 1980s card stock usually has a slightly rough matte feel; if yours feels like very slick plastic, you may be staring at a later reprint—though for a sealed set, this is less likely.
- Printing quirks: some card decks had minor color misregistration or tiny typographic inconsistencies that endearing to collectors, not deal-breakers.

## Condition and Grading for Vintage Expansions

### What Does “New and Sealed” Really Mean Here?
In the realm of collectibles, “new and sealed” can range from pristine mint to merely unused with a few minuscule creases. For this No. 133 expansion, you’re largely looking at a box that has never been opened, with the potential risk of slight box aging, corner wear, or a seal that’s brittle rather than supple. The value proposition here hinges on the seal integrity and the absence of any moisture or odors that suggest the box spent a decade in a damp closet.

### Grading Considerations for No. 133
- Mint (M): The box is pristine, the seal is intact, no signs of handling.
- Near Mint (NM): The box is clean, with minor imperfections on the exterior but no structural damage.
- Excellent (EX): The box shows light wear, but contents are presumed untouched.
- Good (G) and below: If you fear you’re in the land of crumple, you’re probably better off buying the open-set as a separate item and saving the sealed one for display.

The grading conversation is often more nuanced with expansion sets because the core of the “sealed” experience is the ritual, not just the component quality. If you’re buying as an investment, you’ll want to verify the seal integrity and, if possible, secure a provenance that confirms it hasn’t been resealed—a claim that can be a red flag in the world of vintage collectibles.
