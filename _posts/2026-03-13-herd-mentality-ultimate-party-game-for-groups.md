## What is Herd Mentality?
Herd Mentality is a party game about predicting group consensus. In each round, a prompt card is revealed. Everyone writes down an answer that they think the rest of the group will find popular. Then all answers are revealed, and the “herd” picks one of the most common responses. If your answer matches the herd’s chosen response, you score points. The more you thread the needle between “clever” and “common,” the more points you rack up.

This is not a game about being the loudest or the most outrageous. It’s a game about reading vibes, spotting patterns, and embracing the chaos that comes when a dozen people try to sculpt a shared memory from a handful of words. The humor tends to be social rather than slapstick, which means you’ll often hear a flurry of “Oh, that’s so true!” followed by giggles and the inevitable: “We are definitely the most predictable group on the planet.”

The core appeal is familiarity. Everyone has opinions, experiences, and a ventriloquized sense of what “the herd” would choose. Herd Mentality taps into that common ground and amplifies it, turning ordinary dinners into a living meme machine. It’s the kind of game you can bring to a barbecue, a dorm-room marathon, or a chaotic family gathering where someone always brings a fruitcake that looks like a rejected prop from a 90s CGI movie.

## Components and Setup
A typical copy of Herd Mentality contains a deck of question cards, a pad of answer sheets for each player, pencils, a timer, and a small score board with tokens. Some editions include dry-erase boards or a compact dry-erase marker, depending on the publishing run. The minimalism is deliberate: there aren’t fancy props to juggle, which lowers the barrier to entry and keeps the focus on the round itself.

Setup is a breeze: shuffle the question cards, deal each player an answer sheet, hand out pencils, and pick a starting player. The timer is set for a brisk 60 seconds per round—enough to draft something clever without letting the room devolve into a two-hour TED Talk about the ethics of predicting others’ answers. In practice, rounds fly by in 3–5 minutes, and that pace is part of the game’s charm.

In terms of accessibility, Herd Mentality scores well. The prompts skew toward the broad and relatable—things you’d encounter in high-school group projects, on social media feeds, or during late-night college dorm debates. There are occasional prompts that lean risqué or cheeky—part of the spice that elevates the party vibe, but not so extreme as to alienate a family-friendly crowd. It’s a broad-appeal design.

## How to Play: A Round-by-Round Walkthrough
1. The Prompt Card: The round begins with the reveal of a prompt. Examples include “Best excuse to leave a boring meeting,” “What would you say to get out of a surprise group photo?”, or “A fictional product that would totally fail because of one essential design flaw.” The prompt sets the tone for the entire round.
2. Writing Time: Each player writes down an answer that they think others will consider popular. The fun emerges in the tension between individuality and groupthink. Some players lean into inside jokes; others aim for generic but widely shareable responses. You’ll see a mix of subtlety, cleverness, and occasionally shameless pandering to the herd.
3. Revelation and Scoring: All answers are revealed. The herd (the majority) then votes on which answer best represents the group’s typical choice. In many editions, players who contributed the chosen answer score points. In some variants, you get a smaller sprinkle of points for being part of the herd but not the outspoken hero of the round—this keeps everyone engaged, including the shy optimists who fear judgment yet secretly love attention rings the bell of victory.
4. Rinse and Repeat: The next round begins with a new prompt and a new herd, often with the same players. The score board accumulates points, and the session ends when the deck runs dry or you hit a pre-agreed target score. The optional “fast rounds” variant can speed things up for larger groups and keep energy at fever pitch.

The flow is intentionally simple, which is the whole point. The fewer rules you have to memorize, the more you can GIF-keyboard-smash yourself into a moment of shared hilarity. And the more you allow yourself to lean into the group’s vibe, the more likely you are to stumble into comedic gold that becomes the talk of the party for weeks.

## Who Should Play Herd Mentality?
- Large groups: The game shines when there are 6–12 players in the room. The more people you add, the richer the herd dynamics become. Shared jokes multiply, and so do the opportunities for misreadings and hilariously wrong predictions.
- Friends who enjoy inside jokes: If your crew already has running jokes about cereal mascots or obscure 90s sitcoms, Herd Mentality gives those jokes a platform and a chorus line.
- Social hosts: If you like to watch dynamics unfold, this game acts as a social amplifier. You’ll observe how different personas respond under the pressure of “what would the herd do?” It’s like watching a group therapy session, but with more chips and fewer couches.
- Newbie-friendly circles: People who aren’t into heavy strategy or long setup will appreciate the quick ramp-up. The game rewards creativity and social perception more than memory or math skills, which makes it accessible for players who prefer vibes over rules.

Who might not love it? If you’re a single-player or a lone-wolf gamer who thrives on heavy strategy and perfect information, Herd Mentality may feel like a social butterfly wearing your favorite hoodie. It’s not a brain-burner in the same way as some strategy titles; it’s a social amusement ride with occasional spikes of cleverness. Also, groups that prize “no repeats” might find the game’s herd-chasing logic a little frustrating if everyone circles the same popular answers.

## Tips for Getting the Most Out of Herd Mentality
- Embrace the herd, but bet on the rogue: The best rounds come when a player nails a surprising yet plausible answer that still aligns with the group’s vibe. Don’t be afraid to go a little rogue if you sense the herd leaning toward a safe but boring pick.
- Use inside jokes strategically: If your group has running gags, a tailored prompt can leverage them for an extra layer of laughter. Just be mindful not to overdo it—consistently leaning into a tightly knit joke can alienate newer players.
- Encourage light strategy, not analysis paralysis: The timer is your friend. If you overthink, you’ll miss the moment. Aim for the first instinct that also feels broadly correct. The herd rewards timely, confident choices more than slow, pedantic ones.
- Rotate the “herd” role: Let different players be the one who reads the herd’s choice aloud or who handles scoring. This keeps energy high and distributes the spotlight in a natural way.
- Mix up prompts with A/B options: If your group wants to experiment, you can introduce a variant where you predict a majority choice between two options. It’s a neat twist that scales well with varying group sizes.

## House Rules and Variants for Extra Flavor
- Lightning rounds: Cut the timer to 30 seconds per round for a zippy gaming session. Great for big groups where you want more rounds in less time.
- No duplicates, no problem: Add a rule that the same answer cannot be used twice in a single round. This challenges players to think more creatively while preserving fairness.
- All-play “draft the herd”: After seeing all answers, each player secretly chooses one they think best represents the herd, and the person who wrote that chosen answer earns a bonus. This adds a layer of strategy and subtle persuasion.
- The parlor trick variant: Allow players to present their answer aloud before writing it. This adds performance value—some players become street-performers in 60-second bursts, which is a delightful change of pace.

If you’re chasing a more structured, competition-forward vibe, you can also pair Herd Mentality with a quick post-game debrief where players describe why they picked their answers and what they believed the herd would choose. This turns a simple party game into a social experiment, minus the ethics compliance forms.

-Codenames: Both games rely on grouping dynamics, but Codenames emphasizes wordplay and deduction, while Herd Mentality thrives on social read and herd psychology. If your group loves wordplay puns, Codenames might be your jam; if you crave the chaos of a collective hunch, Herd Mentality is the better fit.
- What Do You Meme? or Monikers: These games lean into topical humor and inside jokes, often with a heavier emphasis on laughter and pop culture. Herd Mentality sits a notch below in explicit humor but excels in broad accessibility and repeat playability with a long tail of inside jokes.

In short: the game is not a hard-core strategy title, but it’s not a one-note gag fest either. It lives in a sweet spot where social chemistry matters as much as clever writing, and that’s why it tends to endure in friend groups that rotate hosts every few weeks.

The production is clean and unpretentious, which matches the game’s philosophy. Aesthetically, the art direction leans toward playful, approachable humor rather than edgy shock value; this helps Herd Mentality stay inclusive and easy to introduce to new players who might be wary of party games with a sharper edge.

Accessibility considerations are important here. The prompts avoid heavy reading requirements, which helps non-native speakers or players with variable literacy levels keep up without frustration. You’ll still want a comfortable gaming environment: enough space for people to write, a quiet corner to reduce distraction, and a few snacks that aren’t too crunchy to interrupt the discussion between rounds.

## Practical Thoughts: Timing, Space, and Social Energy
- Timing: A typical session with 6–8 players can run around 45–90 minutes depending on how many rounds you play and how chatty your crew gets. If you’re playing with a larger group, plan for more rounds or set a target score to cap the session. The timer is your friend here; don’t let a single round become a group therapy session about who’s “actually” the herd leader.
- Space: You don’t need a grand room. A round table or a comfy living room setup where everyone has enough space to scribble their answer is perfect. The more comfortable the players, the more likely they are to scribble wilder answers and enjoy the ride.
- Social energy: The game thrives when players feel safe to be silly. If someone seems hesitant, a quick nudge: “No wrong answers here; it’s all about what the herd would pick.” The best laughs come from borderline ridiculous answers that somehow become the crowd’s top pick.
