What’s in the box

One of the first things you notice is how the diacritics affect readability in real time. That is part of the charm: a reminder that language, context, and speed matter as much as raw vocabulary when you’re playing a fast-paced guessing game.

Clue construction and strategy in a language with diacritics
If you’re new to Codenames, you’ll learn the same high-speed planning skills in the Czech edition: think about two or three possible connections, then choose a single word that encapsulates the most efficient path to your team’s target. In this edition, the presence of diacritics nudges your brain toward a sharper, more precise mental map. You might be tempted to lean toward safer clues—generic nouns or categories—early in the game to minimize misinterpretation risk. But the magic of Codenames often comes from taking a calculated risk. The Czech words provide both risk and opportunity: a well-chosen clue can cascade through multiple of your team’s targets, while a miss can send your team spiraling laughingly into a linguistic mudslide.

Strategy tips for spymasters
- Start with shorter, sharper clues. In languages with diacritics, a one-word cue can link two or three cards more cleanly than trying to force a long, elegant clue that relies on a rare Czech phrase.
- Embrace localization-friendly references. Czech culture, geography, and daily life yield a treasure trove of accessible, crowd-pleasing clue options that are still tricky for the opposing team to map.
- Use non-linguistic cues sparingly. The “two cards” or “three cards” flags are still your friends. They can help your teammates tune into your mental map rather than chasing semantic snakes across the board.
- Read the room for language fluency. If your group includes learners of Czech, scale your clues to their comfort level. The beauty of Codenames is inclusion: you want everyone to feel clever, not left in the dust by an advanced pun you forgot to translate.

How to play: the basics and the Czech-specific flavor
The core rules you know from the English edition hold steady here. One team’s spymaster gives a clue that is a single word plus a number. The number indicates how many cards on the board can be connected to that clue. The team then works to guess which cards match the clue while the other team tries to do the same with their own cues. The black assassin card remains a higher-stakes twist you want to avoid at all costs. If you guess one of your own team’s cards, you can keep going; if you guess an opposing team’s card, turn passes to the other team and you’ve just handed them a small victory on a silver plate. If you hit the assassin, you’ve lost the game immediately.

In the Czech Edition, the same rhythm applies, but the language requires you to juggle nuance more often than not. Because every target card is Czech, a single word can connect two or three of your team’s targets in ways that may not be obvious to a non-Czech speaker. The spymaster’s job is to translate their strategy into a single word—and to read the room when multilingual teammates suggest alternate interpretations. It’s a little theatre, a little puzzle, and a lot of social improvisation.

Clue construction and strategy: what works best in Czech
- Use clear, category-free cues for the early rounds. The fewer moving parts in your clue, the better the chance your teammates will understand your intent quickly.
- Build a mental map of word clusters. In a language with rich morphology, some words will share roots or suffixes that help you guide your teammates toward a cluster of cards that map onto a single concept.
- Avoid overly obscure references unless your team is fluent. You’ll save time by aiming for shared cultural touchpoints rather than inside jokes that require a dictionary and a crystal ball.
- Keep an eye on the risk card. If you’re trailing, you might push a riskier clue that tips the game in your favor—but be prepared to explain the leap to your teammates when the board doesn’t cooperate.
