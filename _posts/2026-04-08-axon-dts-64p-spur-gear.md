## Materials, Finish, and Tolerances

Materials matter. You don’t want a shiny, brittle thing in a high-load micro-gearset. The DTS 64P line is typically offered in several materials:

- Delrin: Great for experimental rigs and educational kits. Lightweight, forgiving, and relatively quiet. The downside? Under heavy loads, Delrin can creep and wear faster than metal under the same conditions.
- Aluminum: Common for real robotics projects where rigidity and heat dissipation matter. Expect better wear resistance and less deformation under torque, but you’ll pay with increased weight and a need for precise fitting.
- Steel: For extreme duty cycles or survival kits where you expect the screws to be stressed and the belt to last forever, steel is the heavy metal cousin of the family. It’s not typically the first choice for micro gears due to weight, but it exists for special builds.

Finish and tolerances: with 64P, you want clean tooth profiles. The Axon DTS line tends to emphasize consistent tooth geometry, smooth flank surfaces, and minimal runout. If you see chatter during a test run or feel a rubbing sensation when turning by hand, that’s a sign you might need to deburr a little more, or to check your shaft alignment and mounting screws. A good practice is to verify that your shaft seats are clean, your screws aren’t bottoming out, and your bearing blocks are not introducing lateral play.

Too little lubrication and you’ll see higher friction and wear; too much and you’ll drag the gears through viscous drag. The sweet spot is a thin film that reduces metal-to-metal contact while still allowing the teeth to mesh cleanly.

### A note on lubrication and noise

One of the reasons micro gears are so beloved in hobby circles is the whisper-quiet performance you can coax out of them. The 64P variant is no exception when treated right. Penny-pinching on grease or oil can lead to audible whine at higher RPMs and a hot day in the workshop. A light synthetic oil or a dab of plastic-safe grease (for Delrin gears) can keep things humming without turning your rig into a tiny mechanical banshee. And remember: noise is often a symptom of something else going on—misalignment, burrs, or a loose set screw that’s letting the shaft do a little waltz of its own.

## Modding, Tuning, and Tips for Your Build

Here are some practical tips that won’t require a PhD in gear theory:

- Measure before you buy: Use calipers to verify bore size, pitch diameter estimates, and the width of your gear slot in the gearbox. Don’t assume the drawing is exact to your part in your hand. Small variations compound quickly.
- Deburr with care: Micro gears can be sharp around the tooth tips. A quick pass with a fine file or a gentle deburring tool can save you from wonky meshing.
- Align the axis: Shaft alignment is critical. If your gear isn’t perfectly square to the input shaft, you’ll introduce lateral load that accelerates wear and introduces backlash. Adjustable motor mounts and alignment shims are your friends here.
- Choose the right lubricant: For Delrin, go with a light silicone-based or synthetic wax. For metal gears, a light machine oil or a specialized gear grease works, but avoid thick greases that can cause stiction at low speeds.
- Break-in period: Don’t expect peak performance right out of the box. A few minutes of slow-speed cycling under light load can help seat teeth and reduce initial chatter.
- Temperature check: If your build runs hot, that extra heat can change tolerances enough to alter meshing dynamics. Heat sinks and good airflow aren’t just for big motors; they help micro gear trains stay in their happy place too.

### Maintenance routine
A quick maintenance ritual keeps the gears singing:
- Inspect for wear lines along the tooth edges after the first hour of operation.
- Re-torque set screws after a few cycles; vibration can loosen things that you think are tight.
- Clean the gears when you service the gearbox to avoid dust build-up.
- Re-lubricate sparingly on a schedule that matches your usage: more runs, more lubrication; less use, less lubrication.
