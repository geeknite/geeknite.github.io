## Condition notes and seller disclosures

In the used market, condition notes are your Rosetta Stone. They tell you how much life a box has left in its cardboard body—and how likely it is to play nice with a modern playgroup. Here are the common labels you’ll encounter on a Devastation Expert Starter listing, rewritten for clarity and a pinch of humor:

- Light edge wear: The corners show romance with a table edge. It’s visible upon close inspection and doesn’t affect playability, though it may impact grading if you’re into that kind of thing.
- Card sleeves wear: If the decks were sleeved, you might see signs of sleeve wear or minor border wear on some cards. This is normal for a product that has moved between tables and storage boxes and back again.
- Centering and edge condition: Expect a little misalignment on some cards. It’s a nostalgia badge, not a fatal flaw. If you’re building for fun rather than a tournament, this is perfectly acceptable.
- Staples and inserts: The rulebook and strategy inserts could be creased or lightly scuffed. They’re for reference, not the main event.
- Storage marks: A few marks from being in a box or between decks. These might appear as minor whitening on the card edges or slight surface wear.
- Flood and humidity notes: If the box spent time in a humid arena (the basement poker room of a friend’s apartment counts), you might find a few cards with rare foggy edges. Overall, though, the cards themselves should be readable and playable.

What this means for you: you’re buying a piece of MTG history, not a pristine, never-touched artifact. If you want a showroom condition, you’ll want to buy new. If you want the sense of telling stories about nights swamped with draft queues and blinking mana dials, this might be your jam. The trick is to read the seller’s notes carefully, inspect the photos, and ask questions if the listing is unclear.

### Condition-checking tips before you buy

If you’re considering purchasing this used Devastation Expert Starter, here are practical checks you can perform or request from the seller:

- Request clear photos of both decks, the lands pool, and the rulebook. Look for visible wear on card borders, corners, and surfaces.
- Confirm the decks aren’t missing core cards. A boxed starter should have a cohesive deck list; missing pieces reduce value and playability significantly.
- Inspect the tokens and any promos. If you see token shapes that look worn or printed tokens with damage, you’ll want to consider the impact on your first game night.
- Check for additional inserts or separators. Some sellers include protective sleeves or deck boxes; their absence doesn’t ruin the experience, but it’s nice to have.
- Ask about storage history. Boxes that have spent time in humid areas may show more edge whitening or surface fogging on cards.

Reading these notes and asking the right questions will help you make a safe purchase decision. A well-documented listing is worth more than a perfectly pristine box because it signals trust and honest communication.
