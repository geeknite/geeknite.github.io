## What 4mm and 5mm actually mean in real life
First things first: the numbers. The outer diameter of cable housing isn’t just a trivia contest answer; it dictates how well the housing resists compression under load, how easily it threads into frames and cable stops, and how smoothly your cables feed through long runs, turns, and brake bosses. The 4mm size is the standard for many derailleur and some road brake setups, while 5mm is commonly recommended for brake systems that demand a bit more rigidity and reduced flex, such as certain V-brakes or hydraulic-internal routing scenarios.

Think of it like choosing shoes for a long hike. A 4mm housing is your lightweight sneaker: comfortable for casual riding, lighter in weight, and enough for most road gearing. A 5mm housing is your sturdy hiking boot: bulkier, a touch heavier, but much more resistant to compression when you’re standing on the pedals and the chain is singing under a hill-laden chorus. The trade-off is stiffness versus weight and ease of routing. In practice, if you’re building a daily commuter or a drop-bar road machine with long stretches of smooth pavement, 4mm often suffices. If you’re tackling steep grades on a mountain bike or a city go-anywhere setup with aggressive braking and longer cable runs, 5mm can feel like a safety feature you forgot to mention in the spec sheet.

### How diameter affects feel and performance
- Compression under load: 5mm housing can be stiffer, which reduces the amount of “squish” when you pull the lever hard. This can translate into quicker braking response on rough descents but can also feel a touch harsher in the lever feel.
- Friction and efficiency: with proper liners and good fittings, both sizes can be efficient; the real constraint is how well the housing is terminated and whether there’s any kinking along the run.
- Routing flexibility: 4mm is more forgiving in tight bends, smaller downtube routes, and frames with tight cable stops. 5mm can struggle with narrow passages unless you’ve got careful sizing and smooth bends.
- Weight and cost: 4mm generally weighs less and costs less per meter than the 5mm counterpart, but the price gap isn’t dramatic unless you buy premium brands in bulk.

This is where the geeky part gets fun. The nominal diameter is just the starting signal; the real performance is a product of the housing’s build quality, the liner material, the type of end ferrules you use, and how clean your routing is. If you’ve ever had a cable housing that binds in a frame notch or refused to thread cleanly through a small ferrule, you know that the best diameter in the world won’t help if you don’t plan the route properly.

## Pricing reality: price per meter across brands and categories
Now the money talk. Per meter pricing for bike outer casing varies widely based on brand, material, liner type, and whether you’re buying in a pre-cut kit or as a bulk spool. Here’s a practical snapshot you can sink your cycling wallet into:

- Generic/entry-level 4mm housing: roughly $0.50 to $0.95 per meter. These are the “get the job done” options for most city bikes and light use. You’ll see these bundled in budget kits or sold as generic cabled sets at big-box bike shops.
- Mid-range 4mm housing: about $0.95 to $1.60 per meter. Expect better liners (e.g., nylon or PTFE) and smoother inner feeds. These are popular for everyday riders who ride more than 3 days a week and want a bit more glide when the weather is uncooperative.
- Premium 4mm housing: $1.60 to $3.00 per meter. Fiendishly smooth, low-friction liners, and often more robust end ferrules. Great for those who chase whisper-quiet shifts and a bike that feels like it’s gliding on air.
- Generic/entry-level 5mm housing: $0.70 to $1.20 per meter. The 5mm variants tend to sit around this range for many budget lines. If you need the extra stiffness for braking performance, this is often enough for casual builds.
- Mid-range 5mm housing: $1.20 to $2.20 per meter. More robust coatings and liners, better resistance to compression, and a nicer feel on aggressive braking.
- Premium 5mm housing: $2.00 to $4.00+ per meter. The high-end end includes special liner materials (PTFE, low-friction glides), excellent abrasion resistance, and a finish that treats you like you’re building a turbine scooter for a superhero. These are the dream-weaver options if you’re chasing top-shelf feel and longevity.

Prices can spike when you add ends, ferrules, cable kits, and shipping. If you’re buying on a per-meter basis, calculator mode is your friend: a 3.0 meter run that transitions through a couple of frame bosses will add up, while a longer, linear route might be more economical per meter when buying in larger spools. The key is to compare apples to apples: are you buying the same liner quality, ferrules, and end fittings? If not, you’re not just paying for diameter—you’re paying for the whole friction-fighting ecosystem.

## How to choose based on application
Let’s translate the diameter debate into real-world scenarios. Here are common use cases and how to think about diameter choice:

- City commuter with a short daily ride: 4mm is usually fine, especially if you value light weight and easy routing. Budget-friendly 4mm options will do the job and won’t leave your wallet in tears when you replace it next year.
- Road setup with long, smooth runs: 4mm is a solid workhorse. If you’re chasing a silky shift and don’t ride in extreme conditions, this diameter typically checks every box.
- Mountain bike or trail rider with aggressive braking: 5mm can be a smarter choice for braking efficiency and durability over rough terrain. Longer runs and tougher dirt can compress standard 4mm more than you’d like, and a little extra diameter helps maintain performance.
- Hybrid or gravel bikes: these often blend uses, so you might prefer 4mm for light shifting lines with occasional stiff braking or 5mm where you have longer braking segments on tougher terrain.
- Tight frames or special routing: if your frame has tight bends, 4mm tends to thread through easier. If you must go 5mm, plan for smoother routes with gentle bends and consider pre-bent ferrules to minimize kinks.

### Quick tips for choosing a diameter without overthinking it
- Check your current setup: what is already working? If your brakes feel fine and the shifts are smooth, you may not need to upgrade to 5mm unless you’re chasing a specific performance gain.
- Consider the run length: long straight sections with a few sharp bends tend to be more forgiving with 4mm; long, brutal braking zones and rough terrain can benefit from 5mm.
- Match with lever feel: you’ll notice a difference in lever action. If you frequently ride with gloves or in gloves, a stiffer housing (often 5mm) can feel more predictable.
- Don’t forget linings and ferrules: the diameter alone isn’t everything. A better liner can dramatically improve performance and longevity across both diameters.

## Installation tips and field notes
A good install beats a perfect spec any day. Here are practical tips that will save you time and headaches:

- Measure twice, trim once: measure your exact routing path before cutting. A minute miscalculation can cause you to waste a meter or more of material.
- Avoid sharp bends: aim for gentle curves and round ferrules at the frame openings. Sharp bends can cause kinking and poor housing performance, regardless of diameter.
- Pre-lube only in the right places: avoid dumping lubricant inside the housing unless you’re specifically using a liner that benefits from it. Most modern housings rely on the liner to reduce friction, not lube inside the run.
- End ferrules matter: choose ferrules that fit snugly and are designed for the diameter you’re using. A loose ferrule is a pretend upgrade and will lead to rattle, misalignment, and the occasional dramatic squeak.
- Secure the run: zip ties, cable guides, and frame-mounted clips help keep the housing stable. A wandering housing is a whisper in your ear every time you brake.
- Test before you ride: a quick check of the shifts and brakes after routing ensures nothing is binding. If something feels off, recheck the bends and ferrules rather than forcing the housing to bend more.

## Real-world testing and personal notes
In my own lab, a.k.a. the garage that occasionally smells like a workshop and pizza, I swapped 4mm and 5mm runs on a city-derived bike with a mix of commuter lanes and a couple of steeper hills. I measured: price per meter, ease of routing, and the lever feel under a standard daily ride. The results were instructive, with a few surprises:

- The price delta between mid-range 4mm and 5mm is often small enough to ignore for most riders, unless you’re buying premium variants by the spool. If you’re price-conscious, start with a mid-range 4mm for most uses and reserve 5mm for braking-specific routes.
- In a straight line, the 5mm housing isn’t a magical brake-dispelling talisman, but it does feel a touch stiffer. That stiffness translates into perceived immediate response during heavier braking, especially on longer descents. It’s not a magical acceleration, just a consistency improvement you notice after a few rides.
- On tight city turns, 4mm wins for ease of threading and less risk of kink-induced friction. If your bike is a pure urban flier with a lot of small frames and corners, 4mm is often the practical choice.
- Liners and coatings matter more than diameter when it comes to long-term quietness. A good PTFE-lined 4mm can feel smoother than a basic 5mm without a liner advantage.

These findings aren’t a formal scientific paper with peer reviews, but they reflect the kind of practical tradeoffs you’ll encounter in day-to-day bike maintenance. The bottom line is simple: diameter matters, but the rest of the setup matters more. The best discount you can offer yourself is to plan your route and pick a diameter that aligns with your typical ride profile rather than chasing the latest marketing buzzword.

## Where to buy and what to watch out for
The bike-casing market is a mix of factory-direct online stores, traditional bike shops, and warehouse-style retailers. Here are practical tips to avoid ending up with the wrong product:

- Read the product spec: confirm the diameter (4mm or 5mm) and the liner type. Some listings call it “4mm housing with reinforced liner,” which can mean different things across brands.
- Check kit contents: if you’re buying a kit, ensure it includes ferrules and end caps compatible with your frame. Some kits are missing these critical pieces and can derail a project.
- Consider compatibility with your lever and derailleur: certain levers require specific housing sizes for the best performance. If you’re upgrading for a new build, double-check compatibility first.
- Look for reputable sellers: trust primarily those with a track record for cycling components. The internet is full of discount-bait traps and questionable hybrids that may not deliver the expected performance.
- Read real user reviews: short blurbs can help you gauge how a particular 4mm or 5mm line behaves under actual use conditions (city traffic, rain, mud, etc.). If the reviews mention binding, stiff feel, or fraying under minor wear, take note.

Pricing varies by region and currency, but the general guidance above should help you set a rough budget. If you’re buying from a store, you can often negotiate a small discount for multiple meters or a kit that includes necessary ferrules. If you’re buying online, factor in shipping, especially if you’re purchasing small quantities from a retailer that ships from overseas.

## Quick install tips and maintenance mindset
- Keep a tidy workspace: small screws, ferrules, and other hardware can hide in the creases of a workbench. A magnetized tray helps.
- Plan the route with your inner cable in mind: a smooth feed makes for less friction and a more precise shift or brake action.
- Inspect frequently: a quick look at your housing after a few months of weather exposure can save you from a sudden squeak or a total hardware failure.
- Replace in pairs: if you’re replacing a long run, consider doing both sides or the entire setup if you’re chasing smoother performance across the drivetrain. It’s more about feel than simply one length at a time.

## Final recommendation
If your ride is mostly urban, with a mix of light hills, and you want a blend of cost efficiency and dependable performance, a mid-range 4mm housing is the pragmatic default. It offers good friction characteristics, easy routing, and broad compatibility with most modern derailleurs and road brakes. If you’re a weekend warrior who tackles long descents with aggressive braking or you run long routes with frequent downhill segments, consider stepping up to 5mm, especially if your frame routing is a bit on the spicy side and you want extra compression resistance.

In short:
- 4mm: great for most road and city riders who value light weight and easy installation.
- 5mm: a safer bet for braking-heavy setups and rougher routes where extra stiffness can help maintain control.

If you’re unsure, start with a mid-range 4mm kit that includes essential ferrules and a basic liner, then ride a few weeks. If you notice significant flex, rubbing, or spongy braking response during heavy braking in descents, upgrade to 5mm or try a premium 4mm with a superior liner. Your future self on a long ride will thank you for not overthinking it and just riding the bike you’ve built.
