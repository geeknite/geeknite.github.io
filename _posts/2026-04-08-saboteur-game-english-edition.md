## How to Play: The Gist (Roles, Turns, and Tunnels)

### Roles: Miner, Saboteur, and the Never-Boring Middle Ground
In Saboteur, players are secretly assigned roles. Some are miners who want to locate treasure by building a path from the start card to a treasure card. Others are saboteurs who subtly – or not-so-subtly – derail that plan. The beauty lies in the tension: you may be a miner who suspects your best friend is secretly sabotaging you or a saboteur who pretends to be a loyal miner to avoid tipping your hand.

The number of saboteurs is relative to players, so you’ll spend the early rounds debating who might be under the hood while someone quietly slides a trap card into the mix. The English text makes it easy to memorize your role and your goal without needing an onlooker to translate the rulebook for you every other minute.

### Turn Structure: Draw, Play, Hint, Cross Your Fingers
A typical turn looks like this: draw a card, play a card (path, action, or tool), and hope your plan holds together. Path cards extend the tunnel network from the start to the treasure cards. Action cards can block, remove, or fix tools – you know, the usual “touch nothing, because it might explode” vibe. The cunning bit is that even if you’re a miner, your path can be ruined by someone who is not your friend (or maybe your friend wearing a different hat).

Turn after turn, players reveal whether they’re keeping a low profile as saboteurs or pretending to be earnest miners. The social element is the real star here: guessing who to trust (and who to betray) becomes more important than the path you’re laying. The English edition makes these social negotiations easier because you’re not squinting at tiny, unreadable text while the clock ticks down on “the perfect moment to flip the card.”

### The Endgame: One Path, One Winner, Several Crying Participants
The game ends when a path reaches a treasure card or when the deck runs out, depending on the version you’re playing. If a miner completes a path to a treasure, they win; if saboteurs derail the plan before a successful path is formed, they win. In practice, you’ll often get a few rounds where no one is clearly the villain until the last couple of turns, at which point someone dramatically reveals their guilt with a well-timed “I was sabotaging all along” line that becomes a shared joke for the evening.

The humor comes from the ridiculousness of some play cards and from the situation that unfolds around the table. The game’s real engine is interaction. You cheer when your path holds, you groan when a saboteur seed sprouts a twist, and you plot next-turn strategies while trying not to reveal your own role. In a sense, Saboteur is a social experiment disguised as a quick, competitive romp. It teaches you to observe, to bluff, and to pivot when the table’s dynamics shift dramatically after a single card draw. The English edition ensures you can focus on those moments instead of deciphering the rules on every other turn.
