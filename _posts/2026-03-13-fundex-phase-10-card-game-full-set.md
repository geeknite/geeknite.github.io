## How Phase 10 is played in the full set
The heart of Phase 10 remains what it is: a race to complete ten distinct phases, each with a different combination of cards. If you are familiar with the base game, you will find the full set a natural extension rather than a radical detour. The core mechanics operate like this: every round, players draw and discard cards, attempt to lay down their current phase, and hope that the luck gods align with their strategic choices. The full set spices that up with extra phase cards, more nuanced scoring, and perhaps a slightly longer window to recover if someone swipes your plan out from under you with a wild card that you did not see coming.

In quick terms, Phase 10 is a mix of old-school rummy and a sprint to assemble order out of chaos. The full set provides more opportunities for that chaos to emerge, including extra sets, runs, or mixed-phase options depending on which variant the group chooses to play.

### Setup and a quick-start guide
- Shuffle the full deck and place the draw pile face down in the center.
- Each player draws a starting hand, usually ten cards, though in the full set you might find a few variants that shift this up or down by a card or two.
- The player to the left of the dealer takes the first turn. On your turn, you draw one card, then either lay down your current phase if possible or discard a card to the discard pile.
- The hand-management aspect remains the centerpiece. You must complete the required phase by laying down exactly that combination of cards and then continue with the next phase in the cycle, if you are the lucky one who advances.
- The round ends when a player empties their hand. Points are tallied for the other players based on the cards left in their hands, with numbers representing their severity in a gentle, non-judgmental way.
- The full set includes optional rules that let you tweak the pace, whether by shortening the game with a fixed number of rounds or extending it with a longer series of phase challenges. It is easy enough for newcomers to pick up, while keeping a few knobs for veterans to tune.

### Basic strategy you can steal for a few hours
- Early grabs matter: if you see a run that might be viable over a few turns, consider keeping cards in that suit or numeral family rather than chasing a single big play that may not come together.
- Balance risk and safety: a wild can save you a lot of grief, but it can also be used by others to steal a phase if you are not paying attention.
- Watch your discard choices: the discard pile is not just junk; it is a public ledger of what you do not want and what others might be hoping you will reveal next.
- Don’t overextend: Phase 1 is easy, Phase 10 is a marathon. Use early rounds to map out potential phases, then execute when you have the right mix of cards.
