## Sushi Go! Strategy Guide: The Conveyor Belt to Victory

Welcome to Geeknite's definitive, hilariously practical, and occasionally hungry guide to Sushi Go!. If you’ve ever watched a conveyor belt of tiny nigiri slide by and thought, I can outsmart three friends and a dog named Pickle by turning wasabi into wallet-raising points, you’re in the right place. This guide is designed to level up your play from “I’ll take a tasty piece and hope for the best” to “I shall orchestrate a masterful buffet of points and bragging rights.” We’ll cover core mechanics, real-world tactics, common mistakes, and even some spicy variants to keep your game nights lively.

## Quick Overview: What Is Sushi Go! Anyway?

Sushi Go! is a fast, light drafting game where players pass around hands of 7 cards (for three rounds) and choose one card to keep while passing the rest. After all three rounds, you tally points from your collected cards. The deck is a rainbow of simple, bite-sized sushi: Nigiri that scales with wasabi, Maki for race points, Sashimi and Tempura for combo bonuses, Dumplings that scale with size, and the cunning Pudding that scores at the end. Chopsticks let you stash two cards for a future pick, adding a dash of planning and chaos.

For a lot of players, the thrill is in the quick math and the mind games: do you go for a sure-fire Tempura, risking your tablemate snagging the matching card, or do you gamble on Sashimi to go wild in a late-game binge? The beauty of Sushi Go! is that it’s easy to teach, fast to learn, and surprisingly crunchy in its decisions once you push into strategy mode.

## The Core Scoring Tree: What Actually Buys You Points

### Nigiri and Wasabi: The triple-threat
- Nigiri cards are your bread-and-butter. They’re worth 1, 2, or 3 points depending on the type (egg, squid, deluxe). The catch? If you want to juice Nigiri with Wasabi, you’ll need to pair a Wasabi card with a Nigiri later, which triples the base value of that Nigiri in your score. The punchline: Wasabi is a one-shot power-up that compounds with timing and memory.
- Wasabi doesn’t give you points by itself; it acts like a multiplier for the next Nigiri you claim. If you snag a Wasabi, you must also catch a Nigiri in the same round later. If not, you’ve just wasted a card and your tablemates will tease you mercilessly.

### Sashimi and Tempura: The two-card and two-card bonuses
- Sashimi is a high reward if you can grab three of them across the three rounds. The payoff is chunky: three Sashimi cards score 10 points. It’s a classic “go big or go home” play that rarely pays off if you chase it alone. The trick is to balance with other cards so you don’t lose momentum while in pursuit.
- Tempura is a two-card combo worth 5 points when you collect both halves. The game rewards a duo, not a solo chase; the timing becomes crucial: you want the second half to appear after you’ve already secured the first half, and your neighbors should cooperate by not taking the second half away just to spite you.

### Dumplings: The late bloomers
- Dumplings scale with how many you collect. Each additional Dumpling increases the value of your set, creating a “build your own noodle tower” vibe. They’re forgiving and forgiving enough to salvage a round if you’re short on other combos.

### Maki: The race you didn’t know you were in
- Maki yields points if your pile achieves the most, or sometimes the middle or least depending on the variant. It’s a subtle long game you win by paying attention to who’s collecting what and how many rounds are left. It’s not always worth it to chase Maki greedily, but it can punish tables that ignore the race entirely.

### Pudding: The endgame wild card
- Pudding is the true endgame swing, scoring after all three rounds. It rewards you for collecting Puddings and punishes players who ignored them. It’s one of those “don’t forget” cards that has the power to swing final standings, so keep an eye on puddings as the end nears.

### Chopsticks: The two-for-one trickster
- Chopsticks let you swap two cards for a two-card play later. This card is more about tempo than raw points: it can set up a second-round pick that nets you either a bonus card or a crucial missing piece for a combo. It’s a sneaky way to manipulate the flow of the next round, especially when you’re behind or when your neighbor is about to snatch your dream card.

## How to Play: A Simple Framework for Newcomers

### Round anatomy
- There are three rounds. In each round, you draft one card from your hand and pass the rest to the left. The discard pile doesn’t exist in Sushi Go!; your score is all about what you collect across rounds. The passing process ensures that each round you must weigh short-term gains against long-term options. You’re constantly balancing safety (take something you know won’t help your opponents more than it helps you) against risk (take a card that skyrockets your points if you manage to complete a combo).

### Reading the table
- The real skill of Sushi Go! is table awareness. Some players chase a single goal (like three Sashimi), while others keep a balanced approach across card types. Learning to read the table includes noticing who’s chasing Wasabi, who’s building for a Pudding lead, and who’s attempting a late Maki grab. You don’t need to be a mind reader, but a little social deduction goes a long way.

### The power of memory
- Remember which cards have already shown up. If you’ve seen multiple Sashimi cards pass by and your neighbor still hasn’t closed the set, you might hold your nerve and adjust your plan. Conversely, if one player is known to hoard Puddings, you may pivot toward Dumplings or Maki to force them into a riskier late-game decision. Memory is the silent edge in a game where speed masks complex forecasting.

## Card-by-Card Cheat Sheet: When to Keep, When to Pass

- Nigiri: Aim for at least one per round for steady points. If you’re going to pair with Wasabi, plan for the Nigiri that fits your table’s rhythm. If you’re behind on round two, consider grabbing a high-value Nigiri to create a last-minute comeback. 
- Wasabi: Take only if you’re confident you can pair it with a Nigiri later in that same round or the next. Don’t pick Wasabi if you’re not certain you’ll claim a Nigiri that benefits from it.
- Sashimi: If you see two Sashimi cards already out there and you’re likely to see a third, commit to the trio and try to secure the third piece. If not, don’t chase Sashimi into a losing trap; you’ll instead pivot to Tempura or Dumplings.
- Tempura: Seek the second half to complete the combo. If you’re in a tight round, decide whether you can race to a second Tempura before your neighbors complete theirs.
- Dumplings: If you’re ahead on Dumplings and want to lock in a growing set, take a Dumpling on early rounds and keep building. If you’re behind, you can still chase a late surge, but you’ll need to be strategic with the timing of your last few draws.
- Maki: Don’t overcommit unless you’re confident you’ll win the race or at least place strongly. It’s possible to snatch a few points from Maki without going all-in, especially if you’re able to read the table’s path to the top.
- Pudding: Guard puddings if you sense a lead, or bait your opponents by taking a few right now and letting them chase the rest. You’ll rarely see a single Pudding swing the entire game—unless your table is tightly clustered around unique Pudding power. Remember: pudding matters, even if you don’t see it until the very end.
- Chopsticks: Use Chopsticks to set up a future two-card move. It’s not always worth it, but can swing the momentum when you need a precise second pick to complete a critical combo.

## Round-by-Round Play: Practical Plans for Different Table Dynamics

### The Balanced Player: Consistency over Flash
- Strategy: Prioritize a steady stream of small to mid-size points from Nigiri and Dumplings, with a strategic look at Wasabi and Pudding. Your goal is to stay within a comfortable range and let others burn out searching for Sim-lava combos.
- Tips: Don’t chase Sashimi unless you’re confident you’ll land at least two or three. If you’re going for Dumplings, start early and keep building up to three or four by the third round.

### The Pudding Whisperer: Endgame Gamesmanship
- Strategy: Early puddings should be collected to guard your endgame. You can let others chase Sashimi or Tempura early, while you hedge your bets with Pudding protection. The crucial moment is late-game; you want to be able to count your opponents and know whether you’re ahead or behind so you can pivot toward Dumplings and Maki.
- Tips: Don’t ignore the possibility of a last-minute Maki race; if you see your opponents’ pudding count rising, you may be able to accelerate with a well-timed Maki swing.

### The Aggressive Gambler: Risk and Reward
- Strategy: Jump on high-value cards early and force others to overcorrect. Wasabi plus high-value Nigiri can punish players who prefer safety. Use Chopsticks to set up a dramatic finish.
- Tips: If you’re behind early, swing for Tempura to secure a two-card prize and catch up, then push for Sashimi as you near the end of round two or three to lock in a big points swing.

## Variants and House Rules: Spicing Up the Belt

- Sushi Go Party!: The longer, more complex cousin adds new cards and different scoring rules. It’s a perfect way to scale your strategy as your table grows or shrinks in its appetite for variety. The core mechanics remain the same, but you’ll juggle more card types and more potential combos.
- 2-Player Variant: This is a different beast. With fewer players, the draft pace accelerates and memory becomes even more important. You’ll want to tighten your focus on two or three key combos and cut back on the wild experimentation.
- Team Play: If you’re doing a mini-taction where teams sit across from each other, you can coordinate a shared strategy to maximize your team’s total points. The social dynamics shift from “beat your friends” to “beat the other team with shared planning.”
- Speed Round: For a party vibe, run a 15-minute knock-out where players try to max points with one pass. It’s chaotic but incredibly fun for a quick gaming night.

## Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

- Over chasing a single card type: It’s tempting to pursue Sashimi or Tempura to the finish line, but a balanced approach usually wins more often.
- Ignoring puddings: End-game rewards are real. Don’t neglect puddings just because they seem obscure or tail-end scoring.
- Not paying attention to the table: If you rely only on your own plan, you’ll miss the table’s rhythm. Watch your neighbors’ picks; their moves affect your options more than you think.
- Poor use of Chopsticks: Chopsticks are powerful but situational. If you waste them early, you’ll regret it later when you could have staged a two-card swing to seal a combo.
