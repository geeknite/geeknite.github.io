### Common pitfalls and things to watch out for

Like any booster experience, there are hits and misses. Some commons and uncommons will show up in such numbers that you begin to suspect the set designers were aiming for a narrative rather than a pure power curve. Others will quietly reward your long-term strategy, teaching you why you should collect those snow lands or why foretell can be built into a late game tempo plan. You might end up with multiple copies of a card that look great and produce mild wow factor in the moment, but fail to show practical synergy in your draft environment. That is just part of the pack drama: you watch the cards become a deck, and the deck becomes a story you tell your friends at the kitchen table when your cat sits on your notes and judges you with an innocent meow.

For the curious mind, the foretell mechanic offers a neat twist by letting you exile a card and later cast it for a cheaper cost. It encourages planning, which in turn encourages us to pretend we are already a grand master of the meta. The reality is a mix of clever planning and occasional chaos, because some of those red and black spells come with a payoff that is either spectacular or hilariously underwhelming. The joy of foretell is that you sometimes get a free turn plus a palatable threat on board, and other times you realize you foretold a six drop that would have forced you into a risky tempo play. It is a balancing act that makes the format feel dynamic and unpredictable, much like a conversation with a dragon carrying a bag of nuts.
