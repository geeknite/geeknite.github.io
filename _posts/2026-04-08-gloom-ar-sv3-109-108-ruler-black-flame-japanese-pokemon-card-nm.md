## Pro tips for grading NM and authenticity
Grading NM is as much about process as it is about appearance. A careful eye and a few checks can help you confirm that you are not dealing with a playful counterfeit or a poorly pressed counterfeit mis-login from a late-night online seller. Here are a few checklists that can help:
- Look for sharp corners and a clean, centered image. Slight centering bias toward the left or right is common in sets produced in this era; a truly NM copy should appear balanced.
- Inspect the surface for scratches, scuffs, or micro-whitening along the edges. Non visible when looking from a distance can become obvious under close inspection.
- Confirm the language and set symbol. Japanese prints often have a distinct language script and SKU, and the holo pattern should align with the SV3 design.
- Verify the card back if you suspect forgery. The back design should be consistent with other SV3 Japanese print runs and not show unusual wear patterns.
- For PSA or other professional services, keep the card in its protective case during submission to avoid any wear that might influence the grade.

## Display, storage, and display etiquette
If you have a NM Japanese AR such as this Gloom card, you probably want to show it off without sacrificing its condition. A few practical tips:
- Keep the card in a clean, dry environment with steady humidity around 40-50 percent. Avoid placing the card in a greenhouse or sauna that could accelerate deck aging.
- Use a UV-protective display case or a glass cabinet that blocks direct sunlight and reduces color fading over time.
- Rotate display pieces to minimize long exposure to light and avoid glare that can cause perception errors when you look at the art.
- If you have multiple ARs in a collection, you can mount them in a booklet or create a mini-gallery wall with acrylic sleeves that keep the art accessible and yet safe from accidental touches.
