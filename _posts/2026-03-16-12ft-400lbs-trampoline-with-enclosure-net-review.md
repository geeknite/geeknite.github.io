## Maintenance, Safety Tips, and Longevity
A long-term perspective is essential. A trampoline is not a disposable toy; it’s a modest, mechanical friend that should be respected, maintained, and occasionally pampered with a little love. Here are some practical safety tips and maintenance routines that help maximize longevity:

- Inspect before every jump: look for frayed netting, loose bolts, or signs of rust. If anything looks off, address it before use.
- Tighten and align: ensuring springs are evenly distributed isn’t just a cosmetic preference; it affects bounce quality and reduces wear on the mat edges.
- Keep the area clean: remove leaves, sticks, and other debris that could interfere with bounce or create a tripping hazard on landings.
- Protect from the sun when not in use: UV exposure can degrade padding and netting over time. A simple cover or seasonal storage can extend the life of padding and netting.
- Weight limits aren’t just for show: exceeding the rated capacity increases risk of frame bending, pad wear, or sudden catastrophic failure. Respect the manufacturer’s guidelines for a safer, longer-lasting bounce.
- Seasonal maintenance: after winter or heavy rain, check joints for rust spots and refasten any loose hardware. A small amount of anti-rust spray on the frame can prevent long-term corrosion in damp climates.

In terms of longevity, you should expect the frame and springs to hold up well with moderate use, provided you’re not treating it like a personal playground in the middle of a hurricane. The enclosure net’s material should also hold up under typical sun exposure, though it’s wise to replace it if you notice significant fading, tearing, or misalignment that cannot be corrected with tensioning.

And lest you worry about safety: always supervise children during use, set clear rules (no climbing on the frame, one jumper at a time on the jumping surface, no flips near the enclosure walls unless you’re trained), and maintain a zippy but safe entrance to the net. A “door” is not merely a doorway; it’s a preemptive safety feature, a choreography in which you keep the jumps in check while still letting the fun happen.
