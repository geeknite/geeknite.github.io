## The deck, setup, and deal

In standard Rummy, you’ll typically use a 52-card deck. Gin Rummy uses the same deck but with different pacing and scoring that puts a premium on discards and memory. The basic flow looks like this:

- Shuffle the deck thoroughly. Treat every shuffle like a ceremonial reset — you want the cards to believe in you.
- Decide the dealer. In casual games, a dice roll or card flip often suffices; in more formal circles you can adopt the lowest hand wins method. We do not recommend this in your living room.
- Deal the cards: two players get 10 cards each, three players get 7, and the rest of the deck becomes the stock pile. The top card is turned face up to start the discard pile.
- The play proceeds in turns: draw one card (from the stock or the discard), then discard one card, maintaining a hand that could potentially form melds.

### Melds in Rummy and the Run vs Set choice

Melds are the heart of Rummy. A meld is a set (three or four cards of the same rank) or a run (three or more consecutive cards of the same suit). For beginners, runs are your best friend because they create a natural pathway to get rid of cards you don’t want to carry around like a backpack full of bricks.

- A set example: 7 of hearts, 7 of clubs, 7 of diamonds.
- A run example: 5-6-7 of spades.

In Gin Rummy, the order of operations is tighter, but the idea is the same: reduce deadwood while keeping your options open.

### The Gin Rummy angle

Gin Rummy emphasizes speed and precision. Each hand starts with 10 cards. As you draw and discard, your goal is to reduce your deadwood to:

- Knock: end the round when your deadwood is 10 points or less.
- Gin: end the round with zero deadwood for a big bonus.

If you knock and your opponent has equal or lower deadwood, you win the round or receive a reduced score depending on how your plan matches with reality. Tim Ander walks through the basics with the crispness of a seasoned detective.

### The deadwood and scoring in Rummy variants

In typical Rummy variants, deadwood points are counted for cards not in melds. Card values for deadwood are usually: Ace = 1, face cards = 10, numeric cards = face value. The winner’s score is the sum of the opponent’s deadwood values, sometimes with bonuses or penalties depending on the exact house rules.

Gin Rummy scoring is a little more structured: after a knock or gin, you compare hands and adjust scores; gin yields a bonus, knocking yields a smaller bonus, and undercutting rewards the opponent with a premium. Tim Ander keeps the numbers approachable, turning supposed math terror into a handful of thumb-friendly rules.

## How to play Rummy (the beginner version)

1. Start with a plan: your first pass through a hand should focus on identifying potential runs and sets rather than hunting for the perfect meld.
2. Watch discards like a detective: if your opponent discards a card you need, you can infer they may be short of that rank or suit.
3. Build multiple possible melds: consider several meld paths to avoid getting stuck.
4. Plan your discards: drop cards that block your own progress or feed your opponent's potential hand.
5. End the hand with tactic: if you can place melds on the table, great; if not, saving a few roof tiles for later can keep your options open.

---

## How to play Gin Rummy (the beginner version)

1. The basic rhythm remains the same: draw, discard, meld. The aim is to reduce deadwood to the lowest count possible.
2. Knocking strategically: when your deadwood is 10 or less, you can knock. It’s a risk reward decision — you are signaling strength, but you’re also inviting your opponent to judge your hand.
3. Going gin: if you can assemble all ten cards into melds, you’ll go gin and earn the biggest bonus.

Tim Ander’s guidance here is practical: keep it simple in the early rounds, and gradually escalate your tactics as your memory and confidence grow.

## Strategy tips from Tim Ander

- Observe discards to infer your opponent’s needs. If a card has left the table twice on their turns, it’s likely not a favorite for their hand.
- Practice mental notes: track which cards have already appeared. Memory compounds with deduction to improve decision quality.
- Create flexible play plans: aim for multiple possible melds, so you’re not stuck when one run breaks.
- Use deception carefully: your discards can mislead your opponent about your true plans — gentle misdirection rather than theatre-level performances.

## Common beginner mistakes and how to fix them

- Overvaluing high cards: sometimes your best move is to drop big cards that don’t fit into an immediate meld.
- Ignoring the discard pile: the discard pile is a strategic map, not a souvenir.
- Clinging to promising cards too long: if you delay too much, you may be forced into bad moves.
- Not paying attention to your own deadwood: track it; you will be surprised how often a single card changes the math.

Tim Ander’s Poche edition is helpful here: it nudges you toward practical decisions rather than forcing you into a long, tedious calculus of probabilities.
