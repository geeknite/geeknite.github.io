## Assembly and setup: a journey worth taking

The package is designed for two installers with basic tools and an over-the-top sense of optimism. The first hour is mostly inventory: counting panels, screws, brackets, and the ever-elusive hex wrench. The next few hours are about alignment, leveling, and coaxing the system to sit flush against a wall and still be modular enough to reconfigure if your product team decides to pivot toward a different layout mid-project. The process includes clear steps for attaching legs, securing crossbeams, mounting the screen, and routing cables into designated channels.

One thing to note is weight distribution. With a line of ten users, you want the center of gravity to be as stable as possible. If you are on carpet, you’ll want anti-slip glides; if you’re on hard flooring, throw in some floor protectors and consider a floor mat for the two center desks to reduce noise and vibration when people lean in to speak across the screen. The manuals provide a reasonable set of warnings, including “do not overload the power strip” and “do not attempt to sit on the shared surface,” which is good safety comedy but also a practical reminder that spaces like this aren’t built for baking cookies on top of them.

## Maintenance, care, and longevity

Oak WTE is relatively forgiving. Regular dusting with a microfiber cloth, a gentle wood cleaner for the veneer, and occasional polishing to maintain that warm glow keep the desk looking brand-new for longer. The metal frame benefits from simple wipe-downs to prevent grime from creeping into joints. Given the size and weight, you’ll want to perform annual checks on screws and brackets to ensure the assembly remains tight and the screen stays perfectly aligned.

One practical tip: because this piece is meant to operate as a long-term collaboration station, you’ll want to establish a simple rotation for things like monitor placement and chair adjustments to ensure even wear. In a team of ten, small adjustments, when shared, can go a long way toward maintaining comfort and preventing one user from becoming the de facto “chair bully.”

## Setup tips and best practices

- Plan your layout before unboxing. A rough floor plan with the wall positions, screen sight lines, and chair centerpoints saves time.
- Pre-wire a dedicated power circuit or two for the central strip. You will thank yourself when everyone agrees that every computer, monitor, and charger has a dedicated port.
- Use the central screen as the collaboration anchor, but don’t forget personal space for private work. Encourage individuals to position their monitors at a comfortable viewing angle to reduce neck strain.
- Define a cable-management protocol. A standard labeling system for power, DisplayPort, USB-C, and ethernet cables helps future upkeep and makes room reconfiguration painless.
- Schedule periodic reviews of ergonomic settings. The same way software needs updates, desks and chairs need recalibration to stay comfortable for a team of ten.
