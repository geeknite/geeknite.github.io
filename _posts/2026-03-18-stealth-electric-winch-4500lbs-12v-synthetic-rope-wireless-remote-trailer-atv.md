### Rope Choice: Synthetic vs Steel

Synthetic rope is a big selling point here. It is lighter, safer to handle, and floats in water better. It is also more forgiving on near-edge contact, which means less nail-biting when you are jerking a stuck vehicle. The trade-offs: synthetic rope needs more care in storage and drying after exposure to water, sunlight, and mud. It is crucial to inspect for core fraying and to replace if signs of wear appear. The drum is less likely to suffer fishhook issues when the rope length is wound correctly, and a clean, shaved rope edge reduces wear on the fairlead and rope over time.

If you are a steel-cable purist who loves that satisfying clink and wants a rope that never needs replacement, you might still prefer steel. But if you are a weekend warrior who does not want to fear rope recoil, synthetic is a safer option that aligns with modern ATV and trailer usage. ### Safety First: Handling and Maintenance

Safety is a core feature of any winch you put on your ATV or trailer. A few pointers we learned the hard way:

- Always wear gloves when handling rope. Synthetic fibers can cause rope burn if you slide a rough edge across them.
- Use a proper hook strap or damper when the winch is under load to reduce whiplash effects if the rope breaks or fails.
- Inspect the rope for frays and kinks before each outing. A frayed rope is a red flag you should address before your next trip.
- Keep the control box and electrical connections dry and clean. Water ingress is the enemy of electrical systems.
- If you plan a long recovery or frequent use, consider upgrading the wiring gauge to reduce voltage drop and stay within the winch’s recommended electrical supply.

### Installation: Mounting, Wiring, and Setup

Installations vary; you will want to verify mounting hole patterns and ensure you have a solid bumper or plate to bolt into. The Stealth 4500 typically uses compatible mounting holes with common bumper kits and winch plates. Steps:

1) Park on a flat surface and disconnect the battery. Safety first; we have all learned this the hard way.
2) Mount the winch in the intended position using the provided hardware. If your bumper is lightweight, consider upgrading hardware to heavy-duty bolts and lock washers.
3) Route the power leads: positive and negative wires must be thick enough to handle current without overheating. Some users like to install a fused circuit and an inline switch for added protection.
4) Install a suitable fairlead for synthetic rope. A Hawse or roller fairlead reduces rope wear and ensures clean rope bites on the drum.
5) Run a few test pulls in neutral or with a stationary load to verify that the rope spools neatly and that the remote and wired controls function properly.
