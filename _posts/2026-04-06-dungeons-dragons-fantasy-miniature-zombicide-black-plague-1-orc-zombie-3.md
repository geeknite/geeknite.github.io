## Painting and assembly: tips from the bench
### Preparation and assembly
Like all good miniatures, this Orc Zombie rewards careful prep. Remove mold lines with a sharp hobby knife, wash the piece to remove any residual mold release, and assemble if necessary. Zombicide miniatures are often designed to be snapped or glued in place, but you can get a nicer finish if you dry fit first and adjust any gaps before priming. If you are particularly picky about seam lines, a tiny bit of green stuff or putty can fill the gaps, followed by a light sanding to keep surfaces smooth.

### Color plan that sings
A three-step color plan works wonders on zombie orcs: base coat, midtone, and wash, followed by edge highlight. Start with a desaturated green for the skin to evoke rot without drifting into a lurid neon. Use a burnt ochre or dark brown for the armor plates and convert the rust effects with a combination of thin orange and brown washes. The rotted flesh benefits from a purplish undertone in the recesses, which adds depth and makes the model look more haunted rather than merely dirty.

A practical paint mix you can try: for skin, a mix of goblin green with a drop of sickly yellow and a wash of dark purple in the recesses. For armor, a base of dry brushed metal followed by a wash of thinned brown and a final edge highlight in a lighter metallic to catch the light in the right moments. A bit of red or pink in the wounds can pop nicely if you want to direct your players’ attention to the most menacing spots on the model.

### Brushes and technique tips
- Use a fine detail brush for the teeth and exposed bone, a medium brush for the armor, and a dry brush for the textures on the cloth and skin.
- Dry brushing is your friend here; it highlights textures without requiring a perfect paint job. Just a light touch and a steady hand.
- Washes are key for depth. A dark brown or purple wash will sink into the crevices and sell the decayed look. Don’t overdo it—keep the contrast balanced so the model doesn’t look muddy.
- Layering and glazing allow you to build subtle shifts in color on the armor and skin. Glazes can unify the color family and prevent the model from looking like a mismatch of spots.

If the assembly stage feels like a puzzle, take a moment to plan out the order. Start with the torso and main limbs, then attach any extra bits like shields or weapons.
