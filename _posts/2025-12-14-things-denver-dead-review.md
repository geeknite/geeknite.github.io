## Plot Summary

Before we begin, let's do a quick recap for those of you who might need a refresher. Released in 1995, **'Things to Do in Denver When You're Dead'** is a crime drama that follows Jimmy the Saint (played by Andy Garcia) as he tries to navigate a dangerous job gone wrong. With a cast of colorful characters and an ending that will leave you questioning your life choices, this film is a must-watch for any fan of the genre.

## Blu-ray Features

The Special Edition Blu-ray offers a fresh HD transfer that breathes new life into this cult classic. The picture quality is crisp, the colors are vibrant, and the shadows are darker than my teenage soul. Watching this movie in HD is like seeing it for the first time.

## Performance Highlights

### Andy Garcia as Jimmy the Saint

Andy Garcia delivers a performance that is both suave and tortured. His portrayal of Jimmy the Saint is a masterclass in understated charisma. He's a man with a code, even if that code gets him into a world of trouble.

### Christopher Walken as The Man with the Plan

Christopher Walken is, as always, a delight. His portrayal of The Man with the Plan is creepy, unsettling, and utterly captivating. If you ever needed a reminder of why Walken is a legend, this is it.

### The Supporting Cast

The supporting cast is a who's who of '90s talent, including Steve Buscemi, Christopher Lloyd, and William Forsythe. Each actor brings their A-game, making even the smallest roles memorable.

## Themes and Style

### Crime and Redemption

At its core, 'Things to Do in Denver When You're Dead' is a story about crime and redemption. Jimmy the Saint is a man trying to do right in a world that keeps pulling him back into the darkness. It's a theme that resonates, especially when accompanied by some truly poetic dialogue.

### Visual Style

The film's visual style is a blend of noir and '90s grit. The use of shadows and lighting creates a mood that is both stylish and foreboding. The HD transfer only enhances this, making the film look better than ever.
