## Materials and construction: why acacia wood, and does wicker survive a rainstorm?

Acacia wood is a star player in outdoor furniture. It’s dense, relatively resistant to insects, and carries a warm, honey-toned finish that looks better with age (in the sense that a well-seasoned skillet looks better after years of use). The table top and chair frames are usually kiln-dried to minimize warping, and then treated with a sealant or oil finish. The wicker chairs typically have a resin or vinyl wicker woven around a metal or wood frame. The result is a set that looks premium but won’t crack into a million splinters when you misjudge a temperature of the grill.

Wicker seats and backs are the tricky duo here. Properly sealed wicker resists moisture better than natural reed, and the finish often includes UV inhibitors to prevent color fade. If you live in a damp climate or near the ocean, you’ll appreciate wicker that doesn’t turn brittle after a year. Still, the key trick is to treat them with a light layer of protective oil on the wood, and to store the cushions and fabric covers during heavy rains or winter months (if you have real seasons instead of a long sweater season).

### Build notes you should care about
- Fasteners: Look for stainless steel or coated screws to minimize rust.
- Joints: Check for tight joints; over-tightening can crack wood. Loosen slightly if there’s a squeak.
- Table top: Some versions have a slatted top for drainage; others are solid. Slats reduce water pooling but require proper cleaning between slats.

### Tips for smooth assembly

## Weather resistance and maintenance: surviving sun, rain, and the occasional seagull

Acacia wood, when properly treated, holds up surprisingly well against the elements. It’s not impervious to moisture, and prolonged exposure can cause color shifts or hairline cracks if the wood isn’t properly sealed. The wicker components, especially the seating, are generally weather-tested and designed to shed water rather than soak it. The key to longevity is to protect the set from standing water and to reapply sealant or oil to the wood every season (or as recommended by the manufacturer).

We recommend the following routine for longevity:
- Clean the surfaces with a mild soap solution and rinse well. Don’t soak the wood; you want to remove dirt, not drown it.
- Dry thoroughly and apply a protective oil or sealant designed for outdoor wood. This helps retain color and reduces cracking.
- Cover during heavy storms or use movable furniture covers. This reduces exposure to UV rays, which can slowly fade color, and to rain that can seep into any small cracks.
- If you live near salt air, rinse more often and consider a stronger UV-resistant sealant to counter the effects of salt corrosion on hardware.

In real-world use, this kind of set may survive several seasons with only minor cosmetic issues. The wood’s natural oils help resist moisture, while the wicker provides comfort.
