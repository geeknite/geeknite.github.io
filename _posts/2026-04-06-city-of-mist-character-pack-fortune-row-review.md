## What Fortune Row actually includes
There’s no mystery about the contents once you crack open the card sleeve—the pack wants you to feel the tug of fate across four distinct yet thematically linked characters. Each character is presented with a short primer, their own Distinctions (the Mist’s preferred term for traits that map to the City of Mist system), a few background details, and a couple of relationship hooks you can drop straight into your campaign. The design aesthetic is this: every card is ornate, moody, and a little wicked—like a velvet-clad guide who knows the quickest way to trouble and the cheapest way to leverage it for dramatic effect.

From a usability standpoint, Fortune Row shines. The characters are modular enough to slot into a city already in motion, yet specific enough that they feel like a finished puzzle piece rather than a rearranged jigsaw you’ll never quite finish. If you’re running a noir arc where a street-level syndicate starts to feel like a myth in sneakers, this pack gives you a cast and some ready-made threads to pull.

### The four core characters
- The Spinner (Face/Influence): The Spinner excels at social manipulation, rumor-milling, and turning conversations into leverage. Think of a confluence between a talk-show host and a streetwise mage — someone who can talk a crowd into a decision and then slip away into the fog before you realize your own choices mattered. Distinctions lean on persuasion, information control, and a knack for spotting the weak ties that bind a community.
- The Velvet Grin (Ranged Trickster): The Velvet Grin thrives on clever misdirection and a flair for theatrical getaways. Their skills are a blend of mechanical tricks and an intuition for the crowd’s mood. This is your pack’s misdirection engine; expect faked outcomes, misread intentions, and a few “pinch me, did that just happen?” moments that can catapult a scene from tense to pulsing.
- The Oracle of Pockets (Seer/Insight): This character reads the city’s heartbeat through small, mundane signs — a receipt, a street musician’s tempo, a passerby’s glint in the corner of an eye. The Oracle offers foresight with a practical bent, guiding the team toward opportunities or away from avoidable trouble. They’re the pack’s compass-keeper, but with a wisecrack and a whiskey bottle to boot.
- The Grifter’s Shadow (Manipulator): The final piece of Fortune Row is the shadow that can shadow you back—an antagonist-turned-ally who knows the city’s skeletons better than you know your own. The Grifter’s Shadow thrives on leverage, debts, and the delicate art of turning a threat into a resource. They’ll push the team toward bold moves, or quietly steer you toward consequences that unfold like a trap you laid for someone else.

While the above names are suggested archetypes, each character card invites you to swap in your own flavor. That’s the joy of City of Mist: you don’t purchase a fixed squad; you buy a set of seeds that thrive in your table’s soil.

## How Fortune Row integrates with City of Mist mechanics
City of Mist sits on the bones of a simple concept: the city is a living character, and the players are both detectives and legends. Fortune Row leans into this by giving you four distinct “archetypes” that interact with the city’s mythology. Mechanically, you’ll see three core components emphasized across the pack:

- Distinctions that feel tailor-made for dramatic reveals. The pack doesn’t waste space on generic traits. Each Distinction is crafted to push a scene toward a conflict where the truth about Fortune Row could tilt the balance of power in the city’s underworld.
- Clue bundles that thread through a campaign. You’ll get scene prompts, location hooks, and mini arcs that can be dropped into a preexisting case or used to seed a new mystery. The aim is to keep momentum high without forcing a railroady path.
- Relationship strands that tether characters to the city and to one another. In City of Mist, who you know and who you owe is often more important than what you can physically do. Fortune Row provides a web of connections—familial ties, mentor debts, rival crews—that feels organic rather than manufactured.

For GMs who love a good payoff moment, this pack shines. The moments when the Spinner’s manipulation collides with the Oracle’s prophecy, or when the Grifter’s shadow returns to extract a price, land with satisfying thuds that make players grin and groan in equal measure. The pacing is well-tuned for a mid-level arc: not too bloated, not too skippable, with natural beats that invite improvisation.

## The good, the great, and the not-so-slightly-awkward bits
What Fortune Row nails:
- A strong thematic throughline. The four archetypes feel like they belong to the same family portrait — not a random four-pack, but a deliberate cohort with shared myths and a shared playground.
- Flexible hooks. The included scenarios work well in one-shot sessions or as part of a longer arc. They aren’t so prescriptive that you feel like you’re playing a prewritten game of “how this crime happened,” yet they offer enough structure to give you direction when you’re staring at a blank city block.
- A good balance between roleplay opportunities and tactical hooks. The pack respects the game’s core dynamic: talk, interpret, and act with style more than brute force.

Where it could be smoother:
- If you’re a GM who wants an extensive rules-lite compendium, Fortune Row stays faithful to the base game’s rhythm, which means you won’t find an encyclopedic glossary. It’s a narrative-focused set that expects you to adapt rules with your own table’s tempo.
- A couple of the character prompts could benefit from more explicit scene seeds for modern urban settings beyond the noir core. Still, what exists is a launchpad, not a full rocket.

If you’re new to City of Mist, think of Fortune Row as an introductory battery of archetypes that you can drop into your first urban legend arc without rewriting the map of the city. If you’re a veteran GM, it’s a toolkit for weaving in new legends to expand the city’s mythos without diluting your existing story structure.

## How to run a Fortune Row session: a quick blueprint
Here’s a practical approach you can copy-paste into your GM notes:
- Start with a central rumor that touches on all four archetypes. The rumor should be something the players can chase from a street corner to a back-alley meeting to a rooftop confrontation.
- Assign each character a personal stake in the rumor’s outcome. Maybe the Spinner wants to protect a rumor for leverage, the Velvet Grin sees a payday in the truth, the Oracle foresees a consequence if the rumor goes unchecked, and the Grifter’s Shadow can cash in if the city’s balance tilts.
- Introduce a local antagonist tied to Fortune Row’s network. A mayoral aide with a debt to the Grifter? A street preacher who foresees the prophecy’s exact moment? The city loves to surprise you, but it also loves it when your plan unravels in a satisfying way.
- Build a climactic set-piece that uses at least two archetypes in conflict. A crowd scene that spirals into a misdirection-filled chase across a market, or a staged confession that turns into a carnival of plot twists.
- Close with a fallout beat that leaves the players curious about what Fortune Row will do next. The city should feel alive and forgiving of chaos, as long as it’s profitable chaos with style.

If you want a ready-to-run sample scene, I’ve included a quick seed below that you can adapt to your table:

Seed: The Night Market Ledger
- Hook: A stolen ledger appears to forecast the night’s fortune and misfortune with uncanny precision.
- Stakes: Whoever controls the ledger controls the street’s flow of information, money, and favors.
- Obstacles: The Spinner must avert a public dressing-down from a rival Faction; the Oracle sees a propitious moment that could be misread as a threat; the Grifter’s Shadow is blackmailing a city official; the Velvet Grin’s showmanship is the only thing keeping the crowd from marching toward violence.
- Outcome: Depending on how the players resolve the ledger’s power, the city is either enthralled by Fortune Row or terrified of what might happen when such power becomes public.
