## How to play the game
### Core mechanics
- Each player draws a starting rack of tiles from the bag. The number of tiles on your rack depends on the number of players and the difficulty setting.
- The objective is to use your tiles to form valid words that fit into the shared crossword-style grid. Words must intersect at common letters, creating a network of connect-the-dots that would make a modern word puzzle designer smile or cry depending on their mood.
- On your turn you can rearrange your rack, swap tiles, or place new words on the grid. The twist is that you must connect with the existing grid and not create dead ends that break the crossword logic.
- If you cannot place a tile, you draw from the bag or you can exchange tiles with a penalty that adds a little risk to your choices. The penalty is optional but adds spice to the drama.
- The round ends when one player uses all their tiles or after a set number of moves. Scores are tallied according to the remaining tiles in each player rack, with a little bonus for long crossers and a bonus for clever two-letter connectors.

### The crossword twist in action
The big difference from classic Bananagrams is the grid constraint. You want to avoid isolated words that do not connect to the main network. That means you may temporarily form two or three words to connect later rather than forcing a big single move. The challenge is to balance speed and structure. You can place short, common words quickly to create anchor points, then expand into a broader web of two- and three-letter words to build longer phrases. The result is a satisfying dance of strategy and improvisation, a bit like playing a word game on a board where the board itself reshapes as you go.
