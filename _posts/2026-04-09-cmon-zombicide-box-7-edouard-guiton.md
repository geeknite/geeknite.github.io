## Special Guest Artist Edouard Guiton

Edouard Guiton brings a painterly vibe to a game that has so much black ink in its core that it could survive a solar eclipse. His art on Box 7 skews toward something between enamel advertisement and museum wall label, with a dash of post apocalypse that somehow looks bright and playful rather than grim. The survivors in this box look like they are ready for a gallery opening rather than a subway sprint; the zombies look more like antagonists in a post apocalyptic zombie romance novel. The net effect is one of contrast and personality, which, in a game about survival, adds a layer of character that is often missing from mass produced miniatures.

The sculpts themselves have the Edouard touch. Lines are crisp, the faces carry expressions that range from irritated to dangerously optimistic, and the clothing bears details that catch the light in ways that would make a painter proud. It is not just about looking good on the table; these minis invite you to roleplay with your tabletop as if it were a tiny stage. Expect little micro-stories forming around each miniature as you set up the first scenario and watch your crew argue about which one of them is destined to be the brave hero while the rest run around in comic panic.

## Packaging and Quality

Zombicide Box 7 arrives in a box that is sturdy enough to survive a shipping center that moonlights as a nunchaku dojo. The exterior art is a bold statement piece, a blend of Guiton style and zombie swagger. The inner tray is well thought out with slots that actually hold miniatures in place, which is a small miracle considering how often you end up with a bag full of tiny limbs after an earthquake-level shake. The tray design makes unboxing a theatrical event rather than a frantic rummage through a pile of sprues. This is the kind of packaging that says, you may fight the undead, but we still care about your fingernails.

The materials used feel premium, with nice weight to the metal or plastic of the miniatures, depending on your edition. The bases click into place with satisfying little clicks, and the components slot together with the ease of a well rehearsed RPG party. The boards are a touch glossy, which helps with the art but can be a magnet for fingerprint archaeology; keep a microfiber handy if you want to preserve those crisp line details for photos or future retro reveals on social media.

## Art and Theme: Edouard Guiton s Signature Flavor

The real draw here is the art. Edouard has a way of injecting personality into characters that would otherwise be generic survivors. You can spot a veteran scavenger not by their custom armor but by the tilt of their hat and the swagger of their stance. The zombies, too, aren't mere cannon fodder; they carry little narrative beats in their posture. Some look almost reluctant to lurch forward, others look like they are auditioning for a post apocalyptic zombie romance novel. It is that sense of story sneaking in through the edges that elevates Box 7 beyond a mere tactical resource and into a box that begs to be displayed between battles.

There is also a clever balance of color. The palette avoids the typical grim gray you sometimes associate with zombie chrome; instead you get pops of teal, magenta, and burnt orange that keep the board lively. It is a celebration of color that somehow makes the undead feel more approachable while still terrifying. If you are the sort of player who likes to stage cinematic battles rather than just brutal skirmishes, this box gives you the visual tools to set the mood for your campaign. And that mood matters; it lowers the barrier to playing more frequently because the scenery helps tell your stories before you even roll the dice.

## Miniatures and Scenery

The miniatures in Box 7 are a blend of classic Zombicide charm and new experimental poses. Some survivors arrive with weapons brilliantly sculpted to appear weighty and lethal; others feel a bit more whimsical, which is a nice counterbalance to the constant doom in the zombie ranks. The detail on the faces can convey a range of emotions that you will recognize in your own group: determination, sarcasm, fear, and the occasional charisma that makes you believe you can triumph against impossible odds even if the dice disagree.

As you assemble the miniatures, you will notice Edouard s influence in the hairlines, the creases in clothing, and the tiny accessories like backpacks and pouches. The paint ready parts are ready to prime and paint, and there is a sense that the artist designed the pieces to be approachable for hobbyists who enjoy a little color theory experimentation. If you like to paint your miniatures before games (a pastime we strongly encourage at Geeknite), Box 7 offers a good canvas with some approachable highlights and shading opportunities that do not require a full art school to appreciate.

## Scenarios, Cards, and Replay Value

Zombicide is known for its modular scenarios and the replay value of its mission cards. Box 7 adds new mission cards that reflect Edouard s themes, with small flavor text and some quirky objectives. The scenarios encourage players to think creatively about positioning, resource management, and time pressure. The mission design leans into the character and setting, inviting players to tell a story of their own as the undead surge across the board. The balance between risk and reward remains the beating heart of Zombicide, and Box 7 adds a few new wrinkles that keep long time players engaged without feeling like a forced upgrade for casual players.

We tested a couple of the new scenarios across multiple play sessions. The results were telling: the new mechanics introduced by Box 7 rewarded players who stacked the deck with careful planning, but did not punish groups who preferred a more reckless approach. The mission objectives often required you to split your party, manage your ressources, and coordinate the timing of your special actions. It is a good sign that the designers kept the game dynamic without letting it drift into chaos; you will have moments where the board seems to laugh at your plan, and moments when you realize the plan was not so clever after all, but still gratifying.

## Components and Quality Control

The quality control across the board for Box 7 is solid. The rulebook is thorough yet approachable, with clear diagrams and a friendly tone that aligns with the packaging art. The boards lie flat and resist warping, an important feature for anyone who plays on a soft table surface or a laptop stand that doubles as a gaming table. The dice, tokens, and reference sheets are well organized, with minimal confusion as you navigate a dense setup. It would be nice if there were a few more color-coded markers for new players, but the current array remains perfectly usable for groups with a bit of patience.

There is a small trade off for the art connoisseur: the more intricate sculpts may require a slower wipe down of finger oils during play or photos. This is not a major complaint; it simply means Box 7 is better displayed when you take a moment to pause and admire the craft between rounds. It is not a barrier to play, but it does remind you that you have purchased something that straddles the line between a game and a collectible piece of art.

## Value, Price, and Accessibility

Price for Box 7 sits in the upper mid range for Zombicide expansions. You are paying for the art direction, the larger number of miniatures, and the potential to inject a strong visual identity into your campaign. If you are a long time Zombicide player who collects everything and wants to display a shelf of boxes that looks like a high end museum, Box 7 is a strong addition. If you are a more casual player who buys expansions sporadically, Box 7 still offers enough content to feel worthwhile, especially if you enjoy adding narrative flavor through art and new survivor dynamics.

From a pure gameplay perspective, there is a lot of value packed into Box 7. The scenarios add variety without breaking the core cadence of the game. The balance of new walkers, higher tier weapons, and the interplay between new and classic elements creates a fresh challenge while preserving the core experience that fans know and love. If you crave new tactical puzzles, Box 7 delivers with several board layouts that demand careful movement, smart use of barricades, and timely use of your special action cards.
