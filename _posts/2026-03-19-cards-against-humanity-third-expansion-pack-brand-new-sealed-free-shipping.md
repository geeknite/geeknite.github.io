Setup is the same as any CAH night: you introduce the deck, designate a Card Czar (the player who picks the winning black card), deal a hand of white cards to each player, and start reading prompts. If you’re the type who enjoys rules-lawyering only in the most glorious way, you won’t be plunged into a new rulebook; you’ll be asked to decide which of your friends is most likely to ruin the night with a perfectly-timed response.

## The not-so-small print: sensitivity, consent, and keeping it fun
Let’s be real: Cards Against Humanity sits at the intersection of shock humor and social dynamics. Some cards can feel bold, crossing lines that aren’t everybody’s jam. If your group includes folks who are sensitive to topics, consider establishing a quick ground rule before opening the box: players opt-in to the level of risqué content, or you designate a “kid gloves” round with a smaller hand size to keep things light. The goal is to entertain, not to torment your friends into a social exile. The Third Expansion amplifies the power of punchlines, but it also amplifies the need for consent, empathy, and a good sense of humor about yourself.

## Play tips and strategies: maximizing the LOLs
- Build a rotation: Start with a mix of players who know each other well. Alternate the role of Card Czar to spread the spotlight and prevent one person from becoming the de facto punchline machine.
- Read the room: Some prompts demand quick, witty responses; others require a more thoughtful, longer setup. Tailor your choices to your group’s energy level.
- Save the golden lines: Keep a running list of quotable lines. You’ll thank yourself later when you’re trying to revive a bored party with a nostalgia throwback.
- Mix expansions with care: If you have multiple sets, try different mixes (base game plus third expansion, or two expansions with a rotating card pool). This keeps the game fresh and reduces the chance that the same jokes replay in the same old rhythm.
- Mind the ratio: If your group contains players who prefer less provocative humor, consider using the “PG-13 mode” by removing the most extreme black prompts and focusing on punchlines that rely on wordplay or absurdity rather than explicit topics.
