## Painting and Display Tips
This Abomination begs for a dramatic paint job that highlights its predatory nature. Here are practical tips if you want to make the fur sing and the eyes gleam:

### Color palette and basework
- Fur tones: desaturated grays and charcoals with a few streaks of dark brown to break up the surface in natural-looking variations. Dry brushing with lighter gray or even a touch of white will help bring out the sculpted fur ridges.
- Underlayers: a deep, neutral undercoat helps the fur color pop without looking muddy. A midtone blue-gray undercoat can add moody coolness if you want the model to read as a winter hunter.
- Eyes and maw: crimson or amber eyes can give a predatory glare. For the maw, use a base of pink or reddish flesh tones with a wash that emphasizes the depth of the throat.
- Claws: bone or dirty ivory works well, with a thin dark wash to bring out the notch details.

### Techniques to try
- Dry brushing to highlight fur textures: lighter shades on the raised surfaces give your Abomination a “frost-bitten” look that catches light from odd angles.
- Wet blending on the jaw and forelimbs for a more organic, fleshy texture that makes the model feel alive and dangerous.
- Eye gloss: a tiny dot of gloss varnish on the eyes can create a spark that draws the viewer’s gaze to the face—a small but effective storytelling trick.

### Display and photography
- Lighting: if you can, set up a small two-light or three-light rig to avoid harsh shadows that obscure details in the fur. A neutral white balance helps keep the fur tones true rather than washed out.
- Backgrounds: a dark, matte environment makes the Abomination pop. You can add a subtle texture with a model railroad ballast or a DIY dungeon floor overlay for extra drama.
- Angles: try a low-angle shot to emphasize the creature’s size; a three-quarters view usually gives a good sense of both mass and menace.
