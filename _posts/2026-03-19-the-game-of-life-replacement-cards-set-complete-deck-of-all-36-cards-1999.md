## Practicalities: storage, durability, and maintenance

If you’re picking up replacement cards, you’ll want to think about how they’ll live in your home beyond the initial unboxing moment. The 1999 deck is designed to be drop-in compatible with the original box, but there are a few tips that help them survive for years:
- Use a small card binder or a designated card sleeve for the 36-card suite. This protects edges and keeps text clean.
- Consider a storage box with a divider; the last thing you want is a misfiled card that sends you into a two-minute treasure hunt during the last spin of the night.
- For families with younger players, a card carrier with a lid helps prevent accidental baby-to-card chess matches—or worse, the family dog turning it into chew-toy couture.

Regarding durability, if you’re accustomed to modern UV-coated supplier-quality cards, you might notice that older print runs lean toward a practical but less aggressively coated finish. It’s not a flaw; it’s a historical artifact. If you want a long-term home for them, a light spray of archival protectant or a sleeve can do wonders without altering the feel too drastically.
