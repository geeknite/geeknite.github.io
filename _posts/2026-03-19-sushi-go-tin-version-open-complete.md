### Setup and Player Count
- Player count: 2-5 players. If you are playing with two players, you’ll want to pretend the game is a duel where you’re always one turn ahead of your friend. Three to four players is the sweet spot; five works fine, albeit a touch chaotic in the best possible way.

### The Drafting Mechanic
Each round, players are dealt a hand of seven cards (or a variant based on player count). You pick one card and pass the rest to your neighbor. The drafting continues until all cards are claimed and you have a delicious assortment of sushi cards on your plate. The scoring is where things get spicy—the goal is to assemble the most valuable combinations of cards like Nigiri, Sashimi, Maki, and the famous Pudding token that looms as a wildcard wildcard of endgame scoring. Nip details: Nigiri scores depend on its type and the number; Sashimi requires three cards to score, and Maki tokens grant points to the leader at the end if you survive the shouters of the scoring board. You’ll learn the nuances quickly, which means no one is truly safe from scapegoating when the last pudding token is revealed.

### Card Types and Scoring Nuances
- Nigiri cards: Simple but potent. You collect them in the hopes of big point values per piece, but you’ll pay the price of losing the opportunity to collect other sets. 
- Sashimi: The iconic trio rule; you must collect sets of three to score, but any two becomes nothing more than a stylish two-piece plate. 
- Maki: A fun area-control vibe; the person with the most Maki tokens scores extra points at the end. 
- Tempura: You want two of these for a decent kick. Missing out feels like ordering two tasters and getting half a bite. 
- Dumplings: A rolling ladder; the more you collect, the better the payoff, but you risk overloading with the wrong types. 
- Pudding: This token is both a late-game surprise and a potential heartbreak if you misread your path to the finish line. At the end, the player with the most pudding points wins a bonus that often decides the final standings, so yes, pudding is not just dessert in this world.

Strategy Corner
----------------
To master Sushi Go! Tin Version Open, Complete, you don’t need to memorize every possible card interaction, but it helps to know a handful of forecasting strategies.

- Reading opponents: The key to winning is not just what you take, but what you pass. If you see a friend chasing a Sashimi triplet, you can pivot early to grab Nigiri that will complement your own future scoring path while blocking them.
- Pudding timing: Pudding tokens often shift who leads at the end. If you can secure a pudding lead by mid-game, you might be able to secure the final round’s advantage without having to chase every single point in the middle.
- Tempo management: The heart of the game is managing tempo. If you lag behind for two rounds, you’ll be playing catch-up with a bunch of points that are hard to monetize. If you run hot, you risk overcommitting to a set that won’t maximize your final score. Balance is key.
