## Overview

Zombie Kittens is the irreverent sequel/companion to Exploding Kittens that asks a simple question: how many undead cats do you need to ruin your friends' day? In this 2-5 player card game, players draw, play, and sabotage their way to victory while the living and the undead blend into a chaotic furball of chaos. If you enjoy light strategy, silly humor, and a dash of RNG, this is your next party staple.

As with all things Exploding Kittens, the humor is heavy on the meme, the absurd, and the pun. The product line leans into neon pinks, groan-worthy puns, and cartoonish gore without crossing into truly grim territory. That balance is deliberate and commendable: it invites a wide audience from salty college roommates to the aunt who still laughs at dad jokes.

In this review, we’ll unwrap the card types, test the rules for clarity, rate the production quality, and consider whether this little undead deck deserves a permanent slot on your table. ## What's in the box

This is the moment to gauge value before shuffling your moral compass and your deck. The Zombie Kittens box is straightforward: a deck of cards, a small rulebook, a couple of tokens, and a healthy dose of undead puppies... wait, not puppies—kittens, obviously. The card count sits in the mid-range for a party game: enough to keep everyone engaged, but not so many that you need a day off to remember all the card names.

### Setup

Setting up Zombie Kittens is delightfully simple: shuffle the deck, deal a starting hand to each player, and lay a few curse cards on the table as optional mischief toys for later. The rulebook is short enough to read aloud during a coffee break, but deep enough to provoke discussion if you’re into deck-building-lite experiences.

### Turn structure and flow

A typical turn is a sprint: draw a card, play a card, maybe invoke a special ability, and then discard to a maximum hand limit. The core loop resembles Exploding Kittens in spirit: you’re trying not to blow up, you’re trying to mess with others, and you’re trying to keep from laughing so hard you drop the deck. The game’s tempo is brisk, which means you rarely feel like you’re waiting for the old CRT to reboot when your turn comes around.

### Card types and what they do

- The fun begins with the typical mix of attack, skip, and reverse cards, but Zombie Kittens adds flavor with undead-themed classes of cards: e.g., “Grim Reaper Snaps” that force opponents to draw, “Morgue Mantle” that hides your hand once per round, and “Zombie Swap” that rearranges players’ hands in a ludicrous display of chaos.
- There are also “Cat-astrophe” cards that cause board-wide mayhem and require a quick comedic role-play from the table. Yes, you read that right: sometimes the best strategy is to describe your own move with the gravitas of an improv scene captain.

The card art is bright, punchy, and very meme-forward. The undead-cat motif is charming, not terrifying; it invites players to lean into the high-energy silliness rather than recoil.

## How to play in practice

This isn’t a rulebook judgment; it’s a practical map for your first 30 minutes that will get you in the game faster than you can say “bad pun penalties.”

### Turn-by-turn walkthrough

1. Draw a card from the deck. If you draw an exploding kitten, you better have a mitigation card handy or you’re out—by “out,” we mean you’re out of the round and the remaining players cheer at your misfortune.
2. Play as many cards as you want from your hand, within the limits dictated by card types. You’ll chain reactions, steal cards, and pin down opponents with a flourish of theatrical gestures.
3. Keep an eye on the table dynamics. The quintessential Zombie Kittens moment happens when two players lock horns over a single mid-game risk—an exchange of glances and a whisper of “I dare you.”
4. End your turn with a quick discard if you’re over hand limit. The round continues until someone triggers the end condition (usually when a certain number of cards have been played or a timer has run out).

### Strategy and tactics

- Positioning matters, but not in the way you’d expect. You can’t control the entire deck, but you can often control the timing of your big plays. The best strategy revolves around predicting your opponents’ risk tolerance: if they’re the type who will chase every monster under the bed, you can push them to misplay.
- Hand management is essential. You don’t want to fill your hand with chaotic “yet-more” cards only to draw a bad mitigation at the last moment. Balance is the name of the game: keep a few defensive cards for the inevitable onslaught and pump up your offense when the table gets too complacent.
- Targeting and diplomacy play a surprisingly large role. You’ll find yourself negotiating with players using a blend of feigned charity and cunning strategy. If you’ve ever watched a high-stakes poker game where the players are also juggling plushy zombie cats, you’ve got the vibe.

### Randomness and luck factor

Zombie Kittens embraces luck as a spice rather than a recipe. The draw-luck influences who can pull off the big plays, but the real skill lies in timing and social psychology. In a party game, your most powerful moves aren’t always the cards you hold; they’re also the words you utter and the faces you make when you resolve a chaotic chain of events.

The artwork does what it’s supposed to do: it sells the theme without hammering the joke into the ground. ## Comparisons: Zombie Kittens vs Exploding Kittens

If you’ve played Exploding Kittens, you know how that engine works: a mix of direct action cards, misdirection, and a sustaining rhythm that rewards risky moves and pun-drenched banter. Zombie Kittens is both a spiritual successor and a refinement of that design. It cleans up a few rough edges you might have noticed in the original while introducing a few clever twists that keep the formula feeling fresh.

- Pacing: Zombie Kittens tends to move quicker on average due to streamlined turn structure and more decisive actions. The game doesn’t drag, but it doesn’t feel like a speedrun either; it hits a pleasant middle ground suitable for a 2- to 5-player table.
- Theme: The undead-cat aesthetic is a definitive upgrade for players who enjoyed the humor in Exploding Kittens but wanted a more cohesive board-room of jokes, puns, and cat callouts.
- Depth: Both games reward clever misdirection; Zombie Kittens adds a few new layers of card interactions that create interesting chain reactions. It requires a bit more attention to exact card text, which means you’ll replay for the aha moments rather than the pure luck of the draw.

## Variants, expansions, and what’s next

For players who want more chaos, there are a few official and unofficial expansions in the ecosystem. The base Zombie Kittens deck is robust, and expansion cards often introduce new retrofits to the core mechanic: more ways to swap hands, more ways to heal or doom an opponent, and more zany “cat-astrophe” events.

A note on balance: for every crazy card, there’s typically a counter-move. The design encourages playful competitiveness rather than outright oof-em-in matching. If you’re someone who loves deckbuilding or deeper strategy, you may crave expansions that introduce surface complexity without tipping the humor scale out of the window.

## Replayability and longevity

In party game land, replayability is king. Zombie Kittens offers enough variety to stay fresh for a handful of game nights. You’ll get different table dynamics depending on who’s at the table and how animated everyone chooses to be. If you rotate groups, expect a few cycles before you feel you’ve truly mastered the timing of every card and how to bait your friends into blunders.

If you crave a longer-lived experience, pair Zombie Kittens with other social games from the Exploding Kittens family for marathon nights that feel like a micro festival of chaotic card play. The humor scales nicely with audience size, and you’ll notice the room light up as the table erupts in running gags and outrageous puns.

## What players will love or dislike

Pros:
- High-energy, laugh-forward gameplay that’s easy to learn but hard to master.
- Solid component quality with a playful, non-threatening undead-cat theme.
- Short play sessions that fit perfectly into a party-night format.
- Great social dynamic that shines when players lean into improv and banter.

Cons:
- The humor can be a turnoff for players who prefer a more serious tone or refined strategy.
- Some cards can feel overly swingy, which may frustrate players who dislike heavy permutations.
- The rulebook is intentionally breezy; for first-timers, a quick explanation is enough, but there are a few edge-case interactions you might only discover mid-game.

## Who should buy Zombie Kittens?

If you love party games with a comedic edge, if you’re hunting for a lightweight card game for mixed-age groups, or if you’re a veteran Exploding Kittens player who wants more of that same energy with a new coat of undead paint, this is a strong candidate. It’s also a good choice for families who want a game that’s easy to pick up but still offers some clever moments.

On the other hand, if you worship the craft of heavy strategy, or if you are trying to maximize brain-burn in your game nights, you might find Zombie Kittens too chaotic. It’s not minimal-luck; it’s a social spectacle that can swing in unpredictable ways. That’s part of the charm, but not everyone’s cup of tea.

## How to incorporate into your game-night rotation

Because the game clocks in around 20-30 minutes per session, you can slot it between longer euro-games or heavier tabletop RPG sessions. A recommended plan: pair it with one or two lighter games, then finish with a dessert round of Zombie Kittens to cap off the night with a chorus of laughs. If you’re hosting, consider a brief “rule-of-thumb” run where you do a 15-minute warm-up to let newcomers witness the chaos before diving in.

## Final verdict

Zombie Kittens nails the tone: it’s chaotic, silly, and surprisingly strategic when players lean into the chaos. It isn’t trying to reinvent the wheel; it’s polishing the wheel until it shines with neon green undead gloss. It plays fast, plays loud, and plays to the room—exactly what a party card game should do.

If you’re buying this for a group that thrives on banter and quick wits, you’ll get more than your money’s worth. If you’re shopping for a deep, long-form strategy exercise, you’ll want to look elsewhere. For everything else, Zombie Kittens is a solid, entertaining pick that becomes a go-to whenever you want a quick, riotous break from the routine.
