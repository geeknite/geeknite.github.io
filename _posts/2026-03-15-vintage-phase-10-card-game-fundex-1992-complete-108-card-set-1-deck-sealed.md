## The Phases: A Quick Refresher (Why 10 Phases Is Brilliant and a Little Frustrating)

Phase 10's core mechanic is simple: complete a phase to advance to the next one. You don’t jump from Phase 1 to Phase 7 in a single heroic stunt, you earn every step with a mix of planning, bluffs, and occasional luck. Here are the ten phases, outlined for clarity:

- Phase 1: Two sets of 3
- Phase 2: One set of 3 and One run of 4
- Phase 3: One set of 4 and One run of 4
- Phase 4: Run of 7
- Phase 5: One run of 3 and One set of 4
- Phase 6: Two sets of 4
- Phase 7: One set of 5 and One set of 2
- Phase 8: One set of 5 and One set of 3
- Phase 9: One set of 4 and One run of 4
- Phase 10: One run of 4 and One run of 5

Phase 7 through Phase 10 demand more flexibility and more careful hand management. Wild cards add dramatic swing, while Skip cards can break a rival’s momentum in a way that is morally questionable but mechanically delightful.

### The Draw, Discard, and Turn Rhythm

Play proceeds in a familiar rhythm: draw a card, either lay down your current phase or keep a growing hand, then discard to end your turn. The goal is simple in spirit but rich in possible missteps: complete your current phase and press on to the next. Wilds act as all-purpose substitutes, but misusing them can waste your chance to finish later phases. Skip cards delay your opponents and can be used to create brief windows of advantage, which sometimes leads to glorious tableside taunting and occasionally tears on the sofa cushions.

## Strategy and House Rules: Getting the Most from a Vintage Set

A vintage deck is not a blank canvas but a well-worn map. Here are strategies that help you maximize the fun:

- Learn the phases and keep a mental checklist. If you know what you need for Phase 6, you won’t waste time recalculating on the fly.
- Use Wilds proactively. If you can secure a Wild early, it can set you up for multiple future phases; if you hoard it too long, you may regret the moment it finally comes into play.
- Timing of Skip cards matters. A well-timed Skip can swing a round by forcing others to bicker over the discard pile while you quietly plan your next move.
- Discard with intention. For new players, discarding rarely used cards can help opponents finish their phase, but when you’re in a heated race, every discarded card becomes a potential trap or salvation for someone else.
- Prepare for Phase 9 and Phase 10. The later phases demand more flexibility; if you’re behind, you’ll want to leverage the Wilds and your ability to read the table to pivot your strategy.
