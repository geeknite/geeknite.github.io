## How to Play (Very Rough Guide for the Time-Constrained Geek)

Throw Throw Burrito is a duel-meets-dodgeball title featuring three core concepts: match cards, burrito throws, and the glorious chaos that erupts when someone shouts a ridiculous line. Here is the gist with a bit more flavor:

- Setup: Each player draws cards and builds a working deck. Guard your burritos like precious fossils, because you will throw them, and you will probably regret it in a heartbeat.
- Objective: Score the fewest points by dodging opponents’ burrito throws and by triggering burrito battles through card combos. It is a race to avoid facial hits while trying to keep a straight face for the video call audience or your neighbor’s dog who thinks the burrito is edible comedy.
- Turns: You take turns drawing cards and attempting to complete sets that trigger burrito battles. When a battle happens, everyone else might grab their burrito and start a strategic lob, ideally toward cards or the table rather than faces. The energy climbs as cards collide and players realize they have basically invented a dodgeball heist at a dinner party.
- Endgame: The round culminates with a final sweep of cards and a last-second burrito throw that can swing the leaderboard faster than you can say team victory or mispronounced Felony Burrito in a kitchen-closet aria.

Rinse and repeat until the box looks disappointed that the chaos has left. The rules are straightforward enough for a weekend group to grasp within a couple of rounds, but the extra layers — such as card-based missions and optional house rules — give you plenty of room to customize the experience for your squad.

If you have played other light party games, you will recognize the DNA: approachable rules, quick rounds, and enough chaos to feel like you stepped into a goofy alternate universe where physics is optional and laughter is mandatory.

## Tips for Maximizing Your Chaotic Fun

- Embrace the chaos: If you overanalyze, you might miss the point. The joy comes from the ridiculous moment, not from computing the optimal path of a plush projectile.
