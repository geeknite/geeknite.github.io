## What is the Citadel Medium Shade Brush Anyway?

The Citadel Medium Shade Brush is part of the shade brush family from Citadel, designed specifically for applying washes and shades to miniature surfaces. The idea behind a shade brush is simple in concept and chaotic in practice: use a larger, soft bristle tool to push dye into recesses, pool pigment in crevices, and create controlled shadows without losing the high points you worked so hard to lay down with your base colors. The medium size is meant to be a middle ground between broad coverage and delicate control. It is not a sculpting tool for fine detail work, nor is it a sledgehammer for bulk coverage. It sits in the sweet spot where you want to shade quickly but still maintain some precision on armor panels, creases, and little nooks where a human model might have a sweat bead of doom.

From a distance this brush looks innocent enough: a long handle, a reasonably fluffy bristle head, and a calm, unassuming demeanour. Up close, you can tell there is some intent at work. The bristles are soft enough to lay down shade without pulling pigment across the surface, yet firm enough to avoid dipping into every micro detail and turning a crisp edge into a blurred rumor. It is the difference between a thoughtful shadow and a messy smudge. If you have ever chased a shade into a rivet head and seen it recoil in horror, you know the value of a brush that can respect the geometry of a model.

## Design and Build: What You Get for Your Paint-Splattered Dollars

The Medium Shade Brush feels balanced in the hand, with a comfortable grip that doesn’t threaten to spin like a helicopter if you apply a little pressure. The bristle bundle is a generous size but not a behemoth; enough surface area to push shade into recessed lines, but not so much that you paint your miniature like a wall. The bristles themselves are soft yet responsive, which means you can feather the product when needed or apply a heavier coat to create a smoky effect in heavier armor panels. The ferrule is snug, which matters more than it sounds. Loose ferrules are the enemy of a hobbyist with a deadline and a rainstorm outside.

In practice, the brush holds a respectable amount of wash without dripping or pooling too aggressively on flat surfaces. That said, you still need to manage your paint load. The medium shade brush does not forgive laziness. If you dunk it into a swamp of shade and then wipe only half of it on a rag, you will get a messy result and a lot of blotchy geometry. It is not magic; it is physics and practice. The trick is to keep the bristles just damp enough to lay the shade in the recesses and then pull back to preserve the raised areas. Too much pigment, and you end up with a goopy blot that resembles a bad inking job more than a deliberate shadow pass.

One nice touch is the bristle taper toward the tip. That taper helps guide shade into tight corners without floodlighting the model. If you have ever tried to lean a shade into a narrow gap with a standard brush, you know that the medium size helps you reach those stubborn creases without turning the whole panel into a muddy mess. The handle length is comfortable for long painting sessions, and the weight distribution keeps your hand from cramping after a couple of hours of non stop shading experiments.

## How It Differs from Other Shade Brushes

Shade brushes come in a few sizes, and the medium is often the most versatile for mid range models. The small shade brush is superb for precise shading in tiny crevices or around fine edge lines, but it can feel needlessly fussy for larger areas. The large shade brush covers bigger swaths quickly but risks pooling pigment in a way that reduces control. The Citadel Medium Shade Brush aims to offer a balance: enough surface area to cover moderate regions with one pass, but with a nimble enough head to tuck shading into recessed details. Some painters find the medium size ideal for vehicles, beasts of burden on the tabletop, or characters with heavy armor where shade needs to settle into panel lines without swallowing the entire silhouette.

When compared with other brands that also claim to be medium, the Citadel option tends to have a longer bristle life and a more forgiving tip. The quality control tends to be consistent enough that you can pick up a new brush and feel confident it will behave like the last one you bought, which is not something you can say about every brush in your kit. Caveats include the usual caveats: bristles may shed a bit, the ferrule can loosen with heavy-handed scrubbing, and you should always shape the tip after cleaning so it returns to its original geometry. If you want a robust, middle-ground brush for shading that doesn’t require a tiny championship ceremony to celebrate every time you pull pigment across a seam, the Medium Shade is a strong candidate.

## Hands On: Painting with the Medium Shade Brush

Imagine you are mid battle with a miniature on the table. You have already laid down a clean base coat. The next step is shading to show off depth and form. This is where the Medium Shade Brush shines or where it learns to run like a frightened otter, depending on your technique. Here is a practical approach that keeps things sane and results readable on the tabletop.

### Step one: Prepare your shade load
Take a small amount of shade and load it onto the brush. You want the brush to be damp, not dripping. A good rule of thumb is to test on a piece of scrap plastic or on the underside of a non important model if you are practicing. The aim is to have slightly viscous color that flows into crevices and along recessed lines without flooding flat surfaces. Wipe off excess pigment on a clean cloth before you approach the real model. Waste not, want not, and also because a sloppy application makes your glaze look like a smudgy fingerprint from a tiny space pirate.

### Step two: control the pass
Begin with a light pass along edges and into the deepest recesses. The idea is to insinuate shade in a controlled fashion rather than painting across the entire surface. Use the edge of the bristles to skim along lines, letting gravity and capillary action do the work. If you see pigment pooling in a flat face, lift the brush slightly and rotate the head to guide the shade back into the recesses. This is where the medium size earns its keep: you can cover a decent amount of area while still staying precise enough to maintain crisp highlights elsewhere.

### Step three: layering the shade
Shade is not a one and done affair. You may need to repeat the light pass in spots to build depth organically. Let each layer dry before applying the next if you want defined shadows. If you are aiming for a softer look, you can slightly water down the shade or torch the brush with a gentle sweep to encourage a more diffuse look. The medium shade brush is not a magic wand for perfect shading, but it does reduce the number of awkward passes you need to reach that balanced look you see in official miniatures. Practice yields a steady hand, and a steady hand yields fewer mistakes and more dramatic results on the tabletop.

### Step four: edge considerations and cleanup
As you approach raised edges where you want to preserve a crisp light seam, you will find the brush head behaves predictably with little wandering. If you accidentally drag shade onto a raised edge where you want light, you can quickly burnish back with a clean brush or a damp cloth. When you finish, rinse the brush under lukewarm water and reshape the bristles with your fingers to keep the head from splaying. A quick shake and a rest on a brush stand helps keep the bristles aligned for the next session. Maintenance matters; a happy brush returns the favor with reliable shading work.

## Maintenance and Longevity

Every tool needs care, and brushes are no exception. The Medium Shade Brush is relatively forgiving, but neglect leads to frayed tips and a stiff head that refuses to participate in your shading choreography. A few rules to keep it singing:

- Rinse with mild water after use to remove pigment. Hot water can loosen stubborn pigments and damage the bristles over time.
- Use a gentle soap or a dedicated brush cleaner if you are dealing with oil based products or heavy pigments. Do not scrub the bristles into submission; you are refinishing a delicate instrument, not doing a home improvement project.
- Gently reshape the brush while damp and lay it flat to dry. Do not stand it on its head, as that can cause pigment to run into the ferrule.
- Avoid mixing paints across multiple shades without cleaning the brush in between. Color contamination is the silent killer of shading fidelity.
- Store with the bristles protected or in a brush pot to maintain the head geometry. A bent bristle is a symphony of regret.

If you take these simple steps, your Citadel Medium Shade Brush will join you in many successful projects. It will not cure bad painting habits, but it will help you execute shade passes with a level of polish that makes you want to show off your work instead of hiding it behind a gaming table.

## Why You Might Reach for This Brush Often

The medium shade brush truly earns its keep when you have models with a lot of recessed details and panel lines. Tanks, power armor, fancy cloaks with many folds, and faces with crisp eye sockets all benefit from a shade that can settle into grooves without pulling pigment into the flat surfaces. It is not a magic cure for every shading task. For very fine panel lines or micro details, a smaller shade brush or a different technique may be more appropriate. For mass shading of legions, however, the Medium Shade offers a productive balance between control and speed. In Geeknite terms, it is a reliable sidekick that does not demand all your attention like a clingy mentor, but still provides meaningful support on the battlefield of color.

## How to Build a Small Shortlist of Best Practices

- Always test on a spare piece first. Pretend you are auditioning a candidate for a very important role and you do not want to ruin your day one model.
- Keep the brush hydrated but not soaked. A damp brush is the difference between a crisp line and a painty blur.
- Use the right amount of shade. Too little is a waste of time, too much leads to pooling and loss of detail.
- Clean between sessions to maintain performance. Residual pigment can dry into a crust that ruins your next shading run.
- Pair with a reliable base coat and top highlights. A strong foundation and a clear light source make shading far more forgiving.
