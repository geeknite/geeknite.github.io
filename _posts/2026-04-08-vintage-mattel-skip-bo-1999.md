## How Skip-Bo Works
Skip-Bo is a race of logistics and sequencing. You’re not just playing numbers; you’re choreographing a small coup of cardboard.

### Setup
- Each player starts with a stock pile of 30 cards, face down, with the top card flipped face up.
- You draw to a hand of five cards.
- In the center, four build piles begin empty. Each build pile can be started with a 1 or a card that helps maintain the ascending sequence as you build.
- The rest sits in the draw pile.

### On Your Turn
- You may play any card from your hand or your stock pile to the center build piles if it continues the sequence (1, 2, 3, …). You may also use wild Skip-Bo cards as any needed number.
- You may build multiple actions in a single turn if the cards line up for you. The thrill of chaining is real.
- If you cannot or don’t want to play, you may drop a card onto one of your four discard piles. The top card of each discard pile is accessible on later turns.
- After your actions, you draw back up to five cards in your hand (if possible).

### Ending a Round and Winning
- The endgame is dramatic: when you reveal and place the last card from your stock pile onto a center build pile, you win. The table may erupt into cheers, groans, or a strategic silence depending on your group’s vibe.

### A quick note on mischief and strategy
Wilds are powerful; use them to smooth the path to your stock pile, but don’t waste them on a dead boat. The best players use wilds to bridge awkward numbers that would otherwise stall a big build pile.

## Playthrough: A Sample Round
Let’s walk through a mock three-player round to give you a feel for the cadence.

- Step 1: Setup. Each player has a stock pile of 30 cards, the top of each pile is face up. The center gets four build piles with the first few numbers popped on top.
- Step 2: Hands are dealt. Five cards in each player’s hand, the rest waiting in the draw pile.
- Step 3: Turn 1. Player A plays a 1 onto one build pile, then a 2 onto the same pile if a card is in hand or from stock. A wild may be used as any number to propel a pile forward.
- Step 4: Discard and draw. If Player A can’t extend a build pile, they discard a card onto a discard pile and draw back to five cards.
- Step 5: Turn 2 and beyond. Player B and C follow similar logic, trying to reveal their stock pile’s top card to begin a sprint toward completion.
- Step 6: Endgame tension. If someone reveals the last card from their stock pile and places it onto a build pile, the round ends with a flourish.

Note: The exact card order depends on shuffle and luck, which is the delightful variable that makes every round feel like a fresh heist.

## Strategy and Tactics
- Early planning beats late panic. Focus on getting at least one build pile running so you have a continuous source of play cards.
- Wild card management is everything. Don’t squander a wild at the wrong moment; you’ll regret it when the next card you need is trapped under a stock card you can’t reach.
- Discard piles are your strategic leverage. You want top cards that you can draw into in a future turn; if the top card is useless, consider shuffling it to another pile.
- Know when to push your stock pile and when to hold. If your stock top card is a number you can place, do it. If it’s a dead end, give your hand a chance to find a different route.
- Endgame tendencies: When someone is close to finishing, other players spring into action. It becomes more about disruption and last-minute card scouting than straightforward arithmetic.

## Variants and House Rules
- Quick-start variant for younger players: reduce stock pile to 15 and simplify the build piles to two.
- Cooperative variant: Instead of racing to deplete your stock, the group works to complete a target number of rounds, focusing on team strategy rather than personal win.
- Timed sessions: Set a timer for 15 minutes and see who can get the most stock cards off in that window.

## Care and Condition of Vintage Cards
- Storage: Keep the deck in a cool, dry place away from sun at all times. The older cards may show slight yellowing, which adds character.
- Handling: Gentle shuffles, careful flips, and avoiding water are your best friends.
- Sleeves: If you want to extend the life of the stock cards, consider protective sleeves, although this will change the tactile feel you remember.
