## Unboxing: First Impressions

Fortune Row comes in a compact box that looks like it was designed by a fortune teller who lost their sense of color. The artwork on the cover leans into the noir-blues and neon-pinks that City of Mist is known for, which signals that this ain’t your grandma’s historical minis line. Inside, you’ll find a nested foam tray with individual blisters and two odd little plastic sprinkles that look suspiciously like tiny dice or dice-adjacent components. In a good way, this packaging telegraphs care and a sense of “these figures are meant to be handled, not just ogled.”

What’s included varies by the bundle you choose, but the standard Fortune Row starter typically delivers a squad of 6–8 heroes, each with a distinctly different pose and a unique base design. Some older readers might recall that City of Mist’s core vibe is about the mythic masquerade in modern settings. The hero figurines amplify that theme by making the disguises tactile: you can lift a cape, inspect a trench coat, or tilt a hat and pretend you’re listening to the city’s whispers.

As a packaging note, the bases are generous for dynamic poses but not so oversized that you’ll trip over your own table tiles. If you’re planning a heavy display diorama, the included bases are serviceable, but you’ll probably want to swap them for something more cinematic later on. And yes, there are instructions printed on the inner tray—a small victory for those of us who forget to check the tiny pamphlet until after Christmas lights are already up.

### The Sculpt Quality: Style vs. Realism

In City of Mist, style often trumps strict realism, and Fortune Row is no exception. The sculpts favor tall, narrative silhouettes that scream “investigator with a secret identity” rather than “professional bodybuilder who reads philosophy at night.” The faces have character—the slight scowl, the quizzical eyebrow raise, the way a hand glides along a coat lapel—and those micro-expressions are the reason you’ll actually remember each figure after color-washing them.

The armor plates and fabric folds are exaggerated in a way that translates well on a tabletop. You’ll notice that weapons are stylized rather than hyper-accurate; these aren’t Warhammer-Imperial-chassis armaments. They’re faithful to the game’s pulp-noir vibe: a pistol that looks like it belongs in a detective’s pocket, a staff that reads more “arcane art piece” than “straight-up blade.” If you’re the kind of painter who loves feathered hats, trench-coat buttons, and little arcane sigils etched into cuffs, Fortune Row delivers.

If you’re a stickler for realism, you’ll find some places where the scale feels a touch off—some figures have slightly elongated limbs for dramatic effect, while others have wider stances for dramatic balance on sloped terrain. It’s a stylistic choice, not a manufacturing flaw, and it feeds the overall comic-book vibe City of Mist craves. The paint-ready surface is decent out of the box; you’ll want to hit the metallic accents with a little care to avoid losing the sculpt’s line work under a heavy primer coat.

## Miniature Quality: Material, Fit, and Assembly

Fortune Row figures typically use a standard modern mini resin/plastic mix. The material is sturdy enough for regular play sessions and tolerates a casual “slap-dab” approach to assembly without shattering. If you prefer assembly-heavy kits, you’ll appreciate how cleanly the parts fit together with minimal pinning. If you’re new to minis, fortune favors the patient: you may want to take a slow approach to removing pour lines and filing nubs to preserve the sculpt’s sharp lines.

One feature that stands out is the alternative build approach: some figures include modular accessories that can be swapped for different scenarios. That’s a nice nod to City of Mist’s flexible storytelling—the idea that a hero could be more than one thing in different sessions. It’s a clever way to keep the same sculpt feeling fresh across weeks of nights-rounds while still delivering consistent base sizes.

Practical tip: use a hobby knife, a pair of small files, and a gentle touch when removing mold lines. The lines are present but not aggressive, which helps if you want to preserve the subtle fabric creases and the tiny sigils on cuffs.

Fortune Row’s heroes inhabit a world where myth isn’t a single power but a constellation of small, story-driving moments. The figurines embody that ethos. Their silhouettes suggest a story behind every gesture: a detective whose stakeout is a fortune-teller’s parlor, a vigilante with a secret oath, a medic who patches the night’s wounds while the city coughs up neon. In practice, the models work well as both PC (player character) stand-ins and as narrative NPCs. ## Playability: How Do They Fit Into City of Mist Sessions?

City of Mist operates with a mix of narrative micro-goals and general combat-lite mechanics. Fortune Row figures are designed with that cadence in mind. They serve as visual shorthand for your players and as a quick-reference tool for storytelling beats. Here are a few ways to integrate them effectively:

- Character anchors: Assign a Fortune Row figure to each investigator to remind everyone of their factional allegiances and personal arc. A quick glance at the figure signals “this character’s myth is about deception” or “this hero’s fortune is tied to a shadowy benefactor.”
- Scene prompts: Use a hero’s pose to inspire a scene’s mood. A figure with a raised fist and a cloak fluttering can signal a tense standoff; a character with a hand extended and an open palm can hint at a bargaining moment with a shadowy city patron.
- Status markers: For games that track narrative tokens or mystical charges, the base can double as a token platform. A tiny painted gem or a sticker on the base can represent a power-up or a debt paid to the city’s underworld patrons.

If you’re a GM who likes to run “prestige sessions” with bespoke showpieces on the table, Fortune Row offers a ready-made aesthetic that helps set expectations for your players: this world is stylish, a little dangerous, and always watching you through the rain-slick neon gutters.

## Painting and Display Tips

If you’re new to painting figures, Fortune Row provides a forgiving canvas. Here are some practical tips to get the most out of your minis:

- Start with a crisp undercoat. A black or dark gray undercoat tends to make the neon accents pop. If you’re fond of a more dramatic look, a white undercoat gives you brighter color separation.
- Focus on contrast: the City of Mist vibe leans into contrast—dark suits with saturated accents, pale faces with deep shadows. A quick wash (brown for leather, blue-gray for capes) helps push those folds into the background while the highlights read on the foreground.
- Use metallics sparingly for weapons and gear; the “myth in the street” theme benefits from a touch of metallic shine to hint at enchantment without overpowering the figure’s silhouette.
- Seal with care: a matte sealant helps protect the paint from greasy tablehands and snack-time paint smudges. A satin finish can also reduce glare during streaming or photo sessions.

If you’re feeling extra creative, consider basing the figures with urban textures: a sidewalk gravel mix, a few micro-stones for a rain-pocked alley vibe, or a tiny scenic slip for a rooftop scene. The Fortune Row figures lend themselves to these basing methods because their stories live in the spaces between the city’s lights and the shadows under its awnings.

## Painting Gallery: Sample Palettes and Quick Techniques

While this is a text-based review, we encourage you to imagine the following palettes as starter ideas, not gospel:

- Noir Night: charcoal coats with deep burgundy accents and silver filigree, set against pale skin and subdued blue-gray shadows.
- Neon Whisper: electric blues and pinks on capes, with muted tan or gray for skin tones to keep the neon from overpowering the model’s silhouette.
- Street Sage: earthy browns and olive greens for the trench coats, with brass accents for the odds-and-ends, and a muted red for a weapon grip or badge.

Quick technique tips:

- Layering: build up from a mid-tone base to darker shadows and lighter highlights for a cinematic look. Don’t be afraid to re-wash with a slightly different shade to deepen the crease lines.
- Dry brushing: use a light hand on raised surfaces to emphasize textures on fabric, leather, and metal without obscuring the sculpt’s detail.
- Wash placement: apply washes to recesses first, then dry the raised areas. This yields a more readable figure at gaming distances.

The end result is a group of minis that look cohesive on the table and maintain recognizable individual silhouettes from across the room.

When you compare Fortune Row to generic modern-sci-fi or fantasy lines, you’ll notice two things: (1) the aesthetic heart is stronger in Fortune Row for narrative play, and (2) the parts quality and fit are consistently reliable, which means fewer fiddly misalignments and more time painting or gaming.

## Final Verdict: Should You Buy Fortune Row?

If you read this far, you’re likely asking: is Fortune Row “worth it” for a City of Mist table? The short version: yes, with caveats. If your table loves a strong narrative emphasis, a splashy noir vibe, and characters that read clearly from across the room, Fortune Row is a strong buy. If your campaign is more dungeon-crawler focused or if your players prize relentless technical realism, you might find Fortune Row’s stylized silhouettes a touch less ideal for deep tactical play. That doesn’t mean you can’t enjoy them; it just means you might want to pair them with other lines to enrich the stage’s mood without compromising your chosen playstyle.

As with any miniature line, the real value comes from the stories you tell with them. Fortune Row gives you reliable, visually striking pieces that help you tell those stories faster, with less explanation. The silhouettes are readable, the sculpts are expressive, and the whole package matches the game’s promise: myth and modern life intertwined in a neon-soaked city where every corner might be a clue or a trap meant to bend your fortune.

## Final Recommendation

- If you’re after a compact, visually distinctive set for City of Mist that emphasizes narrative storytelling and table presence, Fortune Row is a solid pick.
