Where the speed actually matters

We often see bursts of 14,500 MB/s in synthetic tests, but the question is whether that translates to noticeable gains in real workloads. We expect to see speed advantages in tasks like game level loading and large file transfers, especially when you are moving big datasets or 4K/8K media between storage and RAM. One of the most important parts of any Gen5 evaluation is to examine sustained performance over time. Gen5 drives can thermally throttle if they get too hot or too long under heavy load, so we keep a close eye on temperature curves and the onset of throttling. In our tests, the T710 maintains impressive reading performance for short bursts and delivers solid sustained performance in warm conditions, but like many high-end NVMe drives, it does appreciate a good airflow or an aftermarket heatsink for long sessions of heavy lifting.

Thermals, cooling, and long-session behavior

Thermal management is a big theme for Gen5 drives. The T710, like many Gen5 modules, can get warm under sustained heavy loads. Our testing shows that under a modest workload the drive remains comfortable, but in a fully loaded workstation with no aggressive cooling, you can expect the heat to climb. If you plan to push this drive for hours at a time, consider an inline heatsink or a good chassis fan arrangement for your desktop build, or a laptop with a robust cooling curve. The upside of a Gen5 drive is that the heat is manageable with practical cooling; the downside is that you might want to keep an eye on temperatures during marathon editing sessions. 

Thermal throttling behavior and how to avoid it

When the drive hits its thermal limits, you will see a drop in peak speeds to preserve integrity and avoid damage. This is a normal protective measure and not a failure. The key is to design your system to avoid sustained extreme loads, which many enthusiasts do by pairing the drive with a capable cooling solution and ensuring good airflow within your chassis. The T710 handles throttling gracefully, and in most day-to-day workloads you will not reach the ceiling of its capabilities.

Compatibility and OS support

This is a PCIe Gen5 NVMe drive, so you need a motherboard with Gen5 support to extract the full potential. If your motherboard is still on Gen4, you can use the drive, but the speed ceiling will be the Gen4 interface, which means you won’t see the glow of those headline speeds. Windows, macOS, and Linux installations are straightforward, with standard NVMe drivers. If you intend to use the T710 in a system with PCIe Gen5, ensure your BIOS is up to date so you get the best possible handshakes from the controller. For laptop users, check your thermal design power and cooling as laptops are not all built with the same airflow in mind.
