## Overview
Phase 10 is among the classic gateway games that many grownups now own out of sentiment and skepticism about their life choices. It is a rummy-style card game where players attempt to complete 10 sequential phases rather as if they are climbing a project ladder: first a couple of sets, then a run, then more complex combos, all before a cruel timer called the other players drawing to you. The Fundex edition from 2002 is one of the more beloved early prints, known for its bright colors, simple rules, and a certain plastic-wrapped aura that says you are about to embark on some constructive family competition rather than a high-stakes tournament of brainpower.

### What makes Phase 10 tick
Phase 10 uses a standard deck plus special phase cards. Each player must complete Phase 1 through Phase 10 in order, though you can skip around within a round by meeting the requirements of whichever Phase you are on. The catch is that players can only move forward after they lay down their cards in exactly the right combination: two sets of three, a run of seven, and so on. The game blends rummy-like hand management with a racing element: who can complete the next phase fastest and who will be the last to finish? The 108-card deck includes numbers in four colors, wild cards, and skip cards, creating a colorful yet methodical chaos that keeps players engaged and mildly annoyed in equal measure.

## Gameplay Experience
The heart of Phase 10 is the phase ladder. The first two phases are straightforward: collect two sets of three cards of the same number or color (depending on how the phase is defined in a given rule variant). As you climb, the requirements become more intricate, demanding runs, mixed color sets, and a steady supply of both luck and skill. The New Old Stock edition doesn’t redefine the rules; it preserves the core mechanic while letting you experience the tactile joy of a deck that has never seen an actual game played in a kitchen or a living room floor that has become a temporary zoo of snacks and paw prints.

### A typical round pattern
- Each player draws a card to start the turn, then discards one to the discard pile.
- The active phase for the round is visible on your player sheet or in the instruction booklet; you must try to complete that phase by laying down qualifying sets or runs.
- After you lay down a phase, you can move on to trying the next one on subsequent turns. If you finish all ten phases, you win, or more commonly, you are the player with the lowest score when someone hits the end of the round count.
- Penalties apply for cards left in hand at the end of a round, encouraging efficient play and minimal stalling tactics.

## Playability and family dynamics
Phase 10 is not a heavy game. It’s designed to be taught in minutes and played in about 45 minutes to an hour, depending on how many players you have and how aggressively you strategize. The New Old Stock version can spice up a family game night with its history-laden box and a few stories about unboxing day. ### Tips for new players
- Focus on the phase at hand, but keep an eye on your discard pile. Strategic discards can force opponents to help you complete future phases by exposing cards you can use later.
- Don’t hoard cards for too long. A balanced hand reduces the risk of getting stuck at the end of a round.
- Learn the wilds and skips; they can swing a round from almost perfect to chaotic in a single turn.
- If you are teaching new players, consider starting with a shorter variant of Phase 10 that only uses Phase 1 through Phase 5 to ease them into the rhythm before you unleash the full ladder.
