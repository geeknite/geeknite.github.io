## Overview and first impressions
Zombicide remains one of the most approachable co op shooting games on the market. It plays well with new players, and it scales nicely for experienced groups who want to argue about door placement and whether to rescue the kid or the cat. This Lucio Parrillo expansion sticks to what CMON does best: deliver a visually stunning set with enough mechanical sparkle to justify the price tag, without turning your gaming table into a murder scene from an art gallery.

In the box you will find two Survivor miniatures, a handful of new zombie types, new equipment cards, and a set of plastic bases that make it easier to stage your own catastrophe. The artwork by Parrillo is the centerpiece here, with bold color palettes, dynamic poses, and a sense that every piece of plastic is auditioning for a role in a post human fashion show. The cover art is unapologetically loud, and if you like your zombies with a lot of swagger — well, you are in the right aisle of the board game cosmos.

### The two survivors: a closer look
The two survivors included in the pack carry a theme that feels fresh yet familiar. They are not simply reskins of existing characters; they bring distinct playstyles that work well as a duo in a standard Zombicide session, and their flavor rides nicely on top of Parrillo visuals.

#### Survivor 1: The Fixer
The Fixer is your go-to for “make do with what you can salvage” gameplay. They start with an assortment of tools and a knack for improvisation. In game terms, The Fixer shines when you have to interact with the environment rather than brute-force your way out of trouble. Expect better success rates when you are attempting to lock doors, jury-rig a barricade, or repurpose a mundane item into something that resembles a weapon. The character art by Parrillo shows them with a smirk and a tool belt that seems to contain everything except an actual repair manual. The modeling is sharp enough to double as a micro sculpture display when the table is quiet between waves.

Mechanically, The Fixer pairs nicely with a more aggressive ally, creating a classic corkscrew of synergy. The two Survivors in this set complement each other: The Fixer can shore up the environment while the other survivor charges forward. If you like to approach Zombicide as a puzzle, The Fixer offers you several more levers to pull before you push your way through a horde. Parrillo captures the essence of a good tech-savvy survivor — someone who would rather patch the infrastructure than beg for mercy from the zombies.

#### Survivor 2: The Scout
The Scout is a speed-focused character that thrives when you need to outrun or outmanoeuvre the undead. Think of them as the character who finds the safe path through a cluttered corridor, who can sprint to the next room to set up a better defense, and who is not shy about leaving a teammate behind to lure a zombie into a carefully placed trap. The miniature is dynamic, with a lean silhouette and an expression that says, I know what you are thinking and yes, I am doing it anyway.

The Scout introduces a few extra tactical options that mesh well with The Fixer. In a typical scenario you can deploy The Scout to check corners, trigger traps from range, and come back with more information about the board state. This is a nice thematic hook for players who want a more explorative vibe, rather than simply dice rolling and whacking zombies into oblivion. Parrillo makes the character look like someone who would rather live to tell the story than shout about their glory on the battlefield.

### New Zombicide zombie types and flavor
The expansion is not all about two survivors; it also introduces new zombie variants that shake up the way you approach a room. The new models lean into Parrillo’s signature style: they are mean, they are cool, and they look like they would absolutely paint your nails while chasing you down the hallway.

- Brutes with a more menacing presence: bigger models that require multiple hits, and that punish sloppy targeting with devastating close-range bites.
- Agile stalkers: faster, more tactical zombies that threaten to overwhelm if you focus too much on the big bads and neglect the little ones.
- A few flavor zombies that carry special rules meant to interact with the new survivors. These are not just reskins; they carry a story beat that ties back to the artist’s interpretation of a post apocalypse aesthetic.

From a tactics standpoint, the new zombies push players to diversify their approach. It is not enough to run straight into a room and splash attack; you will need to pace your team, coordinate your two survivors, and keep an eye on your card draws so you do not end up with a bad hand when the brutes arrive.

### Artwork, flavor, and the Parrillo touch
Lucio Parrillo has a long history of producing art for horror and fantasy settings. In this expansion, his attention to detail translates into superb miniature sculpts and pack artwork. The color palette tends to be bold, with high contrast that makes the miniatures pop on the table. If you are a fan of his other work, you will find common threads here — the way he handles textures, the sense of motion, and the juxtaposition of heroism against visceral danger.

The cards and components also benefit from his aesthetic. Even the mundane equipment cards feel like collectible items rather than throwaway spacers. The result is an expansion that looks good enough to display between sessions, which is a nice perk for players who like to pretend their hobby room doubles as a small gallery.

## Contents and quality check
All CMON expansions tend to be well stocked in terms of plastic and cardstock, and this pack is no exception. You will find:
- 2 survivor miniatures with bases
- A handful of zombie minis with cast detail
- New equipment cards that slide into your existing deck
- New encounter cards with sneeze-worthy artwork
- A few tokens for tracking conditions on the board
- Rulebook addendum that clarifies how the new pieces integrate with the base game

The plastic quality is typical CMON: durable, well-trimmed, and designed to withstand the fury of a table that has decided to survive another wave. The head-to-body fit on the miniatures is clean, with minimal flash that would require extra prep. If you like to keep your minis pristine, you will still want to wipe them down after a particularly grim playthrough; Parrillo style tends to invite dramatic scenes after a dozen turns, and you do not want permanent marker smudges on your zoms or survivors.

One thing to watch for is the balance. The two new survivors are interesting and mechanically distinct, but the presence of the new zombies can tilt the board in subtle ways. If your group has a particular rhythm that works well with your baseline zombies, you might want to do a quick test run before a full campaign. The good news is that the designers included enough safeguards in the expansion to avoid one side feeling completely overpowered. You can still have a tense night and a couple of laughs while projectile slime hits the mat.

## How it plays with base Zombicide rules
If you are familiar with Zombicide, you know the core mechanics: players take turns taking actions, managing survivability, and trying to keep the horde at bay. This expansion slots into the ritual without requiring a full rulebook reread, which is a win. The new survivors use their unique abilities to unlock new tactics that feel like a natural extension of the core game rather than a radical departure.

The new environment-oriented tricks open up a few clever maps, allowing for better use of line of sight, door placements, and trap setups. The two new survivors can set up complementary plays where one screens the other while they push forward to collect items or trigger an objective. It does not break the pace; it enhances it with a little more planning and a few more options when the dice refuse to cooperate.

If you are planning to run a short session or a longer campaign arc, this expansion provides enough content to justify adding it to your collection. It is not a dead end; it is an evolving toolkit that invites you to experiment with different crew compositions and room layouts.

## The verdict and final recommendation
So should you pick up the Lucio Parrillo expansion pack for Zombicide? If you crave more personality on the table, if you want a pair of survivors that feel distinct and flavorful, and if you like your zombies with more flair than a runway show, then yes, this is a welcome addition to your board game shelf. The two survivors provide a complementary dynamic that keeps the team balanced while still enabling interesting combos. The new zombie types inject a fresh challenge into the mix, preventing the game from becoming predictable and repetitive. The production value, the art direction, and the overall polish make this a standout in its tier of expansions.

On the fence about price? Consider how often you play and how much you value the aesthetic experience. If you have a dedicated gaming group that meets up regularly and enjoys themed sessions with a robust art direction, this expansion will likely be frequently chosen over other, more generic add-ons. If you are a casual player who enjoys quick sessions and a lighter flavor, you may want to sample this expansion first in a demo night rather than committing to a full campaign right away.

## Final recommendation
- If you want more character and a stronger sense of style in your Zombicide games, go for it. The two survivors bring different tools to the table and the new zombies push you to rethink room layouts and teamwork.
- If you are all about the mechanics and want a pure numbers game, you will still enjoy the expansion but may not walk away feeling wowed by the art direction alone. The mechanical gains are real, but the wow factor is in the visual storytelling and thematic cohesion.
- If you are a collector of artist collaborations or if you love Parrillo style in particular, this is a must pick up. Aesthetics aside, the expansion integrates cleanly into your existing rules and brings meaningful new options to your missions.

Overall score: A solid addition that improves gameplay variety while delivering a feast for the eyes. It does not reinvent Zombicide, but it adds a strong, thematically cohesive layer that elevates a good game into a memorable one.
