## How to play (Cooperative mode)
### Setup
Two teams? No, you can run a single cooperative squad or split into two rotating crews if you must. The entire group lays out the 5x5 grid, the updated cooperative key is shuffled and placed face up, and the spymaster, who is asymmetrically the star of this edition, takes the briefing mat. The rest of the players are on the same side of the table, in the same boat, sharing the same doom clock. The goal is simple in theory: uncover all safe words before the shadows close in. In practice, the number of words you can safely reveal depends on the difficulty level you choose. The clock is not optional; it’s a heartbeat that reminds you this is a puzzle you can solve together, not an escape room that hates your life choices.

### The turn structure
This is where the fun gets crunchy. On your turn, the Spymaster gives a single clue word and a number. The clue word should connect as many of the non-hot words on the grid as possible, with the number indicating how many words are meant to align with the clue. The catch? The team must interpret the clue collectively and decide which words on the grid to reveal. In the cooperative edition, you are all in this together, which means a misread can still be recovered by a bold, team-spirited push. Perfect morale is as valuable as perfect logic here. If you reveal a safe word, you’ve earned a point and the turn passes to the other side. If you reveal a developer’ s worst nightmare—an assassin word—the team loses a life. If the assassin word is revealed, prepare for groans, a few dramatic sighs, and a chorus of “we can do better next time.” The timer’s tick is your friend and your enemy, because time pressure is the spice that makes a good clue land with that satisfying click.

### Clues and communication
The cooperative twist nudges players toward collaborative phrasing. The Spymaster must translate their brain’s messy network of associations into a clue that everyone can understand without giving away the plan to rival factions (i.e., your cat who loves to knock over the game boards). This is where the game grabs you by the keyboard and asks you to type slower, think bigger, and curse less loudly when a clue lands on the wrong word. The grid words are chosen to be ambiguous enough that the team will wrestle with multiple fits. This is the heart of the experience: shared discovery. Your group dynamic matters more than individual brilliance. In a way, it’s therapy with a timer and a pile of clever words.

## Strategy and tips
### Beginner path
If this is your first rodeo with Codenames 2025 Cooperative, focus on building team language. Create a set of short, safe cue words that naturally map to common words on the grid. Practice with a timer until collaboration feels almost instinctual. Avoid chasing the most words on a single clue; accuracy and shared understanding are more valuable. Use the first round to understand each other’s communication quirks, then lean into that in subsequent rounds. Remember, you are aiming for synergy, not a solo performance.

### Intermediate play style
As you get comfortable, your team should start using more abstract connections. The key is to triangulate meaning with minimal risk. If the grid includes multiple words that could plausibly connect to a clue, you might opt for a smaller set of safe words to begin with, then build to more ambitious links as trust grows. You’ll also begin to recognize your teammates’ preferred mental pathways: some of you love indirect associations, others prefer direct, almost painfully literal links. Balance is the secret sauce here.

### Advanced tips
In this edition, timing and risk assessment are the differentiators between a good round and an epic failure montage. When the team faces a tough board, consider issuing a cautious clue that relates to a cluster of two or three words with one clear anchor word, rather than a broad clue that might mislead many. If you have a word that could be linked with several others, talk through the possible interpretations aloud. The strength of a cooperative game lies not in clever hidden thoughts, but in visible reasoning everyone can follow without a translator. The more transparent your thought process, the fewer moves you’ll waste arguing about it later.
