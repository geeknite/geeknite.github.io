## How to play this edition

### Setup and first turn
Like any Codenames game, you split the players into two teams. One player per team acts as the clue giver while the others try to guess based on the single one word clue and a numeric hint. In the HP edition, the clue giver should look at the grid and pick a word on the board that connects to a handful of HP terms you want to guide your team toward. The number at the end of the clue tells your team how many cards on the board are tied to the clue. The trick here is to balance a clue that is clever and safe enough to avoid leading your team into guessing an enemy agent or the spooky killer word, which in HP terms might be a dark term or a negative conjuration card.

The first round quickly becomes a dance between wordplay and your team’s HP knowledge. Some clues will land beautifully, others will veer toward the unpredictable. The HP edition rewards players who have a decent memory for characters and objects from the HP world, but it also punishes those who rely too heavily on obscure references. The safe route is often to anchor your clue in a known HP concept and then trust your team to make the leap.

### The flow of a typical round
1) Clue Giver announces a single word and a number
2) Team members discuss and select cards on the grid they believe match the clue
3) If they hit their own agents, they remove those cards; hit a neutral card, nothing happens; hit a bad card, their turn ends
4) If they find the enemy agent, the opposing team narrows their chances on the next round, which adds a dash of strategic misdirection
5) The round continues until one team clears all their agents

In the HP version, you will encounter clues that nod to houses, magical objects, professors, and famous spells. This is not a problem for most adult players, but younger players or non HP fans might need a moment to map a clue to a card. The design team has done a decent job of aligning the words with HP lore without turning the game into a trivia marathon. It remains a word guessing exercise first and a lore exercise second, which is exactly the kind of balance that works well for party games.
