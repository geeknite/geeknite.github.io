## What exactly is Saboteur The Lost Mines
Saboteur The Lost Mines is a thematic spin on the classic Saboteur framework. You are dwarves digging through a treacherous mine while some players secretly sabotage the team. The twist with The Lost Mines is the added terrain and objective that shifts the endgame from pure path building to a mini quest for a hidden mine chamber. The players who survive the mine maze and the sabotage attempts might find themselves clutching the coveted map fragment, while others end with nothing but a smug grin and a story about how they almost won by accident.

Amigo produced this edition in 2019, bringing their usual sturdy components and a nod to the classic Saboteur vibe. The box includes a mix of treasure cards, obstacle cards, mine passages, role cards, and the ever important lantern tokens that allow you to reveal or conceal loyalties as the game threads its chaotic path. The pre owned copy you see on shelves likely includes the base game elements plus any expansion or variant components that came with that run. When you pick up a used copy, you are not just buying a game; you are acquiring a artifact with potential fingerprints, victory tales, and perhaps a dent from a long box drop that happened during a particularly boisterous game night.

## Components and condition: what to expect in a pre owned Amigo 2019 edition
A used Amigo 2019 edition is as much about the vibe as it is about the components. Here is what to look for and how to interpret it:

- Box condition: If the box lid is a little loose, count that as character. A mild shelf scar can indicate a well-loved game. Major crushing, water damage, or warped boards should raise a red flag and perhaps a price negotiation deliverable.
- Cards: Look for creases or marks on cards. A laminated card that has begun to peel might still be fine, just be mindful of shuffle reliability. If the card backs are worn, it can affect the game flow— but in Saboteur, a worn back might also be your secret weapon as a polluting rumor agent.
- Tokens and markers: Lantern tokens and map fragments should be present and not battered. Missing or bent tokens can be replaced with DIY substitutes, but check how strict your local seller is about completeness.
- Player boards and role cards: Inspect to ensure they are legible. The art is not shy; you want to know if a role is the villain or the hero at a glance. If the role art is partially smeared, a sharp eye can still deduce loyalties, but you might miss the dramatic reveals that make the game sing.
- Seals and inserts: The internal tray helps during play. If the insert is broken or misaligned, you can still play, but expect occasional chaos when you attempt to return the tokens to their rightful place.

What does this mean for value? A well cared for pre owned copy can be the sweet spot for budget minded players who crave the fun of Saboteur without paying the new copy premium. The Amigo 2019 edition still looks good on a shelf, the artwork pops, and the components deliver the playful mischief that Saboteur is known for. But always factor in the potential for minor page wear on the treasure cards and the occasional cosmetic scuff. It is a pre owned product after all, not a pristine museum exhibit. The key is to assess it with the ruthless efficiency of a miner who knows every tunnel has a trick behind it.

## How to play in a nutshell (for the curious newcomers)
If you are new to Saboteur style games, here is the non terrifying elevator pitch. You are dwarves, you are mining for treasure, you will encounter paths that may lead you toward the Lost Mines or toward a cliff that collapses under your own poor planning. Some players hold secret roles as saboteurs. Their job is to derail the expedition without exposing themselves as traitors. The rest of the players must cooperate, albeit reluctantly, to gather loot and reach the mine exit before the saboteurs crash the party with misdirection and sneaky card plays.

The Lost Mines edition adds a specific map twist and hidden chamber objective that pushes players away from a straight line to the treasure. The path matters, the alliances matter less, and your ability to read fellow miners becomes as important as your ability to avoid stepping on a mine cart track that squeaks a little too loud.

## Game components in detail
- Cards: There are three main types of cards you will shuffle through during the game: path cards, action cards, and treasure cards. Path cards determine how you connect tunnels, action cards allow you to alter the board or sabotage an opponent, and treasure cards become points at the end. In the pre owned edition, these cards show signs of life. They have been shuffled, played, and perhaps kissed by good luck or bad luck. Either way, you will redraw and continue digging. 
- Role cards: You will be assigned roles in secret. Some players are loyal dwarves, others are saboteurs. The beauty is that you often do not know who is who until a critical moment when someone flips a card and the truth emerges with dramatic flair. If you pull the saboteur role, your aim is to mislead and hinder your allies without tipping your hat. This is where the social deduction part of Saboteur reveals its inner goblin. 
- Tokens and lanterns: Lantern tokens reveal or hide what is real. The theme of light vs darkness runs through the game like a stubborn beard in a windstorm. A lantern can be the difference between seeing a trap or stepping into it. 
- Rulebook and insert: The rulebook is concise and forgiving, which is a relief in a game that thrives on chaotic misdirection. A well kept insert helps the game maintain order during the post dungeon chaos of a four player session.

## Gameplay flow: the core loop
- Setup: Each player receives a role card face down and receives a set of starting resources. The map is laid out with path cards. The Lost Mines objective is placed, creating a light at the end of the tunnel but also a potential trap behind every boulder.
- The turn: On your turn you typically play a path card to expand the tunnel network, possibly laying a trap, and then you may perform an action card. Your choices sculpt the mine for yourself and you may train your crosshairs on nearby miners to coax them into revealing their loyalties. 
- Endgame conditions: The game ends when the exit to the mine is reached or when the Lost Mines objective is fulfilled, depending on the precise variant you are playing. In the Lost Mines edition the final stretch is a chilling test of trust and detective cunning, because the hidden objective can swing the results in surprising ways.

## Strategy notes: how to approach a Saboteur night with The Lost Mines
Strategy in Saboteur is a zigzag across the mine walls. Here are a few tips to help you survive the chaotic tunnels:

- Read the room but not the card sleeve: Mine runs are social games. The true power often lies in reading how folks react when you drop a key path card into play. Do they smile? Do they glower? Do they hide their lantern in their sleeve? These are the signals you want.
- Role management is king: If you get a loyalty card, use it to guide your choices. If you end up a saboteur, your goal is to sow just enough doubt to tilt others toward wrong decisions without exposing yourself.
- The early game is a dance of risk: Opening the first few tunnels sets the tempo. If you can guide others toward a safe route while secretly blocking the best treasure path, you gain a subtle advantage that can snowball.
- Targeted sabotage matters: Use action cards on key moments. A well placed sabotage can derail a rival while you quietly advance your own route. Timing is the difference between triumph and a chorus of groans.
- The Lost Mines objective makes the ending tense: Don’t forget that the final objective is not just about reaching the end, but about who reaches it while carrying the right cards or who loses the last map piece to a cunning play.

## Strategy quick hits by player count
- With 2 players: Saboteur becomes a tense duel of wits with quick back and forth. You need crisp misdirection and quickly sealed plans to win.
- With 3 to 4 players: The social dynamics shine. You can feint, accuse, defend, and chase across several rounds before someone reveals a crucial truth.
- With 5 to 6 players: Chaos escalates and a brave heart is needed. The more people you have, the more probable a wild gambit will unfold. Expect louder laughter and maybe a little shouting when someone pulls off a dramatic last turn flip.
