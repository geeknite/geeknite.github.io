## How to play in a sentence
Saboteur is a finger-llicking blend of deduction and push-your-luck path-building. Each player secretly takes a role: most players are miners trying to carve a path to the gold, while one or more players act as saboteurs, working against the miners without revealing their identity. On your turn you either lay down a card to extend a tunnel or play a card to change the tunnel’s direction. Your goal is to connect a path from your starting area to a goal tile that is reachable; the saboteurs want to scramble the tunnel so that no honest miner can reach a goal card before the round ends.

## The feel of the game: why it clicks in mixed company
Guest players, family nights, or a group of friends who pretend to be dungeon masters at every opportunity will enjoy the social tension. You can almost hear the whispers: I bet you’re a saboteur. The trick is that there is no permanent scar or claim to high ground; a saboteur can be a quiet one, biding their time, and suddenly they sock the miners with a well-timed block or forced misdirection.

### Tactics and tips
- Reading the table is half the game. If someone keeps placing cards that bend the tunnel away from any reasonable path to a goal, they may be a saboteur. If someone is loudly celebrating every long shot, they’re a gambler in the wrong casino (or just excited about being in the spotlight).
- The power of cooperation: sometimes the best move is to cooperate with a hidden saboteur to prevent a more dangerous play by someone else. It’s politics in a tiny cave, which is both funny and a little sad.
- The stash of tokens is not just for bragging rights. In many rounds, the final path length correlates with risk; shorter, quicker routes often reward risk-takers, while longer routes reward careful tunneling and a little luck with the card draws.

## Playthrough: a mini-scenario to illustrate flow
Picture this: five players, two saboteurs hidden in the crowd, and a row of path cards ready to be laid down. The miners begin by placing a straightforward path card, thinking they can catch a glimpse of how the dragon of the dungeon (metaphorically) will do them wrong. The saboteurs, wearing their metaphorical cloaks, quietly add a diversion card. There is a moment when a miner lays down a card that looks promising but ends up blocking their own route, which triggers the silent auctions of suspicion around the table. The hero of the round is the one who can read the room, or at least pretend convincingly that they can read the room, and then place the next card to create a threadbare path to one of the treasure-goal tiles.

There is always a moment where someone calls out: I knew it, you’re the saboteur. Then the group collapses into laughter, because the game thrives on that spark of ridiculous accusation, which is exactly what makes social deduction games both delightful and a bit ridiculous in the best possible sense.

### How to set up quickly
- Shuffle the path and goal cards.
- Deal role cards to players depending on the count (and the number of saboteurs).
- The starting point tells players where to place the first card; the first path card can be a little tricky if you want to start with a simple corridor or jump straight into a fork that invites misdirection.
