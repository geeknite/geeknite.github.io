# Zombicide Black Plague: Wulfsburg, Huntsman & Knight Boxes — Partially Painted, Fully Chaotic

If you’re a gamer who believes that a nice rulebook is a decent substitute for therapy, then Zombicide Black Plague with the Wulfsburg, Huntsman, and Knight boxes is your kind of chaos. I finally cracked into the trio of add-ons that have been haunting my storage bin like a pile of unpainted goblins: Wulfsburg (the big city expansion), Huntsman (the towering archer with questionable posture), and Knight (yes, a chivalrous shield-bro with a penchant for stomping undead). The good news: the parts are here, the painting is… partial, and the game is as replayable as a goblin’s mood after a moonlit banquet. The bad news: I now own more zombie-killing miniatures than I can responsibly display without a crash course in medieval diorama logistics.

This review is a hybrid unboxing-meets-play-through diary, with a dash of sarcasm and a dollop of practical painting tips. If you’re here for a dry checklist, you’ve come to the wrong castle. If you’re here for nerdy enthusiasm, you’re in the right citadel. Let’s dive into the chaos, the carnage, and the sculpted chaos of metal and plastic that makes up Black Plague with these three big boxes.

### The components and their carnivalesque chaos

The Wulfsburg box is the centerpiece. It includes city tiles, walls, a few terrain pieces, and enough zombie spawn cards to make you question your life choices after the first failed door check. The Huntsman box adds a set of ranged units and unique zombie models that feel like they’ve been dipped in an autumn forest and then squeezed into plastic. The Knight box brings a heroic silhouette into the fray—think of him as the guy who arrives with a shield so large it doubles as a makeshift shelter if you need to hide from a swarm.

The miniatures are a mixed bag of detail and practicality. Some sculpts pop with fluted armor, snarling faces, and heroic poses. Others are a bit wooden—especially if you’ll display them without painting. But that’s the charm of Zombicide: even the not-so-glam miniatures look good when you’re flinging zombies at them with abandon. The sculpting is clean; the seams are minimal; and the bases have enough texture to keep the figures stable during a sneeze-induced tabletop tremor.


## Painting status: partial glory and the battlefield of brushes

Let’s be honest: this is the part that will either seduce you into a painting frenzy or remind you that you own a chessboard of undead. The boxes are partially painted, which means some minis have color applied while others remain primed or unpainted. The advantage here is obvious: you can stage a few heroes with some splash color, then pretend you’re doing the rest later while the zombies keep shuffling in for the dramatic photo. The disadvantage is that the army look can be inconsistent from piece to piece, which may bother you if you’re a purist who craves uniformity.

My approach was a two-step plan: paint what I’ll use in the near term and shelf the rest for a rainy weekend when the weather is prime for painting in the living room while the family complains about the noise. The partially painted state is also an opportunity: you get to practice color palettes that compliment the base tones, experiment with washes on the metal accents, and decide whether you want the Knights to look like gleaming silver protectors or weather-beaten veterans of a thousand battlefields.

If you’re planning to repaint these, here are quick tips that helped me avoid a full-on painting meltdown:

- Start with a unifying scheme. Pick two to three base colors and keep armor tones consistent, so the army looks cohesive on the table.
- Use washes on armor and cloaks to bring out the detail. A couple of thin coats beat a single heavy one every time for depth.
- Dry brush highlights on fur or textured surfaces to give depth without gnarly edge highlights that scream “I’m a tabletop legend.”
- Protect your work. A light matte seal helps the paint survive the inevitable tabletop brushes and accidental finger smudges during intense gameplay.
- Don’t over-commit. It’s okay to focus on the pieces you’ll actually use in your campaigns first; you can circle back for the rest when time allows.

## How the expansions integrate with Black Plague: rules, synergy, and chaos

Zombicide Black Plague is already heavy with rules, dice, and a rhythm that can feel like a chaotic dance between you and a dozen undead. The Wulfsburg, Huntsman, and Knight add new layers: new terrain, new zombie types, new objectives, and new abilities for survivors to leverage. Thematically, it feels right: you’re moving through a medieval city under siege by the plague, with a knight charging ahead and a hunter picking off zombies from a distance while the Wulfsburg blocks routes and creates choke points.

Rule-wise, the expansions don’t rewrite the core. They augment it with new tiles, new spawn rules, and new objective cards. The risk here is balance: if you go heavy on terrain advantages, you can stall the board and drag out the game. My recommended approach is to introduce one new element per session: perhaps start with Wulfsburg’s city tiles, use the Knight’s aura around a chokepoint, and then bring in the Huntsman when you’re ready for tighter range dynamics. This helps new players acclimate to the flow of the game without getting overwhelmed by a forest of new rules.

## Gameplay impressions: swing, buzz, and the occasional heroic faceplant

In a few sessions with these three boxes, I found the following patterns:

- The Knight’s shield wall is invaluable in press-turns where you’re overwhelmed. He becomes a moving obstacle that forces zombies to take awkward routes, buying time for your ranged units.
- The Huntsman thrives in corridors and narrow streets. When you’re fighting through Wulfsburg’s alleyways, he rewards positioned firelines with extra hits and critical successes—if you roll well, you’ll feel like a master hunter and a terrible person for letting the others do all the risk.
- Wulfsburg’s terrain pieces create synergy with objective cards that require you to hold or clear certain zones. It’s a good design that forces players to think beyond “kill everything” in favor of “control the map and protect your survivors.”

Despite the best intentions, there are moments of mild friction:

- The number of tiles can lead to table clutter. If you’re playing on a small surface, consider a cut-down map or a dedicated play area to keep you from knocking a tower over during a dramatic zombie charge.
- The partially painted minis can be visually distracting mid-game if your display lighting is suboptimal. A few illuminated spots on the table do a lot to unify the look during dramatic moments.
- There’s a mild learning curve when mixing expansions. I recommend keeping one expansion active at a time until players are comfortable with new stats and spawn rules.

On the gameplay front, the trio offers a gratifying, slightly brutal experience. It’s not about “easy wins” but about clever positioning, calculated risk, and occasional ironic laughter when a plan barely sticks the landing while a plastic goblin tries to crawl into your boot.


## Painting tips for faster table-ready miniatures

If you’re excited to finish what you started and want quicker table-ready results, here are practical painting strategies that won’t turn your hobby into a permanent side quest:

- Prime in a color that complements your color palette. Black or white primer works for most metal and cloth tones, but a coat of gray can help speed up mid-tone decisions.
- Base coat in large clusters. Use a broad brush to lay down base coats on multiple figures simultaneously, then switch to smaller brushes for details.
- Pick a single metallic color and apply a wash. A brown or black wash adds depth without drowning the details in metallic sheen.
- Use color accents sparingly. A red cloak or blue trim on a knight can create focal points that pop on the tabletop without demanding hours of precision.
- Seal after the pigments settle. A matte seal keeps your work intact during tabletop mayhem and prevents the red from looking glossy under bright lighting.

If you’re new to painting, you might consider a modest starter kit and a scheduled painting night with friends. It’s a social activity that also yields tangible tabletop results, instead of a lonely vow to finish “someday.”


## Storage, organization, and transport: keeping the chaos portable

Zombicide boxes are one thing; transporting the riot of plastic is another. Wulfsburg, Huntsman, and Knight combine into a sizeable collection of tiles, tokens, and miniatures that can overwhelm a coffee table or a closet shelf. Here are some practical organization tips I’ve found useful:

- Use a dedicated miniatures tray for each faction, labeled clearly. It speeds setup and reduces the risk of misplacing a zombie spawn card in the sofa cushions.
- Invest in a foam insert or corrugated plastic tray. It protects the miniatures during transport to gaming nights and keeps paint chips from turning into a modern art exhibit on the car seat.
- Store terrain tiles in a flat-folder system or stackable trays to preserve their sculpted details. You don’t want a corner of a city block to crack under pressure from a backpack strap.
- Keep spare bases and extra tokens in a zip bag labeled with the expansion. It’s the little things that save you ten minutes of rummaging mid-game when you realize you’ve misplaced a key token.

## Value, value, value: should you buy these three boxes together?

Budget aside, the Wulfsburg, Huntsman, and Knight packs bring strong thematic value to Black Plague. They don’t rewrite the core rules, but they add enough tactical variety to keep campaigns lively across multiple play sessions.
