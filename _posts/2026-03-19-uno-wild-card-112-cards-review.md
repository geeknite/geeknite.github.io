## How to Play: A Quick Start Guide for Chaos Enthusiasts

UNO is, at its heart, a simple sprint to zero with a color-matching mechanic and a couple of sneaky tricks. The 112-card version doesn’t reinvent the wheel; it just stretches the wheel into a fun, wobbly ellipse. Here’s a friendly, no-snooze-start guide you can bring to your living room battlefield:

1) Setup: Shuffle the 112 cards thoroughly. Deal seven cards to each player. Place the rest face-down as a draw pile. Flip the top card to start the discard pile. If that card is a Wild or Wild Draw, follow the card’s instructions before anyone draws.

2) Turn order: Players take turns matching a card by color or number, or by playing a Wild card. If you don’t have a playable card, you draw one and hope for mercy (or a better card to humiliate your friends with on the next turn).

3) Wilds and Wild Draw Variants: The 112-card deck adds several new wild types. Some variants let you skip players, reverse the direction, or force everyone to draw two or four cards unless they can counter with a lucky Wild. The marquee addition is a handful of “New Rule” cards that change how the round ends or who gets to call Uno. Use these sparingly at first—watch as your group mutates from casual game-night friends into a cacophony of “I can’t believe you did that.”

4) UNO call and endgame: When you have one card left, you must yell “Uno!” If you forget, a standard penalty exists (draw a card), but you’ll quickly discover the 112-card deck has its own etiquette: yelling Uno isn’t enough; you’ve got to earn the right to do it. The first player to discard all cards wins. The game ends and a brief victory lap begins, usually accompanied by participants raising their hands like a tiny, exhausted crew celebrating a successful space launch.

What makes this version feel modern is how much the new Wild and Rule cards rub elbows with the traditional UNO core. It’s not a different game with a similar name; it’s an upgrade—like swapping a bicycle for a unicycle with glitter. And if you’re worried about the kids being overwhelmed, fear not: the base rules remain approachable, and you can choose to use fewer Wild cards to keep the pace brisk without spiraling into total mayhem.
