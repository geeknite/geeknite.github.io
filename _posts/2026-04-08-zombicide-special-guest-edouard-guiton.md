Welcome back to Geeknite, the place where cardboard boxes explode into adventures and coffee-powered dice decide your fate. Today we dive into a sealed treasure chest that promises more than just a few zombies and a little bit of doom. Yes, we are unboxing Zombicide Special Guest Edouard Guiton, a Nuovo Sigillato drop from CMON that somehow makes art nerds and zombie slayers fist-bump in the same room. If you thought Zombicide was a one-note apocalypse, think again. This Special Guest edition swaggered into the room with Edouard Guiton’s name on the cover and a painting brush behind its back like a rogue Lannister who actually pays his debts with color swatches.

Box opening is a ceremony in itself. The slipcover slides off with a whisper, the inner tray smiles at you, and the contents lie there like a well-behaved horde waiting for your strategy to happen. Nuovo - Sigillato means exactly what it says in the fine print of gamer thrill: nothing has been touched by mortal hands beyond your own, and that is where the magic starts. The box itself looks sharp, the typography is bold, and Edouard’s signature flare leaks out through the art on the cover with the confidence of a painter who just found a fresh palette. If you love CMON glossy box presentation, this one will drink your attention like a zombie drinks brains, but in a tasteful, non-gross way.

First impressions matter, and this edition nails them. The miniatures look crisp, the plastic feels sturdy, and the color separation is deliberate rather than haphazard. This is not the kind of set you tease apart with a dull knife; it is the kind you invite to tea first, and only then set a base coat on your favorite survivor. The box art features Edouard in a heroic pose, brush in hand, surrounded by a horde that looks equal parts menacing and amused. It’s that rare thing where the artist’s ego and the game’s brutal premise actually complement each other instead of fighting for space on the table.

In the spirit of proper unboxings, we start with the components. A typical Zombicide set includes a modular game board, a stack of scenario cards, a pile of zombie miniatures, a host of survivor figurines, and a treasure trove of tokens. This Special Guest edition sticks to that formula but adds a handful of twists that make it feel like more than just a paint-by-numbers expansion. The art cards, for example, are not just pretty pictures; they’re functional, loaded with tiny easter eggs in Edouard’s signature style. If you pause long enough, you’ll notice little nods to his other projects, a brushstroke of humor, and maybe a zombie who looks suspiciously like a certain style icon you’ve seen in a gallery.

The rulebook is a compact companion rather than a tyrant. Zombicide has always rewarded players who bring cooperative chaos to the table, and this edition leans into that with a few tasteful tweaks. The most glaring changes are designed to honor the guest artist without turning the game into a math lecture. A small set of new survivor abilities sits alongside refreshed mission cards, each mission themed around Edouard’s approach to art and combat. It’s the kind of design decision that makes you feel like you are playing a living painting rather than a rigid board game.

The miniatures deserve a paragraph all their own. Edouard’s involvement isn’t just cosmetic; his aesthetic seeps into the sculpts like color through a filter. The survivors wear outfits that feel recognizably arty yet practical in a zombie apocalypse. The zombies themselves wear expressions that say they know they’re about to lose and still insist on posing for a dramatic dramatic close-up. It’s childish in the best possible way: a reminder that even in the end of times, you can still enjoy a tiny figure with a big personality. If you are a painter, you’ll want to break out the magnifying lamp and a good set of brushes because these minis scream for hobby time.

In terms of box contents, the Special Guest Edouard edition presents a balanced pack. You get a standard Zombicide core with new art cards and maybe a couple of extra tokens to remind you that this is not a vanilla run. The production value remains high, the board conforms to the expected modular grid, and the tokens feel tactile enough to justify a scale of loot rather than mere counting. If you are a collector or a fan of Edouard’s work, this edition serves as a handsome artifact that you’ll want to display on your shelf when the zombies rest a moment.

Aesthetically, the Edouard Guiton touch is not a gimmick. The artist’s influence appears in the coloring choices, the shadows on the survivors, and the overall mood of the mission cards. This is not an assault of neon; it’s a confident use of color to guide your eye through the scene and tell you, without words, where the danger hides. If you’ve read enough zombie literature to know what good atmosphere feels like, this edition nails the mood. The game does not become a painting class; it remains a fast-paced, co-op experience with a vibe that suits Edouard’s signature style.

Gameplay wise, this edition sticks to Zombicide’s core skeleton: cooperative play against a dynamic horde, driven by player actions, with a dash of procedural tension from the tiles and spawns. What changes here are tweaks and additions that flavor the experience rather than overhaul it. The new survivor cards offer unique micro-goals and synergy with Edouard’s art cards, sometimes best summed up as “paint the town red and kill the zombies with style.” You’ll find it easier to set up and teach because the rules are familiar but the content feels fresh. The missions encourage exploring different parts of the map and using terrain to your advantage, which is a nice wink to the painterly approach—planning a composition before you commit to the brush, so to speak.

The rule explanations are crisp, the examples clear, and the learning curve remains friendly. If you’ve played Zombicide before, you’ll take to the new cards naturally; if you’re new, you’ll still be able to jump in without spending an hour poring over a glossary. That balance between accessibility and depth is one of Zombicide’s strengths, and the Special Guest edition preserves it while giving you more painterly personality to latch onto.

From a solo and campaign standpoint, the box can still be enjoyed by a lone gamer, though the feel is even better with a couple of friends or a tabletop full of rival painters who want to show off their brushwork while the zombies shuffle toward your barricade. The solo experience benefits from the extra mission variety, which helps to scratch that itch when you don’t have a full table to assemble. The campaign structure remains satisfying, with a light overarching arc that gives you a reason to go back to the same city block and attempt the next scenario with different survivor combinations.

The production values extend to the artwork and accessories. The inner tray is tidy, the punch-outs clean, and the color-coded components help you distinguish survivors from zombies at a glance. There is a tactile quality to the cards and boards that makes you want to flip more than you strictly need to; you’ll instinctively pick up the deck and admire the finish before you realize you’re about to engage in zombie mayhem. It’s a small joy, but in a game that often leans into doom and gloom, these little touches matter.

If you’re coming from a standard Zombicide or from other CMON titles, you’ll appreciate how Edouard Guiton’s edition respects the core design while letting his artistry breathe. It’s not a radical departure; it’s a deliberate refinement. The editor’s choice to keep the core mechanics intact while layering in thematic art and new content is a good reminder that you can innovate without breaking what makes the game comfortable and familiar. The result is a product that feels like a well-curated art book with an actual game inside rather than an art book that happens to have dice and plastic tokens stapled to it.
