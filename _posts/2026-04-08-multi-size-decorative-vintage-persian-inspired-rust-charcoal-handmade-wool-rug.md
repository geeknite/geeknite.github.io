Rule of thumb: create a “landing strip” for furniture legs. If a rug is too small, your furniture can all end up on rug corners, looking like a kid wearing shoes that are too big. If it’s too large, you risk a space feeling “rug-forward” rather than room-forward. A good setup keeps at least the front legs of all major furniture pieces resting on the rug, or at minimum, the front two legs if you’re working with a tighter footprint.

To visualize: in a standard 12x14 living room, a 8x10 or 9x12 rug often fits well under the main seating arrangement. For open floor plans, a large rug can unify multiple zones, like a shared quest area where the quests are the sofa, coffee table, and the occasional gaming console.

## Care and Durability
Durability is where the wool and the weave earn their keep. The rug’s construction should resist everyday wear—kids, pets, and the occasional spill—without showing heavy footprints. Here are practical care tips:

- Vacuum regularly with a suction-only vacuum, avoiding beater bars on high pile. The goal is to respect the pile’s integrity while removing dust that would otherwise accumulate in the knots.
- Address spills quickly. Blot with a clean cloth (don’t rub) and use a wool-safe cleaner if needed. Treating stains flatly helps prevent the colors from migrating, which is the opposite of what you want in a color story.
- Rotate every 6–12 months to ensure even wear and prevent one side from looking more seasoned than the other.
- Professional cleaning every 1–2 years depending on foot traffic keeps the fibers lively and the motifs crisp.

One practical benefit of wool is flame resistance and natural stain resistance, which buys you time to grab that book or your phone before panic ensues after a spill. Also, wool fibers can feel warmer underfoot in winter and still stay cool in summer due to natural breathability. In geek terms: wool is the L1 cache of rugs—fast, stable, and hard to replace once you’ve used it.
