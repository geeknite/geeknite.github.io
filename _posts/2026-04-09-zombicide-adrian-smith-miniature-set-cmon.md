## What's in the box

Opening the box feels like unwrapping an artifact from a long-forgotten cathedral cellar. You’re greeted with a pair of deluxe sculpts and a set of conversion-ready bits that invite you to reimagine your standard survivors and zombies as something a little more Rococo and a lot less generic.

- Padre Johnson mini: a stern, priestly figure who looks like he could bless your dice, curse your undead, and still find time to sermonize about the virtue of saving two cents on a resin cleanup. 
- Bones b8 mini: a skeletal survivor that has obviously taken a few notes from the crypt about texture and bone structure. Think skeletal reliefs with a hint of Hollywood undead chic.
- A handful of Gothic scenery bits: arches, tombs, and a couple of vampire-reaction stand-ins that look like they walked straight off a panel from a Smith painting.
- Character cards and ability tokens that slot neatly into the standard Zombicide setup, so you don’t have to redesign your entire rulebook to enjoy them.
- A small poster of the set’s concept art, which makes you feel like you’re buying a mini-collector’s edition rather than a board game component.

Inside you’ll find the usual CMON quality: crisp detail, clean mold lines, and a couple of kooky assets that remind you this is not your grandma’s Zombicide expansion. The packaging is sturdy enough to survive a raving gamer on a Friday night, and the miniatures are protected well enough that even if your gaming table is a disaster zone, these two figures look like they came straight from a gallery show.

## Design and sculpt language

Adrian Smith brings his characteristic blend of dark fantasy, Gothic architecture, and slightly sardonic humor to these minis. Padre Johnson wears clerical robes with a medley of roped belts and pious adamance, while Bones b8 is a hollow-eyed survivor whose skeleton is so well-defined you could swear you hear the click of old mechanical joints when you rotate the pose.

The sculpting leans into the painterly vibe rather than the smooth, glossy look you see on some modern plastics. Feathers, fabric folds, and bone textures are all emphasized with a slightly rough surface that actually benefits painting by giving depth without requiring an army of undercoats. It’s not a super-clean anime-smooth model; it’s more like a dark cathedral window with a splash of color behind it—dramatic, cinematic, and a touch rebellious.

From a distance, the set has a unified look: architectural ornamentation, Gothic arches, and a consistent palette that suggests a shared universe. Up close, though, each miniature has micro-details that reward careful painting—tiny cross-shaped engravings on Padre Johnson’s vestments, or the ragged cloth beneath Bones b8’s torque of bones, which gives you a narrative cue about where this hero might have come from.

If you’re curious about how the set compares to other CMON releases, this feels more painter-friendly than some of their more “photo realistic” lines. It’s not a break with their standard; it’s a refinement—more character-led, less stock zombie interference, which is a refreshing change for table presence and display value.

## The minis: Padre Johnson and Bones b8

Padre Johnson is the gravity of the set. He feels like someone who would stop a horde with a blessing and then medal-waltz into a cruciform stance just to remind you who’s boss. His robe creases, the rosary beads catch a little light, and his face is resolutely calm despite the world turning into a plywood apocalypse around him. In a party of survivors, Padre Johnson reads as the moral compass with a backstory that would make a good sidequest in a campaign; you can almost hear him reciting a verse as you steady your spray bottle of varnish.

Bones b8, on the other hand, is the character that feels like it belongs in a midnight arena: a skeleton with a stubborn look, a protective stance, and just enough tatter to suggest a long life of skirmishes and improbable escapes. The bone texture is well defined, but not overly skeletal to the point of being fragile; it’s robust enough to survive a couple of paint layers and a table edge collision or two. If you’re into converting zombie lookalikes, Bones b8 offers a chunk of biological geometry that can be repurposed into other undead characters with minimal effort.

Both minis scale well with a standard Zombicide board. They don’t tower over your other survivors, nor do they disappear into the skyline behind a zombie apocalypse. They sit nicely at table height, which is important if you’re playing a long campaign and want your minis to stay legible for your players after the fourth round of chaos tokens.

## Material quality and assembly

CMON’s plastics for Zombicide have gotten better over the years, and this set is no exception. The plastic density is on point for handling and painting. You won’t feel like you’re assembling fragile candy canes, but you’ll still want to wash and sprue-off any leftover flash before priming. A couple of tiny flash bits exist in recessed crevices; a careful sharp blade and a steadier hand will do the trick.

As for assembly, Padre Johnson is straightforward: base, robe, staff, and head. Bones b8 follows a similar path, with a couple of small bone pieces that need to align with a torso frame. The connection points are clean and relatively forgiving; you can tweak pose between a couple of options without much drama. If you’re one of those players who enjoys swapping heads, there’s enough pin-joins to allow some passive customization without turning your hobby into a full measurement ritual.

In terms of durability, the paint can adhere well with standard basing materials and primer. If you’re a seasoned painter, you’ll probably enjoy the slight texture the surface provides, which helps you achieve dry brushing or subtle glaze work without fighting with a super-smooth surface that begs for a heavy-handed brushstroke.

### Painting and display tips

- Prime in thin coats, preferably with a neutral gray or black to bring out the detail of the folds and bone textures.
- For Padre Johnson, build a gentle blue-gray or slate color in the robe and work a warm ochre into the edges of the trim to mimic candlelight in a chapel.
- For Bones b8, use a bone base with a cool gray wash to bring out cracks and hollows; then dry brush with a slightly lighter bone color to accent the raised edges.
- Use a gloss varnish on the bone pieces if you want that fresh, almost polished bone look, or a satin/matte finish to keep it grounded and less museum-like.
- Base with a modular look: cobblestones, eroded tiles, or a small tombstone piece can harmonize with the Gothic theme and give your display a storytelling base.

## Display and care

Display is where these minis shine. Place Padre Johnson and Bones b8 on a shelf or display case that’s not directly sunlight. The painted versions look fantastic under controlled lighting, and the subtle highlights will draw compliments from friends who claim to be “just here for the loot.” If display isn’t your thing, you can still keep them on the table as a constant reminder that you once summoned an undead priest to fight a horde of digital text messages.

Care-wise, it’s the usual miniatures protocol: wash, prime, paint, seal, enjoy. Avoid aggressive handling, especially with the more delicate robes and bone fragments in Bones b8. If you have pets that view miniatures as chew toys, consider a display case or a higher shelf, since the undead sometimes attract curious feline attention.
