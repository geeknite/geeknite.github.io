Introduction

If you have ever stared into the glossy gloss of a CMON box and thought this could only get weirder in the best possible way, congratulations: you are exactly the target audience for Zombicide Special Guest Miguel Coimbra Expansion. This is the kind of release that makes your shelf look origin-story-snobbish, like a comic book store main character who insists on a dramatic pose every time you lift the lid. Miguel Coimbra, a master of ominous arcs and characterful silhouettes, lends his signature vibe to two fresh survivors and a handful of zombified chaos that just screams mood.

Note, as with any CMON drop that winks at a creator, this is a celebration as much as it is a game add-on. You are buying not just a pile of plastic bits and card stock, but a slice of Miguel’s artistic brain on your table—slightly sharper, more neon, and with a healthy dose of existential dread for your next midnight skirmish.

The Box and What Is Inside

The box feels like a Zeppelin of tabletop terror: sturdy, a little heavier than you expect, and wrapped in a foil accent that catches the light like a menacing eye in a cathedral window. Inside, you’ll find two survivors, a handful of weapon and zombie cards, mission cards, a couple of new enablers, and a set of punchy tokens that make you believe you can actually track a zombie’s mood ring with a calculator—okay, not literally, but you get the vibe.

This expansion leans hard into the art style Coimbra is known for: elongated shadows, stark silhouettes, and a color palette that hits you with contrast in ways that make even a tired player sit up and go, wow, that is crisp. The cards carry that same feeling—minimal text, maximum character—so you can quickly grasp what your new survivor is about without needing a PhD in flavor text to decode the mechanics.

Design and Theme

Miguel Coimbra is a name that evokes cinematic shadows, tangled alleyways, and that moment when you realize you have been staring at a half-lit panel long enough to fill a novella. The expansion foregrounds his aesthetics in both the art and the flavor of two new survivors. You’ll notice a few recurring motifs: stark negative space, stylized edges that make zombies look almost like living silhouettes from a noir poster, and items that glow with a trace of danger. It’s not just about “new stuff on the table”; it’s about a new tone, a more cinematic mood, and a sense that your encounters with the undead are now framed like a comic panel where everything is on the edge of a punchline you don’t quite want to hear.

Two Survivors, Two Paths

This expansion introduces two survivors who feel distinct, thematic, and surprisingly practical on the table. In Zombicide fashion, their abilities aren’t just numbers; they’re little storytelling devices that push you to improvise and coordinate. Survivor A is the fast and nimble type — think skittering through tight corners, trading a normal move for a burst of speed that lets you slip into a position the board’s geometry clearly did not expect. Survivor B is the sturdy option: more cautious, with better survivability and gadget-like tools that feel almost cinematic when you pull off a clutch play that buys your crew a little more breathing room. Together they create a dynamic duo who can cover each other’s blind spots, which is exactly the kind of synergy that keeps a campaign feeling fresh rather than a drag through a hallway of samey combat.

While we are reaping the vibe, let’s break down the gameplay implications: the new survivors come with specific starting gear and a couple of unique passives that encourage you to split tasks—one to lock down a corridor while the other rails the push for a kill on a tougher zombie. In practice, this means you will be tempted to craft little micro-strategies, which is the heartbeat of Zombicide co-op play: you feel smart, the board feels dangerous, and the zombies feel just a half-step smarter than your average thrust-and-swing routine.

Gameplay Impressions

If you are a veteran of Zombicide, you know the drill: you balance risk versus the inevitable wave of walkers, you juggle the draw deck, and you hope your rolls don’t betray you at the wrong moment. What this expansion adds is a couple of interesting new levers to pull without tipping the game into chaos. The two survivors’ abilities interact with the core rules in a way that feels additive rather than compensatory—they don’t overshadow the base game; they complement it with new paths through the map and new decisions at key moments.

Co-op Dynamics

In a typical two-to-four player run, these survivors shine when your party aims to split tasks: one charters a route for a grab-and-go objective while the other defends against a surge. This leads to a few satisfying turn orders where you feel you pulled off a tiny orchestration, a moment of tabletop jazz where your crew’s moves align with the zombie cadence in a way that makes your heart race slightly more than it should for a board game. The kit doesn’t demand specialized roles beyond the survivors; instead it nudges you toward micro-coordination without requiring you to reprogram your brain for a different game system. If you enjoyed the pacing of late-stage missions in base Zombicide, you’ll likely feel similarities here, with an extra shot of noir precision and artistic flair.

Component Quality

CMON’s production quality remains consistent with their other releases: solid cardboard stock, glossy cards with readable typography, and minis that paint well with a reasonable level of detail for the size. The new weapons and zombie minis match the aesthetic, and the tokens are clear enough to prevent any misread during a heated moment. The physical weight of the box is a pleasant reminder that you are getting a significant chunk of play into the price of admission, and the sealed status adds that extra stamp of desirability for collectors who like to brag about minty-fresh acquisitions.

Thematic Flavor and Humor

A Zombicide drop is rarely shy about its sense of humor, and this expansion wears that pride like a stylish scarf. You’ll find little nods to Miguel’s world: silhouettes that look like they stepped out of a dark poster, creepy silhouettes that somehow feel 3D on the card art, and a few flavor line prompts that could have been dialogue in a low-budget horror comedy. The jokes, when they land, feel earned—precisely because the art and flavor work in tandem to create a mood rather than a one-liner gag that dissolves after a single refresh of the memory. If you enjoy a campaign with a noir-tinged atmosphere and a touch of art house sensibility, this expansion will scratch that itch and then some.

How It Fits into the Zombicide Ecosystem

This isn’t a stand-alone experience; it is designed to slot into your existing Zombicide lineup with minimal drama. If you already own other expansions, you’ll appreciate how this release threads into the fabric: a couple of new cards that weave into the mission deck, new survivals that synergize with familiar gear, and a few new objective triggers that feel like living within a shared universe rather than a separate module. For players who treat Zombicide like a living tabletop city, this expansion adds a new district to explore with a distinct architectural style and a recognizable resident artist’s voice guiding your steps.

Value, Sealed Status, and Collectibility

Sealed status has its own conversation in the hobby space. If you love the thrill of unsealing a brand-new batch of plastic and cards, this expansion offers that moment with a bit more ceremony thanks to Coimbra’s brand. The two survivors themselves become talking points at your gaming table, not merely because of their abilities but because of the art direction that makes them feel alive the moment you pop the lid. In terms of value, CMON typically prices these at a premium for collectors, but if you are in the mood for a more cinematic Zombicide experience with a creator’s signature style, the extra premium can feel warranted. It’s not a must-have for casual players, but it is a meaningful addition for those who want to curate a more vivid, gallery-like board game shelf.

Setup, Play Time, and Replayability

The setup is typical CMON: open, sort, and assemble. If you are a veteran, you’ll be in and out in a handful of minutes; if you are a newer player, the clear art and compact card layout help rule explanations feel approachable rather than intimidating. Play time will align with your normal Zombicide cadence—roughly an hour per mission for standard maps, with longer sessions for more ambitious sweeps. Replayability, as always, comes from the mission variety and the different ways the two survivors can be used in tandem. The art and mood provide atmospheric reasons to replay the same map with different tactics, adding a fresh coat of noir to each run.

Comparisons to Other Expansions

If you own a couple of other Zombicide expansions, you will look at this one as a stylistic cousin with a sharper edge. The two survivors here are balanced to complement a wide variety of team compositions, unlike some min-max heavy packs that can feel gimmicky in group play. In terms of content density, this expansion lands in the sweet spot: enough content to justify its capsule, but not so much that you need to rebuild your entire playstyle. For players who enjoy the storytelling cadence of campaigns, you’ll appreciate how this release nudges you toward mood-driven missions as much as mechanical optimization.
