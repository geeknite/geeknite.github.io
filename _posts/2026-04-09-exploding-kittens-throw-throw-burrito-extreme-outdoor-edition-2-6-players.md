## How to play outdoors
Throw Throw Burrito Extreme Outdoor Edition keeps the core rhythm of the original: draw a card, claim a pair, and dodge if you’re unlucky enough to be in the crossfire when the burritos soar. Outdoors adds a few practical twists:
- A wider real estate means more space to maneuver, which raises the skill ceiling but also the potential for spectacular fails. Your lawn becomes a battlefield where the only rule is: never stare directly into the sun while chasing a burrito.
- Wind direction matters. On a breezy day, a burrito can travel longer arcs and land in the most dramatic fashion, preferably near someone who swears they aren’t competitive… until the burrito hits them in the thigh.
- Boundaries are a must. Mark safe zones and keep a buffer around the grill, pool, and the sprinkler head that seems to love stealing the show.
- The pace changes. In an indoor setting you can control the tempo with table space; outdoors, the weather, terrain, and audience laughter can accelerate or decelerate the game with gusto.

### Setup
1) Clear a rectangular play area, ideally on grass, with a few safe zones. 2) Shuffle the deck, place it face down, and designate a player to be the first deck-operator. 3) Each player takes a burrito and positions themselves at a reasonable distance from the center. 4) The first player draws a card and follows the instruction. 5) Score by forming pairs. Every mismatch invites chaos, in the form of a loud, joyous, mildly aggressive burrito toss.

### Card actions and burrito toss rules
The cards determine whether you’ll draw more cards, steal pairs from others, or trigger special effects. The burritos are the real wildcards; they’re not just projectiles—they are tokens of chaos that encourage improvisation. The rules emphasize safety, but the spirit demands performance. A well-timed burrito toss can shift the entire round, turning a quiet card-draw into a mini-sporting event complete with a chorus of “woo-hoos” and a few mock-dramatic gasps.

### Scoring and endgame
Each successful pair earns you points. The variant’s extension in the outdoor edition can introduce bonus points for executing stylish hits or saving a partner from a dodged burrito by deflecting it with the deck, which is technically possible but should be treated as a last-resort showpiece. The game ends when a player crosses the target score, which is adjustable based on the size of your group and the time you’re willing to sacrifice to outdoor chaos. The ending is always a spectacle: players attempting to congratulate the victor while trying not to slip on grass that just learned New Bond villain level physics.

## Outdoor edition design and safety
The design team deserves credit for balancing whimsy with practicality. The burritos are soft, the cards are durable, and the rulebook includes a dedicated safety section that’s surprisingly thorough for a game about flight paths and giggles. Outdoor play requires additional safety considerations:
- Eye protection is recommended, particularly on bright days when glare can obscure your burrito arc.
- Clear the area of fragile decor and any breakables. The last thing you want is a ceramic planter becoming a casualty of card-induced chaos.
- Keep pets and small children away from high-velocity throws. The goal is laughter, not emergency vet humor.
- Hydration breaks are not optional. In the sun, you’ll lose more energy than your burritos lose momentum.

Weather can either help or hinder your strategy. A light breeze makes for dramatic, almost cinematic arcs; a gusty afternoon turns the game into a wind-driven carnival ride. It’s all part of the charm, and you’ll remember these moments with the same fond confusion you reserve for the weirdest phase of a long-scale RPG campaign.

## Strategy and tips for victory
There’s a logic to chaos, and chaos has a few rules:
- Control the pace. If you’re in the lead, don’t become a target by pushing too hard. Let others babysit the burritos while you position to capitalize on a misthrow.
- Use physics to your advantage. The wind direction becomes a compass; aim where the burrito will travel, not where you want it to land. The physics are forgiving enough that even bad throws are entertaining—what matters is theater, not precision.
- Read your tablemates. People underestimate the social dynamics here. You’ll learn who’s genuinely quick on their feet and who clutches the closest chair in fear. Use that to your advantage when deciding who is next on the deck.
- Share the fun. If you’re winning too easily, invite a milder form of chaos, like cheering on a “friendly” stitch-up or orchestrating a dramatic band of burritos that lands on a single player’s feet.

House rules can boost the fun:
- The “Burrito Rescue” rule: if a player saves another from a burrito by stepping into the arc, they both gain a tiny bonus point and a temporary shield hat gesture (which can be as simple as tapping the head with a foam burrito as a theatrical flourish).
- The “Sun’s Eye” rule: when the sun is directly in someone’s eyes, they gain a 2-point bonus when resolving a pair—because the glare is practically a game mechanic here.
- The “Wind Gambit” variant: when you declare a turn, the group collectively determines the wind’s direction with a triumphant spin of a yard towel. If the direction is correct, the player who attempted the move gets a bonus; if wrong, chaos multiplies for everyone else.

## Getting the most from your outdoor setup
To maximize the experience, consider a few practical tweaks:
- Create a “seasoning zone” with props like extra hats, sunglasses, and a few foam shields that players can use to stage dramatic entrances. The more ritual you add, the more entertainment you get from otherwise simple card draws.
- Bring a small ground rug or mat to mark the “combat zone.” It helps with safety while keeping the chaos visually readable for photos and social media clips. You’ll want to capture the moment where a burrito nearly hits a camera and instead lands gracefully on a couch cushion.
- Use a dedicated referee for the final round. They can keep track of points, call fouls (yes, there are minor fouls in this sport), and maintain safe distances while still letting the group feel the adrenaline of a close game.
- If you’ve got kids around, trim the rules to emphasize fun and safety. The beauty of a backyard tournament is that you can scale the intensity to fit your audience without losing the joyous energy.
