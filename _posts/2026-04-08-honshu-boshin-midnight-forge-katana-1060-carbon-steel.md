## The Blade: 1060 Carbon Steel and the Midnight Myth
### What is 1060 Carbon Steel?
1060 carbon steel sits in the sweet spot of “affordable and sturdy.” It’s a plain-carbon steel, not a fancy alloy, which means it’s easier to heat treat and tends to be more forgiving than harder steels. For swords, 1060 is a common choice for beginners and hobby smiths because it’s more likely to tolerate imperfect heat treatment than higher-end steels and, importantly, it sharpens reasonably well without turning into a razor that would require a surgical license to wield. That said, 1060 is not an edge-retention champion; it’s a workhorse steel that’ll hold up to practice and casual cutting with appropriate maintenance. If you’re buying this katana with the intention of cutting through a stack of pine or old tatami mats with the reckless enthusiasm of a dragon, the 1060 will likely surprise you with its resilience—but don’t expect it to behave like a top-tier forged blade.

### Bevel, Edge, and Cutting Geometry
Geometry matters. The Midnight Forge’s edge geometry is designed to be user-friendly: a robust edge that doesn’t demand perfection from your grip or stance. The single bevel on most katana blades is what gives you that clean, crisp cut in the hands of someone who knows how to commit to the motion. If the product label markets this as double-edged, we’re obligated to note that such phrasing typically refers to the blade’s edge profile along its curvature, or simply a flashy marketing line. Either way, you’re not likely to be hitting the same edge twice in a single stroke—the venerable curvature of a katana ensures that you’ll engage the edge at a shallow angle during the initial slice, allowing you to feel the blade bite while preserving a safe, manageable edge. For beginners, this means easier learning and fewer “oops” moments as you learn to control your swing.

## Maintenance: Keeping the Edge and the Moment Alive
Maintenance is not glamorous, but it is essential. With a 1060 steel blade, your routine should be simple and repeatable:
- Wipe after use to remove moisture and fingerprints. 
- Lightly oil the blade’s surface to deter rust and to prolong the edge’s life.
- Check the habaki and menuki for any loosening, and re-tighten or adjust as needed.
- Avoid soaking in water or leaving the blade submerged for long durations; it’s not a submarine, despite what your fantasy channel might tell you.
- Sharpening: start with a coarse stone if the edge has become noticeably dull, then move to finer grits, finishing with a light strop. If you’re new to sharpening, seek a local class or a reputable instructor—there’s nothing cooler than learning a skill that makes you feel like a space-ninja and not just a guy who bought a sword on a whim.
