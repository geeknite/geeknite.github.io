It is a precision tool designed to apply mid sized layers to models with a steady hand and a patient spirit. The idea behind a layer brush is simple: deposit color in such a way that the transitions between shades are smooth, subtle, and believable. The Medium Layer Brush sits between the small and large variants in the Layer family, offering a balance of control and coverage that many hobbyists crave when tackling capes, leather, metallics with a gentle highlight, or skin tones without going full overcooked roast beef. In practical terms, it means less time scrubbing out mistakes and more time watching your miniature look like it actually has depth instead of merely existing in a single color shell.

This brush uses a synthetic sable-like hair blend typical of modern Citadel tools. ## Performance in Practice

### Flow, Load, and Control

Flow is the most mysterious of the brush gods. It is the thing that decides whether your mid tones look like natural shading or an accidental neon disaster. The Medium Layer handles flow nicely when loaded with a modest amount of paint—the goal is to avoid pooling at seams or dragging in a texture that shouldn’t be there. The bristles hold enough paint to cover a reasonable area without requiring a constant reload. When you hold the brush at a shallow angle and glide across the surface, the stroke remains controlled, with a gentle edge that helps bring out a sense of volume without overwhelming the surrounding details.

Transparency and layering go hand in hand. The idea behind a medium layer is to push mid tones into the recesses and then pull the highlight back in the next layer. This brush makes that process a little easier because it refuses to flood the surface with paint. Instead, it gives you a steady, even distribution that reduces the risk of uneven gaps forming in feathering areas or the hairlines of a cloak.

### Basecoat vs Layer vs Edge

A common workflow is to lay down the basecoat with a broader brush, then switch to a medium layer for mid-tones and transitions, and finally use an edge brush for crisp highlights. The Medium Layer Brush fits nicely into the second slot. It’s not too big to dabble in broad areas, but it’s not so small that you’re wrestling with precision pressure to avoid altitude changes on tiny rivets. In practice, I used it on a cloak with a couple of mid-tone steps, and the brush delivered a soft but noticeable gradation that looked consistent across several panels.

One caveat: if you’re aiming for ultra-clean edge highlights on ships or armor plates, you may want to switch to a dedicated edge brush for the final crisp lines. The Medium Layer is better thought of as the color gradient advocate, the guy who smooths the ocean of color before you lay down the bright lighthouse beacon of a highlight. It shines in blended transitions rather than sharp, crisp separations.

### Volume vs Coverage

The coverage of the Medium Layer is respectable. It doesn’t go full coverage across a large surface as a large basecoat brush would, but that’s part of the design philosophy. You can build up color gradually to achieve a natural shading effect rather than forcing an opaque layer that hides texture. If you work on leather or fabric, you’ll appreciate how you can coax a velvety finish without losing the underlying detail. The brush’s tip is pointed enough to drop more precise lines where needed, but broad enough to push color along the area you intend to shade. It’s a sweet spot that many brushmasters crave when they want both control and efficiency.

## Care and Longevity

In the world of miniature painting, brush care is a relationship. A brush that is treated like a disposable tool will fail you, while a brush that is respected with proper cleaning and shaping will keep you company for a long campaign. The Citadel Medium Layer Brush is no exception. Cleaning after a session with water and a gentle soap or a dedicated brush cleaner keeps bristles supple and avoids that dreaded “stiff-wire” feeling after a few uses. If you accidentally leave it loaded with a stubborn pigment overnight, a careful soak in warm water and a rinse with mild dish soap will often revive it without harming the bristles.

Storage matters too. I recommend storing your brushes with the bristles pointing up or horizontally in a way that prevents the ferrule from collecting water. A dry, cool environment helps preserve the brush’s snap. If you are a serial tremor spiller during the weekend gaming sessions, consider a brush guard to keep the bristle shape intact between uses.

## Practical Painting Scenarios

Here are a few real-world scenarios where the Medium Layer shines and where you might want to pick a different brush:

- Cloaks and Cloth: The Medium Layer is excellent for mid-tone transitions on cloaks. You can tip the brush toward the surface at a shallow angle and pull color into the recesses, creating a sense of depth without heavy-handed shading.
- Leather and Furs: For leather components such as belts or straps, use the Medium Layer to paint mid-tones and then follow with a darker shade in the creases. The soft edge helps blend into the highlights without creating harsh seams.
- Skin Tones: When working skin tones, the Medium Layer can be used to build subtle shading. You can apply a veil of mid-tone color over the base and use a lighter wash or highlight to finish. The brush’s control helps you avoid overloading the surface with pigment, which leads to a more natural skin gradient.
- Armor: On armor panels, the Medium Layer can bridge the gap between base and highlight. It creates a smoother transition across the surface, which is particularly satisfying on curved shapes.

If you are not sure where to start, a simple workflow is to lay down a base color, apply a mid-tone with the Medium Layer, then enrich the mid-tone with a slightly darker shade in the recessed areas, and finish with a light edge or highlight using a smaller brush. It’s a practical sequence that yields solid results without needing a PhD in painting theory.

## Careful Tips and Tricks

- Load sparingly: It is tempting to flood the brush with paint. Resist this urge. The Mid Layer does its best work when you have a modest paint load. You can always add more, but you cannot erase a heavy hand.
- Use thin paints: If your paint is too thick, you will get a clog and an unwanted texture. Thin your paints to a consistency that is almost water-like, but not quite. You want the paint to flow, not glop.
- Gentle strokes: Let gravity do the heavy lifting. Short, confident strokes perform better than long, jittery sweeps that look like a homework assignment gone wrong.
- Clean between colors: Keep your palette fresh, especially when working with light colors over dark. A quick wipe on a clean palette surface helps prevent muddy transitions.
- Maintenance matters: After painting, rinse the brush, reshape the bristles gently, and let it dry flat to maintain the tip shape. If you neglect maintenance, the brush will betray you in the middle of a highlight pass.
