## How CODENAMES Plays: The Rules in Practice
At its core, CODENAMES is a word association engine. There are two teams: red and blue. Each round has a designated Spymaster for each team who can see a key card indicating which words on the board belong to their team, which belong to the other team, which are neutral, and which word is the lone assassin you must avoid at all costs. The Spymaster gives a one-word clue plus a number indicating how many words on the grid relate to that clue. For example, a clue like Ocean 3 might point your team toward three ocean-related words in the grid.

The teammates then discuss and interpret the clue, using their shared lexicon to decide which words to guess. They can keep guessing up to the number given by the Spymaster, plus one extra guess if they’re feeling confident. This is where the game’s flavor comes from: smart clues reduce the number of guesses needed; risky clues can backfire dramatically if you pick someone else’s word or, worse, the assassin word.

If you guess a word that belongs to your own team, you continue; if you guess a neutral word, your turn ends but you stay in the game; if you hit an enemy word, the turn ends and the other team will have a chance to guess; and if you hit the assassin, the game ends immediately with the deadliest of consequences: your team loses the round instantly. The tension is real, and it’s mostly mental: no blood, no dramatic car chases, just the crisp click of a wooden tile as you reveal the wrong word.

### Setup, Round, and Turn Structure (H3)
- Setup: 25 word cards laid out; a key card that only Spymasters can see; team colors chosen.
- Clue phase: Spymaster gives a one-word clue and a number.
- Guess phase: Teammates discuss and reveal up to that number of words; you can opt to stop early.
- End of turn: If you hit a non-matching word or an opponent word, the turn ends; the other team gets to go.
- Win condition: Either team successfully identifies all their words on the grid before the assassin is revealed and before the other team completes theirs.

The rhythm of play is what makes CODENAMES memorable. You’ll have rounds that feel like a glorious miscommunication between a pair of star-crossed linguists, and rounds that feel like a strategic chess match with words as pawns. The whole experience is balanced by the Spymaster’s ability to craft clues that unlock multiple words at once while steering clear of trap words that will blow up your squad’s cover. This balance between risk and reward is what makes CODENAMES feel fair even when your team makes the kind of poor guess that would embarrass a middle school spelling bee champion.

## Strategy: How to Approach CODENAMES (H2)
If you want to win more often than your friends, you’ll need a blend of linguistic intuition, team chemistry, and risk management. Here are some practical strategies you can import to your next session.

### For Spymasters (H3)
- Prioritize multi-word connections: clues that can plausibly link three or more target words are golden. A single clue that covers two or three words will usually swing a round in your favor, though you must be careful with potential crossovers into your opponent’s territory.
- Map your grid: before giving a clue, quickly visualize which words are yours and which could be dangerous. A good Spymaster makes mental notes about which words to avoid and which words will be proven as long-tail hits.
- Manage risk with a plan B: don’t lock yourself into a single motif. If you see a cluster of potential words but all carry obvious crossovers, consider a safer clue that still gains momentum. You can always escalate in the next turn.
- The assassin is not a taboo word; treat it as a real risk that you are projecting onto the team. Your aim is to avoid the trap without sacrificing your offensive tempo.

### For Teammates (H3)
- Listen for the intent, not just the words: the Spymaster’s clue is the starting gun; your interpretation is the propulsion system. You and your squad should discuss possible word-linkages and align on a consensus before guessing.
- Communicate loud enough to capture the vibe but precise enough to avoid chaos. A well-timed chorus of reasoning beats a single loud guesser every time.
- Don’t overthink the obvious: players regularly overcomplicate clues that are actually meant to hit several words in a straightforward way. The joy of CODENAMES is often in the surprising, almost banal connections.
