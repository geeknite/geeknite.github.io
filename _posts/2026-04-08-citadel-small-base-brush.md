## What is the Citadel Small Base Brush?
The Citadel Small Base Brush is a compact, pointy-tipped tool designed for base layers and tight perimeter work on miniature bases. It’s marketed as a dedicated base brush that can lay down even coats on base textures—gravel, rubble, rusted metal, tufts, and ground cover—without smearing paint across the model’s feet. The goal is simple: a tiny head, a mild taper, and a handle that won’t threaten to derail your entire painting session.

From a quick glance, the brush signals “precision, not speed” with a compact head, a fine tip that holds its shape, and a short handle that reduces wrist fatigue. Citadel’s brushes are color-coded and tend to have stiffer bristles than budget options, which many hobbyists appreciate for the sense of control. The Small Base sits between the larger base brushes and the tiny dry brushes in Citadel’s lineup. It’s not meant for lay-in highlights or heavy dry-brushing; it’s meant for crisp base layers and careful application.

If you’re curious about what base brushes exist beyond Citadel, you’ll often see comparisons with Army Painter’s base brushes and sable-hair options from other brands. The goal is to decide what your workflow looks like: synthetic bristles that hold a lot of paint or a more responsive bristle that can slip into crevices without pushing paint into the wrong places. The Small Base Brush claims to be the correct first step to create depth and texture that reads at tabletop distance.

## Build quality and design
### Bristle material and feel
The Small Base Brush uses a synthetic mix common to Citadel’s line. The bristles come out a bit stiff on first use, which ironically helps with control when painting textured bases. The stiffness is functional: you can apply base coats with a light touch and still maintain precision in cracks and crevices. With time and washing, some hobbyists report bristles softening, especially if you get a little aggressive with solvent washes. The advice remains: treat it well, and it will treat you with the loyalty of a well-trained squad.

### Ferrule and handle
The brush rides on a small, sturdy ferrule that feels solid enough for regular basing sessions. The grip is intentionally compact to reduce fatigue when you’re hunched over a dio for longer periods. Ergonomically, it’s a win: not the plushest thing you’ll own, but it gets the job done without requiring you to visit a trapeze artist gym afterwards.

### Size and reach
As advertised, it’s a compact tool. The head is small enough to reach around tiny base details but not so small that it’s a one-trick pony. It’s ideal for rubble, cracks, soil lacunae, and the rim edges where you don’t want paint to drift onto the model. If you’re painting large flat sections, you’ll want a bigger brush for speed, but for precise work, this brush is a comforting ally.

## Performance on bases
### Base coats that behave
The primary test for any base brush is whether it lays down a clean, even coat over varied textures. The Small Base Brush is surprisingly capable here. It holds enough paint to cover textured terrain without flooding, and the fine tip allows you to avoid clumps and paint puddling in texture cracks. If you’re working with textures such as sand, gravel, or tiny pebbles, you can achieve an even coat without ending up with a mottled, muddy finish.

Practical tip: avoid overloading the brush. It’s not a glide-through-everything tool; it’s a precise instrument. A light touch helps preserve texture and keeps paint from pooling in crevices or along the base rim. The paint consistency matters too: aim for something similar to a well-made coffee—neither too thick nor too thin.

### Reaching tricky edges
The small head makes it easier to get around jagged rocks or placed textures where you want an even base tone without bleeding onto the miniature. The point allows you to guide paint along crevices while keeping safe distance from the model’s fantabulous feet and knees.

### Blending and consistency
It’s not designed as a blender brush. For smooth gradients, you’ll want a softer tip with more bounce. That said, you can still coax subtle transitions along the rim by applying a light wash and then pulling it toward the edges with the same brush. It won’t replace a dedicated blending brush, but it has surprising versatility for a brush of its size.

### Textures and official textures
Tip: this brush plays nicely with base textures like sand, grit, cork, and sculpted basing materials. It’s able to push paint into crevices without hiding detail, which is critical for achieving a grounded look.

## Bristle quality and longevity
### Early-life behavior
First uses reveal bristles that hold shape well. It won’t shed like a waterfall of fur, but occasional stray bristles after aggressive washing happen. The method to prevent fraying: wash with warm soapy water, reshape while damp, and air-dry with bristles down.

### Long-term durability
Durability is decent. If you’re painting daily and scrubbing hard, you might see bristle wear. Casual painters who care for the brush and store it properly should enjoy months of use. The key is avoiding harsh solvents and storing the brush upright.

## Care and maintenance
### Cleaning routine
Rinse in lukewarm water after use until it runs clear. Use mild soap if pigments stain bristles (earthy tones can cling). Gently work bristles with fingers to avoid tangling. Do not soak or leave water in the ferrule; dry thoroughly.

### Storage tips
Keep in a clean, dry place away from dust. A brush holder that keeps bristles in a natural shape is ideal. Traveling? A cap helps protect the head, but many prefer quick access over protection.

### Replacement and maintenance mindset
If bristles shed or head loses shape after many uses, consider replacing or rotating in another brush. It’s not fragile; it just has a shelf life. The price point is reasonable, making it easier to rotate when you want a different feel.

## Tips and techniques for using the Small Base Brush
- Start with the right paint consistency: a base coat that’s a touch thinner helps the bristles push into texture cracks.
- Use a light touch: the bristles should do the work, not your palm.
- Angle the brush to reach edges: a shallow angle helps paint into nooks without splashing onto the figure.
- Work in stages: apply and verify, don’t overwork a single pass; texture loves a little breathing room.
- Combine with washes or dry brushing: to add depth, a restrained wash in crevices then a careful highlight around rim can yield a layered look.
- Clean promptly: pigments clinging to bristles will stain; clean early to preserve life.

## Real-world basing workflow: a step-by-step guide
Let’s walk through a plausible basing scenario and show how the Small Base Brush can be your faithful sidekick.

Step 1: Plan the base narrative
Before you pick up paint, decide what the base will say. A ruined city street? An alien seabed? A lunar quarry? The base’s texture should support the miniature’s story.

Step 2: Prime and pre-texture
Prime the base, then lay down a textured foundation using basing composes like sand, grit, or cork pieces. This is where the Small Base Brush will help you hit tight corners around rubble.

Step 3: Base color with micro-coverage
Dip the brush into the base color and apply using a light touch. Use short strokes that feather into texture. The aim is an even base layer that reads well in the paint’s first pass.

Step 4: Edge control and rim painting
Use the Small Base Brush to carefully paint the rim of the base near the model’s feet. The smaller head helps you keep the color away from the model.

Step 5: Wash and shade
A restrained wash can bring out cracks in the terrain. Use the brush for placement of the wash in crevices, then pull the color lightly outward to simulate shadows.

Step 6: Dry-in highlights
If you want to highlight edges, a light dry brush can be done after the base coat, but avoid heavy dry-brushing that would erase texture.

Step 7: Final adjustments
Touch-ups and final crisp lines around the base edge will anchor the texture.

Step 8: Optional texture layering
If your base includes multiple textures (sand, rock, and grit), switch to a slightly stiffer, smaller brush for the edges between textures to preserve crisp boundaries.

Step 9: Sealing and protection
A light matte seal after basing helps lock the texture and color in place without washing out the micro-details you spent time on. Keep your Small Base handy for touch-ups after sealing if needed.
