## Build, Assembly, and Table Presence
The actual assembly work depends on your preferred level of customization. The six dual packs often involve multiple parts per figure, including weapons, scarves, cloaks, and small accessory pieces like pouches or weapon belts. Here are a few practical notes for builders:

- Tools: A decent hobby knife, a set of nippers, and a pinch of patience will get you through the majority of the work. A fine-tipped hobby knife is your friend for cleaning mold lines.
- Glue vs. plastic cement: If the minis are resin, you’ll want CA glue or super glue with a bit of tackiness. If they’re hard plastic, standard plastic cement or super glue with a fast-dry formula works well. Always test fit first; it helps avoid last-minute re-pinning when you realize that the cape attaches at a weird angle.
- Assembly time: Plan for several sessions. The first session usually covers the base assembly, the second for weapons and accessories, and the third for priming and painting base coats. If you’re a speed painter, you can push through in a single weekend, but don’t skip the priming ritual—this is the difference between “distinct features pop” and “muddy details on a brown swamp.”

When on the table, these minis have strong silhouettes and dynamic poses that read well even from across the room. They pop under standard gaming lighting and with a bit of varnish they’re not just pretty; they’re durable against the inevitable table nudge that happens during a heated tactical decision.
