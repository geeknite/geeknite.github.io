## How complete is complete? A caveat about open-box realities

The term Open Box implies some variables:
- Codes and digital bonuses: if present, often expired or redeemed by the original owner. Don’t count on them unless you’re buying from a seller who explicitly guarantees that all digital content remains unclaimed. If codes exist, test them early and be prepared for surprises.
- Accessories: posters, art books, and soundtracks can be tucked away; if you’re missing one, you’re looking at a discount that may not actually save you money in time.
- Discs: surface scratches can range from cosmetic to problematic. If you plan to rely on these discs for actual gameplay (instead of digital downloads), test them in your Xbox One and (preferably) your Series X for cross-compatibility concerns.

The bottom line is: treat open-box as an imperfect but often delightful window into a collector’s past. If you accept some risk of missing pieces and potential cosmetic flaws, you’ll be rewarded with a genuine piece of gaming history that you can actually hold in your hands while shouting, “Take that, digital-only era!”

## Practicalities: handling, storage, and long-term care

If you plan to keep this edition as a shelf ornament as much as a player, consider a few practical steps:
- Store away from humidity and direct sunlight to minimize spine warping and surface oxidation on the steelbook.
- Use archival sleeves for the art book and posters—framing, in particular, requires UV-safe glass to prevent yellowing over the years.
- Regularly inspect the discs for micro-scratches. A cheap, simple disc cleaner can help, but if the disc is significantly damaged, it may be more cost-effective to replace rather than risk a corrupted save or a crash during critical game moments.
- If you’re hoping to recover any lost digital content, track the codes from the inside cover (if present) and verify their status on the official platform before buying a replacement.
