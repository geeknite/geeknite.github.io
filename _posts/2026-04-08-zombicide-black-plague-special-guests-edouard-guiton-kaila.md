## Theme, Artwork, and Components

Black Plague is a fantasy-hued version of the original Zombicide with a medieval blood-and-bones flavor. The minis are colorful, the terrain cards are crisp, and the zombie models—though not as terrifying as their modern-day cousins—still manage to look threatening when you’re staring down a throng of winged necromantic horrors.

Edouard and Kaila bring some extra flavor to the components: they treat each piece as a potential character with a backstory and a plan. The guests don’t just read the cards; they narrate them. The little details—the way a knight’s shield clangs against a zombie’s ribcage when you push through a corridor, or the sound the board makes when a searing fire spell lights up the cavern—turn the standard gameplay into a little theatre of the absurd. It’s wonderful, a little chaotic, and a reminder that co-op games live or die on the table chemistry more than on the box art.

The art direction remains strong: bright, bold, and dungeon-blue. Thematically, the setting holds up under close inspection, with the siege engines, knightly orders, and necromantic silhouettes giving you plenty to marvel at between the dice rolls. The components hold up to repeated use, though you’ll likely need a box of minis to separate the more stubborn zombie types. The image-heavy components actually help the table feel bigger, as if the party has wandered into a grand hall instead of a kitchen table and a coffee-stained poster of a goblet.

## Where The Strategy Meets The Smile: Crafting Your Session

To maximize the guest effect, here are a few practical tips:
- Schedule guest nights with a flexible tone. Let Edouard’s planning be the map and Kaila’s improvisation be the wind.
- Use objective-driven scenarios that can benefit from both precise play and daring improvisation. If you pick a mission that’s too linear, you’ll miss the joy of the guests; if you pick something too chaotic, you’ll lose the core structure.
- Keep house rules light. The whole point is to enjoy the moment and the people, not to chase the perfect timing of a lightning-quick doom flip.
- Have a backup snack plan. Zombies and snacks are a natural synergy; nothing softens a tense moment like a plate of cheese and bread that you’re allowed to feed to your team or to yourself in a moment of dramatic silence.

If you want to see how different playstyles blend, consider matching Edouard’s methodical approach with Kaila’s improvisational spark in a few “guest nights” across several campaigns. You’ll discover different table personalities, and you’ll probably laugh until your dice melt into a happy puddle of plastic miniature chaos.
