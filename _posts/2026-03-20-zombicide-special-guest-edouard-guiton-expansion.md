## Introduction
In the pantheon of zombie board games, Zombicide stands as that one friend who shows up with a chainsaw and somehow still manages to be charmingly polite. That is the vibe when you crack open Zombicide Special Guest Edouard Guiton, an expansion that feigns humility while cranking the chaos dial to eleven. Edouard Guiton, if you didnt know, is the guest designer who slapped his signature on a set that insists you have to roll with whatever the world throws at you and then asks for a refill on coffee and a larger bag of sanity. The Special Guest line is CMONs way of inviting a guest to the party, only this time the guest not only brings a cake but also a fully loaded armory and a dozen new ways to die dramatically on a living room floor. In this review we will dissect whether this expansion adds meaningful flavor, introduces new mechanics that actually fit the core game, and whether the price tag justifies the inevitable late night moral reckoning about the number of broken sprues in your space on day three of a campaign.

## Unboxing and components
Opening Edouard Guiton Special Guest is a ceremony of cardboard and plastic that has the suspicious smell of a good time. The box feels heavier than it should be for the number of components contained inside, which is a nice way of telling your bank account to panic quietly while you pretend you are a responsible adult with hobbies. Inside you will find a collection of miniatures for new survivors and new zombies, a handful of big meeples that resemble a small parade float when placed on the map, new ability cards, a couple of double sided map tiles, and a stack of tokens that you will promptly misplace under a couch cushion forever. The quality is typical CMON: sturdy, well-printed, and a little more polished than your average home printing project. The miniatures have the usual baseline of crisp sculpts and dynamic poses, with Edouard Guitons signature flair for dramatic action shots that look suspiciously like the designer would rather be drawing manga than culling zombies.

A few notes on the physical design include the clever integration of a few modular components that slot into existing Zombicide boards with minimal fuss. The new map tiles are compatible with base game boards and reprinted content, which means you do not have to swap your entire table arrangement to accommodate this expansion. The cards are well-laminated and color-coded in a way that would make a kindergarten teacher proud, which is to say you wont confuse a Sharpie scribble with a card instruction mid firefight. The only caveat here is that if you are a port-muller who keeps the core game in a pristine box inside a museum case, you might want to invest in a storage solution that can accommodate the extra sprues, tokens, and standees without triggering a second mortgage on your bookshelf.

## The guest designer approach and Edouard Guitons design ethos
Special Guest expansions have a reputation for either delivering a breath of fresh air or piling on a couple of gimmicks that run out of steam after a single session. The Edouard Guiton edition leans heavily into a narrative that feels like a blockbuster crossover event where your favorite survival archetypes collide with the cinematic flair of a director who knows how to frame a shot and also knows you will die in the next room if you blink. The designers approach here blends signature tactical options with a few new mechanics that tilt the balance just enough to feel novel without breaking the core rhythm of Zombicide. The end result is a product that respects the players time and the players willingness to manage more cards, more rules, and more opportunities for glorious near misses.

The new cards carry thematic voice lines and a few direct references to Edouards design style, which helps the session feel cohesive rather than a random mishmash of ideas. The art on the new survivor cards communicates their roles at a glance, enabling players to quickly coordinate without needing a decode ring for each turn. It is the kind of thoughtful touch that makes a long campaign feel less like a grind and more like a well produced heist movie where the crew occasionally argues about who wore the wrong sneakers to a heist.

## Mechanics and balance: what exactly changes the game
This expansion does not reinvent the wheel; it refinements the experience. The base Zombicide engine remains intact: you have a squad of survivors, you move, you take actions, you fight abominations, and you hope the board gods are merciful enough to allow you to live long enough to brag about it on the blog. The Edouard Guiton expansion adds a handful of new survivor profiles with unique starting equipment and special rules. These survivors introduce new synergies and new decisions about which card to draw, which to discard, and when to push your luck with a reroll. The new zombies are not merely more of the same; they come with behavioral tweaks that press your group to coordinate, share resources, and not get tunnel vision when a boss spawned behind your back.

The core mechanism that the expansion introduces is a module style ability that triggers on specific conditions. For example, a survivor might gain a temporary stealth ability that reduces the chance of triggering a zombie alarm, but only if you are occupying the same tile as another survivor and you announce the plan aloud to the table. This creates a social contract that is essential for success in Zombicide and helps to counter the dice volatility. It also nudges players toward a more cooperative approach, which is a welcome shift for groups that occasionally fall into a doom loop where one hero takes all the hits while the rest debate the ethics of looting.

As for balance, the expansion is gentle. It increases the difficulty by a modest margin, enough to keep a seasoned group on their toes but not so much that a casual crew gets completely wrecked on the first mission. The new equipment cards carry evocative art and sometimes interesting side effects that encourage experimentation. There are a couple of cards that are either ultra powerful or underwhelming, but that is a familiar tune in Zombicide expansions and part of the ongoing dance that keeps the game interesting over multiple playthroughs. The designers have occasionally sprinkled small adjustments across the level design so that you cannot simply brute force your way through every mission by sending your strongest hero into the teeth of the horde. It is a welcome reminder that in Zombicide, planning, positioning, and timing are as important as numbers on a die.

## Maps and scenarios: the world expands without collapsing into chaos
Zombicide expansions generally come with a handful of new missions and a couple of modular tiles that allow you to craft distinct board layouts. The Edouard Guiton expansion continues this tradition with 2 to 3 scenarios that feel like a natural extension of the core box, but with a few added wrinkles that keep things fresh. The missions tend to weave the new survivor abilities into the objective structure in ways that reward careful planning and creative problem solving. You might need to escort a specific survivor to a location while under pressure, or you may need to defend a tile long enough to trigger a final event that ends the round with a flourish. The tile design is crisp and readable, with a clear separation of neutral zones, safe rooms, and alarm triggers. The new tiles also integrate with existing maps so you can mix and match without fear of mis-synchronization.

From a visual perspective, the scenarios feel cinematic yet practical. The art direction helps you feel like you are in a contained film set rather than a laboratory accident waiting to happen. The balance between atmosphere and playability is a tough line to walk, and this expansion handles it with enough discipline to be enjoyed by veterans while still offering something new for newcomers. The missions reward teamwork and careful resource management, rather than simply sending more zombies at you to see if you can survive the onslaught. If you have ever wished for a little more narrative weight in Zombicide without tipping over into storybook territory, Edouard Guitons expansion delivers a satisfying dose of thematic flavor.

## The new survivors and zombie types
The expansion introduces a handful of new survivors with built in bonuses that encourage specific playstyles. Some survivors excel at ranged suppression, others thrive in close quarters, and a few offer hybrid options that let you flex between roles depending on the state of the board. The new zombies bring a few behavioral cues that require your team to adjust tactics. For example you might encounter an abomination that is more dangerous when you trigger several alarms in quick succession, which nudges players toward a more measured approach rather than racing into the next tile and hitting the panic button with a flourish. The net effect is that the players must share information, coordinate actions, and maintain a calm, almost zen like focus while the horde claws at the edges of the map.

In practice this translates to several memorable moments that break up the routine of run and shoot. You will find yourself discussing whether to fence in a zombie to buy a round or to push forward and risk a wave of reinforcements that could ruin your day. The psychological aspect of coordinating with a team under time pressure becomes a real draw, and the feel of a tightly choreographed escape increases the sense of accomplishment when you pull off a successful combo that would have sounded impossible on a casual run through the core game.

## Strategy tips and tricks
- Coordinate early: The new survivor abilities reward players who talk through their moves. The person who speaks first often wins the dice gods favor.
- Manage alarms like a responsible adult: Alarm triggers are powerful but expensive; use them sparingly and only when your plan includes a clear exit route.
- Use the new equipment cards to diversify your approach: Dont stack all your hopes on one weapon type; mix and match to handle both hordes and bosses.
- Don’t ignore map control: The tile layout remains a critical factor; occupying choke points and controlling exit routes can be more valuable than pure firepower.
- Remember resource economy: Surviving in Zombicide is about ammo and items as much as it is about kills; plan your looting and sharing accordingly.
