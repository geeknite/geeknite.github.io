## The Guests: Edouard Guiton, Doud, and Yomiko

### Edouard Guiton: The Artist on Call
Edouard Guiton’s figure in this box isn’t just a repaint of a generic survivor; it’s a character with a story etched into his jacket seams. If you’ve followed Guiton’s art across other CMON projects, you’ll recognize his penchant for cinematic silhouettes, dynamic weapon poses, and a slightly rebellious posture that says, “I broke all the zombie rules and I’m still cool.” The sculpts show subtle drama: a hip cocked just enough to imply a narrative, a scowl that reads as “I’ve seen it all and I’m still here,” and a belt buckle that probably has a backstory you could write a novella about if you’re into that bedtime-dramedy vibe.

The practicality of Edouard’s model is solid: easy to assemble with a couple of pinpoints for alignment. The shield and weapon combinations feel plausible for a survivor who’s seen the worst and refuses to go quiet. You’ll likely want to pair him with darker washes and a minimal highlight to avoid losing the sculpt’s crisp lines in middle-of-the-table lighting.

### Doud and Yomiko: The Survivors With Subtext
Doud is the kind of survivor who evokes the classic grit of “I’ve got your back, but I’m also going to crack a joke about the situation.” The sculpt’s posture implies movement; he feels like the guy who charges into danger while someone yells, “No, not yet!” Then, Yomiko arrives with a grounded, pragmatic vibe—more institutional knowledge than swagger, but with a quiet confidence that makes you forget you’re about to butcher a dozen undead in a single round.

From a gameplay perspective, Doud and Yomiko bring distinct silhouettes to the table, which helps when you’re differentiating targets during play. Their equipment and poses suggest an interesting mix of melee and ranged capabilities that can be tuned to your preferred playstyle. If you’re building narrative campaigns, these two offer enough personality to anchor a few over-arching stories without overshadowing the core cast you’ve grown used to.

Assembling and painting Doud and Yomiko is straightforward: the parts click into place with a satisfying snap, and their faces are expressive enough to translate your own painting style. If you’re a player who likes to give each survivor a voice, this box makes that process easier because the sculpts themselves scream personality.

## Tabletop Aesthetics: Painting, Display, and Thematic Vibes

The visuals here are a mixed bag of practical and aspirational. The sculpts look great on a standard 25-28mm table, and the new survivors’ gear gives you an opportunity to experiment with color palettes you might not otherwise use. If you’re into painting speed, Edouard Guiton’s figure benefits from a restrained approach: a dual-tone scheme with selective highlights tends to pop well under game lighting without looking candy-coated in the final glaze.

Recommended painting approach:
- Use a dark base for the coats and a midtone highlight along the edges to bring out the texture. A touch of red or brass on small details will draw the eye without overwhelming the face sculpt.
- For Doud and Yomiko, consider a subtle skin tone variation to emphasize character differences. You don’t need to go overboard; a light wash in the creases can deepen the realism without making the figures look muddy.
- The weapons can benefit from a light metallic wash to bring out the metal texture, followed by a dry brush in a slightly lighter metallic to catch the raised edges.

Display-wise, the box’s content looks dynamic on a mid-range shelf: it’s not so flashy that it distracts from the rest of your collection, but it’s polished enough to stand out in your display case. If you keep a dedicated display for Zombicide minis, this guest box belongs in the “conversation piece” section next to your most dramatic diorama setups.
