## Materials and Build
Sisal is a natural fiber harvested from agave plants; it’s stiff but stronger per fiber than many synthetic options. The Cloudburst line uses tight plies and a relatively uniform pile, which means less shedding than you’d expect from a natural rug, but still measurable in the realm of science: small fiber fragments that molt during the first weeks of use—normal, not apocalypse-level shedding. Timbers in the name come from the dyeing process—Timber Dust is a colorway that reads as a soft charcoal with hints of taupe.

Key points:
- Fiber: 100% natural sisal with a tight weave
- Backing: commonly latex-free or with a natural latex backing in some variants (check your SKU)
- Color: Timber Dust — a mid-tone gray-brown that pairs with wood tones, leathers, and bold accent colors
- Durability: excellent for high-traffic zones like entryways, hallways, and living rooms with kids or cats

Pro-tip: natural fiber rugs like sisal respond to moisture poorly, so keep spills minimal and blot quickly.

## Performance and Durability
Sisal is not as soft as wool, but it’s engineered for long wear. The Cloudburst Timber Dust rug will stand up to daily foot traffic, office chairs (if you lock the wheels), and occasional spills. The weave helps resist matting in high-traffic zones, and the natural color palette tends to mask small stains. It is not a hydrophobic miracle, though; if you spill coffee or red wine, act fast by blotting—don’t rub, as rubbing can push the liquid deeper into the fiber.

For households with pets, the rug’s sisal fibers are known to be scratch-resistant, but there’s a caveat: nails can catch on the coarser texture. Consider a sensitive underfoot alternative for nursery spaces or rooms where barefoot lounging is common. A well-chosen rug pad under the Cloudburst Timber Dust will improve stability, enhance bounce, and protect the rug’s underside from wear.

Edge and seam integrity: natural fiber rugs may have slight variance in weave around edges. This is a character factor, not a defect. If you’re shopping for a room with a lot of door sweeps or transitions, check the SKU for edge finishing details—some versions add a subtle bound edge that reduces fraying.

## Maintenance and Cleaning
Natural fiber rugs require a mitigated maintenance routine:
- Vacuum regularly with a brush attachment on a low suction setting. Avoid beater bars that can lift fibers.
- Blot spills immediately with a clean white cloth or paper towel. Do not rub, as rubbing can push the liquid deeper and distort the fiber structure.
- For minor stains: use a mild soap solution (a few drops of dish soap with water) and blot. Rinse with a light damp cloth and blot again.
- Avoid excess moisture; sisal fibers do not love soaking baths.
- Rotate every few months to ensure even wear across the pile.

Pro tip: keep a consistent vacuum schedule; neglecting the rug leads to more embedded dirt and quicker dulling of the timber-dust color palette.

For stain emergencies, test cleaning on a small inconspicuous area first. Natural dyes can react differently to detergents than synthetic counterparts.

A maintenance note for zero-waste enthusiasts: some retailers offer green cleaners specifically designed for natural fiber rugs; those cleaners may preserve the rug’s color better than generic products. Check the care label on your SKU to see if a recommended cleaner exists.
