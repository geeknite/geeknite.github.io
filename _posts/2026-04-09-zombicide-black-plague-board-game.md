Introduction

If you’ve ever wanted to stage a tiny apocalypse in your living room without actually destroying your furniture, Zombicide: Black Plague is the game for you. It’s CMON’s most famous horde simulator with plastic miniatures, modular boards, and enough dice to make you reconsider your life choices—particularly the ones that led you to believe you could handle a two-hour game after a long day of work and a questionable snack choice. In short: it’s loud, it’s chaotic, and yes, it’s gloriously, wonderfully replayable.

In this review, we’ll walk you through what Black Plague actually is, how it plays, what it does right (and what it does embarrassingly wrong), and why you’ll still find yourself coming back to it even when your friends keep killing you on the second turn like you’re a magnet for bad dice. If you’re new to Zombicide, think of it as a cooperative zombie slasher movie where you control the screaming survivors and the zombies tend to win only when you stop paying attention.

Artwork and components

A big part of Zombicide’s charm is its presentation. Black Plague doesn’t just add more numbers to the zombie count; it upgrades the aesthetic from “cute plastic people in dull coats” to “fantastic, grim-dark tavern brawl of doom.” The board tiles are modular—think Necronomicon-level map pieces that snap together with the confidence of a Lego master who’s just discovered glue—and the zombie miniatures are crisp enough to make you want to paint them, which is a terrible idea that you’ll still do anyway because hobby guilt is real.

The components list is long enough to make you feel like you’ve bought a hobby for life. You get: a big pile of survivors with unique skill trees, an equally big pile of zombie types (runners, fatties, scorchers, etc.), dice that double as tiny curses, tokens for items and turn events, and a flood of cards that occasionally include the word “Boom!” on them just to remind you that you’re playing a game about explosive chaos.

Setup and learning curve

For new players, setup can feel like assembling a small IKEA kitchen island in the dark while blindfolded. When you’re dealing with a modular map, a handful of character boards, weapon cards, and an entire undead army that has opinions about your tactical choices, you’ll want to allocate a solid 15–30 minutes just to get the battlefield ready. The rulebook is fairly approachable, but Black Plague has enough quirks to trip you up if you skip the introductory turns or ignore the little icon cues on the character boards. The core loop—move, search for loot, fight zombies, repeat—feels intuitive after the first couple of rounds, but you’ll still argue with your friends about loot allocation and who gets to loot the “legendary” sword that’s basically a longer spear with more swearing in its flavor text.

Gameplay overview

Zombicide is a cooperative, scenario-driven campaign in which up to six players attempt to survive waves of zombies while achieving mission objectives. Black Plague takes the base game’s zombie Apocalypto and doubles down with knights, herbalists, and peasants who swear by their weapons and their will to live. The core mechanics revolve around行动 (actions), line of sight, and dice-based combat that usually punishes you for rolling anything other than a “meh.” Each survivor has a handful of actions per turn (move, search, pick up gear, trade, or attack), and the zombies—carefully tuned to be both the scariest friend you’ve ever had and the most cartoonish threat you’ll ever face—pile onto the board in ever-increasing numbers.

A typical round begins with the players planning their order, then the zombie AI activation by the game’s rules, which results in a nice little crescendo of roars and a dramatic pile of plastic limbs appearing out of nowhere. The weapon cards offer distinct advantages—bows, swords, axes, and several “magical” or at least pseudo-magical items—that add tactical depth. You’ll find yourself balancing risk and reward as you decide whether to search for a better weapon (risking a new zombie spawn) or to push forward, hoping your luck holds and your team doesn’t get surrounded by a chorus line of undead extras.

Thematic and design notes

Black Plague’s theme is its own kind of chaotic medieval nightmare—a plague-ridden, zombie-infested version of a knightly crusade that somehow manages to be funny in the right knobs-and-levers sort of way. The minis, the board art, and the varying zombie types work together to create a coherent world where your heroes aren’t just survivors; they’re hapless celebrities in a zombie apocalypse who forgot to bring a map and a snack—but definitely brought a battle-axe. If you’re into grimdark fantasy or just enjoy games that let you scream at your friends while staring down a stack of 3D-printed corpses, you’ll feel right at home.

Rules, strategy, and pacing

Strategy in Black Plague centers around planning turns with foresight and, honestly, a bit of luck. You’ll need to manage two big resources simultaneously: actions (per turn) and loot (per search). Your survivals bring skills that scale as you gain experience, and you’ll eventually unlock more powerful gear that lets you delete zombies with the dramatic flair of a fantasy dragon-slaying montage. Pacing often hinges on the dice luck and how you read the board’s deployment of enemies. When luck goes your way, you’ll feel like a tactical genius. When it doesn’t, you’ll realize that a single poor dice roll can turn your hero from the party’s paragon into “the guy who rolled three-two-two and ran into a line of fire.”

The good, the bad, and the useful

Pros:
- Rich theme and high production values that justify the price tag.
- Deep tactical options and meaningful character progression.
- Replayability through modular boards, multiple scenarios, and diversified loot.
- Excellent co-op play that shines when you’re coordinating with friends who know how to talk through a plan—before chaos erupts.

Cons:
- Setup can be lengthy; the more modular you make the board, the longer it takes to get going.
- The game can feel punishing for new players, especially when unlucky dice hit early and the board morphs into a zombie traffic jam.
- The sheer amount of components might be intimidating for casual gamers or those with limited space.

A note on balance and expansions

Black Plague has a robust ecosystem of expansions and variants, which help keep the game fresh once you’ve played the base scenarios to death. The balancing act improves as you incorporate the right expansions for your group; the wrong combination, however, can turn the experience into a frustrating grind that feels less like a heroic rescue and more like a math problem with blood and teeth. If you’re going to expand, consider starting with one or two well-regarded add-ons that emphasize more scenarios and new zombie types rather than a thousand unique loot cards that cause information overload on every turn.

Gameplay tips and tricks

- Focus on a core group of survivors you actually like; it’s hard to manage six distinct characters when you’re juggling six different skill trees. That said, diversity can be the key to success if you enjoy a lot of synergy.
- Prioritize search tiles that grant better weapons earlier in the game. The gap between a basic sword and a legendary weapon can be the difference between a heroic standoff and a character’s dramatic fall on Turn 2.
- Learn the zombie spawn rules and use the AI timers to predict the flow of danger. The moment you treat the board like a living monster, you’ll start planning turns like a tactical general, which is how you survive long enough to brag about it online later.
- Don’t ignore the helpers. The herbalist and other “support” survivors are less flashy than swordsmen with axes, but they can be the glue that keeps your party from crumbling under an onslaught of doom.

Illustrative moments and memorable incidents

If you’ve played Black Plague, you know the moments: the dread of turning a corner and discovering a line of runners, the sudden clamor as a horde swarms through an alleyway, and that one turn where everyone rolls spectacularly well and somehow you all walk away with a slice of glory. The memories aren’t just about winning; they’re about surviving together with your messy, sometimes-bad-as-hell decisions and laughing about it afterward.

Comparison with the base Zombicide

Black Plague is essentially the medieval, dragon-slaying cousin of the original Zombicide. If the base game is a sprint through a cemetery in flip-flops, Black Plague is a full-on armored knight charging the breach with a broadsword that doubles as a flashlight. The hero classes are more flavorful here, and the setting—necromancers, knights, and ghastly plague imagery—pumps up the atmosphere. The core mechanics—dice-based combat, action economy, and loot-based progression—remain, but the presentation and scope are more expansive. If you enjoyed the first Zombicide, Black Plague is a natural upgrade; if you were indifferent to the original, this version is less forgiving but more cinematic, which can swing your opinion either way depending on your mood and your dice luck funnel.

Cultural and community takeaways

Zombicide has become a cultural staple in the modern board game hobby in a way that few licensed tactical games manage. It’s accessible enough to attract newcomers, but robust enough to engage veteran hobbyists with its tactical depth and modular expansion path. The community around it—modders, painters, and scenario designers—adds a healthy layer of life to the game’s already sizable lifespan. The comments section of any forum will likely be full of opinions about the best survivor builds, the most efficient search routes, and whether the “legendary” loot is truly worth the investment of time and paint for the figs.

Board state, pacing, and turning the tide

One of Black Plague’s strengths is how it rewards careful planning without punishing players for daring to try something new. You’ll see sequences where everyone coordinates a clever chain of actions to trap a wave of zombies on the edge of the board, followed by a misstep that lets a stray zombie slip into the survivors’ line and cause chaos. That oscillation—between brilliant tactical execution and spectacular misfortune—mirrors the best moments of any co-op you’d care to name, and it’s not surprising that those moments become in-jokes within your gaming group.

The bottom line on accessibility

Black Plague isn’t the easiest entry point if you’re brand-new to tabletop gaming. There are a lot of components, many of which demand careful organization, and there’s a steep learning curve for players who aren’t comfortable with asymmetric power progression and simultaneous turns. That said, once you’ve got a couple of sessions under your belt, the game settles into a rhythm that is both satisfying and theater-grade. If you’re ready to commit to the experience—with enough players who can keep up and friends who don’t mind losing dramatically—Black Plague delivers.

A few practical considerations

- Space requirements: If you’re playing solo, you’ll be fine with a coffee table. As a group game, you’ll want a larger surface or a dedicated gaming area to keep the board legs from turning into an improvised obstacle course.
- Time investment: Expect 90–120 minutes on average for a standard scenario with 4 players. More if you add expansions or opt for longer campaigns.
- Storage: Armor up for a sizable storage space. The miniatures, cards, and tokens multiply quickly, and the last thing you want is to misplace a critical component in your own gaming lair.
- Maintenance: If you have kids or pets, consider seeking a dedicated storage bin with labeled compartments. Nothing ruins a heroic plan like a cat walking over the board and stepping on the “Legendary Shield of Overconfidence.”

Where to begin and what to expect next

If you’re new to Zombicide as a franchise, start with the base Black Plague box and a couple of recommended expansions that add scenarios and fresh mechanics without flipping the entire balance onto its head. The expansions are where the game shines: new zombies that require new tactics, new survivors with unique abilities, and scenarios that can stretch your imagination in ways the base game’s simple “kill all the things” objective rarely does. If you’re a veteran, you’ll probably know which expansions suit your playgroup best, and you’ll have strong preferences about the ratio of chaos to strategy that makes your table sing.

User experience and final reflections

After a dozen play sessions spread across three months, Zombicide: Black Plague remains a staple in our rotation. It’s not flawless, but its flaws are part of the charm: you learn to plan around your poor luck and you find yourself cheering for a lucky crit that saves the day. The game’s appeal is not just in its mechanics but in its capacity to turn a group of people into a roving theatre troupe of tactical improvisers facing down an ever-approaching horde. There’s a balance to be struck between planning and improvisation, and Black Plague provides enough scaffolding to keep the latter from becoming pure chaos—unless you want it to, in which case, you can absolutely derail yourselves with just the right set of “Boom!” cards.

If you’re shopping for a party game with real bite, this one’s a strong candidate. If you’re hoping for a gentle Sunday experience with light, quick turns, you might want to brace yourself or pick another title. Either way, you’ll leave the table with a story to tell and a few extra zombie silhouettes that you probably don’t need but somehow still want.

Final recommendation

- If you crave a sprawling, thematic co-op with a satisfying escalation and the kind of chaos that makes a night memorable, go for it. Black Plague is a classic for a reason: it nails the vibe of a desperate defense against overwhelming odds and makes every victory feel earned.
- If you’re concerned about setup time or you want something brisk for a casual game night, consider a lighter title or a single expansion to test the waters before fully committing to the Black Plague pathway.
- If you’re already sold on the zombie aesthetic and you’ve got a group ready to commit to a longer, more intricate co-op campaign, this is a superb centerpiece for a weekend-long campaign or a series of themed gaming nights.

In the wild, the zombie apocalypse has a loud voice. Zombicide: Black Plague translates that roar into an intense, tactile experience that invites you to improvise, coordinate, and celebrate tiny moments of control amid the roiling chaos. And yes, it’s worth the mess and the price of admission—if you’re in the mood for a big, goofy, heroic co-op that still has teeth.
