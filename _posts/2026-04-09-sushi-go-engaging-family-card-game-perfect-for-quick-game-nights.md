## What is Sushi Go! exactly
Sushi Go! is a card drafting game where players are assembling a menu of sushi items over several rounds. Each card in the deck shows a distinct sushi piece such as nigiri, maki rolls, tempura, sashimi, or wasabi. The goal is to collect the best combinations to score points. Most scoring happens when you assemble certain sets or win the most pieces of a category compared to other players. The charm is that there is a lot of small decision-making packed into a short play window, so the game feels strategic in a delightful bite-sized way.

The artwork is bright and friendly, the sushi icons are easy to read, and the rulebook is mercifully straightforward. There are no tricky corner cases that require a whiteboard. Instead you get a solid rhythm: draft, pass, reveal, score. Rinse and repeat for a handful of rounds. The result is a table that looks like a friendly cafeteria exploded into cute cards and the players look like they are coordinating a coordinated napkin wave. If this sounds silly, that is because it is. And that silliness is part of Sushi Go!'s genius it lowers the barrier to entry while offering room for micro-plays and near-micro-adaptations to your house rules.

## How the drafting works in practice
The heart of Sushi Go! is the draft. Each round, you are dealt a hand of cards. You select one card that you want most and place it face-down in front of you. The rest of the hand is passed to the next player. After everyone has picked, the chosen cards are revealed and you move on to the next round with a new hand that comes from the card you just received. This continues for a few rounds until the deck runs out and scoring begins.

A few practical notes that help you actually enjoy this process rather than babysit a rulebook:

- Each round flows in a predictable rhythm. The passing direction can reverse in later iterations depending on house rules or expansions but the default is pass left. This symmetry creates a nice tension and reduces the chance a player feels stuck with a bad hand too long.
- Your first instinct is often correct, especially for new players. The value of a card is not just its face points it is the potential it unlocks with later cards and the cameos your neighbors might bid for. You should consider not just what you want on your plate but what you can steal from someone else’s table later.
- The game is short enough that you can run a couple of practice rounds to get the hang of the tempo. The first round is a warm-up the second round is when players catch their rhythm and the third round is where the real bragging rights begin or end depending on your probability luck and the alignment of your hand.

A typical game plan is to identify a couple of stable targets early and adjust as you pick. If you love tempura or sashimi you might go after them early and hope your neighbors chase something else. The joy of Sushi Go! is that you can chase a specific strategy or you can let the cards carry you wherever they want. It’s a gentle version of push-your-luck without the risk of losing your mind or your lunch money.

## Card types and how they score
Here is the quick taxonomy you can memorize while you are waiting for pizza to arrive or your turn to come around again:

- Nigiri: A simple score source these come in 1 2 or 3 point values depending on the card. If you have Wasabi tucked away at the table you can double the next Nigiri worth which is a glorious little combo when it happens.
- Maki rolls: This is a small race during the round who has the most Maki cards wins a chunk of points and the second-most collects a smaller chunk. The scoring is a little mini-competition that often mirrors which player is feeling lucky or hungry for a pattern.
- Tempura: A set of two Tempura cards earns you points. The moment you assemble the duo you feel like you have achieved an ancient dragon craving. It balances the quick wins with a longer-term strategy.
- Sashimi: Three Sashimi cards equal a larger single score. It is the classic risk-reward card type a player can chase but it often requires you to invest in three separate draws which can be dicey but rewarding.
- Dumplings: These climb in value with more Dumplings collected. They scale nicely and can swing from modest to spicy if you find yourself collecting several in a row. It is a simple engine that rewards persistence more than speed.
- Wasabi: The Wasabi card is not a direct point card but a multiplier for the next Nigiri you play. It is a tiny orchestral cue that can lead to a spectacular bouquet of points if you manage to align your next card with a Wasabi on the table.
- Puddings: Some versions include Puddings that count for points at the end of the game. They add a little extra flavor for longer campaigns or when you want to add a light strategic layer without making the game heavier.
- Chopsticks: The Chopsticks card is a trick card you can carry for a future turn. When you decide to use it you take two cards in a single turn. This is a powerful tactical wrinkle that rewards planning and memory and a dash of cognitive gymnastic fun.

If you keep these categories in mind you will get better at pairing cards in ways that maximize your score while keeping your opponents honest with their own ambitions. The key is to avoid getting locked into a single path you should be flexible enough to take advantage of the cards your neighbors hand you and to pivot your strategies between rounds.

## Strategies for families: from rookie to confident play
If you are new to drafting you can still be competitive without memorizing every corner case. Here are a few practical tips that tend to work well in most family groups:

- Start with a quick target: pick one card you want early in the round and try to secure it before your partner distracts you with a meltdown about dessert. The aim is to lock in a couple of reliable scoring paths before the round ends.
- Read the table, not just your hand: keep one eye on how many Maki cards are out there and how many cards remain in the deck. If someone is likely to dominate Maki this round and you are far behind you might pivot toward Nigiri or Tempura to squeeze out points elsewhere.
- Don’t ignore Wasabi: Wasabi is a tiny multiplier that can yield big rewards if you can line up a high-value Nigiri with it. It is easy to overlook, but when used on a 3-point Nigiri it transforms a modest card into something that feels glowingly magical.
- The late-round shuffle matters: as rounds wind down, you should think about what your opponents will likely pass to you, and how that affects your potential scoring. If you can anticipate a hand that will produce a high-value Nigiri for you in the next round you might grab Wasabi and plan accordingly.
- Balance risk and reward: chasing a full sashimi or full dumpling set is satisfying but tricky. If you find yourself in a position where a single card holds your fate, you might settle for reliable mid-range scoring rather than gambling on a hard-to-complete set. The fun is in the flow of choices not in a single perfect run.
