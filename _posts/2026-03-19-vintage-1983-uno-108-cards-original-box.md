## A Quick History: UNO and the 1983 Reissue
UNO began in the wild, yellowed days of the early 1970s under the watchful eye of Merle Robbins. The game’s concept was sleek: four colors, a handful of numbers, and a strategy that could be summed up as “don’t let your friends win while you immediately forget the rule you just invented.” The 1983 edition marks a pivotal era when the deck expanded to 108 cards, packaging constraints loosened, and the box art started to look less like a math textbook and more like a bold statement of “play now or regret later.” This era also captured the aesthetic you’ll recognize in the picture frames of many a late-80s sitcom: bold typography, high-contrast colors, and cardboard that felt substantial enough to survive a dozen neighborhood tournaments.

From a gameplay perspective, the 108-card setup preserved the classic mix: numbers 0–9 in four colors, two copies of each number card (except 0 in some variations), plus two copies of each action card (Skip, Reverse, Draw Two) per color, and the ever-charming ensemble of four Wild and four Wild Draw Four cards. This structure ensured that even while you could predict the cards in your own hand, the deck’s composition still made every hand a delightful lottery of chaos and drama. If you’ve played modern UNO, you’ll notice the bones are the same; what changes is the skin and the swagger.
