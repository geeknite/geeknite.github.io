Zombicide: Black Plague is the kind of game that makes you feel like you live in a medieval fantasy tavern while also being chased by undead old ladies with knitting needles. Then along comes Green Horde, a leafy expansion with fancy goblins, lumbering orcs, and a menace that’s less “plague” and more “heh, you forgot to bring garlic.” And right when you think you’ve seen all the creeping dread the Zombicide universe can throw at you, Abominabunny hops onto the scene wearing a smile that says, I’m cute, I’m fluffy, and I’m going to ruin your day in the most adorable way possible. This review is here to tell you how these three elements- Black Plague’s grimy grit, Green Horde’s wild menace, and the Abominabunny’s adorable apocalypse- blend together, why they work, and how to maximize your chaotic, produce-splattered table presence without sacrificing your sanity.

## Overview

If you’re coming to this trifecta fresh, here’s the short version: Black Plague delivers a medieval fantasy zombie apocalypse where you’re raiding dungeons, looting treasure, and slapping undead with swords until they stop getting back up. Green Horde adds a new ecosystem of enemies and a shift in mood from dark scuffles to glorious, chaotic skirmishes in grassy lanes where the undead might just get goblin-auxiliary support. Abominabunny is the silent, fluffy intruder that redefines “boss fight” by combining cuteness with unreasonably dangerous combat mechanics. The result is a crunchy, sometimes ridiculous, always tense co-op experience where planning matters and dice hate you a little bit more with every failed initiative.

This isn’t just a purchase; it’s a puzzling social experiment: can you coordinate your knight-collective to survive while a homicidally cute rabbit watches from behind a banner, churning out terrible puns and even worse spawns? Spoiler: you can, but your table will remind you for weeks that you definitely should have brought a larger tray for all the popcorn you’ll spill when Abominabunny telegraphs its next swing with a twitch of its ears.

### Components and Build Quality
The kit feels like someone asked “what would happen if a dwarf smith and a woodland sprite collaborated on a board game?” The miniatures are chunky, satisfying to grip, and the detail level is sturdy enough to inspect with a magnifying glass while you mutter about the ethics of producing a zombie army with goblin support in a medieval fantasy setting. The Green Horde introduces new faction tiles, spawn tokens, and encounter cards, all of which slot in with the same high-contrast artwork fans of Zombicide have come to expect. The Abominabunny itself is a delight: big enough to be imposing, small enough to be arch-nugget adorable, and equipped with a couple of pun-laden attack lines that land just as hard as its bite.

There’s a lot to love on the table: you get to see Orcs glare at you with a determination that would shame your gym’s motivational posters, and then a bouncy, hop-happy rabbit zooms across the board like it’s auditioning for a nature documentary on “What Not to Do in a Zombie Apocalypse.” It’s chaotic, it’s silly, and it somehow feels right at home in a game that has you wading through blood and beer while plotting a practical route to safety.

## The Cast: Black Plague, Green Horde, and Abominabunny

### Zombicide: Black Plague — The Ground Beat
Black Plague remains the core engine: dice-driven co-op with skill checks, team-based decisions, and plenty of moments where you realize you forgot to bring a key card and the dungeon you’re in is now basically a trap. The system is crisp: you coordinate actions, manage your survivors’ inventories, and push zombies off the board by any means necessary. The vibe is grimy medieval horror with a dash of merciless humor; your heroes aren’t invincible, but they’re stubborn enough to keep swinging until the last die tumble of the night. The expansion doesn’t necessarily reinvent mechanics, but it adds new terrain and enemy archetypes that force you to rethink your approach: swordsmanship isn’t always enough when your knights start to resemble pin cushions for orc arrows and green slime.

### Zombicide: Green Horde — The Forest Comes Alive
Green Horde shakes up the mix by giving you a whole new fauna to fear. Think sneaky goblins, striding greenskins, and a set of “forest ambush” events that tempt you with treasure—only to unleash a chorus line of orc spawn that demands your attention. The pacing shifts: you’ll move through the dungeon with more caution, because the greenskin faction likely has better pathfinding than your average zombie and more tricks up their sleeves than a pickpocket on a road event. The expansion does a good job of keeping the core mechanics intact while introducing fresh mission dynamics: new objective cards push you to plan multi-phase pushes, coordinate your team’s strengths, and triage during chapters where a goblin shaman can flip a scenario on you with a well-timed card flip. It’s more tactical, more chaotic, and somehow more cheerful about its own chaos—because goblins are basically tiny, violent tricksters who happen to be terrifying when you misjudge their numbers.

### Abominabunny — The Cuddle that Endangers All
Abominabunny is the poster child for “you think it’s cute, you’re about to regret it.” It’s a fluffy, wide-eyed disaster wearing a grin that telegraphs “go ahead, underestimate me.” In practice, the bunny isn’t just a boss with a cute model; it’s a confluence of unique attack patterns, defensive quirks, and a morale swing that can flip the table faster than a sudden zombie surge. Mechanically, Abominabunny introduces a different threat calculus: you’ll need to allocate suppression resources specifically to keep it from hopping into the wrong corridor and multi-targeting your survivors. There’s an almost comic inevitability to its presence—the team’s plan will be compromised by a fluffy, unstoppable bunny that hops where you didn’t expect, spawns new zombies in the path you hoped would block the next wave, and then stares at you with those impossible eyes that say, “I’m adorable, deal with it.” It’s a masterclass in balancing fear and delight: you fear the impact of its next move, but you can’t help but smile at how cinematic it becomes when Abominabunny charges in with mechanical precision and undeniable charisma.

## Gameplay Mechanics Through the Three-Way Lens
The heart of this triple-threat is how the mechanics interplay. You’ll find that:

- The Green Horde adds new enemy behavior that rewards smart positioning and crowd control; it’s less about sheer brute force and more about reading the board like a strategist in a warroom—only with more magic mushrooms, less water, and definitely more decapitated goblins in the art.
- Abominabunny introduces a unique “threat bubble” mechanic that can force you to split your team’s focus. It’s not a simple “kill it first” encounter; you’ll have to decide when to invest in keeping it pinned down versus securing a corridor to stop a wave that’s about to come in behind you.
- Black Plague remains the baseline that anchors everything. The core rhythm—move, explore, fight, scavenge, repeat—stays intact, ensuring that even as you add a goblin matryoshka of threats, you’re still playing the same chess game with dice instead of pawns.

All told, the three components work together in a surprisingly cohesive way. The Green Horde doesn’t feel like a gimmick add-on; it changes the strategic math of the game. Abominabunny doesn’t feel like a one-note joke; it’s a genuine variable that can tilt a mission from “we’ll barely pull this off” to “we’re not out of the woods yet, but we’re smiling about it.” And Black Plague remains a strong base that you can bring to other expansions if you’re a collector. The result is a campaign experience that remains tense without taking itself too seriously—a rare balance in a genre where “tower of bones and doom” is often the default mood.

## Table Talk: Thematic Coherence and Humor
If there’s one thing Geeknite loves, it’s a game that can threaten to ruin your night and then pull you back into the light with a quip about tactical patience and nerdiness. The trio hits that sweet spot. The aesthetics lean into the fantasy-horror vibe with a wink. The Abominabunny, in particular, nails the power-comedown arc: you’re terrified, you’re laughing, and you’re scrambling to adjust your plan while the bunny telegraphs its most devastating attack with a curl of its whiskers. It’s ridiculous in the way a good fantasy show is ridiculous: you’re never quite sure if the next act will reveal a dragon with a carrot as a weapon or a goblin with a playlist that somehow derails your entire strategy.

From a table-top perspective, it’s also genuinely funny watching your seasoned survivors grow more cautious as the rabbit’s plan unfolds. It’s not just about killing undead; it’s about coping with a threat that looks like a cuddle bear but moves like a threat assessment algorithm. The humor is not jokey for joke’s sake; it’s earned from the tension, the near-misses, and the absurd speed at which a bunny can alter the course of a mission. In a genre that sometimes feels like a grim ritual, Abominabunny provides a welcome swing of the pendulum back toward joy without diminishing the stakes.

## Install, Setup, and Playthrough Flow
Like most Zombicide experiences, setup is the major time sink–in a good way. You’re alphabetizing tiles, shuffling factions, and debating whether to convert a “nightmare beast” encounter into a side mission for more loot. The first few rounds feel slow as you figure out the new enemy behaviors and how to distribute your survivors across the board. Then, as you settle into a rhythm, your choices become sharper: you anticipate spawn patterns, you protect vital choke points, and you coordinate multi-hero actions that maximize the squad’s DPS while minimizing the risk of Abominabunny hopping behind your line.

The Green Horde’s greenskins reward you for controlling the space. You’ll start to appreciate doors and corners not as mere obstacles but as lever points to nudge the action toward favorable outcomes. The goblins’ trickery, when it’s properly anticipated, becomes a puzzle rather than a random nuisance. And the Abominabunny? It’s a dynamic puzzle: you must parse its stance and choose whether to engage with a two-turn window of opportunity or to bait it into an alternative route with a decoy plan. The fun is in the constant adjustment—like listening to a stand-up comedian experiment with new material while you’re in the middle of a dungeon crawl.

## Solo Play, Co-op, and Table Dynamics
If you’re a solo player, the trio still shines—though you’ll feel the pressure of covering multiple roles and keeping the bunny’s unpredictable moves in check. It scales well with cooperative play and doesn’t require a full party of five to be meaningful. The keys to enjoying a solo run are planning ahead, embracing flexibility, and leaning into the habit that every mission ends with a cliffhanger that would make a fantasy epilogue jealous.

For groups, the dynamic of three distinct threat types means you’ll often see someone playing the more ranged, strategic caster-type role while others go for frontline tanking and board control. The third seat becomes the bunny’s designated counter-plan: someone who can react quickly to sudden bunny movement and redirect the team’s focus before a wave collapses on your survivors. The social element is strong here: it becomes an ongoing joke about who got tagged by Abominabunny and who pulled off the clutch save to avert a chain-reaction of zombie spawns in closer corridors. The mood becomes a blend of heroic fantasy and improv comedy, which is exactly the kind of synergy Geeknite adores.

## Art, Table Presence, and Collectibility
Visually, the box is a feast. The Green Horde’s greenskins pop against the dark, iron-heavy palette of Black Plague, and the Abominabunny model—well, it’s one of those miniatures that begs for a diorama. If you’re into painting or customizing, this trio gives you excellent opportunities to blend color schemes: maybe a mossy green for goblin skin, a battered bronze for knight armor, and a fluffy white for Abominabunny’s fluffy fur, with pink-tipped ears that somehow imply danger. The table presence is elevated across the board: your game night looks more epic when you can point to a towering pile of loot, a taxidermy-grade bunny, and a row of goblin banners that flutter with imaginary wind. It’s not just about the mechanics; it’s about making your table a stage for a tactile, kinetic narrative.

## Strategy and Tactics: Getting the Most Out of It
If you want to maximize your enjoyment and your odds of a successful mission, here are the Geeknite-approved strategies:

- Prioritize mobility: Green Horde encounters tend to punish rigid, stand-and-fight playstyles. Your team should aim to stay mobile, with at least one member who can move quickly to block or redirect spawns.
- Use the Bunny as a staging ground: Don’t burn resources chasing Abominabunny on every turn. Instead, create a plan where the bunny’s movement funnels zombies toward your strongest fighters or away from your loot hoard.
- Loot management matters: The loot economy in Black Plague is already a puzzle. With Green Horde, you’ll want to be deliberate about when to press for critical items and when to retreat to secure a safer zone for regrouping.
- Communication is king: The trio thrives on careful, team-based planning. Callouts like “eyes on the bunny” or “hold the left flank” are not just cute in-game quotes; they’re life preservers when chaos erupts.
- Adapt to the encounter deck: The Green Horde’s deck changes the tempo of the game. Expect more ambushes, more back-and-forth, and more opportunities to turn the tide with a clever play that looks risky but pays off big.

## Final Recommendation
If you crave a cooperative dungeon-crawl with a medieval-tinged zombie apocalypse vibe, then Black Plague combined with Green Horde, spiked by the Abominabunny’s chaotic charm, is a strong contender for your shelf. It provides a deeper tactical challenge than the base game alone, without sacrificing the lighthearted fun that makes co-op gaming so appealing. It’s not merely a monster-hunter simulator; it’s a campaign engine that invites you to craft a story with your friends, to experiment with strategy and humor, and to accept that on any given night, a bunny in a scarf could be the real wildcard that decides whether you live to loot another day.

If you’re new to the brand, pick up the base game first to get a feel for the core mechanics, then add Green Horde and Abominabunny to ramp up the chaos. If you’re a veteran, this trio offers a fresh lens through which to view your favorite missions and a surprisingly satisfying level of thematic cohesion that makes the world feel cohesive rather than stitched together. Either way, you’ll end your session with a sense of accomplishment, a few new inside jokes about bunnies that can disarm you with cuteness, and perhaps a scratched table edge from a particularly dramatic last stand.
