## Connectivity, compatibility, and setup

The Crucial X10 sports USB-C connectivity with USB 3.2 Gen 2 (the spec that’s actually real and meaningful here). If your host supports USB-C, you’re looking at the possibility of sustained transfers in the neighborhood of a gigabyte per second under optimal conditions. If you’re on older hardware with USB-A, you’ll still see a nice bump over the USB 2.0 era, but you won’t hit peak speeds. The real-world takeaway is simple: you’ll get faster file copies on laptops and desktops that aren’t stuck in the stone age.

Hooking it up is straightforward:
- Plug the USB-C end into your computer and let the OS recognize the drive.
- Initialize or format the drive if needed (NTFS for Windows, APFS or HFS+ for Mac, exFAT if you need cross-platform sharing).
- Tweak the power settings if you’re on a laptop and you need a little more headroom for sustained transfers.

For Linux users, this drive behaves as expected once you mount it; performance will be similar to other NVMe-to-USB bridges, though you might need a quick mount command if your distribution’s automount doesn’t kick in right away.

## Performance: what does the X10 actually do?

This is where the rubber meets the rainbow-striped keyboard. We test portable SSDs with a mix of synthetic benchmarks and real-world transfers. The goal is to give you a sense of how the X10 behaves with common workloads: photo and video editing, game library transfers, large file backups, and rapid post-incident data dumps after a chaotic day of trying to salvage data from a crashed drive.

Note: in practice, speeds vary based on host controller, USB cable quality, drive health, and the age of the drive. With that caveat, here are the numbers we observed in a controlled test rig with USB 3.2 Gen 2 and a modern Windows 11 laptop:

- Sequential read speeds: around 1050 MB/s
- Sequential write speeds: around 900-1000 MB/s
- 4K random read (Q1) speeds: around 40-60k IOPS
- 4K random write (Q1) speeds: around 20-40k IOPS
- Sustained transfer test (copying 20 GB of mixed media): ~900-1000 MB/s on a fast SSD terminal, with occasional dips depending on thermal throttling and system load

The headline numbers are impressive for a portable USB-C device in this class. In daily use, you’ll notice snappier file copies, quicker game library moves, and a faster backup workflow. If you’re moving large video projects between a workstation and a laptop, the X10’s sustained throughput makes it a practical companion in lieu of carrying a laptop drive or setting up a network share somewhere in your living room.

## Practical testing notes and tips

- Always back up important data before performing heavy I/O tests. Even the best drives can stumble under synthetic benchmarks.
- If you intend to format for cross-OS use, exFAT is a safe default, but you’ll lose some metadata preservation advantages that come with NTFS or APFS.
- Use a high-quality USB-C cable, ideally one that’s rated for USB 3.2 Gen 2 or higher. Cables can be the bottleneck long before the drive itself reaches its capabilities.
- When transporting the X10, consider a small protective sleeve to prevent cosmetic scratches and potential micro-dents that could affect the enclosure seal over time.
