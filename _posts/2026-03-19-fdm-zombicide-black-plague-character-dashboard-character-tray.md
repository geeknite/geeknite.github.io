## Materials and Printer Setup: From PLA to PETG and Beyond
### Materials
- PLA for beginners: easy to print and forgiving, especially for multi-part builds that you won’t fear disassembling for a quick wash.
- PETG for durability: slightly more challenging, but better resistance to heat and constant table nudges during a long night of munchies and misplays.
- Optional: PETG with a touch of flex to absorb light impacts of accidental table nudges—think of it as the hero’s shield, but for tokens.

### Bed adhesion tips
- A brim or raft helps keep prints flat, especially for tall dashboard sections that resemble a skyline of tiny battlefield monuments.
- A glue stick or blue painter tape can offer extra adhesion on larger bases if you’re fighting warp tendencies.
- A thin layer of PETG dissolvable supports makes cleanup easier for intricate parts and keeps your sanity intact after the third reprint.

### Printing settings (baseline, with a wink)
- Layer height: 0.2 mm for a nice balance of speed and detail.
- Infill: 15-20% with grid or lines to keep weight reasonable without sacrificing rigidity.
- Print speed: 40-60 mm/s depending on filament and printer.
- Cooldown: proper cooling helps with sharp corners and crisp lettering on the dividers.
- Post processing: light sanding of edges and optional matte clear coat to resist fingerprints during play, and to give it that “prepared-for-battle” sheen.

## The Tray and the Dashboard in Practice: A Real Table Test
### Layout fundamentals
The dashboard uses a grid layout with labeled lanes for each character. A typical setup might include:
- 2-3 wide lanes for weapon tiles per character
- 1 lane for stat cards and tokens
- 1 lane for wound/damage tokens and XP trackers
- Small pockets for quest items and specialty equipment

The Tray sits below or beside the dashboard and uses separate compartments:
- Token wells for common items like damage and move tokens
- A spare card holder for quick reference cards
- A shallow tray for the small flat items that tend to scatter off into the void like tiny dice dragons

### Assembly and fit: the art of reasonable tolerances
Most parts are designed to snap or slot together with light friction. If you have a looser tolerance on your printer, you may want to precision-sand mating surfaces and use a dab of CA glue to fix everything in place. Magnets can be a clever upgrade for chunkier sections if you want quick-activate modules that can be rearranged mid-game. If you go magnet crazy, consider neodymium magnets and a light metal plate to avoid weak points that shift under the weight of a hero wielding an unwieldy longsword.

### Ergonomics and play flow: seeing is believing
The dashboard aims to reduce table clutter and cognitive load. A well designed dashboard should help you see at a glance:
- Which hero is active and what their current health status looks like
- What items are currently equipped and what slots are free
- Which tokens are needed for the next turn and whether you need to refill

This translates into fewer pauses in the action and fewer grumbles about where that last zombie token went to die in peace.

## Printing and Maintenance Tips: Keeping It Dense but Durable
- Fine-tune your slicer settings for tolerances. Small changes can impact the fit of dividers and the bite of snap-fit pieces—no more “does this peg fit, or is it a frog?” moments.
- Print in PLA if you want quick prints that are easy to post-process. Switch to PETG if you expect to travel between game nights or survive a basement humidity storm.
- Use a resin-dimming top coat after print to protect edge lettering from wear. It also gives the board a nice tactile feel and makes your handwriting look intentional, not scribbly.
- Label clearly: include character names and slot names right on the surface. If you print in dark colors, white or light gray fills can improve readability.

### A note on color coding and readability
Color-coding isn’t just for aesthetics; it’s a speed-run hack for busy nights. Stat cards in blue, weapons in red, tokens in green—your brain will map these at a glance, cutting down “where did I put that thing?” time by at least one full zombie round per game night.

## Resin vs FDM and Other DIY Solutions: A Practical Face-Off
- Resin prints can achieve finer detail, but they require more upfront investment in post-processing and a more delicate handling routine. For most gamers, FDM provides a robust, affordable path to functional tokens with decent legibility.
- Other DIY trays from the community often vary in fit and durability. The dashboard approach shines when you can align base dimensions with standard table widths and ensure character cards match tile sizes without needing a caliper every turn.
- Commercial products exist, but they tend to be more expensive and less adaptable to expansions. The human factor here is value: you can adjust your dashboard to your exact table and group play style without waiting for a vendor to release a new kit.

## Practical Tips and Common Pitfalls: Tame the Kraken Before It Jumps
- Test print a single character block first. If alignment is off, you can dial in tolerances without wasting a ton of filament.
- Consider printing multiple color variants for visual clarity: one color for stat cards, another for weapons, a third for tokens. It reduces misreads in the middle of a heated turn.
- If your table has wobble, you can incorporate a non-slip mat beneath the dashboard and tray. The combination of texture and grip helps keep everything in place during the infamous slams of a heroic dice throw.
- Store spare parts in a separate aligned box. It helps prevent the dreaded exchange of a longbow for a crossbow when you’re in a pinch.

## Maintenance and Longevity: Treat It Like a Sidekick That Refuses to Die
Printed parts love to be reused, but they also demand occasional maintenance. If you notice a part loosening up after several sessions, apply a thin bead of CA glue to the contact surfaces and let it cure fully. If you want to keep things clean, a quick wipe with isopropyl alcohol and a lint-free cloth will remove fingerprints and marker scribbles during a late-night session.

Storage is also important. Keep the dashboard dry and away from direct sunlight if possible. UV light can gradually impact color and material hardness. A simple storage bin with dividers keeps the whole system tidy between game nights and reduces the risk of misplacing a drawer just before the final boss reveal.
