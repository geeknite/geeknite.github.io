## What is the Character Dashboard and Tray
The Character Dashboard is essentially a modular board that sits beside the game mat and gives each character their own little command center. It includes slots for:
- Character stat cards
- Equipped items and weapons
- Wounds and XP trackers
- Inventory items and quest items
- Mini reference tokens for status effects

The Character Tray deepens this by providing flat storage for tokens and small tiles. The tray sections are sized to snugly hold the standard Zombicide Black Plague kit: cooldown tokens, weapon tokens, survivor cards, and the big bad boss cards. The goal is simple: reduce table clutter, speed up upkeep, and give the party a sense of organization that would shame a tidy librarian.

## Design Philosophy
This project lives at the intersection of practicality and playfulness. The design choices prioritize:
- Modularity: components snap into a grid so you can customize per table size and per expansion pack.
- Readability: high contrast labeling, clear dividers, and large font areas so you can glance at a card and know what you need to grab.
- Durability: for FDM prints, the parts must hold up to a little table bashing and occasional wet-wipes cleanup.
- Compatibility: the dashboard should work with the standard box contents and still leave room for extra expansions you dutifully bought on a whim.

Typography nerd note: the aim is not to replicate the exact card sizes to a millimeter, but to create ergonomics that feel natural to the user. In board game design, a small change in reach or visibility can shave minutes off a session and turn a skirmish into a satisfying strategic ballet rather than chaos-based chaos.

## Materials and Printer Setup
### Materials
- PLA for beginners: easy to print and forgiving, especially if you are new to multi-part builds.
- PETG for durability: slightly more challenging, but better resistance to heat and bumps during a long night of munchies and misplays.
- Optional: PETG with a light flex to absorb light impacts of accidental table nudges.

### Bed adhesion tips
- A brim or raft helps keep prints flat, especially for tall dashboard sections.
- A glue stick or blue painter tape can offer extra adhesion on larger bases.
- A thin layer of PETG support or dissolvable supports makes cleanup easier for intricate parts.

### Printing settings (as a baseline)
- Layer height: 0.2 mm for a nice balance of speed and detail.
- Infill: 15-20% with grid or lines to keep weight reasonable without sacrificing rigidity.
- Print speed: 40-60 mm/s depending on your filament and printer.
- Cooldown: proper cooling helps with sharp corners and clear lettering on the dividers.
- Post processing: light sanding of edges and optional matte clear coat to reduce fingerprints during play.

## The Tray and the Dashboard in Practice
### Layout fundamentals
The dashboard uses a grid layout with labeled lanes for each character. A typical setup might include:
- 2-3 wide lanes for weapon tiles per character
- 1 lane for stat cards and tokens
- 1 lane for wound/damage tokens and XP trackers
- Small pockets for quest items and specialty equipment

The Tray sits below or beside the dashboard and uses separate compartments:
- Token wells for common items like damage and move tokens
- A spare card holder for quick reference cards
- A shallow tray for the small flat items that tend to scatter off into the void like tiny dice dragons

### Assembly and fit
Most parts are designed to snap or slot together with light friction. If you have a loose tolerance on your printer, you may want to precision-sand mating surfaces and use a dab of CA glue to fix everything in place. Magnets can be a clever upgrade to chunkier sections if you want quick-activate modules that can be rearranged mid-game. If you go magnet crazy, consider neodymium magnets and a light metal plate to avoid weak points that shift under the weight of a hero with an unwieldy longsword.

### Ergonomics and play flow
The dashboard aims to reduce table clutter and cognitive load. A well designed dashboard should help you see at a glance:
- Which hero is active and what their current health status looks like
- What items are currently equipped and what slots are free
- Which tokens are needed for the next turn and whether you need to refill
This translates into fewer pauses in the action and fewer arguments about where that last zombie token went to die in peace.

## Printing and Maintenance Tips
- Fine-tune your slicer settings for tolerances. Small changes can impact the fit of dividers and the bite of snap-tarts, I mean, snap-fit pieces.
- Print in PLA if you want quick prints that are easy to post-process. Switch to PETG if you expect to ship this kit around to game nights or to survive a basement humidity storm.
- Use a resin-dimming top coat after print to protect edge lettering from wear. It also gives the board a nice tactile feel.
- Label clearly: include character names and slot names right on the surface. If you print in dark colors, white or light gray fills can improve readability.

## Comparisons: Resin vs FDM, and Other Solutions
- Resin prints can achieve finer detail, but they require more upfront investment in post-processing and a more delicate handling routine. For most gamers, FDM provides a robust, affordable path to functional tokens with decent legibility.
- Other DIY trays from the community often vary in fit and durability. The dashboard approach shines when you can align the base dimensions with standard table widths and the character cards match the tile sizes without needing a caliper every turn.
- Commercial products exist, but they tend to be more expensive and less adaptable to expansions. The human factor here is value: you can adjust your dashboard to your exact table and group play style without waiting for a vendor to release a new kit.

## Practical Tips and Common Pitfalls
- Test print a single character block first. If the alignment is off, you can dial in your tolerances without wasting a ton of filament.
- Consider printing multiple color variants for visual clarity: one color for stat cards, another for weapons, a third for tokens. It reduces misreads in the middle of a heated turn.
- If your table has wobble, you can incorporate a non-slip mat beneath the dashboard and tray. The combination of texture and grip helps keep everything in place during the infamous slams of a heroic dice throw.
- Store spare parts in a separate aligned box. It helps prevent the dreaded exchange of a longbow for a crossbow when you are in a pinch.

## Maintenance and Longevity
Printed parts love to be reused, but they also demand occasional maintenance. If you notice a part loosening up after several sessions, apply a thin bead of CA glue to the contact surfaces and let it cure fully. If you want to keep things clean, a quick wipe with isopropyl alcohol and a lint-free cloth will remove fingerprints and accidental marker scribbles during a late night session.

Storage is also important. Keep the dashboard dry and away from direct sunlight if possible. UV light can gradually impact color and material hardness. A simple storage bin with dividers keeps the whole system tidy between game nights and reduces the risk of misplacing a drawer just before the final boss reveal.
