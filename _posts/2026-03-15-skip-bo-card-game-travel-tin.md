## How to Play: The Quick and Quirky Rules
Skip-Bo is a game of sequencing, speed, and a little bit of sabotage; the travel tin version stays faithful to the core mechanics while trimming some frills for portability. Here is the quick cheat sheet for those who want to start playing right away, with a bit of Geeknite wit sprinkled in.

### Objective
Be the first player to discard all of your stock pile by playing cards in sequential order onto one or more of the center build piles. Build piles must progress from 1 to 12; you can place a Wild card (Skip-Bo) as a stand-in for any number to help you out of a tight jam.

### Setup
- Each player gets a stock pile, face down, with its top card visible. The number of cards in your stock pile varies by player count and house rules, but in most common setups, you start with around 15 stock cards.
- The center area holds up to four or more build piles, which you add to by playing cards in rising order.
- The remaining cards form a draw pile. On your turn you draw up to five cards in your hand (or as many as you can with fewer draws left).

### Turn structure
- Draw up to five cards if your hand is not full.
- Play cards from your hand or from your stock pile to fill or continue the center build piles in ascending order.
- You may play multiple cards in a single go if your cards line up with the required sequence.
- When you cannot play, you must end your turn, and your opponent gets their turn.

### End of round and scoring
The round ends when a player empties their stock pile. In a travel tin setting, rounds are short and wonky enough to allow for several wins in a single family game night. Scoring can be done by counting the number of cards left in opponents stock pile, with small bonuses for using wild cards strategically. The exact scoring method can be adjusted by house rules, which makes this a great game for experimentation and chaos. 

### Strategy quick tips
- Prioritize building piles that unlock more possible plays on your next turn. If you can extend two build piles simultaneously, you are in a better position than someone who has only one option.
- Use wild cards to save you from dead ends, but try not to waste them on sequences that people can easily replicate in the next turn.
- Keep an eye on the table state; if your opponents are close to finishing their stock piles, consider aggressive plays to block or speed up the final pushes.
