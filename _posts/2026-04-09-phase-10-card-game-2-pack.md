## How to Play: A Friendly Refresher
If you know Phase 10, this section will read as familiar with a friendly wink. If not, Phase 10 blends set building with the chase through ten phases, and the 2 Pack simply expands the playground. Here is a longer refresher tailored to the two-deck experience:

- Objective: Be the first to complete all ten phases across both decks. Each phase has a specific requirement, such as two sets and a run, or a mix of a run and a set. You must complete them in order, but the dual decks offer more outs and more opportunities to pivot when a path locks you down.
- Turn structure: On your turn you draw, decide to keep or discard, and if possible lay down a completed phase. If you cannot, you keep chipping away at it. The two decks mean more visible paths to success but also more cards to track, which amps up the chaos in a good way.
- Wilds and skips: Wild cards substitute for any needed card in a phase, which is perfect when your plan hinges on a particular card that is not coming. Skips can slow others or be used as strategic tools to delay someone who is inching toward a critical phase. It is a social dance that makes the table feel alive rather than a math exam.

Two decks escalate the chaos and the joy. It does not morph Phase 10 into a heavy strategy game; it expands surface area for clever plays, dramatic reveals, and the occasional glorious misplay moment when the last needed card drops into your hand.

## Accessibility, Family-Friendliness, and House Rules
Two decks are friendlier to some and tougher to others. For newer players, the extra options can be an overwhelming buffet. A simple house rule to ease newcomers: limit the number of phases considered active at once or allow a single deck to be the primary focus for a few rounds before flipping to both decks. For families with younger players, expect a longer session but also a richer opportunity to teach counting, pattern recognition, and the art of patience. If you crave a lighter vibe, consider alternating rounds—one round with a single deck, the next with both—so the pace remains approachable.

House rules worth trying:
- Phase pause: declare a round as a phase sprint where players ignore completed phase requirements on one deck for the sake of momentum on the other deck.
- Wild balancing: allow wilds to count for any card on just one deck at a time to reduce perceived luck slams back-to-back.
- Memory gates: for every round, designate one player as the memory gatekeeper who can remind others about discarded cards at the cost of a small, friendly penalty that yields table laughter.
