## How to play: the vibe, the rules, the chaos

Exploding Kittens is famously easy to learn, and the Party Pack keeps that spirit alive while letting the chaos bloom in more players’ hands. Here’s a high-level overview of how a round tends to go when you’ve got a full table and a pint of caffeinated bravado on hand.

### Setup

- Shuffle the deck(s) and deal a starting hand to each player. The exact number depends on the edition, but you’re generally going to be staring at a handful of cards that will either save you or doom you on your very next turn.
- The Exploding Kitten card remains the central threat. If you draw it without a Defuse card (or something equally clever), you’re eliminated from the round. If you’re playing with a big group, expect a few dramatic exits and a chorus of gasps that would make a soap opera jealous.
- The Defuse cards act as your shield against doom—think of them as the “nah, not today” button for the big red button that says EXPLODE.

### Turn structure
- On your turn, you draw a card and then have the option to play one or more cards from your hand to manipulate the flow of the game. This might involve forcing another player to draw additional cards, canceling someone’s effect, or steering chaos toward the next target.
- The rest of the group watches, waiting for the moment when someone makes a questionable decision about tempo and risk. It’s social deduction without the hidden identity part—unless you count the hidden agenda of “I want to be the last one standing so I can collect the bragging rights.”
- If you draw an Exploding Kitten and you don’t have a Defuse card, you’re out of that round. The drama continues with the survivors until a winner emerges. The party aspect means there’s more laughter and more whooping when someone outsmarts the room with a perfectly timed card combo.

### Strategies for big tables
- Card memory matters more than you’d think. In a larger group, you’ll see different card types more often, which means you can plan around what seems to be circulating in the center pile. Keeping track of what’s been used can help you decide whether to risk drawing or to rely on clever card plays to shuffle the deck in your favor.
- Timing is everything. In a big party, someone usually overplays a hand. The sweet spot is to keep your big, game-changing cards in your pocket until you absolutely need them. But beware: keeping too many power plays too long can backfire when someone else drops a surprise on you and you’re left with nothing but a shrug and a disappointment in card draw order.
- Read the room and the table. If your friends are competitive but also easily distracted by memes, you can lean into chaos and coax them to draw more cards when they’re not ready for it. If your crew is more chill, you may want to rely on synergy and defensive plays to hold the line.

## Strategy notes: what to aim for and what to avoid

- Prioritize Defuse cards, but don’t hoard them like dragon treasure. You’ll want to use them at the moment when the table screams for it, not when you’re fat with confidence.
- Memorize the direction of the deck and the cadence of the house rules. If someone in the group loves a particular combo, you’ll want to anticipate it and respond with a counter-strategy that buys you a few extra moments.
- Use misdirection to your advantage. A well-timed misfire or a playful bluff can cause rival players to waste their best plays on your turn, letting you slip past the most dangerous moments.
- Don’t underestimate the power of a simple “draw and pass” tactic. Sometimes, forcing someone else to draw can be more valuable than pulling off a dramatic combo yourself, especially when your own hand is light on defenses.
