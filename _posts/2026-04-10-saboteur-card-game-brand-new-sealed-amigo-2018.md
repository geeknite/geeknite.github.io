### How to Play in the Quick-and-Dirty, No-Dusseldorf-Drama Way
Saboteur is deceptively simple to learn, with a core loop that keeps showing up again and again: build a path, decide when to stop and flip the path, and deal with the person who decides that your tunnel is a work of art—though the art is mostly a hazard sign and a couple of troll faces.

- Each player starts as a dwarf, secretly assigned a role (Saboteur or Peaceful Dwarf). The Saboteurs want to derail the tunnel to the gold; the Peaceful Dwarves want to reach the treasure.
- On your turn, you draw a card and may play a card to extend the tunnel, block a tunnel, or use a special action to alter the course of events. The “build a tunnel” cards connect to other tunnel cards, forming a path from the starting point to a treasure.
- The round ends when a player reveals a treasure path that reaches the gold, or when a Saboteur successfully sabotages so that no path can reach the treasure—leading to a glorious meltdown of laughter and accusations.
- The scoring is simple: the players who connect to the treasure get points; Saboteurs get punished by rude serendipities of fate. The round resets, new roles are dealt, and the drama recommences with fresh promises of chaos.

The beauty here is how restrictions breed creativity. You’ll imagine clever tunnel layouts, maybe try to “play nice” with the Saboteurs by constructing a path that passes by a foolhardy trap in a way that seems helpful but is actually a trap. The interplay between “I think I can build something that’s too perfect to fail” and “oh boy, I just pulled the wrong card” is the magic sauce. The game’s length is friendly: typically 20-30 minutes for a full slate of players, which is perfect for a group that wants a quick dip into drama without spending all night arguing about whether the treasure is worth it.

### Setup, Playtime, and Table Etiquette
Setup is brisk: shuffle, deal roles, lay out starting tiles, and you’re ready to draft your dastardly plan. The trickiest part for first-timers is the concept of “secret roles” and how the sabotage works within the tunnel-building mechanic. A quick read-through of the rulebook, followed by a practice round, typically irons out most confusion. As players become familiar with the flow, the rounds become more about sneaky collaboration and micro-alliances than about heavy strategy. You’ll find yourself asking questions like: Do I risk extending a tunnel for a teammate’s benefit if it could empower a Saboteur later? How do I obfuscate my own role while helping everyone else move toward the gold?

Playtime is pleasantly short—roughly 20-30 minutes for a standard 3-5 player count, with slightly longer rounds for larger groups. If you’re playing with six players, you’ll likely have one or two “that was unexpected” moments per round, and that’s part of the charm. Because the deck is finite and the path-building adds verticality to the layout, every round feels dynamic rather than a stale, linear sequence.

### Strategy Guide: How to Win (Or at Least Not Be a Walking Sabotage Meme)
Here are some lightly tactical tips that emerge after a handful of plays with new players and seasoned Saboteurs alike:

- Read the room, not just your cards. Part of the fun is guessing who might be a Saboteur. If someone consistently sabotages, you may want to befriend them in the short term and pivot to a different strategy later.
- Balance risk and reward. The more tunnels you extend, the closer you get to treasure (and closer to being sabotaged). Sometimes it’s better to shore up a safe path rather than take a bold, risky move that could derail everyone.
- Card management matters. Don’t burn your best action cards too early. Save ideas for a late-game burst of tunnel-building that both helps you and catches others by surprise.
- Don’t overcommit to a single plan. Saboteurs will disrupt your best-laid tunnel if you press too hard. Keep alternatives in your mind and adapt as new cards hit the table.
- Use the environment to your advantage. In small groups, social dynamics can become the real weapon. A well-timed joke, a whispered suggestion, or a feigned disagreement can steer the table’s attention away from your true intent.
