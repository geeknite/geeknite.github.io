## How to play this edition: tips and house rules

### House rules for extra fizz
- Fizz Rule: If someone yells Fizz at the moment they play a card, everyone must take a tiny sip from a beverage of their choice before the next turn. Hydration is important, even during a stadium-level UNO brawl.
- Bottle Cap Bid: In a two-player game, whoever calls bottle cap first gets to peek at the top card of the draw pile once per round. Use this to build suspense and pretend you have a tiny psychic salesman voucher in your brain.
- Cola Rush: If you play a Draw Four, you must say a Coca-Cola slogan from the era before the next player draws. If you forget a slogan, you draw two extra and your PR team cringes in solidarity.

### Playing with standard UNO decks
The Coca-Cola edition plays cleanly with a regular UNO deck, so you can mix and match for longer game nights. The Coca-Cola visuals add a stylistic layer rather than changing the core mechanics. If you want to go authentic, keep the Coca-Cola cards to the edges and let the rest of the standard UNO cards take the center stage. This keeps the game balanced and preserves your memory of the 90s advertising blitz.

## Condition, mint value, and what to look for when you buy
If you are chasing mint condition, brand-new copies, here is how to gauge the value and verify authenticity without losing your time in anxious eBay storm:

- Box integrity: Look for crisp edges, intact shrink wrap, and no crushing marks on the box. You want the box to whisper pristine rather than scream handled luggage. A perfectly sealed set is worth a premium if the inner tray and inserts are also pristine.
- Inner contents: Ensure the deck is complete (108 cards is the standard). Check for any misprints or date stamps that align with 1998 promo cycles. Some collectors prefer original promotional inserts; if yours has one, it can add a nice bump to value.
- Card backs and faces: The Coca-Cola branding should be consistent with the era. If you see heavy factory-esque misalignments or obvious wear, that can cut the value but might still be a fun display piece.
- Authenticity signs: Some sets ship with a small COA or a promo sticker that mentions Coca-Cola. If you find a little label, that’s a good sign. If you find nothing, don’t despair — the novelty and design still carry weight as a retro item.

Estimated market vibes: sealed, pristine copies can fetch a premium among nostalgic collectors, while opened but near-mint variants still charm the right auction hunter. If you are buying for display rather than play, sealed status matters more than perfect card silvering. As with most niche promos, supply is limited and demand for the right buyer can push prices into the mid-range collector bracket. Do not expect a modern board game price tag; think more along the lines of a well-preserved vintage toy you would see in a mom-and-pop shop.
