## Synthetic rope vs steel cable

This is the part where the enthusiasts will argue in the comments about whether steel is still king. Here is a Geeknite stance: synthetic rope wins for most trail use cases. It is lighter, easier on your hands, and floats. It can fray if abused, so you want to inspect the rope regularly, especially near the hook and the drum. Steel cable has its benefits in certain heavy-duty, industrial environments, but for ATV and UTV recoveries, synthetic is typically the smarter choice.

### Pros of synthetic rope in a nutshell
- Lighter and easier to handle
- Safer to touch during hooking up and rigging
- Floats on water and sinks more gracefully than steel in mud
- Less likely to whip your friend during a sudden release

### Cons
- Susceptible to abrasion and UV degradation if left exposed
- Needs regular inspection for frays or cuts
- Can be more expensive to replace than cheap steel in some markets

Labwork has included basic protective sheathing around the rope drum and an end cap that minimizes rope slip. A well-looked-after winch rope will outlast a cheap counterpart by a surprising margin if you treat it with respect.

## Installation and mounting notes

If you are comfortable with basic tools, you can install the Labwork 4500 kit yourself. Here are the major steps without turning your garage into a disaster movie set:

1) Mount pattern: The winch uses a standard three bolt pattern. You will need a sturdy bumper or mounting plate with the right spacing. If you do not have a plate, a bar that can accept the bolts will do, but you want something strong enough to hold the load.
2) Wiring: Run the power wires from the battery to the winch, ensuring you have a proper power fuse and an inline disconnect. The last thing you want is to forget the fuse and have a small firework show in your engine bay. 
3) Rope and hook: Feed the synthetic rope through the fairlead and onto the drum, then secure the hook with a proper stopper and a safety strap to prevent injury. 
4) Remote and switch: Install the remote receiver so that you can operate it without standing directly on top of the winch. 
5) Test: With the vehicle secured, test the directions, the rope pull, and the brake. Use a calm environment for the first test to avoid unintended surprises.

Common mistakes include not securing the mounting plate properly, misrouting the wiring, and not paying attention to the rope alignment. Take your time, and do not rush the test. A little patience now saves a lot of frustration later.

## Safety and maintenance guidance

Winching can be dangerous if you do not observe the basics. Here are a few safety reminders that we endorse:

- Wear gloves when handling rope to avoid rope burn or rope burn inspired injuries
- Inspect rope regularly for frays or cuts; replace immediately if you find any damage
- Use a stable anchor point; never winch from a vehicle that could shift or roll
- Keep hands, hair, and loose clothing away from the drum
- Never exceed the rated line pull; respect the rating to avoid dangerous rope snapping
- After use, inspect the remote, the wiring harness, and the switch; make sure there is no corrosion or wear

Maintenance tips include washing off mud after use, lubricating moving parts as recommended by the manual, and storing the rope in a dry environment to prevent mildew and weakening.
