## How to Play: Core Mechanics and Round Flow
Sushi Go is a pure, elegant micro-engine of risk and reward. The Collector's Box does not rewrite the rules; it refines the experience. If you know Sushi Go, you know the drill: each round, you select one card from your hand then pass the rest to the left. You collect a hand with a variety of card types that score differently at the end of the round. The game typically plays in 15 to 20 minutes, which means a few quick rounds, a few fast decisions, and a lot of laughs as you realize your scoring trajectory is going nowhere while your friend collects three Makis and a shiny dumpling that somehow banks more points than your entire strategy.

### Core Mechanics
- Card drafting: A micro-utility that combines probability with human psychology. You want to block others from taking a card, but you also want to secure a set you know will pay off later. The Collector's Box makes this easier by keeping cards organized and easy to scan. You can quickly identify which color of sushi cards you are chasing and how many rounds remain to secure them.
- Set collection: Each sushi type has its own scoring rule: nigiri cards reward raw point values, sashimi rewards a small set when you manage to collect two or three of them, tempura rewards points for pairs, and maki credits points to the leader at the end of the round. The pudding card is the surprise floor that can sabotage your planning if you misread your opponents. In the Collector's Box, the readability helps you see these sets forming without needing an epiphany moment.
- End game scoring: The final tally is where the tension emerges. It is more satisfying if you can look across the table and see the numbers align into a satisfying orange glow of victory or a comedic flatline of defeat. Either way, the box's organization helps you keep track of these numbers in a calmer, less chaotic fashion.

### Round Flow
- Start with a hand of cards, typically seven in the base game. The Collector's Box ensures the suits and types are well-spaced visually so you can quickly glimpse your options.
- Pick one card, pass the rest. This is the moment where you practice restraint—resisting the urge to take the sassy card that your neighbor needs and instead opting for the safer pick that may not scream victory but ensures you survive the round with some structure.
- Reveal and score the round as needed. The scoring is straightforward, but the fun level skyrockets when someone makes a bold move that suddenly triggers a chain reaction of point accruals.
