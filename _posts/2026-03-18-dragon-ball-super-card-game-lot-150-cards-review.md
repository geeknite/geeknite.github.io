## What you actually get in a 150-card lot

In a typical Dragon Ball Super Card Game lot, you’ll receive 150 cards. ### Holos and sheen: The visual side

Holos are the star of the show in a lot like this. The holo pattern in DBSCG cards is often a foil overlay on the card image with a glossy finish. The effect is stunning but also tricky; glare can be unforgiving when you try to photograph your new haul for Instagram. In real life, holo cards pop at the right angle and catch light that makes your friend squint and say, "Dude, did you get the rarest one?"

### Build quality and playability

Dragon Ball Super Card Game uses a fairly standard card stock that feels slightly thicker than a typical trading card. The texture is as important as the art for shuffling and durability. In a 150-card lot, you get a mix of card types: energy, characters, events, or battle cards, depending on the set. The gaming experience hinges on how the cards are distributed; you want enough variety to build at least two viable archetypes or at least one meme deck that your group appreciates.

### Card list overview: Common, Uncommon, Rare, Secret Rare?

The distribution can be a little random, but there are patterns. A lot will contain a cluster of common and uncommon cards you’ll want to play with, along with a few rares that make the deck-building process feel like a treasure hunt. If you’re lucky, you’ll sneak a rare foil or even a Secret Rare—though the latter tends to be an event more than a given. If you’re trying to complete a collection, you’ll want to consider the value of duplicates. For a casual player, duplicates can be both a blessing and a curse: more cards to practice with, but fewer unique options for your deck.

## Value for money: price vs content

The financial part of the equation always matters, especially when you’re buying in bulk. A 150-card lot is often priced to reflect the "fun starter" aspect rather than pure collectability. ### The “lot” experience: Sorting and sleeving

Part of the joy of a large lot is the process: you sort by rarity or by character, you shower yourself in card sleeves with some cute Dragon Ball art, and you pretend you’re assembling a championship deck while also binge-watching a DB anime episode that you’ve already seen twelve times. We recommend sorting by rarity first, then by character, and finally by set or theme. It’s basically adult-living-room archaeology, except the treasure is full of cardboard superheroes.

### Practical tips for the first week

- Sleeving: Use sleek, acid-free sleeves to avoid damaging the holo foils. A good method is to sleeve the holo cards first so they don’t rub against the other cards in the deck during transport. 
- Storage: Keep the bundle in a cool, dry place; humidity can cause the foil to dull over time. 
- Sorting: Color-based sorting (Blue for water-based characters, Red for fire) can add a little flair and reduce decision fatigue at tournament time.

## Practical buying guide: what to watch for

- Condition: Card edges and holo surfaces should be free of major nicks, scratches, or warping. In a lot, you could still enjoy a good display, but the value decreases if there are serious cosmetic issues.
- Duplicates: Expect duplicates; that’s part of the design. If you’re building a deck, duplicates can be your best friend; if you’re a collector, you might want to trade duplicates for rarer cards.
- Packaging: The lot might come in a simple stack, a sealed bag, or a small box. If the packaging looks suspiciously worn or opened, you may want to ask questions about the contents and return policy.
