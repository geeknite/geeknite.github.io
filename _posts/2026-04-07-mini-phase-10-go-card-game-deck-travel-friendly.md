## How to Play (In Brief)
I won’t pretend this is a full-on rules session; that would be cruel to your patience and my keyboard. Here’s the gist of playing the Mini Phase 10 GO! Deck in a typical travel-friendly setup:

- Objective: Be the first to complete a set of predefined phases and maintain a competitive score. The exact phases can vary, but they usually involve collecting runs (consecutive cards of the same color) and sets (card groups of the same number).
- Setup: Each player is dealt a small hand. The rest forms a draw pile, with a discard pile next to it. The first player to meet the current phase’s requirement earns that phase and moves on to the next, often cycling back if you miss a beat and have to repeat a phase.
- Turns: On your turn, you draw one card from the draw pile or the discard pile, then you discard one card to end your turn. The goal is to assemble the necessary combinations to advance your phase before your opponents.
- Phases: The deck is built around phased objectives, which change as you progress. The GO! version condenses these into a travel-friendly sequence that preserves the flavor of Phase 10 while offering quicker transitions.
- Scoring: Most travel-friendly editions use a simple scoring rule: complete a phase, you earn points; the player with the lowest score by the end of a pre-determined number of rounds wins. Some variants allow negative points for late completions, which is a fun way to remind you that you should have paid more attention to the Shuttle Gate announcements.

Translation for the non-nerds: you’re trying to assemble small, manageable sets and runs, you’re dealing with a finite number of cards, and you’re racing to avoid getting stuck in a phase you can’t complete in the time you have left. It’s the same core feel as the big Phase 10, just in a micro, easy-to-pull-out-at-a-restaurant-knock-on-the-table kind of way.

## Variations and House Rules
Many groups adopt small tweaks to keep things interesting. A few common house rules you might enjoy:

- Quick fire rounds: Limit the time to complete a phase to 1-2 minutes per round. This amps up the tension, especially when you’re looking at a long layover and a looming connection.
- Pass-and-play mode: If you’ve got three players and one seat open, take turns with a shared draw/discard pile to create a more chaotic, fast-paced experience.
- Scoring twists: Add a point penalty for missed phases or extra points for completing multiple phases in a single round. It’s a nice way to add a little bite to the competition without breaking the travel vibe.

As always, keep your house rules friendly. The whole point is to have fun, not to trigger a round-robin scoring meltdown in the middle of a security checkpoint queue.
