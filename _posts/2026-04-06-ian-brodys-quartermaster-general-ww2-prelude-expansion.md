H2: What is Prelude Expansion?

Prelude is not just an add on; it is a reimagining of the early war phase, where supply routes are a muddled rumor and your platoon commander has the charisma of a stapler. The expansion introduces a prelude timeline that sets up the geopolitical climate, then tosses you into a compact, fast-paced series of scenarios that tease what the full WW2 theatre would become. The purpose is twofold: to give players a crisp, narrative entry point into the larger system, and to provide quick, replayable micro-games that can stand alone or segue into the main game.

The core rules of Quartermaster General remain intact, which means you still get simultaneous action selection, dice-driven outcomes, and the elegant chaos that happens when eight to ten players attempt to coordinate a supply chain without talking over each other. Prelude adds modules that scale the complexity up or down, depending on how much you want to bleed your brain today. If you like your wargames with a side of improv comedy, you will feel right at home.

H2: How It Changes the Base Game

Prelude is designed to be compatible with the base game, not a replacement for your favorite wrongheaded strategy that works only in a fever dream. It introduces a few deliberate changes that alter pacing, tension, and group dynamics without turning the entire system into a horned goat of complexity.

H3: Turn Structure and Pacing
One of the most noticeable changes is pacing control. Prelude scenarios are designed for shorter play sessions, but with enough decision density to keep the table from dozing off. Actions are quick, but the consequences can ripple across multiple rounds, especially when you start chaining supply events and prelude-specific triggers.

H3: New Weather and Logistics Layer
The prelude adds a light weather/logistics overlay that models how environmental quirks impact supply lines. Think rain that slows movement, port delays, and the occasional occupation of a critical crossroads by a disgruntled merchant fleet. It is thematic, thematic means spice, and spice means you won’t remember why you started the scenario, only that you had fun dodging supply failures with a grin.

H2: Thematic and Mechanic Deep Dive

This is where Prelude truly earns its keep. Ian Brody has a knack for weaving narrative into mechanics without sacrificing the crunchy bits that seasoned players crave. The prelude expansion leans into a few signature ideas:

H3: Supply Chains as a Character
In the base game, supply lines are abstracted. Prelude turns them into something that resembles a slow-motion board game about moving crates through a city block. The new tokens and paths require players to think about what is being moved, where it originates, and how delays ripple through the theatre. The effect on strategy is profound: you start planning not just for immediate reinforcement, but for how your supply chain will look two turns from now.

H3: Event Deck as Narrative Engine
Prelude introduces a modular event deck that can swing the mood from tense to absurd in a heartbeat. The events are varied, skewed toward historical plausibility but with enough room for your group to improvise, banter, and blame the dice for your own strategic misfires.

H3: Morale and Reputation Tracks
A light morale system adds social pressure to performance. Players can gain +1 morale for timely deliveries, but lose morale for overcommitting resources, leading to deliciously awkward moments where a single decision could tip a whole camp into chaos or calm, depending on how the table feels about it.
