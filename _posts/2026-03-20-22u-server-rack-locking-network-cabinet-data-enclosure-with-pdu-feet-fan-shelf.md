### Dimensions and fit
This unit is typically designed around standard 19-inch equipment, which means you can mount most servers, switches, patch panels, and network appliances with ease. The 22U height is a sweet spot for small to mid-sized deployments: enough space for a handful of devices, a PDU, and a couple of shelves, but not so tall that you’re crawling on a ladder to install a new patch panel. Depth is a frequent sore point in 19-inch enclosures; the standard options hover around 600 mm (23.6 in) to 800 mm (31.5 in). The exact depth varies by model, but the common choice tends toward 600–700 mm for balance between airflow, cable management, and rack space. If you’re planning to cram bulk cabling or require front-to-back airflow with hot spots, double-check the depth measurements against your gear and cable management strategy.

### Security features
Locking doors are an obvious feature, but the quality of the lock matters. A cheap cam lock will fail when confronted with a determined intern; a decent cam lock with a hardened barrel makes it harder for prying hands. The locking system on a 22U enclosure is designed to deter casual access and provide a path toward a formal security process. Some cabinets also offer keyed rear doors, which is a nice touch if you want to restrict access while still allowing some technicians to work from the back.

### Cable management and airflow
Cable management is where a lot of 19-inch enclosures either shine or crumble. A cabinet’s value climbs dramatically when it helps you route cables cleanly rather than leaving them to aggressively tangle behind the rails. This 22U model typically includes vertical cable channels with adjustable doors and pass-through holes grommeted to keep dust out and airflow relatively unobstructed. The rails are generally perforated or have perforations in the doors to improve heat dissipation. If your gear runs hot, you want to ensure the cabinet either supports internal fans or has vents that won’t starve the hardware of fresh air.

Speaking of fans, many 22U enclosures come with one or two 120 mm or 80 mm fans. The objective isn’t to blast cold air at your servers like an industrial air cannon, but to keep hot air from pooling around the hottest components. If you’re running high-density gear or blades, you may still want to add aftermarket fans or a dedicated cooling strategy. Remember: the best fan is the one you don’t hear screaming in the middle of the night because it’s doing its job without becoming the star of a white-noise playlist.

## PDU, power, and energy sanity

The integrated PDU simplifies power distribution and power-on organization. In most packages, you’ll get:

- A handful of IEC outlets (C13 or C19, depending on region and model)
- A power switch or control feature (sometimes with a resettable breaker)
- A cable to the main power source
- Optional data monitoring (in more expensive dashboards)

For a home lab or a small business rack, this is a major convenience. It helps you avoid daisy-chaining power strips and reduces the risk of overloading a single outlet. If you’re planning to expand with more devices, check the PDU’s outlet count and whether it supports individual circuit protection for critical gear.

## Shelves and accessories

Shelves are a godsend when you’re not entirely sure what goes where. The 22U cabinet usually ships with one or two adjustable shelves. These shelves let you mount non-rack-mount equipment like a modest NAS box, test equipment, or small switches that don’t fit 19-inch rails. Cable management arms, tie-down bars, and extra grommets are also welcome additions. The more you can route wires cleanly and separate power from data cables, the happier your network will be. A neat enclosure reduces the risk of accidental disconnections and makes maintenance much faster.

## Installation and first boot

Setting up a 22U cabinet is a multi-step but straightforward process:

1. Position the cabinet where you want it and lock the casters if you’ll be leaving it stationary. 
2. Attach the mounting rails to your desired depth. 19-inch equipment slides into place with standard hardware. 
3. Install any shelves before you start mounting devices so you don’t block access to the rails. 
4. Mount your rack-mable devices starting from the bottom to the top, to optimize weight distribution. Heavier devices go lower; lighter devices go higher. 
5. Route cables through the vertical channels and ensure you’re not pinching power cables with data cables. 
6. Attach the PDU and plug into a proper power outlet. 
7. Close doors, lock them, and do a quick test: boot up gear and observe airflow. 
8. Tag cables and keep a simple map to ease future maintenance. 

The first boot is always a little ceremonial. You’ll run through cat-5 vs cat-6 vs cat-6a debates in your head while your server does its thing. The cabinet makes this process smoother than a DIY cable spaghetti experiment. The locking doors keep the drama out and the prying eyes away, which is particularly valuable in shared spaces or small offices where someone might move a patch cable out of curiosity and cause an unexpected outage.

## A few caveats and comparisons

No product is perfect, and a 22U enclosure is no exception. Common pain points to watch for include:

- Depth: some wide devices or thick cable bundles might push the limit of how much you can physically fit behind front rails. Always measure your gear and compare to internal depth.
- Ventilation: depending on your environment, the cabinet may require additional cooling or fans. If your gear runs hot, consider upgrading the fans or adding a hood/ducting solution to improve airflow.
- Noise: fans, especially in closed spaces with poor ventilation, can create noticeable white noise. If your workspace is shared or quiet, factor this into your planning.
