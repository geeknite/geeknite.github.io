## Safety and Parental Controls: What You Need to Know
Safety is the quiet star of this theater. The enclosure net and the padded poles are designed to reduce the risk of falls and scrapes. Here are the key safety points you should keep in mind:

- Age and weight guidelines: The manufacturer typically recommends a specific weight limit and uses guidelines for age. Do not exceed those limits. Toddlers grow quickly, but the plastic balls the kit includes do not grow with them, so you may have to rehome some of the extraneous equipment after a year or two.
- Netting and door: The zipper should stay closed during use. Teach toddlers to hold the zipper when entering or exiting to avoid accidental pinching. A little parental oversight goes a long way.
- Ground surface: Place the trampoline on a flat, non-slip surface. If you have uneven ground, use leveling tools or a sturdy mat to minimize wobble. A wobbly trampoline is a stage for dramatic pratfalls, and nobody wants a dramatic pratfall in the middle of a play session that is supposed to be cute and educational.
- Surrounding space: Ensure there is ample clearance around the trampoline to avoid collisions with furniture or walls. The best play spaces feel like a sanctuary for bouncing rather than a gladiator arena. Keep a small play zone nearby for dolls, plush animals, or imaginary villains that threaten the peace of the living room.
- Supervision: There is no substitute for adult supervision. Toddlers learn to bounce with reckless abandon, and the adults must provide steady guidance and quick judgment. It is a lot like adulting, but noisier and more colorful.

## Safety Reminders and Practical Play Tips
- Always supervise sessions. Toddlers bounce with the precision of a tiny meteorologist predicting impact, which means a watchful adult can prevent many accidents.
- Use the zipper door as the primary entry and exit method. Teach the toddler how to use it properly to avoid pinched fingers.
- Keep the play sessions short and fun. Toddlers have limited endurance and can get overwhelmed if the bouncing goes on too long. Short bursts of play with enthusiastic breaks work best.
- Regularly inspect the frame, legs, springs, and net. Look for rust, loose bolts, or sagging netting. If something looks off, address it before the next session.
- Create a rotation of activities around the trampoline to prevent overuse injuries and to keep the toddler engaged. Jumping is great, but mixing in other activities like building blocks or soft obstacle courses can round out the play experience.
