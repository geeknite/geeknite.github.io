## How to play UNO Junior
### Setup and basic rules
- Each player starts with a handful of cards, and the goal is to discard all of them.
- You can play a card that matches either color or a symbol on the card on the discard pile.
- If you can’t play, you draw a card from the draw pile.
- The game ends when a player has one card left and calls out Uno—assuming Uno is a term your household approves without a small preflight discussion.

### Kid-friendly twists that actually help learning
UNO Junior swaps out some of the adult-level complexity with simple prompts that help children recognize patterns and sequences. Expect emphasis on color naming, simple counting, and the satisfaction of finishing a round. The simplified playstyle reduces argument potential and keeps the game moving, which means fewer parent brain farts about how many cards you should draw and more time for high-fives and celebratory dances. You get the fun of UNO without needing a degree in game theory or a medical dictionary for kids’ tantrums.

### Pacing and turn-taking strategies
For kids, timing is everything. The fast pace means you cycle through a lot of color exposure in a short window. Adults can lean into the role of enthusiastic cheerleader, which is basically the best part of any family game night. Encourage your little one to name colors aloud as they play, which doubles as a learning exercise and a way to keep them engaged in real time. When in doubt, clap on the two-pull plays and celebrate the tiny strategic glimmers with a thunderous high-five that might shake the couch cushions loose.

### When to choose UNO Junior over classic UNO
