## New Cards and Mechanics
This expansion introduces new card types and keywords that broaden your tactical toolkit without making every game feel like a clone of the last one. Expect a handful of fresh concepts that push you to rethink resource and threat management:
- Symbiote Attachments: Personal upgrades that attach to Venom (and possibly allied heroes under the right conditions) to unlock temporary buffs and new interactions. Maintain pressure while shielding the attachment from encounter-based removals.
- Host Upgrades: Encounter-side or quest-specific upgrades that tilt the balance and invite you to trigger a ‘host’ event for extra value.
- Aggressive Trump Cards: Big-punch cards that shine when you’ve met timing conditions. They reward memory and careful sequencing more than sheer luck.

From a design standpoint, the symbiote system feels thematic and mechanically coherent. You’re not simply upgrading your hero for bigger numbers; you’re cultivating a micro-ecosystem on the table that grows, interacts, and occasionally betrays you in the most entertaining way possible. The new cards sustain Marvel Champions’ identity while injecting a fresh tempo that shakes up your usual rhythm.

## Deck-building and Strategy Notes
When you start building decks around Venom, you’ll want to balance how much you lean on symbiote mechanics versus your core hero toolkit. Here are practical tips that helped our group thrive (and occasionally crash in glorious fashion):
- Tempo over brute force: Venom rewards you for hitting momentum turning points. Don’t waste a big payoff early unless you’re confident you can sustain pressure afterward.
- Multirole coordination: In a four-player game, designate a primary board cleaner, a secondary threat responder, and a support deck for the rest. The symbiote layer can amplify smaller decisions into meaningful outcomes if chained well.
- Resource pacing: Don’t hoard cards for a single “big turn.” Venom’s identity rewards continued pressure rather than a one-turn wipe. Draw when you can, spend when it hurts to wait for the next round.
- Edge-case planning: Some of Venom’s best moments come from reading the table and exploiting the right vulnerability window. Discuss timing with your team and practice your best “still sane after all this” plan in friendly games first.

A few deck archetypes to try:
- Fast draw + tempo push: Pair Venom’s signature cards with allies or control cards that accelerate your draw. Build a rhythm that culminates in a satisfying, heavy punch.
- Hybrid control: Mix precise removal with threat-countering counters. Clear threats while keeping pressure on the boss.
- Symbiote-forward aggression: Lean into the core mechanic—attachments unlock strong on-curve upgrades and cash in for a decisive finish.

In practice, Venom isn’t a walk in the park — it’s a hero pack that rewards experimentation. If you like small, well-timed swings that feel earned, Venom could become a favorite. If you prefer safe, predictable turns, you’ll still get some tasty moments but fewer jaw-dropping, table-flipping ones.
