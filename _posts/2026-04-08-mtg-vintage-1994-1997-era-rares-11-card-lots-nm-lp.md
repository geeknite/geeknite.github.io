## What to look for in photos and descriptions
The photos don’t lie—most of the time they exaggerate, but they also reveal honest truth. Here’s what to scrutinize when you’re evaluating a listing like this.

### Card condition markers to sanity-check
- Corners: Are they crisp? Any softening or chipping? In NM cards, you should see almost no cupping or whitening near the corners.
- Edges: Watch for color bleeding along the edges or light whitening along the bevels. In LP cards, mild whitening or edge wear is common; in NM, you want clean edges.
- Surface: Look for scratches, dings, or scuffs that would suggest heavy handling. A few minor surface flaws can still land a card in NM, but major scratches can drop value fast.
- Centering: Vintage cards often have imperfect centering due to older production standards. If a lot claims NM and you see obvious bias toward one edge, that’s a red flag.
- Foiling and gloss: Some rare cards of the era used foil or heavy gloss. Foil wear or color shifts can dramatically change the appearance and evaluation of a card.

### Authenticity and reprints
The 1994–1997 period is notorious for counterfeit attempts, especially on high-demand rares. When a listing includes high-demand cards, you should demand clear photos of the card back, borders, and any set-specific hallmarks. If you’re new to this, consider asking for close-up shots of the face and the watermarks (if applicable) or even a video to verify edge wear. Reputable sellers will respond promptly with multiple images and, ideally, a short video.

### The “NM/LP” label: what it actually means in practice
- NM (Near Mint) is a tough standard for a lot of 1990s cards because many survived casual play but not the tournament grind. The difference between NM and LP can be subtle: a hairline wrinkle, a slight whitening at a corner, or very tiny edge wear can flip the grade. If you’re buying licensed NM/LP from a credible source, you should expect a photos-and-accuracy guarantee.
- LP (Light Played) often accepts more pronounced edge whitening and minor surface flaws. If the listing emphasizes NM/LP, you should press for detailed photos to differentiate between the two.

## How to evaluate value without a calculator in hand
- Compare absolute price points for NM/LP versions across multiple marketplaces. A single card sometimes drives a lot’s value more than the rest combined, but the 11-card ensemble matters too.
- Look for duplicate sets or common print runs. A mix of Alpha/Beta and Unlimited from the same era can influence pricing because of misprints, border variations, and collector demand.
- Check for condition variance among cards. If several cards are near-mint with sharp corners while others show whitening, you may want to negotiate a price that reflects the distribution rather than just the overall label.
- Consider the “presentability” factor: does the lot look nice as a piece of display nostalgia? Some buyers are more willing to pay for display quality than for raw playability, especially when dealing with aesthetic cards from the early era.

## Packaging, shipping, and authenticity tips
- Ask for a robust shipment plan. Vintage cards are fragile, and a single bumpy ride can turn a well-graded lot into a disaster. Insist on rigid backing, bubble wrap, and tracking. If you see harsh packaging language in the listing, consider it a red flag.
- Request hi-res photos of each card. The more angles you see, the less you’ll rely on a seller’s description and your own memory. Prefer sellers who offer short videos or time-stamped photos to prove the condition and presence of the card.
- Require a return window for authenticity. If a card is misrepresented or you suspect a counterfeit, having a return option protects your investment.
