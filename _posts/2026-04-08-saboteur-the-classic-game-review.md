## Introduction
Saboteur is one of those pocket-sized social experiments that somehow ended up being a cult classic despite not taking itself too seriously. On the surface it looks like a cute dwarven mini-dungeon crawl, but underneath it is a brisk, sly little game about trust, deception, and the unreliable compass of human intention. If you have ever heard the words trust no one and yet still wanted to exchange your friends for a handful of gold coins, Saboteur will feel like a perfect fit.

The base idea is delightfully simple: players are dwarves who either dig for gold or sabotages the path to it. Some players are secretly on the saboteur side, while others are honest gold diggers trying to reach a treasure card. The twist is that you only know your own role, not your neighbors’ roles, which means every turn becomes a little improv show where you decide whether to lay a path card that helps your crew or a path card that helps your saboteur buddy sabotage the tunnel without you realizing it.

If you like quick games that fit on a coffee table, never require a referee and somehow generate enough drama to fill a tavern, then Saboteur is likely to earn a soft spot in your heart. It is the kind of game you can teach in five minutes, play in 15, and still spend an hour arguing with someone about whether that last card should have been played or saved for the next round. It is also a frequent guest at game nights where the table talk matters more than the actual math, which is exactly where the Geeknite vibe lives.

## What is Saboteur and How It Works
### The premise in a paragraph
Saboteur places players into tiny mining teams. Each dwarf starts with a role card that is known only to that player. Some players will be Saboteurs — they are secretly sabotaging the tunnel to prevent others from reaching the gold. The rest are Gold Diggers — their quiet mission is to construct a functioning tunnel that leads to at least one treasure card. The game ends after a certain number of rounds or when a treasure is revealed and the path to it has been sufficiently connected. The societal contract here is simple yet deliciously dangerous: you must decide who to trust as you lay cards, but you can never be sure of anyone’s true motive.

### The components
A standard Saboteur deck features a mix of path cards that extend the tunnel, action cards that let you peek at or reshuffle things, and role cards that declare sabotage or digging intent. On the table you’ll arrange a small network of paths from the starting point to a treasure card, with the goal of aligning tunnels that lead to gold while preventing foes from collapsing the entire operation. The art is cheerful, the iconography clear, and the overall package fits comfortably in a mid-sized box that you can tote to any living room, office break room, or chaotic gaming night at a friend’s house.

### The setup that tests your life choices
At the outset, each player draws a role card and one starting hand. The role cards are the spicy meat of this sandwich: some dwarves will be revealed as saboteurs, others as gold diggers, and a few might be ambiguous enough to cause existential dread. The board is simple: a starting point, a couple of goal cards that indicate treasure, and a deck of path cards. The trick is to pick a path that ties your team to a treasure while avoiding dead ends that give Saboteurs a perfect opportunity to sabotage without being visible.

## How to Play: The Turn by Turn Flow
### Roles and secrets
Saboteur rewards misdirection but punishes outright reveal. The Saboteur players want to keep their identity hidden until a perfect moment appears to derail the line of sight to gold. The Gold Diggers must carefully infer who might be sabotaging while contributing paths that will eventually connect to treasure. The tension is baked into the very first hand you draw as someone may be secretly plotting against you in a way that only becomes obvious when your tunnel collapses in a spectacular cascade of 2D cardboard misfortune.

### The flow of a turn
Each turn in Saboteur involves playing a path card or an action card. Path cards extend the tunnel toward a treasure, bend the corridor, or occasionally block a potential path a bit too realistically for a fantasy dwarven cave. Action cards allow you to break or reshuffle the deck, peek at another player’s role, or force a discard that might rescue a collapsing plan or ruin someone else’s strategy. When a treasure is revealed, the round ends, and scoring is done. Scores tilt in favor of those who made it to treasure, with the Saboteurs trying to reach treasure for their own goals while minimizing exposure of their identity.

### The social deduction engine
This is the heart of Saboteur. The mere act of laying a card can be a form of communication. You signal intent through movement, not explicit statements, which means you can be honest, humble, and yet still the saboteur wearing a poker face. You learn as you play that trust is a fickle currency; it can buy you a single turn, but it cannot guarantee a clean victory. The social aspect is what keeps people returning again and again, even after they have been the saboteur for the fourth time and swore they would never do it again … until the next game night when the group hands you the same role and the cycle repeats.

## The Player Experience: Laughter, Suspicion, and How It Feels to Be a Dwarf
### A game that thrives on personality
Saboteur shines when the room is loud and lively. The moments when a player explains their latest path to the treasure with a confident grin, only for another player to abruptly flip a card that renders that path a dead end, are the moments that give the game its teeth. It is a party game disguised as a light strategy title. It invites playful deception, banter about who you would trust with your life if your life were literally a cardboard tunnel and a treasure waiting at the end of it.

### The tension curve
The early rounds are optimistic; everyone has ideas and almost no one has a complete picture of who is who. Mid game, a couple of saboteur steps become obvious as certain paths derail unexpectedly. Late game, you are either sprinting toward a treasure with a chorus of accusations and defense or you are frantically repairing a tunnel that has just collapsed in glorious fashion. Either way, the room tends to be louder and more animated than when you started, and that is a win in a social game designed to keep you talking even when the music stops and the snacks are gone.

## Strategy and Meta Considerations
### Skills you can actually apply without calculus anxiety
- Reading the table: Observe who is contributing to a path that looks suspiciously likely to reach a treasure and who suddenly discards a critical move. If someone sabotages a neat route that would have quickly led to gold, their motive becomes a little clearer.
- Timing your sabotage: Saboteurs often wait for a moment when the room is distracted by a flaming hot debate about which path to take. A perfectly timed sabotage can derail the round without exposing your identity. The trick is to understand when it is safe to take a risk and when to lay low.
- Card economy: In many rounds you will face decisions about discarding cards and preserving options for a better route. Saboteurs love to force others to burn through their hands while keeping their own options artificially open.
- Trust taxonomy: Not all gold diggers are naive. Some players learn to hedge their bets by aligning with different players on different little corridors, creating a micro-alliances that might break apart the moment the treasure card is revealed.

### Common missteps and how to avoid them
- Over-sharing your intent: If you keep explaining your moves with a heroic monologue, you will quickly give away your role or make others suspicious for no reason beyond drama. Instead of announcing your plans, let your actions speak and save the confidences for when they count.
- Over-optimizing routes too early: A tunnel that looks perfect on turn two can become a trap on turn five if the saboteurs patiently wait for their moment. It is often better to take a flexible, multi-path approach rather than building a single glorious golden staircase to glory.
- Ignoring social cues: Saboteur is as much about people as it is about cards. If you ignore the vibe in the room, you may miss a crucial signal that someone is about to flip the table on you with a last-minute sabotage card.

## Practical Tips for Beginners
- Start with a transparent mind, not a transparent mouth: be mindful that you cannot reveal your role. Let others draw conclusions about you from your moves, not your commentary.
- Read the table before rushing forward: a group that immediately starts sprinting toward treasure may be hiding Saboteurs who are trying to force a rush decision.
- Distribute risk: when you have to discard cards, think about future rounds. You want to keep options open for later turns rather than burning your best moves early.
- Use early information to steer, not to punish: if you gain knowledge about another player’s role, use it to influence future teamwork rather than to claim victory or burn bridges that you might need later.
- Keep the laughter going: even when the tunnel collapses, the social aspect remains the real treasure. A good joke about why your path looked perfectly clear but led to a cave-in can salvage any tough round.
