## Assembly: how hard is it to put together?
You’ll typically get a set of pre-drilled holes, hex screws, a small screwdriver or hex key, and a simple manual. If you’ve ever assembled a bookshelf that’s more procedural drama than assembly, you’ll survive this with minimal swearing. The most important tips:

- Start with the base pieces first. Attach the legs to the sofa frame, then to the arms. Verify alignment before tightening fully.
- Use a little wood glue on the end grain if you’re worried about looseness; but don’t overdo it or you’ll glue your fingers to the furniture—trust me, this is not the bonding time you want.
- Tighten screws in a cross pattern to avoid warping. Don’t over-tighten; you’ll strip the wood or snap a screw head, and that’s a speed-run to “rebuild the set.”

Most sets can be assembled within 60-90 minutes by one able-bodied person with a little patience. ## Durability and weather resistance: will it survive patrons and pets?
Durability in outdoor furniture depends on a few things: wood density, protective finishes, and how often you neglect to cover the set during rain. Acacia wood is relatively resilient; it offers better moisture resistance than softwoods like pine and relatively good stability if kept dry during heavy rains. The protective sealant helps with UV resistance too, which means your color won’t fade into a pale memory after two summers.

- Exposure to sun: Expect some color change as the wood ages; it’s a natural process. Regular cleaning and re-sealing will slow down the aging curve and keep the wood looking vibrant.
- Rain and humidity: The wood expands and contracts with moisture changes. If you let water pool on the surface, you increase the risk of ring marks or shared damp time with cushions. Use a breathable cover during prolonged rain or winter storage.
- Pests: Acacia is not a magnet for insects in the same way certain tropical woods are. Still, it’s wise to inspect for signs of wood-boring insects and maintain a light protective layer to deter critters.

One note: metal hardware should be corrosion-resistant. If you see any rust on exposed screws, replace them with stainless-steel equivalents sooner rather than later. A little proactive maintenance prevents your set from looking like a medieval ruin after a harsh winter.

## Maintenance and care: simple routines that pay off
Maintenance is not glamorous, but it’s the secret sauce of keeping your wood looking like it does on day one. Here are a few practical routines:

- Clean regularly with a mild soap solution and a soft cloth. Avoid harsh chemicals that strip the sealant.
- Inspect annually for loose joints and tighten as needed. Winter is a good time to perform a quick check-up.
- Re-seal every 1-2 years, depending on exposure. A light coat of sealant protects the grain and reduces drying cracks.
- Cushion care: store cushions indoors when not in use or use weatherproof covers. Quick-drying fabrics will save you the heartbreak of mildew after a surprise rain shower.

The goal is to keep the wood looking warm and inviting, not to turn it into a museum piece. Your grass will thank you for furniture that’s comfortable, functional, and not a hazard to your evening rituals.

## Comparisons: wood options and why acacia earns its keep
If you’re evaluating this set against teak, eucalyptus, or resin-based options, here’s a quick mental model:

- Teak: A premium wood that ages beautifully and can be extremely durable with proper maintenance. Costs more upfront but might reward you with longer life and a richer patina.
- Eucalyptus: A more affordable hardwood that can look great but may require a little more maintenance to prevent surface checking and color fading.
- Resin or synthetics: Great for ultra-low maintenance and light weight. They may not capture that warm wood aesthetic or the “wood that has character” vibe.

Acacia sits in the middle ground: durable enough for outdoor use, visually appealing, and more budget-friendly than teak. Its grain offers a natural warmth that blends nicely with greenery and outdoor textiles. If your goal is to create a space that feels inviting rather than showroom-perfect, acacia is a solid bet.
