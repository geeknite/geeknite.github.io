## Exodus in context: what the set tries to do

Exodus came on the heels of strong mono and multi colored themes and offered a sandbox where synergy mattered more than raw bomb power. The design philosophy of Exodus emphasized card advantage stacking, mana acceleration, and flexible answers that could hinge on a single line of text to swing a game. It was a set that rewarded you for knowing your 1cc to 4cc windows, your tempo flips, and the subtle differences between a 2/2 body and a 3/3 body with a relevant ability.

From a collecting standpoint, Exodus is also a historically interesting set because its commons and uncommons are often the most overlooked but still carry significant playability in casual formats. A lot of people build what we might call a “nearly complete” commons and uncommons deck to get a playable, affordable, and aesthetically satisfying subset without diving into the rarities that cost a small mortgage. The Nearly Complete Common Uncommon Set is about quality over quantity, efficiency over flash, and humor over despair when you open your eighth duplicate of a certain red common that somehow keeps showing up in your unsleeved binder like a persistent, flame-willed beetle.

For those new to the terminology, commons are the first layer of a set, typically cheap and mass-distributed. Uncommons sit above them, with better effects or more powerful abilities. In Exodus, these two classes include a surprising number of iconic support cards that enable players to build playable archetypes without needing top-tier mythics. If you’re trying to craft a budget-ready, environment-friendly deck that still has bite, the commons and uncommons are your best friends—and sometimes your worst nightmare when you discover you’ve drafted 17 green two-drops and realized you only built a red deck. It happens.

## The Nearly Complete Common Uncommon Set: why this subset matters

The concept of a Nearly Complete Common Uncommon Set is not about perfection; it is about practical completeness. You want enough variety to play a wide range of games, enough consistency to actually win matches, and enough control to avoid diving into the expensive rares and mythics that define at least one corner of the ecosystem. Here is why commons and uncommons make sense as the centerpiece:

- Affordability: Commons and uncommons are the most accessible cards for new collectors and budget players. You can assemble a cohesive deck and a satisfying collection without breaking the bank.
- Playability: In many older sets like Exodus, a well-chosen group of commons and uncommons can cover multiple roles: early game pressure, removal, protection, and mid to late game finishers, depending on your color mix. The right mix of these cards can deliver a surprisingly robust experience in casual formats and cube-esque environments.
- Collectibility: There is a lot of charm in the card art and the flavor of late 90s MTG. Completing the commons and uncommons gives you the sense of finishing a puzzle rather than simply hoarding the shiny bits on the top shelf.
- Deck-building discipline: Focusing on these two rarities teaches discipline. You’ll learn to spot value in card draw, utility creatures, and tempo plays that often get overlooked when you chase expensive rares.

That said, obtaining a Nearly Complete Common Uncommon Set is not a free ride. There are stumbles along the path: print runs with odd distributions, reprint cycles that change value, and the occasional card that becomes an under-the-radar staple in a specific casual format. The trick is to recognize the archetypal lanes on Exodus: red burn and aggression, blue control with countermagic and bounce, green ramp with big bodies, and white weenie strategies that sometimes outcrypt your opponent’s hand. The commons sit at the crossroads of these lanes, offering flexible options rather than a single blueprinted path.

## Card categories you are likely to chase

Rather than listing every names, which would make your binder feel like a 90s phone book, here is a useful framework for thinking about the kinds of commons and uncommons that typically loom large in Exodus collections:

- Early game pressure: Creatures with efficient stats for their mana cost, allowing you to develop the board while your opponent is still tapping lands. These are the backbone of a speedy, aggressive approach and often appear as versatile two-drops that scale well into the midgame.
- Removal and disruption: Uncommons that answer threats with minimal mana or that enable you to fight back when your opponent tries to accelerate. This category often includes efficient removal spells and targeted disruption with decent splash damage to the board.
- Card advantage and filtering: Draw spells and search effects that keep your hand full when you’re trying to sprint through your deck. In Exodus, a number of uncommons are designed to slightly tilt the balance toward better card selection without breaking the bank.
- Utility creatures: Creatures that have abilities beyond their raw stats, such as card drawing on death, healing, or small anthem effects. These cards create the subtle engine that can power a long game plan.
- Multicolor and synergy enablers: Commons and uncommons that let you build cross-color strategies, or that enable two or three card combos. These are the hidden gems, the cards that show their teeth once you’ve assembled the right board state.

When you start focusing on these buckets, you’ll quickly see that a nearly complete set is less about a precise card count and more about ensuring you can reliably draft or play a few solid archetypes that survive the test of casual play.

### A note on archetypes you might chase in Exodus

In Exodus, as in many sets of its era, you’ll find the heart of several archetypes hiding among the commons and uncommons. A typical budget build might emphasize a tempo or aggro path with efficient little creatures and cheap removal. Another common path is a midrange or control shell that leans on a handful of uncommons to stabilize the game, draw more cards, or shut down certain strategies. The key is not to go overboard with a single color or strategy but to pick a color trio that gives you reliable playlines from turn 1 through turn 7 or 8, with a couple of finishers that can end the game before your opponent sees the top of their deck again.

For those who love color-splashed decks, Exodus commons tend to reward you for experimenting with splashy options in multiple colors without chasing the expensive mythics. The result is a collection that feels rich and varied, even when the price tag stays pleasantly non-epic.

## Collecting advice: how to assemble a nearly complete set on a budget

Here are practical steps to build your Nearly Complete Common Uncommon Exodus set without turning your living room into a cardboard zoo:

- Start with a goal: Decide on a target number of distinct commons and uncommons. A practical starting point is to aim for 90–110 distinct cards that cover your primary archetypes, plus 15–25 useful duals or utility cards that bridge color gaps.
- Build your color skeleton first: Choose 2–3 colors you want to primarily support and gather basic early drops, removal, and card draw in those colors. Then add in a few flex picks from the remaining colors to ensure you have answers to common strategies.
- Prioritize condition and consistency: For commons and uncommons, you can save money by selecting near mint or mint cards in the core pieces, but don’t get hung up on flawless copies of every card. A light playset with steady condition often works just fine for casual play.
- Use multicolored synergy as a guide: Exodus has a fair amount of cross-color synergy. If you find a good typical 3-color combination, gather several commons and uncommons that support that triad rather than chasing a dozen cards that don’t interact.
- Leverage swaps and trades: Trading is a huge part of collecting. If you’re stuck on a particular card, offer a trade that covers multiple complementary pieces. A curated swap can jumpstart your set completion without burning a hole in your budget.
- Consider alternative printings: Exodus has seen reprint cycles and later sets reusing similar card names. You can often substitute a similar commons or uncommons from later sets that share the same functions at a lower price point. It’s not strictly authentic to 1998, but it keeps the gameplay intact.
- Condition checks before purchase: When buying used or vendors online, ask for gatefold pictures, ask about surface wear on edges, and whether any creases affect readability. A well-described card in good condition is a gem even when the price is modest.
- Track your progress: A simple checklist helps huge. Use a notebook or a small digital sheet to track which commons and uncommons you’ve secured and which ones remain elusive. The satisfaction you get from crossing off a few dozen lines is unmatched.

## Practical card considerations: what to look for in Exodus commons and uncommons

When you comb through the long list of common and uncommon cards in Exodus, you will encounter different classes of utility. Here are a few practical guidelines to help you choose well:

- Early creatures are your tempo enablers. They set up a game state where you’re on track to apply pressure every turn. Look for creatures with efficient power and toughness relative to their mana cost, ideally with some extra effect that stacks later (like a small protector or a tiny ping on your opponent’s board).
- Removal that is cheap and targeted matters more than you think. In the common and uncommon space, removing a key blocker or breaking through a stalemate can win games, especially in a format where topdecking can swing outcomes for multiple turns.
- Card advantage matters. A draw spell that draws two on turn three can translate into a win if you can keep your hand topped off. Filters that help you find your finishers at the right moment are priceless for a Nearly Complete Set.
- Utility is king. Cards with extra effects beyond raw stats—like adding mana, enabling a combo, or stealing a function from your opponent’s board—add a lot of value to a budget deck.
- Synergy across colors. Exodus supports cross-color synergy reasonably well. If you can anchor a family of cards that play nicely across two or three colors, you’ll be able to adapt to whatever your opponent brings to the table.

## Market reality: price, availability, and value retention

In the wild world of MTG, prices for Exodus commons and uncommons can be surprisingly volatile depending on the local meta and online marketplaces. What you’ll often see is that the most useful commons in terms of deckbuilding endurance retain value, while some uncommons that look spicy on paper fade into the background once the next nostalgia cycle hits. Your best bet for a Nearly Complete Set is to pick long-term value pieces that you’ll actually use in casual play rather than chasing a handful of marquee uncommons that you may rotate out of your deck in a few months.

If you approach the market with patience, you’ll discover that completing a strong set is less about scoring a single lottery card and more about a curated path: a handful of reliable playables here, a few flexible duals there, and a couple of green ramp cards to keep your mana curve honest. You’ll still have moments of pure triumph when you find a sleeper common that completely changes a match, but those moments feel earned rather than bought, which is the whole point of the exercise.

## The deck building angle: how a Nearly Complete Common Uncommon Set shines on the table

A well-chosen Exodus commons and uncommons deck can be surprisingly resilient. Here is how you can leverage your set to build decks that feel intentional and colorful without requiring the entire spectrum of the set:

- Tempo-based aggro shells: Use efficient early creatures with inherent synergy plus low-cost removal to pressure opponents. The goal is to win by turn four or five, not to slog through a six-turn grind that costs more than your rent.
- Midrange parity: For players who enjoy trading cards for tempo back and forth, uncommons that offer card draw or small wheel effects can keep your engine humming while you apply pressure with your more aggressive commons.
- Utility-based control-lite: You can include cheap counters, bounce effects, and targeted disruption to manage the flow of the game while your few finishers close things out.
- Multicolor experiments: If you’re brave, you can build a three-color shell that uses a handful of cross-color commons and uncommons. The payoff is a deck that feels cohesive and dynamic, even if you don’t own the rare gold mines of the era.

When you test these ideas in a local game night or in a casual league, you’ll notice the difference between a set built around a handful of flashy rares and a set built around predictable, repeatable interactions. The latter is not only more satisfying but also less stressful on your wallet, which is a nice bonus.
