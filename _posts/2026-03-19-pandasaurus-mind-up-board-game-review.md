## How to Play
Mind Up blends memory and speed into a fast paced round structure. On each turn a player reveals a sequence of clues from the top of the deck, and players race to replicate the sequence using their own set of tokens. The trick is that you can only use tokens in a limited order, which forces players to pay attention and plan ahead. Miss a step and you risk losing tempo, which is the currency of these rounds.

### Turn Structure
- Reveal a small sequence of clues from the deck
- All players study the sequence for a short period
- On signal, players simultaneously place tokens according to their memory of the sequence
- Correct sequences earn focus tokens and advance along the brain track
- Mistakes cost tokens and may push you back on the track

The tension comes from the simultaneous action and the need to read not just your own memory but to infer what others remember. There is a friendly bluffing element as players pretend to remember a sequence they did not actually memorize, which leads to some hilarious miscommunications and the occasional accidental victory dance when someone nails a tougher sequence.

### Strategy and Tips
While Mind Up leans into accessibility, there is room for actual strategy if you want to improve your performance. Here are a few tips that can help you maximize your score without turning the game into a math olympiad:
- Focus on short term memory anchors: break sequences into chunks of two or three and memorize those blocks rather than the full sequence at once
- Watch for rhythm patterns: many sequences have a cadence to them; recognizing this rhythm can help you anticipate the next element
- Manage tempo with your neighbors: sometimes it helps to time your moves to stay just ahead of the pack without rushing
- Don’t be afraid to bluff a little: a confident but plausible misdirection can throw off a careful observer and swing momentum your way
- Use tokens as pressure relief: if you are unsure, placing a token in a conservative position can prevent a costly misstep

As you play more, you begin to notice recurring patterns in clue types and sequences. This is where repeat players can build a sense of mastery. However, Mind Up remains a social game at its core, not a solitary puzzle. The joy often comes from watching a friend misremember a sequence in a dramatic fashion and then getting it right on the confident second attempt.
