## Overview
Sushi Go! is a tiny treasure chest of a game that fits on a coffee table and in a pocket if your pocket is suspiciously large. Designed by Phil Walker-Harding and published by Gamewright, Sushi Go! takes the big idea of card drafting and condenses it into a 10 to 20 minute sprint of light strategy, quick math, and a surprising amount of bragging about who has the best sushi card combos. The concept is simple enough for a kid to grasp and deep enough for adults to pretend they are calculating probability at a top analytics firm while munching on cheap wasabi. The result is a staple in family game nights, a quick warm-up before a longer game, or a solo timer piece for those evenings when you want your own sushi conquests.

### What you actually get
In Sushi Go! you are assembling a conveyor belt of sushi cards as they pass around the table. Each card belongs to a type with its own scoring rule. The deck is balanced to reward planning without becoming nitpicky. You will encounter nigiri, maki rolls, sashimi, tempura, and the enigmatic pudding. The joy of the game is that you neither know what will come next nor how others will snatch away your dreams of a full maki roll, yet you can still make reasonable choices every hand. The cards are small and sturdy, with bright, goofy artwork that makes the table look like a cartoon buffet rather than a strict math exercise.

## How to Play: a quick refresher
The core mechanic is drafting via passing. Each round begins with everyone receiving a hand of cards. You pick one card to keep and place it face down in front of you, then pass the remaining cards to the left (or right, depending on the round). Everyone does this simultaneously as rounds proceed; once all hands are exhausted, you reveal your collected cards and score.

Key elements to watch:
- Nigiri are worth fixed points. They often require careful pairing with wasabi cards to maximize value.
- Maki rolls score for the largest set by the end of the round. Practical upshot: sometimes you want to deny your neighbor a maki card to prevent them from winning the big payout.
- Sashimi requires collecting three of a kind to get points; otherwise two success cases produce nothing. It is a classic trap: go for three, risk not completing them.
- Tempura pays off when you collect a set of two—this is a small but satisfying payoff for patient drafting.
- Pudding cards are kept aside and scored at the end of the game, which adds a longer tail to the scoring and makes late-round decisions more interesting.

The round structure is short, with typically 3 rounds when playing the base game at 2-5 players. That means a full game can slip into under 20 minutes if everyone is playing cool and not overthinking micro-decisions. The quick tempo makes it an excellent palate cleanser between heavier titles, and its lightness often makes it a great dip for non-gamers who want something with personality.

The great thing is that you can still salvage a good score with pudding in the later rounds.

## Strategy and tips
Sushi Go! rewards flexible planning and a bit of financial cunning (in your own mind, of course). The following tips can help you chase victory without becoming the table bully:
- Start with a simple plan and adapt it as the round evolves. The ideal path is usually to pick a card that advances your scoring while denying someone else a card that would push them further ahead.
- Understand the three dominant scoring tracks: nigiri for fixed points, maki for the lead in a round, and pudding for end-game pieces. Tempura and sashimi offer strong returns if you time them correctly, but do not chase them if you are not likely to finish.
- Track what other players are collecting. If someone is clearly chasing a particular set, you can use your own picks to block them while grabbing a secondary scoring line.
- Use the end-game pudding trick to your advantage. You can hold pudding until the end and outmaneuver opponents who are chasing short-term points.
- The best approach is generally balanced: do not overcommit to any single card type unless you have a strong reason and mean to finish strong.

### Block and counterplay
When you want to be a little mischievous without becoming a table villain, a simple tactic is to take a card that would create a strong synergy for the next player. You will sometimes see a frantic scramble to form a specific set and the table becomes a light-hearted chaos ball. It is the charm of Sushi Go! and part of why it remains enjoyable after multiple plays.

## Why Sushi Go! is great for families and casual gamers
Sushi Go! is accessible and friendly. The rules are easy to parse within a round or two, and the quick cycles mean you can carry on with dinner or other activities on the same night. It is a game where even if you do poorly in one round, you can bounce back in the next one. The art and humor make it feel less like heavy strategy and more like a party with friendly competition.

The game functions as a social lubricant: players talk about the choices they see on the table, reminisce about the last roll of maki, and you often end up making a few jokes about the sushi puns that appear on the cards. It is not a deep dive into structural complexity, but it does provide a meaningful and satisfying decision layer, especially for a group of players with mixed experience.
