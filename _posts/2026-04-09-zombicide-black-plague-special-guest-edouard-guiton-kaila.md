## What’s in the Box: Components and Chaos Theory
Zombicide Black Plague is dense, glorious, and delicate enough to shatter your soul if you tilt the box the wrong way. Here’s what you can expect when you crack that lid for the first time.

- A thick, sturdy game board depicting a medieval city map—streets, towers, plague pits, and the occasional coffin-shaped doorway to doom.

Edouard and Kaila lean into the components the moment the tray-lid opens. It’s a playful, almost affectionate critique that signals both respect and: yes, chaos can be well-organized chaos.

## Gameplay: How the Zombies Keep You Honest
If you’ve played Zombicide at all, you know the basic gist: you and your group push into a city swarming with undead, complete objectives, gather loot, and try not to die spectacularly. This review, however, places a spotlight on the moment-to-moment decision-making that makes Black Plague so compelling—and occasionally exasperating.

### Turn Structure: Actions, Movement, and Mayhem
Each survivor gets a handful of actions per turn: move, search, attack, interact, and sometimes sprint. Edouard emphasizes the “tempo” of the turn—how you sequence actions matters. A bad sequence can leave you with two choices: defend a doorway with compromised numbers, or abandon the room to pursue loot that seems just out of reach. Kaila, meanwhile, loves the “window of opportunity” where you see a path through a corridor lined with impassable zombies, and you realize you have to pivot, fast, or lose the entire squad.

The board becomes a living thing: doors open, zombies spill in, and you measure the distance between your group and a holy relic of loot that could save your life—or doom it. Sometimes you’ll roll a critical success; sometimes you’ll roll a critical failure that makes you question your entire life choices and your taste in dice design.

### Combat and Loot: A Dance with Death
Combat in Black Plague is a mix of dice-chucking and tactical positioning. Your swords may shine, but a bad initiative can flip the map into chaos faster than you can say “double-tap.” The guests discuss two core ideas:
- Positioning: You want the survivors to form a protective arc, shielding the more fragile members while you threaten the zombie swarm from the flanks. Edge cases abound, like a zombie creeping behind you because you forgot to close the door behind you.
- Loot priority: Some loot cards are guaranteed winners, others are traps that appear to be worth it until you realize the item you coveted is cursed with a dragon’s personality. Edouard recommends assigning roles early: one loot-hunter, one defender, one map-reader, and one chaos-embracer who is willing to take the risk for a bigger payoff.

### Difficulty and the Campaign Arc
The campaign mode in Black Plague is where the game earns its stripes. It’s not a one-off skirmish; it’s an entire narrative arc with consequences that ripple from session to session. The campaign can feel like a slow-burn tragedy or an all-out sprint to collect enough items to outpace the plague. Edouard loves the sense of escalation — as you progress, the zombies become deadlier, the rooms become tighter, and the map becomes less forgiving. Kaila counters with the reality that sometimes you just need to squeak by with one last survivor who can hold the line while the others sprint to safety.

## Thematic Tones: Humor, Horror, and Heart
Zombicide Black Plague isn’t trying to book a grim meditation on mortality. It leans into swashbuckling heroism with a campy medieval vibe. The humor comes not from jokes about the undead, but from the human moments: the moment you realize your healer is the only one who can save the group, the moment your knight charges into a wall of zombies because the dice betrayed you, and the moment you realize your rogue has looted the last treasure and now must outrun a goblin horde with nothing but a frying pan.

Edouard’s pun game is strong here. He riffs on “plague bricks” and “zombie hieroglyphs” that appear when you read the card text aloud. Kaila uses the narrative angle to remind players that even in the darkest dungeon, you can still tell a story about friendship, glory, and the time a necromancer tripped over a spear and fell into his own illusions. It’s goofy, it’s affectionate, and it’s a perfect match for a game that thrives on exuberant chaos.

## Strategy and Tactics: How to Survive and Even Have Fun Doing It
If you’re reading this and thinking, “I want to win without turning my life into a spreadsheet,” you’re in luck. The following strategic notes come straight from the special guests, with a Geeknite twist.

- Early Control of Each Zone: Don’t ignore the edges of the map. Securing choke points buys you two precious rounds to decide which room to loot next.
- Team Roles with Personality: Assign roles that play to each player’s strengths and quirks. A healer who jokes about “the lunging zombie with a bright future” can keep morale high while the cleric actually keeps people alive.
- Resource Management: The item deck is a finite resource. Don’t loot everything in sight; sometimes the best move is to fence in the loot to give your team a clear objective rather than a last-minute scramble.
- Risk vs. Reward: Every raid door opens a new set of decisions. Small risks can lead to big rewards, but a big risk on a bad turn is just an invitation for the Grim Reaper to drop by for a listenable monologue about your choices.

Edouard notes the elegance of Black Plague’s tension curves: you can feel a crescendo of danger when multiple doors are opened, and the undead start converging on your strongest survivor. Kaila loves the moment when you pivot from “do I grab loot here?” to “do I hold this hallway and let the others extract?” The drama is the point, and the game delivers with a smile and a growl.

> Pro-tip from the guests: Always leave one path open where the least valuable hero can retreat to safety if the situation becomes untenable. It’s like building an emergency exit plan for your feelings.

## The Gauntlet: Pros and Cons (Honest, with a Smile)
As with any beloved game, there are trade-offs. Here’s a quick ledger of what you might love or tolerate about Zombicide Black Plague, as discussed by our Special Guests.

Pros:
- High replayability: Different scenarios, different maps, different zombie ensembles, and a campaign mode that rewards long-term planning.
- Thematic immersion: The medieval fantasy aesthetic is coherent and appealing—more churros than gloom, if you know what I mean.
- Co-operative tension: It rewards teamwork without turning into a “one-savior saves all” grind. Everyone has a voice, even when you’re failing spectacularly.
- Accessibility: Rules are approachable enough for newbies to pick up in one session, while still offering depth for veterans who want to optimize paths, loot routes, and action economy.

Cons:
- Setup time: It’s not a quick pickup-and-play. You’ll want a solid chunk of time, space, and a prayer to the cardboard goblins that nothing escapes the table.
- Rule density: The campaign layer and some combat nuances can feel heavy on the first few sessions. Patience and a sense of humor help a lot.
- Component fragility: Some miniatures and cards can wear down after multiple campaigns if you’re a heavy-handed dice roller. Handle with care and maybe a small storage organizer.

The guest dialogue ultimately lands on a balanced verdict: Black Plague is not only a game; it’s an experience. If you love cinematic battles with a heavy dose of luck, narrative momentum, and the occasional “how did we survive that?” moment, you’re in for a treat. If you prefer a strictly math-driven puzzle where every decision maps cleanly to a 2x2 matrix of options, you might want to scholar up elsewhere. For the rest of us, the story is worth the gamble.

## Final Verdict and Recommendation
Would I recommend Zombicide: Black Plague? Yes, with two caveats. First, you’ll want the right players: a crew that enjoys cooperative chaos, a little strategy, and a lot of laughter. Second, set aside a chunk of time and a generous amount of table space. Do not attempt this on a coffee table unless you enjoy the slapstick art of clattering dice and the dramatic sigh that comes with realizing your plan required more tunnel vision than a mole in a library. The game rewards bold play, clever positioning, and a dash of theatrical confidence—the kind of vibe Edouard and Kaila embody perfectly in this session.

If you’re new to Zombicide, start with a standard scenario, get the feel for the pace, then consider a campaign run for a longer, more satisfying arc. If you’re returning to Black Plague after a break, the guest-once-more energy of the special guests adds a fresh lens: this is not just a game, it’s a storytelling workshop where your team’s resilience is the plot twist.
