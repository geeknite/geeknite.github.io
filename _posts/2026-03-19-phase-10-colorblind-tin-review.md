## What is Phase 10, and what does Color-Blind Edition change?
Phase 10 is a classic rummy-flavored card game where players complete ten phases (like sets and runs) before everyone else. Traditional Phase 10 relies heavily on color to differentiate sets, runs, wilds, and skips. The Color-Blind Edition replaces the color cues with distinctive symbols or shapes that are easier to distinguish at a glance. In practice, this means fewer squints, fewer “is that blue or green?” debates, and more actual gameplay instead of staring at your cards like you’re solving a crime clue in a neon-lit alley.

What stays the same: the turn structure, the way you draw and discard, and the occasional “someone stole my plan with a Skip.” What changes: the icons on the cards, with the shapes (and sometimes fonts) designed to be as distinguishable as possible for color-blind players. This is not a gimmick; it’s a solid accessibility boost that doesn’t require extra apps or tactile braille cards (though that would be cool too). ### Why symbols over colors? A quick explainer
- People with color vision deficiency can differentiate shapes, patterns, and icons faster than they can differentiate hues that blur together.
- Symbols can be designed to remain recognizable in dim lighting, which is perfect for late-night game nights when someone insists the “mushroom” icon is the “triangle” icon due to the lamp’s shade.
- It helps kids learn to identify patterns, not just memorize “this shade means X.” It’s math-in-mouth, but in card form.

## How to play with the color-blind edition: a quick refresher
Phase 10 is a race to complete ten phases. Each phase is a specific combination of cards (like two sets of three, or one run of seven). You’ll draw from a draw pile and discard to a discard pile. When you complete a phase, you lay down those cards in front of you and move on to the next phase. The first player to complete all ten phases wins.

What changes with color-blind symbols is how people identify the card suits of each phase. Cards that used to be grouped by color are now grouped by symbol. The gameplay mechanics—draw, discard, complete a phase, move to the next—remain the same. The legend on the instruction sheet explains which symbol corresponds to which color family, and it’s designed to be easy to reference on the fly.

If you’re new to Phase 10, you’ll want the quick rules section. For seasoned players, you’ll appreciate how the symbols cut down on the “which color is this again?” debates during late-stage phases when people are clutching their chins and contemplating life choices.

## Strategy tips: making color-blind-friendly planning easier
- Plan circles, not colors: When you’re aiming for a phase that’s a two-set plus a run, map out which symbols you’re likely to need—then collect them in your hand. This reduces frantic card juggling when you reveal the final phase.
- Keep a “safety” card in hand: If you’re unsure about a card’s identity, hold onto a wildcard symbol until you’re forced to play it. It buys you time to deduce what you’ve got.
- Monitor discard signals: If someone is discarding many of a certain symbol, they might be chasing that type of card. That could inform your own strategy and whether you try to block them with a hold or a skip.
- Remember the Wilds: Wild cards still behave like wilds, but with color-blind glyphs, you’ll use them strategically to fill in gaps for your current phase rather than just for a placeholder.
- Endgame pressure: As the phases stack and fewer cards remain, the game becomes about forcing the right cards into your hand. Don’t reveal your plan too early; let your opponents chase red herrings while you casually assemble the final piece.
