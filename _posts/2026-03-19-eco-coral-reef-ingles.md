## Language and English for Specific Purposes (ESP)
### Vocabulary Spotlight
Eco Coral Reef centers vocabulary around coral reef biology and ecosystem services. Here are some terms you’ll encounter and why they matter:
- reef: the underwater structure formed by calcium carbonate skeletons built by corals
- coral: the living animals that secrete calcium carbonate skeletons
- polyp: the tiny individual coral animals that multiply to form a reef
- zooxanthellae: algae living inside coral tissues that provide energy via photosynthesis
- symbiosis: a mutually beneficial relationship between two organisms
- bleaching: the loss of color and health in corals under stress
- biodiversity: the variety of life in a given ecosystem

### Pronunciation Guide (Quick Snippet)
- zooxanthellae: zoh-ox-an-THUH-lee
- polyps: POL-ips
- symbiosis: SIM-bee-OH-sis
- bleaching: BLEECH-ing
- reef: REEF

## The Science Made Accessible
### Core Concepts Covered
- The reef as a living structure: calcium carbonate skeletons built by corals, forming complex habitats that support thousands of species.
- Mutualism between corals and zooxanthellae: algae provide energy in exchange for protection and access to light.
- The food web: from corals and algae to fish, invertebrates, and apex predators.
- Human impacts: warming oceans, acidification, pollution, overfishing, and destructive fishing practices.
- Conservation actions: marine protected areas, responsible tourism, and reducing plastic pollution.

### Why It Matters for Non-Scientists
Understanding coral reefs isn’t just for marine biologists. It’s a lens into climate health, biodiversity, and sustainable living. ## Hands-On and At-Home Extensions
### Mini-Activities to Try with ESL Learners
- Build a mini reef in a bottle: use a clear bottle, water, a dab of salt, and safe aquatic decorations to simulate a reef environment. Learners describe what they see in English, naming terms like polyps, fish, and algae.

## Common Misconceptions About Reefs (and How the Resource Addresses Them)
- Misconception: Corals are plants. Correction: Corals are animals made up of tiny polyps that build reef structures, often in symbiotic partnership with algae.
- Misconception: Bleaching means death immediately. Correction: Bleaching stresses corals and can lead to mortality if conditions don’t improve; it’s a warning sign more than a final verdict.
- Misconception: Reefs don’t move. Correction: Corals are sessile, but reefs are dynamic landscapes shaped by currents, currents, and creatures that roam among them.
The module gently corrects these ideas through visuals, plain-language explanations, and quick checks, which makes it a friendly way to counter common misunderstandings in ESL contexts.
