## How to play in this micro universe
### Setup and goal
The setup mirrors the classic Skip-Bo albeit on a much smaller stage. Each player gets a stock pile, you lay out a few build piles, and you try to shed your stock before your opponents. The winning condition remains simple: be the first to empty your stock pile. The twist here is that the tiny cards demand a different rhythm; you can place a block of cards on build piles with the precision of a careful ant, but you can also slip and slide depending on how much space you have on the table.

### Turn flow in a blink
Your turn consists of drawing the top card from your stock and trying to play it on one of the build piles in ascending order. You can also lay down cards from your hand if you have playable sequences. If you cannot play, you discard to your personal discard pile and pass the turn. The rest of the core Skip-Bo rules still apply: you need to manage sequences, you can use wild cards, and you want to clear your stock pile before your rivals do.

The tiny cards require a slightly different pacing. You may be tempted to rush, but the micro deck rewards calculated moves rather than frantic speed. It is a gentle reminder that big decisions live in small packages.

### A quick reference to the core differences
- The deck is scaled down, so you will encounter the same number cards but in a small, portable format.
- The table vibe shifts from dramatic table talk to subtle table talk, because you will probably be whispering while trying not to drop a card on the floor.
- Handling is easier for those with small hands, but less forgiving if you drop a card in a crack the size of a coffee spill.
