## The Special Guest Lineup and their artistic fingerprints

### Lucio Parrillo on the heroic survivors

Parrillo brings a bold, high-contrast look to his survivors. Expect thick lines, dramatic lighting, and a sense of the gallery rather than the battlefield. His approach elevates silhouettes so the heroes read strong on the table even from across the room. The poses are dynamic, with a bit of swagger that says the survivor is more likely to charge headfirst into a horde than run for safety. The sculpting team under Parrillo keeps the figures readable, but the real joy comes from how his color choices respond to the enamel and wash layers we see in the box. Painting tips besides the usual base and shade would be to lean into edge highlighting and clean up with a steady hand after a wash to keep the dramatic line work intact. Parrillo often uses warm skin tones with cooler light sources which translates beautifully on the tabletop. In short, you get heroes who look like they could bench press a bulldozer and still look good in a closeup shot.

### Edouard Guiton and the grotesque elegance

Guiton brings a more visceral vibe to the mix. His characters carry a certain horror cinema influence with expressive anatomy and a taste for grotesque detail. The advantage here is a set that feels like a field guide to the weird and wonderful in the zombie world. Guiton does not shy away from texture; you can feel the rough hides and torn fabrics in your mind as you glancingly study the minis. The dark values work well with Zombicide chaos, creating a contrast that can be used to your advantage when planning your paint by numbers. The result is not merely a survivor with a gun. It is a survivor that seems to have lived ten lives and will probably pick up a few more before the next game night. Tips for Guiton styled minis center on heavy dry brushing for texture and selective bright highlights to prevent details from vanishing under base coats.

### Miguel Coimbra and the moody end of the spectrum

Coimbra walks a line between epic fantasy and moody horror. His minis often feel cinematic, with lighting that seems to come from a source you cannot quite pinpoint. The mood is thick and the sense of danger is tangible enough that you could almost hear distant moans when you stare at the figures. Coimbra excels at sculpting drama into a small package; the faces tell a story and the clothing folds read like a painting rather than a simple model. For painters, Coimbra styled miniatures invite you to experiment with chiaroscuro, ensuring your blacks feel deep and your reds pop without overpowering the composition. Expect a more painterly approach with these pieces, which is a refreshing change of pace in the zombie universe where many sets default to grittiness over personality.

## Sculpting quality and paintability notes

The three guest artists push the bar for sculpting in ways that matter when you actually paint. Parrillo tends to deliver clean, well defined muscle groups and a heroic silhouette that reads well at table distance. Guiton introduces textures like torn fabric, scars, and pet peeves that only a true horror artist would design. Coimbra leans into moody lighting cues, which makes the minis dramatic under varied table lighting. The combination makes the set surprisingly versatile for painters who want to explore three distinct storytelling approaches in one box. The casting quality is generally solid, with minimal flash and good mould lines that can be cleaned up with a steady hand and a hobby knife. You should expect some resin flash here and there if you are dealing with older production runs, but nothing that breaks the overall experience.

Paint strategy wise you can approach the trio as three chapters in a single extended painting session. Start with Parrillo for hero clarity, move to Guiton for texture time, and finish with Coimbra to lock in the moody atmosphere. If you want a more methodical approach, you can paint all the bases first to unify the board presence and then pick up the figures one by one to refine faces and hands. A practical tip is to pre-shade with a mid tone to help the dark tones in Coimbra and Guiton stand out, then apply selective highlights to keep the figures from looking flat. A little glaze can help unify the different skin tones and textures across the three distinct styles, which makes the final tabletop look cohesive rather than patchwork.

## Painting tips tailored to the three artists

- Parrillo style tips: emphasize bold edge highlights and clean line work. Use a bright white or cream highlight on the top surfaces to mimic the dramatic lighting that Parrillo favors. A quick dry brush on fur and fabric helps bring out textures without washing away the crispness of the sculpt.
- Guiton style tips: focus on texture. Use stippling and thin glazes to build up the grotesque textures on clothing, exposed skin, and torn fabrics. A limited palette with a strong contrast in skin tones helps his pieces read as visceral and alive on the table.
- Coimbra style tips: push mood with controlled black washes and deep blues in the shadows. Keep the faces readable with selective highlights to preserve the storytelling aspect. If you like, add a subtle cool light source to the helmet or armor to create the cinematic vibe Coimbra is famous for.
