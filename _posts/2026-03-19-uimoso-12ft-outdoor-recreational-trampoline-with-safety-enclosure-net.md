## Setup and Assembly

Assembling a 12 ft trampoline is a bit like assembling a bookshelf: you need a clear space, a little patience, and a few extra hands to avoid pinching fingers. Here’s the practical guide to getting it right:
- Find the level ground: The entire setup relies on a flat surface. If you’re not blessed with perfectly even turf, you’ll end up with a wobble that makes your dog think it’s a new chew toy.
- Frame first: Connect the frame pieces with bolts and washers. A partner who can hold pieces in place is invaluable here. The process usually takes 60–90 minutes depending on your expertise and the number of snacks involved in the snack break.
- Attach springs: Each spring must hook into the frame and mat. Use a spring puller or a helper with good grip strength. It’s the most satisfying part of the process because you feel like you’re actually building something, not just staring at a pile of metal tubes.
- Build the net: The enclosure net slides around the frame. Ensure the net is taut and the zipper runs smoothly; test the door a few times before putting a kid near the trampoline. A few minutes spent here saves hours of panic when the little one runs toward the edge during a bounce session.
- Safety check: Check all connections for tightness, confirm the net’s tautness, and verify that there are no protruding springs or rough edges. Do a gentle bounce test to confirm even tension across the mat. If anything feels off, disassemble and adjust rather than risk a face plant on the first full-bounce test.

## Safety, Weather, and Longevity

The safety net and padding are the main line of defense against injuries, but there are still a few things to watch for. Check the net for tears at the start and end of each season. If there are signs of wear, replace the net to maintain the enclosure’s integrity. The padding around the springs helps prevent minor scrapes from accidental contact with metal parts, but it’s still wise to educate kids about spacing and jumping safely to avoid unnecessary scrapes and bruises.

Weather is another factor. Prolonged exposure to sun is not kind to any outdoor equipment, so consider covering or storing the trampoline during winter or heavy rainy seasons if you live in an area with severe weather. A simple, fitted cover can extend the life of the mat and padding, while the UV-stable properties of the mat help preserve color and elasticity. If your climate experiences strong winds, anchor the trampoline properly according to the manufacturer’s guidelines and avoid placing it near fences or trees that can act as wind tunnels.

## Maintenance and Longevity

Maintenance isn’t glamorous, but it’s the secret to a trampoline that defies the clock. Regular inspections of the frame bolts, leg feet, and springs are essential. Tighten loose bolts and recheck the net’s tension every few months. If you notice fading on the mat, consider a mat replacement after several seasons of heavy use. The obvious benefit is that you’ll avoid a saggy surface that looks like a pool of marshmallows dropped into a pit of gravity—nice thought, but not something you want to see while your kids bounce above the hedge.

Keeping the area clean is beneficial too. Leaves and twigs can accumulate and become tripping hazards or cause issues with the springs. A quick sweep every week or so can prevent long-term wear and tear.
