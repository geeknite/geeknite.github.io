## What is UNO ALL WILD
### All Wild, All the Time
UNO ALL WILD takes the familiar UNO deck and swaps nearly every card into a wild-card archetype. In this version, there are fewer purely numbered cards and more situations where you declare the color, swap hands, or randomly flip the board into a new color universe. The premise is simple: any card can become a Wild Card that changes the color to whatever you declare, plus optional effects that flip, skip, or force a draw—depending on the house rules you adopt. The core loop remains UNO-ish: you want to be the first person to shed all your cards while chaos erupts around you. The twist is that the deck now rewards creative color-changes and wily tactical bluffing more than raw arithmetic of numbers.

### The Box Contents

It’s cheerful chaos—the kind of aesthetic that makes you want to hurl a celebratory foam finger at your own incompetence.

## How to Play: The Core Rules, with a Wild Twist
### Basic Flow, Before the Carnage
- Match a card by color or, if you have a Wild Card, declare the color you’re aiming for (the color you choose remains in effect until someone changes it again).
- If you can’t play, draw a card. If you still can’t play, pass. Classic UNO logic with a wild new flavor.
- When you’re down to one card, you must shout UNO. In ALL WILD, the stakes for not yelling UNO are more palpable because, well, everything’s a wild card. The chance of accidentally flipping the color you didn’t intend makes it deliciously nerve-wracking.

### All Cards Are Wild: The Rule of Chaos
The naming gives it away, but the practical effect is where the giggles happen. Each card—whether a number, a Skip, a Draw Two, or a Reverse—can be treated as a Wild Card, thanks to the design and some optional rules. In practice, many groups will designate a few staple Wild Cards at the start (for example, any card can choose a color, or a particular symbol denotes a color-changer). The exact local rule set is part of the charm. It’s a “pick your chaos” situation that can scale up to truly ridiculous rounds with a shared wink at the table.

### Strategy: How to Win When Everything Is Wild
- Play for tempo: In a deck where colors can be changed on a whim, you want to force your opponents into cycles where they either hold on to color-changing cards or risk losing their grip on the color chain. 
- Save Wild Cards for late game: Don’t blow your all-wild cards on early passes. This is a social game as much as a strategic one; your best play might be to force your neighbor into a tough color choice right before someone else notices their own dwindling hand.
- Watch the table energy: If you sense a surge of desperation, you can lean into wild color changes to maximize pressure—swapping to a color that’s not in someone’s comfort zone can cause chaotic misplays and pile-ups that you’ll remember for weeks.

### House Rules: The Flavor You Need
UNO ALL WILD plays beautifully with a few shared house rules. Here are a few we’ve tested with friends:
- All-Wild Switch: At the start, pick five cards that will function as Wild Color-Change triggers; those are your “wilds” for the round. 
- Wild-Only Draw: If you can’t play a card, you draw a card as usual, but if you draw a Wild Card, you instantly declare the color. It creates a mini-chase for who can leverage the next wild color shift best.
- UNO-2-Skip: When a player is caught with exactly two cards, they must declare a goofy face while their opponents decide whether to skip or reverse. It’s silly; it works.

These rules aren’t mandatory, but they help adapt the chaos to your group’s tolerance for madness. If your family is less about chaos and more about quiet strategy, you’ll probably want to rein in the wilds or revert to classic UNO for a more predictable night.
