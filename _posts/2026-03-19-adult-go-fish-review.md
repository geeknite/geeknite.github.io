## How to play: the quick and the not so quick

Go F*** Yourself is designed for a group and typically works best with 4 to 8 players. The exact numbers vary by edition, but the principle stays the same: collect cards by asking for ranks, but with each request comes a chance to participate in a dare, a confession, or a bold challenge. The exact mechanic can be summarized as a social dance where asking for a card triggers consequences that may be funny, awkward, or delightfully uncomfortable — in a good way, obviously.

### Setup

- Shuffle the main deck and deal a fixed number of cards to each player (usually 5). The rest becomes a draw pile. 
- Place the token pile within easy reach. Tokens often represent points, stares, or the probability of a future dare. 
- Decide on a baseline rule set. You can keep things tight with simple prompts or unleash the full carnival of commentary by including extra dare cards for late-night sessions.
- Establish consent boundaries. This is critical. A simple rule: if a player says stop, everyone respects it. The game remains funny without crossing lines that kill the vibe.

### Turn structure
Each turn plays out like classic Go Fish but with a cunning twist:

1) On your turn you ask another player for a rank card. For example, you might say, I need two kings or I am currently collecting a full house of awkward truths, depending on your mood.
2) If the player holds the card, they hand it over and the turn continues. If not, the drawer draws a card from the deck and the turn passes to the next player.
3) Whenever you collect a set, you reveal it and award yourself a token. The tokens accumulate toward the endgame victory condition, but there is always a twist: you may be asked to perform a dare instead of taking a token.

What makes the game shine is the interplay between strategy and social obligation. You can bluff, you can confess, you can attempt a witty pivot, and you can misinterpret your own feelings for the sake of a joke. The whole thing is a live experiment in how far you can push humor before someone shouts out a safe word and the moment becomes even funnier because of the unspoken boundaries you just set.

## The humor engine: what lands and what misses

The central feature of this game is humor, and humor can be a fickle beast. When the prompts lean into shared experiences — dating mishaps, awkward travel stories, or the frustrating grind of a group project — the laughs come naturally because most players recognize the truth behind the joke. When the prompts aim for the low-hanging fruit or rely on shock value without context, you risk a reaction that lands as mean-spirited rather than clever. The sweet spot is when the prompt invites storytelling, self deprecation, or playful roast that requires you to listen as much as to perform. 

To help you calibrate, we suggest starting with a few lighter rounds, especially with a new group. If the room is a little tense, begin with dares that involve nothing more than a dramatic reading of a trivial text or a mock award for the best failed plan that week. As the group warms up, you can tilt toward the more daring territory, but with a safety valve in place — a designated stop rule, a shared chalkboard for unsaid boundaries, and a commitment to end rounds on a high note rather than a cringe.

### A few tips to tailor the night
- Start with a shorter first session, 30 to 45 minutes, to warm up the group. You can always extend with a second round.

- Mix in non card based activities mid game to keep energy up. A quick round of a tongue twister challenge or a silly impression contest can re-ignite the room when momentum stalls.
