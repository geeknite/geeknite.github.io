## Anatomy of a well-cut 0.72 ct diamond: a nerdy tour
Here’s a quick breakdown that mirrors how an optical engineer would analyze the stone:
- Table width: a table that’s neither too wide nor too narrow fosters even light return. A well-proportioned table helps avoid hotspots where the viewer can see dark patches.
- Crown height: if the crown is too high, you’ll see more light leakage at some angles; if it’s too shallow, brightness may dip when the stone is tilted. A balanced crown height keeps sparkle consistent across head movements.
- Pavilion depth: a good pavilion angle keeps light trapped inside, creating a lively sparkle while avoiding dark areas at the bottom.
- Facet alignment: equal facet sizes and precise alignment create a uniform sparkle that reads as sophisticated rather than chaotic.

In short, the science of the cut is what makes colorlessness, fire, and brilliance work in harmony. The diamond’s performance is a product of precise craftsmanship. The diamond is a tiny machine for focusing light, with beauty as its primary function and physics as its engine.
