## What is Herd Mentality?
Herd Mentality is a party game designed to reveal how groups think together. Each round, one player draws a prompt card. The rest of the players secretly write an answer they believe is the herd’s likely choice. After all answers are collected, players reveal and then vote on the answers they think match the herd’s actual choice. The scoring rewards alignment with the majority and punishes, with a light touch, those who stray too far from the herd’s vibe. The result tends to be a blend of cleverness, sociability, and a dash of risky bravado.

The core loop is simple, which is exactly what makes it so effective at a party: low friction, high laugh-to-rulebook ratio. Because the prompts are designed to be relatable or silly, most players have something to contribute, even if their handwriting mimics a caffeine-deprived preschooler. The tension comes from whether your answer will be a clever consensus pick or a hilariously offbeat outlier that somehow pays off.

### Jekyll and Hyde of the herd: prompts and tone
The prompts range from innocuous to cheeky. ## How to play, in a nutshell
1) Gather players and designate a herd leader for the round.
2) The leader draws a prompt card and reads it aloud.
3) All players secretly write an answer that they think the herd will choose. The clock is ticking, and your handwriting is about to get judged by standards you didn’t even know you had.
4) Everyone reveals their answer and then votes for the answer they think the herd would pick.
5) The herd’s actual choice is tallied, and players score points for matching the herd, and extra points for clever deviations that still fit the herd’s vibe.
6) Rotate the leader role and repeat for the desired number of rounds.

The elegance of this structure lies in its simplicity. It’s easy to onboard new players, quick to play a few rounds, and just enough to spark conversation while still leaving room for silly improvisation. The clock’s presence adds pressure that keeps the energy high, but not so much that it derails the conversation. You are not computing orbital trajectories here; you are trying to predict how a group of your friends would respond to a question about pop culture, shared experiences, or everyday preferences.

### The herd dynamic in practice
In practice, you’ll notice several recurring player archetypes:
- The Safe Harbors: write answers that are universally agreeable and non-controversial. They rarely cause drama, but they can pile on the points when the herd is bored.
- The Rebels: the folks who will go bold with a quirky answer, hoping the herd recognizes their unique humor. They are the dramatic engine of the round and often the loudest in the room after a successful play.
- The Nostalgists: answers rooted in shared memories or inside jokes. They sometimes fly under the radar, but when they land, they land hard.
- The Observers: they watch patterns and then time their counter-moves to trap the herd. Their vibe is cool, calm, and occasionally terrifyingly insightful.

When these dynamics collide, the room may resemble a social experiment with a slightly chaotic but ultimately affectionate tone. If the group is too focused on winning, the humor can feel mechanical. If the group leans into the chaos, the rounds become a riot of laughter and pop culture callback chains that go on longer than a Trollsong in a DnD campaign.

## Practical tips for hosts
- Start with 6 to 8 players for a balanced energy level; adjust up or down depending on room size.
- Arrange seating so that new players can observe the herd behavior without feeling overwhelmed.
- Assign a friendly moderator who can gently steer the round if conversations get too rambly or if a prompt lands on sensitive territory.
- Use a playlist with light, upbeat music to keep momentum high but not distract from the game flow.
- Consider a rapid-fire variant with shorter prompts to keep a high tempo on busy nights.

## Variants and house rules
If your crew loves tinkering, try house rules to add variety:
- Quickfire Mode: a shorter time limit per prompt to encourage rapid-fire answers.
- Rebel Round: one round where players must intentionally write the least herd-like answer and then justify it as if it was the best answer ever.
- Memory Lane: prompt cards about shared group experiences; players earn extra points for accuracy and storytelling.

House rules can help you tailor the experience to your group, especially if you have a lot of veterans who want something new while keeping the vibe light and inclusive.

## Comparisons to other party titles
If you are weighing Herd Mentality against other party staples, here is a quick snapshot:
- Fibbage offers deception and bluffing; it rewards misdirection more than social alignment.
- Wavelength is a communication game where you try to align with others by describing a concept; it has more structured debate.
- The Mind emphasizes silent cooperation and timing; it’s less about social insight and more about nonverbal cues.
- Telestrations is drawing-based miscommunication; it leans into visual humor rather than verbal guessing.

Herd Mentality sits in a sweet spot between light party fun and social experiment. It doesn’t aim to be the most clever or the most strategic. It aims to be the most fun when the group is ready to laugh at themselves and at each other in a supportive, affectionate way.
