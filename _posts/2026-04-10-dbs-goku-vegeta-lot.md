## Deck Building Tips with a Goku Vegeta Lot
Building a deck from a mixed Goku and Vegeta lot requires a clear plan and a willingness to cut something that looks shiny but doesn’t support your core engine. Here are practical tips to help you maximize the value of your lot without turning your play area into a chaotic arcade cabinet.

- Establish a primary leader path: Decide which Saiyan do you want to drive your initial plan. Goku for speed or Vegeta for control? You can even build a hybrid that switches gears mid game, but you will pay in consistency. Pick a path and collect cards that support it first.
- Balance your energy curve: Ensure your deck has enough cards that you can play on turns 1 through 3 without needing perfect topdecks. If your lot leans toward late game finishers, include cards that accelerate energy or offer early tempo.
- Include draw and filter: A few cards that let you draw or filter your hand reduce the risk of bricking on energy or key combos. Consistent access to your best cards matters more than a single punch move that misses.
- Add removal and counterplay: You want at least a small set of removal or counter cards to keep the opponent from executing a one turn kill plan. This is especially important in a mixed lot where you might encounter a strong counter meta.
- Build around synergy, not just raw power: The real joy of a Goku Vegeta deck is the way two archetypes complement each other. Look for cards that provide shared benefits or that unlock cross synergy between the two halves of the deck.
- Sideboard for flexibility: If you plan to participate in local tournaments, a 15 card sideboard that focuses on specific matchups can be the difference maker. In a lot with both Goku and Vegeta, a flexible sideboard often wins more games than a pure top end engine.

## Safety, Grading, and Handling Tips
If you intend to keep some of the more valuable cards pristine, here are a few practical care tips. Use soft sleeves, avoid excessive rubbing of the foiling, and store the cards in a cool dry place. If you plan to submit some cards for grading later, separate the potential candidates from the rest of the lot and keep a small inventory log of what you think deserves a closer look. Grading is a separate hobby within a hobby and can be worth it if you have a few marquee cards that you believe will increase in value or display quality.

When dealing with lots online or at the store, verify the card counts, confirm the rarities listed by the seller, and ask for clear photos of any foils or edge wear. The better the initial description, the less you have to negotiate once you have the cards in hand. A little due diligence goes a long way toward avoiding costly disappointments when a lot arrives and you realize you were sold a miscount or a mislabelled item.
