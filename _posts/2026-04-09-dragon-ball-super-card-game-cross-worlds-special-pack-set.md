## Mechanics and Strategy

Cross Worlds introduces a handful of mechanics that encourage cross-theme synergy, dimensional travel vibes, and a little bit of risk-reward design. The Special Pack Set showcases examples of these mechanics through emblematic cards that can slot into established archetypes or act as novelty pieces in casual games. If you’re chasing the meta, you’ll likely want to build around the cards that enable multi-world combos, which reward careful sequencing and resource management without requiring you to turn your playmat into a chalkboard of complex math.

### New Mechanics Introduced in Cross Worlds

- World Link: a mechanic that lets you link a character across another dimension for a temporary buff. It’s like a dorm swap with power-ups – you get a little boost, but you have to manage your toggles to keep the synergy from collapsing.
- Dimensional Shift: a resource mechanic that requires you to manage a separate currency to unlock big plays on turns 3 through 5. It rewards planning but punishes impatience.
- Protector of the Cross: defensive units that protect important cards from removal, or can divert attention to lesser threats while you set up your big finish. These cards often come with a cost, but they’re the glue that makes multi-card combos viable.

## Deck-Building and Playstyle Advice

If you’re the type who logs into the game with the goal of building a deck that is actually fun rather than a mono-perfect winning machine, Cross Worlds Special Pack is a friend. The cards are designed to be flexible, meaning you can slot them into different strategies without hitting the ceiling of your deck’s potential. The key is to identify two or three core engines and then test how the World Link and Dimensional Shift mechanics interact with those engines.

A practical approach:

- Start with a two-world core: pick a World that buffs your typical lead character and another World that provides defensive options.
- Add a couple of non-related support cards that help with hand filtering and resource generation.
- Include a foil star if you’re chasing the “look at this bling on the table” factor, but don’t rely on it to win your matches.

We also recommend trying different sleeves and playmats that emphasize color-blocking to help you visually track the World Link interactions during a tense match. ## How to Build a Deck with Cross Worlds

Building around Cross Worlds involves constructing a synergy engine that leverages World Link for tempo and Dimensional Shift for big plays. Here’s a practical starter list to get you going:

- Lead: a World-friendly antagonist or hero that gains value through cross-world interactions. The exact name is less important than the synergy pattern.
- Support: 2–3 generic units that generate card advantage or board presence while you set up the engine.
- Engine cards: 2–3 World Link cards that unlock backups or enable multi-step combos.
- Removal and disruption: 2–4 cards that handle opposing threats or stall the opponent’s tempo.
- Utility: 2 copies of a foil variant that can swing the mood of the match and give you board presence in the late game.

Playstyle notes:

- Focus on early game tempo with cheap units to pressure your opponent while you charge Dimensional Shift resources.
- Be mindful of your card draw pacing; don’t flood your hand with too many reactive options that you can’t deploy promptly.
- Don’t overcommit to one single finish; hold back a couple of threats to close the game in the last two turns, or risk a slippery stall into a draw.
