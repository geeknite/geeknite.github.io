## Introduction
If you ever mixed a crossword puzzle with a superhero team-up and sprinkled in a dash of espionage, you’d be tasting the flavor of Marvel Codenames—USAopoly’s branded twist on the evergreen party game that somehow still manages to be both chaotic and oddly satisfying. This isn’t just a reskin; it’s a full-on geek-culture cosplay where your brain plays host to a thousand tiny Tony Starks arguing about word association while your friends pretend they understand the “secret identities” of 25 innocuous words on the table. Marvel Codenames is the kind of game that makes you realize that the real superpower is the ability to guess “Wakanda” without revealing your own agent color to the room.

The premise remains delightfully simple. There are two teams, red and blue, each with a spymaster who sees a layout of 25 codename cards. The non-spymaster players give one-word clues to guide teammates toward their team’s agents while avoiding the assassin. The Marvel edition keeps that core loop intact but adds a glossy superhero veneer, a few extra card design quirks, and a setting that screams “Hero Comics Night.” If you’ve ever whispered a hint like “Stark—tech” or “Shield—organization” during a game of Codenames, you’ll be right at home here—and you’ll probably bicker about which character pairings make the most sense faster than you can say “Hawkeye’s arrows.”

Let’s be clear: this is a party game at heart. It thrives on energy, jokes, and the occasional heated debate about whether Spider-Man is a better clue for “web” than “wall-crawler.” If you’re into heavy narrative experiences or long, crunchy strategy crunches, you might want to tune your expectations accordingly. Marvel Codenames isn’t a strategy tome; it’s a social engine designed to squeeze laughs and light strategic thinking out of your crew, ideally with pizza nearby and the TV on in the background for dramatic timing.

## How to play (overview)
### Setup
Setup is quick, especially if you’ve played Codenames before. Shuffle the 25 Marvel-themed codename cards and lay them out in a 5x5 grid. Decide which team goes first and set out the red and blue agent covers (or simply designate who’s the spymaster for each team). The spymaster sees which 9 cards belong to their team, which 8 belong to the other, and the assassin card’s location(s), and then the game begins. The Marvel edition sometimes reframes certain roles with thematic flavor, but the core mechanic remains: give a one-word clue plus a number to guide teammates toward your color while avoiding the assassin. 

### Basic rules (the gist)
- The spymaster gives one-word clues that relate to multiple codename words on the table. The number indicates how many words on the table relate to that clue. For example, if the spymaster says “Stark-2,” their teammates should guess two cards related to Tony Stark or Stark-related concepts.
- Teammates discuss and select cards. If they pick a card of their own color, that card is covered with their color and remains in play. If they pick an opponent’s card, it’s revealed and the turn ends. If they hit the assassin card, the game ends immediately in the other team’s favor.
- The goal is to identify all of your team’s agents before the other team does. Sympathetically, you’ll sometimes be wrong and laugh about it later at a table full of friends who refuse to admit they secretly know who Groot is.

### Special Marvel flavor and how it shifts the vibe
The Marvel version adds thematic hooks in the clue options and some card flavor that can influence your choices. The spicy energy of the team’s roster can inspire bolder, pop-culture-rich clues from the spymaster, or it can tempt you to go for a risky clue that ties three or four words together. The risk is real: a well-crafted cue can swing the game dramatically, but a misread cue can mean you accidentally signal “Thanos” when you intended “Infinity” and suddenly your entire plan collapses into a cosmic meme.

## Strategy and tips
### General gameplay strategy
- Use compound references that tie multiple words together in a way that only your team is likely to understand. For Marvel, you can exploit shared narrative beats, character relationships, or famous team-ups. A clever clue like “Avengers-3” might point to three words connected to known Avengers, but beware: other players may also grok those connections depending on their pop culture literacy.
- Balance risk and reward. A clue that connects five words is tempting, but if you misfire, you’ll hand the opponents an opportunity to grab points with calculable risk. It’s like dating in your 20s: big swings are exciting but require a steady hand and a good memory of what you’ve already revealed.
- Pay attention to table talk. The social aspect of Codenames is where the fun lives. The more your teammates discuss and debate, the more ridiculous your group’s storytelling becomes—and the more fun the whole session is going to be.

### Tips for spymasters
- Start with safer clues that connect two or three clear words, then escalate only if you’ve got strong team consensus. For example, “Web-Slinging-2” might link Spider-Man and a web-related word, if your table has a strong Spider-Verse affinity, whereas “Stark-1” could be a gateway to a focused clue that narrows in on Tony-related terms.
- Be mindful of the assassin. If you know the board’s layout well, you can craft cues that intentionally avoid the assassin’s related words. The secret sauce is to let your team feel smart without opening up a trapdoor for the other side.

### Tips for players
- Listen for shared knowledge across the team—television references, memes, or college days playing obscure RPGs. The more non-linear your common group knowledge, the more delightfully wacky your clues can become.
- Don’t overthink the obvious. Sometimes the easiest clue (even in a Marvel context) is the best one—and the table will appreciate you not constructing a 12-step puzzle for a 5-word board.
- Use humor to your advantage. The one-word clue doesn’t have to be strictly accurate; it needs to be clever and communicative. If your clue makes people groan and grin at the same time, you’ve probably nailed it.

### The balance of luck and skill
Marvel Codenames sits in a sweet spot where luck helps and skill sustains. The initial layout is random, which means every session feels fresh. The victory, however, comes from the team’s collective ability to translate vague associations into confident picks and to recover gracefully when a misstep happens. If you’re the kind of group that enjoys friendly competition, this game will quickly become a staple, and you’ll find yourself constructing inside jokes that survive multiple replays.
