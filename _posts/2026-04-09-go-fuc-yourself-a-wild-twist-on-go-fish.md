## How to play Go Fuc Yourself: quick guide
This section is not a substitute for the rulebook, but a practical map for getting started while the coffee kicks in. The goal remains the same: collect four of a kind for as many ranks as possible. The twist cards and power cards are what push a typical round into the realm of memorable chaos.

### Setup
- Shuffle the basic deck and deal a small number of cards to each player (the exact amount varies with player count but usually not more than five).
- Separate the twist and power cards into their own piles. Shuffle them into place so they appear at random moments.
- Decide who will be the lead for the first round. The lead will typically draw first and set the tone for the night.
- Place the remaining cards in a draw pile within reach for all players.

### Turn flow
- On your turn, you can request a rank from another player. If they have cards of that rank, they must hand over one card and you get to ask again. If they do not, you draw from the pile and your turn ends; this is the Go Fish moment you know from back in the day, but expect it to be interrupted by a twist soon after.
- Twist cards introduce sudden changes: swapping hands, forcing table-wide bluffs, or triggering mini-challenges that test your ability to stay calm while your opponents celebrate an unexpected boon.
- Power cards layer in further disruption: a time-stopper that freezes the table for a moment, a swap card that allows you to rearrange the hands in a limited way, or a mirror card that flips some loyalties and makes you second-guess your earlier reads.

### The twist mechanics
The twist cards are carefully designed to be flavorful but not punishing. They appear at intervals that preserve momentum rather than derail it. For instance, one twist might require all players to pass their hands to the left after a successful request, creating a brief round of chaos while everyone recalibrates. Another twist could compel players to reveal a card from their hand to the table after a bluff attempt, turning deception into a transparent sport. These tools keep the game feeling fresh without devolving into a math-heavy ritual.

### Special cards and powers
Power cards are the spice. They are not so frequent that you can rely on them, but they show up often enough to shape decisions in meaningful ways. The most satisfying moments tend to be when a power card aligns with a bluster you have been preparing since the previous round. The humor arises not from the card alone but from the narrative you build as the card is played. Your friends suddenly become characters in a running comedy of errors, and you ride the wave to a surprising victory or a glorious, spectacular failure.

A typical round clocks in around 10 to 15 minutes, depending on the number of players and how aggressively people bluff. If you crank up to 6 or 7 players, expect the tempo to accelerate and the probability of shouting a silly rule to escalate.
