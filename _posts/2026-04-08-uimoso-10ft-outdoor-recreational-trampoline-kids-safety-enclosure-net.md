## Safety considerations and age recommendations
Backyard safety is not a one size fits all situation. The general guideline is to supervise children at all times while bouncing. The enclosure net helps, but it does not replace adult supervision. A few practical rules to consider:
- Do not exceed the weight limit specified by the manufacturer. This helps preserve the frame tension and prevents excessive strain on the springs.
- One child per bounce session to minimize collisions. If siblings insist on bouncing together, have them use the trampoline in turns or designate a separate activity for turn-taking.
- Keep the area around the trampoline clear of hard objects and toys. A shallow ring of soft grass or mats can cushion any accidental falls when climbing on or off.
- Dry surfaces are crucial. Wet mats can be slippery and increase the risk of slips and falls, so check the weather before allowing a bounce session.
- Regularly inspect the safety net for tears or seams that might loosen; address issues promptly to maintain a safe play environment.

For younger children, we recommend simplified play that emphasizes balance and learning basic bounce control rather than attempting high jumps. For older kids who crave a bit more challenge, set rules and boundaries to keep sessions safe.

## Maintenance and longevity
Outdoor gear requires some care, and trampolines are no exception. A few habits can extend the life of your unit:
- Inspect springs and padding annually for signs of wear. Replace worn padding to maintain safe landings and prevent exposed springs from causing injuries.
- Clean the mat with mild soap and a soft brush. Harsh cleaners can degrade the surface over time.
- Keep the frame dry when not in use to reduce rust risk. If you live in a damp climate, consider a breathable cover that protects from rain while allowing moisture to escape.
- Check the enclosure net for sagging or tears and address quickly. A minor tear can escalate if ignored.

With a little maintenance, your Uimoso trampoline can be a feature in your yard for several seasons. The key is to stay proactive and not wait for a dramatic failure before you act.
