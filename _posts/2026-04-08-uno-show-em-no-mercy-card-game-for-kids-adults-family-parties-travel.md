## What is UNO Show Em No Mercy?
UNO Show Em No Mercy is a spicy take on the classic card game, designed for kids, adults, and anyone who has ever argued about whether a Draw Two should count as four because your fast fingers deserve less punishment. It adds new Mercy cards that flip the script in unpredictable ways, along with silly dares, quick challenges, and a few house rules that encourage table talk, collaboration, and the occasional strategic betrayal that would make a soap opera blush.

### Components
- UNO deck (108 cards) with standard numbers 0–9 and action cards: Skip, Reverse, Draw Two, Wild, and Wild Draw Four.
- Mercy cards: a set of add-ons that trigger lighthearted penalties or rewards.
- Rule booklet: a compact guide that tries to be helpful while also insisting you bring snacks.

### How to play (basic flow)
1. Shuffle the deck and deal seven cards to each player.
2. Flip the top card to start the discard pile.
3. On your turn, you match the card by number, color, or symbol. If you can’t, you draw a card.
4. When you have one card left, you shout UNO or risk penalty (the exact penalty is partially merciful, partially cruel, depending on the crowd).
5. Mercy cards introduce side triggers: a mercy card might skip a punishment, force a swap of hands, or turn the Draw Four into a playful, non-punishing variant for the round.

### Mercy rules and boardroom-level chaos
The centerpiece of Show Em No Mercy is the Mercy mechanic. It’s a set of special cards and optional house rules that let players bargain, bluff, or bribe their way to victory. A few examples:
- Mercy Swap: Trade hands with the person to your left for a single round.
- Mercy Freeze: Lock a player’s hand for one turn (they keep it but can't play anything).
- Mercy Pass: Skip a penalty, even if you should take it, once per game.
- Mercy Dare: Complete a silly dare to avoid a penalty or earn a bonus draw.

Note: The exact behavior of Mercy cards can vary by edition and house rules. Always check your copy’s rules and tailor them to your players before you unleash chaos.

### Design tips for better sessions
- Pre-game: Agree on a brief explanation of Mercy rules to avoid confusion. A 2-minute rule alignment is all you need to avoid long debates.
- Role-play: Let players talk like game show hosts; the showmanship adds the extra fun.
- Inventory of snacks: A bowl of chips near the game area reduces the temptation to snack during play.
- Alternating turns: If you want to reduce table chaos, alternate who explains the rules each round to avoid my turn your turn what’s the rule again.
