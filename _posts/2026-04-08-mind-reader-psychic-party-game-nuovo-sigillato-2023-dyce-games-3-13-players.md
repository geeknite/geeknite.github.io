## What Mind Reader is and how it sits in the party game ecosystem
Mind Reader sits at an intersection between social deduction, abstract storytelling, and quick-fire improv. The core premise is simple: one player acts as the Mind Reader and tries to infer a concept or phrase that the other players are hinting at through a series of prompts. The more effectively the hints guide the group toward the target, the closer the guesses land on the mind tracker. The twist is that hints are ambiguous by design; clarity is the enemy of good mind reading and the friend of hilarious misinterpretation.

In practice, you will go around the table with one Mind Reader and several clue-givers. The clue-givers attempt to nudge the Mind Reader toward the target concept using a carefully chosen mix of associations, metaphors, and indirect references. The Mind Reader then weighs those clues, places guesses on the mind tracker, and weighs the others’ reactions to determine who actually understood the prompt best. The tension grows as rounds accumulate and players start to learn the subtle tells of over- or under-using a clue.

If you have played Dixit, you will recognize a kinship in the reliance on metaphor and imagery. If you have played Codenames, you will recognize the push-pull of giving enough information to guide without giving away the entire strategy. Mind Reader leans into the social aspect more than pure eureka moments, which makes it a great choice for gatherings that value conversation, storytelling, and shared laughter over straight competition.

## The setup, flow, and round structure
Setup is brisk. Shuffle the deck, lay out the mind tracker board, and pass the starting clue cards around. The Mind Reader role rotates, so every player gets a chance to steer the narrative and reveal their own mental radar. Each round unfolds in three acts:

1) Prompt reveal: The Mind Reader draws a prompt card that encodes a concept, object, or phrase. The prompt sets the target for that round.
2) Clue delivery: The other players, one by one, place a clue token on the table or directly on the mind tracker to indicate what they think will guide the Mind Reader toward the target. Clues can be cryptic, literal, or a mix; the more creative, the more entertaining the round.
3) Guess and resolve: The Mind Reader studies the clues, makes a guess on the mind tracker, and then players reveal whether the guess is on target. Points are awarded for accuracy and for successful misdirection. The brain-bending part is that the Mind Reader must balance guiding with suggesting the correct concept without giving away the answer too easily.

The standard round length sits around 6 to 9 minutes depending on table size and familiarity. With 3 players, you may get a sharper pace and fewer tangential detours; with a full 13 players, you get an orchestration of whispers, laughter, and dramatic pauses that can feel like a live improv show with a board game attached.

### Setup tips for a smooth start
- Pre-shuffle the prompt deck and keep it handy to avoid downtime between rounds.
- Use a timer with a soft chime; you want the room to feel urgent, not chaotic.
- Decide early who is allowed to pass on a clue and whether a limited number of passes is allowed per round.
- Keep a calm facilitator if your group includes very talkative players; a gentle moderator can keep the pace from stalling.

### Example round flow
A round might begin with a prompt card that hints at a concept like a famous movie quote or a well-known idiom. Clue givers might attach tokens representing ideas like sound, color, or a body action. The Mind Reader then places a guess such as a phrase or object that aligns with the majority’s cues. The tension comes as some players subtly nod while others squirm, showing they read the clues differently. If the Mind Reader lands on the target concept, the table scores two points; if they narrowly miss, the closest guess gets a consolation point. If the Mind Reader nails it on the last clue, extra bonus points may be awarded to the clue givers for clever misdirection. The round ends, laughter erupts, and the next player takes the Mind Reader role.

## Variants, house rules, and keeping the vibe fresh
Every group has its own rhythm, and Mind Reader can accommodate that with a light touch on the rules. Here are a few variants that work well in real life:

- Short rounds variant: reduce the number of clues and shorten the timer by a few minutes to speed things up for late-night sessions.
