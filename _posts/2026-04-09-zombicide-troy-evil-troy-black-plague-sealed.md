## Unboxing and First Impressions

The box itself feels sturdy enough to survive a polite zombie apocalypse. Guillotine Games tends to keep a consistent production quality across its lines, and the Troy & Evil Troy packaging adheres to that standard. The artwork on the front is loud and dramatic—classical columns, a heroic silhouette struggling against a tide of undead, and a couple of variant hero figures that look suspiciously like they wandered off a gladiator poster. Inside, you’ll find a well-organized insert with plastic bags for tokens, punch boards for terrain bits, and the usual panoply of miniatures that Zombicide players have come to expect.

The new and sealed status is very much in evidence here. The plastic wrap glistens, the inner trays snap into place, and nothing rattles like a loose cat in a cereal box. If you’re someone who polishes models straight out of the box, don’t expect a miracle right away; the figures in Zombicide are typically unpainted and require your chosen brushes, but they are crisp enough to look great on a table as-is.

A quick note on component quality: you’re not paying premium resin prices here, but you’re getting solid plastic miniatures, sturdy tokens, and a card deck system that remains efficient and surprisingly readable after multiple re-encounters with the word “Zombicide” on a card sleeve. There’s a tactile satisfaction to turning over a tile and seeing a new enemy type barrelling toward you, even if you’re just playing for the first time in a quiet kitchen with a cat judging your dice luck.

## Components: What’s Inside the Box

- A slate of new miniatures for Troy and Evil Troy, plus a handful of historically themed survivors who are clearly not here to take a nap on a basilica floor. The sculpts are clean, and while you may notice some minor molding seams (as with most Zombicide plastics), they’re not going to ruin the tableau on your table.
- Card decks: These include the mission cards, spawn tables, and the special event cards that define the “Troy” and “Evil Troy” dynamic. If you’ve played Black Plague, you’ll recognize the cadence—quick draws, mixed values, and a few surprising twists that change how you might plan your turns.
- Tokens and tiles: You’ll see the usual assortment of swords, shields, and terrain tiles that can quickly rearrange the battlefield once you introduce the new set pieces. The quality is robust enough to survive casual play sessions and a couple of enthusiastic dice rolls.
- Rulebook and quick-start guide: The rulebook is compact but dense enough to offer a handful of new mechanics that expand on the base game. Expect some historical flavor text, a few new monster abilities, and a re-iteration of the core action system: move, search, fight, and survive.

The overall feel is that of a well-loved, slightly theatrical expansion—one that adds character without redefining the core loop of Zombicide Black Plague. It’s not a total reimagining, but it’s a credible attempt to inject mythic flavor into the familiar “kill all the zombies before they overwhelm you” framework.

## Thematic and Mechanical Snapshot: Troy Meets Zombicide

Troy & Evil Troy is all about balancing mythic pomp with practical game design. The “Troy” side tends to emphasize heroism, larger-than-life feats, and tactical positioning near the famous gates and columns that show up in the expansion art. The “Evil Troy” side flips the coin—dark alt-versions that push riskier plays and more punishing success criteria. The essential trick is that these two archetypes interact in ways that can either complement or complicate a party’s plan.

From a mechanical perspective, you’ll notice some new card types and each side’s fortress-like aura translates into certain board-wide effects. You’ll have moments where you need to choose between heroics and survival, which is classic Zombicide in tone but with a hint of mythic storytelling that can be surprisingly satisfying on a chilly gaming night.

If you love the base Black Plague system and crave a little extra spice for your co-op nights, Troy & Evil Troy does a solid job providing a thematic overhang without turning the game into a heavy rules grind. The designers have clearly balanced the new elements so you’re not forced into a had-to-be-there metagame; instead you get a few tasteful twists you can lean into or ignore depending on your group’s mood.

## Gameplay Flow: Setup, Turn Structure, and Narrative Twists

Setup remains the familiar choreography: lay out the board, shuffle the monster and event decks, place the survivors, and prepare to roll the dice. The new components slot into the existing setup with minimal fuss. If you’ve already installed Black Plague in your living room, Troy & Evil Troy sits on top of that foundation like a decorative pillar with a slightly more intimidating silhouette.

Turn structure follows the canonical Zombicide model—players take a set of actions (move, search, attack, open doors, etc.), then the zombie surge advances. The expansion adds a handful of new enemy types and a couple of “triggered” events that can swing the tide dramatically if you’re not paying attention. The net effect is a more dynamic flow, where you might think you have control of a corridor and suddenly a new threat appears from an unexpected angle.

The narrative layer arrives in the form of mission cards and character prompts. Troy’s heroic baseline invites you to press forward and clear the map with a flourish, while Evil Troy invites you to navigate morally gray choices that can reward you with powerful but dangerous benefits. It’s not a full-blown campaign system, but the micro-narratives do add a sense of occasion to otherwise pragmatic turns.

## Solo Play and Cooperative Dynamics

Like many Zombicide variants, this expansion plays best as a co-op. The solo mode, while functional, is less about dramatic storytelling and more about managing the board against an aggressive enemy deck. If you’re a solo gamer who enjoys puzzle-like optimization, you’ll appreciate the added complexity from the Troy & Evil Troy mix, especially when the board demands you pivot your plan mid-turn due to a sudden surge or a new objective card.

For co-op groups, the expansion can serve as a nice way to refresh a familiar night. The two versions of Troy act as foils to each other, creating decision points where you must consider alliance and risk. In practice, you’ll find that the best nights with this set are when your table is willing to discuss “what would Troy do” in a given situation and then decide whether the safe route or the heroic route best serves the mission.

## Replayability and the Variants You Get

Replayability is a key strength of Zombicide: Black Plague in general, and Troy & Evil Troy adds a layer of character-driven variability to that mix. The new mission cards reframe typical objectives, sometimes forcing you to think about the layout of the map rather than simply stacking the deck and mowing through waves. The Evil Troy variants can dramatically alter risk calculations. It’s a clever tension: you want to lean into the power of the Evil side, but the cost of doing so can be high, sometimes flirting with the line between cunning and reckless.

If you’re someone who enjoys long campaigns, you might want to integrate this expansion into a larger running narrative using your own home-brewed story arcs. The material provides enough flavor and structural hooks to make that feasible without needing a separate campaign book.

## Thematic Fit: Does Troy Belong in Black Plague?

Thematically, Troy & Evil Troy attempts a cross-genre blend: the epic grandeur of myth meets the gritty, grim reality of a zombie outbreak. It’s a risky combination—tone clashes can derail a session if the players crave straight-forward dungeon-crawl lighting, not a history lecture with undead. In practice, the expansion mostly succeeds by leaning into the drama rather than trying to force a historical re-enactment of Trojan battles. The characters feel like they could be part of a tavern tale that spirals into an alley fight with a legion of undead. Fans of the franchise who enjoy flavor text and situational prompts will likely appreciate this approach.

On the downside, the narrative patches can feel a bit uneven if you’re playing with players who prefer a clean, trackable objective list. You’ll want to facilitate some discussion beforehand about how narrative outcomes should influence the post-game recap so the campaign doesn’t feel like a random highlight reel of near-misses.

If you’re someone who owns multiple Zombicide boxes, this expansion is a nice bridge piece—something to refresh your evenings without the cost and complexity of a full-blown new edition.
