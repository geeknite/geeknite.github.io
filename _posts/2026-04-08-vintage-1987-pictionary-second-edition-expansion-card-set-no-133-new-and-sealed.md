## Collectibility, Condition, and Market Considerations

### New and Sealed: A Blessing and a Curse
The status "New and Sealed" is a double-edged sword for collectors. On one hand, you have the freshest possible version of the product—no user wear, no coffee stains on the margins, no kid’s crayon fingerprints marring the backsides. On the other hand, the sealed status can complicate future price discovery if you actually want to verify contents. In a strict sense, the box is a time capsule. In practice, you may want to open it to confirm there are indeed 50-100 cards and not a mysterious smoking card that somehow survived the test of time with heroic discoloration. If you’re a collector who cherishes mint-in-box items, No. 133 checks a lot of boxes: rarity within a specific edition, nostalgia alignment, and the aura of “this could be your only chance” energy that collectors chase.

### Condition Grading: Why Sealed Isn’t Always Better
Although “sealed” is desirable, the reality is that the card stock could be perfectly fine even after decades and the adhesives of the shrink-wrap may have degraded. If you’re evaluating this item for a display shelf or a photo shoot, sealed status is alluring, but you should also be prepared for potential inner contents that might have shifted, bubbled, or even settled in odd ways. A careful seller who can provide close-up photos of the interior, the seal integrity, and a note about any box wear is worth more than a generic “mint” claim. For the average buyer, a fair price is often determined by the seal’s condition, the absence of moisture damage, and the overall presentation—box art, spine integrity, and whether the cards smell faintly of 1980s dreams or a more blunt chemical memory.

## Display, Storage, and Care for a 1987 Time Capsule

### Where to Put It: The Display Dilemma
For a piece like No. 133, display matters. A glass-front cabinet with climate-controlled air is ideal for preserving the box’s color and the ink’s punchy personality. If you don’t own a curio cabinet, consider a dedicated shelf with consistent lighting that doesn’t bleach the colors. The “boxed display” approach can be a conversation starter at a game night, while also letting you show off the original shrink wrap in a way that doesn’t coax a choking hazard from your cat or toddler.

### Storage Solutions: Card-In-Box vs. Card-Box-in-Box
If you open the set for any reason, keep the cards in an archival-safe binder or a small card file with acid-free sleeves. If you’re a purist who wants to preserve every possible detail without opening, a display-friendly approach is to keep the box on a riser so the art is visible and the corners stay crisp. Some collectors use silica gel packs to fend off moisture, although you should never forget that the silica gel doesn’t taste as delightful as it sounds, and you should definitely keep children away from it.

### Care and Handling: The Gentle Art of Not Crunching Card Stock
Handle cards with clean hands; oils can cause minor discoloration over the years. If you decide to handle the cards for a display photo, do so slowly and with a light touch, because nobody enjoys the heartbreak of a wrinkle that costs you a miniature meltdown on social media. For the truly devoted, a soft microfiber cloth can help you gently polish the surface of the card stock for those crisp, glossy micro-moments in your photo shoot.
