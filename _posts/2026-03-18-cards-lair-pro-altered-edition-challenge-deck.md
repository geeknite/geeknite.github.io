## How the PRO Altered Edition changes the game

The Altered Edition is designed to shake up the base game’s engine. The core loop—draft, acquire, and cash out—remains, but the altered rules introduce challenges that force players to adjust strategy on the fly. Here are some of the big shifts:

- Altered Rule cards can trigger at random intervals or when certain conditions are met. This means your engine-building can get hijacked by a rogue moon phase that suddenly doubles the cost of a key card.
- Challenge markers impose escalating penalties if you ignore them. It’s like a timer on your greed, except the timer occasionally calls a dragon for a surprise boss fight.
- The “Lair” mechanics allow players to build up a zone on the table that unlocks powerful combos—but also makes it easier for opponents to poke at your lair and disrupt your plans.
- Some cards switch their effects mid-game depending on the state of play. This adds a layer of cognitive gymnastics that might trigger a few groans from veteran players who believed they had found a stable engine.

In practice, the Altered Edition rewards flexibility and risk-taking. ## Gameplay mechanics: what actually happens at the table

The PRO Altered Edition doesn’t reinvent deck-building; it refines and re-tempoizes it. Here’s a rough sketch of the flow, with the caveat that Altered Rule cards can shotgun a few curveballs your way:

- Setup: Each player starts with a small starter deck and a personal lair mat. You shuffle, deal, and prepare to draft from a shared center deck that includes both “normal” and “Altered” cards.
- Turn structure: On your turn, you’ll typically draw, play, and pass. Playing cards generates resources (gold, energy, or the game’s thematic equivalents) that you can spend on purchasing more powerful cards or triggering your lair’s functions.
- Altered Rules phase: At the start of several rounds or when certain triggers occur, an Altered Rule card is revealed. These rules might modify costs, card effects, or end-of-turn bonuses. The stack of rules can become a Santa-sized pile of present boxes that you wish you hadn’t opened.
- Challenge phase: The Challenge markers push players toward decisions that come at a cost, such as discarding a key card or sacrificing lair territory to gain a temporary advantage. Negotiation and timing matter here; you’ll want to manage risk without turning the table into a courtroom drama.
- Endgame and scoring: The game ends when certain thresholds are met (usually a timer or deck depletion). Scoring is a mix of collected cards, lair control, and successful execution of combo chains. The scoring often reveals itself only at the end, which is half strategy and half poetry of numbers.

What works well here is the tension between advancement and disruption. The Altered Rules create tension that’s flavorful rather than punitive, which is a fine line to walk in card design. If you’re the type who gleefully embraces chaos in a constructive way, you’ll love the dance. If you’re a stickler for crisp, linear progression, you’ll probably grumble about experiencing the game like a ship tossed by a friendly but mischievous sea god.

## Strategy tips: how to approach the Altered Edition

- Learn the baseline engine first, then introduce the Altered Rules gradually. A good rule of thumb is to set up a few practice rounds where you don’t score, just to feel out the altered state space.
- Prioritize lair development without losing the ability to adapt. A strong lair is a powerhouse, but if an Altered Rule tanks your lair’s early gains, you’ll be playing catch-up for a long stretch.
- Track tripping hazards. Some Altered Rules trigger off specific card types or actions. Keep an eye on those triggers and plan a fallback path so you don’t get blindsided.
- Use the mystery cards as wildcards. They’re intended to inject uncertainty and surprise; don’t be afraid to bend your plans when a Mystery card reveals a neat synergy with your current hand.
- Don’t overcommit to singular strategies. The charm of the Altered Edition is its disruption. A diversified approach that can pivot between offense, defense, and economy tends to be more robust.
