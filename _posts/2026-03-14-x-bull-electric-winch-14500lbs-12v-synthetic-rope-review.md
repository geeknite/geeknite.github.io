### Safety first: tips and best practices

Recovery work is inherently dangerous. Even a well built winch can bite if you forget basic safety. Always wear gloves with good grip to protect your hands from rope burn. Never stand between the winch and the load; keep yourself and any bystanders clear of the line during operation. The included wireless remote is a welcome convenience, but you should always have a failsafe plan and a second person acting as a spotter. Inspect the rope for frays before each outing. Synthetic lines can begin to accumulate micro-frays long before it becomes dangerous; a quick visual inspection after every outing is wise. Finally, never exceed the rated line pull rating. The 14500LBS label is a maximum for likely conditions in ideal, controlled environments; in the real world you will want a margin of safety and some common sense.

For more on general recovery safety, you can check Wikipedia and other educational resources, but keep in mind that real world practice is what truly saves you when your rig ends up in a slippery patch of mud.

### Maintenance, care, and longevity

A winch is not a plug and play gadget you store in the attic and forget about until you need it. Regular inspection of the rope for frays or damage is essential. With a synthetic rope, frays can be less obvious than with steel, so a quick visual inspection after every outing is wise. Keep the drum and fairlead clean from debris that can cause rope wear or heat buildup on the line. Lubricate the winch gear and motor bearings according to the manufacturer’s guidelines. If you have a sealed motor housing, you can skip the heavy disassembly; otherwise a light touch of lubricant around the accessible points is fine. The battery and wiring should be checked periodically for corrosion and secure connections. A well maintained winch is a safety device that pays back your careful habits with reliability and confidence on the trail.
