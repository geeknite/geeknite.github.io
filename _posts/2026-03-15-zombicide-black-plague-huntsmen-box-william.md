## What’s in the Huntsmen Box—and does William actually matter?

The Huntsmen Box is an expansion that wears its ambition on its gilded sword hilt. It introduces new zombie types with a dash more menace, a fresh objective layer, and a named survivor who brings a particular flavor to the party: William. Will he be the hero who saves the kitchen-sink dungeon raid, or the one who reminds you that even heroes can run away at a key moment while you scramble for that crucial objective marker? The answer, as with most Zombicide questions, lies somewhere between “strategy gold” and “dice gods, why have you forsaken me?”

### The miniatures and their vibe

- William, the titular survivor: A grizzled, moustached field-operative with a reliable backpack and a suspicion that the safest plan is to not be in the line of fire when possible.
- Huntsmen zombies: These aren’t your everyday shamblers. They’re better dressed and more dangerous, with crossbows that don’t miss their mood, and an emphasis on stalking through chokepoints with a sense of swagger. They remind you that fashion-forward undead can be terrifying when backed by tactical positioning.
- Supporting sculpts: Terrain cues and encounter markers that nod to Inns and Streets atmosphere. It’s a small-but-fine touch that makes pre-game setup feel like you’re staging a scene from a historical drama with a zombie twist.

The miniatures are crisp enough for domestic painting projects, while remaining forgiving for hobbyists who want to paint something dramatic and still avoid professional-level stress. Scale alignment with base game minis matters for tactical engagements, and the Huntsmen play nicely with the existing set, avoiding the “this doesn’t fit” syndrome that some expansions fall into.

### The content selection

The box isn’t a revolution in core rules, but it polishes a few aspects of the Zombicide: Black Plague experience. There are new objective cards, additional encounter tiles, and the usual raft of minor rule clarifications. If you’re setting up an undead ambush on your table, you’ll find the box nudges you toward more thoughtful planning, tighter timing, and a small dose of risk-versus-reward drama. It’s not simply “more bad guys”; it’s “more strategic choices, with some cinematic flair.”

## Theme, flavor, and the William effect

### William as a character

William is more than a stat block with a crossbow; he’s a narrative pivot. He offers synergy with ranged weapons and allows for quick repositioning, which can be the difference between a clean execute and a dramatic downfall. In practical terms, his card set leans into the classic survival dilemmas: when to sprint, when to hold, and when to call in a temporary cease-fire to reclaim momentum. If you enjoy cinematic moments—like a hero diving behind a cart to pop a headshot as the room erupts in zombie chaos—William gives you those moments with a bit less melodrama and a lot more tactical potential.

### Huntsmen as a design decision

The Huntsmen introduce a new threat vector that nudges players toward smarter room traversal. They’re not just “enemies with bows”; they represent a more dynamic threat that compels you to think about line of sight, cover, and crossfire management. The tension they create lands in the right places: you’ll find yourself asking not just “how many can I slay this turn?” but “where do I secure the objective while staving off this armored archer squad?”

For seasoned players, the Huntsmen pack brings enough novelty to justify a few table-hour sessions without turning the entire game on its head. It’s a measured infusion of tactical chess with a cinematic Gothic vibe, not a radical rework of the core rules. If you like your zombie carnage with a dash of aristocratic menace, this box fits.

## Gameplay experience: how it feels at the table

### Setup and learning curve

Setup takes a touch longer than a vanilla game because of the extra components—the Huntsmen, the extra objective deck, and William’s card stack. The rulebook remains accessible to beginners while offering enough nuance to challenge veterans who enjoy keeping a mental mapping of dozens of potential turns. The learning curve is more about discovering the new tactical options than deciphering completely new rules. In other words: expansion design done right. It invites experimentation without imposing a heavy cognitive load.

### On-table dynamics

Two big dynamics dominate when the Huntsmen are on the map: pressure and positioning. Huntsmen alter the battlefield by forcing players to think about line of sight and room geometry in a way the base game rarely insists upon. You can’t simply sprint into a room and swing wildly; you must consider where cover is, which survivors have the best line of sight, and how you’ll maintain control of key choke points as the undead press in.

The result is a more cinematic experience: a dance of turns where timing matters, and a single well-placed shot or a perfectly timed sprint can swing the entire encounter. The weight of decisions feels meaningful, which is what you want from a good expansion.

### William’s impact on flow

William can shift the flow of a turn in satisfying ways. His movement options and synergy with ranged units increase your tactical toolkit, letting you stage dramatic comebacks or hard-earned retreats that still end with you securing a critical objective. It’s not a magical cure for every bad roll, but it does add a layer of agency that makes your wins feel earned and memorable.

The content-to-cost ratio remains favorable, offering enough material to justify multiple play sessions without demanding an enormous time investment or an entire re-write of your house rules.

## A note on components and painting tips (expanded)

- Prime and base: A light gray primer works wonders to bring out highlights and metallic edges. It’s a small step that pays big dividends when you move into washes and dry brushing later.
- William’s armor: Lean into a steel base with muted blue accents to suggest battle-worn nobility. Subtle chipping and weathering can sell the “seasoned veteran” vibe without tipping into overkill.
- Huntsmen leather: Rich browns with burnt umber washes give contrast to their cloth and bone accents. It’s a nice balance between practical knightly garb and the macabre fashion statement that the Huntsmen represent.
- Terrain touches: A few splashes of blood-red ink on floor tiles can set the stage for a last stand moment. It’s a visual cue that the room is about to become a dramatic battle theater.

If you’re a painter who loves gradients, you’ll appreciate how forgiving the kit is with shading and edge work. If you’re more of a “paint in the gaps” player, you’ll still get a striking finished product that reads well on the table from across the room.

## Practical setup tips and housekeeping hints

- Sort miniatures by type as soon as you open the box. It speeds up setup and reduces the “which zombie is which again?” headaches later.
- Use resealable sleeves for new objective cards. They’ll survive travel and keep the set tidy in a bag with dice and paints.
- If you’re planning a painting night, schedule a longer session with ambient music. William’s armor benefits from careful lighting to bring out the metallic sheen, and you’ll thank yourself later when it looks good in photos.
- Store cards and tiles in labeled organizers to keep track of which deck belongs to which scenario. It’s not glamorous, but it saves you from frantic rummaging at 7:50 PM on game night.

These aren’t groundbreaking tips, but they’re the kind of practical wisdom that makes long-term board gaming nights smoother and more enjoyable. The Huntsmen Box rewards attention to presentation, pacing, and planning.

If you want to extend the life of your Huntsmen Box beyond a few game nights, consider these light house rules and narrative hooks:

- Dark Corridor Modifier: In any room with multiple Huntsmen, require a failed ranged attack to trigger a reroll for the next enemy’s turn. Creates tense standoffs and more dramatic finales.
- William’s Duty Quests: Create mini-objectives for William that must be completed to unlock a deeper story arc. For instance, securing a landmark objective in a specific order could grant a temporary ally buff or a unique ability for one turn.

Campaign-style play rewards patience, planning, and a willingness to let the dice tumble in ways that feel cinematic rather than purely lucky.
