## Condition, Grading, and The Invisible Card Monster
Let’s be real: 1995 printing methods weren’t exact equals across all boxes and lots. Your 118-card haul will likely show a spectrum of wear. If you’re planning to grade, you’ll want to do a quick triage:
- Edges and corners: Look for whitening or chipping, especially on the tips of the corners. If the corners are sharp, consider a reasonable price premium.
- Surface: Check for scratches or scuffs on the gloss. Some surface wear is expected; too much and you might be better off as a display piece than a playing card.
- Backs: Some back patterns wear differently due to shuffling. If backs are consistent, you’re in luck; if not, price accordingly.
- Creases and bends: Especially if the lot has seen a long life in a garage, you’ll encounter a few bend marks. These are usually the first to condemn a card’s resale value.
- Color fade and print flaws: 1990s printers did their best with the resources available; some color shift is part of the charm, others are a deal-breaker for serious collectors.

If you want to pursue grading, you’ll likely be dealing with a non-professional service for a lot of this size. But you can still establish a fair asking price by sorting into \"minty,\" \"good,\" and \"played\" piles, and by giving an honest description in your listing.

## Storage, Display, and Photography Tips
A 118-card lot begs for a plan. Here are some practical tips:
- Storage: Use card sleeves (standard size for most CCGs) and a small binder or card box that’s designed for trading cards. Keep the lot away from moisture and sunlight; the 1995 inks were not the most UV-stable of the era.
- Display: If you want to display, consider a wall-mounted display case with a \"Mission Briefing\" vibe. A glass-tined shadow box with a few representative cards arranged in a storyboard can be a fantastic decorative piece for a game room.
- Photography: When photographing, use even lighting and a neutral background. A top-down shot of the full 118-card spread is great for sales listings; a few close-ups on notable cards help buyers gauge condition. A rotation shot (cards shown both face and back) can provide a quick condition read.
- Handling: Clean hands, no greasy fingerprints, and a gentle touch. Old card stock is not at its best when bent by enthusiastic thumbs.

## Sorting, Pricing, and Selling: A Practical Guide
To turn this lot into something you can actually move, you’ll want a practical approach. Here’s a simple workflow:
- Inspect and separate: Make piles for hero/missions, villains, equipment, and any other sub-types. Use a sticky note system to label the piles.
- Condition-based pricing: Use a simple ladder. Mint-ish: 60-75% of planned price. Good: 40-60%. Played: 20-40%. The exact numbers depend on your market, but this framework helps you stay consistent.
- Photo-rich listing: Show photos of the back and the front for a few standout cards; include close-ups of corners and any flaws. Honesty is your friend here.
- Shipping plan: If you’re selling online, offer safe shipping options; fragile items deserve protection.
- Involve the community: Post in appropriate forums, subreddits, or marketplaces, linking to your own post for context.

## Quick Sorting Checklist
