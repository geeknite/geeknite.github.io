## How to Play: A Quick Rundown (And Then the Kitchen Sink)

The core mechanic is simple enough for a first-time guest to grasp while someone else makes coffee in the kitchen:

1) Setup: Shuffle the dilemma cards, place chips within reach, assign the first judge, and pass out tokens. The judge role rotates clockwise so everyone gets a turn to be both the philosopher and the microphone).
2) Draw and Respond: The active player draws a dilemma card and reads aloud its two options. They must defend their preferred option for 60 to 90 seconds (the timer is a merciful reminder that this is a party game, not a courtroom).
3) Rebuttal and Voting: Other players react and vote on whose defense wins. Voting can be done with chips or by selecting the “like” option on the table.
4) Scoring: The winner earns ethics chips, and the judge tallies up points. Rinse and repeat until the box yields to gravity or someone concedes defeat due to laughter exhaustion.

To keep things spicy, many hosts implement house rules. My personal favorites include:
- The Twitter Rule: Condense your argument into 280 characters or less. It’s surprisingly liberating and hilariously brutal.
- The Elevator Pitch: You must propose a solution within the length of a hypothetical elevator ride. If your audience rides the vibe with you, you gain a chorus of nods and a few impressed sighs.

## Strategy and Tips: How to Maximize the Fun (Without Losing Friends)

- Lean into the ridiculous. The more you embrace a wild defense, the more laughter you unlock. An over-the-top Socratic monologue about returning a dragon’s wing as collateral becomes a legend the next time you host.
- Use ethics chips thoughtfully. They aren’t just currency; they’re narrative tools. A late-game chip can swing a debate or derail another player’s agenda, depending on how you wield it.
- Rotate judges. The judge’s perspective can become a mini-game in itself, keeping energy high and discouraging one side from milking a single angle for too long.
- Prepare a quick pre-game warm-up. A dozen one-sentence prompts before the main event can prime everyone for creative defenses and speedy arguments.
- Set boundaries before you start. A quick “we’re here to have fun” reminder prevents rancor and ensures the topic stays lighthearted.
