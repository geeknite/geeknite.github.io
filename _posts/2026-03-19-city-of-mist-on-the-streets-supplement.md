Introduction
City of Mist: On the Streets is the street-level add-on that turns your noir city into a living, wheezy subway train of secrets. It sharpens gritty, neon-drenched drama for players and hands GMs a toolkit with alleys, corners, and the occasional latte-fueled plot twist. In this expanded review, we dive into what the supplement adds, how it plays with the core rules, and whether it earns a place on your shelf between the dice and the duct tape. If your table is tired of grand conspiracies and wants a slice that tastes like rain and copper pennies, On the Streets might just be the salt that makes your broth boil over.

What�s inside On the Streets?
This supplement leans into the urban sprawl and street-level mythology with a wink and a shoulder shrug. It introduces new neighborhoods, new street-level myths, and a toolkit designed to keep players out of a loud boss fight every session while still delivering momentum. If City of Mist is the city-wide myth factory, On the Streets is the alley behind the factory with a vending machine that dispenses plot hooks in small caffeinated doses. The tone aims for grit with a dash of whimsy, and a surprising amount of real-world texture (rain-slick pavement, questionable coffee, and a saxophone that seems to have a personal grudge against your character's backstory).

### New Loci and Neighborhoods
- The Neon Market: a sprawling bazaar of neon signs and questionable bazaars where information changes hands faster than a speedrun. Think vendor stalls that hawk both dreams and stolen memories in the same breath. Expect micro-scenes where a memory swap goes wrong and you end up with someone else�s regrets as a souvenir.
- The Whispering Docks: fog, plastic-wrapped packages, and someone always watching from the gangplank. A place where every wave carries gossip and every crate carries a secret. Here, the dockside mythos feels like a bodega of whispers—you can buy truth if you pay with caution.
- Rooftop Haven: a wind-whipped hideout perched above a city that never sleeps, complete with a wind tunnel and a questionable potted plant that may be listening. The rooftop is not just a vantage; it is a social stage where deals happen in the gusts between rain showers.
- The Glass Terrace: a glassy district perched above a rain-soaked street, with reflection traps and a social circle that knows every rumor before it happens. The Glass Terrace plays with observation and distortion; a single reflection might reveal a truth your players have yet to notice.
- The Velvet Wharf: a coastal noir district with hidden tides, perfume smugglers, and a river of secrets poured from a broken bottle. The wharf smells like ethical compromise and sea salt, with a crew that can slip from polite conversation to a jailbreak in a single scent.
- Night Market Overlook: a stairwell oasis where vendors operate under tarp awnings and the city itself feels like a living badge that refuses to be scanned. A great place for stakeholders to argue and for players to discover clues that only reveal themselves to those who linger.
- The Clockwork Expanse: an industrial belt where time is taxed and every machine ticks to a different clock. This new locus provides a mechanical flavor that complements the neon undercurrent perfect for a heist or a chase that bends time in small, practical ways.

### Street-level Mythos and Clues
- Urban Mirages: short-term myths anchored to a location for a single session, perfect for weekly arc B plots and mood shifts rather than epic sagas. They hitchhike on atmosphere and become a running joke or a haunting that dissolves after one rainstorm.
- Informants: a new pool of contacts with varying reliability and a taste for dramatic reveal moments. Players learn to read trust as a currency with interest paid in trouble. The informants can leak information, misdirect with charm, or flip sides when the price is right.
- Clue Chains: modular clues that link from a street incident to a larger mystery, designed to be adjustable so you can weave momentum without forcing a player into the hero role. The design aims for a fabric you can rearrange in under five minutes.
- Street Ghosts: residual memories left in a location that whisper into the minds of those who linger too long. They can reveal past crimes or forecast future misdeeds if your players listen closely. Ghosts are less about jump scares and more about texture—think a chorus of echoing footsteps and a scent of lilac rain.
- Night Market Signals: coded signals in signage and music that guide the cunning through the night. Decoding them can open access to back rooms, or invite trouble if misread. The signals can be used to seed side plots and to reward sharp listening with secret doors.

### Gear, Grit, and Hackery
- Streetwise Tools: gadgets that work under neon lights and when the bartender forgets to turn off the security cam. These tools are practical and flavored with noir charm rather than lab-grade polish. Some tools require a little improvisation, which is where the charm lives.
- Quick Hacks: one-page rules for improvised tech and social engineering that do not require a PhD in CSI. They are modular, fast, and designed for clutch moments rather than a full reset of the rules. Expect simple dice pools, a handful of thresholds, and re-usable prompts for NPCs.
- Vehicle Options: a handful of affordable rides and the occasional cursed taxi that knows your name. Think street-legal meets spirit-bound, with a price tag you can absorb if your heroics get loud enough. The book leans into the tension between convenience and risk when you push the pedal to the myth.
- Social Flairs: micro gestures and taunts that influence a scene without dominating it. Small tools for big conversations, because sometimes a look says more than a paragraph of dialogue. It�s the difference between a glance across a smoky bar and a monologue about fate.
- Improvised Gear: everyday items upgraded into devices with a dash of myth and a pinch of risk. A coffee cup becomes a beacon, a umbrella a shield, a wallet a map to the city soul. The book celebrates the idea that story-driven gear can be good enough and still feel magical.
- Pocket Relays: tiny devices that transmit whispers between players and NPCs in a crowded room. A single pocket relay can turn a passive observation into a tactical reveal, or become the misdirection engine for a chase.

### Encounters and Seeds
- Ready-to-use Scenes: compact encounters you can drop into a night, with a single act beat, a new clue, and a twist. The aim is momentum with minimal prep, so you can keep the session at street level without overcooking it. These scenes are designed to slot into a night�s flow, with optional remix ideas if the players surprise you.
- Faction Tension: mood boards for rival groups that can push or pull players like a streetcar on rails. The tension can swing with a rumor or a betrayal, not necessarily a full-scale war. Expect opportunities for side quests that humanize the antagonists and reveal their vulnerabilities.
- Thematic Hooks: ideas that latch onto noir style and modern myths to make every session feel like a mini film noir, complete with shadows, rain, and a saxophone that generally means trouble. Hooks are designed to spark mood as well as plot, enabling a session to breathe even when players drift between scenes.
- Urban Prologues: short, cinematic openings that place the players at ground zero of a mystery before they know it. Prologues help you frame the city as a character with a life of its own.
- Red Herrings: items and rumors that point in wrong directions just enough to keep players guessing without returning to a dead end.

### Layout, Art, Accessibility
- A clean, readable layout that does not require a treasure map to navigate. The layout is designed to be practical and mood-supportive without getting in the way. The typography supports quick glances during tense moments.
- Artwork that captures the glow of rain-soaked streets, a vibe that sits nicely between cyberpunk and mythic urban legend. Expect panels that evoke mood more than exposition and a cover that telegraphs mood more than exposition.
- Accessibility notes: a reminder that the book is not just vibes; it should be playable by people with different accessibility needs. Font choices, color contrasts, and alt text for art are kept in mind so everyone can join the rain party. The supplement includes a few printable accessibility checklists and tips for running sessions with screen readers.

### Using On the Streets in Your Campaign

#### Integration with Core Rules
On the Streets plugs into the City of Mist system with a few tweaks, not a complete rewrite. Mask usage, tags, and scenes get a little more blue-lit nuance, but you will not need a spreadsheet the size of a phone book to keep track. The focus is on micro-stories, not epic world-shaking threats, though a micro-threat can still feel epic when delivered with a rain-soaked look and a saxophone that refuses to shut up.

#### GM Tools
- Scene maps: compact, printable maps for alleyways, bars, and street corners that will not melt in the wash. They are designed for quick drop-ins and can be customized with ease.
- Faction playbooks: quick-start guides for factions that want to be the cool bad guy with a soft spot for cats. They help you seed rivalries without turning the city into a chessboard.
- Clue templates: modular clues that slot into a scene and can be swapped depending on how players approach the investigation. You can rearrange, remix, or repurpose in minutes.

#### Ramping and Pacing
- Session pacing: a few well placed reveals can take a night from meh to to be continued. The pacing tips emphasize street-level tempo over cinematic blast radius.
- Difficulty notes: tips for balancing street-level danger against the dramatic import of each clue. The idea is to create tension without forcing the table into a fixed heroic arc.

#### A Quick Scene: The Neon Alley
One night, you drop the players into an alley where the neon lights flicker in a rhythm that matches the beat in their bones. A boarded storefront hides a doorway into a forgotten tunnel. The clue chain leads to a street vendor who sells dreams in exchange for memories. The players must decide whether to steal a memory, bargain for it, or let it go. This mini scene uses the new loci and clue chains to keep momentum high without collapsing into a boss fight. The scene supports multiple endings depending on how players negotiate with the memory seller and whether they choose to protect a minor NPC from a vendetta.

#### More Quick Scenes to Spark a Night
- The Echo Bus: a city bus that never stops and carries passengers who claim to be memories of people the world forgot. A passenger can reveal a forgotten crime if the players prove they can listen without blinking.
- The Rainbound Crosswalk: a crossing that floods when a myth passes by. The clue path leads to a dealer of rainwater potions and a choice that can reshape a neighboring district's weather pattern for a night.
- The Lantern Shopfront: a tiny shop that keeps light alive in the darkest alleys. The owner sells guidance and misdirection in equal measure, and a single choice can tilt the chapter toward mercy or vengeance.
- The Midnight Ferry: a ghostly ferry that appears on foggy nights, carrying passengers who forgot where they came from. Clues on the boat hint at a larger scheme tied to a city-wide blackout.

### Art, Layout, and Production Values
The art direction stays faithful to City of Mist neon noir while On the Streets tightens the focus. You will see more day-to-day city life—the kind of details that make your players lean in: a rain-streaked window, a coffee cup left with a lipstick kiss, a mural whose eyes seem to follow you as you walk by. The layout is clean enough to be printable on the back of a napkin, but with the polish of an actual book. The production values support a mood rather than a wall of text, which is essential when you want the players to feel the city breathing around them. Expect a few new chase diagrams and flowchart silhouettes to guide you during tense sessions.

### Pros, Cons, and Final Verdict

Pros
- Tight integration with the core system and a palpable shift toward street-level storytelling
- Strong focus on urban myth, texture, and investigative momentum
- Flexible clue chaining and flavor that can slot into many campaigns without forcing a particular conspiracy
- A toolkit that thrives on micro-scene design and quick-start momentum that respects a single-night play rhythm

Cons
- Some players may want a bigger city-wide conspiracy rather than slice-of-life mysteries
- A few sections assume a noir tone that might not fit every group
- The vibe is very noir; players who prefer sci-fi or high fantasy may need to adjust tone for fit

Final verdict: On the Streets is a welcome addition for groups who want to keep their stories grounded in a real, rain-soaked city full of texture, with breadcrumbs to tempt a detective into late-night adventures. If your table thrives on social intrigue, offbeat mythic reveals, and quick-start scenes you can drop mid-session, this supplement is absolutely worth a look.

### Final Recommendation

If you prize sharp, street-level myth-making and you want a toolkit that makes your sessions feel like episodes of a stylish TV noir, On the Streets earns a sturdy recommendation. It is not a conspiracy box; it is a portable, mood-soaked toolbox for bringing City of Mist to life in the alleys, bars, and back doors of your city. The content is forgiving of missteps and generous with improvisation prompts, making it a dependable companion for campaigns that breathe between the panel borders of a big arc.
