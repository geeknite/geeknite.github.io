 Welcome, fellow geeks, to a mining town where the rocks talk back and the dwarves are drama magnets. Today at Geeknite, we pit two Amigo card games against each other in a battle for tunnel supremacy, gold fever, and trust issues so extreme you could mine them into a new cave system. Yes, we’re talking about Saboteur and Saboteur 2, two brothers from the same crafty family who both want to steal your heart and your treasure—sometimes quite literally in the same round.

 ## Introduction: Why two Saboteurs, you ask?
 Saboteur, in its essence, is a social deduction card game about dwarves digging tunnels to find gold. The twist is that not all dwarves want the same thing: some are miners with honest glow-in-the-dark ambitions, others are saboteurs who’d rather unleash a cave-in and watch the chaos erupt. The original is a crowd-pleaser at family game nights and at con suites when you want a quick, punchy session. Saboteur 2, the sequel, doubles down on the chaos with new roles, more cards, and options to bend the story even further toward glorious mayhem.

 In this two-for-one review, we’ll cover both titles, how they play, what they improve on from each other, and who should buy which. If you’re a purist who loves the classic vibe, you might lean Saboteur 1. If you’re a chaos connoisseur who wants more mischief in a single night, Saboteur 2 is your jam. By the end, you’ll know whether to keep one copy on your shelf, or to start stacking them like stacked mine carts—ready for a convoy of comedic betrayals.

 ## The Cast: Saboteur I and Saboteur II
 
 Saboteur I — The Classic
 Saboteur I comes with a lean deck of path cards and a simple, bright premise: build a tunnel from the starting point to a treasure card. The clever bit is the role assignment that happens behind the scenes. Some players are miners who want to reach the treasure by laying down safe tunnels. Others are saboteurs who secretly throw in extra turns, dead-ends, or fake paths to push the miners off course. The round ends when a team reaches the treasure or when a misstep forces the tunnel to collapse. It’s fast, it’s chaotic, and it’s perfect for newcomers and veterans alike.
 
 Saboteur II — The Sequel with Upgraded Mischief
 Saboteur 2 shakes the moral foundation of your gaming table with additional roles and more path cards. It adds twists like new sabotage actions and variants that let players mix in different victory conditions. The extra variety makes every round feel fresh while preserving the core mechanic you love: trust is a liar’s best friend and a miner’s best nightmare. If you’ve played Saboteur 1 dozens of times and want something new without throwing away the core concept, Saboteur 2 is a natural progression.

 ## Componentry and Aesthetic: Amigo’s Touch
 
 Both games come in a compact box with a healthy pile of cards and a handful of wooden or cardboard tokens that represent gold and victory conditions. The art style leans cartoonish and warm, with dwarves that look ready to argue about who deserves the gold more than who deserves the last piece of bread. The tile-like path cards feel tactile enough for casual play, and the color coding helps newbies identify roles at a glance. The card stock is sturdy enough for repeated nights of punch-destined, coffee-fueled play sessions, but if you’re planning a festival, you might want to sleeve the cards to ensure longevity.

 The Amigo edition shines with a friendly, accessible design that invites both players who enjoy party-game vibes and those who prefer a touch more strategy. It’s not a mind-bender like some heavier titles; it’s a social game that thrives on storytelling, misdirection, and the occasional dramatic gasp when the tunnel suddenly collapses and someone yells, “Trust me, I did not sabotage that on purpose.”

 ## How to Play: A Quick-Start Guide
 
 Saboteur I (core ideas)
 - Setup: Each player receives a hidden role (miner or saboteur). The mine tunnel layout is represented by a grid of path cards starting at a fixed entrance.
 - Objective: Miners want to connect the entrance to a treasure card at the far end; saboteurs want to derail that plan.
 - Turn structure: On your turn, you can lay a path card, play a special action card (to block, rotate, or redirect paths), or draw. The path cards connect to form a tunnel, but walls and hazards might appear if you’re crafty enough to slip them in.
 - End condition: A mine shaft that reaches the treasure ends the round. If the treasure is reached, miners collect gold; if sabotage collapses a tunnel or misleads, saboteurs collect the glory.
 
 Saboteur II adds more layers
 - New roles and variations keep the tension high, with extra sabotage tools and alternate victory paths.
 - Expanded deck means more combinations of paths and events you can drop mid-game, keeping players on their toes.
 - The setup remains breezy, the playtime remains social, and the chaos increases in a delightful way.
 
 For folks who adore reading the room, the saboteur gameplay rewards you for spotting tells, reading table talk, and predicting who might be planning to ruin the tunnel before anyone even finishes their first turn. If you’ve played the classic, Saboteur II offers a deeper pool of mischief while maintaining the same approachable rhythm.

 ## Strategies and Table Talk: Reading the Dwarves
 
 If you’re a Miner, you want to maintain a calm, collaborative aura while quietly shading information about your candidates for the path to the treasure. Share enough information to keep your fellow miners invested, but not so much that the saboteurs know exactly where to place their sabotage. If you’re a Saboteur, you win by spreading doubt—don’t be too obvious, but don’t be so cryptic that your fellow saboteurs betray you. The best saboteurs act like a chess coach on caffeine: guiding others toward the gold while pretending to be a loyal partner.

 In Saboteur II, with more roles and cards, you’ll want to adapt mid-game. If a player has positioned themselves as a hero of reliability, you might slip a “dead end” card to force them to reconsider their route. The more chaos you generate early, the more you’ll learn about who is likely to betray you next round—and that makes subsequent rounds even more deliciously tense.

 ## A Word on Theme and Tone: Humor First, Betrayal Second
 
 The theme of underground mining is a fun canvas for social dynamics. The dwarves bicker about lanterns, about who gets to lead the way, and about whether the treasure is worth the risk of a collapse. The humor isn’t dark; it’s light, goofy, and perfect for a night where you want to bond with friends or family while also trying to outwit them. The game rewards storytelling almost as much as it rewards clever card placement. If you’re the type who loves a good-table talk moment, Saboteur delivers in spades.

 ## Player Counts and Session Tempo
 
Both Saboteur and Saboteur 2 shine at group sizes. They scale from smaller groups around a coffee table to larger gatherings where you squeeze extra chairs into the living room. The typical session runs 20 to 40 minutes depending on player count and how many times you momentarily blindfold reality with your own misdirections. That makes it ideal for game-night slots between longer Eurogames and party nights where you just want to laugh at how a tunnel becomes a metaphor for your dating life—now with more gold and no relationship advice required.

 If you’re aiming for a quick round while waiting for the pizza to arrive, Saboteur hits a sweet spot. For a full game-night marathon, you can stack Saboteur 2 to keep the narrative going and watch the room devolve into a chorus of mock conspiracies.

 ## Artwork, Components, and Setup Time: What to Expect
 
 Amigo’s production values here lean toward approachable and dyed-in-the-wool gamer-friendly. The art is bright, the icons readable, and the cards are sturdy enough for repeated showdowns at your coffee table. Setup time is modest; you can be game-ready in under five minutes once everyone knows their role shuffle. The only potential pitfall is that the game’s pace can slow a touch when players overthink their bluffing or when someone insists on a perfectly narrated tunnel-building sequence. If you’ve got a group that thrives on banter, you’ll adore that; if you’re after a heavier, more mechanical experience, you might want to pair Saboteur with something else in your rotation.

 ## The Amigo Experience: Why This Publisher Works for This Theme
 
Amigo Spiele has a knack for producing games with accessible rules and strong social components. Saboteur leverages that design philosophy: easy to teach, quick to play, and immensely replayable. The two entries give you a progression from pure, classic chaos to more chaotic but balanced chaos. If you’re new to the Amigo catalog, Saboteur is a friendly entry point; if you’ve already enjoyed it, Saboteur 2 is a natural next step to deepen your misdirection game without changing the core bones you already love.

 ## Final Recommendation: How to Build Your Saboteur Night
 
- If you’re new to the universe and want a fast, social, endlessly replayable pick, start with Saboteur I. Its rules are clean, its tension is immediate, and the laughs come free with the first round.
- If you crave more spice, Saboteur II stacks the deck with new roles and extra cards to keep the mischief flowing for longer rounds. It’s the ideal follow-up for groups that love a long-running campaign of deception at game night.
- If you run a large gaming night or you’re curating a “mining-masquerade” theme, you might actually want both in rotation. They pair nicely, especially after a few rounds when players are already comfortable with the core mechanic and just want to see what new chaos a different role can unleash.
- If you’re pausing between heavier titles or want a “light but sharp” palate cleanser, Saboteur remains a solid choice that won’t derail the group’s energy and will keep you laughing longer than you thought possible.
