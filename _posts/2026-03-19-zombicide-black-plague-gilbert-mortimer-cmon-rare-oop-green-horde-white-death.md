## Who are Gilbert and Mortimer?

Gilbert and Mortimer aren’t your garden-variety survivors who learned how to swing a blade and whistle a jaunty tune while dodging undead. No, these two are a pair of seasoned, borderline-idiosyncratic veterans who bring a flavor of “war-til-you-drop” to the board. Gilbert is the stubborn old-timer with a soft spot for practical jokes that only a board-game table could appreciate. Mortimer, his partner in crime (and undoubtedly in snacks), is the caretaker of old-school tactics, a walking encyclopedia of bumbling bravado, and the kind of survivor who makes you wonder if you should have brought a larger bag of dice just to keep up with his schemes.

If you’re cataloging them by hobby archetypes, Gilbert would be your grumpy-but-lovable tactician, while Mortimer is the enthusiastic, risk-loving optimist who keeps the plan chaotic enough to survive the night. Put them together, and you get a pairing that plays with the same energy you’d expect from two people adjusting a fire extinguisher’s nozzle while a Molotov of zombies dances in the background. Their synergy isn’t just mechanical; it’s a micro-story at the table. You’ll be telling stories about their stubborn decision-making long after the last card is drawn, and that’s the point of a good CMON character pack: to spark narrative as much as to buff a party’s stats.

The miniatures themselves are a quintessential CMON experience—crisp lines, dynamic poses, and enough character in the faces to make you feel like you’ve interrupted a conversation between an old war-tale and a campy horror spoof. If you’re into painting, Gilbert’s grizzled beard and Mortimer’s weathered goggles give you ample texture to play with. If you aren’t, their static poses still read well on the board and don’t demand that you become a miniature-artist-in-residence to appreciate them.

## The Rare OOP Quandary

The “Rare OOP” tag is a double-edged sword. On one edge, it makes Gilbert and Mortimer the Holy Grail of Zombicide collectors, the kind of item you see priced in the upper echelons of secondhand marketplaces, the same way you might watch a vintage action figure slowly appreciate in the light of a bad decision. On the other edge, it makes the decision to add them to your collection a form of investment that’s almost as nerdy as the hobby itself.

OOP status isn’t a moral judgment; it’s a practical reality. CMON has re-released some components, re-sculpted some minis, and rotated through different bundles. Gilbert and Mortimer exist in that breadcrumbs-on-the-floor space: you can track the resin crumbs and the plastic footprints of a limited release long enough to piece together how they eventually ended up where they are today—prized, scarce, and a little bit mythical among players who started collecting during the group’s “Season 1: the Great Miniature Hunt.”

As a reviewer, I’ll admit: the chase adds to the lore. It increases the dramatic tension when you finally spot a mint condition set on a well-known auction site at a price that would make your mortgage lender raise an suspicious eyebrow. But that same chase can obscure the actual gameplay value. The pair’s capabilities must stand on their own, regardless of price tags and collectible cycles. And in that department, Gilbert and Mortimer tend to deliver more than a polite nod—they bring flavor, tactical options, and a reason to re-engineer a round even when the dice are conspiring to end your run in a single zombie queue.

If you’re new to the OOP market, think of Gilbert and Mortimer as a dream of “what if this pair had a more meaningful impact than just decorative status?” The answer, in practice, varies by your table’s vibe. Some groups treat them as a key strategic piece, enabling a tempo shift that keeps the board’s narrative moving. Others treat them as a collectible, something to admire across the table while the rest of the team mashes the nearest zombie with a plank. Either way, rarity amplifies the conversation around the game, and that’s not nothing.
