Special Guest Boxes in Zombicide are a bit like surprise party poppers for tabletop gamers. You know something big is coming, but you are not always sure what shape the confetti will take. The Adrian Smith edition leans hard into the artist moniker, offering striking artwork, a heavier emphasis on flavor, and a set of components that feel premium without turning your wallet into a hero sandwich of regret.

### First impressions

When you crack the lid, the color palette does not whisper it is going to be a quiet affair. It screams in a glorious, messy way about ruined castles, moonlit forests, and monstrosities that look like they learned art from a heavy metal album cover. The minis have that sturdy, slightly oversized look that Zombicide fans know and love, which is perfect for painting or for letting your friends pretend they are playing on a diorama rather than a board game.

### Box contents and components

The box includes a curated mix of miniatures, character cards, and a smattering of art-forward extras that make you feel like you are collecting a limited edition gallery piece rather than just another box of plastic troops. You get a handful of poseable survivors with distinctive sculpts, a couple of new zombie variants that carry the Adrian Smith aesthetic with gusto, and a boss-lord that looks like it walked straight out of a poster for a monsoon of mayhem. There is also a premium cardstock insert and a compact art booklet featuring concept art and color proofs that would feel at home on a coffee table in a metal band rehearsal room.

### Build quality and presentation

If you have bought some of the other special edition Zombicide sets, you will recognize the care here. There is a sense that you are not merely collecting pieces but owning a small, curated piece of art that also happens to function as a game resource.

## The Adrian Smith touch: art meets tabletop horror

Adrian Smith is known for a particular flavor of fantasy and horror art that blends epic grotesquerie with dynamic composition. On the table, this translates into minis that seem to pose against you—whether they are the survivors standing defiantly with their makeshift armor or the zombies that look as if they are mid-screech, their faces frozen in a moment of chaotic grace.

### The two faces of art temptation

The special guest box is a demonstration of how art can elevate gameplay. When you look at the paint jobs and the sculpting, you get the sense that you are not watching generic zombie chaos but a narrative in miniature form. The survivors have silhouettes that read well from across the table, which is crucial in a game that thrives on player engagement and quick recognition of threats. The zombies, meanwhile, carry the “we are here to ruin your day” vibe with a level of detail that makes you want to pick up a brush and try to replicate it—if only your hand did not tremble with the adrenaline of a sudden dinnertime surge of walkers.

### Theme integration

The art direction aligns with Black Plague’s medieval fantasy setting. Dark stone textures, elemental accents, and armor plating on the survivors reflect a synergy between the two brands of chaos that Zombicide has exported to fans. This is not a loose artistic license; it is a deliberate attempt to weave Adrian Smith’s signature into the fabric of the game’s world. If you enjoy crossover fantasy art, this box scratches that itch with a robust, playable hitch of atmosphere.

## Contents breakdown and gameplay impact

While I am not going to pretend that a single box will reinvent Zombicide Black Plague, there are tangible gameplay touches that the Special Guest Box adds to the experience. The following is a practical read on what actually affects your turn, your dice, and your post-game resin-dust cleanup.

### The miniatures you actually use in a game

Two new survivor minis bring fresh sculpts to your party. Their equipment and outfits are designed to look like they have navigated the Black Plague world with grit and stubborn optimism. You can imagine the conversations they would have if they could talk: why carry a long bow into a dungeon full of undead, or why insist on wearing armor that would probably get snagged on a doorframe every single round? The survivors are not only visually distinct but come with unique ability cards that subtly shift how you approach problems during a run. Expect a boost to ranged weapons, some tactical movement bonuses, and a few “oh no you didn’t” moments that remind you why cooperative play is both strategy and theater.

Two zombie variants are included as the shadow side of the coin. They import Adrian Smith’s aesthetic into the swarm in a way that looks simultaneously graceful and terrifying. These variants are not just reskins; they carry altered stats or abilities that require you to adapt your plan mid-game. The box leans into the sense that the zombie hordes have learned from their mistakes as the campaign presses forward—each new wave carrying its own flavor of dread and a reminder that not all necromancy is created equal.

### A special boss, with a name that demands respect

The boss miniature (the big boss monster that can end your run in an instant if you pull the wrong lever) is a highlight. It’s bigger, it’s meaner, and it has that extra sheen of “art commission” that makes it feel like you have earned a trophy for surviving a particularly brutal encounter. The boss card aligns with the new art direction, delivering a few new mechanics that change how you approach the final table battle. It is not a gimmick; it is a genuine hard bump to the game’s late-stage tension. If you like the “boss goes wild” moment in many co-op games, you will appreciate how this version of boss chaos lands in your play sessions.

### Cards, boards, and the tactile joy of components

The card stock in the Special Guest Box is sturdy enough to survive a couple of campaigns without warping. The survivor cards and the boss card feature enhanced art with a zoning that makes it easy to read during frantic turns. There are a handful of tokens and perhaps a small double-sided board section used to represent new environmental hazards or thematic zones. The tactile experience of flipping a card, placing a token, and feeling the weight of a new tile is a reminder of why we collect tabletop components in the first place: the joy of interaction as a physical thing, not just a mental map.

### Value and price considerations

Value is a slippery concept in game expansions. If your table is the type that collects Deluxe Editions and uses premium paints to tint every miniature, the Adrian Smith Special Guest Box will feel like a justified splurge. For casual players or those on a tighter budget, the box might feel more like a luxury upgrade than a necessity. In the end, the value boils down to your appetite for art, for narrative depth, and for the quick nostalgia that comes from adding a new artist’s signature to your favorite game. You are paying for art, for a set of unique sculpts, and for a few new gameplay hooks that can refresh a familiar loop.
