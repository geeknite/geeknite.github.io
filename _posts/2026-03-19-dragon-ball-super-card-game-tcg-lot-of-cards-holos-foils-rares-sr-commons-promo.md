### Holos and Foils
Holos and foils are the glitter of the pool. They catch the eye like a neon sign in a dungeon and they do not always translate into strategic value. The key with holos is to understand that not all shine is created equal. Some holos pop with pure color and crisp borders, others are a tad muted but still pleasant on the table. Expect a distribution that skews toward a handful of strong holos and a larger group of mid tier shine. 

From a playability standpoint, holos often correspond to rares or higher rarity cards that have slightly higher impact in terms of effect or synergy. They are the kind of cards that can tilt the balance in a mid game swing when drawn at the right moment. If you are chasing a particular archetype, holos can be the difference between a deck that looks flashy and a deck that actually performs. Use the holo pool to anchor your deck tech, but do not rely on them to be your only strategy. Inevitably, you will need some reliable commons to get your energy and combo engine running. 

When you sort the holos, consider the following: is the holo a key finisher, a utility card, or a defensive shield? Are there foil versions of the same card that offer a better effect or slightly better cost? Some foils are more collectible than practical, but in a meta where players chase chase cards, even a flashy foil offense piece can shift the social dynamics of a match.

### Rares and SRs
Rares and SRs bridge the gap between casual collection and competitive deck building. In this lot, you will likely find a handful of rares that are perfectly serviceable in a wide range of decks, supported by SRs that push toward more specialized synergy. The key to exploitation here is to map your rares toward a coherent engine. A rare with a strong on play effect can anchor a curve in the early mid game, while an SR that enables a specific combo can carry you through a critical turn or two when you cannot draw your premium pieces. 

Shop talk aside, you want to identify the rares that actually enable your deck, rather than simply occupy space. If you pick up a handful of SRs that complement your core strategy, you can avoid the dreaded filler pit where a deck is forced to jam in cards that do not contribute to a clean plan. The best SRs in a lot like this often function as part of a multi-card combo, where one piece by itself is decent, but the synergy makes the entire sequence worth it. 

### Commons and Uncommons
The backbone of any deck is its commons. The commons are the unsung heroes who get you to your big plays without breaking the bank or forcing you to rely on a lottery draw. In this lot, you should expect a decent contingent of commons that can fill your early game role, accelerate energy, or draw you deeper into the stack. Common cards shine when used to set up consistent turns and to smooth out mana or energy ramp. Think of them as the engine oil of your deck, preventing the gears from grinding to a halt. 

When evaluating commons, pay attention to the low cost, high impact candidates. Some commons will offer perfect tempo plays that keep your opponent from setting their own plan. Others may provide essential removal or counterplay at a price that barely holds the line. Do not overlook the uncommons either, which often sit in a sweet spot between power and cost. They can be the difference between a deck that stalls and a deck that executes a reliable plan. 

> Tip from the trenches: separate the pool into core, flexible, and fallback cards. Core cards stay in your main list, flexible cards can slot into multiple archetypes, and fallback cards are only used when you need a tech answer to a specific meta problem. This triage will save you hours of sleeving and sleepless nights while building.
