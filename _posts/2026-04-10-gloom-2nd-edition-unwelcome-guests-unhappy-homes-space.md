Gloom is a game about making your family regret its life choices. If that sounds harsh, it’s because Gloom leans into the tragedy like a goth at a sunny beach. The 2nd Edition already did a sterling job of making you root for the character you’re about to accidentally doom, but the Unwelcome Guests, Unhappy Homes expansions, and the Gloom Space detour are the kind of triple-threat combo that makes you snort with laughter and weep into your scorepad at roughly the same time.

In this review, we’ll dive into what each expansion brings to the table, how they interact with the base game, whether the void-friendly space vibes actually fit the Gloom mood, and if you should splurge for the full bundle or cherry-pick the add-ons. If you want to nerd out with the original gloom lore first, you can check our [primer on gloom basics]({{ post_url '2024-10-01-gloom-primer' }}). For expansion-specific rumination and a few spicy tales, there’s also a deeper dive under [Unwelcome Guests]({{ post_url '2025-02-14-gloom-expansion-unwelcome-guests' }}).

With all that said, let’s descend into a pit of fabulously morose mood cards, gently haunted basements, and starry corridors that look suspiciously like a black hole made of felt.

## The Core of Gloom 2nd Edition
Gloom’s core logic is simple on the surface: you want your family to experience the most spectacularly tragic misfortunes, while your opponents push their characters toward sunshine and virtue. The 2nd Edition refines card stock, streamlines the gloom-charm aesthetic, and improves readability so you can doom your relatives without asking them to decipher your cryptic soul-scorching handwriting.

What makes Gloom stand out, beyond the melancholy atmosphere and the very satisfying “ding!” of a truly awful fate, is its storytelling potential. The cards aren’t just tools for scoring; they’re prompts for your own micro-dramas during play. Add the Unwelcome Guests, Unhappy Homes, and Gloom Space expansions, and you’ll have a sandbox full of macabre comedy that is more theatrical than your last family Zoom call.

## Unwelcome Guests: The Nightmarish Doorbell Rings
Unwelcome Guests introduces a handful of new guests who crash the gloom party uninvited but somehow necessary. These aren’t your average poltergeists; they come with their own little narrative hooks, quirks, and synergies that can tilt the balance in unpredictable ways.

### What’s New in the Box
- A fresh cadre of guest cards with distinct negative effects that often chain together, creating deliciously cruel sympathy spirals.
- New rules to track “guest visits,” which can complicate timing and force you to adjust your doom strategy mid-snack break.
- Thematic art that feels both stylishly ominous and a touch cheeky—your uncle’s fortune-telling ghost has never looked cooler on a card before.

### How It Changes Gameplay
The core twist of Unwelcome Guests is that your doom envelope isn’t entirely under your own control anymore. Guests can alter the order of doom, occasionally forcing you to derail a long-term plan to prevent a single, spectacular catastrophe. You’ll find yourself politely begging a poltergeist to hold off for one more turn, all while another family member looks on with that “are you serious” eye-raise only seen after you announce you’re going to doom your in-laws next.

### Thematic Flavor vs. Mechanical Weight
If you love the flavor of gloom, Unwelcome Guests nails it. It adds personality without dragging the tempo down into analysis paralysis. However, the additional bookkeeping can feel a little fiddly for new players who still haven’t squeezed all the fun out of “play a card, doom a face.” It’s not heavy, but it does lean into the “keep track of misery” vibe a bit more than the base game.

## Unhappy Homes: The House That Spooks Built
Unhappy Homes shifts the architecture from haunted mansions to more domestic dread. Where Unwelcome Guests introduces external influences, Un Happy Homes focuses on the internal microcosm of each family, turning rooms into mood-state zones with their own twists.

### What It Adds
- Room-based effects that interact with the family cards, creating synergy opportunities or deadly mismatches if you’re not careful.
- A few card types that make “home improvement” feel like a curse—because it is, obviously, in a world where you’re rolling doom dice instead of dice of fortune.
- A refreshed aesthetic that makes the gloom feel domestic, which is both funny and unsettling at the same time.

### Tactical Shifts
A well-balanced strategy with Unhappy Homes involves carefully sequencing your plays so you maximize the negative impact while keeping control of the narrative you’re weaving. The rooms encourage you to think in terms of “story arcs” instead of immediate card-by-card doom, which is a welcome shift for players who enjoy the storytelling aspect of Gloom more than the pure optimization. If you’re a completionist who wants all of the flavor without sacrificing tempo, this expansion strikes a nice balance—though you’ll want a little patience for the bookkeeping here as well.

## Gloom Space: The Final Frontier of Doom and Glee
Gloom Space takes the melancholy from the manor and shoots it into orbit. If you’ve ever wondered what would happen if Saturn’s rings were made of mood cards, this expansion is your answer. The space theme is more than a cosmetic re-skin; it recontextualizes doom in a setting where gravity is optional, and existential dread is perfectly acceptable as a standard airlock conversation.

### Thematic Brilliance
The sci-fi lens is surprisingly compatible with gloom’s core premise. You still manage miseries for your family; you just do it in a vacuum with little intention to return to Earth. The art direction leans into cosmic noir, and the imagery of astronauts doom-faring through a black hole of reception emails is nothing short of delightful.

### Mechanical Innovations
- New space-themed doom tokens and event cards that alter how you calculate misery, sometimes forcing you to stack multiple misfortunes in a single cosmic event.
- A lightweight “gravity” mechanic that changes how you lay out and resolve family outcomes, adding a tiny but elegant layer of spatial planning to a game that thrives on narrative momentum.
- A few oddball interactions that make you rethink when to deploy a particular card, because in space, timing is less about turn order and more about orbit alignment.

### Gameplay Pacing
Gloom Space tends to accelerate the mood as a session progresses. You’ll feel like you’re being tugged along by a funhouse-mirror space captain who keeps telling you that your doom is “in the next orbit,” only to reveal another orbit and a couple of extra misfortunes you hadn’t anticipated. It’s goofy, it’s tense, and it somehow makes your table feel like an interstellar ghost ship crew that forgot to clock out after a night shift. If you adore high-variance, narrative-style doom, Space is a glorious addition.

## Mechanics, Mood, and the Art of Card Comedy
Across all three expansions, the core loot is: more cards, more stories, and more opportunities to misinterpret your own family’s fate. The 2nd Edition already has clean card stock and legible typography; the expansions preserve that readability while introducing cards with bolder color palettes so you can spot your doom at a glance mid-heckling your opponent across the table.

### Card Design Quality
The art direction weaves gloom with humor. You’ll see silhouettes, moody shadows, and just enough whimsy to remind you that this is a game you play with friends, not a horror film you watch alone in a cave. The expansions maintain the same tactile feel as the base set so you don’t have to re-learn the stack management you’ve grown to love (or hate, depending on your competitive streak).

### Rule Clarity and Learning Curve
If you’ve played Gloom before, you’ll be back in the saddle quickly. The expansions don’t radically rewrite core rules; they layer in new card types and triggers that your brain will adapt to with a single light groan. If you’re new to Gloom, you’ll pick up the base rules first and then dive into expansions as you’re comfortable—the learning curve is friendly, and the humor acts as a reliable mnemonic for complex interactions.

## Representation, Artwork, and Accessibility
Gloom has always done well with art that’s accessible but atmospheric. The 2nd Edition maintains clear iconography that reduces that moment of “wait, what does this symbol do again?” You won’t be flipping back to the rulebook mid-game as often as on some heavier card games. The expansions add new vocabulary (guest types, room effects, space phenomena) that you’ll pick up through play rather than rote recitation.

From an accessibility standpoint, the game remains approachable for mixed groups: casual players enjoy the storytelling; power players enjoy the subtle optimization that comes from knowing when to doom the right character to maximize narrative payoff. The expansions add variety without overwhelming complexity, which is the sweet spot for a party-friendly yet gamer-pleasing experience.

## Replayability and Table Dynamics
One of the enduring strengths of Gloom is how replayable it is, given its modular mood and the randomness of cards. Unwelcome Guests, Unhappy Homes, and Gloom Space each add new arcs and antagonists, which means you’re not going to see the same doom sequence twice in a single campaign. The balance between luck and strategy remains a central theme; the expansions shift the balance toward storytelling, with enough unpredictability to keep sessions fresh.

## Value, Setup, and Longevity
If you’re the kind of person who will eventually complete a “Gloom collection” and empty your piggy bank in glorious spectral fashion, the bundle is tempting. The setup time remains quick, which is essential for a game that thrives on table banter and quick doom turns rather than heavy downtime. The cost is a function of how you value replayability and thematic variety. If you love Gloom’s vibe and want to taste-test all the expansions to see what sticks, getting the full bundle can be a satisfying long-term investment.

If you’re primarily a solo or two-player household, you’ll still get a ton of mileage here. The expansions scale well, and the narrative prompts translate nicely into shorter play sessions when you’re squeezing in a few chapters of gloom between chores.

## Final Verdict: Should You Dive In?
If you already adore Gloom 2nd Edition, the Unwelcome Guests and Unhappy Homes expansions will feel like a natural flavor upgrade rather than a radical overhaul. They preserve the core pacing and emotional cadence while injecting new characters and conundrums that keep the narrative engine running smoothly. Gloom Space, with its space-clustered doom and cosmic oddities, is the wildcard you’ll either love for its audacity or mock for its sci-fi silliness—depending on whether your crew enjoys a space oddity or a space oddity that’s secretly a mood card.

For players who want a fuller, more varied gloom experience without stepping into a heavy math labyrinth, this trio of expansions is a slam dunk. The balance is solid; you’ll still feel tension in every doom reveal, and you’ll be delighted by how a single card can twist the story in a dozen different directions. If you’re new to the hobby and want to test the waters with a robust and approachable game, you could start with the base Gloom 2nd Edition and sprinkle in the expansions only after you’ve built a floor of memes and misfortune with your regular gaming group.

In terms of price-to-fun ratio, the bundle lands in the “worth it if you’re committed to a long-term gloom lifestyle” category. If you’re in the market for a gift that guarantees a night of giggles and groans, this set delivers. It’s not merely a collection of cards; it’s a living, breathing doom narrative you’ll tell around the table again and again, with new punchlines each session.
