### The base rules in a sentence
Skip-Bo is a race to shed your stock piles by playing cards in the correct order into build piles. You can only place cards in ascending order from 1 up to 12, and you can use wild cards to substitute any number when needed. The catch is that you must have a hand of five cards and you need to manage your stock pile carefully because running out of stock means you are out of the game unless someone is generous enough to bail you out with a wild card. It is a rhythm game with numbers and a social twist that makes players cheer for the luck and douse the flames of envy when an opponent hits a perfect run.

## How to play quickly and with style
This section is for the impatient who want to understand the flow without memorizing every minor rule. The core loop goes like this: draw a card, look at your five hand cards, attempt to place cards into your build piles in the correct order, draw again, and repeat until you have no stock cards left. Each turn you can do multiple plays to clear your hand and feed your stock. The wild cards add spice by substituting any needed number, but they are limited; you cannot simply replace every missing piece with a wild card. The strategic arc comes from deciding when to use wilds, which cards to hold for future turns, and how to block opponents from completing their runs.

### Turn structure and common moves
- Draw two cards from the stock for your turn, then you can lay down any number of cards from your hand to build piles, as long as the numbers ascend.
- If you place a card that is not immediately useful, you can discard it to your personal discard stack or to a central discard array, depending on the variant you are playing.
- When you finish laying down cards, you draw back up to five cards in hand. Your goal is to keep your stock pile shrinking while keeping your options open for future plays.
- Wilds are your burn cards. Use them when a missing number would otherwise block your progress, but be mindful not to squander them on small gains.

The Complete Edition shines when you play with a mixed group of players who span ages and temperament. Beginners can lean on others and catch up quickly, while experienced players can craft micro strategies that make you feel like you just discovered a Sudoku that prints money.

## Strategy notes and tips
Skip-Bo rewards smart planning more than brute luck, though luck still plays a part in who wins and who ends up apologizing for tossing a card into the wrong pile. Here are some practical tips to improve your odds without turning game night into a classroom drill:

- Manage your stock pile like a tiny dragon hoard. Every card you burn from stock is a small victory, so plan your moves to release a chunk of stock with minimal waste.
- Use wild cards sparingly. They are like the peak performers of the deck, capable of carrying you across a rough patch, but they are not unlimited. Save them for moments when you can clinch a critical run or disrupt an opponent who is about to win.
- Keep a mental eye on the board state. If you notice a specific number is missing across multiple builds, an opponent might be eyeing that number on their next turn. Your response should balance speed with defense.
- Build in alignment with your hand. If your hand contains a sequence that can be moved to the build piles, prioritize those moves to free up your hand and accelerate the process of shedding cards.
- Be adaptable. The best laid plans can crash and burn if someone drops a wild card at the wrong time. The beauty of Skip-Bo is that flexibility often trumps raw power.

## Quick start guide for a 15 minute intro
1) Set up the game: shuffle the deck, deal five face down to the stock each player, place five face up in your hand. 2) The starting build piles are in the center. 3) On your turn draw until you have five cards in hand. 4) Lay down as many cards as you can from your hand into the build piles in ascending order. 5) When you can no longer play from your hand, discard one card and end your turn. 6) The next player goes. The race is on. The card counts may be random, but your decisions can be deliberate. 7) When a player finishes their stock pile, the round ends and you count the chips or points to determine the winner if you are playing a multi round format.
