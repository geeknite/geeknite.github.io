## How to play: the core loop
The No Mercy edition keeps the core UNO flow intact, but with changes that require you to head-to-head with the game’s own sense of justice. Here’s the streamlined version:

1) Shuffle and deal seven cards to each player. The rest becomes a draw pile. The top card flips to start the discard pile. If the first card is a Special or Draw card, follow its effect immediately.
2) On your turn, you may play a card that matches the color or the number/symbol on the top discard. If you can’t play, you must draw one card. If the drawn card is playable, you may immediately play it.
3) Special cards behave as they do in classic UNO (Skip, Reverse, Draw Two, Wild, Wild Draw Four), but with a No Mercy twist described below.
4) The round ends when a player runs out of cards, but the No Mercy mode tacks on mercy counters and merciful-appearing punishment for the rest of the table as the game continues.

The key shift comes with Mercy tokens and penalty cards. After certain plays or at the end of each round, players accumulate Mercy tokens or must draw penalty cards that stack. The mercy currency is meant to keep the pace moving and to give players a reason to “save face” while still facing a deliciously punitive endgame. You’ll quickly learn that mercy tokens are as valuable as actual mercy, especially when someone is one card away from victory and you’ve got a handful of reverse cards in your hand.

## The No Mercy twist explained
The No Mercy twist is where the party begins. There are two main features: Mercy tokens and mercy/penalty cards.

- Mercy Tokens: Earn them by successful plays that cause a chain reaction of color changes or by special combos. They can be used later to cancel a small penalty or force a re-draw of the top card from the draw pile. It’s a nice nod to “clever plays” but not so punishing that it isn’t fun.
- Mercy and Penalty Cards: The mercy cards are basically good-luck cards that reduce your penalty by a small amount, while the penalty cards force you to draw additional cards or perform silly tasks (like finishing your turn with your non-dominant hand, which is exactly the kind of humiliating challenge you want at a family game night).

This design yields a tension curve: early rounds are breezy, middle rounds feel like a slippery slope, and late rounds descend into glorious chaos where someone’s plan to “once again run out on the last card” meets the other players’ cunning misfires, and suddenly you’re counting mercy tokens like a tiny caffeinated accountant.

## House rules and variants
One of the joys of UNO is that you can customize it. No Mercy is no exception. Here are a few house-rule ideas that keep the party rolling:
- Mercy limit: cap the number of mercy tokens any player can hold at once to avoid token hoarding.
- Mercy-to-penalty conversion: allow players to trade mercy tokens for a small penalty reduction, but not vice versa.
- Team mode: pair players into teams; team members can swap hands at certain intervals, with mercy tokens shared between teammates.
- Quick-fire mode: set a shorter draw pile and reduce the number of cards drawn at penalties to speed things up.

## Strategy and tips for surviving the No Mercy gauntlet
- Read the table: The real skill is understanding when to push for a no-mercy moment and when to hold back. If you’re too aggressive, you’ll be out of mercy tokens before round two.
- Manage mercy tokens like a tiny wallet. Don’t waste them on minimal threats; save them for decisive moments when you can swing the endgame your way.
- Keep track of who has which cards: if you notice a player who’s racking up penalty cards, you might time your plays to force them into the next big penalty discard. This is not morally pure, but it is strategically delicious.
- Use wild cards strategically: a Wild or Wild Draw Four can reset the table’s tempo and give you a momentary advantage. But be mindful: using it at the wrong moment may reveal your hand to the wrong people and invite scalding looks from your sister.
- DON’T overthink the penalties: some of them are silly, some are painful, and some are downright hilarious. Embrace the chaos; that’s half the point of Uno No Mercy.

## How this version stacks up against classic UNO
- The base gameplay is familiar, but the No Mercy twist adds a real-time social dynamic. Instead of a purely mechanical sprint to UNO, you get tactical social play with mercy tokens and penalty cards that require you to adapt on the fly.
