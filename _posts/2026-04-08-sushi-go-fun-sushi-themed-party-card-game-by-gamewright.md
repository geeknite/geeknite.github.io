## What is Sushi Go!

This section is your primer on the core idea behind Sushi Go!. The game is built around a straightforward drafting mechanic: each player starts with a hand of five cards. Each round, you pick one card from your hand and pass the rest to the next player. This creates a game-wide tension: you want cards that fit your plan, but you also want to disrupt others’ plans. The deck runs out after a handful of rounds, and the highest total wins. It is fast, frenetic, and charmingly silly—the perfect antidote to heavy Euro games on a bad day.

The card types form simple, bite-sized strategies: you want to collect sets of similar sushi for points, time your passes to block opponents, and occasionally chase the pudding card as a sneaky game-decider. The pudding, in particular, is a sly latecomer: it doesn’t yield immediate takedowns in the moment, but at the end of the game, it can swing a close finish. If you misplay, you’ll end with a pile of chalk-white rice and an existential crisis about what went wrong with your life choices. If you play well, you’ll look like a sushi savant with a knack for predicting what your tablemates will do next.

In the base game you’ll find a deck of around sixty cards and a handful of counting aids. The cards are sturdy enough for repeated shuffles, and the designs scale well for small hands and large paws alike. The setup is simple: shuffle, deal five cards to each player, and go. No complicated timers, no complicated scoring sheets, just a handful of minutes before you’re in the thick of it.

## Gameplay mechanics

### Drafting and passing

The centerpiece of Sushi Go! is the drafting mechanic. On each turn, you pick one card from your hand and pass the others to your neighbor. This creates a game-wide tension: you want cards that fit your plan, but you also want to disrupt others’ plans. The speed of passes and the simplicity of decisions make the game feel like a rapid-fire auction, except you’re bidding with chopsticks instead of dollar bills.

### Scoring and card values

Different card types score in different ways. For example, completing a set of a certain number of tempura cards yields points, while sashimi wants you to collect three cards to net a chunk of points. Nigiri cards have their own fixed values, and dumplings scale up as you collect more of them, turning a simple pile into a mini ladder. Wasabi acts as a multiplier: if you pair a later nigiri with a wasabi card, you often boost your points significantly.

Pudding is the wild card: it’s scored at the end of the game and often serves as a tiebreaker. If you’re ahead in pudding, you’ll want to keep your lead, but if you’re behind, you’ll need to chase pudding in the final rounds to have a shot. The end-game scoring is what often tips the balance in a close contest, which is a nice design touch that rewards both short-term and long-term planning.

### Game length and pacing

The pacing of Sushi Go! is one of its strongest suits. The rounds are short, the decisions quick, and the table chatter stays light. In a party setting, you can squeeze in several rounds in under an hour, which makes it ideal for game nights where you want something that’s fun but won’t outstay its welcome. If you’ve ever participated in a long-winded negotiation in another game, you’ll appreciate the clarity and briskness here.

### Strategy for different numbers of players

At 2-3 players, the drafting loop becomes intimate and you’ll have sharper cutlery ready to carve out your path. At 4-5 players, the variability increases as more hands come into play, increasing the chances that someone else will snatch a crucial card before you can claim it. The magic of Sushi Go! is how it scales gracefully: you still feel engaged with multiple players, and the game retains its responsiveness even as the table grows. It’s a rare trait for a compact card game to pull off so consistently.

## Strategy and depth

Sushi Go! rewards versatility: you’ll want to be able to adapt to what you’re dealt and to what others are taking. A few practical tips:
- Track the table’s tendencies. If your neighbor is always chasing dumplings, you can pivot to other sets to avoid feeding them the cards they want.
- Balance short-term gains with long-term potential. A card that completes a small set now might block a larger scoring opportunity later if you can couple it with wasabi or another sushi card.
- Use chopsticks deliberately. The chopsticks card is a two-step tool: you can use it to draft an additional card on a future turn, but only if you manage your hand correctly. It’s a tiny engine that rewards planning.
- Endgame awareness matters because pudding becomes a factor only at the end. If you’ve been ignoring pudding for most of the game, you might be surprised when the final tallies swing in a surprising direction.
