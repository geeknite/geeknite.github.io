## Who Are Fred, Ross, Will, and Helen?
If you’re like me and enjoy little personality-driven touches in your tabletop components, you’ll probably appreciate the naming convention. The designers clearly had a sense of humor: four dashboards with distinct vibes, almost like party members in a zombie RPG that forgot to read the instruction manual about moral alignment.

- Fred: the steady, reliable filler who always looks for a safe route and a spare blade. He’s the “no-nonsense” dashboard that keeps you from sprinting into a horde with your finger on the wrong counter.
- Ross: the risk-taker who reminds you that sometimes you need to sprint through the courtyard to catch the trap before it triggers the fire trap. He’s the one you trust to take a calculated risk—most of the time.
- Will: the optimist who believes there’s a loot drop around the corner, and probably a secret door as well. He’s the cheerful counterpoint to the grim reality of the zombie apocalypse.
- Helen: the tactician who has a plan for every turn of the night. Helen is the dashboard that reminds you to check your inventory, confirm your items, and not get swallowed by a single bad dice roll.

Naming them isn’t essential to function, but it does add a dash of personality to the table. It also gives your group a ready-made way to discuss who’s up for the “execute the plan” phase versus the “hey, someone feed the zombie with a snack” phase. If you play with a regular group, you’ll notice how these little character cues translate into smoother turn orders and better flow during chaotic rounds.

## Setup, Use, and Maintenance
Setup is straightforward. You simply place the dashboards in front of each player, align the sections with your typical play style, and you’re good to go. A few quick checks:
- Ensure the action and move sections are clearly visible to everyone at the table. If someone needs a magnifying glass, you’ve got bigger problems than the dashboard.
- Keep a damp cloth handy for quick cleanups between rounds. The laminated surface resists smudges well and doesn’t leave ghost marks when you wipe away spilled coffee or melted chocolate from snacks.
- If you’re using magnets or any add-ons, test them before the first round to verify a stable layout. A loose magnet is less dramatic than a zombie horde, but it can disrupt the flow of a tense moment.

In daily use, the dashboards tend to lighten the cognitive load; you can focus on strategy and storytelling rather than diagramming your inventory on a napkin. The downside is that if you’re a “rule-as-written” purist and you frequently modify the standard rules, you may need to adapt the dashboard to accommodate your house rules. It’s not a blocker; it’s a reminder to tweak the board, not the rules.

## Alternatives and Comparisons
- Magnetic token sets: Some players prefer magnetic components for quick detach-and-replace action tracking. Magnetic options can be excellent for aggressive players who like fast resets between rounds, but you may lose some of the clean, non-magnetic elegance the Zombicide dashboards provide.
- Card-size trackers: A lot of players gravitate toward card-based trackers that can slip into the box with the rest of the game. These are portable and sometimes cheaper but may lack the immediate readability that a dedicated dashboard offers.
- DIY setups: If you enjoy tinkering, you could craft your own dashboards with a printable template and a sturdy laminate. The downside is you’ll likely lose the cohesive design and professional finish you get from a dedicated set like this.

In our reader poll, a decent chunk of players favored a dedicated dashboard for the reasons above: readability, consistency, and a tactile “this is my space to rule the table” vibe. If you’re exploring these options, consider what your table values most—speed, durability, or a thematic look—and choose accordingly.
