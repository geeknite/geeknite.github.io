## Performance and Compatibility: Will It Mesh with Your Rig?
A good pinion is all about how it plays with the spur gear and, more importantly, how well it translates motor torque into wheel speed without gobbling up life expectancy. The 32P format is a classic choice for 1/10-scale and many 1/8-scale setups, particularly when you want a predictable gear ratio with a wide selection of spurs.

### Mesh and Setup Tips
- Start with the standard mesh method: back off the spur until the gear just barely touches, then back off 0.05–0.15 mm. If you can’t get a stable mesh without binding, you might have a mismatched spur: either wrong pitch or wrong tooth count.
- Use a drop of threadlocker on the grub screw’s threads after torqueing to the recommended 0.8–1.2 Nm range for small M2.5–M3 screws (check your servo/ motor mount for max torque). You don’t want the pinion creeping under load.
- When mounting, make sure the pinion sits flat against the motor shaft collar. A tiny gap can give you a jittery mesh and early wear.
- Lubrication is minimal here. A light coat of synthetic gear oil or a drop of dry-lube on the teeth is enough. Over-lubricating can attract dust and grind up the tooth surfaces faster.

## Durability Under Load: How It Handles the Real World
The steel pinion’s design intent is to survive torque surges without snapping teeth or producing a wobbly smile when you hammer the throttle. In high-traction situations—think tarmac or carpet with a strong brushless system—the pinion’s steel composition and machining contribute to a longer life than lighter alloys.

- Under high torque, a well-meshed 32P pinion shows minimal tooth surface deformation. The teeth stay sharp enough to maintain engagement without skimming across the spur.
- In terms of wear, 32P pinions tend to wear more progressively than their 48P cousins because the teeth are larger and take more contact load. Hot Racing’s heat-treated steel helps mitigate this by maintaining tooth integrity longer under repeated high-load cycles.
- The bore integrity matters here. A perfectly seated 5mm bore reduces runout and prevents the pinion from slipping during aggressive cornering or wheelspin. That means less chance of strip-outs mid-lap.

If you’re using a heavy 6S-compatible system or a high-torque crawler drivetrain, you’ll appreciate how this pinion holds up to the stress compared to cheaper, softer alloys. Still, no pinion is invincible—mis-meshing, overtightened clamps, or overheating will eventually take a toll. Consider this gear as a high-quality insurance policy rather than an invincibility charm.

## Maintenance and Care: Keeping the Steel Happy
Maintenance for a steel pinion is straightforward, but neglect shows up fast on the track. Here’s a practical routine that keeps your 32P pinion singing for seasons:

- After every few runs, wipe the teeth with a clean cloth to remove dust and polymerized lube. This prevents grinding grime from turning into micro-sandpaper on the gear surface.
- Inspect for micro-chipping after each big race. If you spot wear that looks like tiny notches along the tooth edge, it’s time to reassess mesh and motor torque.
- Re-torque the grub screw after a handful of runs. A loose screw is the fastest path to a missed-shift moment or a shredded tooth—both bad days.
- Lubrication is optional but beneficial if you’re in powdery environments or running longer practice sessions. Use a light synthetic oil on the teeth; avoid heavy grease that can attract grit and hinder heat dissipation.
- Store in a clean, dry area. Corrosion isn’t a concern for steel that’s properly lubricated, but moisture plus dust is a recipe for gnarly wear.

When you combine a well-meshed drive with this pinion’s steel durability, you’ll find maintenance becomes less of a chore and more of a ritual that coincides with how many packs you want to run in a season.
