Introduction
Zombicide Green Horde with No Rest, the Horde Box, and a scattershot of 3D obstacles lands on the gaming table like a swarm of green-skinned mayonnaise. You bring partially painted miniatures into a cooperative survival chaos game, and you still find yourself asking questions you didn’t know you had about orcs, common sense, and the moral implications of throwing axes at shambling crowds. This review is part diary, part field report, part stand-up routine for nerds who pretend they know how to run a campaign without a spreadsheet. If you came for a concise verdict, you probably opened the wrong box in the right mood.

What this unholy trifecta offers, in short, is a bigger table presence, more zombies for your brain to pretend it can outsmart, and enough 3D terrain to make you feel like you’re playing in a tiny, very optimistic dungeon. The partially painted minis turn this into a vibe: some soldiers gleam with factory paint, others sport primer and the courage of a caffeinated raccoon. It’s messy, glorious, and a little ridiculous, which is exactly what a Zombicide session should feel like when the apocalypse knocks on your living room window during a Sunday brunch.

Unboxing and Components
Base Game and No Rest expansion
Zombicide Green Horde is a cooperative face-off between you and hordes of undead greens that look suspiciously like your neighbor’s lawn after a rainstorm. The base game introduces a straightforward objective: survive long enough to manage to leave the map without praying for a mercy rule. The No Rest expansion adds a twist: a little extra tension, a few more doom cards, and some variant rules that encourage persistence. If the base game is a sprint, No Rest is a marathon with a cliffhanger. It isn’t a world-ending addition, but it rewards players who can balance aggressive multi-attack turns with careful positioning.

Horde Box
The Horde Box is the kind of expansion that makes you feel seen by your hobby’s more menacing cousin: the point-tiling, tower-summoning, zombie-swarming cousin. It introduces additional swarms, new zombie cards, and dense populations that can turn a four-hero party into a tactical puzzle where every decision matters more than the last. It’s not just more zombies; it’s more variables to juggle: line of sight, movement speed, and the ever-looming threat of a perfect storm where you can’t recover enough survivors to hold a doorway.

3D Obstacles
3D obstacles are the party-starters. Brass knuckles for your table, if you will. These terrain pieces transform a flat mat into a slice of isometric chaos: walls, barricades, crates, and odd bits that catch the eye and snag a few zombies for long enough to create a dramatic moment. They change the geometry of every encounter, forcing players to think in 3D terms about cover, line of sight, and whether a survivor can climb a stack of crates to escape an oncoming tide. It’s tactile, visually satisfying, and a little merciless—the kind of thing that pushes you to actually plan a route rather than rely on RNG’s whims.

Partially Painted Minis
You’ll find a mix of finished knights and winter coats, and minis that look like they were primed in a hurry by someone who discovered a new shade of green halfway through the session. The partially painted state means you’ll have moments of “ooh that’s sharp” followed by “please don’t zoom in on the shoelaces.” It’s the kind of aesthetic chaos that oddly lends character to the board. You get the sense that you’re stepping into a living, breathing world where some survivors have been at the painting station for hours while others were assembled during a coffee break. The result is a table that feels personal and alive, even when the rules feel like a door hinge you never quite fixed.

The Components in Practice
The core cards, weighty cubes, and dice all feel solid enough to survive a few table tosses. The 3D obstacles click into place with a satisfying thunk, and the terrain pieces actually offer meaningful tactical choices rather than decorative fluff. A good 3D setup gives you the sense of playing in a real space instead of a laminated map with cardboard walls. The Horde Box swells the number of threats, but the design keeps the core rules intact: you still roll, you still move, you still decide which survivors to push forward into danger. It’s just more of that universe you love, with bigger stakes and a bigger smile on the undead’s faces.

Gameplay Experience
Setup and First Impressions
Setting up this fusion of base game, No Rest, Horde Box, and 3D obstacles is a meditation on patience. You’ll achieve tabletop zen by aligning miniatures, snapping terrain, and cursing softly at the moment you realize you forgot to bring extra batteries or a tape measure. The 3D obstacles require a few setup steps, but the payoff is immediate: your board looks like a battlefield from a grim fairy tale and your friends know you mean business. The partially painted minis contribute a lot to the mood. Some models gleam with bold color and careful shading; others wear the pale glaze of “we’ll finish this when we have a day off.” It’s imperfect, but that very imperfection makes the game feel personal and kind of lovable.

Turn Structure and Flow
Zombicide is still a turn-based emergency response exercise: you pick up actions, you hope the dice gods are merciful, and you try not to chain-react your way into a dramatic failure. The No Rest variant adds a few global events that tilt the balance toward longer campaigns and more risk, which in turn makes the Horde Box feel even bigger. Movement becomes a puzzle: where can a survivor run to avoid being boxed in by a 3D obstacle cluster? Which door should you fortify to delay a flood of undead long enough to save one more teammate? The answers are never simple, which is what makes this system so compelling on a party night.

Strategy with Partially Painted Minis
Painted minis illuminate the board’s spirit, but partially painted ones keep you honest. They remind you that you’re still playing with colleagues who have real lives, which is either endearing or terrifying depending on how your last campaign went. The partial paint job introduces a micro-element of randomization: one player may take pride in a colored-coat hero, while another must rely on the color-blocks to guide their memory about which piece controls the right doorway. The game rewards players who plan around this mixed state. You’ll find yourself valuing consistency over flash, yet you still admire the occasional flash of a well-painted figure that makes you want to slump and declare a victory toast to color theory and luck.

Table Presence and Aesthetics
This is where the synergy of 3D terrain and painted minis shines. The table becomes a slice of a small, grim world rather than a scaled chessboard. The green of the zombie masses contrasts with the bright, almost comical survivorship outfits; the 3D obstacles create eye-catching silhouettes that guide the eye around the board. The aesthetic is imperfect in the most charming way: a battlefield that looks like it’s been kicked around by the world’s most earnest gamers. It invites storytelling. You’ll find yourself narrating a mini-scene for each action: a survivor ducking a zombie, a crew member climbing a crate to grab a supply crate, a last-second barricade going up as the horde’s shadow thickens. It’s theater, not just a game.

Scenarios, Campaigns, and Replayability
The No Rest idea nudges you toward longer, more persistent campaigns. You’ll be tempted to string together a marathon session where a half-dozen scenarios bleed into one big arc. The Horde Box adds enough new threat cards that you’ll want to run a few “doom-heavy” sessions to test your group’s resilience. The 3D obstacles unlock new tactical opportunities in each scenario, forcing you to rethink line of sight, cover, and escape routes. The net effect is a richer narrative experience, where you can tell stories like: we barely escaped a nightmarish siege, we barricaded a doorway with a row of crates, and we learned the hard way that zombies don’t respect your creative use of a decommissioned wardrobe as a barricade.

The Good, The Bad, and The Ugly
- The Good: A bigger, bolder sense of scale without losing the core cooperative heart of Zombicide. Terrain becomes a character in the story, not a mere backdrop. Partially painted minis add personality while keeping the budget sane.
- The Bad: Setup time can stretch from “fun planning” to “we should have started earlier.” It’s not a quick-along-the-table game; expect a ritual. The No Rest variant can feel punishing for casual players who prefer a lighter session.
- The Ugly: The sheer number of zombies in the Horde Box can lead to table maintenance chaos if you’re not careful. You’ll want to designate a dedicated zombie-tracking spot and maybe a referee to keep everybody honest about who’s up next.

Practical Tips for Running Green Horde with No Rest and 3D Obstacles
- Pre-sort your components the night before. It cuts setup time dramatically and keeps the mood light when you inevitably misplace a die or two.
- Invest in a larger table or use modular playmats. The 3D terrain looks great, but you’ll thank yourself for extra space when the horde starts stacking up.
- Use a bit of painter’s tape to define safe zones or objective points on the mat. It helps novices and veterans alike track who controls what around the chaos.
- Lean into the partially painted minis during storytelling. The visual cues help players remember who has what ability, which adds momentum to the game’s narrative arc.
- Rotate players as you advance through a campaign. It keeps everyone engaged and allows slower painters to contribute meaningfully without dragging the table through a single model’s painting backlog.

Design Philosophy and Geeky Rant
Zombicide is a game that invites you to embrace the chaos, and this setup embraces it with a wink. The Green Horde’s aesthetic is intentionally cartoonish, which is a perfect counterpoint to the horror-movie vibe you might expect. The No Rest expansion nudges the difficulty without turning the game into something only experts can handle. It’s a clever balance, a design choice that asks players to be creative rather than simply roll higher dice. The Horde Box is not for purists; it’s for people who want a bigger canvas, more noise, and a little more strategy in their survival. And the 3D obstacles? They’re the cherry, the squishy cherry, the one that gets stuck to your fingers and makes your brain think in three dimensions—and sometimes in a few extra layers of sarcasm.

Final Thoughts and Recommendation
If you crave a robust, scenic, and story-heavy Zombicide experience that leans into longer campaigns and bigger sets, Green Horde with No Rest, the Horde Box, and 3D obstacles is a strong pick. It’s not a casual, quick-play Sunday thing; it’s a table-wide affair that rewards players who enjoy both tactical depth and narrative flair. The partially painted minis add personality without turning painting into a gatekeeping barrier, which means more friends at the table and fewer excuses to hide behind assembly glue and primer shrieks. If you love zombie hordes, cooperative problem-solving, and a little bit of tabletop theater, this blend is worth your time and your table space.
