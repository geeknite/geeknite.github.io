H2: What is Zombicide Black Plague, Anyway?

Zombicide Black Plague is not just your grandma’s zombie game. It’s a sprawling, co-op dungeon-crawl-with-armor that drops you into a plague-ridden medieval city where the dead are thirsty, and your unique hero abilities are as vibrant as your dread of turning into a statue of teeth and swords. The combat system is built around dice that deliver the hits, the misses, and the occasional “oh no I rolled two face icons and three bad outcomes.” The board is a modular grid of tiles, colors, and plastic monsters so tiny you could swear you’re playing inside a grain of rice if you’re not careful with your nails.

In a normal box, you’d expect the dice to be the size of small dice, not the philosophical kind that makes you question the nature of existence. In the demo unit, however, the absence of dice was more than a minor inconvenience; it was a philosophical prompt. What does a zombie-in-the-togas feel like when the main tool to fight it literally isn’t there? How do you simulate a successful strike when your rules say you need a dice to determine outcomes? The short answer: you improvise. The long answer: you debate, you substitute, you test substitutions, you hyphenate more adjectives than a Tolkien glossary, and you still have a lot of fun because it’s Zombicide and you’re surrounded by friends who also own a dozen different dice that rolled a bit better last game night.

For context, if you’re curious about the recipe for the game’s magic, the rules rely on a combination of action cards, combat dice, and tile effects. You’ll roll attack dice to deal damage, search dice to uncover loot, and zombie dice to spawn new threats. The demand for the dice is kinetic: you roll, you decide, you might survive the next turn or you might discover that your cleric is allergic to undead and the necromancers haven’t invented anti-allergies yet. The demo unit’s missing dice didn’t erase the core rules; it reframed them into a playful scavenger hunt: substitute a die from a different game, use a spare D6 with a custom face, or simply assign outcomes in a cooperative agreement that the dice will be found in a moment of dramatic tension.

H2: Setup, Components, and the Missing-Dice Dilemma

The setup for Zombicide Black Plague is typically straightforward: assemble the modular board tiles, allocate hero characters, set the zombie spawns, place the equipment cards, and, importantly, gather the attack and search dice. Missing dice complicates things. It’s not just a matter of “we can’t roll” but also a question of how to communicate outcomes. In our session, the group iterated through a few substitution strategies:

- Substituting generic six-sided dice where the faces were repurposed to approximate icons. It’s like giving your gladiator a plastic sword instead of a metal one—the performance is rough, but the intent is noble.
- Using a handful of coin flips to mimic random outcomes, which is perfectly amusing until you realize coins are not dice and they don’t map cleanly to the icons on the cards. Still, it’s a vibe.
- Assigning outcomes via a consensus rule, i.e., “if three of us agree, we’ll treat it as a hit.” This is the democratic approach—popular among players who enjoy negotiating with the undead as though they’re the last call at a medieval tavern.

Even with substitutions, the demo’s atmosphere retained its swagger. The art and miniatures still carried the same weight; you could feel the bite of the bite-y zombies and the heroic flourish of the survivors, even if the dice were missing in action. The included components, aside from the dice fiasco, were solid: the miniatures are crisp, the tiles have sturdy art, and the rulebook—though dense—is approachable with the right sense of humor.

H2: Dice Mechanics: What the Dice Do, and Why Missing Dice Hurts

The dice in Zombicide Black Plague aren’t mere randomizers; they’re the tempo of the whole play experience. Each color has a specific job: attack dice determine whether you land a hit, maybe trigger a special effect, while search dice decide whether you uncover loot that can change turn dynamics. The icons printed on the dice carry bite-sized information: a successful hit, a failure, a special effect, a critical success, maybe a misfire. They are not simply numbers; they are a language that the table speaks collectively.

When the dice vanish, the table must improvise a dialect on the spot. This isn’t just “we can’t roll now”; it’s “we’re improvising a new grammar for combat.” It’s messy, funny, and occasionally surprisingly elegant. The substitution approach can actually highlight the game’s resilience: the core decisions still come from players, not from the dice alone. You’re forced to talk through each action, to narrate outcomes, and to lean on your tablemates’ ideas about how a hero should behave under pressure. In a world where RNG often rules, a missing-dice scenario becomes a chance to tell a better story about teamwork, planning, and the ridiculous luck of being alive in a fictional plague city.

H2: Gameplay Experience in the Demo Unit with Substitutions

We ran through a couple of scenarios to test how far substitution could carry the experience. Here’s a high-level sketch of what happened:

- Scenario 1: A small group of survivors faces a handful of zombies on a narrow street. Without attack dice, players agreed that any roll that would have been a hit becomes a narrative success if the hero described an action that plausibly executes the kill. The table voted on the outcome to reflect the tone: heroic, brutal, or accidentally comic. Outcome: a tense but laugh-heavy moment that still advanced the narrative arc.
- Scenario 2: A search action requires dice to determine what loot is found. We substituted a random card-draw method to simulate discovery: if the group agreed, a loot card could be revealed or a “no loot” outcome would be substituted. Outcome: fun, a bit chaotic, but still spoon-fed by the deck’s storytelling pressure.
- Scenario 3: A boss zombie with multiple attack patterns. No dice? The group leaned on a timer-based system: after a set number of rounds, the boss would perform a predefined move. It wasn’t identical to the original rules, but it produced rhythm—an essential element of any tabletop battle.

The important takeaway is: missing dice don’t auto-lose you the game. They force you to switch from the mechanical to the interpersonal, which can be a surprisingly enriching experience if you approach it with patience and a sense of humor. It also invites you to appreciate the role of components beyond the dice—the board’s design, the card art, and the tiny figures that remind you why you fell in love with tabletop gaming in the first place.

H2: Substitutions: Ethics, Fun, and Moderation

Let’s talk about substitutions with a straight face for a moment. Substituting non-dice components is a valid, sometimes even joyful, way to keep the game moving when you’re missing something. But there are principles to keep in mind:

- Keep substitutes faithful to the spirit of the game. If you’re substituting a die with a coin, make sure there’s a clear, agreed-upon mapping between coin outcomes and die icons.
- Use substitutions as a temporary measure, not a permanent hack. The point of a demo unit is to showcase the product; substituting too heavily can obscure the system’s design.
- Document the house rules. If you’re playing with friends who might want to replicate the substitution at home, write it down in the margins of the rulebook or on a shared note so that future sessions aren’t plagued by “wait, what did we agree again?”

In our tests, these guidelines helped the group remain engaged and amused. The laughter that follows a surprising narrative outcome is, in many ways, as valuable as a clean dice roll. The ghost of the demo dice becomes a character in its own right—a placeholder who occasionally makes a cameo when someone says, “Remember that time we rolled nothing and still beat the boss?” The consensus: missing dice aren’t a deal-breaker; they’re an invitation to improvise and to lean into the game’s communal storytelling bones.
