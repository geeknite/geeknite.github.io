## How to play UNO Stacko: setup, flow, and the occasional catastrophe

Setup is straightforward: assemble the tower according to included instructions (or your preferred wobble-tolerant method), shuffle the UNO deck, and decide the order of play. Variations exist across editions and household rules, but here’s a robust baseline you can start with:

- Place the tower in the center of the table where everyone can reach the blocks comfortably.
- Shuffle the UNO deck and deal a starting hand, or use a draw-and-play flow with a face-down deck.
- On your turn, draw a card and check its color or symbol. You then select a block that matches that color or symbol. The aim is to remove a block while keeping the tower upright.
- If the tower wobbles or falls after your move, you face a penalty or are out of the round, depending on your house rules. The tower’s collapse often signals the moment when the table becomes a stadium of suspense—cheering, groaning, and occasional nudges in the direction of gravity.
- The round typically ends when all blocks are removed or the deck runs out, depending on how you want to pace the game. Some groups favor rapid rounds, while others savor the longer, more dramatic collapses.

## Strategy and tips: stacking your way to glory

Here are a few practical tips to improve your odds of keeping the tower standing long enough to claim victory:

- Study the deck layout: If you know common color distributions in the UNO deck, you can anticipate which colors might appear and plan block selections accordingly. This isn’t cheating; it’s situational awareness with a dash of confidence.
- Start with center blocks for leverage: The center blocks often influence the tower’s balance. Removing them with care can create a more favorable later phase for the rest of the stack. But remember: you’re playing with blocks, not a ruler.
- Consider grip textures: Some blocks offer better leverage due to surface textures. If you can, select blocks with favorable grips to build a subtle early lead.
- Read the room: If the table is tense and the room’s energy is buzzing, you might opt for a slower, steadier approach. A bold, showy pull can pay off—or backfire—depending on the table’s mood.
- Don’t overthink the final moves: The last few blocks are where the glory—or the face-plant—comes out. Trust your instincts and stay economical with your moves. You’re not a robotic arm; you’re a human with a pulse and a sense of humor.

If you crave a stricter variant, you could introduce a “no-look” rule for a single turn to heighten chaos, or implement a timed “last longer than the wobble” constraint for tense, rapid-fire rounds. The beauty of a lightweight title like Stacko is that you can bend rules to fit your crew’s vibe.
