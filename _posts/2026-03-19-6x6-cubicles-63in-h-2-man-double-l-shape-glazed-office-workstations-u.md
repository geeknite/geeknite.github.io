## Assembly and installation: a survival guide

### Pre-assembly tips
- Clear the area: remove chairs, plant stands, and that arbitrarily heavy box labeled “fragile” that’s been sitting under your desk for six months. 
- Create a “go-to” toolkit: a basic set of Allen keys, a screwdriver, and a rubber mallet. Yes, we’re making it sound like a heist, but assembly becomes easier when your hands aren’t searching for tools mid-instruction. 
- Check the parts list twice. There’s nothing quite like discovering that you interpreted a diagram as a request for spare screws after you’ve built a component. 
- Have a helper: two brains make sense of an exploded diagram and two spines can hold a panel while you align a screw. 

### Assembly flow
The general workflow goes something like: assemble the base frames, attach the surface panels, secure the uprights, install the glazing or privacy panels, then tighten everything until your cat tries to claim the highest post. Expect a few “this is not in the instructions” moments, followed by the “aha” moment when the pieces finally align and you realize you’ve created something that bears a startling resemblance to a desk. The best part? You can do it with a coffee in one hand and a monitor arm in the other—multitasking at its finest.

### Safety first
Always double-check load capacities for monitor arms and any under-desk accessories. The last thing you want is a loose clamp turning your monitor into a javelin during a keyboard swipe. If your floor isn’t perfectly level, consider shims to stabilize the workstation. A slightly wobbly setup is a nightmare for video calls and a hazard if you’re trying to balance a coffee mug on the edge of the desk while explaining your quarterly forecast.

## Functionality: ergonomics and productivity on a budget

### Ergonomics and user comfort
The dual-workstation design invites shared ergonomics across two users who still want personal space. The correct monitor height, keyboard position, and feet alignment will keep you from hunching like a Quasimodo in a weather update. ### Cable management
One of the trickier parts of any cubicle is cable routing. The outer glazing means fewer deep channels for cabling, so you’ll want to plan: anchor a power strip under the desk, use cable raceways, and think about the path to the nearest outlet. If your landlord loves surprise extension cords, you’ll want to keep any extra slack tidy and hidden so you don’t trigger the office CSI when someone trips over a dangling power line during an important Zoom call.

### Acoustic considerations
Glazed panels do not magically block noise; they sometimes reflect sound like a futuristic echo chamber. If your work requires intense concentration, consider adding acoustic panels or desktop dividers on top of the cubicle. The takeaway: you’ll trade some openness for additional focus, which is exactly what most teams want if they’re reading those quarterly reports with more numbers than a sudoku puzzle.
