## How to Play

### Core Rules

- Players take turns removing one block from any level of the tower, excluding the topmost completed layer. The aim is to test your nerve without unleashing a cascade of timbers.
- After removing a block, place it on the top of the tower in the same orientation as the block above. The rotation base may cause the stack to shift subtly, increasing or decreasing the current level of risk.
- Once a block is placed on top, the turn ends and the next player begins. If the tower falls, the culprit is the one who caused the tumble.
- The game continues until one player has all of the blocks toppled or you declare a winner by reaching a preset score, depending on house rules.

The rotating base introduces a delightful tension: it isn’t enough to pick the right block; you must time your move to ride the micro-tilt that the base imposes. The platform adds a kinetic layer that makes the decision tree go hey-hey, a little chaotic, a little magical, and absolutely theatrical in a living room full of friends.

### Variants

- Solo Challenge: Push yourself to complete a self-imposed constraint, such as removing only from edges for an entire round. It’s a test of patience and discipline when the tower politely says no.
- Team Tumble: Pair up players with alternating turns inside a team. Teams negotiate risk, sometimes turning into humorous bargaining sessions that resemble a tiny office dispute over a block’s honor.
- Time-Limit Tumble: Add a timer to speed up decisions, elevating the laughter as players frenzy over which block to pluck before the clock strikes doom.
- Expert Mode: Start lower in the tower to simulate tournament-style pressure as the base becomes more treacherous with height. It’s physics with a scoreboard and a snappy comeback.

### Strategies and Tactics

- Block Type Awareness: Middle blocks are often safer to remove than outer ones, but removing from the middle can speed up disaster if the top becomes unstable. Learn to read the subtle tilt cues of the base to guide your choices.
- Sequence Planning: Don’t plan too far ahead. Gravity has memory, but the rotating base is a prankster. A flexible plan usually beats a rigid blueprint because the base will betray you at least once per game.
- Rhythm and Tempo: Some players whip through turns to keep others off balance; others savor a slow, methodical approach to savor the drama. Both work; it’s just a matter of group vibe.
- Edge vs Center Dilemma: Edges invite audacious plays with dramatic tumbles; centers offer a steadier platform that tests precision and nerve. The trick is to read your opponents and choose blocks accordingly.
