## Overview: What is Codenames Anyway?

Codenames is a word game that pits two teams against each other in a guessing game with a twist. The table becomes a hub of wordplay, deduction, and highly questionable puns as players try to identify their team's agents by revealing a grid of words that are linked through clues provided by the spymaster. Vlaada Chvatil designed this game to be accessible to casual players while still rewarding clever clue-givers who think like a stealthy word-slinger. The Czech Games Edition box is packed with color, cards, and a sense that you could become a deliciously competitive intelligence agent, or at least that you can bluff your way into their top-secret cafeteria line.

In gist, one team’s spymaster gives a one-word clue followed by a number, indicating how many of the grid words relate to that clue. The other team then guesses those words in an attempt to identify their own agents, while avoiding words that belong to the opposing team, as well as the infamous Assassin word that ends the round immediately and hilariously for everyone involved.

## How to Play (For Those Who Avoid the Manual Like a Final Boss)

Codenames is deceptively simple in principle, but it scales beautifully with the group’s size and energy. Here’s a quick guide to the core flow of a standard two-team game.

### Setup
1. Lay out a 5x5 grid of word cards. In some variants, a 4x4 grid is used for smaller groups, but the classic is 5x5. Each word is a potential agent word.
2. Each team designates a Spymaster who will give clues. The Spymaster sits on the same side for easy communication with their team but cannot see the words on the table unless the layout includes a spyglass (no, not in this edition).
3. A key card is used to determine which words belong to which team, which are neutral, and which is the Assassin. In most sealed editions, you’ll flip a key card that shows a color-coded pattern; only the Spymasters should know this pattern.
4. The opposing Spymaster receives the same rule book, and the rest of the players prepare to guess while trying to stay within the boundaries of the clue given.

### Turn structure
- The Spymaster gives a one-word clue plus a number (for instance, “Animal 3”). The clue must be a word that relates to one or more of the grid words. The number indicates how many cards on the grid relate to the clue. If a few words relate, you can push your luck a little higher—but that opens you up to misdirection.
- Team members discuss and make guesses, one at a time. They may pick a word card to reveal it. If the revealed word belongs to their team, the card is covered with the team’s color. If it belongs to the other team, it ends up that team’s color on the card and the turn ends. If it’s a neutral word, the turn ends with no color added. If it’s the Assassin, the team immediately loses the game.
- The round continues with turns until one team has guessed all its agents or an Assassin interrupts the flow.

The genius of Codenames lies in the tension between a single cunning clue and the wild, wonderful leaps of association that players conjure. The Spymaster must balance specificity (to avoid the opposing team and the Assassin) with breadth (to give enough clues that the guessing team can approach the goal). A well-chosen clue can connect six or seven words in a single mental thread; a poor clue can end the round in the most entertaining way possible.


### Strategy nuggets (without teleporting to genius-hood instantly)
- Learn to leverage homonyms and compound terms strategically. A clue like “Fruit 4” could be a dream if your grid contains words like Apple, Banana, Lemon, and Pear — but be careful: your opponents are listening for the same connections and will pounce on accidental crossovers.
- Use the number to your advantage: start with a smaller number to maintain control, then build. If your team is confident, you can push the number up to encourage more aggressive guessing. The exact cadence depends on the personalities at the table; the only universal rule is don’t be a mean Spymaster if your friends hate despair.
- Watch for the Assassin. The moment you note a word that could be dangerous, your team should be wary and avoid it. But then again, sometimes the best players bait the Assassin for a misdirected win. It’s a delicate dance between fear and flourish.
- The role reversal between Spymaster and Guesser is where the party breathes. A good Guesser can rescue a poor clue with sheer logic, and a clever Spymaster can turn a dull grid into a brilliant puzzle. The dynamic is where the magic happens.
