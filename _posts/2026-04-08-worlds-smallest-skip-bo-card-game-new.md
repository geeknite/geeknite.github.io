How to Play in a Micro World

Skip-Bo, for the uninitiated, is essentially color-coded card cycling with stock piles and wild cards. The world’s smallest version tries to replicate the core mechanics in a package that can fit on a coffee table or inside a monocle case. The rules aren’t rocket science; if you’ve played the regular Skip-Bo, you can adapt quickly. The objective remains to be the first player to play through or pile up to a fixed target by using numbered cards and wild cards to build sequential stacks. In micro form, you’ll be dealing with smaller numbers, but the mental load stays roughly the same. The game requires you to stay sharp as you juggle stock piles that involve a lot of detection, distribution, and a surprising amount of finger gymnastics.

Playability in real life: practical tips

- Use bright lighting or a big-screen phone to help you read the numbers. The numbers can be small, but the numbers are important. Without clear reading, you’ll be sacrificing your ability to optimally place the cards and you’ll end up with confused looks and misplays.

- Shake lightly. Micro-shuffling is a thing, but aggressive shuffling can cause misalignment. Gentle flicks work better; treat the deck with respect and you will preserve the life of your micro deck.

- Use a table or smooth surface. The mini-cards will slide, but the surface matters. A coffee table with a nice finish makes the micro game feel that much nicer.

- Have a magnifier handy if you’re playing in low light or with older eyes. It sounds silly, but it helps.

- Use the included instructions as a guide; the micro version replicates the core mechanics but you may want to alter the number of stock piles for a faster or slower game.
