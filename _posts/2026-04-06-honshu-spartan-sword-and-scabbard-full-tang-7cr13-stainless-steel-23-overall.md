## Specs and materials

### Blade and spine

The blade is listed as 7Cr13 stainless steel. For those not in the loop, 7Cr13 is the steel common in budget blades that balances cost with modest corrosion resistance and a reasonable ability to hold an edge with proper maintenance. It’s not the steel you’d trust for long-term cutting of heavy targets, but it’s perfectly fine for display, cosplay, and light practice. The blade geometry is typical for a budget Spartan accessory: a relatively flat bevel with a mild hollow grind that reduces weight and improves piercing ability along with quick draw. The blade length comes in around 21 to 22 inches with the extra bit for the tip giving you the total 23 inches overall. The tip geometry tends toward a practical point, great for cosplay stabs and ceremonial poses, less ideal for serious piercing tasks or heavy cutting practice.

### Full tang and handle

The claim of a full tang matters here. The tang runs the length of the handle, contributing to rigidity and a perception of durability. In practice, budget swords often hide a partial tang or a hidden tang that ends at the hilt for safety and economics. With the Spartan, the handle feels substantial enough for display and light practice; it won’t win any awards for endurance in full contact martial arts, but it’s firm enough for casual dueling in a well-lit hotel hallway (we do not recommend hallway duels, but we know you’ll test the boundaries).

The grip is a standard synthetic material with enough texture to prevent slippage when your hands get sweaty after a long day at the con or during a dramatic cosplay fight scene. Long sessions might require fingerless gloves if you’re worried about blisters; otherwise you can pretend you’re a valiant hero who has faced worse than a bit of foam armor.

### Scabbard and harness

The scabbard is functional and well-suited to completing the cosplay aesthetic. It’s not a high-end leather scabbard with brass fittings, but it looks the part and protects the blade during display and transport. The belt attachment is straightforward, making the sword easy to wear on your cosplay belt or over your shoulder for a dramatic entrance. It’s not going to survive heavy outdoor use without some care, but for convention use and stage cosplay, it does the job.

## Maintenance and durability

### Care basics

7Cr13 stainless steel isn’t a miracle worker. It resists rust reasonably well if you dry the blade after use and avoid soaking it in corrosive environments. A light wipe with a dry cloth after handling will go a long way. If you plan to duel with this blade in cosplay, avoid prolonged exposure to moisture and acidic cleaners. A soft cloth with a tiny dab of mineral oil is enough to keep surface rust at bay and to maintain that fresh-from-the-box look.

### Scabbard care

The scabbard might attract a bit of dust and lint; a quick wipe with a soft cloth will remove most of it. If you notice the blade rubbing against the scabbard, check for alignment and tighten any loose fittings per the manufacturer’s instructions. In most cases, the scabbard’s job is to protect the blade during display and transport, not to be a heavy duty sheath used in actual combat. Treat it with care, and it will treat your blade back nicely.

### Longevity with proper use

Budget swords like this one don’t expect to outlive a four-figure piece, but with decent upkeep, you’ll enjoy many cosplay mornings and photo shoots. The blade may show micro-scratches after frequent use, but a simple polish with a soft cloth and a non-abrasive metal polish can restore the finish. Don’t aggressively sand or grind the blade; you’ll ruin its geometry and void the warranty (which is not a thing you should rely on for budget gear anyway).
