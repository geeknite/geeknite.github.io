### The Box Content: What you actually get in Wulfsburg, Huntsman, and Knight

The Wulfsburg monster of a map box is the centerpiece here. ## Partially Painted: The Reality of Hobby Progress

Let’s talk about the elephant in the workshop: partially painted means you have a baseline to build from, not a finished masterpiece. The parts that are painted show clean lines, good shading, and a coherent color story. The motherload of the terrain, however, begs for a weathered concrete or moss effect, a splash of metallic rust on the gates, and perhaps a touch of blood that doesn’t scream “newly minted plastic.” The Huntsman’s quiver and the Knight’s shield have potential for some precise highlighting and edge lining that would elevate them from “display piece” to “table-ready titan.” If you’re new to mini painting, this is a noble challenge: you get to practice layering, dry brushing, and a tiny bit of ink work on the costumed zombies while the bigger pieces demand a steadier hand and more patience.

One practical note: the scale of these models is forgiving for beginners but still demands a little bit of measurement discipline. If you’re painting alongside a friend who swears by speed painting, you’ll likely have a friendly debate about whether to use quick washes or a more deliberate approach. The reality is that you can get from “half-painted chaos” to “reasonable tabletop presence” with a few consistent steps: base coat, shade, dry brush, and a careful glaze where needed. The rest is just keeping your hands steady and avoiding the dreaded spill of brown washes onto your knight’s white tabard.

## Gameplay Experience: Wulfsburg in Action

Zombicide Black Plague thrives on cooperative chaos, and Wulfsburg as a map forces players to balance exploration with the risk of a mob surge. The addition of Huntsman and Knight as survivors can alter your approach in a surprisingly meaningful way. The Huntsman increases your ranged utility, letting you thin the squishy greens before they crash into your shield line. The Knight adds a dependable frontline presence that often buys you a precious round to reposition survivors without sacrificing your leadership role on the board.

The terrain pieces are not just décor; they act as line-of-sight blockers, choke points, and access control. As soon as you realize a row of town gates can funnel zombies into a tight corridor, you’ll start thinking in terms of “traffic management” rather than “pull and smash.” It’s a puzzle, and it’s glorious when you pull off a plan that feels both cinematic and mechanically sound.

One caveat for the painted-but-not-finished crowd: keep the surface clean. Zombies accumulate as if they’ve auditioned for a role in a horror-adventure painting class, and the last thing you want is miniature gore smeared into the edges of a catapult tile. A little careful cleanup with a soft brush between turns goes a long way toward readability on the board. Your future self will thank you when you’re not staring at a grip of tiny figures with paint fingerprints splayed across their faces like a wall-of-screaming‑miniatures.

### Tactical tips for Wulfsburg nights

- Use the Knight to anchor the front line and protect the Huntsman from being overwhelmed in corridor fights.
- Position the Huntsman near doorways and balcony ramps to control entry points before the crowd-by-crowd assault begins. 
- Reserve a few heavy weapons for critical turns when the board erupts with a flood of undead; you’ll thank yourself later.
- Don’t forget terrain features: balconies can grant elevation to your ranged survivors, but they also create risky exposure if you’re not careful with line of sight.

## Painting Progress: From Half-Disaster to Half-Generous Glory

Partially painted is a generous way to describe the current state. The good news is that the color palette is forgiving enough that you can mix a believable medieval vibe without needing a full palette of ancient-fortress shades. The bad news is that a few areas have not yet received their due attention, which means you might find yourself focusing on the “fashionable” parts first—the knight’s tabard, the Huntsman’s cloak, and the rim of a shield—before you get to the rugged town walls and the alley grates. If your aim is to finish everything in a cohesive color family, you’ll want a plan: pick a main tone for the terrain, add a secondary color for doors and accents, and assign a metallic pair for nails and armor edges to break up the monotony of the brown-gray stone.

For players who want to take the next step toward a showpiece board, here are five solid moves:

1) Finish the Knight’s plate highlights with a cool silver edge to contrast against a warm armor color. 2) Add a subtle wash to the Huntsman’s cloak to bring out the folds without turning it into a muddy swamp. 3) Apply a dry brush on the Wulfsburg walls using a lighter gray to create depth and texture. 4) Add subtle rust on metal gates and hinges to hint at long-term exposure to the city’s gloom. 5) Seal with a matte varnish to unify the painted pieces with the terrain and keep muddy fingers from smearing the finish during tabletop sessions.

If you’re curious about finishing approaches, the painting-focused posts I referenced earlier offer a few different philosophies, from “fast tabletop” to “two-step zen.” Remember: you don’t have to be a master-painter to tell a good story on the board; you just have to tell a convincing one with a little time, patience, and obsessive attention to detail.
