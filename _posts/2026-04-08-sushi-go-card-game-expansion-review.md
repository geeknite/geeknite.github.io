Here’s a non-exhaustive look at the new family of cards you’ll meet across the expansion. Think of these as the new flavors you’ll want to chase after your nemesis has already snagged the eel nigiri.

- Secret Menu Cards: These are draftable but worth different points based on whether you collect a matching set by the end of the round.
- Fusion Nigiri: A hybrid card that scores like traditional nigiri but has a conditional multiplier if you can pair it with a specific other card type.
- Miso Gate: A scoring card that only unlocks if you previously drafted a particular sequence, adding a dash of memory and planning to your turn.

## How the Expansion Changes the Game (Mechanics and Scoring)
If you love the base game’s clean drafting and brutal but fair scoring, you’ll be happy to hear the expansion wears a gentle smile rather than a trench coat full of schemes. The core loop remains: pick one card from a hand, pass the rest, and end with a collection of cards that will score. The expansion introduces a handful of interactions that make you rethink your priorities on every turn without turning the game into a spreadsheet exercise.

### New Scoring Dynamics
- Combo Scoring: Some cards reward you for collecting sets of related cards. It’s a little like a mini Sudoku with sushi. It feels clever and a bit cheeky, which is how we at Geeknite prefer our math.
- Hidden Objectives: A subset of “secret” scoring conditions prompts you to plan around what others might be collecting, even though you can’t see their exact hands. It creates delicious paranoia—perfect for heated debate and cardio for the brain.
- Tempo Tactics: A few cards grant extra points for early drafting or for “last act” pickups. This creates a rising action arc in each round, a narrative arc where your hero (you) fights to secure the cherry blossom in the final moment.

### New Mechanics: The Menu and the Multiplier
The Menu mechanic introduces a light, player-driven layer: each round, players can claim a Menu card that alters how points accrue for a small subset of cards. It’s optional, binary in effect, and creates a few spicy “what-if” moments as you measure risk vs. reward. The Multiplier cards provide temporary scoring boosts that can swing a round’s total dramatically when timed with precision. It’s a little like a trading card whisper network among your opponents, only less sinister and more adorable sushi icons winking at you across the table.

These mechanics are designed to be optional for quick sessions or standard for longer play, maintaining the scalable nature of the base game. You can implement the expansion halfway through a night if you’re feeling daring, or you can ride the wholesome, breezy speed of the base game with all the expansion’s flavor locked away for a later binge.

## Strategy Deep Dive: How to Draft Like a Sushi Sensei
If you want to win with style, you’ll need to blend your long-term plans with a healthy dose of misdirection. The expansion rewards planning without punishing you for misfires—unless your misdirection was way off and you accidentally handed your opponent your favorite card with a sigh of cosmic mercy.

### Early Game: Pick Your Targets Wisely
In the expansion, the early rounds are less about grabbing the highest single-value card and more about shaping the round’s ecosystem. If you identify a potential combo you want to chase, you should consider drafting lower-value cards that enable the important pieces later. This is not greed; this is garden-variety sushi horticulture: plant the seeds with a few point-strings attached, and watch them bloom as your opponents chase independent scores.

### Midgame: The Menu Association and Middle-Game Pressure
As the Menu cards come into play, you’ll discover you’re balancing two levers: the direct scoring of your current hand and the strategic positioning for future rounds. The Menu card’s effect is not a slam dunk; it’s a pressure point. You should not always chase the strongest immediate payoff if it means you’re tipping off your plans to others. Be the orchestra conductor who gently nudges the violins into place, not the lead violinist who nails a solo and forgets the rest of the ensemble exists.

### Late Game: Last-Round Calculations and Inevitable Betrayals
The final round is where the expansion shows its teeth—but in a polite, well-groomed shark kind of way. You’ll see players racing to complete combos, while others try to disrupt those combos by stealing key cards. It’s a delicate dance: sometimes you let a rival have a card you want if it means the overall field prevents a bigger threat from scoring enough to win. The key is to stay flexible. If a plan collapses, pivot toward the expansion’s alternative scoring paths and hope your last-move instinct doesn’t betray you.

### Practical Tips from the Geeknite Desk
- Track not just your own potential points but the likely trajectories of your opponents. The new scoring rules encourage a little more social deduction than the base game.
- Don’t ignore the new cards’ synergy with the classic lineup. Often a single new card amplifies two or three older cards’ values in a way that feels clever but not oppressive.
- Use the Menu cards as pressure release valves. They’re designed to add tension without bogging down the pace of the game.
- If you’re playing with new players, consider a gentle onboarding: start with the base game first, then introduce 1–2 expansion cards per session until everyone’s comfortable.

## Variants and House Rules: Making It Your Own
If you’re the kind of player who treats a game night like a sandbox, several house-rule ideas can be slotted into the expansion with minimal friction:

- Tempo Match: Set a fixed number of rounds, and award a small bonus for whoever finishes a pure-score run by the last round. Keeps players honest about sequencing.
- Menu Mastery Draft: Allow players to draft a Menu card at any time, but only if they have a matching set of certain cards. It creates opportunities for dramatic comebacks.
- Short-Play Variant: Remove the most complex scoring cards for ultra-fast rounds (15–20 minutes). Perfect for casual nights or pre-meeting warmups.

Remember: the goal is to enhance fun, not to create a tournament vibe so intense that your table blows a gasket over a single dumpling. The expansion is designed to adapt to your group’s energy, not to force them into a particular style of play.
