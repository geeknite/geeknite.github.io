Ports and Connectivity
----------------------

Here’s where the HP 120W G4 shines, and where it invites you to bring more cables into your life—because that is what life is about: more cables, more possibilities, more procrastination.

- Display outputs: The dock offers multiple video outputs to connect your external monitor setup. Whether you run a single 4K monitor for the pure productivity vibe or a duo for dual-screen shenanigans, this dock is ready to play. The exact configuration may vary by model and firmware, so your mileage may differ depending on whether you’re pairing with a TB3 or TB4 host and which video standards your monitors demand. Expect at least one HDMI and at least one DisplayPort option, with some configurations offering two DisplayPort outputs. This is ideal for a multi-monitor estate without needing a separate video adapter. If you’re juggling a 4K HDR display on your left and a web-browsing plasma screen on your right, you’ll be glad the dock can handle it.

- USB data and charging: The USB hub aboard this dock typically includes a mix of USB-A and USB-C ports for both data transfer and peripheral charging. You can attach keyboards, mice, external SSDs, and other peripherals without fighting for bandwidth. The USB-C ports often support data rates up to USB 3.2 Gen 2 or Gen 3.2, with power delivery passthrough for compatible devices. The USB-A ports are capacious enough for external drives, peripherals, and the occasional phone charging session.

- Network: Expect an Ethernet port for reliable, low-latency network access. If you’re a gamer, streamer, or remote worker, the Ethernet port is the unsung hero that helps you avoid the vagaries of wifi wobble. For most office workflows, you’ll notice the TCP handshake faster and more predictable results than you’d get on a fickle wireless connection.

- Audio: A 3.5mm audio jack is usually present, sometimes a separate mic input, so you can route video meetings through a dedicated audio path. If you’ve got a pair of studio headphones or a decent mic, the dock can become the audio hub of your desk without forcing you to reach behind your laptop every time.

- Power delivery: The 120W power brick is the heart of the beast. It feeds through the TB bus to your laptop while providing port-based charging for plugged-in peripherals that can receive power from the dock. If you’re running a particularly hungry workstation, you’ll be glad to see HP didn’t cut corners on the electrical backbone. The dock is not a miracle worker; if your laptop consumes close to or above the 120W envelope under load, you’ll experience compromises with simultaneous charge and display output. For most mainstream laptops, you’ll be in a comfortable zone where you can render, code, and charge without fatal compromises.

- Build out and cable routing: Cable management on the HP dock is where you can exercise your inner MacGyver. The front-facing ports are convenient for quick connections, while the rear ports and the cable channels help you keep the desk tidy. Realistically you’ll still have cables snaking around the desk, but the dock gives you a central hub to anchor the chaos rather than letting it escape into every corner of your room.

- Compatibility and setup: HP ships with some degree of compatibility across Windows, macOS, and certain ChromeOS devices. In the real world, you’ll likely get Windows and macOS to recognize the dock with minimal fuss. Mac users, if you’re aiming for heavy external monitor usage or high-speed USB peripherals, you’ll want to confirm your macOS version supports the exact combination of HDMI/DP outputs you plan to use. For Windows laptops, you generally get a smoother ride, with Thunderbolt hot-plugging behaving as expected and the dock acting as a central hub for both display and peripheral connectivity.

Setup Tips and Tricks
----------------------

- Firmware updates: Like many docks, firmware updates can improve compatibility and stability. Check HP’s support site for the latest firmware and apply updates when you can. A quick firmware update can fix minor quirks and improve compatibility with newer laptops.

- Cable restraint: The dock’s power supply is not a tiny puff of smoke; it is a real brick. Consider where you place it and how you route the power cable to avoid tension that could loosen connections over time. A little planning here goes a long way toward desk neatness.

- Display order: If you’re using multiple displays, you may want to configure the primary and secondary monitors in your OS settings. Some monitors will default to different ports, so a quick check in Windows Display Settings or macOS System Preferences ensures your main workspace is exactly where you expect it to be.

- Peripherals and hotkeys: If you’re using a keyboard with programmable macro keys or a USB-C hub that provides extra USB-C ports for charging, connect those devices early to ensure they aren’t falling off the bus when you need them most. The dock handles hot-plugging gracefully in most cases, but a tidy setup still pays off during crunch time.

- Compatibility notes: Mac users should be aware that external display configurations can behave slightly differently than on Windows machines, especially when you mix HDMI and DisplayPort outputs. If you’re chasing a particular multi-monitor setup on macOS, validate the exact port configuration with your monitor chain before committing to a purchase.
