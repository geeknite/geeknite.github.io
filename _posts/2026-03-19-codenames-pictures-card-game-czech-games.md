## What is Codenames Pictures?
Codenames Pictures is a word game turned visual. The core idea remains: two teams, a spymaster per side, and a grid of picture cards on the table. Instead of giving one-word clues, the spymaster gives a clue that points to multiple pictures, but with a twist: the clues relate to the depiction on each image. The team then points to cards on the table that fit that clue. The objective? Locate all your team’s agents before the other team does — but with a lot more visual interpretation and a handful of fun misfires waiting to happen.

If the original Codenames is a high-stakes spelling bee with a caffeine addiction, Pictures is a gallery crawl after lunch, where every painting has something to offer and many will be misread by your own team’s imagination. This shift from words to images changes the entire map of risk and reward. The same strategic constraints exist, but the pathways to victory feel more like a treasure hunt through a cartoon museum rather than decoding a single emoji-laden message.

## How to play: setup and overview
Codenames Pictures keeps the same structural rhythm as the original game but swaps the language engine. Here is a compact walkthrough to refresh your memory or set up a new table:

### Setup
- Split into two teams, assign a spymaster for each side, and pick a grid. The standard size is a 5x4 or 4x5 arrangement depending on player count.
- Shuffle the picture cards and lay them face up in the grid so every image is visible to both teams.
- The two spymasters receive a top-secret map indicating which images belong to their team and which are neutral or enemy. If you have never played this version before, wobble your eyebrows at the map as if you are negotiating a peace treaty between interpretive dance troupes.

### The objective
Like the classic, you want to guess all of your agents first. But instead of clustering around a single keyword, you’re chasing clusters of visual ideas. Your clue must connect multiple images, and you want to avoid accidentally guiding your team toward the assassin image, which ends the round immediately in a very dramatic fashion.

### Turn structure and clues
- The spymaster gives a clue consisting of a single word and a number, much like the original. The number indicates how many images on the grid relate to that clue.
- Teammates deliberate and point to the images they believe connect to the clue. They can guess up to the number given by the spymaster plus one extra guess if they feel confident — but beware the assassin image is a game-ending baddie.
- If the team hits a neutral image, the turn ends but the team doesn’t gain a point. If they hit the opponent’s image, control switches sides. If they hit the assassin image, the game ends immediately with a loss for that team.

### A few tips to get started
- Use broad, multi-image concepts to unlock several pictures at once. Think categories, not individual pictures.
- When stuck, examine the edges of a cluster. Often a shared theme connects a few middle images you might have skipped.
- Spymasters, your job is not to be a mastermind of semantic gymnastics but a calm conductor who helps your crew connect ideas without turning the table into a debate club.

## Gameplay depth: strategy without the grind
Codenames Pictures scales well for 4 to 8 players, with a typical round lasting anywhere from 15 to 30 minutes depending on how many big-picture leaps your crew dares to take. Here is a breakdown of the strategic landscape:

### Clue quality and risk management
The better your clue, the more images you responsibly connect. A safe clue might connect two or three images cleanly; a riskier clue could reach five or six. The reward is a potentially swift victory, but the risk of stepping on a landmine grows with each additional connection. The assassin card is not a mere obstacle; it is a plot twist waiting to ruin everyone’s plan with a single misinterpretation.

### Reading the table
Team members become amateur art historians for the evening. They attempt to read color-coded vibes and momentary associations. The best players are not always the ones who spot the obvious link but those who can justify a bold, plausible connection with a couple of sentences that make the room nod and laugh simultaneously.

### Spymaster tactics
Spymasters in Pictures must balance clarity with cunning. You want hints that unlock several cards, but you also need to avoid cascading failures when the gallery of images invites wildly different associations. A well-chosen clue feels almost like a magician's flourish — it wows while never overcomplicating the trick.

## A brief look at setup variance and house rules
Some groups enjoy a little customization. Here are a few light house rules that keep things fresh without turning the game into a brain-scan:

- Time-box turns to keep the energy high. A 60-second window per turn helps keep pace.
- Use a stylized improv rule: after a hint, teammates must describe a mental image for two of the cards they guessed. It adds a storytelling layer that can be hilarious and surprisingly clarifying.
- Optional modifier: if a clue relates to a single image, allow a special reaction clue that clarifies the link but does not count toward the guess total. It keeps things flexible and fun without breaking the core mechanics.

### A note on accessibility
As with many image-based games, players with visual impairments may find some cards harder to distinguish. The game plays well with multiple rounds in a single sitting, but a few additional adaptations — such as larger boards or higher contrast images — can help the whole table participate. The core aim remains the same: you are building a shared story through pictures, jokes, and quick thinking, not surveying a museum solo.
