## Unboxing, Assembly, and the Great Cable Safari
Assembling the 5x5 cubicles is a test of patience, planning, and the physics of screwdrivers. The box arrives with enough hardware to assemble a modest home planet, which is to say: there’s a lot. The good news is that the instructions are reasonably clear, and the pieces are logically labeled. The bad news is that you’ll still spend a heroic chunk of your day aligning panels, tightening bolts, and debating with the manual about the best way to route cables without creating a modern-day spaghetti monster.

### Step-by-step sanity checks
1) Inventory: lay out all panels, panels, and connectors. If you’re missing a bolt, you can probably bluff the supplier with a confident stare; it might work, but don’t rely on it.
2) Frame assembly: the steel backbone is sturdy, which is nice when your team member decides to test “flex” by leaning back with a swivel chair. The frame is heavy enough to require two people in most cases, which is why you’ll see the “team-building activity” captions on the company’s internal Slack for the next few hours.
3) Desk surfaces and returns: the L-shape returns are a clever trick for corner work zones. They give you extra writing space and a place to stash a laptop, monitor arms, or a suspiciously large plant that contradicts the “biophilic design” trend.
4) Fabric panels: attached with plastic clips that feel flimsy until you realize they’re holding up your boss’s hope for quiet in the open-plan office. The fabric is soft to the touch and tends to hide smudges like a magician hides rabbits. It also helps dampen small conversations, which can be both a blessing and a curse.
5) Cable management: there are channels and grommets, which sounds fancy until you realize the real trick is labeling every plug with color-coded stickers that make your admin team swoon with efficient joy.

If you’re looking for a free, play-by-play assembly video, we didn’t film one because, alas, we didn’t want to compete with our own bloopers reel. Still, a well-made assembly guide can save you from the dark path of “adjust-a-thing forever.”

### Assembly tips from the trenches
- Do a dry fit first. You’ll thank yourself when you know exactly which panel goes where without forcing the parts to pretend to be Lego bricks.
- Use a two-person lift for heavy components. Gravity loves gravity; your back does not.
- Tidy up as you go. A clean workspace is a happier workspace, and a happy back is a back that doesn’t mutiny mid-assembly.
- Label cables early. Nothing ruins a Friday faster than a mystery power brick screaming for a forgotten USB-C port.

## Cable Management, Tech Integration, and Practical Nerdiness
One of the less glamorous but crucial aspects of any office furniture is the cable management system. The 5x5 cubicles come with integrated channels and cable trays designed to reduce the classic “what is this tangle of doom?” scenario. In practice, you’ll still need to invest a little energy into routing and labeling, but the design supports a cleaner look than many budget options.

- Cable channels: They run along the underside of the desk surface, guiding power and data cables toward the cable riser or the center of the workstation. This reduces the risk of tripping over a rogue USB-C dongle, which is a little victory for the modern workplace.
- Grommets: There are openings in the desktops that let you thread cables through without creating a universal chair-desk knot. It’s not perfect, but it’s friendlier than a bow tie at a tech conference.
- Power access: Depending on the configuration, you may get a local power strip or integrated outlets. If you’re planning a cluster with heavy GPU rigs or a charging station for a crew of tablets, you’ll want to confirm the load limits and ensure you’re not tripping the building’s power breaker when the team discovers “one more USB-C hub” is a thing.

In short: it’s not “no maintenance” by any stretch, but it’s significantly better than some laminated nightmares you’ve seen in the wild. If you’ve lived through cord avalanches before, you’ll appreciate the attempt at order and the ability to tidy up without turning the desk into a hardware store display.
