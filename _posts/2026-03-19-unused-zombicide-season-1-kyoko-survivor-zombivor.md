## What is Kyoko? The Kickstarter Exclusive Survivor & Zombivor
### Kyoko the Survivor
Kyoko arrives as a survivor with a backstory and a set of creative tools intended to help players navigate the early waves of the zombie horde. In typical Zombicide fashion, the survivor is designed to offer a niche flavor—perhaps an emphasis on stealth and mobility, or a special interaction with terrain or urban objectives. The “unused” moniker can feel a little harsh; Kyoko’s kit is more like an underused secret weapon in a tactical deck: you know she’s there, you know she could shine in the right map, but you haven’t quite found the perfect scenario to unleash her full potential. 

What does Kyoko bring to the table? In many early Zombicide cycles, exclusives were built around a synergy with a few core mechanics: movement, line-of-sight, and the knife-edge of first-contact combat. Kyoko, as a concept, often leans into one of two directions: a speed-runner with a reliable escape route, or a support specialist who can set up future turns for teammates. The exact card text varies by edition and by the official errata slips included in some packs, but the vibe is unmistakably “glimpsed potential.”

### Zombivor Kyoko
The Zombivor concept is one of Zombicide’s quirkiest ideas: you get to play as a zombie whose abilities reflect their survivor past, offering a narrative flip side to the team’s efforts. Kyoko as a Zombivor suggests a creature whose undead mechanics might revolve around dread, unpredictability, or a particular weakness that the survivors need to exploit. The Zombivor variant is often designed to create moments of tension—will the party be surprised by a Kyoko that switches from hunter to opportunist? Will the board feel more alive (or less safe) when Kyoko’s zombie rules kick in? The charm is in the possibility: a single model that can pivot the momentum of a session by altering how a wave of walkers behaves, or how corridors get contested.

This binary pairing—Kyoko as a nimble survivor and Kyoko as a threatening Zombivor—reflects a broader design philosophy: give players a character who can be both a tactical asset and a storytelling pivot. The “unused” reality doesn’t erase those possibilities. Instead, it invites us to reframe Kyoko as a design artifact with latent potential: a survivor who rewards clever positioning and a Zombivor who incentivizes careful map control. The interplay, when triggered in the right map, is exactly the kind of memorable moment that keeps a campaign’s memory alive long after the last zombie falls.

## Gameplay Implications: Where Kyoko Shines (And Where She Doesn’t)
### Kyoko as Survivor: Mobility, Timing, and Zone Control
If you’re aiming to maximize stealth and repositioning, Kyoko’s survivor kit is designed to reward careful route planning. The typical synergy with terrain means you can stretch your action economy across a broader section of the board, reaching choke-points before the swarm closes in. In practice, Kyoko shines in maps that feature narrow corridors, multi-level layouts, or objectives that reward quick extraction rather than face-to-face brawling. The caveat is that on board with aggressive zombie spawns or dense zombie placement, Kyoko’s strengths can become a liability—speed is excellent, but if you sprint into a cluster of walkers, you risk becoming the inevitable icon on the survivors’ death tally.

### Kyoko as Zombivor: The Narrative Curveball
As a Zombivor, Kyoko invites the table to tilt the expected balance. The exact mechanics vary by edition, but the basic idea often centers on a “what if” state—what if the survivor’s experience manifests as a terrifying rebirth? A Kyoko Zombivor can generate tension with sudden changes in behavior or with special attacks triggered by certain board conditions. The strength of this mechanic is narrative tempo: it keeps players guessing, shifting the board’s emotional texture from “we’ll reach the objective” to “how do we survive the Kyoko surge?” The weakness is practical: if the table isn’t enthusiastic about playing alternately as human and zombie, the moment can feel gimmicky rather than strategic. For groups that love theater and turn-by-turn storytelling, Kyoko as Zombivor is a gold mine; for players chasing min-max optimization, it might feel like a spice that’s too spicy for the core broth.

### Starter Items, Special Actions, and Replay Value
Kyoko’s unique starter items and actions (as with most exclusives) are designed to offer a neat, fresh angle on a familiar game. The replay value hinges on how much your group enjoys experimenting with a different first turn or a slightly altered objective. If your typical campaign emphasizes brute-force survival, Kyoko’s kit may feel underutilized; if you’re the sort who loves experimenting with new map setups and asymmetric roles, Kyoko becomes a charming, underutilized engine for fresh sessions. The biggest factor here is your group’s willingness to incorporate a curveball and to remember that exclusives are, at their core, novelty pieces that reward creative play.

Kyoko is a curious relic of Zombicide Season 1: not essential, but not totally irrelevant either.
