## What's in the Box and What It Means for Outdoor Play
The Extreme Outdoor Edition is designed to shrug off the usual indoor fragility of the original burrito-flinging chaos and say, Let’s go outside where the wind is a character and the grass is… also a character. What you actually get:
- 2 foam burritos (think soft, safe, and surprisingly squeezable) for throwing and dodging
- A deck of Burrito Battle cards that mix attack, defense, and mischief
- Score sheets and a timer that looks alarmingly like a science fair project
- A small set of targets that can be placed around your outdoor space
- Quick-start rule book that reminds you to breathe between chaos moments

Our verdict on components: the foam burritos feel sturdy enough to survive a backyard lawn chair’s brutal criticism, but gentle enough that your dog won’t mistake them for chew toys. The card deck is sturdy enough to survive a few boisterous eruptions from players who discover that mercy is a concept the game is not interested in teaching. The timer is delightfully loud, which is excellent for photo moments and terrifying for neighbors trying to enjoy their Sunday brunch.

## Setup: From Box to Backyard Mayhem
Setting up is a ritual that prepares you for war without the dapper uniform. You spread targets around your play area, kick off a quick warm-up (stretch those shoulders, folks—throwing at full power is a musical experience for your tendons), and shuffle the Burrito Battle cards with theatrical flourish. The instructions insist you should not throw burritos indoors, which is a comedy of errors we only mildly disappointingly ignore in practice. Outdoor setup benefits from a flat surface; if you’ve got a gently sloped lawn, you’ll want to optimize your target placement to avoid orchestra-level chaos when burritos take flight.

One of the big wins here is the automatic rebalancing of space. Indoor rooms have furniture; outdoor space has trees and possibly a sprinkler system that loves to audition as a slippery opponent. The Extreme Outdoor Edition embraces these variables by giving you plastic stakes to mark your zones and a timer that doesn’t quit when you accidentally park the burrito in someone’s lemonade stand. For a game night outside, the setup feels like you’re preparing a tiny athletic event rather than just placing a game on a table.

## How It Plays: A Dance of Burritos and Strategy
At its core, Throw Throw Burrito is a dodgeball-meets-card-game chaos engine with foam projectiles. The Extreme Outdoor Edition adds more legroom for improvisation and a few new card effects that reward getting loud and distracted in the best possible ways.

Turn structure is straightforward: you draw a card, resolve its effect, and then you either attempt to dodge or target your fellow players depending on the card you pulled. The catch? You’re in a game where the primary objective is to reduce other players’ score by colliding with their burritos—or by making them miss their own throws. Depending on your group’s level of theatrics, you’ll end up with slapstick moments that look like a live-action cartoon and sound like a very enthusiastic sports announcer.

The deck includes a few classic archetypes:
- Attack cards that force you to throw burritos at others while an announcer’s chant fills the air
- Defense cards that allow you to dodge or block a throw, often with a ridiculous flourish
- Trick cards that swap targets, swap cards, or swap the rules (we’ve seen everything from “every burrito is now double the distance” to “you must raccoon-dance before throwing”) 

The pacing benefits from the outdoor space. Winds can introduce unpredictability in a way that feels almost magical: a mild breeze can turn a direct hit into a near-miss that everyone pretends was a deliberate strategy. The Extreme Outdoor Edition plays well with a slightly competitive-but-amicable crowd. The rulebook encourages quick rounds and minimal downtime, which is essential when you have six players arguing about who touched the burritos last or who owes the other who knows how many rounds of snacks.

If you’re someone who loves competitive games but despises long downtimes, you’ll feel right at home. The game prizes speed, reflexes, and a willingness to lean into the ridiculous. The cards require a quick read and a swift decision because the timer doesn’t care about your feelings—it cares about your screams of delight or terror.

## Outdoor-Specific Pros and Cons
Outdoor play brings a lot of personality to the table. A breezy day changes the dynamic from “we’re playing a game” to “this is an event.” And that’s the beauty of throwing foam burritos in the great outdoors: there’s space for improvisation, misfires, and communal laughter.

Pros:
- Space to maneuver reduces the risk of hitting furniture or delicate setups indoors
- Wind adds a variable challenge that spices up decision-making
- Sunlight makes photos pop, allowing you to make a series of ridiculous, share-worthy moments
- Easy to clean up (foam burritos wipe down and don’t leave permanent stains)

Cons:
- Outdoor elements can muddy the clear reading of cards if you’re playing in bright sunlight or near a grill smoke cloud
- Wind can also turn the game into a chase scene, which some players may enjoy more than others
- The terrain matters: you’ll want a reasonably flat area to reduce the risk of trips and dramatic slips that end up on social media for all the wrong reasons

We recommend a space with boundaries and some ambient supervision (not hover-parents or drones, just a friend with a whistle and a sense of humor). The trailer-park chaos vibe is funny when everyone signs up for it, but it can wear thin if you’re not prepared to laugh with your own misfires.

## Accessibility and Safety: Keep the Fun Safe
We’re dealing with foam projectiles, which is great for safety when compared to harder projectile games, but not perfect. Here are the quick safety notes we recommend:
- Establish a clear throwing zone and a referee or impartial observer who can call safe throws and count-out-of-bounds moments.
- Remind players to avoid throwing at faces. It’s not a sports drill; it’s slapstick theater.
- Use the outdoor environment to your advantage but keep a water bottle handy and a sun hat for those under the summer sun.
- If you have pets or curious kids nearby, designate a separate play area to prevent unintended chaos in the living room.

The rules themselves are straightforward. You’ll have a “no cheap shots” courtesy rule in most groups, but the social energy often bends that line into a hilarious form of friendly mischief. The key is consent and laughter: if someone’s not having fun, switch up players or take a short break rather than pushing through with a forced grin.
