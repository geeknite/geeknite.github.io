## Gameplay Overview: Turn Structure and Core Loops
Saboteur’s core loop is deceptively simple: draw a card, play a card, pass the turn, and watch the tunnel twist and turn under your collective nose. Each player has a role card that remains secret for much of the game. The good dwarves want to reach the treasure by laying down a path that allows them to progress, while the saboteur(s) insert detours, blockages, or misdirective path cards that derail the team’s progress. The tension comes from the secrecy. You can trust no one, which makes it all the more delightful when a trusted ally proves to be an ally at exactly the right moment.

Path cards form the tunnel network. Each path card can be laid in such a way that it continues or blocks a route, creating lanes that may or may not lead to the treasure. The good guys want to assemble a continuous path from their starting point to the treasure reveal, while saboteurs pull the rug out with trick cards that cause the tunnel to collapse or redirect funds—sorry, path cards—into a dead end. The treasure is not a single card; it’s a sequence of cards that leads to a culminating reveal, and that reveal is what ends the round. The timing of this reveal matters. If it happens too early, the saboteurs get a morale boost from the chaos. If it happens too late, the folder of “we did it, we blocked them” becomes awfully heavy in the eyes of the players who just wanted to see gold shine.

In the NIB edition, the card stock’s durability matters when you’re shuffling by hand and performing the occasional “villain reveal” during the game. The shuffle speed and card handling are snappy, and that matters. You don’t want the flow to stall because someone’s card stuck to the other and demanded 15 seconds of “unstick.” The more you play, the more you realize Saboteur rewards memory and reading of tells—both verbal and nonverbal. This isn’t poker, but there are enough micro-expressions and misdirecting statements to keep the table lively even when the path cards look suspiciously straightforward.

If you want a quick example of how a typical round might unfold, imagine a party of five players. The first few cards are laid down with cautious optimism. The saboteur quietly pivots, a misdirecting comment here and there, some subtle card plays that block a corridor, and suddenly two players discover their path leads to a dead end. The tension mounts. Someone else calls the bluff. The round ends with a decision: do you press on, or do you abandon a route that looks too compromised? These decisions are the heartbeat of Saboteur, and the Amigo incarnation keeps that heartbeat reliable without forcing you into a pre-med course in the art of bluffing.
