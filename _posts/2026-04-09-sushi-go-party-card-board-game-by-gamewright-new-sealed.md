## Introduction

Welcome, fellow deck-dabblers and high-stakes napkin collectors. Today we are tearing open a brand new, still-warm-from-the-warehouse copy of Sushi Go Party! by Gamewright. If you crave a game that feels like a culinary arcade cabinet—bright, chaotic, and somehow elegant in its simplicity—this is your ticket. Sushi Go Party! is the party-friendly evolution of the beloved Sushi Go! style of drafting and set collection, but with more menus, more players, and enough cardboard cutlery to start a small kitchen disaster. We will dive into the box contents, the setup, the rules (which are mercifully light), and whether this is the kind of thing your group should buy, borrow, or avoid like an overcooked nigiri.

For the uninitiated, Sushi Go Party! is a card drafting game where players pass hands of cards around the table and select one card per round, aiming to assemble the best combos of sushi dishes. The party edition adds a rotating menu system, so no two games feel exactly the same, and you can customize the number of players with a set of menu cards. If you like fast, friendly, snackable strategy that doesn’t require a PhD in math to enjoy, keep reading. If your gaming group prefers grimoires, Hamiltonian paths, and existential dread in scoring, well, you may still enjoy it, but you might need a larger table and a sense of humor as a side dish.

> Pro tip: treat this like a culinary RPG session. You are rolling through menus, building a feast, and mercilessly snatching the best combos away from your friends. Also, keep napkins nearby. A lot of napkins.

## How to play: setup, turn structure, and scoring basics

### Setup time and player range

Sushi Go Party! scales from two to eight players, which makes it a versatile pick for couples, roommates, and the full-blown dinner party zoo. Setup is a mix of laying out the menu cards, shuffling sushi cards, and distributing the starting hand sizes. If you want to dive right in, you can opt for a quick start by selecting a couple of menus and letting players draft with a reduced deck. The “party” aspect comes from the ability to customize the menu mix to fit your group’s taste for risk, cooperation, or sheer chaos.

### The turn flow

Each round, players receive a hand of cards from the deck and select exactly one card to keep, passing the rest to the player on their left. After everyone picks, a new hand is passed to the right, and you repeat until all cards are claimed. It’s the classic drafting Mechanic A that feels like a fast-paced card-lobby where everyone is shouting, but with more pastry puns and fewer actual pastries. The goal: assemble cards that maximize your scoring potential for the menus you’re playing with that round.

### Menu cards and scoring

This is where Sushi Go Party! earns its spice rack. Each round, you’ll use a set of menu cards that determines which combos score points. Common themes include collecting sushi kinds in ascending or descending counts, forming pairs, or creating long runs. The party edition introduces modular menus, meaning you can mix and match to tailor the difficulty and variability. The scoring can be light enough for newcomers or surprisingly strategic for players who enjoy optimizing sequences.

A typical round might reward you for collecting: nigiri combos, tempura sets, sashimi triples, and quirky specialty cards like dumplings or egg nigiri. The scoring rules are laid out on the menus, with icons that you will start to recognize with repeated plays. The catch: some menus interact in interesting ways. You might be chasing a sushi run while simultaneously trying to assemble a set of sashimi that outscales everyone else. It’s light enough to learn in one sitting, but there’s enough depth to make it a staple for repeat sessions across different groups.

### Game length and pacing

With eight players, you’re looking at a mid-length party game, typically around 20 to 40 minutes depending on how long you discuss and strategize between passes. The shorter rounds come from the rapid card drafting, while the longer, more intricate menus can push you toward the upper end of that range. A key factor in pacing is how much time players take to optimize menus before committing to a dish scatter. If your group loves a micro-strategy challenge, lean into the more complex menus. If you want a quick, social palate cleanser, stick to a few simpler menus and enjoy the chaos as everyone bickers over the best plates.

### Solo and group dynamics

Sushi Go Party! is best with groups. It’s not designed as a heavy solo experience and doesn’t require a complex board state to enjoy. The game thrives on interaction: you’re constantly eyeing what others are collecting, sometimes pivoting your strategy mid-round as opponents pivot theirs. The social energy is high, which makes it a strong pick for family game nights or that friend group that argues about what counts as a proper nigiri topping. If you’re a loner who loves cards, you could technically play two-handed against the clock or test “hot-seat speed drafting” as a party game offline, but the real joy comes from a lively table and the occasional chaotic turn that leaves everyone cracking up.

## Strategy and tips for maximized fun

### Accessorize your menus with a plan, but allow flexibility

The versatility of the menu cards is Sushi Go Party! greatest strength. You can curate menus to favor aggressive drafting, safe sets, or a mix of both. My recommendation is to start with a balanced set that rewards a mix of known quantities (like nigiri and dumplings) with a few risky, high-payoff cards. This gives players a platform to decide between safe scoring or high-variance gambits. As you gain experience with your group, rotate in more challenging menus to keep the game fresh and test your improvisational skills.

### Reading the table: when to snatch plates and when to pass

A core skill is reading the room and forecasting what others might take. If someone is dangerously close to completing a menu, you may want to pivot and nab a card that denies them the opportunity, even if it’s not a perfect fit for your current plan. The drafting mechanic rewards both forward planning and opportunism. The better you become at anticipating menu completion in a single round, the more you’ll feel like you are orchestrating a tiny sushi heist without any need for a trench coat.

### Managing pace and social energy

Sometimes the group slows as people deliberate over a single card. If your table includes players who savor the micro-optimizations, set a soft time limit and keep the pace brisk. This helps keep the emotional energy high and prevents the session from dragging into a “permanent consult” moment where someone pulls out a calculator and a whiteboard. The motto: draft quickly, talk later. If you manage the tempo, you’ll keep the table engaged and ensure everyone gets a fair share of the laughs—and the misplays.
