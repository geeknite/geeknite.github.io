## How to play: setup, turns, and the art of the deal
### Setup and turn structure
The official setup is simple enough for a group of casual players and quick enough to keep interest high in a mixed party. Each player starts with a small hand of cards drawn from the deck. The goal is to accumulate three complete property sets, or to declare victory by the rules your table agrees upon for the round—because part of the fun is the improvisational teamwork you’ll engage in around the table as you negotiate.

A typical turn looks like this:
- Draw a card from the draw pile
- Play up to three cards from your hand, which can be money, property, or a deal-style action
- Use actions to trade, steal, or force small adjustments to property holdings
- End your turn, passing the deck to the next player

The pace is brisk. You’re constantly deciding whether to escalate a deal, pay a Debt Collector, or snap up a property that would complete a set unless someone else snags it first. The social dynamics mirror a tense shuffle of negotiations at a garage sale curated by a gentleman with a monocle and a clipboard.

### Example round: a quick narrative
Imagine three players: Alex, Beth, and Carlos. Alex has a couple of property cards in colors that will soon complete a set but needs one more color to push over the line. Beth holds a handful of money cards and a Deal Breaker, but she has not yet found a way to make Alex commit to a shared deal that would let her jump ahead. Carlos, who tends to play with the calm of a chess player, is just one property away from assembling three sets of his own, but his rivals are circling like sharks with golden cufflinks.

Round begins. Beth draws and surveys her hand. She plays a small deal to offer a trade that looks benign: her green property for Alex’s yellow property plus some cash. Alex counters with a quick, suave raise, suggesting he’ll throw in a small money card if Beth adds a second color to the mix. Carlos then unleashes a Debt Collector that targets Beth, forcing her to play money to pay him back, temporarily loosening his grip on the board. The round devolves into a theatrical negotiation that ends with Alex snagging a crucial property and creating a near-impossible-to-stop three-set scenario. The table erupts with groans and glee. It’s silly, strategic, and wonderfully social—the exact cocktail you want from a card-game spin on Monopoly.

### Strategic takeaways for the patient player
- You don’t always want to cash out your money early; sometimes you hold back to offer value-driven deals that leave others light and you crowned with a three-set finish.
- Deal cards can change the entire dynamic of the round. A well-timed Deal Breaker can instantly turn a near-win into a table-wide groan and a new leader emerges.
- The speed of rounds rewards social audacity. A well-delivered bluff or a clever ruse about a non-existent property can tilt a round in your favor, even if you were behind on the actual card inventory.
