## Material, build quality, and longevity

The “granite” label in the product name is a nod to either solid granite or more commonly granite composite—a mix of natural stone and resin designed to mimic the look of real stone while keeping things practical for kitchen life. In Geeknite land, this means you get a sleek, non-porous surface that resists staining, heat, and daily abuse much better than some budget stainless-only options. The truth, as always, lies somewhere in the middle: it isn’t foolproof, but it’s a solid workhorse.

- Pros: high durability, heat resistance, signal-quiet surface (no loud clanks when you drop a pan), and a look that says “I’m sophisticated, but I still make lasagna.”
- Cons: heavier than standard stainless steel, best installation requires proper mounting support, and you’ll want to avoid acidic cleaners that can dull the finish over time.

Maintenance-wise, you’ll want to give it a quick wipe-down after use and avoid abrasive scrubs that could scratch the surface. A simple daily wipe with mild soap and water should keep the finish looking sharp for years. If you’re curious about granite vs. composite debates, this sink sits nicely in the middle—durable, stylish, and not as fragile as high-gloss enamel, which deserves its own sitcom.

## Fitment, installation, and what you actually need to install it

Size is the star here, but installation is the supporting cast. A 1160 mm length means you’ll likely need a 1180–1200 mm countertop cutout, depending on the mounting style and chosen trim. Undermount installations require sturdy cabinetry and precise cutouts to avoid flex or leakage; surface-mounted (top-mount) variants are a bit more forgiving but may require a thicker countertop edge to maintain a clean line.

- Cabinet width: typically 60 cm (600 mm) base cabinets work with this sink, but you’ll want to verify your exact model’s mounting depth and lip thickness.
- Cutout: measure the inside dimensions of your cabinet cavity carefully, and account for clearance around the drain and any accessories you’ll mount nearby.
- Plumbing: plan tailpieces and trap alignment to ensure efficient drainage with minimal kinks in the hoses. If you’re not confident with plumbing, consider a pro to avoid leaks and headaches.

## Care, maintenance, and longevity: keeping the sparkle alive

Granite surfaces are surprisingly forgiving, but they aren’t invincible. Routine care is straightforward:

- Wipe down after use with a mild dish soap and warm water.
- Avoid harsh abrasives or acidic cleaners that can dull the surface.
- Dry with a soft cloth to prevent water spots and lime scale near the drain.
- Use a non-scratch sponge for stubborn residue; if you must scrub, go gentle.

Sealing is not always required for granite sinks (unlike some granite countertops), but if you’ve got a granite composite, a periodic wipe-down with a non-abrasive sealer can keep the finish looking fresh for longer. In the long run, the key is to treat it like a premium surface: respect, routine care, and a dash of humor when the dishwasher fails to load properly.
