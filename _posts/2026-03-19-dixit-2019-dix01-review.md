## How the game plays
Dixit is often described as a storytelling guessing game. The general flow goes like this:

- A storyteller selects a card from their hand and offers a clue that describes it in some creative way. The clue can be a phrase, a word, a short story, or even a pun that reflects your mood. The catch is to make the clue specific enough to avoid giving away the exact card but broad enough that other players can see a link to theirs.
- Each other player selects a card from their hand that matches the clue, and places the card face down along with the storyteller's card on the table.
- All chosen cards are revealed face up. Players vote on which card they think is the storyteller card.
- Scoring occurs depending on whether players could guess the storyteller easily, and whether the storyteller successfully kept a balance between too obvious and too obscure.

This flow repeats for several rounds, with players replenishing their hands and a new storyteller taking the lead each round. The game ends when someone reaches the preset score or after a fixed number of rounds. In practice, the game tends to stretch as long as your group wants, because the true value lies in the social dynamic rather than the purely mechanical victory condition.

Dixit toes a tight rope between simplicity and depth. The rules are short, but the choices you make can ripple across multiple rounds and influence how others perceive your storytelling style. - Optimal group size: 4 to 6 players tends to produce the best balance between drama and guesswork. With 3, you may end up with too many correct guesses or too many misses to keep the tension. With 7 or more, the chaos increases and the experience can feel more like a collective improv session than a strategy game, which is not bad, just different.
- Play style: Dixit rewards creative minds and lighthearted banter. If your group enjoys improvisation, storytelling games, or art appreciation with a competitive edge, you will feel right at home. If your crew prefers heavy euro mechanics or calculative planning, Dixit may not scratch the exact same itch, but its social cachet can still win you over on a casual game night.
- Teaching the game: Start with a few rounds using broad, playful clues. Gradually increase complexity by using abstract clues and references that require a longer memory of prior rounds. This helps players tune their storytelling voice without turning the session into a puzzle hunt.
