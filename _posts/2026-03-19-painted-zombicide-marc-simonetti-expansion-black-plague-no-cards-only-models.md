## Assembly, priming, and basing

No Cards Only Models implies unboxing becomes a mini sculpture project rather than a role-playing tilt at the table. The assembly is straightforward, with a few common considerations:

- Clean mold lines with a sharp hobby knife or fine sandpaper. The Simonetti sculpts often carry fine, feathered edges that show through if you miss them, so take your time here.
- Pinning larger pieces helps with durability. If you have a particularly heavy mount or a two-handed weapon, consider a pin to keep the pose intact during handling.
- Assembly order can affect painting throughput. Start with the torso pieces and then attach limbs or cloaks once the torso base coat is in place. That way you don’t accidentally snag a cloak while cleaning up the base.

Primer choice matters. White primer will give you the brightest color transitions and is the classic choice for display painting on white surfaces. Black primer can be excellent for deeper shadows on metal and leather, especially when you’re aiming for a grim, doom-filled palette. If you want to keep things simple, gray primer provides a neutral middle ground that plays nicely with most color families.

Basing is where you can turn a good display piece into a story. A necropolis-stone look works wonders for the plague doctors and general undead-forward aesthetics, while a swampy or ruined cathedral base can bring out the gloom for the knights. It’s not unusual to mix basing styles across a single army—this is a display set, after all, and cohesion sometimes means “cohesion with variety.”

## Painting approach: a practical workflow

What follows is a practical, no-nonsense approach to getting these marvellous pieces from gray plastic to gallery display. It’s not a space opera; it’s a well-lit, well-layered, cleanly shaded painting session that yields results you can be proud of without needing a full studio in your kitchen.

### Primer and underpainting
- Start with a clean workspace and good lighting. A quick sanitizer of the model’s surface with mild soap and water helps remove any leftover mold release. Dry thoroughly.
- Prime with your preferred neutral tone. White primers make colors pop; black primers give you deeper shadows right off the bat. If you’re feeling adventurous, gray primer often gives you flexibility to push both light and dark tones.
- Underpainting can be a practical route if you’re going for a theatrical look. For instance, a cool blue wash on metallics under a warm highlight can create a surprising pop when the metal catches the light.

### Base coats and color planning
- Plan your palette around the character archetype. Knights in steel: metallics with rich blues or burgundies; plague doctors: bone, teal, and brass accents; necromancers: charcoal robes with electric-lime accents to echo Simonetti’s color usage.
- Block in base colors with thin, even coats. You want color depth, not thick paint that obscures detail.
- Keep metal and non-metal areas distinct. Use defined color separation to prevent a muddy look on close inspection.

### Shading, washes, and contrast
- Use washes to deepen recesses and bring out bolt holes, folds, and skin textures. A sepia-toned wash in the leather areas or a blue-gray wash in steel gives depth without overpowering the mini.
- For high-contrast display pieces, consider Nonmetallic Metal (NMM) techniques on armor. It’s slower but yields a gorgeous, reflective look without actual metal paints. If you’re unsure, a standard metallic trio (dark wash, midtone, bright highlight) is perfectly serviceable.
- Skin tones benefit from a warm base shade, with cooler shadows and a pale highlight. The goal is a living, slightly unhealthy complexion that reads well in photos.

### Highlights and edge work
- Edge highlighting should be deliberate but not overdone. The goal is to guide the viewer’s eye along sculpture details without turning the model into a steel-edged silhouette.
- For capes and robes, a midtone base followed by crisp highlights at the edges creates a sense of movement. Don’t overdo the edge lines; a little goes a long way on these sculpts.
- Metallics benefit from a bright highlight on the most prominent edges to catch light in display photography. A touch of rust on weathered metal can add character and storytelling.

### Finishing touches and varnish
- A thin gloss on gems or watery surfaces can add interest, followed by a matte or satin varnish overall to unify the piece and protect the paint. For display models, a satin finish often strikes the best balance between look and practicality.
- A final quick glaze across the robe folds or cloak areas can unify the color and pull the palette together.

There you have it: a methodical approach that lets you enjoy the craft without turning your hobby into a full-blown lab experiment. ## Display tips and photography

Having fancy models is one thing; capturing them in a photo that does them justice is another. The Marc Simonetti models have strong silhouettes and bold contrasts, which can be highlighted with thoughtful lighting. Here are a few practical tips:

- Lighting setup: use a two-light key-and-fill arrangement with daylight-balanced bulbs. Position one light at roughly 45 degrees to the subject and the other from the opposite side for balanced shadows.
- Backgrounds: a neutral gray or black backdrop helps the colors pop; you can also try a subtle textured backdrop that echoes a stone floor or cathedral ruin.
- Camera settings: a small to medium aperture (f/8 to f/11), a modest ISO (100–400), and a stable tripod will help you keep the details sharp without blurring highlights.
- Post-processing: a touch of clarity and light adjustment can bring out the intricate lines without over-saturating the palette.
