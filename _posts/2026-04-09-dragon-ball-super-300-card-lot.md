### Condition and NM expectations

Near Mint means you should expect minimal edge wear, no creases, and full glossy stock. In a 300-card chunk, you’ll likely see some variation: a handful of cards may be closer to light-play than NM, and a few might be accurate borderline if you’re unlucky. ## How to sort and triage 300 cards like a pro

### Step 1: Initial pass and damage control

Open the package with ceremony, like you’re revealing a saga. Do a quick surface inspection for readability, water damage, or chunky glue. Establish a few piles: NM, near-mint minus (NM-), played, damaged, and unknown. The unknown pile is where you place anything you’re unsure about. You’ll revisit it after you’ve had time to spread the rest of the cards across your desk in a rainbow of sleeves and team colors.

### Step 2: Sort by rarity first

In many DBS sets, rarities are a predictor of value and utility. Start with SRs (Super Rare) and higher, then move to R (Rare), then to C (Common). This helps you identify potential trade or upgrade targets. If you see a lot of duplicates in the same set, you can either deck-build from what you’ve got or pitch the duplicates for trade credit to fill in gaps.

### Step 3: Set-by-set organization

Dragon Ball Super cards across sets can share mechanics, art lines, and sometimes even the same character arts reused across sets. Grouping by set helps with future resale or binder organization. It also makes it easier to assess the card’s playability in current formats. If you want to be aggressive, you can cross-reference sets with current meta lists and rate each card's value to your own deck-building needs.

### Step 4: Foils and holo checks

Holo or foil cards stand out with more color and texture. They’re often among the more valuable pulls in a bulk lot. Separate foils from non-foils. If you luck into rare foils in NM condition, you’ve found a keeper that could appreciate in value or at least придур... compatibility with your binder’s aesthetic mood (which is totally a thing in nerd culture).

### Step 5: Condition re-check before sleeving

After triage, re-check each card you intend to sleeve. NM cards deserve respectful sleeves; don’t sleep on this step. Sleeves protect your cards from long-term wear, oils from your fingerprints, and your own occasional clumsy coffee spills during late-night deck builds.

## Value, price, and the real cost of bulk buys

### Quick math: when bulk buys pay off

The math behind bulk buys is simple but not glamorous: if you can identify a handful of strong cards within 300, the average resale value and play-time utility of the group might exceed the upfront cost. ### How to price a bulk lot for yourself

- Determine your target deck needs and which cards are non-negotiable for your builds.
- Separate high-demand rares and foils that pop up in your local meta.
- Estimate the value of bulk that remains as binders stuffer, trade fodder, or potential donation to a community event. Many players value their bulk differently, but the core metric is: does the lot improve your playable options more than it costs you in time and energy?

### When to walk away

If you open a lot and see a preponderance of damaged cards, mis-sleeved wear, or a suspiciously uneven distribution that smells like a counterfeit factory (not common, but not impossible either), consider returning to the seller or requesting a partial refund. The internet is full of horror stories about “NM” cards that look more like “N- stands for Neglected” cards. If the deal feels murky, trust your gut and the price tag you’re comfortable with losing if the cards don’t meet expectation.

## Authenticity and fakes: how to protect yourself

Dragon Ball Super cards are printed in large quantities, but fakes can slip into bulk purchases. Here are some quick checks:

- Inspect the card stock and font consistency. Real cards in modern print runs have uniform font weight and alignment.
- Check holographic alignment if the card is holo foil. Fakes often have slightly misaligned holo overlays.
- Compare rare or character design attributes against a trusted reference such as official DBSCG site or a reputable wiki. While you can’t rely on one source, cross-checking helps.
- If something looks too good to be true, it probably is. If a bulk lot claims to be “NM” and includes an extremely valuable SR or secret rare in immaculate condition, proceed with healthy skepticism.

If you’re uncertain about authenticity, consider a small-scale verification with your local game store or a trusted online community. You’re not losing a life gem by asking questions; you’re protecting your collection from becoming a giant stack of future regrets.
