# Zombicide - Black Plague: Wulfsburg (Asmodee Italia) Review

If you thought your dice addiction was under control, think again: Zombicide Black Plague has a new itch to scratch, and Wulfsburg is here to remind you that undead peasants are a great way to spend a Saturday night. The Italian edition from Asmodee Italia brings not just a new map but a whole mood: grimy, medieval, and somehow more chaotic than a tavern brawl after a kebab spill. Strap in, fellow geeks, because we are diving into the Wulfsburg experience, from the plastic jiggle of the survivors’ minis to the poetic despair of rolling three blacks and an overachieving misfire.

## Overview: What is Wulfsburg and why should you care?

Wulfsburg is a departure from the standard Zombicide cadence. You’re not simply smashing undead until the clock runs out; you’re navigating a new map with its own rules, its own sweet spot of danger, and a whole cast of fresh zombies that are as groovy as they are deadly. In short: Wulfsburg is the board game equivalent of upgrading from a moonlighting blacksmith to a full-fledged necromancer who also moonlights as a pastry chef.

This expansion adds a dedicated map board, new terrain tiles, and a cadre of new zombies plus a couple of nasty surprises that remind you that in Zombicide, cooperation is optional—cooperation is only fun when you’re winning. The Italian edition leans into the theme with quality components, language-appropriate rulebooks, and a chic box art that makes you feel like you’re raiding a medieval fortress rather than just resetting a dungeon crawl. The game remains as chaotic as a librarian at a baklava festival, but with better lighting and more screaming.

## In the box: what you actually get

- A large map board that channels a medieval fortress city vibe, with alleyways, market squares, and the occasional ominous cathedral that doubles as a functional death trap.
- Cards, tokens, and a buttload of plastic miniatures that somehow survive the dog toy test and the cat toy test with equal aplomb.
- A handful of new survivor sculpts that scream character: the grizzled hunter, the cunning rogue, the miracle-working monk, and a few others who would probably prefer a nap to a brisk zombie purge.
- Rulebook and quick-start sheets that, with the Italian localization, actually feel approachable rather than a puzzle wrapped in a riddle wrapped in a translation ambush.

If you’ve played prior Zombicide Black Plague sets, the Wulfsburg content slots neatly into that ecosystem but adds enough new quirks to feel fresh. If you’re new to Zombicide, this is a meaty entry point that still teaches you the core mechanics: plan, coordinate, and hope your dice love you more than the necromancer loves a good misclick.

## Thematic design and aesthetic: atmosphere matters

The art direction of Wulfsburg captures that “winter storm meets medieval market” vibe. The tiles show truncated streets, wind-swept plazas, and a cathedral that looks suspiciously like it wants to be a boss fight. The miniatures—yes, even the goofy-looking peasants with their pitchforks—get a little upgrade in scale and detail compared to some earlier Zombicide runs. The Italian edition respects the color palette and the gritty vibe without tipping into gaudy medieval cosplay. It’s not just a board game; it’s a mood board for a zombie siege where your life is measured in dice and desperate decisions.

One delightful design flourish is how the map’s layout forces you to split your party in certain rounds. You’ll tunnel your way into a side street, peel off into a cemetery, then realize you left your buddy behind while a chorus of moaning undead reunites in the center square. It’s messy, it’s glorious, and it’s exactly the kind of chaos that fuels geeky storytelling around the table.

## How it plays: core mechanics you already know, plus some spicy twists

If you’ve played Zombicide before, you’ll recognize the core loop: assign actions, roll dice to attack, terrain and zombie spawns create escalating tension, and the clock ticks in a way that makes you question every life choice you’ve ever made. Wulfsburg doesn’t throw away what fans love; it builds on it with a few carefully chosen tweaks:

- New survivor skills that provide interesting synergies. Some skills let you push through crowds; others help you corner threats without losing your entire party to a stray polar bear—yes, we’re dealing with a wolfish vibe, and it’s not just thematic fluff.
- A set of new zombie types that feel different enough to matter in a game where every die roll can swing the fate of your crew. You’ll encounter threats that require more strategic retreat than a videogame boss fight, which is exactly the kind of chess-with-death that Zombicide thrives on.
- Terrain features crafted to influence line of sight and movement in meaningful ways. No longer can you sprint through a hallway because you know a 6 is coming up; you’ll need to plan routes, block ambush points, and think several turns ahead. It’s a good reminder that in a zombie apocalypse, speed is overrated; positioning is king.

This is not a sandbox expansion—though you could treat it as one if you have a healthy tolerance for sprawling campaigns. The rules are approachable, and the Italian translations keep the game accessible, with enough flavor to keep you immersed without tripping over linguistic oddities.

## Solo, co-op, and the party dynamics

Zombicide shines as a co-op experience; Wulfsburg amplifies this by introducing scenarios that reward thoughtful coordination rather than pure brute force. If you’re playing solo variants (there are fan-made sheets and official-ish guidelines for solo play in many Zombicide communities), Wulfsburg’s layout can be surprisingly satisfying. You’ll simulate a small team of survivors, each with a distinct voice and set of reflexes, and you’ll grumble when a single miscalculated move leaves your entire baseline squad in the red zone.

In a party with 2–4 players, the dynamics tilt toward teamwork and, honestly, a lot of heckling about whose idea was “the best worst plan.” The best plan is often the one that involves sacrificing a sacrificial lamb to draw the right amount of attention from the necromancers. It’s as comic as it is brutal, which is exactly the spirit of Zombicide.

## Difficulty, balance, and replayability

Wulfsburg sits somewhere in the sweet spot for experienced players: enough new elements to feel fresh, but not so many that newcomers feel overwhelmed. The scenarios scale nicely as you add players or push the time pressure up a notch. The balance between luck (the dice) and strategy (where you move and who you shoot) remains intact; in other words, you’re not watching your fate handed to you on a silver platter by a random number generator alone—you still need to think, which is good for your aging brain.

Replayability is solid. The map’s layout variations, combined with different enemy spawns and survivor sets, give you a reason to revisit Wulfsburg multiple times before you decide you’ve exhausted the content. The modular nature mirrors many tabletop RPGs in vibe: you’re not finishing a single path; you’re exploring different routes and different endings, with occasional gory plot twists that would make a good graphic novel—if you’re into that sort of thing and your friends won’t judge you for it.

## Components quality and value for money

Asmodee Italia has delivered a polished box with sturdy components and a satisfying weight to the game. The plastics are robust enough for repeated play; the tokens don’t warp easily; and the art direction maintains that cohesive medieval-zombie aesthetic. In terms of value, Wulfsburg sits at a price point that fans expect for a sizeable expansion, and the content inside justifies the cost if you’re committed to continuing the Zombicide Black Plague journey. If you’re a collector of themed expansions, the Wulfsburg set is one that belongs in your cabinet, not merely on a shelf collecting dust.

A note on longevity: if you’re the kind of player who keeps track of campaign logistics (which survivor has which upgrade, which equipment is in the stash, etc.), Wulfsburg provides enough flavor and scenario variety to make your campaign last longer than a zombie’s patience at a late-night bakery. It’s not a one-and-done thing; it’s a recurring invitation to let the undead crash your tabletop party again and again.

## Strategy tips: how to maximize your run in Wulfsburg

- Map control is everything. Early on, secure a couple of choke points that you can defend without splitting your team in the wrong direction. The wolves and extra zombies tend to funnel players toward narrow corridors; a well-timed shield wall buys you precious turns.
- Coordinate reloads. The survivor who holds the ranged weapons should stagger reloads with the other players, so you aren’t caught with empty hands when a wall of walkers crashes through the doorway.
- Don’t ignore the “special” zombie types. Some of them have abilities that can ruin a perfectly good plan in a single activation. Focus your attack and don’t let a single dangerous unit slip by. A careful wipe is better than a chaotic clearance that leaves you in a worse position than you started.
- Learn from failure. If a run goes south, take notes. Which routes proved deadly? Which terrain pieces turned the tide? Turn each session into a learning exercise—this is not just a game; it’s a tactical workout with a post-apocalyptic backdrop.

## H2: The Italian edition experience: language, flavor, and accessibility

Asmodee Italia handles the localization with care. The rulebooks are fairly readable, the quick-start sheets translate concepts cleanly, and the board legends are understandable without needing a glossary that looks like a doctoral thesis. The flavor text on cards and scenario inserts translates well, preserving humor and menace in a way that feels natural rather than forced. This matters because a good rulebook is a gateway drug to a great game; a poor translation can derail your immersion before your first die is rolled.

The design team did a good job of ensuring that the Italian text doesn’t hinder flow. No awkward phrasing, no translation traps, just a smooth onboarding experience that leaves you free to focus on stabbing undead and saving your own skin—two activities that, if you’re lucky, occur in that order.

## The verdict: should you buy Wulfsburg?

Short answer: yes, if you love Zombicide and you crave a bit more tactical depth, variable map layouts, and a few spicy twists that keep the table on its toes. Long answer: if your group borrows from the same well—fast-paced, co-op zombie shtick with a heavy tilt toward planning and team coordination—Wulfsburg is a welcome addition that adds meaningful choice without overwhelming you with complexity. The Italian edition keeps the experience accessible, and the production quality backs up the price with tangible weight and durability. If you’re a casual player, you’ll still have a lot of fun, but it shines brightest when you’re in the mood for a challenge, a bit of roleplay flavor, and a campaign that demands more than rolling five dice and hoping for a miracle.

In terms of overall quality, Wulfsburg is a solid 8.5 out of 10 for players who want a robust, thematic, and replayable addition to the Black Plague line. There are a few areas that could be tightened—namely a few edge-case spawn rules that could be interpreted differently by different groups—but these are minor niggles rather than game-breaking flaws. If you enjoyed the base game and have a ready-made group that loves strategy mixed with luck, this expansion will feel like a natural evolution rather than a half-hearted add-on.

## Final recommendation

- If you want a heavier, more strategic Zombicide experience that still honors the co-op chaos spirit, pick up Wulfsburg.
- If you’re new to Zombicide, start with the core Black Plague and, once you’re comfortable, graduate to Wulfsburg for a proper test of your group’s synergy.
- If you’re not in love with medieval aesthetics or you hate puzzles, you might still want to borrow Wulfsburg for a single session just to appreciate the design choices—then return to more straightforward zombie mauling.

In any case, Wulfsburg remains a strong entry in the Zombicide canon, marrying a satisfying thematic direction with mechanical depth that makes it worth the shelf space. It’s not the easiest entry point for new players, but for veterans, it’s the kind of expansion that makes you giddy about the next playthrough and the next table fight with the undead.
