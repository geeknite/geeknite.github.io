## Introduction

If you thought Zombicide: Green Horde was enough to turn you into a quasi-druid who negotiates with orcs instead of players, think again. The Friends & Foes expansion—also known as Survivors & Foes in some spellbooks—invites more chaos, more character quirks, and more ways to curse your table as you murder countless zombies and maybe your friendships along the way. In Geeknite fashion, we tested this expansion with the same brutal rigor we apply to late-night pizza choices: by arguing about balance, laughing at the card text, and then playing. Because nothing says “game night” like a group trying to decide whether to go left or right or whether this orc has realized your plan is to “negotiate with them using a shiny trinket.”

### What's in the box

In this expansion, you'll find a curated mix of:

- 6 brand-new survivors with distinct personalities and abilities
- 6 new Foes that provide fresh strategic headaches
- A handful of Encounter and Objective cards that twist the campaign in unexpected directions
- Terrain tiles and tokens to expand your map chaos

The question isn’t “Does it fit?” but “Will it break your table first?” Spoiler: yes, it will. The box is a cheerful jumble of color, cardboard, and enough punchy art to make you forget you just spilled tea on your favorite lizard-man’s chest plate.

#### Component quality

The components feel sturdy enough to survive a goblin roof-raid, a zombie horde, and your cousin’s overly aggressive celebratory high-fives. The survivors come with distinct color palettes and standees that don’t blur into one another during a mid-game sprint across the forest map. The Foes, meanwhile, look a touch more menacing than your typical undead chump — which is exactly what you want when your plan is “kill them, run, repeat.”

The cards are standard Zombicide fare: glossy, with clear icons that you’ll learn to skim while someone yells, “I have a plan!” The plan, alas, will almost always involve stepping into a trap and blaming the dice. But that’s part of the charm.

### Gameplay mechanics: what changed, what stayed delightful, what briefly traumatized your sense of time

Zombicide Green Horde: Friends & Foes adds a few notable twists without turning the game into a completely new beast. The core loop—activate a survivor, roll to attack, count who is adjacent to what—remains intact, which is good news for your friend who memorized the base game’s rulebook like a holy scripture. The new survivors bring individual abilities that encourage synergy or, more often, glorious failure.

- Survivor Abilities: Each new Survivor has a signature ability. Some let you manipulate the zombie pool in creative ways; others grant extra actions if you manage to survive a few rounds without taking a hit. The synergy between survivors becomes a puzzle, and like any good puzzle, it’s either a triumph or a loud, sustained groan as you realize you forgot to bring someone with a necessary skill.

- New Foes: The Foreboding Foes bring different attack patterns. Some are more resilient, some ignore certain terrain, and a few have special abilities that can flip a turn in a heartbeat. They force you to adapt your usual strategy, which is precisely what a campaign wants from you: adaptation, not perfection.

- Encounter Cards: The new Encounter cards shuffle into the existing deck, adding magic items, traps, or molecular-level chaos that opens doors to new tactical possibilities. The “Friends” side of the expansion makes diplomacy with your fellow survivors a possibility, which, in a camp full of grumpy heroes, can either save you or lead to a very dramatic mutiny.

- Campaign Flow: The expansion supports the base game’s campaign structure, meaning you can weave the new survivors and foes into your ongoing narrative without losing track of who is who and why. It’s not a full-fledged campaign book; it’s more like a supplement that respects the rules and adds a few spicy ingredients so your soup doesn’t taste like plain water.

If you’re curious how it compares to the base Green Horde, think of it as the seasoning packet that turns a decent chili into something memorable. The mechanics still feel familiar, the theme remains consistent (orcish fantasy murder, rather than romantic vampire intrigue), and the balance remains surprisingly sane given the amount of chaos you can conjure with a single elf with a frying pan.

### The survivors and foes: a closer look

This section dives into a handful of the new survivor archetypes and foe classes. You’ll meet characters who are all about hustle, grit, and occasionally questionable moral decisions. The Foes bring a mix of goblin ringleaders, armored orcs, and big-bad bosses that can make your hair stand on end even when you’re wearing a helmet.

- Survivor Example: The Lucky Scout
  The Lucky Scout has a knack for finding alternate routes and pulling off risky maneuvers. In practical terms, you’ll want to place this Survivor at the edge of the map and hope for a corridor with cover. The payoff is a mix of better-line-of-sight advantages and the occasional “oh wow, I rolled a critical and discovered a treasure.” The trick is to manage your expectations because luck is a fickle friend—partly a friend when you need a quick escape and partly a foe when you finally realize your dice have betrayed you during the final wave.

- Survivor Example: The Goblin Whisperer
  This one is a cheeky addition that can redirect a foe’s attention or create a distraction that buys time for your squad. It’s not a guarantee of victory, but it’s definitely a heartwarming way to keep the party’s spirits up while chaos roars in your ears.

- Foe Example: The Armored Warlord
  A bruiser that can shrug off a few hits and swing back with heavy intent. The tactic changes from “kill fast” to “hold ground and kite,” which is exactly what your table loves when the numbers get tough.

- Foe Example: The Forest Stalker
  A more elusive enemy that thrives in cover and uses terrain to its advantage. You’ll learn to respect sightlines, elevation, and how to avoid stepping on the wrong tile and triggering a backstory-friendly trap.

Note: You don’t need to memorize every new stat to enjoy the expansion. You just need to enjoy the chaos, the banter, and the occasional victory that feels less like skill and more like a friendly miracle from the dice gods.

### Theming, humor, and how well it fits with the tone

The Green Horde has always leaned into a lighthearted fantasy vibe with a grim undercurrent. Friends & Foes amplifies that tone, offering moments of humor as your survivors argue about whether to go left or right or whether this orc has realized your plan is to “negotiate with them using a shiny trinket.” The humor lands in a way that doesn’t feel at odds with the theme; it’s a wink at the absurdity of it all rather than a jolt to serious tone.

Artwork remains a highlight. The new cards, tiles, and standees are colorful and charming with enough personality to make each encounter feel distinct. The art direction continues to be a strong suit for Zombicide reissues, and Friends & Foes does not disappoint on that front. If you’re the kind of person who stares at the miniatures long after the game ends, you’ll be happy with the visuals.

### Balance and player count considerations

One of the biggest concerns with any expansion is balance: does adding new survivors and foes tilt the game into “you always win if you drew the right card” or does it add enough variability to keep each session fresh? In the case of Friends & Foes for Green Horde, the answer is a cautious but positive yes. The survivors bring new tools to the table, but not to the extent that it makes the base game feel obsolete. The foes are potent enough to threaten even experienced players, but the mechanics are such that learning new strategies remains rewarding rather than punishing.

As with any Zombicide variant, you’ll want to consider the number of players. With more players, the map tends to feel busier; with fewer players, the new encounters can hit harder. The expansion scales reasonably well, though you’ll still want to calibrate your expectations for table space and time. This is not a game that shouts “slower is better”; it’s more like “slower is sometimes more dramatic.”

### Replayability and customization options

Replayability is where this expansion shines. The added survivor and foe variety means you can run multiple campaigns with different combinations of characters and enemies. The encounter deck reshuffles in interesting ways, and you’ll find yourself returning to the setup to try a different synergy. The question becomes not whether you’ll play again, but whether you’ll survive long enough to reach the final boss while arguing about heroics and the proper life choice of using a frying pan as a weapon.

If you’re a die-hard completionist, you’ll want to incorporate the new items into your standard campaign rotation. The rules allow this without requiring you to become a rules lawyer, which is a blessing for most groups.

### Pros and cons: a balanced, tongue-in-cheek breakdown

Pros:
- Adds meaningful new survivors with distinct playstyles
- Fresh foes that require adaptive tactics
- Maintains core Zombicide feel while injecting personality
- Strong art, good components, solid box contents

Cons:
- Some new rules feel like “more of the same” for seasoned players
- A few card texts can be read in several different ways, depending on who is shouting
- The size of the box adds to shelf clutter if you’re a collector with limited space

Note: Despite the potential confusion for new players, the expansion still feels accessible. If you’ve ever taught Zombicide to a table full of new recruits, you know that being able to pick up a mechanic and try it out without hours of pre-reading is a godsend.

### Final verdict and recommendations

In short: if you’re building a Green Horde campaign and want something to spice things up without turning the game into a rulebook on top of a rulebook, this expansion is a smart addition. It’s not a full replacement for the base game, but it does give you enough new content to keep sessions engaging and varied. If you’re a fan of the series and enjoy new character concepts with lively visuals, you should consider adding Friends & Foes to your collection.

That being said, this expansion isn’t necessary for everyone. If you’re relatively new to Zombicide or you’re happy with the base game’s current balance, you might want to wait for a sale or purchase when you’re ready to dip your toes into the expanded pool. It’s always nice to have more options, but it’s not essential.

### Practical playing tips

- Start with two to three new survivors and one or two new foes in your first session to gauge how they interact with your usual loadouts.
- Schedule a game night with both enthusiastic optimists and cunning skeptics; the combination makes for some entertaining debates after a few rounds.
- Use the new encounter cards to create “emergency moments”—cards that force players to pivot quickly and improvise.

It’s a strong choice for players who own Green Horde and want to mix up the pacing, tactics, and table talk. The added survivors and foes give you more opportunities to experiment with different setups, and that’s exactly the kind of replayability modern board games should aspire to provide.
