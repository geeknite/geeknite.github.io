## Real‑World Performance: How It Handles the Meat and the Menace

### Meat Types: From Pork to Beef to the Occasionally Ferocious Chicken Bone
- Pork shoulder and Boston butt: The 650W motor handles these like a sports car handles a daily commute. You’ll get clean, even slices with a minimum of drama. The blade tracks true; you’ll notice fewer wanderings if you keep the tray clean and the meat properly trimmed.

- Chicken bones and small bones: This is where the saw earns its belt. A robust bone saw can take a while to cut through, but with the right blade and a careful hand, you can cleanly separate bone from flesh without destroying the geometry of the cut. ## Cleaning, Maintenance, and Longevity: Treat It Like an Investment, Not a One‑Night Stand
Maintenance is boring in the best possible way. A well‑maintained machine remains reliable and safe. Here’s the routine we recommend:
- Clean after every use: wipe down the frame, guard, and table; remove meat residue from the blade and tray.
- Inspect the blade for nicks and warping; replace if needed. A compromised blade is a safety risk as well as a performance drainer.
- Check blade tension regularly. A loose blade is a recipe for wandering cuts and potential blade crash disasters.
- Lubricate the drive mechanism if the manufacturer recommends it; otherwise, keep it dry and clean. Avoid using heavy oils that could contaminate meat surfaces.
- Store in a dry place to prevent rust and corrosion; moisture is the villain here.
- Periodically recalibrate the guide and guard setup to maintain consistent cuts and safety margins.

If you’re the kind of person who writes a maintenance calendar on the fridge, you’re already halfway there. If not, print this section and pin it next to your cutting station like a reminder from the universe that you still have things to learn about equipment care.
