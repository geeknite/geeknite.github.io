## What is Codenames, really?

Codenames is a social word game at heart. It pits two teams against each other, with a spymaster on each side guiding their teammates toward a set of words spread across a grid. The twist is that the spymaster can only speak one word as a clue, plus a number indicating how many words on the grid relate to that clue. The teammates then debate, discuss, and attempt to identify the correct words before the other team does, all while avoiding a few perils: the assassin word that ends the round instantly, and the neutral words that simply end your turn if guessed.

This is not a game about stealth or gadgets; it’s a game about language, associations, and the social dance that happens when you pair a clever clue with a willing group of word-nerds and pun-lovers. It’s equal parts improv, vocabulary test, and competitive social experiment, with a big tempo shift that happens as soon as someone drops a clue that makes everyone groan, cringe, and cheer in equal measure.

## How to play (high-level)

Codenames is less a tutorial than a social contract. The rules are simple enough that you can pick them up in a single round, yet complex enough to reward cunning clue construction. The core flow goes something like this:

1) Set up the grid with 25 Word Cards face-up. Each word is a potential target or a trap, depending on your perspective and your vocabulary. The grid is a shared stage where every word becomes a potential ally or a potential banishment.

2) Choose Spymasters for each team. These players sit on the opposite side of the grid from their teammates so they can see the entire Key Card that assigns each word’s color.

3) The Spymaster gives a one-word clue followed by a number. The one word should connect as many of their team’s words as possible, and the number indicates how many words in the grid relate to that clue. For example, a clue like “Ocean 2” might point toward “Waves” and “Boat” if those are the red team’s words.

4) Teammates discuss and then guess. They can guess up to the number given by the spymaster, plus one extra if they’re confident, but they must stop if they think they’ve identified a non-matching word or if they realize they’ve reached the assassin.

5) If a guessed word is red, it’s covered with red and the team continues if there are more red words to find. If blue, the guess ends the turn and gives the other team momentum. If the assassin word is revealed, the guess ends immediately and the game ends with a loss for the guessing team. If a neutral word is revealed, the turn simply ends.

6) Play continues with alternating turns until one team has revealed all its words. The first team to finish their set wins. Or the table erupts in laughter because someone guessed a card that didn’t even exist in the mind of the spymaster.

While these steps feel straightforward, the psychological side of the game is where the magic happens. The spymaster must choose a clue that’s precise enough to be useful to their teammates but broad enough to cover multiple words without giving the other team easy targets. The guessers must balance their natural impulse to rush toward a big payoff with the restraint to avoid hitting the assassin. It’s a high-wire act every round.

## Player count, pacing, and recommended scenarios

Codenames scales well with group size, but you’ll notice the best pacing in mid-to-large groups. In a typical Geeknite setup, you’ll want 4–8 players for a tight, interactive experience where the spymaster’s clues spark lively discussion. If you’re feeling bold, you can crank it up to 10–12 players by rotating guesser roles and sharing the spymaster duties in fun, chaotic ways. The more players you add, the bigger the laughter, and the more you risk one perplexing clue that derails the entire night—still a risk worth taking in many cases.

- For a quick social night: a few rounds in 20–30 minutes, lots of reactions, lots of groans.
- For a longer session: a set of 3–4 rounds with a friendly wager on who can assemble the most elegant clue chain (without triggering the assassin).
- For families: the game’s simplicity can be a blessing; even younger players can contribute via word associations, especially if you allow some leniency on clues.

## Clue-building strategy: a tiny guide to not looking like you’re sabotaging yourself

The spicy secret of Codenames is that the best clues often require a deep understanding of the people you’re playing with more than a sturdy mental map of the entire dictionary. A good spymaster pays close attention to the group’s shared experiences, inside jokes, and common knowledge. A few practical heuristics:

- Use compound clues sparingly. “Famous actor” might be clear, but if you don’t share a culture reference, you risk confusion across the table. If your team loves classic cinema, you can use "Kubrick 3" or something similarly specific—but only if your entire team is on board.
- Tie multiple words together with a single clue, but avoid pitfalls. The more words you link, the riskier it becomes, especially if one of the linked words happens to belong to the other team.
- Avoid overly obscure words that only you understand. The best clues are those that your team can interpret quickly, even if it’s not a perfect match to every word.
