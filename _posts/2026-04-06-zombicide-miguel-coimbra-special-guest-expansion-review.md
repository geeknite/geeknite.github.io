## The Miniatures: A Masterclass in Gothic Grotesque

Miguel Coimbra’s signature style is instantly recognizable: elongated forms, dramatic silhouettes, a hint of baroque horror, and a texture that reads as both painter-friendly and intimidating to paint. The expansion’s miniatures deliver that look with a precision that encourages you to pick up that fine detail brush and commit to shading and edge-highlighting that would make a black metal album cover blush.

- Surivors: Coimbra’s survivors are not merely characters with two sticks and a gun. They’re living canvases whose poses convey their personalities—one might look like a stoic hunter; another, a gossamer dreamer who somehow survives by misplacing fear. In terms of scale and aural presence, they feel like you’ve invited a small theatre troupe of daredevil PCs to your kitchen table. Their sculpts are dynamic, their proportions visually interesting, and the little flourishes—laces, buttons, and utility belts—reward careful assembly and painting.
- Zombies: The zombie lineup is where this expansion earns its keep. Expect creeping undead with draped fabrics that catch washes in all the right ways, along with grotesque visages that seem to snarl through deteriorating flesh. The contrast between pale, almost porcelain skin tones and the darker, tattered clothing is a playground for contrast shading and weathering. If you’ve ever wanted a zombie horde that looks like it marched straight out of a vintage horror poster, this is your jam.

The sheer variety is a boon for painters who enjoy making each model tell a tiny story. You won’t be painting five identical zombies and calling it a day; every piece offers a slightly different texture or detail to coax your brush into revealing a narrative. The minis arrive in a way that suggests you’ll be taking your time with them, which is a vibe I’m all for—the apocalypse should be luxuriant, not transactional.

## Assembly and Quality: The Pain, the Joy, the Glorious Biting Point

Like many CMON sets, there’s a feel of “premium, but not precious” with the Miguel Coimbra Expansion. The gluing process for Zombicide minis is familiar if you’ve done it before: you clip, clean the nub, and pin in some cases if you’re a stickler for pose stability. In this expansion, the sculpts benefit from slightly cleaner mold lines than some older releases, which means less fiddling and more painting time. If you’re somebody who measures happiness by how clean the mold lines are rather than how many hours you’ve spent washing parts, you’ll appreciate the slightly more forgiving designs.

One of the more delightful elements is the balance between complexity and playability. The miniatures are intricate enough to warrant an extra session of assembly, but not so fiddly that it becomes a chore. The key is to plan your painting and assembly in stages: first the bases and the largest surfaces, then the smaller details. The result is a unit that looks like it leapt off the page of Coimbra’s art book and into your gaming table—without requiring a full-blown physics degree to assemble.

If you’re a painter who enjoys layering and edge highlighting, you’ll be in your element. The sculpts respond well to washes and dry brushing, making that gothic texture pop. For those who prefer a “just add paint” approach, you’ll find that the designs are inherently stage-ready; a clean base coat, quick washes, and selective highlights go a long way toward achieving a striking table presence.

## Painting Tips: Getting the Most Out of Coimbra’s Line

- Primer choice matters: A neutral gray or white primer helps the shadows pop without lending too much blue or purple cast to the skin tones.
- Use a three-step skin tone approach: base tone, mid-tone, and a careful highlight to bring forward the grotesque features without washing them into the background.
- Wet blending for capes and drapery: the cloth textures invite subtle color shifts that can look fantastic when you take your time.
- Armor and metal: a light metallic dry brush followed by a wash can yield a tarnished, aged metal look that feels right for a post-apocalyptic world where everything has been repurposed.
- Bases: Coimbra’s characters shine on dramatic bases—consider basing materials that evoke crumbling streets or ruined interiors to further amplify the mood.

If you’re new to mini painting, this expansion doubles as a gentle teacher. The sculpts are forgiving enough that even basic techniques will yield results that look competent and evocative. If you’re a seasoned painter, these models offer hours of delight and a few new textures to play with that aren’t present in your standard zombie set.

## Value for Money: Is It worth the buy?

Value is a difficult metric in hobby games, especially with premium expansions. The Miguel Coimbra Special Guest Expansion sits in that sweet spot where you’re paying for art, presentation, and a few gameplay twists rather than just extra plastic. If you’re a collector, a big Coimbra fan, or someone who wants to overhaul their Zombicide experience with a hefty dose of mood, this box feels worth it. The number of fully realized sculpts means you’re getting a more than adequate amount of content for your investment. If you only care about raw combat numbers, you might be less enthusiastic; if you care about tabletop storytelling and aesthetics, you’ll likely be smiling under your paintbrush at the end of the session.

That said, beware the shipping and the price creep that often follows premium minis. If you’re on a tight budget, you may want to wait for a sale or consider acquiring single pieces from a trade—or join a painting night where the shared joy makes the price feel more reasonable.
