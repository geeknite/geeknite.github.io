## H2: Exodus and the Notion of a Common/Uncommon Set

### H3: A quick primer on Exodus in the grand MTG timeline
Exodus arrived in a time when Magic was a little less stingy with its power levels and a lot more forgiving with its art directions. The set is part of the late-90s era that many collectors adore because you could trade with a friend who had a binder full of early set commons and still leave the table with a sense of accomplishment. It’s also a reminder that a set doesn’t have to be a powerhouse to be deeply loved; it can be a mosaic, a story, and a practical toolkit for daily drafting, or at least for spitting out witty banter during a Friday night session.

### H3: Why a Near-Complete Common/Uncommon collection matters
A Nearly Complete Common/Uncommon Set isn’t just about bragging rights. It’s about building a flexible, budget-friendly foundation for deck construction, color pairings, and trades that don’t trigger a mini-arthritis crisis when accountants come calling. Commons and uncommons often contain the true mold that shapes meta shifts in a format. They’re where you discover the “bones” of a set: removal, evasion, ramp, and hand-size management without needing to sell a kidney to find a single rare that might or might not fit your playgroup’s mood.

For the collector, a C/U set is a way to own a piece of the set’s storytelling without capriciously chasing foil versions of the same five cards. For the player, it’s a way to practice efficient deck-building, to experiment with synergy without risking debt. And for the nostalgic, it’s a time machine back to a moment in the hobby when the art on the card mattered almost as much as its rule text.

## H2: The Project: How We Defined Nearly Complete

### H3: What “Nearly Complete” means in practice
In this project, Nearly Complete means we’ve aimed to track every common and uncommon printed in Exodus that didn’t require hunting through booster boxes in a time machine. We’re including reprints where the card’s print run was the same common or uncommon, and where the card would realistically appear in a modern collector’s binder. The focus is practical: can you assemble a complete common/uncommon pool from Exodus with reasonable effort, fair pricing, and a coherent strategy for play and display?

### H3: Our scoring rubric for completion
We judged completion on four pillars:
- Availability: How easy is it to find the card in a fair price range, including bulk lots, local game stores, and reputable online sellers?
- Condition history: How often does the card appear in reasonable condition (near-mint to lightly played) given Exodus’s era and print quality?
- Playability: Do the common/uncommon cards support actually playable decks in casual formats or Commander color pairs relevant to Exodus-era themes?
- Aesthetic value: Does the card have art that ages well or features iconic imagery that makes it a joy to own?

We also tracked color-by-color performance to help you gauge where your budget goes the furthest. White and green staples often come with go-to ramp or removal; blue commons/uncommons tend to swing more toward trickery and response; black adds discard and removal; red leans into direct impact and some spicy burn; and green usually includes big creatures and mana acceleration. In Exodus, where the balance tilts toward multi-color potential and midrange battles, gathering a robust Common/Uncommon core can give you an experience that’s both nostalgic and practically playable.

## H2: The Common Pool: Highlights and Hidden Gems

A common pool should feel like a well-packed starter kit that also surprises you at the end of a long road trip. Here are some guided highlights that stand out in Exodus’s Common pool, along with honorable mentions that may have flown under the radar in modern times.

### H3: White highlights
White in Exodus often centers on access to efficient answers and small combat tricks. Highlights include a handful of anti-fatigue creatures, practical removal in the form of subtle combat tricks, and some lifegain options that age gracefully. If you’re building a White-based deck in this subset, you’ll value these commons for their reliability and ease of use in draft or casual siege scenarios.

### H3: Blue highlights
Blue commons and uncommons are where you’ll find tempo plays, counterplay, and some mind-bending synergies that age surprisingly well. In the Exodus flavor, expect cost-efficient bounce and filtering that help smooth over a chaotic midgame—the kind of stuff you’ll gladly draft when you’re out of options but not out of willpower.

### H3: Black highlights
Black often plays the role of the quiet assassin in a Common/Uncommon set. Removal that’s flexible, discard effects that hurt the opponent more than your own hand, and a few tricky life-swing cards are typical cornerstones. The common pool here rewards patient players who love the slow burn and late-game political plays that black frequently espouses.

### H3: Red highlights
Red is the punctuation mark—bold, direct, and sometimes reckless. In Exodus, red commons/uncommons contribute to quick tempo plays, efficient direct damage, and some surprise burn strategies that can derail a plan right as it’s starting to turn sour. They’re the kind of cards you draft when you want to feel the adrenaline of a last-minute finish while still having a sense of humor about it.

### H3: Green highlights
Green is the backbone of big plays and reliable ramp. In the Common/Uncommon pool, you’ll find efficient accelerators, sturdy mana bases, and creatures with broad, evergreen bodies that scale. If you’re chasing a stompy archetype, greens are your happy place, and their commons are usually the easiest to justify collecting in bulk.

### H3: Multicolor and cross-pollination
Don’t forget the color pairs. Exodus’s multicolored commons and uncommons often carry themes that reward creative deck-building: mana-fixing that doesn’t require precious artifacts, or supersized bodies with thresholds that aren’t as strict as their rares. The practical takeaway is to look for compatibility—the cards that allow you to run three colors with a reasonable mana base—and to chase those that support multiple archetypes rather than one narrow plan.

## H2: The Uncommons: Power, Flavor, and Collectability

Uncommons are where the set’s personality tends to show up in spades. They’re not the slam-dunk cards of the rare slots, but they define how your deck can function in the midgame and give you options that keep you in the game when your opponent’s stashes start to look suspiciously well-rounded.

### H3: Notable uncommons that define the set
In Exodus, the uncommons punch above their weight in both utility and flavor. They provide targeted removal with reasonable mana costs, reliable card draw or filtering options, and some of the most memorable effects that players brag about long after the game ends. They also carry some of the art direction that makes Exodus a nostalgically loved batch of cards—images that capture the set’s chaotic evolution and the vintage vibe of late-90s design. If you’re assembling a Nearly Complete set, these uncommons are the glue that keeps your colors from feeling disjointed and unpolished.

### H3: The art and flavor of Exodus uncommons
Flavor text and art quality in uncommons can be a strong argument for including a card in your personal collection, even if its in-game value has faded. You’ll want to look for pieces that evoke the set’s wild, travelogue feel—the kind of artwork that makes a table sigh with recognition when a card is drawn. These cards age gracefully with proper care, and their rarity makes them nice milestones on the road from “just a common” to “this is a deck-in-a-binder.”

## H2: Strategy for Completing the Set

### H3: Budgeting and strategic buying
Completing a Nearly Complete Common/Uncommon Exodus set should be accessible, but you still need a plan. A pragmatic approach is to buy in bulk where possible: mixed lots of Commons, Uncommons, and a few targeted single cards that you know are hard to find. Trade with local players to fill gaps, and keep a careful eye on seasonal pricing; sometimes a local store will clear out a back room full of exiled exotics for a fraction of what you’d pay online.

### H3: Distribution across color pairs and archetypes
A balanced C/U set supports a number of archetypes. You’ll want a core of commons that help you ramp and stabilize in green, with a stable set of removal and protection in white or blue. Black’s removal helps, while red’s direct damage and generic artifacts can serve to fill gaps in your draft or casual deck. Build with an eye toward your favorite playstyle—some people love the multi-color chaos of Exodus; others prefer the purity of a single-color veteran’s duty. Either way, a complete pool should enable a handful of viable starter decks that feel distinct but cohesive.

### H3: Trade etiquette and community building
The trade scene around Exodus is friendlier than some modern mega-sets. People enjoy sharing a story with a card they pulled once from a booster pack and then found a buyer two years later who would rather have the flavor text than the actual ability. If you’re new, start with a basis of fairness: trade value should reflect condition and demand. Remember to bring a sense of humor about the corners of the trade; the best deals often happen when both sides walk away smiling and slightly poorer in the short term, but richer in the long story.

## H2: Production Trivia and Market Realities

### H3: Print history and printing quirks
Exodus cards come from a time when printing processes were evolving rapidly. You may notice occasional misprints, font quirks, or color shifts that are cherished by some collectors as “character.” These quirks have a narrative value that adds to the charm of a Nearly Complete collection because every corner of the binder holds a tiny, tactile memory. The common and uncommon slots are particularly sensitive to these quirks, which can make a good-condition card feel a little rarer than you’d expect—at least in the eye of a seasoned collector.

### H3: The market today and how to ride the wave
Prices for Exodus commons and uncommons have a stubbornly non-linear profile. Some cards stay stubbornly affordable, while a handful can spike due to deck-building viability, specific archetype nostalgia, or a goofy but beloved art piece. The secret to a successful Nearly Complete run is balance: buy the likely cheap, abundant cards in bulk, and selectively invest in the rarest, most characterful uncommons with durable demand.

## H2: Practical Buying Guide

### H3: Where to look for Exodus Common/Uncommon cards
- Local game stores with throwback trading nights
- Online marketplaces that support bulk buys and graded sellers (always check return policies and grading scale for old sets)
- Trade events at your local community center or comic shop
- Bulk lots from reputable retailers; question any listing that looks too good to be true

### H3: How to verify condition and authenticity
- Request detailed photos of the card back and edges
- Look for mold or whitening on the corners; older cards are more susceptible to edge wear
- Ask for a grading possibility if you’re aiming for display quality, even for a common/uncommon set
- Trust your eyes: if the card’s look feels off, it probably is

### H3: A hypothetical shopping list checklist
- A core group of commons that support multiple strategies in each color
- The handful of uncommons that unlock flexible play and stronger board presence
- A couple of standout art pieces that you’re happy to display or gift
- A few “one-offs” you can trade away later without regret
