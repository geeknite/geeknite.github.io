## How the game is played in a nutshell
The core idea is faithful to Phase 10: complete ten phases by forming target dice patterns on your turn. The dice version makes this feel like a fast-paced, chaotic precision exercise. You roll dice across rounds, hold onto dice you want to keep, and re-roll the rest to try to meet the current phase on your score card. The included rule sheet defines the ten target configurations you must hit to advance through the phases. On your turn, you can try multiple times to assemble a valid configuration by selecting which dice to lock and which to re-roll. The first player to clear all ten phases wins the game, and the rest of the table becomes the jury of every questionable decision you made while chasing that final pattern.

### Turn structure and phase progression
- Each round, players take one turn at a time.
- On your turn, you roll all dice; you may reroll a subset up to two times (Yahtzee-style style).
- The current phase card on your sheet dictates which pattern you must achieve with your dice. This can include patterns like pairs, trips, runs, or more composite shapes, all translated for dice.
- If you meet the phase, you move to the next phase on your next turn and start pursuing that new target. If you fail to meet the phase after your rolls, you just end your turn and wait for the next round to try again, same phase stays in place.
- The scoring system on the card tracks progress and occasionally penalizes you for leaving a phase unfinished across rounds. The cards are surprisingly forgiving but keep you honest about your progress.

### The ten phases in spirit
The exact ten phase targets are printed on the score card. They align with the classic Phase 10 card game’s spirit, often featuring a mix of runs, sets, and straight-forward combinations. The dice version distills these into dice-friendly targets so you can compose them with rolling and re-rolling. Expect a mix of quick hits and stubborn phases that require a little luck and a lot of strategy with which dice to lock in early. The rules encourage planning, but they also reward bold, sometimes reckless, choices that only the dice can justify.

### Strategy tips for phases and rolling decisions
- Lock when a phase is within reach and you have a high probability to complete it with the remaining rolls. Do not chase a phantom pattern that will force you into a long, uncertain journey.
- When in doubt, keep a balance between short-term gains and long-term progress. A quick phase now can set you up for a stronger finish later, even if it means sacrificing a potential perfect hand in the moment.
- Use the two rerolls to fortify your most vulnerable phase. If you have one perfect pattern in view, reserve your rerolls for that configuration rather than chasing small improvements elsewhere.
- Watch your opponents; in a party setting, you can glean their level of risk tolerance by their dice discipline. If someone is all in on big swings, you might pivot to a safer, incremental approach to secure a lead.

## The need for a little context: Phase 10 dice vs the Phase 10 card game
If you are contemplating choosing between the Phase 10 card game and this dice adaptation, here is the gist. The card game is more about collecting hands and creating a long-term strategy across ten chapters. The dice version compresses that experience into shorter cycles and a more immediate sense of luck and on-table chaos. Which is better? It depends on the vibe you want:
- Card game vibes: methodical, planning-heavy, and a tad more forgiving of unlucky rolls since you can manage with cards.
- Dice game vibes: fast, loud, and prone to glorious misplays that lead to laughter and friendly banter. The dice version leans heavy on the social aspect and the quick mood shift after a bad roll.

If you want an even deeper dive into the differences, you can explore fan-made house rules and variants that mix elements from both formats.
