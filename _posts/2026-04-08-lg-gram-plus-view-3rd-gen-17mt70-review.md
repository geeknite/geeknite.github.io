In practice, you can use this monitor as a true plug‑and‑play extension for your Gram 17 laptop, turning the single-laptop workflow into a versatile dual‑display setup—whether you’re drafting code, editing video, or juggling multiple spreadsheets while your editor nags you from the Slack thread.

To kick things off, here’s what you typically get in the box: a 17-inch IPS panel in a slim chassis, a USB‑C/DP Alt Mode capable host cable, a power cable (sometimes optional if you rely on USB‑C PD), a smart stand that folds and tilts, and a quick start guide that you’ll probably skim at light speed while drinking coffee that’s gone cold by the time you reach the third bullet point. The SubMonitor is designed to slide into your Gram laptop’s workflow with minimal fuss and maximum “wow, I can actually work faster now” factor.

### Display quality and color accuracy

Let’s talk pixels and punch. The 17MT70 typically ships with a bright IPS panel that delivers solid color and wide viewing angles. In practice, you’ll notice good color coverage for workflow tasks—photoshop, Lightroom previews, and video editing scratch basic itches without requiring an ICC profile apocalypse. The brightness is comfortable for office use and outdoor scenarios (to a degree), with enough peak luminance to punch through a sunlit desk. It’s not a HDR monster, but for a portable monitor under a Gram, it isn’t pretending to be. The 60 Hz refresh rate is standard for this category and more than adequate for productivity and casual media consumption. Gaming on a portable monitor is possible, but don’t expect buttery high‑frame‑rate performance; this is a companion display, not a gaming beast.

Color accuracy is acceptable for most content creation workflows. If you’re doing color‑critical work, you’ll want to calibrate or rely on your main workstation for true color accuracy, but for reviewing footage on the go or doing design pass checks while traveling, the Gram +View does the job without turning your screen into a garish neon sign.

Two quick notes: you’ll want to consider a protective screen film if you’re using this as a daily carry device. Also, if you’re a photographer who needs precise color for proofs on the road, you’ll probably want to pair it with a color‑accurate external display or calibrate with a portable colorimeter before you trust it for production work.


### Connectivity, ports, and power diplomacy

The hardware diplomacy here is simple and pragmatic. The 17MT70 uses USB‑C as the main knight in shining armor, delivering both data and power when possible. If your Gram 17 supports USB‑C with DisplayPort Alt Mode and Power Delivery, you can run the monitor off the Gram without a separate power brick in many scenarios. If your laptop is a little more frugal with power, you might need to plug in a PD charger to keep both devices fed during heavy workloads.

Ports are typically straightforward: a USB‑C input for video/power, maybe a dedicated USB‑C PD input as a backup power, and sometimes a secondary USB‑C or HDMI input depending on configuration. The goal is to minimize cable spaghetti. In practice, you can run the monitor off a single USB‑C cable in most typical Gram setups, which is a dream for clean desk enthusiasts and people who don’t want to live in a tangle of cords.

If you’re using it with other devices, the 17MT70 still plays nicely, though you’ll have to adapt to different power profiles and cable types. The short version: USB‑C PD is your friend here; HDMI is less common on the latest Plus View iterations, but if you do need to connect to a non‑USB‑C host, you’ll want to verify if your firmware supports alternate input methods before you order.

So, who should actually buy this if you don’t own a Gram laptop already? Here are a few scenarios where the 17MT70 shines:

### Ergonomics and desk setup

Ergonomics matter, especially when you’re looking at a screen for hours on end. The Plus View’s stand and tilt range help set a comfortable viewing angle, but you’ll still want to position the monitor at eye level or use a small riser if your Gram sits low on your desk. The weight distribution is relatively balanced, so there’s less propensity for the screen to topple when you adjust angle. If you’re working on a train or in a coffee shop, a good portable stand or a tiny tripod can help you keep the monitor steady, even in crowd conditions.

Cable management is not exactly theater‑level magic, but it’s decent. If you’re particular about aesthetics, you’ll want to route cables behind the Gram and the monitor so everything feels tidy and purposeful rather than a spaghetti disaster that could cause you to lose your mind mid‑presentation.


### Software, drivers, and quirks

The LG ecosystem tends to be straightforward. There aren’t a ton of driver headaches with a simple USB‑C plug‑and‑play workflow, which is a refreshing change from some other manufacturers where you feel like you’re installing a second OS just to get a second screen to work.

In practice, you’ll likely use your primary OS’s display settings to arrange the desktop across the two screens. You can typically set the second monitor as an extended display or a mirror, depending on your needs. If you rely on power‑user window management tools, you’ll be happy to know that the extra real estate behaves like a standard external monitor without needing any special toggles.

If you’re curious about software features like color presets or on‑screen display navigation, those are typically accessible on the monitor itself. The on‑screen display (OSD) is straightforward: you’ll be able to tweak brightness, contrast, and color modes with a few button presses on the monitor’s bezel. It’s not the most feature‑rich OSD you’ve ever seen, but for a portable monitor in a travel kit, it’s perfectly adequate.


### Battery life and portability reality check

A portable monitor is not a battery powerhouse; it’s a display that piggybacks on your laptop’s power or a USB‑C charger. The Gram +View 17MT70’s portability shines when you’re moving between rooms, airplanes, and coffee shops, but don’t expect to squeeze hours of runtime out of it while pretending to be on a solar‑powered data center. If you’re planning a long remote session, pack a PD charger for the Gram or a power bank with PD output to keep both devices happy. In real‑world terms, you’ll get a solid eight to twelve hours of combined use if you’re moderate with brightness and keep the load reasonable. Crank brightness for a video appearance on a dim stage and you’ll be asking for a top‑up sooner rather than later.
