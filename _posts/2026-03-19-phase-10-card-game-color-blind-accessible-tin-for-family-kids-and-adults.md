## Phase 10 basics and the accessibility promise
Phase 10 is simple on the surface but deep in strategy. Players collect and complete ten distinct phases that include runs, sets, and a mix of card types. The standard deck uses color to separate houses of the deck, but color alone can be ambiguous in a crowded table or under odd lighting. The tin version doubles down on accessibility by adding symbols to each color, bold numeric cues, and a clear icon legend on the rule sheet. The result is a smoother onboarding experience for new players and fewer misreads when the pace of the game ramps up. The core rules remain familiar: you need to complete phases in order, with a hand of cards and a set of actions for your turns. The color blind friendly cues do not alter the core mechanics but they improve recognition during critical moments.

For readers new to Phase 10, a short overview is helpful. The game starts with all players being dealt a hand. On your turn you draw a card, then you discard a card. The goal is to complete a phase on your run, then move to the next one after finishing. The phases escalate from simple sets to more complex compositions. Players who complete phases advance, and the first to complete all ten phases wins. The color blind features of the tin do not change the rules but give the players more tools to identify which cards belong to which phase without relying solely on color.

The color blind cues are visible on the card faces as large symbols and bold numbers, with color names printed in high-contrast typography. The symbol shapes are distinct but not overwhelming, so the visual field remains uncluttered during the fast pace of play. The combination of symbol, number, and color helps players verify their options quickly. The design balances the needs of color blind players with the aesthetics of the deck so that it still looks like Phase 10 rather than a medical kit.
