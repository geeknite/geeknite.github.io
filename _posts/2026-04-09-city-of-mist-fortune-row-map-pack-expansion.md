What is Fortune Row? A quick primer for the curious and the time-strapped: it is a map pack expansion designed to give GMs new urban terrain to spread across a session, campaign arc, or a one-off session that suddenly decided to become an entire season. You get a collection of modular street tiles, alleyways, rooftops, and backlots, all with a moody, neon-drenched aesthetic that City of Mist fans know and love. The map pack is designed to slot into existing City of Mist tables without burning down the GM’s carefully curated city lore, while also offering fresh storytelling prompts that align with Fortune Row’s shady vibe.

The expansion is not just pretty pictures saved as print-and-play. It thoughtfully folds into the City of Mist core rules, presenting new location-based prompts, villainous leverage points, and narrative hooks that feel meaningful rather than tacked on. If you have ever wished the city layout could become a character in its own right, Fortune Row is your ticket to that dream—provided you’re ready to commit to some late-night planning sessions and a little map manipulation magic.

What's in the Box (And Why It Matters)

Fortune Row maps come in a few key flavors that matter in actual play:

- Modular street tiles that snap into a grid, letting you assemble a realistic block party or a claustrophobic back alley with ease.
- Rooftop and interior tiles that expand the chases and hideouts inside buildings, perfect for a midtown heist gone wrong or a rooftop chicane when the thunder rolls.
- A set of encounter cards that pair nicely with the new locations, providing quick prompts for PC threads, rolls, or a sudden shift in the mood of the scene.
- A small but tasty repository of lore breadcrumbs tied to Fortune Row: factions, icons, and micro-events you can drop into a session in under a minute.

The whole kit is compact and not overly precious about its use. It’s the kind of expansion that respects the GM’s time, not just the GM’s wallet. And because City of Mist is all about storytelling through myth underworlds, the pack leans into mood as a mechanic as much as into tiles and tokens.

The components feel sturdy. You’ll find a handful of tokens and stand-ins to represent NPCs and factions—enough to get started without needing a dedicated investment in a separate miniature collection. It’s a pragmatic approach that suits a game designed for narrative improvisation rather than tactical warfare.

How to Run Fortune Row in a Session: Practical Guidelines

Prepping with Purpose

- Skim the encounter prompts and select a few that align with your current arc. Don’t force all six prompts into one session; think of Fortune Row as a flavor supplement, something you can sprinkle when you want to amplify tension.
- Decide on a primary location as the anchor for the session. Use Fortune Row tiles to map out entrances, exits, and lines of sight for any chase or confrontation. If you’re into storytelling tempo, map out a three-beat sequence: arrival, reveal, pursuit.
- Create a couple of “soft” NPCs tied to Fortune Row’s micro-events so the players feel the city is alive and watching. Give them small, memorable quirks that can spark immediate roleplay.

During Play

- Leverage the new locations to force dynamic shifts in the scene. A rooftop chase doesn’t need 15 minutes of table time; with good momentum and clear map cues, you can compress that into a handful of dramatic rounds.
- Use the map to stage puzzles or social challenges that rely on environmental cues rather than brute force. Fortune Row’s tiles lend themselves to architecture-as-a-character moments, where the space itself shapes the outcome.
- Remember to tie the action to Icon-based outcomes. If a villain’s plan hinges on a particular location’s superiority, use a Risk/Resolve framework to escalate the stakes.

Post-Session Reflection

After your Fortune Row session, quick notes about what worked and what didn’t will make the next run smoother. The best campaigns learn from small, repeatable stumbles—like a tile that was too confusing or an encounter prompt that didn’t land. Jot down two or three adjustments you’d want to make next time and you’ll have a modular improvement pipeline for the entire arc.
