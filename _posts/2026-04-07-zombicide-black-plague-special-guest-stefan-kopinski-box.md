## The art and the minis: a closer look
### Kopinski style in miniature form
Kopinski is known for dramatic lighting, stark contrasts, and moody color palettes. Translating that to miniatures is a tricky business — you want the piece to pop on the table but you don’t want it to drown out the gameplay. In this box, the approach is balanced. The figures hold their own against the base game’s sculpts and in some cases may even outshine them in terms of painterly potential. The lighting is often drawn to the faces, which is both a blessing and a curse: you get expressive eyes that tell a story, but it also means you have a bit more work to do on the faces if you want to achieve that Kopinski look.

The color choices are the real talking point. You’ll see a lot of rich violets and midnight blues mixed with rusty oranges and splashes of green that feel almost surreal. It’s not chaos for chaos’ sake; it’s an intentional mood. If you were hoping for a literal “Kopinski copy” of his published pieces, you won’t get it. If you want to capture the spirit — the moody, cinematic vibe — you’ll find a wonderful starting palette here.

### From painter to tabletop hero
For hobbyists who enjoy painting, the Kopinski minis present a neat challenge. They’re not so fiddly that you will give up after ten minutes, but they do require a bit more attention to the faces and the shading on cloaks. Acrylic washes and careful edge highlighting can help you approximate the painterly effect without spending weeks on one figure. If you are a fan of speed painting, you can still achieve a striking look with a limited palette. The key is to focus on contrast, a common thread in Kopinski’s style: strong dark shadows and luminous highlights that make the figures pop under game lighting.

## Painting tips inspired by Stefan Kopinski
- Start with a strong undercoat. Kopinski’s pieces feel luminous; you’ll want a midtone base on the figures that allows the highlights to sing. Consider a cool midtone for blues and purples, followed by a warm highlight to bring out the skin tones and the fabrics.
- Emphasize contrast. Use a darker wash in the recesses to create dramatic shadows. Kopinski’s lighting often hits the figure from the upper left, so think about lighting your panels as though the mini is under a streetlamp.
- Layer subtly. Build up color in layers rather than slapping on paint in one go. The painterly result comes from a series of translucent coats, not a single thick slab of pigment.
- Don’t overdo the highlights on faces. The eyes are the emotional core; a tiny white dot and a delicate edge highlight can be enough. Keep it restrained to preserve the moody vibe.
- Practice on a spare figure. The kit includes extras; use them as test palms for blending and shading before you commit to the main figures. It saves you from the tragedy of a five hour paint job that ends up looking like a watercolor monstrosity.
