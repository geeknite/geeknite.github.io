## What is Quartermaster General and how does WW2 Preludio fit in?

Quartermaster General is a fast card driven wargame that emphasizes momentum, planning, and a dash of luck. In each match you and an opponent attempt to outpace each other by managing resources, deploying units, and triggering events that swing the tempo in your favor. The core mechanic uses a deck of event and unit cards to drive actions, with decisions that feel meaningful rather than rote. The speed is a feature, not a flaw; you get a complete, satisfying game in under an hour, depending on how much trash talk you pack into the room between turns.

WW2 Preludio introduces a WW2 flavour, with new unit types, events, and map features tailored to the theater. The idea is to provide a compact yet flavorful prelude to bigger campaigns, offering a self contained experience that is not a gimmick but a credible take on the era. Expect more attention to air power, supply chain nuance, and the tension of rapid redeployment under pressure. The scenario design leans into the interplay between movement, air interdiction, and logistics while preserving the crisp pacing that QG is known for. If you enjoy a sense of strategic obedience to a ticking clock, Preludio will feel like a well timed joke you are in on with your gaming partner.

A note on scale: this is not a megagame about thousands of troops or a ledger of strategic decisions spanning years. It is a tight, accessible title where a few well chosen moves can shift the balance decisively. That is the charm here: you can feel like a general without needing to memorize a small library of rules. For new players, the learning curve is pleasantly gentle thanks to the quick start guide that distills the key ideas into actionable steps. For veterans, there is enough depth to explore clever uses of events and timing windows that reward careful study and a good memory for what your opponent tends to bluff.

## Components and setup

The box contents are a sturdy mix of card stock and cardboard that communicates a sense of purpose at first glance. You get a map board with a clean layout that highlights key supply routes and chokepoints, a deck of event and unit cards that carry thematic flavor, a set of morale and supply tokens that clink satisfyingly when you shuffle, a compact rulebook with a quick start guide, and two player aids that act like safety rails during your first couple of games.

Setup is refreshingly quick. You pick your side, shuffle the main deck, lay out the map as directed, place initial supply and morale markers, and draw your opening hand. The quick start guide is a pragmatic booster rocket, letting you jump into the first turn in just a few minutes without having to argue about whether you should play card A before card B. The components feel durable enough for regular game night use, with a look that is modern yet respectful of the historical vibe.

### Map, tokens, and readability

The map is compact but expressive, focusing on corridors, rail lines, and critical crossroads rather than a grand theater map. This design choice helps keep the action readable at table distance and reduces time wasted flipping back and forth for reference. Tokens are bright and easily identifiable, and the iconography is intuitive after a short familiarization phase. You may still need a cheat sheet if you want to optimize every sequence from turn one, but you will also be able to enjoy a smooth experience without needing to consult the rulebook every five minutes.

### Cards and flow

The deck comprises a mix of unit, event, and reinforcement cards. Event cards add color and volatility, while unit cards anchor the tactical layer with clear stats and possible modifiers from terrain or events. The balance feels well tuned; you get a sprinkle of luck that spices up each game, but there is enough player choice to avoid feeling like the outcome is entirely in the cards. The balance between risk and reward is one of Preludio strongest suits, encouraging bold plays without punishing you for playing carefully when the moment calls for restraint.

## Mechanics and pacing

### Turn structure

A typical turn begins with a draw phase, where you refresh your hand back toward your starting tally. Then comes the planning phase, where you select cards to play and decide how to deploy your forces on the map. After that you move into the action portion, resolving movement, positioning, and combat via a mix of card effects and stat checks printed on the units themselves. The turn ends with reinforcement decisions and any cleanup needed for tokens on the board. The cadence is brisk, with enough friction to feel consequential but not enough to drag the table into analysis paralysis.

### Card driven actions

The core mechanic is the card driven engine. Each card offers a distinct action or swing, with some cards enabling rapid tempo shifts and others granting a longer term advantage. The challenge is to sequence cards in a way that compounds your momentum while denying your opponent the chance to do the same. The game invites experimentation; you will discover combinations that feel almost cinematic as you chain a maneuver into an air strike, then pivot to a critical supply line while your opponent scrambles to counter.

### Supply, morale, and victory

Supply tokens matter, as they do in most three dimensional warfare fantasies. They cap how effective your units can be and influence morale, which in turn affects how efficiently you can press an advantage. The victory conditions are a blend of territorial statements and objective markers that keep the table engaged without forcing a long, grindy slog. The satisfaction comes from aligning a sequence of decisions that leads to a decisive moment, a micro triumph that you and your opponent both feel in your bones.

## Player experience and strategy

### Early game tempo vs late game refinement

Early in the game you want to establish a robust but flexible supply chain, set up safe routes, and avoid over committing to a single push that might be undone by a cunning foe. The late game rewards disciplined play — allocating reinforcement units, timing a final offensive, and maintaining pressure on key supply routes to squeeze your opponent into a mistake. The balance between early initiative and late game consolidation is a theme that runs through Preludio and is one of its most satisfying elements.

### Practical strategies

A few notes that tend to help in most sessions:

- Control the chokepoints. The map is designed for movement friction, so press through corridors where you can force your opponent into disadvantageous engagements.
- Time your events. Event cards are your wildcards; use them to disrupt your opponent just enough to create a window for your big move.
- Reserve a tiny block of reinforcements. A well timed reinforcement can flip a marginal engagement into a win condition while your opponent is still paying attention to a different front.
- Watch morale as a resource. If morale slips too far, your units lose efficiency at a crucial moment. Keeping morale steady is often more important than a single big battle.

### Learning curve and table vibes

With the quick start guide and clear card texts, new players tend to pick up the core ideas quickly. The table vibe tends to be friendly but focused; there is room for banter and humor, but the game rewards thoughtful play and careful reading of what card your opponent could drop next. If you have played other QG titles, you will feel right at home with the rhythm and the general push of the system, while Preludio gives you a slightly different flavor that keeps things fresh.
