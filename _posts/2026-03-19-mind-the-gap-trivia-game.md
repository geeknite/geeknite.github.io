Before you dive into play, a quick orientation
Mind The Gap is intended to be a social game first and a trivia game second. The questions test knowledge, but the real challenge is to weave that knowledge into a short, entertaining bridge between generations. If you love games that spark conversation and you enjoy hearing personal stories from friends and family, Mind The Gap will feel right at home. If you prefer a straight quiz with a hard scoring rubric and little storytelling, you may prefer a more traditional title. The joy here is in the conversation as much as the points.

Gameplay and structure: setup, rounds, and the gap
Setup is fast. Shuffle the questions and turn over the first prompt. Each round, players or teams draw a set of answer cards and craft a short explanation that ties their chosen answer to the prompt. Then a judge (or a rotating team) scores the responses based on accuracy, imagination, and the strength of the bridge spoken aloud. The timer adds a sense of urgency but never punishes you for taking a moment to think about a memory that relates to the prompt. The real scoring nuance is not who is right but who can connect the dots in a way that feels credible and entertaining. If you can deliver a story that lands with your audience in the room, you earn more points than if you try to rely on obscure facts that only a few people remember.

The gap mechanic in practice
The gap is the name of the game and the core gimmick. The prompts are designed to elicit two things: a fact and a memory. Your job is to connect the fact to a memory in a way that makes sense to the other players. The synergy of facts and storytelling is where the points lie. The more you can recall a shared experience that resonates with the group while explaining why the fact matters in that context, the better your score. This makes Mind The Gap a little educational, a little social, and a lot entertaining. If the group is willing to lean into the premise, you will see a wave of laughter and surprising empathy as players realize that even a generation they thought was alien has something to teach them.
