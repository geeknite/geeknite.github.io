## Build quality and materials

Oak WTE is a marketing label that sounds like a wine pairing but is mostly a specific veneer treatment aimed at a warm, natural grain. In practice, the finish is premium but not fussy. It resists smudges and coffee spills better than a matte black surface, and the subtle grain patterns hide minor scuffs with the grace of a well-trained ninja. The veneer is applied over a sturdy core, typically plywood or MDF, which keeps the surface flat and prevents warping if you place a heavy monitor on an arm or slide a rolling chair across the surface with more exuberance than grace.

The frame is where the longevity shows up. Steel components with powder-coated finishes offer rigidity without the rattle you would expect from a six-person bench. The joints feel tight, and the alignment between the two pods is surprisingly accurate for something that ships in multiple boxes and requires some assembly wizardry. If you ever wondered what it feels like to peer inside a well-designed modular system, this is the moment you get a taste of it. Everything snaps into place with the confidence of a boss bumping a conference call from mute to unmute. It feels sturdy enough to withstand not just regular desk jockeys but also the occasional creative meltdown that involves gesturing wildly and accidentally slamming a palm onto the glass screen hub (we do not recommend it, but it happens).

The cable management infrastructure is the unsung hero here. A series of channels and clips keep power and data lines out of sight yet easily accessible. It helps reduce desk clutter and keeps the workspace looking professional rather than like the aftermath of a tech scavenger hunt in a coffee shop. And yes, there are enough grommets for everyone, so you can thread a reasonable number of USB-C cables, HDMI, and power rails without the portal of doom where cables go to die.

## Ergonomics and space planning

Ergonomics matter more when you have six people sharing a workspace. With three seats per side, you want to ensure each person has enough legroom, access to a comfortable chair, and a reasonably sized monitor distance. The Infinite unit addresses this with a combination of a thoughtfully shaped desk edge, adequate knee clearance, and a seating grid that avoids the dreaded crowding effect that plagues some open-plan desks.

The desk height is adjustable within a comfortable ergonomic range, which is a boon for teams with mixed preferences. If you have a software engineer who prefers a higher desk for a standing stretch during sprints and a designer who enjoys a lower height for precise drawing, this unit can accommodate both by allowing adjustable feet or a modular under-desk support system. The surface is smooth enough to glide a mouse across without the drag of a rough finish, yet textured enough to keep peripherals from sliding away during a particularly spirited planning session.

There is a subtle, often overlooked detail: cable routing. For six people, the number of cables can spiral out of control in a hurry. The design provides dedicated channels and stowage pockets that minimize tangling and keep the center aisle clear for easy movement. In a zoo-like office environment where people wander around with scanners and charity-collecting drones, it’s nice to have a desk that doesn’t look like a cable windstorm after a team hackathon. This is not to say it’s perfect; you still need a plan, modest discipline, and occasional cable management maintenance. But the baseline is solid, and the result is a more serene workspace.

## Maintenance and longevity

A desk this size relies on regular checks to keep it feeling premium. Here are a few practical tips:

- Periodically inspect the cable channels for dust and debris and clean using a soft cloth; avoid harsh chemicals that could degrade veneer over time.
- Check the monitor arm screws and anchor points every few months to maintain rigidity as you adjust screens or slide peripherals.
- Treat the oak veneer with a gentle, wood-safe cleaner to preserve the finish and prevent micro-scratches from evolving into noticeable wear.
- Keep the floor area clean and free of heavy items that could topple the long stretch and stress joints. The last thing you want is a sudden shift that makes your six-person orchestra of keyboards and mice clatter into a cymbal of chaos.

With sensible use and the occasional polish, this workstation should age gracefully like a fine chair, not a disposable gadget that loses its charm after a year of heavy usage.
