## What is Codenames XXL?
Codenames XXL is a variant of the beloved word game designed for bigger spaces, bigger voices, and bigger laughs. The core mechanics stay faithful to Vlaada Chvátil’s design: two teams, a spymaster for each side, a set of word cards laid out in a grid, and a single clue that must connect multiple target words while avoiding the assassin. The XXL edition simply makes visibility and social presence bigger than life. You’ll see oversized word cards laid out on a hefty table or even on the floor, a large key card that spells out blue words, red words, innocent words, and the dreaded assassin, plus tokens that mark which team is which.

If you’ve got a party of eight, ten, or more, XXL feels like a proper expansion even when you’re not counting the words. It’s not just a visual upgrade; it changes the dynamic in subtle, delightful ways. The same tension of “one clue, multiple words” remains, but with bigger stakes and bigger smiles when a lousy clue somehow hits four of your words in a single guess.

### Unboxing and components
- A deck of oversized word cards. The words are the same, but the font is bold enough to be readable from across the room, which means you can actually keep your eyes on the clues rather than squinting at the board from your couch.
- A large grid key card. This is the central map that tells which words belong to red, which belong to blue, which ones are neutral, and which one is the assassin. In XXL, this key is bigger too, making it easier to explain to new players and to refer back to during heated strategy debates.
- Red and blue team tokens. In the original, you use colored agents; in XXL you get oversized markers that wobble on the table like tiny flags at a parade.

### Setup and rules (quick refresher)
- Lay out twenty-five word cards in a 5x5 grid. The XXL edition uses bigger cards, but the grid remains a familiar sea of words you’ll either recognize or learn to respect.
- The red and blue spymasters pick their teams, or you can rotate per round if you’re feeling merciful or mischievous.
- The clue-giver (the spymaster) selects one clue and a number, which is supposed to indicate how many words on the board relate to that clue. The clue must be a single word or a short phrase; multi-word clues can work but risk confusion, which in XXL sometimes becomes comedy gold.
- The guesser(s) on that team continue guessing until they either hit the limit of the clue (the number) or decide to stop early. If they guess a word that belongs to their team, that word is covered with their color, and the team continues. If they hit the assassin, the game ends immediately and the other team wins.
- Play alternates between teams, with the aim of turning all your words red or blue before the other team does. XXL doesn’t change the win condition; it just makes the journey to victory a little more dramatic.

## The big twist: XXL features
- A bigger board, as the name suggests. The advantage here is not just about visibility; it’s about social presence. People at the far end of the room can still feel like they’re part of the action rather than mere spectators while the clue-giver hammers out a mind-bending clue.
- Oversized word cards facilitate group play, floor play, and that classic post-game photo where the whole team is standing behind their pile of words like a football huddle.
- A more generous seat at the table for the guessers. The bigger cards mean you’re less likely to accidentally skim a rival word while someone is squinting and whispering a code phrase.
- The tactical things also change: you can offer more information in a single clue, but XXL encourages players to use that information more cleverly because the consequences of a wrong guess are bigger and more visible on the big board.
- The assassin’s presence feels more—dramatically spooky. In practical terms, you’ll still dread hitting it, but you’ll have more confidence that you won’t forget the penalty if you overstep.

### Clue writing, tips, and strategy
- With a larger field, the number aspect matters more. With a bigger board, a single clue can be more expansive; you’ll hear players propose clues that would be risky in a standard game but feel almost reasonable when you can toast to them with a group of ten who are leaning into the moment.
- The best clues in XXL are those that bridge multiple islands of words without turning into a Rorschach test of pop culture references. A well-chosen clue that ties three or four words together can net a clean sweep, while an overreaching clue can collapse the entire team’s plan in one poor guess.
- Numbers are your friend, but not to the point of mechanical thinking. The “one word with a number” format is the spine of the game, but the XXL edition makes it easier for players to overthink. You’ll want to consider how confident your team feels about each word—sometimes a smaller number, combined with a precise angle, yields more consistent hits.
- Reading the room matters more than ever. In XXL you’ll see reactions from across the room, the body language of a player who’s about to guess incorrectly, and the subtle hints of a teammate who wants to help but knows the risk of pointing something out directly.

If you’re the kind of person who thrives on performance, XXL is your playground. The best remedy is to set expectations: this is a party game, not a chess match; the goal is to have fun, not to assemble a perfect linguistic chain.

It’s not unusual to see games push toward an hour with a stubborn stalemate or two. If you’re planning a game night, schedule XXL toward the middle or end, after people have warmed up with lighter games.
- The pacing is a creative force. You’ll end up with moments where everyone leans in to interpret the clue, and then a cascade of guesses that feel like a rollercoaster of “oh no” and “aha!” The big board gives everyone something to react to, and that reaction is what makes the experience memorable.

### A few quick tips to maximize your XXL nights
- Designate one “clue coach” for each team to help keep the pace. It’s easier on new players and avoids debates that derail a round.
- Use a timer generously. XXL invites more discussion, and a timer helps the table avoid creeping into an hour-long stalemate.
- Establish a few house rules for the first few sessions. For example, limit the number of high-risk clues in a row or allow a “safety pass” if a clue is too spicy to justify the cost of a misstep.
- Mount the board on a low table or across a few chairs so that players can gather around it easily. The more people who can view the grid, the more engaged everyone will be.
- Encourage players to celebrate near-misses as well as successes. The community energy is what makes XXL worth it.
