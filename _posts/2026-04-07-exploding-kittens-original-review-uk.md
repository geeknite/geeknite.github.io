## What is Exploding Kittens, and why did it become a phenomenon?

Exploding Kittens is a fast-paced, kitty-laden card game where players take turns drawing cards and trying to avoid drawing an Exploding Kitten card. If you draw one, you’re out—unless you can play a Defuse card to stop the kaboom and save your whiskers for another round. The core trick is simple: you don’t want to pull the bomb; you want to outlast your friends by playing a mix of weaponized cats, defense maneuvers, and cheeky sabotage. The formula is familiar in some ways: take turns, draw cards, try not to explode; add a social layer of taunts, bluffing, and occasional bribery for defusal, and you have a recipe that scales from a chaotic kitchen table to a livelier party experience.

The game was designed around the idea of quick rounds, short attention spans, and a sense of ridiculous hilarity. It’s one of those games where the deck does most of the talking, while your friends do the screaming and laughing. The card art is intentionally over the top—the titular Exploding Kitten card is both terrifying and irresistibly adorable, which is precisely the vibe you want when you’re trying to coax a group of adults and kids into a chaotic trust exercise where no one knows what card will be played next.

## How to play: setup and basic flow

### Setup
- Each player gets an initial hand of Defuse cards (usually one or two, depending on house rules).
- The Exploding Kitten cards are shuffled into the draw deck, typically one fewer than the number of players, so someone can survive the round until the end.
- The remaining cards fill the draw pile.
- Decide who goes first; this can be done by a quick show of hands, a coin toss, or a totally fair excuse that you invented on the spot.

### The flow of a round
A round is a tense drone of draw, dodge, and dares. On your turn, you draw a card from the draw pile. If it’s an Exploding Kitten, you must reveal it and hope you have a Defuse card to survive. If you draw an Exploding Kitten and don’t have a Defuse, you’re out; the round continues with the surviving players until one kitten-free soul remains.

The heart of the game comes from the action cards. See the Future lets you peer at the top few cards of the deck, giving you glimpses of danger or purity. See the Future can also influence psychological warfare—if you declare you see something and you’re wrong, the room’s laughter might drown out your own ego. Attack cards can force the next player to take multiple turns; Skip cards allow you to dodge the risk parade; Shuffle cards scramble the deck to keep everyone guessing what’s next. Steal cards add a light-handed heist vibe to the proceedings, letting you swipe a card from another player’s hand—payloads of defuse or something more devastating depending on your style.

The UK edition preserves all of this but adds a gentle, friendly tweak for parent-friendly nights: fewer inside jokes that require an encyclopedic knowledge of meme culture. The humor remains bold and a touch ridiculous, which is exactly what a family-friendly game night needs.

### A quick round example
Imagine four players: A, B, C, and you. You’ve drawn your initial hand; someone is already sweating because you’ve declared you’re “totally calm” while you secretly are not. You draw a card and hope it’s not Exploding Kitten. It’s a See the Future, so you peek at the top three cards. You see something that looks risky, a couple of blanks, and a card that might let you reshuffle. You decide to play the See the Future, then use a Skip to dodge a looming threat, buying you another calm moment before the inevitable chaos resumes. Your opponent juggles a couple of Defuse cards, but the table is loud with the kind of banter only a family can deliver at 9 PM. The round continues, a few more laughs, a few more gasps, and then—boom—someone pulls the wrong card and the table erupts in delighted mock horror. And so it goes, until only one player remains who has yet to explode. That is gaming bliss, UK edition branch style.
