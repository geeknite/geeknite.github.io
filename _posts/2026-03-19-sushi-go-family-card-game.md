## How to play in broad strokes

The core mechanic remains delightfully simple: on your turn you pick one card from your hand to add to your personal display and pass the rest to the next player. You do this in clockwise order until all cards have been chosen. After a few rounds you tally up points based on the set collections you have assembled. In the family edition, the scoring rules are streamlined to emphasize easy calculation and quick feedback, so even players who are still learning their numbers can track who is leading without turning the table into a calculus class.

### Turn structure and flow

Each round starts with a hand dealt to every player. You look at your cards, select one to play face down, and pass the remaining cards to your left. Everyone reveals their chosen card in a simultaneous-ish reveal, which adds a tiny bit of suspense and sometimes a lot of playful chaos as someone tries to bluff about their intentions.” The passing continues until all cards in the hand are exhausted. Then you score for the round and all players start fresh with a new hand. The number of rounds is usually short, designed to keep the action moving and ensure that kids stay engaged without losing interest mid game.

The family edition may include a simplified set of round rules and a kid friendly timer that gives you a quick sense of when the round ends. For a busy family, that is a handy feature because it creates a predictable rhythm that can be cued with a cheerful countdown song rather than a pocket watch and a stern look.

### Scoring and end game

Scoring in Sushi Go family edition is designed to be intuitive. Common scoring categories include:

- Maki majority: the player with the most maki stacks at the end of a round earns a big bonus
- Sets of sashimi: typically sets grant points for collecting a complete trio
- Dumplings: larger sets yield higher rewards, with a gentle curve that rewards patience but not obsession
- Nigiri cards: straightforward values that can swing the score but are easy to grasp for younger players

In practice this means you can explain scoring in minutes and have kids track a few numbers on a scrap piece of paper. The end of game scoring is also forgiving; if someone miscounts, there is often a shared laugh and a quick correction, maintaining the friendly vibe rather than turning into a tense scoreboard saga.
