## What is Saboteur and why this anniversary edition matters
Saboteur is a hidden-role, team-based bluff game set in a dwarf mine. Players are miners seeking gold, but among them are saboteurs whose goal is to derail the route and keep the gold out of reach. The game uses a simple deck-building mechanic of path cards to lay out a tunnel from the entrance to a hidden gold mine. Each round ends when a team reaches the required treasure or when the saboteurs sabotage enough. The tension comes from the fact that you rarely know who is on your side, or if there is even a side to begin with.

The core appeal of Saboteur lies in the interplay between going for the gold and reading your opponents. You become a cheap, cheerful detective in a cave, reading tells, negotiating with others, and using tools to shape the tunnel while also trying not to reveal your own identity. It’s a game about social deduction that doesn’t demand a superhuman poker face; it rewards playful speculation, snarky banter, and the occasional theatrical accusation that lingers in the air long after the card is played.

The Anniversary Edition leans into the things players already loved while updating the aesthetic and improving durability. In a world where many card games rely on pretty fronts to hook players, Saboteur offers a practical reason to invest in a nicer edition: components that tolerate the rough handling of family game night, while still feeling elegant enough for a coffee-table moment. Expect sturdier card stock, crisper art, and a cohesive design language that makes the board, tokens, and cards feel like a single, cohesive universe rather than a patchwork of indie-art vibes.

## How the rounds flow: from entrance to treasure
Saboteur is built around a simple loop that feels satisfying in its momentum. Here is how a typical round unfolds in the Anniversary Edition, without getting lost in the weeds:

1) Setup: The dealer distributes role cards; prospectors look happy with their fate, saboteurs smile with a little more menace. The tunnel begins at the entrance; the first few path cards land on the table to set the initial course.
2) The dig begins: Players take turns placing a path card to extend the tunnel and reveal or block progress towards the gold. Some turns involve playing a tool or a map card to accelerate or misdirect the route.
3) Bluffing and conversation: The social element takes over as players try to deduce who is who while masking their own identity. The rule of thumb: never reveal everything on turn one; let suspicion accumulate like coal dust in your beard.
4) The midgame churn: As more cards come into play, you begin to see the pattern of who is pushing for gold and who is trying to derail everyone else. A saboteur might front-load a bad route in the hope of a bigger payoff later; a prospector might press ahead with a bold, transparent plan that invites discussion and debate.
5) The climax: A reveal or a deadline triggers the end of the round. If the proctors reach the treasure or a map card exposes the correct chain to the gold, the round ends with a flourish. If the saboteurs succeed in blocking, they revel in their cunning for a moment before the group erupts in friendly banter about who walked into the mine with the wrong hat.
6) Scoring and reset: Points (or the round’s equivalent) are tallied, the roles are shuffled, and the next round begins with fresh nerves and new opportunities.

What makes the flow feel special is the way information is always a moving target. You do not need perfect information to enjoy Saboteur; in fact, you have to enjoy the process of guesswork and discussion more than you enjoy certainty. The end result is a game that feels like a tense boardroom meeting in a dwarven mine, with more jokes and less workplace drama.

## Roles and bluffing: prospector vs saboteur
A core pillar of Saboteur is the dramatic tension between two archetypes: the prospector, who wants to carve a straight path to the gold, and the saboteur, who wants to twist that path into a detour. The Anniversary Edition does not reinvent these roles; it refines their presentation and ensures the balance remains satisfying across a wider player count.

### The prospector: balanced and bold
- Goals: Reach the gold by laying a valid tunnel path from the entrance to the treasure. You want to guide your team toward an efficient route while preventing the saboteurs from accidentally guiding you into a dead end.
- Strengths: Clear objectives, strong social dynamics, and opportunities to press forward with confident road-building. A good prospector can steer conversations subtly, nudging teammates to support a particular route while discarding obvious tells.
- Weaknesses: Overconfidence can lead to misplays; a strong saboteur will flood the path with deceptive cards, making your team doubt your choices.
- Strategy tips: Use maps to reveal promising paths and coordinate with your allies when possible. When uncertain about an ally's intentions, rely on small, consistent moves rather than flashy plays. The goal is steady progress rather than a heroic single turn that does all the work.

### The saboteur: misdirection and mayhem
- Goals: Stop the group from reaching the treasure, or at least ensure the path does not reach the gold quickly. The saboteur thrives on uncertainty, misdirection, and the occasional dramatic lie.
- Strengths: The ability to scramble the tunnel layout, force discussions, and exploit the group's momentum for humor and chaos. You can often win by simply keeping everyone off balance long enough to ruin the route.
- Weaknesses: If you reveal your allegiance too early or become the focus of too much scrutiny, the round might end with you exposed and demoted to a role with a smaller payoff.
- Strategy tips: Balance deception with careful timing. A well-placed map later in the tunnel can cause the prospector to double down on a risky route. Use tools to reframe the discussion and plants seeds of doubt about the route’s validity. And remember, the best saboteur sometimes pretends to be the most helpful teammate—until the last moment when the mine collapses into laughter and relief.

The symmetrical tension between these roles is what gives Saboteur its staying power. In the Anniversary Edition, those tensions are sharpened by cleaner iconography, quicker card recognition, and a slightly cleaner rulebook that reduces the friction around what counts as a legal path. The game remains approachable for newcomers and remains a satisfying test for veteran players who want to test their bluffing chops without wading through a thousand pages of clarifications.

## Strategy notes by player count and table feel
Here are a few quick notes you can weave into your house rules or your standard strategy:

- Three players: The dynamics are intimate and spicy. Lots of reading of tells, but there is less room for deliberate misdirection; you’ll want to rely on quick routes and fast conclusions. The game can feel brisk and punchy, which is perfect for a short game night.
- Four to five players: The balance of two saboteurs against several prospector players can create a lively chess match of deception. The group has time to form alliances, pretend to be supportive, and then pivot dramatically when a map flips at a key moment.
- Six to eight players: Expect bigger conversations, more dramatic accusations, and longer rounds. You get more misdirection and more opportunities for players to reveal strategic preferences for certain routes or map cards. We suggest using a timer to keep the speed up if the group enjoys lengthy debates.

There are also practical add-ons you can borrow if you want to customize Saboteur for your table:
- Quick map variant: Limit the number of map cards available per round to accelerate decision-making.
- Team mode: Pair players into daylight-to-night teams where two players collaborate more openly but must still avoid revealing their actual roles to the other teams.
- House rules for re-shuffle: After a set number of rounds, shuffle all discarded cards and reintroduce a few surprises to keep the game feeling fresh.
