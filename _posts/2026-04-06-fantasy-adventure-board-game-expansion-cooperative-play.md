## What’s New: The Core Skeleton of Co-op Joy

### New Roles, New Tricks
The expansion introduces a slate of new heroic roles tailored for team play. Some roles are traditional archetypes—tank, healer, mage—while others tilt into more unconventional duties like “Harbinger of Laughter” who boosts morale and occasionally defuses traps with a witty pun. Each role comes with a unique action tree that encourages players to plan together rather than jockey for the spotlight. The board is peppered with team-based objectives that reward players for coordinating movements, not merely rolling higher numbers.

New abilities emphasize synergy. You’ll see: coordinated attack chains, protect-and-pass mechanisms, and shared resource pools that force players to discuss whether a pivotal spell should be cast now or saved for a Nope-This-Is-Important moment later. In practice, this means fewer “go rogue, solo win” turns and more “you weave a shield wall while I summon a dragon’s breath” moments.

### Bosses That Test Your Duo, Trio, or Quartet Dynamic
The expansion adds a few mid-to-late-game boss encounters designed for 2–4 players. Boss AI is a piece of art—deliberately punishing but fair, with meaningful telegraphs that require players to read the room (and the board) rather than simply hoping for a perfect dice roll. Bosses often shift between phases, each phase pushing players to reallocate roles: suddenly the healer is needed to stabilize the frontline, and a mage might need to step into a support role to keep the team alive during a brutal cliff-face attack. It’s a test of planning under pressure, but the art and music cues do a surprisingly good job of selling the tension.

### A Modular, Mutating Board
The board expansion uses modular tiles that can be mixed and matched to create a variety of layouts. It’s not just cosmetic: certain tiles alter the tempo of encounters, shifting from claustrophobic corridors to sprawling ruins that demand different movement strategies. The designers clearly wanted to avoid “one map fits all” syndrome, and they succeeded by giving each layout a distinct rhythm that can favor different team compositions.

### Shared Victory Conditions and Personal Stakes
In many co-op games, players care about the win condition, but the new expansion adds a twist: some scenarios include personal quests for characters, with outcomes that ripple across the team. Completing a personal goal can unlock temporary bonuses or change the next encounter’s objective. This keeps people invested even if their character’s survival seems tenuous, sparking a lot of table chatter about whether someone “should” take a hit to complete a personal milestone. It’s a clever push-pull that rewards cooperation without turning into a charity project.

## How It Plays: The Flow of a Session

### Setup and Learning Curve
Expect a bit more setup time than the base game, particularly if you’re crafting a multi-scenario campaign. The expansion includes setup cards for different maps and a few new rule sheets. The learning curve isn’t a wall, but it’s a gentle incline. The game offers a quick-start mode that drops you into a pre-built scenario with a streamlined rule set if you want to test the waters before committing full mental calories to a longer session.

### The Turn Structure: A Dance of Movement, Actions, and Morale
Each player still gets a standard action economy, but the cooperative twist encourages you to bench-test your turns with teammates. You might see a sequence like: Player A sets up an ally buff, Player B positions for an optimal multi-hero strike, Player C handles resource management or trap disarmament, and Player D coordinates an end-of-turn morale boost that can swing the next round’s odds. It sounds heavy on paper, but in practice the rhythm feels natural once you’re in the groove. The payoff is a sense of synergy that makes you feel clever—like you actually created a plan that other humans could follow.

### Scenarios and Campaign Progression
This expansion leans into a campaign mode where decisions carry consequences across adventures. You’ll be making calls about which allies to recruit, which artifacts to pursue, and whether to pursue a morally ambiguous path that could unlock powerful tools—or unleash a new threat. The narrative threads weave together in a satisfying way, especially if you enjoy a sense of shared destiny rather than a self-contained sprint through a dungeon.

### Pacing and Difficulty
Pacing is variable, depending on your group size and how aggressively you pursue objectives. The co-op design tends to lengthen the late-game complexity, which is great for groups that relish epic finales and the adrenaline rush of near-failures turned into glorious saves. If you prefer shorter, punchier sessions, you can dial down the number of concurrent threats or set a “friendly-fire” no-go rule for the first couple of games to let everyone acclimate. The difficulty curve respects players’ time while still delivering a satisfying challenge for veteran table-talkers.

### Character Progression and Personal Stakes
As you play, your heroes gain capabilities that reflect your team’s growing bond. Progression is both modular and meaningful: you’re not just collecting XP for the sake of power; you’re shaping your party’s identity. There are upgrade paths that feel distinct across roles, which helps avoid the trap of a party that all feels the same at higher levels. It’s not perfect—some upgrades can feel a touch niche—but the overall arc lands with a satisfying sense of momentum.

## Balance and Longevity: Can It Scale with Your Table?

### Player Count Variability
The expansion is designed for 2–4 players, with some scenarios scaling well to a full table. In our playtests, two players felt brisk and tense, with a nice emphasis on role specialization. Three players produced lively interplays of strategy and counterplay, while four players sometimes risked a little crunch due to the number of simultaneous objectives. The designers attempted to balance with scaling rules, but as with any cooperative title, the more cooks you have in the kitchen, the longer it takes to reach consensus. The good news: there’s a built-in, optional “fast mode” for groups that want to speed through encounters without sacrificing the storytelling stake.

### Replayability
Replayability is strong. With multiple scenarios, several boss variants, and alternative paths in the campaign, you’re unlikely to feel like you’re re-scraping the same map over and over. Randomized encounter cards keep the decisions feeling fresh, and the personal quests ensure that even repeated playthroughs can reveal new narrative threads and strategic combinations. If you’re into long-term campaigns that feel consequential, this expansion shines in that space.

### Balance Concerns and Ergonomics
One potential friction point is that some roles can feel more powerful when combined with particular others, which is a classic pitfall in co-op design. The good news is that the expansion includes a “balanced start” variant and several optional house rules you can adopt to dampen any unevenness. The result is a game that remains accessible for newcomers but carries enough nuance to reward experienced players who enjoy theorycrafting and optimization.

## Strategy: How to Make the Most of Cooperative Play

- Communicate early and often: The expansion rewards planning, but it punishes hesitation. At the table, you’ll want a quick check-in ritual: “What’s the objective on turn one? Which ally can we recruit now?” The more you align your intentions, the smoother the session will feel.
- Define roles quickly: In a 2-player game, assign a primary and secondary function for each hero. In larger groups, establish a lead for each objective and a communicator who coordinates everyone’s actions. It’s less about who has the best numbers and more about who can anticipate what the team needs next.
- Manage morale and resources as a team: The morale mechanic isn’t just flavor; it actually shifts how risks are calculated. Read the board’s mood, and don’t be afraid to delay a kill shot if it secures a longer-term advantage. Sometimes the smart move is to stabilize rather than shove for glory.
- Pace your escalation: Boss fights have phased complexity. Don’t sprint into the second phase with half your team’s resources spent. Save a few tricks for late-game desperation and you’ll thank yourself when the last boss crash-lands into a dramatic finale.

### Quick Setup Tips for New Groups
- Divide roles early, print your plan, and put your plan on the table for everyone to reference.
- Run through the quickstart scenario once before tackling the campaign. It’s worth the time: you’ll learn the rhythm without feeling overwhelmed.
- Use the campaign map’s neutral zones to practice coordination. It’s a forgiving sandbox where you can test which combos feel natural.
