## Introduction

Gloom, the card game about making your family as miserable as humanly possible, returns in a gleefully coffin-shaped package with Keith Baker at the helm. The second edition is not just a fresh coat of black ink on translucent cards; it is a refined celebration of dark humor, dramatic misfortune, and the kind of storytelling that makes you question your own life choices as you giggle inappropriately at a cartoon graveyard.

If you pick up this brand new sealed copy, you are signing up for a 30 to 60 minute ride through a cheerful mausoleum where the lightning bugs double as mood tamers and the plastic transparencies do more stage lighting than a low-budget high school play. This review will walk you through what changed, what stayed delightfully bizarre, and whether this sealed treasure is worth cracking open in the first place.

## What is Gloom Second Edition?

Gloom was originally designed by Keith Baker as a push your luck, storytelling, card-drafting roller coaster where players pit doomed families against each other. The core gimmick is delightfully simple: you have a hand of transparent, tinted cards. You lay them on top of family members to accumulate misfortunes while trying to make your opponents’ characters as miserable as possible. The more misfortune you pile on, the higher your score—until someone finally ends a round with the least morbid family score, which, in the world of gloom, translates to you losing because you care too much about your relatives.

The second edition updates the rules for clarity, improves card stock durability (translucent cards can be unforgiving to the fingernail if you hate your own handwriting, which is a vibe), and polishes the overall presentation. It keeps the same core loop: play Story cards to twist the plot, accumulate negative statuses on your characters, and then ruthlessly reveal the scores when the round ends. The result is a game that feels like a Victorian gothic novel written by someone who binge-watched Monty Python and then forgot to eat their turnips.

## In the Box: Brand New Sealed Joy

Opening a brand new sealed Gloom 2e is a micro-adventure in itself. ### Components and Card Stock

The heart of Gloom Second Edition is its card stock. The translucent gloom cards have a frosted, almost frosty look that catches the light in a way that makes your setup feel theatrical. The colors are slightly muted compared to some modern card games, which is perfect for that “dimly lit parlor at midnight” aesthetic. The fact that you can layer these cards on top of each other to create composite stories is a design choice that is unique to Gloom and still feels fresh after multiple replays.

There are a few different types of cards in the mix:

- Gloom cards: the translucent characters that form the backbone of your doom arc. They sit within a light, airy hierarchy of happiness and misfortune, like a family tree you’d rather not examine too closely.
- Story cards: these are the plot twists, disasters, and comical curses that drive the drama. They are often a little dark, a little silly, and always entertaining to reveal.
- Condition and modifier cards: these tiny helpers adjust the scoring or the effects of other cards. They keep the math from becoming too grim, which is nice for the sanity of players who forgot to bring their calculator to the parlor.
- Score tokens: heavy enough to feel real, light enough that you won’t fear them sinking into the couch cushions.

Storage is straightforward. The box is designed to keep the translucent cards from warping and the opaque cards from jostling. If you are the kind of person who loves organized chaos, you will appreciate the tucked-away slots and color-coded stacks. If you are the kind of person who loves chaos with a side of coffee, well, you will also love it because you can pretend the box is a haunted cabinet that holds your emotional baggage too.

### Card Art and Theme

Keith Baker’s art for Gloom has always leaned into a playful macabre. In 2e, the art feels a touch crisper and more cohesive than the original run, but the sense of quirky gloom remains intact. You’ll see expressive faces, exaggerated features, and little detail flourishes on each character that suggest a full, ridiculous backstory even before you start telling it. The tone hits a sweet spot: it’s dark and whimsical at the same time, which is exactly the mood you want when you are stacking cards to obliterate someone else’s family with a smile and a sigh.

## How to Play in 60 Seconds (Overview)

If you want the short version before we go deeper, here it is: each player controls a family, cards are stacked to add misfortune and plot twists, and you want your family to be the most miserable by the time the round ends. Then you reveal scores and the person with the lowest score wins the game as the most tragically lucky survivor of the gloom.

### Setup

- Each player chooses a family to maneuver. The families come with their own starting happiness level, which is typically high and safe. You want to take that happiness and gradually crush it using Story cards and pileups of gloom.
- Shuffle Story cards and place the deck where all players can reach it.
- Deal a starting hand of Gloom cards. The exact number can vary depending on the number of players, but the vibe is: more players means more opportunities to mess with someone else’s family, and less personal mercy.
- Each player also takes a few score cards. You will reveal your scores at the end of the round to measure how tragic your family’s life has become.

### Turn Structure

On your turn, you typically do three things:

1) Draw a Story card (or two, depending on the variant you are playing).
2) Play one or more Gloom cards to your own family or to an opponent’s family (the goal is to apply misfortune, not to be kind). You can layer effects to build a narrative of doom.
3) Discard a card or two as required by the rules. The trick is to keep your own family’s happiness lower than your opponents while masking your own catastrophes with a flourish of humor.

### Gloom Stacking and Storytelling

The core mechanic is stacking gloom on top of characters. Each card represents a calamity or a misfortune that, when layered, creates a larger story. You can tell a minutely dramatic tale or a grand, sweeping epic of misfortune. The game rewards witty narration and clever card placement. It’s not just about numbers; it’s about the drama you create in real time as you reveal the effects.

### End Condition

The round ends when all players have taken a pre-determined number of turns. Then you reveal the final scores. The twist is: you want the lowest score. The catch is that the scoring is not just numerical; it’s narrative. Your cards tell a hidden story about how unlucky your family has been. The humor comes from the fact that the more misery you pin on others, the more you may be elevating your own dramatic arc to a tragic masterpiece. It’s a balancing act of sympathy and saboteur energy, and it is glorious to watch unfold.

## Why Gloom 2nd Edition Stands Out

There are plenty of card games that promise unique mechanisms. Gloom's second edition survives because it embraces its own gimmick with gusto and then cuts the fluff that wore out the original's charm. Here are a few reasons why this edition shines.

### Humor Meets Tragedy in a Perfect Brew

The humor in Gloom is not slapstick; it’s the wry, pseudo-Gothic humor that comes from watching a family of adorable, sad creatures endure the worst life has to offer, all while you joke about it with your friends. The Story cards provide the narrative sparks, and the translucent gloom cards ensure that the misery looks elegant and cinematic rather than clumsy and chaotic. The blend is intoxicating in the way a dark comedy ought to be.

### Accessibility Without Dizzling out Complexity

Gloom is easy to pick up: you learn the basics in a few minutes, and after one or two rounds you’re weaving elaborate misfortunes with surprisingly little cognitive overhead. The second edition clarifies rules that used to obscure a few players, which means you can get to the good storytelling faster. It’s a game that invites new players in rather than scaring them with a 400-page rulebook and a specter of doom.

### Social Engineered for Laughter and Light Debate

This is a social game through and through. It is best played with people who enjoy heckling in good humor, who appreciate a well-timed punchline, and who can handle a little dramatic irony. The table becomes a stage where everyone’s inner narrator emerges. Practice your best “oh no, the tragedy!” line and you’ll be rewarded with hearty laughter and the occasional mock-solemn bow as you reveal the last misfortune card.

### Replayability and Freshness

With a deck full of Story cards and a diverse cast of Gloom characters, each session feels different. The sealed copy you just opened holds the same potential as every other copy, but the interactions you generate depend on timing, alliances (temporary or ruthlessly strategic), and the kind of misfortune you think will shock your friends enough to scream into their drink. The second edition keeps this variability intact while smoothing edges that previously caused confusion at the table.

## A Sample Playthrough: A Tiny Tale of Grim Gratitude

Let us fantasize a tiny two-player round to illustrate the flow. You have a family member named Griselda, who is perpetually in a good mood but has a tragic backstory waiting to be burdened with gloom. Your opponent places a Story card that says Griselda will find a long-lost relative, which, in the world of gloom, translates to both a dramatic memory and a new set of misfortunes for the poor girl.

On your turn, you lay down a Gloom card that suggests Griselda dislikes thunderstorms. The thunderstorm triggers a cascade of other misfortunes that comment on her life choices, increasing the drama. You narrate a short scene: a candle flickers, a window creaks, and the audience—your fellow players—nod knowingly as you talk about the time she almost joined a circus, but the clowns were too optimistic for her gloom. The misfortune stack grows as you add a card about a pet parrot who learned to tell fortunes but only predicts doom for Griselda’s love life. The table roars with laughter and groans, a perfect balance that keeps everyone engaged rather than scanning their phones for the nearest escape hatch.

As the round nears its end, you reveal the scores. Griselda sits at a low score, partially due to your expert storytelling and partially due to the curveball effects of the Story cards. Your opponent tries a last-ditch plan to reset the narrative with a capricious twist, and the table erupts in a chorus of mock-outrage. The end feels like a tiny tragedy staged by witty friends, and you can’t help but think: this is tabletop gaming at its most characterful and entertaining.

## How Gloom Stacks Up Against Other Card Games

Compared to other storytelling or dungeon-crawling card games, Gloom offers a lighter, more table-society-friendly experience. It is not about dungeon maps or grinding through encounters; it is about the theatre of misfortune and the social dance of out-quirking your friends. It shares some DNA with games like Mysterium in terms of storytelling but rejects the role of a single mystic or medium; instead, you become the author of a shared, ever-changing family saga. If you crave crunchy combat, you may not be the target audience, but if you want clever misfortune and a room full of giggles, Gloom is hard to beat.

## Strategy and Tactics: Where to Focus Your Efforts

- Balance is your friend: stacking too many gloom cards on one character may cause you to overshoot the target, which can be funny but ineffective. Keep a careful eye on both your own family and your opponents’ families to maximize dramatic effect.
- Story cards are kingmakers: invest in strong Story cards that interact with the common misfortunes and can reverse the tone of a round. Narrative control is a surprisingly powerful tool.
- Reading the table: the best moments come from players’ reactions. A well-timed reveal can swing the mood of the table, win you moral applause, and tilt the score in your favor without needing to shout at the rules.
- Player count matters: 3 to 4 players yields the richest social dynamics, while two players still works well if you crave intimate storytelling and some extra malice.

## Tips for New Players

- Don’t fear the gloom; embrace the joke. The rulebook is there to help you tell a story, not to choke your creative impulses.
- Take notes mentally. The best moments in Gloom come from how you connect one misfortune to another and how you present the narrative to others.
- Use the fan-favorite line: the longer the tale, the more likely you are to craft a memorable moment that helps you win the round through storytelling rather than brute misfortune.
