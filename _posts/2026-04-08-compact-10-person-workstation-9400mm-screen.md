## What is the Compact 10-Person Workstation?

At a glance, this setup looks like a conveyor belt of productivity designed to squeeze ten coworkers around a long, continuous surface. The core idea remains simple: maximize collaboration by providing a single, expansive interaction space while maintaining compact footprints through modular bench segments. The centerpiece is a 9400mm (9.4 meters) display surface that runs along the bench line or wraps around a corner in a U-shape, depending on the floor plan. The display can be a joint LED wall, a high-resolution OLED strip, or a set of bright, evenly lit panels—whatever your vendor supports—that projects shared content to everyone in range.

In practice, you’ll get ten ergonomic workspaces on the periphery, each with its own monitor arm, keyboard tray, and a degree of separation that’s enough to avoid elbow collisions during a moment of keyboard-dramatics. The system is designed for teams that value real-time visuals, multi-user editing, and the occasional social break where someone yells, Pause the video, I need to show you something on the big screen! and everyone nods in chorus like synchronized swimmers.

### The 9400mm Screen: Real or Myth?

A 9.4-meter screen is the star of this show. ## Design and Build: Materials, Modularity, and Cable Control

One of the first questions you’ll have is: can this thing be assembled in a sane timeframe without enlisting a small army? Short answer: yes, with caveats that come with any modular system. The workstation is built from modular bays that slot together like a colossal, grown-up LEGO project. Each bay houses seating, a monitor arm, a keyboard tray, and a discrete, ventilated power compartment that keeps cables mostly out of sight and out of trouble.

- Materials: Expect a mix of steel for the frame, high-density plywood or MDF for the surfaces, and a top-coat that resists coffee spills and the occasional snack crumble. Edge banding—PVC or wood veneer—prevents the desk edge chess that happens when ten people reach for pens and roll chairs into the hallway.
- Modularity: The bench segments snap into linear or L-shaped footprints. Each segment can be swapped, rotated, or removed with a minimal toolkit. Great for post-quarter reorgs or reconfiguring for new projects without moving a squad of relocation ninjas.
- Cable management: The spaghetti factor is real. The build includes a centralized trough system with grommets, cable trays, and channel covers to corral power, HDMI/DP, USB-C, and network cables. Extra space is baked in for future IO, because tech ages faster than a TV rerun.

Visuals matter: surface textures and color palettes should reduce glare and increase readability of the long display. Matte gray, deep blue, or warm wood tones create a welcoming vibe rather than a sterile facility vibe. And yes, you’ll want to consider acoustics because a big shared desk can turn a quiet room into a chorus of keystrokes and the occasional my mic isn’t picking me up moment.

## Ergonomics: Comfort, Posture, and Social Distance on a 9.4m Stage

Ergonomics on a long, shared surface is a delicate act of balancing personal comfort with team synergy. Each workstation typically features:

- Individual monitor arms with height and tilt adjustability to accommodate different eye levels and angles.
- Keyboard trays that sit at a comfortable elbow angle, reducing forearm strain and enabling wrist-friendly typing.
- Task lighting integrated under the monitor arms to reduce glare and provide a calm ambience.
- Adjustable chairs with lumbar support, armrests that don’t interfere with a neighbor’s chair, and wheels smooth enough to glide across surfaces.

But there’s more to it than furniture. The 9400mm display creates a natural focal point. The downside: privacy concerns, noise discipline, and personal space management. Consider soft partitions or privacy screens for bursts of sensitive work, acoustic panels or white noise solutions for calmer collaboration, and etiquette guidelines to prevent hand-cramp shuffle when someone encroaches.

In practice, ergonomics shine when the team collaborates. The long line of sight makes design reviews and whiteboarding faster. The risk is sensory overload if you don’t turn this into a well-managed system. Rotating tasks and defining task-specific zones near each seat helps keep people productive without feeling overwhelmed by the central wall of content.

## Tech Stack, Connectivity, and the 9400mm Display Experience

A long bench process requires a robust, flexible hardware stack. Here’s what you typically see in the 10-person setup:

- Central display: LED wall or multiple large-format panels spanning the length. You may choose a uniform bezel for a clean look or accept slight seams for easier maintenance. Resolution tends to be 4K or higher, with color calibration baked in during installation.
- Individual workstations: each seat gets a dedicated PC or a docking-friendly laptop setup. Docking stations provide USB-C power, display daisy-chaining, and network connectivity for a clutter-free desk.
- Power distribution: a centralized PDU under the trough reduces the risk of power strips across the surface and helps with surge protection and easy maintenance.
- Network and collaboration: gigabit or 10-gigabit Ethernet, plus Wi-Fi as a backstop for mobile devices. Some configurations include a small on-table touch interface for quick status checks without diving into the main display.
- Perimeter devices: a camera bar for group calls, a built-in speaker array for announcements, and mic pods to ensure voices carry across the bench.

The “shared screen” concept has quirks. If several people push content at once, establish a protocol: who gets primacy? Do you queue via chat? Does a clipboard app arbitrate what’s shown? A little governance goes a long way toward avoiding chaos and keeping the team synchronized.

## Setup and Installation: A Practical, Slightly Humorous Guide

Getting this monster into a room is half the fun. Here’s a practical, not-too-serious walkthrough to get you from cardboard boxes to productivity in roughly one work-week and a few heroic coffee runs:

1) Measure the room and the doors. A 9.4-meter display will require corridor clearance and potentially ceiling-mounted supports if you’re going for a wall-to-wall install.
2) Plan the footprint. Straight line, L-shape, or U-shaped command center? Sketch the floor plan and mark electrical outlets, network drops, and heat sources.
3) Prepare the cable troughs. Pre-routing cables saves you from a future modern-art sculpture on the floor.
4) Assemble bay by bay. Don’t rush. The modular approach rewards patience; misaligned bays yield a wobbly bench and stern glances from your team.
5) Calibrate the display. A 9.4-meter panel requires color calibration, gamma correction, and a parallax-free optimism about the center.
6) Test with a live group. Run a mini standup to check audio, video, and whether the keyboard trays hit someone’s knee. You’ll learn about spatial tolerance in those moments.

Document the process with photos. The Geeknite audience loves a good “before and after” slide, and a timelapse of ten people attempting to onboard a video is a priceless asset.

## Day-to-Day Use: Productivity, Fun, and Occasional Chaos

Does this behemoth actually boost productivity? It depends on how you use it. Real-world scenarios where the compact 10-person workstation shines—and where it trips you up:

- Collaborative design sprints: real-time feedback, shared assets, rapid iteration. A central anchor accelerates convergence on a single vision.
- Brainstorming sessions: the long screen doubles as a giant whiteboard substitute; wire up mood boards, sprint plans, or retro boards for post-mint analysis that keeps everyone engaged.
- Software demos and reviews: the big canvas ensures no one misses crucial details; assign seats to stakeholders and run multi-speaker demos without laptop-swapping chaos.
- Training and onboarding: share examples, code, or workflows simultaneously to speed learning for new hires.

The flip side:
- Privacy-sensitive tasks: partitions or privacy software required; you cannot turn off a portion of the panel for one seat, so plan accordingly.
- Noise and distraction: a cacophony of keystrokes and discussions; acoustic panels or quiet zones help.
- Personal furniture conflicts: ensure etiquette, designate chair zones, or signage for personal space boundaries.

In short, if your team thrives on collaboration and shared visuals, this workspace can be a catalyst. If you’re chasing deep, solitary focus, you’ll need a plan to carve out private space. The 9400mm display becomes a communal screen—the whiteboard with better color accuracy and more cable spaghetti.
