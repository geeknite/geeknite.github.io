## What is Throw Throw Burrito?

TTB is a party game designed to pair mischief with a decent dose of chaos. The core concept is simple: you play cards to collect sets while simultaneously hurling two plush burritos at other players during “burrito fights.” It’s a mashup of memory, set collection, and dodgeball theatrics—without the blood, pain, or lawsuits (usually). The brand-new sealed copy provides the pristine components, a crisp rulebook, and the inevitable questionable decisions you’ll make after three rounds of snacks and someone yells “Burrito duel!”

### Components of a Brand-New Experience

Likely included in a brand-new sealed box:

- Two plush burritos (brightly colored, irresistibly squeezable). The feel of a brand-new plush burrito is a sensory delight: soft, squeaky, and suspiciously full of “possible future bruises” energy.
- A deck of cards (usually around 100-110). The cards feature goofy art, punny phrases, and the occasional “fight” action that triggers a burrito throw.
- An illustrated rulebook that attempts to be helpful while also acknowledging the chaos you’re about to unleash.
- A few token bits (or none, depending on edition). If you’re lucky, you’ll discover a tiny plastic get-out-of-jurrasic-jingles token that you’ll pretend is important during a round.

## A Deep Dive into the Components

Two plush burritos lead the charge. They are not real burritos—don’t be fooled by the squishy texture; they’re the props for dodging chaos. ## How to Play: A Practical Yet Chaotic Overview

Setup: Place the burritos in the center of the table, or in your living room floor as appropriate. Shuffle the deck, deal a handful to each player, and ensure the rulebook is within reach for those who are too competitive to rely on memory alone.

Gameplay: On your turn, you might draw a card, play a set, or trigger a burrito fight. The core loop is to be quick, to avoid getting hit by burritos, and to assemble interesting card combinations that let you claim points or draw penalties for your opponents. The “fight” cards cause players to fling burritos toward each other (not at people’s faces, ideally—keep safety in mind). It’s all about tempo, chaos, and the occasional triumphant shout.

Counterplay: The thrill of TT B is that it punishes hesitation. If you overthink your draw or hesitate too long, you’ll get burritoed by fate (and possibly a plush burrito to the forehead). The brand-new copy ensures cards are sharp, edges are intact, and the burritos are perfectly primed for their first flight.

Endgame: The game ends when someone reaches the target score or when the deck runs out (depending on edition). In most playthroughs, the endgame feels like a sprint to the punchline: who will cause the biggest laugh, who will get the last banter, and who will be left with a handful of pity cards?

Strategy: While TT B is a party game, there’s actually a surprising amount of strategy in card-drafting, risk assessment, and the timing of burrito throws. You’ll learn to combine set-collection with social mischief, and you’ll quickly discover that the best strategy is often “wing it with confidence and let chaos do the math.”

House rules: This is where TT B shines. You can introduce timer rounds, add extra dares for players who are allergic to laughter, or even create a “burrito jury” to judge who dodged the most ridiculous throws. The brand-new sealed copy’s rules are crisp, but the real enjoyment comes from how you adapt them to your social circle.
