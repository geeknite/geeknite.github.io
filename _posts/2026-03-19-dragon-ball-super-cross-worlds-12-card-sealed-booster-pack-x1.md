### Commons and uncommons: the bread and butter
Commons provide the baseline for every deck you try to throw together. In Cross Worlds, the commons often come with efficient energy costs and reliable effects that can kick-start your early aggression or stabilize in the midgame. The uncommons, meanwhile, begin to tilt the balance toward more nuanced plays: board presence, removal options, and subtle tempo shifts that reward players who track the sequence of turns with the dedication of a detective binge-watching a crime caper.

The key with commons and uncommons is not to undervalue them. A strong early game often comes down to a handful of well-known, well-timed plays that can snowball if your opponent misses a beat. In sealed formats, where you cannot fully rely on a pre-curated strategy, those early plays often determine your chances of staying competitive past turn six or seven when the board state starts to resemble a paintball field with fewer rules and more chaos.

### Rares and foils: the heart of the hunt
Rares in Cross Worlds are where the set’s aspirations show up in card form. They carry more impactful effects, often with better synergy hooks that can anchor a deck’s core plan. ## Playability and strategy in sealed format
In sealed play, you are not equal to the luxury of drafting your perfect curve. You are handed a sample of potential that you must maximize with a little improvisation and a lot of table chatter. Cross Worlds emphasizes tempo and momentum. You want to press your advantage when you have it, but you must also plan for a plausible late-game where life totals matter and your opponent starts to accumulate blockers and answers you did not see coming.

A practical approach to sealed with Cross Worlds is to identify a core plan by round three: what is your strongest early aggression, what is your midrange plan to close the game, and what is your single best finisher that can surprise the opponent who thought they had you boxed in. The remaining cards should be tuned around this core plan, providing removal to keep the opponent from overextending, a couple of card draw options for consistency, and a couple of tech choices that punish specific archetypes you expect to encounter in your local meta.
