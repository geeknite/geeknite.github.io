# Bird & Mercs - Batman Miniature Game Review

Yes, dear readers, today we dive into a game that sounds like a gag from a Gotham comedy club: Bird & Mercs in the Batman Miniature Game universe. No, it’s not a spoof where Robin opens a poultry shop in Crime Alley (though that would be a look). It’s a fan-loving, table-topping homage that riffs on the iconic Batman MBG system with a twist so nerdy you can hear the Batarangs whistling through the air. If you’ve played the base Batman Miniature Game by Knight Models, you’ll know the vibe: skirmish-level heroics, street-level grit, and enough plastic and resin to fill a Bat-cave shelf with tiny torches of victory. Bird & Mercs takes that vibe, dusts it with a dash of irreverence, and asks: what if Gotham’s misfits had to deal with a parallel hustle—bird-themed mercenaries who prefer the rooftop run to the alley ambush?

And yes, there are birds. There are mercenaries. There is Batman. And there is you, hero of (tiny) justice, left to decide whether to swoop in with a brood of beaks or negotiate with cackling mercenaries who bring a toolkit of gadgets and shotgun shells to every rooftop exchange. It’s weird, it’s funny, and it somehow works. If you’re a fan of MBG’s core rules but want something a little… feathered, this is your ticket.

## Overview: what is Bird & Mercs in this universe?
Bird & Mercs is best described as a fan-creation that layers a niche, cinematic twist on the Batman MBG engine. The premise leans into two opposed factions that feel native to Gotham’s nightscape: The Birds, a cadre of avian-inspired operatives who flirt with aerial moves and feathery misdirection; and The Mercs, a ragtag group of mercenary types who can be bought, bribed, or bored into chaos by Batman’s endless vigilance. The rules are not a separate game; they’re a modular expansion that can slot into your existing MBG games with a swap of faction cards, new missions, and a few bespoke rules that give these two sides their distinctive flavors.

From a design perspective, Bird & Mercs nods heavily to MBG’s action economy, where order dice, initiative, and character activation drive the tempo. The Birds’ rules emphasize agility, flanking, and feints; the Mercs lean into firearms, gadgets, and opportunistic plays. It’s a tonal shift, not a mechanical revolution, which is exactly the sweet spot MBG players enjoy. If you’ve ever wished for a scenario where a winged scout dives in to disrupt a mercenary snare and then exits stage left with a feathered flourish, this is your moment.

## The Box, The Miniatures, and The Look
Like many MBG variants, Bird & Mercs shines most when you can imagine the tabletop as Gotham’s midnight stage. The model selection for a “Birds vs Mercs” skirmish tends to be a curated trio of core leaders and a handful of henchmen, with a mix of avian-themed miniatures and gritty mercenaries. As with any fan-made faction, the exact component list varies depending on the edition you’re using and the local printing you’ve managed to cop from a friend with a 3D-printer named Klaus.

Visuals-wise, the Birds are sleek: slender silhouettes, winged capes, beaked masks, and a palette that can range from matte black to iridescent blues and greens. The Mercs are grittier, with a patchwork of jackets, goggles, and gadgets that feel like they’ve walked out of a late-night heist and into a rooftop shootout. If you’re painting, you’re in for a treat: metallics, leathers, and a dash of neon for those gadget LEDs. The box art often telegraphs the mood: “we’re here to complicate Batman’s life, one beak at a time.”

Jekyll-and-Hyde note: the Bird & Mercs artwork is not just a pretty face. The visual language helps with the narrative: a Bird unit might project dominance over rooftops, claiming high ground; Merc units can establish choke points and conversion lanes for creating dynamic crossroads on the board.

## Rules and Mechanics: a quick refresher and what Bird & Mercs adds
If you know MBG, you know the baseline: activation, order dice, speed, strength, defense, and fatigue. MBG rules are surprisingly elegant for miniatures skirmish: you roll dice per stat, manage a fatigue pool, push your units to do impressive feats, and try to trigger narrative interactions that swing momentum. Bird & Mercs sticks to this core, but adds some bespoke layers that emphasize the birds’ aerial play and the mercs’ gadget-driven chaos:

- Flight and Roosting: Birds can use short bursts of flight to reposition, taking risk but gaining rare ambush moments on high ground. Strategically, you’ll want to place your birds to threaten two directions at once, forcing your opponent to split resources.
- Feathers and Feints: Some Bird cards include feint-like effects that can misdirect enemy shots or disguise a withdrawal. It’s a tiny mechanical nod to the birds’ natural cunning and adds a little bluffing to the otherwise straightforward MBG engine.
- Gadget Roulette: Mercs bring a bag of tricks—grenades, zip lines, net cannons, you name it. They come with utility dice or limited-use cards that let you reroll a failed moment or anchor a retreat. This creates moments where a Merc struggles to breach a Bird’s defences, then suddenly flips the table with a well-placed gadget.
- Corruption, Not Just Crime: A clever thematic twist is the “corruption” mechanic (you can bribe or flip objectives) that nudges a mission’s outcome away from brute force into cunning negotiation. It’s a subtle reminder that Gotham’s moral lines are fuzzy and often humorous when you roll a misfired gadget and watch a Merc accidentally shoot their own plan.

Note: the balance of these elements is very much dependent on your playgroup and the edition you’re using. As with any fan-made expansion, your mileage may vary, but the charm is in how these prompts incentivize thinking beyond the straightforward “move, shoot, repeat” MBG rhythm. ## Gameplay and Scenarios: how a match plays out with Bird & Mercs
A standard Bird & Mercs encounter plays out in lines with MBG: take turns, complete a sequence of actions, and try to complete an objective while surviving the other side’s counterplay. Where Bird & Mercs shines is in how it treats space and tempo:

- Rooftop Domination: The Birds naturally prefer vertical play. Your plan is to ping-pong between rooftops, pecking mercenaries from above, and forcing them to choose between chasing you or protecting their own backs. The map becomes a chessboard of height, with the Birds using elevation to gain line-of-sight advantages.
- Narrow Alleys and Lanes: The Mercs excel at forcing engagements in tight spaces where gadgets can connect with limited cover. You’ll see turns where a net-launcher disables a Bird for a critical moment, letting a Merc squad advance and secure a loot objective.
- Objective-based Missions: Most Bird & Mercs tables revolve around a loot, hostage, or intel objective that requires players to coordinate movement, collect items, and return to a safe zone. The gadgets become tools for creating escape routes, and the birds act as disruptors to tilt the objective race.

In terms of length and pacing, Bird & Mercs tends to run a touch shorter than a typical MBG campaign, favoring intense skirmishes with crisp turns that feel cinematic rather than epic. If you prefer long, sprawling campaigns, you can bolt on a custom progression system or borrow MBG’s standard campaign modules and apply them to your Bird & Mercs crew with minor rule tweaks.

## Balance, pacing, and the “fun factor” on the table
Balance in fan-made content is a fickle beast. Bird & Mercs leans into asymmetry in a way that feels purposeful rather than punitive. The Birds aren’t stronger at all tasks; they’re stronger at one thing: exploiting vertical space. The Mercs aren’t tankier in straight lines; they’re clever and dangerous at the moment they catch a window to use a gadget or push a flank.

The end result is a game that rewards clever positioning and good narrative beats more than raw dice stops. You’ll see games swing on a single cat-and-mouse decision: do you commit a useful bird to a risky aerial maneuver, or do you stay grounded and risk the mercs exploiting your retreat? Either choice can win you the game if you played the moment well.

## Painting, models, and hobby considerations
As with any MBG line, Bird & Mercs provides a strong hobby hook. The Birds offer opportunities for airbrushing highlights on feathering, gradient cloaks, and metallic beak accents, while the Mercs invite leather textures, weathered armor, and gadgets with a personal, messy patina. If you’re into painting, you’ll find that these two factions encourage a wide range of techniques:

- Birds: feather layering, subtle iridescence in plumage, high-contrast beaks, and reflective wing cuffs. Use a glazing technique to push light through darker blues and greens on the plumage.
- Mercs: weathering, leather browns, metallics for rivets and gadgets, and a practical approach to wear and tear. Weathering powders and stippling will give you that gritty Gotham feel without blowing your budget.

If you’re new to MBG painting, this is a friendly entry point because the two factions ease you into different textures without requiring a perfect base coat. ## Table setup and terrain tips
Bird & Mercs love a skyline palette: rooftop edges, HVAC ducts, and stealthy zones that block lines-of-sight. Terrain genius here is to create vertical depth with a few high points and some ruined urban scenery for flavor. A mix of cardboard rooftops, foam building facades, and a few clear acrylic markers for “danger” zones gives you a Gotham feel without breaking the bank.

Terrain tips:
- Use shadows liberally. The Birds benefit from higher ground but can be snared by terrain that blocks their flight paths.
- Create hazard zones that reflect gadget interactions—grates, vents, and scaffolding can become traps or routes that shape the match.
- Keep objective markers visually distinct. A couple of neon-tinged loot crates really sell the scenario and help you track progress during rounds.

## Who should buy Bird & Mercs? a practical verdict
- MBG veterans who want a playful twist and new tactical considerations without abandoning the core engine.
- Hobbyists who enjoy painting two distinct aesthetics (birds vs mercs) and who love the “story on the table” vibe.
- Groups that enjoy narrative campaigns and wish to explore a Gotham microcosm where feathered operatives and gadget-wielding mercenaries collide.

## The endgame: how does it feel after the final roll?
In the best moments, Bird & Mercs captures the cinematic swoop of a Batman scene: a Bird flanking an exposed Merc, a device misfiring, a rooftop chase that ends with a punchy, almost cartoonish close. You’ll laugh at the absurdity of it while also admiring the crisp moment-to-moment decisions that matter. The game doesn’t pretend it’s a deep military sim; it’s a skirmish game that wants you to feel like you’re playing in a comic book, where your choices shape a few panels rather than a full chapter.

Pros tight enough to justify a separate purchase:
- Fresh thematic flavor without derailing MBG’s core DNA

Cons to consider:

## Final recommendation
Bird & Mercs is a charming, clever addition to the Batman MBG ecosystem. It delivers a thematic twist, clever mechanics that reward tactical creativity, and plenty of hobby potential. It’s not a must-have for every MBG purist, but for players who relish a wink and a nudge at Gotham’s feathered vigilantes and gadget-wielding mercs, it’s a joy. The rules additions are gimmick-light and tastefully integrated, letting you keep MBG’s core flow while injecting new storytelling opportunities. It’s approachable for newcomers who already love MBG, and it offers a nice jump for veterans yearning for a lighter, more narrative-driven night.

If you’re building a Batman MBG night with friends who like a dash of humor and a lot of table art, Bird & Mercs is a strong pick. You’ll likely end up painting two squads instead of one, because who can resist a beaked captain and a net-cannon-laden mercenary? The birds will discover high ground. The Mercs will discover you. Gotham will be mildly annoyed. And you, dear reader, will have earned a tidy shelf-full of souvenirs from a night of chaotic, comic-book glory.
