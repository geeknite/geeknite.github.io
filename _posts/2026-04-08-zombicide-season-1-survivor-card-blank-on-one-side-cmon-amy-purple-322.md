If you’re the kind of collector who notices the brushwork on the goggles or the tiny scuff on the boot of a survivor, you’ll enjoy the micro-details that Amy Purple apparently threw into the lower-left corner of the frame. Amy Purple’s credit here is a nod to the artist’s ongoing influence on the look and feel of the line, and the 322 designation reads as a quiet wink to the number nerds in the audience who collect production codes the way they collect shiny dice.

Here’s a practical note for you: the visual balance is pleasing. The portrait doesn’t overwhelm the card’s function, and the blank side invites creative use rather than mindless repetition. ## Why a blank-on-one-side card matters (or not)

Let’s address the elephant in the cardboard box: why would a survivor card be blank on one side? The answer is not as dramatic as you might fear. It isn’t a secret cheat code; it’s a celebration of modularity. Here are several practical angles to consider:

- Creative flexibility: The blank side can be used by players who enjoy homebrew rules or who like to track special conditions without cramming more printed text onto the official card. You can jot in temporary modifiers, or keep a running log of morale bonuses with a dry-erase marker (are you sensing the setup for a long campaign already?).
- GM shorthand: The blank side can be used by the GM to indicate survival status or narrative cues that don’t fit the printed text. It’s a way to keep the table focused on the story rather than the code on the sheet.
- Collectibility and showmanship: Some players treat blank sides as a canvas for fan art or custom art overlays. It’s a nice way to personalize your table without resorting to full custom cards that might flood your play area with variance.

From a game design perspective, the blank side is not a fundamental mechanic improvement; it’s a creative flourish. It’s a reminder that not every piece of hardware in a game has to scream its function every second. Sometimes the best tool is one that invites you to define the tool in your own words.

If you want to see how other players integrate blank or customizable components into their Zombicide sessions, our community roundups include several anecdotes about players scribbling their own stat blocks on spare index cards or repurposing the back of other survivor cards for quick-reference quotes. ## Practical tips for using the blank side at the table

If you plan to actively use the blank side, here are some practical guidelines to keep your games smooth and fun:

- Use a dry-erase marker: A single, fine-tip dry-erase marker will let you annotate the blank side without damaging the card or leaving ghost marks after eras-—if you care about cleanliness, consider a microfiber cloth and a dedicated wiping schedule.
- Create a quick legend: Before your first session, agree on a simple set of symbols or shorthand for what you’ll write on the blank side. A tiny star for a temporary morale boost? A question mark for an upcoming event? A small heart for a survivor bond? The simpler, the better.
- Avoid permanent changes: Since this is a casual game, keep your customization reversible. Don’t permanently alter the card; you want to preserve the art and the official data for when you pass the controller to a new GM or a new group of players.
- Shadow-lore approach: Use the blank side sparingly to avoid clutter. The best use is to prompt a moment of table talk rather than to overshadow the core rules with a paragraph of scribbles.
