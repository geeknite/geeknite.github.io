## Troy: The Huntsman Extraordinaire
If there’s a trump card in this box, it’s Troy—the titular Huntsman promised in the box’s marketing and delivered in both personality and in-game utility. Troy is designed to be a nimble, tactical option that rewards positioning and efficient removal of threats rather than brute force. In a Black Plague meta where big swords and high-attack survivors often claim the glory, Troy adds a more surgical flavor: pick your targets, exploit the terrain, and sneak past the zombie tide with a hunter’s precision.

From a narrative standpoint, Troy feels like the box’s emotional core: a grizzled veteran who has learned to talk the language of fear and use it as a tool. In game terms, Troy typically has abilities that reward you for keeping a low profile in the early rounds, setting up ambushes, and punishing the undead for stepping into the wrong corridor. Some reviewers joke that Troy is a perfect character for “I swear I only wanted to plant one trap,” and the box happily encourages that mischief.

If you’re a painter, Troy’s figure offers clean lines for shading, minimal risk of ugly over-paint, and a few iconic features (a hooded cloak, a crossbow or similar hunting implement) that look great with a realistic bone-white base and muted leather tones. If you’re not a painter, the kit still plays well on the board; even a tabletop-standard paint job can transform a table full of beige plastic into a cinematic battlefield where each miniature somehow looks like it belongs in a hunting saga rather than a miniature misadventure.

## The Gameplay Rhythm: How Huntsmen Box Changes the Game
Zombicide Black Plague thrives on a delicate rhythm: you balance resource management, action planning, and hero positioning against a pool of fresh zombies that keep rushing toward your chain of survivors. The Huntsmen Box injects new rhythms into that flow in several meaningful ways.

### Ambush and terrain leverage
The Huntsmen introduce a handful of Ambush-style dynamics that reward players for using line-of-sight, terrain features, and measured movement. The new cards and rules inserts nudge you toward map control: instead of sprinting from objective to objective, you’re actively shaping the battlefield, forcing the undead to funnel through chokepoints where your hunters can pick them off with surgical precision.

### Survival and risk management
Troy and his fellow Huntsmen encourage a risk-management approach rather than pure aggressive tempo. You’ll sometimes deliberately trigger a smaller cluster of zombies to set up a bigger, more lucrative chain reaction later. The box’s risk-reward schema is not unlike a tabletop version of a heist movie: you sacrifice a little safety in the moment to guarantee a bigger payoff in the next couple of turns.

### Player interaction and table dynamics
If your table craves the “two heros vs. many zombies” dance, the Huntsmen Box gives you more turns where you may be forced into collaborative positioning: you set up cross-fire with your friends, you trigger a trap or two on a zombie spawn to maximize damage, and you watch your fellow players coordinate to hold a corridor while one brave soul advances toward the objective with Troy in the lead. It’s not just a set of extra stats; it’s a social dynamic that can turn a routine skirmish into a memorable sequence of “we pulled it off, by the skin of our teeth” vibes.

## Painting and Hobby Tips for Twitchy Nights
If you’re planning to diversify your army’s palette with the Huntsmen set, here are a few quick pointers to help you get the best results without turning painting into a full-time job:

- Start with a strong undercoat: neutral gray or black spray helps the metallics pop without fighting with skin tones.
- Use Contrast paints strategically on leather gear; it saves time and gives you consistent shading across multiple minis.
- For Troy, a muted cloak color paired with a bone or gray cloak edge adds a cinematic silhouette that reads well on a busy board.
- Don’t hesitate to dry-brush a touch of silver on metallics—swords and armor look far more “boss fight” when you do.
- If you’re new to basing, a simple sand-and-brown-wash approach can make the whole unit look ready for a hunt without requiring a full basing kit.

## Tabletop Setup: A Practical Playthrough Plan
Here’s a practical, no-nuss approach to integrating the Huntsmen Box into a typical Black Plague session:

1) Empty and sort: Unpack the miniatures and lay out the mission cards. Separate the Huntsmen survivors from the zombie minis to avoid accidental misplacement.
2) Build the terrain scenario: Use a few basic cardboard walls or terrain sets to recreate a hunting hall or forested corridor vibe—something that makes ambush tactics feel meaningful.
3) Deploy Troy with care: Place Troy in a position where he can leverage the objectives without overextending into heavy zombie concentrations on Turn 1.
4) Start with a simple mission: Run through a low-risk objective to get everyone comfortable with the Huntsmen rules before diving into a harder scenario.
5) Track resources: With new gear cards, you’ll want to keep a careful eye on how many ammo and health tokens you’ve spent vs. how much you’ll need for a late-game push.
