## What is Saboteur?
### Hidden Roles and Path-Building
Saboteur is a clever blend of hidden roles and path-building. Each round, players receive role cards that determine whether they are a Miner or a Saboteur. The Miners cooperate to build a continuous path from the start to one of the goal cards, while the Saboteurs secretly attempt to hinder the path, perhaps by discarding the right cards at crucial moments or by blocking the tunnel with a detour card. The English edition is particularly friendly for families because the text is legible, and the rules are concise enough that you can teach a brand-new player in under five minutes.

### Components and Theme
The game includes a deck of path cards (which you place to create routes through the mine), a set of action cards (to help or hinder others), role cards, and gold nugget tokens. The art style is lighthearted and cartoonish, with a humor that suits kids without meaning the game is silly or low-stakes. The theme—a group of dwarves attempting to locate treasure—feels timeless and a little Viking-meets-video-game in vibe, which makes it appealing across generations.

## Setup and Components
To set up Saboteur, shuffle the path and action decks, deal role cards to each player, and then deal a handful of cards to each player. The mine path is created by laying down a start card and then players take turns placing path cards to extend the tunnel toward one of several goal cards. The gold that can be claimed at the end only goes to those who finish the path successfully, which creates a healthy incentive for collaboration—until someone decides to sabotage.

The English edition does a nice job with labeling, iconography, and the font sizes on the cards. A common quirk in family editions is that the card text may use simple, kid-friendly wording, which helps younger players understand their options without requiring a law degree in dwarf semantics.

## How to Play: A Quick Start Guide
### Turn Structure and Core Actions
On a typical turn, you draw two cards and may play one. You can place a path card to extend the tunnel, or you can play an action card to either help yourself or hamper someone else. There are also cards that reveal a player’s role, cards that block paths, and cards that let you move or rotate existing path segments. The objective is to create a continuous tunnel from the start to one of the gold-ending cards.

### End of Round
When a path successfully reaches a goal card, the round ends. The miners who contributed to the successful path share the gold, while saboteurs score nothing for that round. If the path is blocked or if players exhaust their hand, the round ends and a fresh one begins with new roles for everyone.

### End-of-Round Scoring
The game is typically played in multiple rounds, with a running tally of gold or points. The exact scoring mechanics vary depending on the edition, but the idea remains the same: miners want to reach the gold, saboteurs want to see chaos and misdirection, and everyone else is trying to keep the table from breaking into a giggle fit.

## Strategy and Family Gaming
Saboteur rewards flexible thinking. There isn’t one set of “best moves”—there are multiple lines of play that depend on who you’re sitting with and how the role cards land. For families, here are a few practical tips:
- Pay attention to the map: As soon as you see a path being laid, you can infer who might be sabotaging and adjust your strategy.
- Use the Map card to reveal roles when it’s safe and helpful, not when it would give the saboteurs more information than you want them to have.
- Coordinate with other Miners to choose which route to pursue, but beware the Saboteurs’ tricks—sometimes you’re being steered into a trap you didn’t design.
- Don’t overcommit to a plan: If you lock your team into a single possible route, a single well-timed sabotage card can ruin everything.
