## Overview
### What is Fortune Row?
Fortune Row is the city’s seam—the place where luck is bought, barter is common, and the shadows practically applaud when you roll a natural 20 in a parking garage. The Accessories Pack builds on that by offering a set of narrative props, character cues, and sensory hooks that players can physically interact with. In practice, it’s a way to transform abstract vibes into tangible moments: a card that hints at a dead lead, a coin that glitters with an unusual amount of misfortune, a scarf that smells faintly of rain and regret. TheDesign hallmarks lean into that “mythic noir” feel City of Mist fans know and love, but with a brighter sheen and a slightly more theatrical flair.

The pack arrives with a mix of 12–ish items, depending on the exact edition you pick up. You’ll find a handful of narrative cues (cards you can draw during scenes), a set of physical props (coins, tickets, tokens), and a small deck of mood cards designed to push scenes from “quiet investigation” to “neighborhood-wide conspiracy in progress” within a single session. The intent is simple: give GMs something tactile to hand to players, something to flip when the plot lurches, and something to clutter the table so everyone knows you’re playing a game about masks and myth.
### Contents
Here’s a rough breakdown of what you’ll typically find in the Fortune Row Accessories Pack. Note that exact contents can vary by edition or regional printing, but the spirit remains the same:

- A curated set of fortune-themed cards with evocative prompts. These aren’t just flavor text; they’re designed to spark scene ideas, NPC reactions, and quick-cut flashbacks when a player pulls a card mid-scene.
- A pile of props: one or two metallic tokens, a few cloth items (a scarf, a ribbon), and a handful of paper props (photos, notes, and a docket card). The metallic tokens have a satisfying heft, and the paper props are printed on a heavier stock than you’d expect for something that sits on a table between combats.
- Mood cards with color-graded backings. These are wonderfully chaotic in a “pick a card, any card” sense—perfect for inserting a wrinkle into a scene even if you’re not fully sure what your players will do next.
- A fortune keycard or two, which are designed to be held by players as “permission slips” for dramatic reveals. You might hear a chorus of “my key is turning, you’ll see…” when one is slapped onto the table.
- A small guide for GMs with scene hooks and quick-start setups. It’s not a full module, but it gives you a toehold if you’re juggling NPCs and trying to avoid the dreaded “blank page” moment in the middle of a session.

The packaging matches the aesthetic of Fortune Row as a location: ornate borders, a muted palette with pops of teal and gold, and a tactile feel that’s a step above your average card box. If you’re a collector who cares about presentation, this pack scratches that itch. If you’re in it for pure functionality, you’ll also be pleased—the items are clearly designed to be used in real, actual games, not just to look pretty on a shelf.

## How Fortune Row fits into the City of Mist ecosystem
### Integrating with gameplay mechanics
City of Mist emphasizes masks, powers, and the way a character’s secret identity clashes with a city that’s always watching. The Fortune Row Accessories Pack reinforces that by providing tangible anchors—the items that feel like they’re part of the mythos rather than generic ﬂuff. GMs who weave the pack into scenes can use tokens to mark “ritual significance,” mood cards to pivot the emotional tempo, and fortune cards to foreshadow twists. The key is to leverage these tools to tighten the storytelling loop: prompt, reveal, consequence, and then another prompt.

Think of the pack as a toolkit that’s designed to prompt narrative choices rather than dictate them. It’s easy to fall into the trap of “show-and-tell” where a GM hands out props as if players are required to treat them as canonical clues. Fortune Row discourages that. It invites players to decide what each item means in the context of their character’s identity and the day’s conflict. That means more role-playing, more character-driven decisions, and fewer minutes wasted debating “is this important?”

### Narrative seeds and scene hooks
One of the pack’s strongest features is the way it seeds scenes without forcing a specific plot beat. The mood cards can be drawn mid-session to push the story into a drift toward consequence. For example, a card might read: “The thing you fear most is also the thing you need most.” The GM can interpret this as a prompt for a risky choice or a flashback reveal. It’s deliberately ambiguous enough to accommodate different playstyles—from fast-paced detective work to slower, character-portrait-driven sessions.

That flexibility is crucial: City of Mist audiences range from “one-shot enthusiasts” to “campaigners who live in the World of Mist.” Fortune Row accommodates both. For a quick session, you can lean on mood cards to keep action moving; for a long campaign, you can thread the cards into recurring motifs—hints of a grand conspiracy or the city’s mythic understructure—that reappear in later sessions like a well-timed motif cue.

## Practical usage tips
### First session with Fortune Row
- Introduce a single prop early on. A tangible object gives players a shared focal point and a cue for later callbacks.
- Assign a “fortune” narrative stake to one character. Have a card or token become their personal stake that the GM can push or pull as needed.
- Use mood cards to frame the tone. If the party wants to solve a mystery, a mood card might push a twist that re-centers the stakes on personal costs.

### Mid-campaign twists
- Reserve a couple of the stronger mood cards for late-session cliffhangers. If you flop a card after a difficult choice, it should force a consequence that reshapes next week’s arc.
- Use tokens as “ownership markers.” If a player’s character earns a token, you can declare the token as an artifact they can leverage in a future scene. It gives a sense of progress and weight to choices.

### GM tips and caveats
- Don’t be afraid to remix. The cards aren’t sacred; they’re prompts. If you like a certain vibe from a different pack, you can cross-pollinate the tools for a unique table experience.
- Be mindful of pacing. The best use of Fortune Row items is to quicken tempo, not derail the session with six-minute debates about what a prop means. If a card or token stalls the table, move on and circle back later.
- Make it personal. Tie at least one item or card to a character’s backstory. Personal stakes make the moment more memorable than a generic reveal.
