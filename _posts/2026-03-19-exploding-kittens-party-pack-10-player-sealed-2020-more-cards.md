### Setting up for a big group
Setting up for 10 players is not a complicated chore, but you want to make sure there is enough seating, space for cards to spread, and a clear path for players to reach their large deck without elbowing someone else’s drink. The Party Pack typically adds a handful of larger pool cards that help fill the table more evenly and keep everyone involved. A good practice is to assign a table host who can manage the flow of the game and remind players when to pause the drama for a quick bathroom break—because at 10 players, chaos is real, and bathrooms are sacred.

### The pacing at scale
With 10 players, the pacing shifts noticeably. You move from a quick 15- to 20-minute round to something that can stretch to a half hour if everyone is debating the best way to bluff or whether to let a table speak for a moment. The extra cards introduced in the Party Pack give players more opportunities to alter outcomes, which means more near misses, more dramatic reveals, and more opportunities to groan and laugh in equal measure.

## How to play with 10 players

The base rules still apply: draw, play, and avoid the exploding kitten while using Defuse cards to survive. The Party Pack adds cards that invite more strategic depth without requiring intense rule learning. Here are a few practical notes for running a session with a full table:

- Start with a familiar flow: shuffle, deal, designate first player, and let the chaos begin. The extra cards should be introduced gradually to prevent confusion and to let players acclimate to the bigger deck.
- Save your Defuses for the moments they matter most. In a larger group, a single Defuse can define a high-stakes turn. The party pack makes those moments more frequent, which is exactly what drama lovers crave.
- Use a simple rule variant if your players are brand new: you may impose a soft timer for turns to avoid endless deliberation. The goal is to keep energy high, not to transform the game into a social experiment in patience.

### Strategy quick hits
- Bluff effectively by feigning confidence when you are about to draw. The larger table means more people are watching and less likely to call your bluff on the first draw.
- Remember that some cards allow you to steal turns, peek ahead, or force other players to draw. Use these with a light touch. If every move looks like a power play, you will wear out your welcome by round two.
- Pay attention to the table: who is hoarding Defuses, who looks nervous, and who keeps laughing at every draw. These tells guide your decisions and help you time your big plays for maximum effect.
