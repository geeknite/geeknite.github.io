## What’s inside the Core Set 2021 Planeswalker Deck?

The Planeswalker Deck is designed to be a turnkey experience. It’s not a luxury car with a four-point harness; it’s more like a reliable sedan with a neon spoiler and a plan of world domination that won’t burn a hole in your card binder. Here’s what you can expect to pull from the sleeve:

### The Planeswalker card
Each deck centers around a signature Planeswalker. This is the deck’s north star—your go-to planeswalker on the battlefield, with a few abilities that, theoretically, win you games. The real strategy is to support that Planeswalker with a small but solid suite of cards that complement its abilities rather than try to rewrite the entire color pie on the fly.

### A 60-card deck tailored to a color identity
The bulk of the deck is 60 cards built around the Planeswalker’s color identity. If you’re new to MTG, think of it as a curated collection of spells, creatures, and a pinch of removal that flows with the Planeswalker’s plan. Expect a mix of removal, utility creatures, some ramp or card draw, and a mana base tuned to the color—though not perfectly tuned, because nobody wants a mono-red deck that only draws mountains and misfires on every turn.

### A booster pack from the same set
Yes, there’s a booster pack included. This is basically a small teaser trailer for the movie you’re about to watch. Open it, and you’ll get a glimpse of more fancy cards that could slot into your 60-card deck in a pinch—or at least distract your gaming group while you try to pretend you didn’t peek at the rare card you pulled. It’s not a full draft experience, but it’s a nice little incentive to upgrade your collection without feeling greedy.

### A basic land assortment and tokens
The deck comes with a starting mana base (lands) and some token cards so that you can actually play the creatures you pull without inventing your own cardboard forest on the kitchen table. There are no fancy sleeves in the box (if you’re lucky, you get a couple of basic land cards with art that would make your shelf jealous), but there’s enough to get you going in a casual game or two.

### A primer on the rules and a spine for upgrades

For returning players, it’s a great excuse to shuffle, smile, and see what the color identity can still do after years of nerfs and new mechanics.

## How the deck plays: a practical guide

Let’s move past the list of components and talk about what it’s like to actually play one of these decks. You’ll love it if you enjoy games that begin with a modicum of confidence and end with a heroic dramatic sigh as you realize you forgot to draw a basic land for the fourth time in a row.

### Core gameplay loop
1) You land the Planeswalker on or around turn 3, provided you draw a hand that doesn’t commit a cardinal sin: “no lands, no plays.” 2) Your Planeswalker starts doing something useful—burning away your opponent’s threats, sapping their resources, or simply looking fabulous on camera. 3) You supplement with a handful of creatures, removal, and utility spells to protect the Planeswalker and patch holes in your own strategy. 4) If the stars align and your deck’s little engine starts humming, you can keep applying pressure until your opponent concedes or your life total becomes a dainty decoration on a cake that is you… not quite there yet, but the dream is nice.

The actual feel is a little like learning to drive a car with a friendly autopilot: you’re not guaranteed victory, but you’re not guaranteed a traffic ticket either. It’s accessible, and there’s a satisfying rhythm to the turns when you start chaining effects and landing your Planeswalker’s ultimate (or at least a few decrees that feel like “boss mode”).

### Key strategies and pitfalls
- Strategy: Play to your Planeswalker’s strengths. If your Planeswalker leans into board presence, pepper your field with midrange threats to keep pressure up. If it leans into control or card advantage, lean on removal and draw effects to out-resource your opponent.
- Pitfall: The deck is built for beginner-friendly reliability, not for multi-turn, meme-worthy combos. If your glory-goal is to lock in an infinite loop, you’re probably going to be disappointed. The Planeswalker Deck is a “play well enough, then upgrade” product.
- Strategy: Learn when to defend versus when to pressure. There will be moments where you need to hold back to arrest the opponent’s big play instead of throwing all your creatures at them and watching your Planeswalker stare down a board wipe with a sigh that could melt a glacier.

### Upgrading on a budget
If you’re joining MTG with the Planeswalker Deck and want to push it beyond “the starter experience,” you don’t need a full new single-figure deck worth of cards. Here are a few low-cost upgrades that deliver big returns:
- Add a couple of fetch or shock lands (depending on your color pairing) to smooth your mana and improve your ability to cast spells on time.
- Include one or two removal spells that hit a wider range of targets; the deck tends to benefit from some extra disruption rather than just “throw more bodies at the problem.”
- Insert an extra copy of your Planeswalker’s best synergy cards, if they exist in the current set. You’ll thank yourself when you can reliably protect your Planeswalker or give it an extra push.

All of these changes can be implemented with minimal investment while drastically increasing your play experience. It’s a bit like upgrading a starter pizza with pepperoni and extra cheese: still pizza, but way better.

## Color-by-color insights and favorite cards

Even though the Planeswalker Decks share a common format, they shine differently depending on the color identity. Here’s a quick, spoiler-free snapshot of the flavor and the kind of plays you can expect from each deck:

- White Planeswalker Deck: Focus on resilience, damage prevention, and efficient small creatures. The plan is to set up a durable board state and wear your opponent down with steady, reliable threats.
- Blue Planeswalker Deck: A dash of card draw and counter magic with tempo. Expect puzzles and complications—it's less about brute force and more about making your opponent misplay by overvaluing a projected line.
- Black Planeswalker Deck: Removal-heavy, with some value engines and resource denial. The vibe is “grab the best of your opponent’s stuff, and force them to regret their life choices.”
- Red Planeswalker Deck: Aggressive tempo and burn. The deck aims to close games quickly and theatrically, sometimes at the expense of longer-term plan coherence.
- Green Planeswalker Deck: Ramp and big creatures with a comfortable mana curve. It’s a safety blanket for players who love big bodies and natural growth, plus the occasional trample for good measure.

The real joy in these decks is not that they’re perfect constructions; it’s that they’re a launch ramp for real decks. You’ll end the night with a better feel for what you want to pursue in the future—bigger, louder, flashier, or more subtle—and you’ll do so without spending an arm, a leg, and possibly your pride.

## Play night tips: maximizing the experience

- Start simple: Don’t try to craft a grand strategy on night one. Let your Planeswalker deck do the heavy lifting while you learn the basics of timing and card advantage.
- Play with friends, not against an oracle: If you’re teaching someone, consider keeping the matches casual with a clear teaching moment after each round.
- Use the booster for learning: That included booster pack is your first real taste test for how future cards slot into your deck. If you pull a spicy rare, consider how it could fit the Planeswalker’s theme. If not, it’s still a new card to discuss and ponder.
- Don’t fear sideboarding too much: If you’re playing with a group that’s into “serious” formats, you might want to consider a few sideboard tweaks after you’ve sat down for a couple of plays and learned your local meta.
- Track your mana base: The best upgrade is a smoother mana flow. If you’re consistently stuck on two lands or only drawing mountains like you’re trying to resurrect a fire pit, consider adding a couple of non-basic lands or adjusting your land counts for the color mix.
