## Practical Aspects

### Size and Placement
Rugs are not one-size-fits-all accessories; they are spatial scaffolding for a room. The Lost City Blue Geometric Rug is offered in several common sizes that fit a variety of layouts. The most popular choices are 5x8 and 6x9 for living rooms, with larger 8x10 options for open concept spaces. The geometry has the potential to create focal points in odd corners, which is exactly what we want when the cat wants to claim that corner as the new throne room.

### Rug Pad Considerations
A good rug pad is not optional; it is a necessary investment. The Lost City rug plays best with a medium-density rug pad that offers grip without creating a frigid, stiff feel. For wooden or tile floors, a 5 mm to 8 mm pad tends to be ideal; for thicker carpets, you might want to account for a little extra height. The pad reduces slippage, extends the life of the rug, and makes vacuuming less of a cardio workout. I tested the rug with both standard and extra-thick pads and found that the extra-thick pad accentuated the cushion while keeping the edges from curling. If you want to avoid the old rug sliding across the floor like a rebellious snail, the pad is the unsung hero in your decorating story.

### Maintenance and Cleaning
Maintenance is not glamorous, but it is the adult version of a quest log. Vacuum regularly to prevent dirt from embedding into the fibers. Use a vacuum with a gentle setting and a nozzle that is not too aggressive, otherwise you risk curling edges or fiber pulls. Spills should be blotted immediately with a clean cloth; do not rub, as that can push the spill into the weave and create a stubborn stain that resembles the chalk drawing from your kid's science fair project. For stains that refuse to yield, test a small inconspicuous area with a mild detergent solution. Do not use bleach on polypropylene-based carpets, and avoid hot water because it can cause color bleeding in dyed textiles. In actual playthrough, I found that most everyday stains vanish after a careful blot, a little patience, and a willingness to pretend you know what you’re doing.
