## How to Play: Core Rules with Paw Patrol Flair
This section explains how the Paw Patrol edition modifies UNO for a family audience. If you already know UNO, this will feel familiar with a few paw-sitive changes.

### Setup and objective
- Shuffle the deck and deal seven cards to each player to start. The top card of the draw pile is flipped to begin the discard pile.
- The objective remains the same: shed all your cards first. The twist is that certain cards trigger Paw Patrol style prompts that can influence the turn order or draw penalties.
- Paw Patrol prompts appear on designated cards. When you play such a card, you read the prompt aloud and apply the effect as described in the rules sheet. These prompts are designed to be simple enough for kids while still offering a dash of strategy for adults.

### Central actions and color matching
- Match a card by number or color, just like standard UNO. When you cannot, you draw a card and your turn ends.
- Special cards like Skip, Reverse, Draw Two, and Wild still exist, but some Paw Patrol themed prompts may substitute or augment their effect. For example, a mission card might skip the next player if a pup states a rescue in progress or force a color change to reflect a new mission color.
- The Wild and Wild Draw Four cards function as in classic UNO, but the rule sheet adds a friendly Paw Patrol twist that should be explained at the table before play begins to avoid confusion.

### Paw Patrol prompts and mission effects
- The mission prompts are short phrases that mimic the spirit of Paw Patrol missions. They can modify turn order, force an extra draw, or require players to skip their next turn if a certain condition is met.
- These prompts are designed to be quick and easy for kids to understand. The adult players may find them charmingly silly, especially when a pup reminds you that teamwork is key to saving the day.

### Endgame rules and house rules
- The endgame remains one card after you have a single card left; you shout UNO to signal your impending victory.
- If you forget to say UNO when down to one card, the standard rule applies: you draw two if caught. In Paw Patrol mode, an alternate penalty might be a short mission prompt that adds a bit of suspense rather than immediate punishment, which keeps the mood light and fun.
- House rules are common in family settings. The Paw Patrol edition encourages players to set a timer for quick rounds, which is a smart move for younger players who can lose focus after a long day at the lookout.

## Playing Tips and Strategies for All Ages
Even in a light family variant, there are tricks you can use to maximize your fun without turning the night into a demand for a conference on probability.

- Start with a simple plan: Do not overthink the early turns. The goal is to get a feel for which colors are in play and which numbers are on the desk. As the deck evolves, you can adjust your approach.
- Use the paw prompts strategically: Some prompts encourage you to change color or affect the turn order. Use these when you can disrupt a dominant player or when you have a favorable combination of cards in hand.
- Pay attention to your opponents' hand sizes: If players are close to finishing, you may want to hold onto a Wild or a Wild Draw Four for the tense final stretch rather than using them early.
- Communicate clearly with younger players: A quick explanation of a mission prompt can prevent confusion and promote a positive atmosphere at the table.
- Switch speeds for different groups: For younger players, keep rounds short and celebrate near wins. For older players, you can accelerate the pace with shorter breaks between rounds, which keeps the energy high and the table engaged.
