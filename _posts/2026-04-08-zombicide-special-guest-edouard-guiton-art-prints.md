## The artist behind the prints

Edouard Guiton is known for strong line work and dramatic silhouettes. In the Zombicide prints, the silhouettes are crisp, with a limited but effective color range. The design language is moody and cinematic, with a focus on negative space and dynamic composition. The character poses convey motion and tension, as if the undead are about to step out of the frame and into your living room.

In the prints, the undead are not just mobs; they are characters with personality. The use of color is restrained: deep blues, muted reds, charcoal blacks, and the occasional splash of neon to draw the eye to a focal point. The result is something that, when hung on a wall, can be mistaken for a scene from a noir graphic novel rather than a mere game supplement.

The production notes indicate that the prints use archival paper with a matte finish, chosen to avoid glare during long gaming sessions and to preserve color integrity when framed under LED lighting. The texture adds to the tactile experience of owning a piece rather than simply admiring a digital render.

## Print selection and design language

The prints cover a small subset of the game’s cast but feel representative of the larger world. Edouard Guiton uses a design language that emphasizes silhouette over detail. This choice makes prints easier to read from across a table, an important practical factor when players are moving models, rolling dice, and keeping track of multiple threats. The composition often places the main figure slightly off-center, creating a sense of impending motion that mirrors the pacing of a game turn.

The color palette is intelligent. It uses cool neutrals as a base and introduces a few warm accents to guide the viewer's eye. In practice, this means that when you glance at the wall between turns, your brain can latch onto a hero or zombie without feeling overwhelmed by the art. The prints manage to be bold without being loud—a tough balance that many game art pieces struggle to achieve.

## Player experience and display tips

There is no wrong way to display art prints, but there are better ways to maximize the impact at the table or in your game room. Here are a few practical tips to help you showcase Edouard Guiton inspired art without turning your living room into a gallery that smells faintly of glue and nostalgia.

- Frame choice matters: choose frames with UV-protective glass to safeguard the colors from aging and fading. The wall is a living canvas; frame it to complement the prints rather than fight with them.
- Lighting matters: warm LED lights help bring out the mood in the prints without washing them out. A dim lamp behind the viewer can create a dramatic glow that makes the silhouettes pop without creating glare on the wall.
- Thematic grouping: consider aligning the prints along a central axis with the game table on one side and a comfortable seating arrangement on the other. The prints then act as a visual narrative that guides the eye from one player to the next.
- Rotation and variation: if you have several prints, rotate them occasionally to maintain a sense of novelty. The same wall can feel fresh without new purchases every month.

### Display arrangements in smaller spaces

If you are working with a compact gaming room, you can create a micro-gallery on a narrow wall. Use a gallery rail or a simple set of mounted frames to hang a linear sequence of prints. The effect is a traveling show in your home that does not require a large footprint.
