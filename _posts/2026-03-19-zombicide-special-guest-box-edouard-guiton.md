## Box contents and first impressions
So what exactly is in this Special Guest Box? In short: a curated slice of Zombicide that feels like a crossover episode between a design studio and a tabletop war room. The box typically includes: two guest miniatures designed by Edouard Guiton (or themed around his aesthetic), a handful of new survivor and zombie sculpts, a set of unique gear and equipment cards, optional punchboard tokens, and a tactical scenario booklet that doubles as a love letter to both the game’s core engine and the designer’s personal style.

### The miniatures (the art you can actually use on the table)
Let’s talk statues, baby. The two survivor miniatures designed for this box push the line between characterful and functional with a sculpting approach that’s unmistakably Edouard’s: clean silhouettes, exaggerated poses, and a bit of retro-futuristic flair. They’re not just pretty faces; they’re practical tools for your tactical decisions. The primed areas capture face details well enough that you can read emotion in a sneer when a zombie groans its final effort. The sculpts are designed to be usable in a standard Zombicide setup, which means you won’t have to re-learn how to measure a haunted alley. If you’re into painting, the surface textures provide a forgiving but rewarding palette: you’ll see micro-textures on leather jackets, subtle scratches on metal, and the occasional symbol that hints at the designer’s signature motifs.

### The new zombie and special rules
The Special Guest Box doesn’t stop at pretty sculpts; it often introduces a couple of new zombie types that feel like they belong in a gritty music video rather than the usual warehouses. Expect at least one “soundwave” zombie that disorients players with a sonic motif card, and a tougher variant that acts as a mini-boss for mid-game skirmishes. The rules intro for these additions is usually concise but layered: you’ll find a few new keywords, a couple of synergy rules, and a numbered mini-rulebook that nudges you toward exploring a few new strategies rather than forcing you down one linear path.

### Gear cards and tokens
A typical guest box includes a handful of gear cards that match the designer’s aesthetic: modular tools, improvised weapons, and specialty gear that can turn a bad round into a clutch moment. The tokens mirror the vibe of Edouard’s world—sleek icons, a touch of gloss, and a few symbols that feel like Easter eggs if you look closely. The production quality here is a mid-to-high tier for a boxed set: punchy card stock, crisp artwork, and a consistent color palette that makes the whole box feel cohesive rather than a grab bag of random bits.

### The booklet and scenarios
The scenario booklet in a guest box is where the party gets real. You’ll find a few linked scenarios that tie back to Edouard’s design themes, plus some alternate objective formats that spice up repeat play. The writing tends to strike a playful tone—think designer diary meets game instruction manual—without diving into corner-case rule-maths that ruin a night of dungeon crawling. If you’ve run through standard Zombicide campaigns before, you’ll notice the booklet pushes you toward quick setup, tight balancing, and a nudge toward playing with a bit more personality than your average “kill all zombies” session.
