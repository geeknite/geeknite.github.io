## How to Play with 10 Players: The practical guide to you not losing your cool

Playing Exploding Kittens Party Pack with a big crew requires a few tweaks to the usual flow. Here’s a concise, practical guide to pulling off a smooth ten-person session without turning the living room into a war zone.

### Setup and pacing in a crowd
1) Pick a central proposer: appoint a player to manage turn order and draw piles. This tends to reduce table chatter and helps people know when to stop arguing about who’s next. 2) Shuffle thoroughly: the bigger the deck, the more dramatic the misreads. A good shuffle prevents predictable handouts and keeps people on their toes. 3) Define a soft timer: with ten players, rounds can stretch. A 30-second or 45-second per-turn limit is a fair compromise to keep momentum and reduce “I’m thinking” fatigue.

### Turn structure reimagined for ten
- On your turn, you may use a See the Future card to peek into the future and plan your network of chaos. This is the moment to implement a strategy that would make a chess grandmaster nervous—and you can do it without the chessboard.
- The core decision is: do you take the risk of drawing a card or use an attack/defuse combo to shift risk to someone else? In a bigger group, the math gets spicier. You’re statistically more likely to pass that bomb to someone else, which is the exact enabler for dramatic table talk and occasional “I’m not mad, I’m just disappointed” monologues.
- If a kitten explodes, the round continues with the remaining players. That is, unless you happen to get a second exploding kitten in which case… well, the explosion returns and someone else revels, while you slowly become a cautionary tale told around the dinner table next game night.

### Card interactions you’ll actually encounter
- Exploding Kitten cards: the central tension generator. When drawn, you’re on your last life unless you can Defuse with a Defuse card. In a 10-player game, you might hand off temporary immunity and watch the room melt with laughter.
- Defuse cards: rare highlights that save your bacon. Pro tip: saving Defuses for the late game is the savory strategy because late-game drama is where the glory lives.
- See the Future: this card let you peek at a handful of upcoming cards. With ten players, this can derail someone’s plan in glorious, dramatic fashion.
- Attack/Skip/Shuffle: a precise trio of tools that let you shift momentum, skip your current turn, or randomize the order. In ten-player mode, these cards become a social weapon more than a tactical one.
- Nope: the universal “cancel” card. Its power scales with the social dynamics of the table; the more players, the more dramatic the Nope battles become as everyone claims they “totally saw that coming.”

## Strategy, humor, and the social dynamics of chaos

A ten-player game is less about making perfect plays and more about managing social signals. The Party Pack thrives on the reactions, not just the moves. Here are a few takeaways that consistently improve the vibe without making you feel like a villain:

- Read the room, not just the cards. In big groups, intentions are often visible through facial trowels and eyebrow waggles. If you can detect when someone is about to unleash a chaotic attack, you can pre-empt, redirect, or simply enjoy the show.
- Let the chaos happen. The best moments aren’t the most efficient plays; they’re the moments when a player misreads a hype card, and everyone erupts in laughter. This is the game’s core appeal: a shared narrative that grows with every round.
- Build a tone that supports fun over dominance. In family nights, younger players may want to avoid harsh punitive plays; the Party Pack allows gentler, more inclusive moments (like passed cards or cooperative defenses) that still deliver the same adrenaline rush.
