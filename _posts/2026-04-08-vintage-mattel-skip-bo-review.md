## How Skip-Bo Works (A Quick Recap for People Who Forgot to Pay Attention in Math Class)
Skip-Bo is a race game. Your objective is simple on the surface: be the first to Empty Your Stock Pile. The box promises chaos, and chaos is what you’ll get once you realize that sequencing is less about perfect order and more about bluffing your way to a clean table photo before someone yells “Uno!” except there is no Uno here, just a pile of numbers and a lot of friendly sarcasm.

### The Core Mechanics
- Each player starts with a stock pile facedown. The top card of that pile is visible, but the rest stays a suspenseful mystery.
- On your turn, you draw up to five cards in your hand (or more, depending on how chaotic your life is that day).
- You may play cards from your hand and from your face-up discard piles to create sequences on the central build piles, advancing from 1 to 12.
- Skip-Bo wilds are the “wildcard” of the pack—the card that says, “I’m the boss. I’m whatever you need me to be, until I’m not.” They can stand in for any number in the sequence, which makes them incredibly useful and infuriatingly destabilizing at the same time.
- When you can’t play any cards, you discard and draw, hoping for the right combination to appear. The game isn’t a perfect model of problem-solving; it’s a social experiment in attention management and occasional despair when the last card in your stock pile refuses to cooperate.

The rule set in this vintage edition is lean enough to learn quickly but nuanced enough to feel satisfying for multiple play sessions. You’ll quickly discover that patience, timing, and a little bit of smug misdirection are the real currencies of the game.

### Turn Flow: A Day in the Life of a Skip-Bo Round
1) Draw phase: You draw up to five cards. One hand, several possibilities. 
2) Play phase: You attempt to place cards on the build piles in ascending order. You may also play from your hand to your own discard piles, which is a strategic option to keep your stockpile hidden behind a personal fortress of numbers.
3) End phase: If you’ve emptied your stock pile, you win. If not, you pass the turn and—this is crucial—watch your opponents panic as they realize you didn’t waste a single move.

In a real game, these steps turn into a rhythm that can feel like a lighthearted dance or a chaotic drum circle. You’ll find yourself plotting two or three steps ahead, while your friends try to salvage their own playlists of “almost there” cards. And yes, there will be that moment where a perfect series looks yours for the taking, only for a wild card to swoop in and rewrite the ending in less compassionate terms than a movie montage with the clock ticking down.
