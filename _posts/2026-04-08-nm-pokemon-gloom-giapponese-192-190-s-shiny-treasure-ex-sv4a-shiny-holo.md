## Condition and Near Mint Reality

When we label a card NM, we imply near perfection in corners, edges, surface, and centering. Real-world NM is a spectrum, and Japanese prints tend to hold up differently under light, thanks to slightly different stock, gloss, and printing tolerances compared to their English counterparts. For this Gloom, a true NM example would show minimal edge wear, sharp corner points, and a holo surface that looks almost wetsanded in its smoothness rather than scratched or fingerprinted. You want a clean surface with even centering; a hint of whitening on the corners is not a crime, but heavy whitening or scuffs definitely lowers the NM evaluation.

In practice, you’ll see a few common NM-ish realities on this card:

- Slight edge whitening along the very tip of some corners, often the result of long-term storage in a binder sleeve without rigid protection.
- A few micro-scratches on the holo surface, visible only under macro photography or direct overhead lighting.
- Slight misalignment in text and barcodes because, well, factory QC in the era of the print run was not the SSL-grade standard we demand today.

If you are hunting strictly for a flawless showcase piece, you’ll want to verify: are the holo patterns intact, is the surface smooth, and is there any significant whitening that would impact the overall visual appeal? The good news is that when you do find a truly NM specimen, the card shimmers with a life-like presence, as if Gloom herself walked out of the card and invited you to mispronounce her name in a public space.

## Market Trends and Pricing Realities

Market values for Japanese NM Gloom cards can be a rollercoaster. In the wild, the value is influenced by several factors:

- Language and region: Japanese prints often fetch a premium among collectors who appreciate the art direction and set-specific quirks.
- Stated condition: NM, near mint, is a moving target. A card labeled NM by a seller may show minor flaws that affect value once scrutinized with a macro lens.
- Holo pattern and scarcity: The SV4A Shiny Treasure subset has its own limited-run vibe that can push price upward compared to non-holo or non-shiny variants.
- Grading culture: Modern markets incorporate grading from services like PSA and BGS. A card that survives a 9 or 9.5 grade can realize a premium, but the older Japanese prints sometimes see variations in how graders apply standards.

## Visual Details and Surface Care Tips

The holo surface on this Gloom needs careful attention. Here are practical tips for preserving the luster while you enjoy the card:

- Store in a high-quality screw-down or binder sleeve with a backing board to prevent bent corners.
- Keep away from direct sunlight that can cause rainbow burn or yellowing of edges over years.
- If you photograph, use diffuse lighting to avoid blown-out reflections on the holo; macro photography helps reveal micro-scratches that might influence a buying decision.
- If you ever consider professional grading, only submit if the surface shows no heavy scratches, no whitening beyond typical edge wear, and centering falls within acceptable ranges for your chosen service.

Collectors often debate whether a card with a tiny whitening speck on a corner is still NM. The consensus in many communities is that a negligible whitening speck counts as minor, but more than a minor amount can push the grade down. It’s a personal threshold, but with Japanese prints, you’ll notice that many collectors tolerate a little corner wear if the holo remains pristine and the image remains captivating.

## Collectors’ Notes: Grading, Ambiguity, and Documentation

When you are collecting cards like this Gloom, you become both a detective and a curator. Documentation matters. You want clear photos showing the holo pattern, the edge coloration, and a shot of both the front and back to verify that the card has not been re-glossed or tampered with. In the world of vintage Japanese prints, some minor border impressions or factory marks can exist and still be considered NM by many buyers. It is essential to discuss with sellers and ask for high-resolution scans if you are buying online.

Here are a few questions to pose to a seller:

- Can you provide macro photos of the holo surface and corners?
- Are there any whitening spots or edge chips? If yes, where are they located?
- Is the card unaltered, with no recoloring or surface treatments?
- Does the card come with any authentic packaging or a certificate of authenticity for the SV4A set?

If you’re a buyer who loves numbers more than stories, a consistent grading standard is essential. A PSA or BGS grade for this card may push the price upward, but grading is not a guarantee of value in every market. The best approach is to compare raw NM examples to graded copies and decide what balance of risk and investment you’re comfortable with.
