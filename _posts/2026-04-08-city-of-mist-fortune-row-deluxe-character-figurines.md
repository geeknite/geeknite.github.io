## Introduction: A Tabletop Gift That Belongs on a Neon City Billboard
If you’ve ever wished your City of Mist sessions had the cinematic flair of a noir movie trailer, the Fortune Row Deluxe Character Figurines are here to deliver with a wink and a velvet-lined wooden crate. This isn’t just a set of miniatures; it’s a curated mini-world that sits on your table, ready to argue with your narrative and win the vinyl-club lottery of vibes. The Fortune Row represents a micro-slice of New Boston’s street-level mythos, where legends and ordinary folks collide at red lights, rain-slick alleys, and coffee shops that know too much. If your game night is missing a little swagger, this set might be the nudge it needed.

In this review, we’ll dive into what you actually get, how the figurines perform on the table, and whether the price tag makes sense for your campaign. Let’s roll dice and find out if Fortune Row is a treasure or a tasteful plastic detour.

## What You Get in the Box
### Box contents and scale
The Deluxe Fortune Row box arrives with a sturdy, glossy exterior and a weight that says, I’m here to stay through three campaigns and at least two house rules. Inside, you’ll find six character figurines, each standing around the common 28-32mm scale that the City of Mist community has learned to tolerate with grudging affection. The bases are round, half-obscured by shadows, or feature a tiny bit of urban debris—perfect for giving the impression of a character stepping out of a neon puddle and into your encounter map.

### Poses and personality on the tabletop
What makes Fortune Row feel special is not just the pose, but the storytelling energy each figure radiates. There’s a mix of dynamic action stances and quiet, cold-eyed stares that tell you “this person knows the city’s secrets and is probably wearing them like a trench coat.” The sculptors clearly studied film-noir reference material, street photography, and a few too many late-night shadow hops through alleyways. The result is a set that reads well in photos, streams beautifully in unlit corners of your gaming room, and still looks good when you inevitably drop one on the carpet and pretend it was part of the narrative all along.

## Sculpt, Detail, and Paint Quality
### Materials and construction
The figures use a solid resin (not cheap plastic) with a pleasing heft that prevents them from tipping over when you sneeze, which is nice if you’re prone to dramatic wind gusts during tense scenes. The resin takes paint well, with a crisp level of detail in facial features, clothing seams, and tiny accessories. The gloss accents on hats, cuffs, and jewelry catch the light in a way that makes your table feel haunted by prop designers—without the actual haunting, which is probably for the best.

### Paintwork and weathering
Paint jobs range from “paint-by-number noir” to “subtle, characterful weathering.” The standout pieces have crisp highlights and a few delicate weathering splatters that suggest years of rain and bad decisions in the city’s backstreets. There are occasional minor paint within lines issues (a stray highlight on a sleeve here, a lip line there), but nothing that would ruin a figure on a standard gaming table. It’s not a warhammer-level competition piece, but it’s certainly a model you’d be comfortable placing near the cinematic splash screens of your map.

### Sculpt fidelity and character expression
The expressions lean into feigned calm and the occasional glint of trouble—perfect for characters who carry the weight of a city’s myths. Some faces lean a touch caricature, which fits the game’s stylized tone. If you’re someone who wants hyper-realistic miniatures, you may feel a tinge of disappointment; if you’re here for the mood and the vibe, you’ll be pleasantly surprised. The silhouettes are readable even at a distance, which is important when you’re running a table where players keep leaning forward to inspect every tiny detail.

## Assembly, Modularity, and Customization Potential
Fortune Row is designed to be display-friendly out of the box, and you’ll notice minimal assembly requirements. Most figures arrive with integrated bases and a stable stance. If you’re the careful type who likes to pin pieces, you’ll find that the rhythm of gluing is gentle to you, not a gauntlet you must bolt through. A quick note: some models have delicate accessories (hats, scarves, and jewelry) that can be fragile if you’re a heavy-handed handler. Take your time, use a hobby knife for clean breaks, and consider a light enamel varnish to keep those accents from flaking.

Customization fans will find several avenues for personalization. The clean, slightly stylized design invites you to repaint in your preferred palette or to weather the outfits with a few washes for an aged, streetwise look. If you’re fond of basing, the plain bases let you craft your own urban diorama—a little street rubble here, a coffee cup there, a neon sign behind a figure’s shoulder blade, giving you a diorama that doubles as a narrative beat sheet.

## Bases, Display, and the Tablescape Experience
### Base design and stability
The base design emphasizes stability without sacrificing the dramatic tilt that gives a sense of motion. The figures don’t wobble when you nudge the table for dramatic emphasis, which is essential when your group’s air-doubling the importance of a crucial dice roll. If you’re playing a campaign with many vertical angles (loft balconies, fire escape stairs, rooftop getaways), you’ll appreciate the slightly wider bases that keep a character from disappearing behind a crucial prop or a sneaky player’s own coffee mug.

### Display options and photography tips
Because City of Mist is as much about storytelling as it is about dice, you’ll probably want to photograph your table for the ‘gram or your game-night stream. Fortune Row shines in front of a backlit scene or a neon-filter backdrop. For photos, place a small LED panel behind the figures to bring out the subtle metallic accents on jewelry and the reflective stripes on a trench coat. If you’re streaming, a quick overhead shot will reveal the crisp silhouettes without losing fidelity to the tiny facial details; a low-angle shot will emphasize the figures against a moody city backdrop, as if they’ve just stepped off a shadowy corner and into your audience’s feed.

## Value for Money: Pricing, Competition, and Use-Case Scenarios
### Pricing thoughts
Expect Fortune Row to sit in the mid-to-upper tier of City of Mist merchandise. It’s not a budget purchase, but it isn’t a vanity splurge either. The price reflects the sculpt quality, the resin material, and the packaging that makes it feel like an event rather than a random addition. If you collect City of Mist items or if your table needs a pop of visual storytelling, Fortune Row pays off in atmosphere more than in tactical advantage.

### Who should buy this set?
- New players who want a cinematic table presence without having to source a dozen individual minis.
- Veteran players who want a high-quality display piece that doubles as a narrative cue on the table.
- GMs who want to stage a “show” moment in a campaign, letting characters’ silhouettes tell the story at a glance.
- Painters and hobbyists who enjoy the challenge of a slightly stylized resin figure with enough surface detail to justify a good brush session.

### A note on compatibility
Fortune Row is designed to be used alongside City of Mist’s official assets and other third-party minis. The scale is friendly for standard 28-32mm setups, and the style meshes well with urban-noir aesthetics. If your campaign uses a more high-fantasy or cyberpunk tone, you might find that Fortune Row’s vibe anchors the atmosphere more effectively than your existing minis. If you’re unsure, try a test setup: a map, a single figure in the backline, and a dramatic lighting test. If you start to feel like the set belongs in your city’s mythos, you’ll know you’ve found your vibe.

## How It Plays: Narrative Utility on the Table
### Enhancing storytelling with visual cues
In City of Mist, the visuals matter as much as the mechanics. Fortune Row gives you a tangible set of archetypes to reference during a scene: who’s the street-smart fixer, who’s the mysterious informant, who’s the quiet enigma waiting for their moment. The figurines serve as a quick visual shorthand for players, reducing the need for long descriptive paraphrases and letting the table focus on interaction-driven storytelling. A well-placed figure on a corner just behind a struggling character can communicate tension, allegiance, or impending betrayals without a single line of dialogue.

### Campaign integration ideas
- Use Fortune Row to anchor a location’s mythos: each figure can represent a faction, a rumor, or a recurring obstacle in the neighborhood. Have city legends transcribed onto a backstory sheet, with dice outcomes that reveal how a legend influences a scene.
- Introduce a recurring non-player character (NPC) who embodies Fortune Row’s core traits. The figurines become Mysterious NPC tokens, guiding players through plot beats with a visual prompt rather than a written cue.
- Create a “crime-scene tableau” moment: you set up the figures in a dramatic composition, narrate what the audience sees, and then allow the players to interpret what happened, heightening suspense.

### Quick play tips
- Keep the figures visible to all players; use a dedicated display area or a shallow tray at the edge of the map to avoid clutter.
- When you want a dramatic reveal, a slow pan across the six figures as you narrate the scene sets the mood better than a monologue ever could.
- Use alternate paint schemes to differentiate factions in your game. A simple color-code (blue for informants, red for enforcers, etc.) helps new players understand relationships quickly.

If you own other deluxe minis or live in a market with many campaign-specific figurine sets, you’ll want to compare the Fortune Row to those. Here’s a quick mental checklist:
- Thematic fidelity: Fortune Row nails the urban-noir vibe with a modern-day occult twist. Other sets may lean into fantasy or sci-fi more heavily; Fortune Row sits squarely in the street-level mythos space.
- Assembly hassle: Fortune Row is relatively straightforward. Some rivals demand more glue, more pinning, more patience. If you’ve got the patience, more complex sets can offer greater display variety; if you want quick setup with mood, Fortune Row fits that bill.
- Value: In a price-competitive market, the balance of sculpt quality, material, and presentation weighs in Fortune Row’s favor for players who want a set that photographs well and plays nicely on a standard table.
