If you asked a shelf of geeky board-game fans to pick their favorite problem to solve, the pile of Kickstarter exclusives would win by a landslide. There’s something delicious about a figure that never quite hit the light of day, a tiny hero and a zombivor duo that snuck into the backer kit like a caffeine-fueled Easter egg. Today we dive into one particularly elusive specimen: the Unused Zombicide Season 1 Kickstarter Exclusive Survivor and its Zombivor counterpart, Bastian.

In the wild west of crowdfunding, Season 1 was a pioneer. It introduced fans to the idea of sold-out, backer-only content, sculpted minis that would haunt your display case with a mix of longing and envy, and an IP that somehow manages to be both goofy and grim at the same time. ## What is Zombicide Season 1, and Why a Kickstarter Exclusive?

Zombicide Season 1, released by Cool Mini or Not (CMON) and friends, is a cornerstone in modern board-game minis culture. It wasn’t the first zombie-flavored skirmish game, but it did something smart: it married cooperative gameplay with a modular, scalable system that encouraged players to mix and match survivors, trait cards, and a horde of walkers that would make even a software update nervous.

Season 1 introduced a core cast of survivors and their zombivor equivalents — archetypes that could breathe new life into the base game if you were lucky enough to get the exclusive variants. Kickstarter backers often got access to sculpts and play pieces not released to the general public, and the “Exclusive Survivor” and “Zombivor” variants became something of a badge of honor. The Bastian duo is one such badge: a survivor that never saw a full, formal retail release and a Zombivor that whispers “you could have had me if you funded early enough, friend.”

The term “unused” in the product’s label is both a blessing and a caution sign. It signals rarity, yes, but it also hints at a story: a kit that may have been printed in limited numbers, left in a stack, and then forgotten as the game line evolved. This is not a case study in perfect production; it’s a case study in the romance of almost-there content. And that romance, as we all know, can be expensive — and deeply satisfying when framed properly on a shelf.

## The Survivor: Bastian — A Character Study in Miniature Heroism

Bastian the Survivor — yes, that is a name strong enough to belong on a knight’s banner — is the kind of figure that makes you want to hold a tiny, metal-tipped umbrella and shout, “I will protect you, tiny village!” The sculpt exudes confidence: broad shoulders, a stance that says, “I’ve seen a horde of ice-cream-fueled zombies and I still have time to fix your broken fuse box.” The kit usually pairs Bastian with standard-issue Zombivor companions, turning the pair into a dynamic duo that looks every bit ready for a rooftop chase sequence on the way to a safehouse that’s probably long out of reach on the map.

From a painter’s perspective, Bastian is a dream and a dare. His armor plating isn’t just “gray metal” gray; it has nuances, scratches, and stories embedded in the sculpt. The headgear and goggles offer a fantastic play opportunity: you can paint them to be gleaming and reflective, or weather them to appear as if they’ve spent a winter in a meth lab of wind chills. The figure’s hands are sculpted to hold gear, which opens up the possibility of customizing his tool set with a knife, a flashlight, a pistol, or an improvised torch — all depending on your party’s flavor and your affinity for post-apocalyptic cosplay.

In the lore-adjacent sense, Bastian embodies the “everyman hero” archetype: a survivor who looks competent without being an ostentatious symbol of invincibility. He’s not Hitchhiker’s Guide to the Zombified Galaxy; he’s the guy who would drive the motorcycle if the apocalypse had a Sunday rush hour. The Zombivor version of Bastian — the undead variant that represents what would happen if he failed a critical check and became part of the territory’s challenge rating — offers a complementary vibe: decayed armor, a haunting eyes-and-sockets corpse-portrait, and a carryover of that stubborn survivor spark. It’s a stark reminder that in Zombicide, you don’t just fight zombies; you fight the idea that you could survive forever.

## The Zombivor Bastian: A Haunting Echo of Valor

Zombivor figures are a special kind of dual threat: they give you a zombie version of a survivor, which can be used in the game as a special unit or simply as a thematic collectible. Bastian’s Zombivor variant adds a layer of storytelling to the table: a survivor who is now a hulking, eerie remnant of his former life. The zombivor design typically features weathered textures, torn fabrics, and an aura that implies “this is what you become when the world forgets your name and your ammo.” It’s the moral of the story in miniature form: heroism persists, even when the body no longer does.

The sculpt often follows a consistent aesthetic with other Season 1 Zombivors, which means it can sit comfortably on a shelf next to its peers without clashing with modern sculpts. The challenge, of course, is that the exclusivity of the piece makes it rarer than a decent meme in a tech conference. For collectors, this is part of the appeal: the ability to present a pair that feels like a time capsule from the early Kickstarter era, a reminder of a moment when backers were trusting a tiny team to deliver a big, glamorous fantasy.

## Unboxing, Condition, and the “Unused” Dilemma

Let’s talk reality: if you’ve landed an unused Season 1 Kickstarter exclusive, you’re likely dealing with a figure that has sat in a box longer than a lot of modern gamers have played around with it. Packaging can show the signs of time: a little yellowing on the cardstock, possible minor scuffs on the clamshell, and the odd glue remnant that hints at a careful storage process rather than rough handling. The plastic miniatures themselves often emerge in pristine condition, with clean molds and minimal flash if you’re lucky — a rare occurrence but not impossible.

Maintenance for these pieces is straightforward but worth repeating. Wash the figures gently in warm soapy water to remove any mold-release residue that might hinder the paint job. A soft toothbrush can help scrub away residual sprues or flash. After washing, pat dry and let them air-dry completely before priming. Given the “unused” status, you’ll want to preserve the pristine look, so consider a high-quality primer and a base coat that won’t obscure the sculpt’s fine details. If you’re a purist, you might also store them in a display case with UV-filtering glass — not because the miniatures will fade in sunlight overnight, but because a museum-like atmosphere is part of the fun.

In terms of playing the game, the “Exclusive Survivor” and the “Zombivor” variants can be integrated depending on your group’s house rules. Some groups treat exclusives as generic survivors with special backstories; others assign them unique traits that reflect their Kickstarter status. The important thing is to maintain balance with your core set and ensure that everyone knows the kit’s provenance. The fun lies not in breaking the game’s economy but in weaving a narrative around your squad – a story where Bastian has seen more back alleys than most and still refuses to surrender.

## Accessibility, Market Value, and the Collector’s Dilemma

Let’s address the elephant in the post-apocalyptic room: market value. Unused Kickstarter exclusives tend to carry a premium, but that premium is a double-edged blade. On the one hand, you have a rare piece that can appreciate on the secondary market as backers who missed out become curious collectors. On the other hand, the longer a piece sits unsold in a shelf, the more risk you shoulder: potential yellowing of plastic, less resale demand as newer versions (and newer exclusives) replace the limelight, and the possibility of counterfeiters attempting to capitalize on the aura of rarity.

If you’re evaluating whether to buy or trade for the Bastian duo, consider these factors:

- Condition: An untouched seal is not always a guarantee of mint condition. If the kit has been stored in a damp space, you might see minor warping or moisture-induced discoloration. Check the sprue attachment points for any signs of stress, and inspect the base for any cracks.
- Completeness: Confirm that you have both the survivor and the zombivor versions, plus any original cards or tokens that came with the set. Some packaging may have lost accessories over the years, which can affect value or playability.
- Documentation: Some backers preserved the original Kickstarter materials, which can include backer numbers or “Kickstarter Exclusive” stamps. Retaining these materials can add a nice touch to your display. If you’re missing items, consider whether you’ll accept a replacement card or substitute pieces for display-only purposes.
- Authenticity: In a world where replica items drift around like parasitic scavengers, it’s wise to verify your source. Authentic exclusives typically carry a trustworthy provenance, and prices reflect this certainty. If a deal seems too good to be true, it probably is. Use reputable platforms and ask for close-up photos of the sculpts, packaging, and any serial numbers if available.

In terms of value, you’ll often see prices buoyed by the nostalgia factor. Season 1 exclusives aren’t just about the miniatures themselves; they’re about the era of crowdfunding that brought them to life. If your goal is to display rather than to game, the decision becomes more about aesthetics and conversation pieces than raw competitive value. For players who prioritize playability, you might weigh whether you’ll ever bring Bastian to the table again or if the duo will stay in the cabinet as a cinematic centerpiece.

In terms of practical play, you’ll want to ensure that base sizes and collision rules are clear on your table. If you’re a purist, you can attempt to track “which character” by backstory alone and use the exclusives as purely narrative tokens that don’t influence mechanics beyond their story aura.

## Display, Storage, and Long-Term Care

If you plan to keep Bastian and his zombivor in a display piece rather than a glorified coaster, here are a few practical tips:

- Use a display case with UV protection to slow down any potential color shift from light exposure.
- Store in a dust-free environment. A soft brush every couple of months keeps the miniature looking crisp and pristine.
- Consider a micro-diorama behind the figures to provide context without distracting from the minis themselves.
- Keep them away from heat sources. Plastic and resin can become brittle with extreme temperature changes.
- If you plan to re-base, do so carefully. It’s not uncommon for older sculpts to have base sockets that aren’t perfectly compatible with modern stands; a little sand, cheap basing materials, and a steady hand will do wonders.

For hobbyists who want to pair the display with narrative content, you can include small plaques describing the backstory you’ve written for Bastian (even if it’s entirely fan-made) to give visitors a sense of immersion. A tiny, well-placed blurb can upgrade a display from “nice miniatures” to “miniature theatre.”
