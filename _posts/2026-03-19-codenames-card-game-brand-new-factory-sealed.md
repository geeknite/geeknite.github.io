## How CODENAMES plays in a nutshell (for the uninitiated)
Codenames is a team‑based word association game that thrives on indirect communication. Two teams race to locate their own agents on a grid of word cards by guessing from one‑word clues given by their team’s spymaster. A successful clue points your team toward multiple related words on the grid, while a poor clue can help the other team or even blow up in your face with an assassin card causing immediate heartbreak.

Key roles:
- Spymaster: The clue giver. This person has to connect as many of their team’s words as possible with short, cunning hints.
- Field Operatives: The guessers. They use the spymaster’s clues to find the right cards, while also trying not to stumble into the assassins or opponents’ words.

The base gameplay feels like a tense game of word association meets team trivia. The container of words becomes a parallel universe where a single clue can unlock a river of connections, or plunge you into a swamp of impossible associations. And in the factory‑sealed version you own, you get the added joy that the deck’s old friends have not been mistreated by casual game night calories.

## Practical tips for a great CODENAMES night
- Start with two or three practice rounds. The first few rounds help your crew calibrate the pace and the types of clues they like.
- Set a friendly tone. No one needs a perfect memory for the word bank on the first night. It is okay to slow down and laugh at the occasional misinterpretation.
- Use a timer judiciously. If you are playing in a noisy venue or with chatty participants, a timer can keep things moving without turning into a test of endurance.
- Rotate spymaster roles. This not only spreads the burden but also exposes you to different clue strategies. A good spymaster can swing a round with a single, well‑chosen word.
- Consider house rules. The base rules are plenty robust, but adding a quick house rule about no repeating the same clue word helps avoid repetitive gags and, more importantly, reduces head‑scratching.
