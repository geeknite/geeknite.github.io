## Painting and Assembly Tips

### Preparation and primer

- Wash parts in mild soapy water to remove release residues before assembly. Dry completely.
- Prime in light grey or white to maximize contrast for metallics and bright colors. A mid-tone primer can work if you intend to keep a darker overall aesthetic.
- Pin and assemble any delicate joints; the cloak can be brittle when glued directly. Use a small drill or pin vise if you want maximum durability on the hip and cloak seam.

### Color ideas and palette

- Skin: a pale elven tone with a touch of blue-grey in shadows to convey cold sea air. A thin glaze of blue across the cheekbones can give the elf a haunting, otherworldly look.
- Hair: ash blonde or silver white with a cool highlight to reflect the moonlight on the deck. A subtle wash of sepia into the recesses helps define strands.
- Cloak: deep navy or emerald with purple highlights to imply magical influence or noble lineage. A gradient start at the hip and flow outward to the cloak tips can create a sense of movement when the model is on the table.
- Armor and sabre: metallic steel with a darker shade in the crevices and a bright edge highlight. You can add a touch of NMM (non-metallic metal) on small areas for a flash of style without turning the model into a metal scarecrow.
- Base: weathered deck plank or storm-tossed stone depending on your scene. Dry brushing with a lighter colour on the raised edges can create the illusion of worn boards and salt spray.

### Step-by-step painting outline

1) Base coat the skin with a pale, cool tone. 2) Apply a mid-tone for the cloak and hair; block in shoulder and arm shading. 3) Paint the sabre with a base metallic and shade down the blade edge with a darker wash to simulate a sharpened, gleaming edge. 4) Highlight the hair with lighter greys to simulate light catching on the strands. 5) Add edge highlights to the cloak and cloak hem with a lighter hue to emphasize movement. 6) Apply a thin glaze to unify the model’s palette; a very light blue or green glaze can unify skin tone and hair while maintaining the cool, sea-weathered look. 7) Dry brush the base to bring out board textures and add a few darker shadow layers in the recesses of the deck for realism.

### Assembly tips for fidelity

- Align the hand and sabre carefully before gluing to avoid a bent blade look. A little dry fitting goes a long way.
- The cloak often hides seam lines; you can use wardrobe-like seam clean-up to blend the cloak into the torso seamlessly. A fine file or hobby knife can remove visible seams if you want a pristine finish.
- If you want extra dynamism, consider bending the cloak slightly with warm water to give it an extra wave on the final painting dip.

## Paint Guide: A Summary for Beginners

If you are a beginner painter, this model is an excellent practice piece for learning how to handle contrasting fabrics and metallics without getting overwhelmed. The cloak offers a great canvas for gradient work or two-tone shading. The face provides a tiny yet satisfying focal point that can be rewarding with careful eye painting. The sword introduces metallic edge highlighting without requiring advanced NMM techniques right away.

- Start with a clean primer and a broad base coat for skin, hair, cloak, and weapons.
- Add shading with a mid-tone wash on the cloak and skin; aim for a cool-toned wash to emphasize the elven winter light.
- Work the metallics with edge highlights to keep the blade readable on the table, then apply a tempered wash to the blade to simulate wear.
- Finish with a splash of color on the base to tie the miniature into your campaign’s scenery. A little salt spray on the deck can be painted as a few pale specks to simulate sea spray without making the base too busy.
