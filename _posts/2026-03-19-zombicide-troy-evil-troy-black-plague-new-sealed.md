## Overview
Zombicide in Black Plague is a glorious set piece of cardboard, plastic, and fantasy warfare. The addition of Troy and Evil Troy brings a slice of mythical drama to the zombie apocalypse that somehow fits with the tongue-in-cheek humor of Geeknite. This copy is new and sealed, meaning the plastic wrap still clings to the box like a clingy ghoul. If you are a collector or a retail patient, new and sealed is the dream of every polyhedral heart.

## What's in the box
The main attraction is two premium miniatures, Troy and Evil Troy, sculpted with exaggerated heroism and a dash of villainy. The miniatures come with glossy finishes and dynamic poses that look crisp on the table and dramatic in photographs. Alongside the peacocking heroes are new character cards that bring unique abilities to the Black Plague base game. You also get tiles or tokens to integrate into the campaigns, plus a rulebook update that explains how these characters interact with the existing survivors and horrors. Yes, there are also a couple of dice and standees to keep things lively.

## The miniatures and sculpts
The Troy figure is a muscular archetype that would make a centaur jealous, with a cloak that suggests he’s about to lead a charge into a dragon’s mouth. Evil Troy, by contrast, wears a more skeletal grin and a cloak that looks suspiciously like it was knitted by a necromancer. The detail on both miniatures is strong enough to make you want to paint them—although you should resist until after you have learned to survive your first 3 missions.

### Quality of components
The quality of the rest of the components matches expectations for a premium expansion: thick plastic base stands, readable tokens, and well-illustrated cards that avoid the trap of being too busy to understand at a glance. The color palette ties in with Black Plague but gives the new characters enough contrast to shine on the tabletop. The box insert is robust enough to survive years of shelf rotations, which is essential if you own more expansions than you can fit on a table.

## How Troy and Evil Troy change the game
This is where the expansion earns its keep: new heroes alter the tempo, the threats, and the feel of the game. In co-op zombie scenarios, you want a handful of risk-takers who can step into breach moments without collapsing the entire party into a pile of frustrated memes. Troy brings a brand of heroism that buffs adjacent survivors or triggers special effects when used with certain weapons. Evil Troy is the other side of the coin—he flips the vibe, turning a noble march into a suspicious, occasionally reckless sprint through a trap-laden dungeon.

The synergy with Black Plague is the highlight here. The medieval setting is already prone to moral ambiguity and heavy weaponry, and these two characters fit right into that style. They give you options, like enabling a bold rally cry or a calculated pivot when the board gets messy. If you know how to utilize their skills, you can clear objectives faster, face fewer undead spawns, or at least stack the deck for a dramatic finale.

### Rules and balancing
Balancing is always tricky in Zombicide expansions; the base game's zombie hordes can be ruthless, and an overpowered character can ruin the feel. Troy and Evil Troy sit at a comfortable middle ground for most campaigns. They are not overpowered enough to erase the challenge or trivialize the labors of your teammates. They reward smart play: focusing on positioning, reading the board state, and leveraging synergy with other survivors. You will still fail missions, often spectacularly, but when you succeed with a clever use of a new ability, you feel like a tactical genius instead of a cheat.

## Thematic notes
The Trojan myth plus zombie siege makes for a weird, wonderful blend that should be a hallmark of a certain style of geeky tabletop silliness. The art direction leans into grandiosity while maintaining the grim humor of a zombie apocalypse. The setting provides opportunities for roleplay moments that feel silly but in a good way. It is the kind of expansion that invites you to narrate an epic scene around a coffee-stained gaming table.

## Sealed status and value
New and sealed is a status both for sellers and players. For collectors, sealed boxes are a reliable investment, preserving not just the pieces but the potential for future resale value. For players, sealed status means you can open at your leisure, but you also have the option to keep it closed and pretend you are still evaluating your life choices. The value proposition here ties into how many campaigns you intend to run with Black Plague. If you love this edition and plan to run long-term co-op campaigns, Troy and Evil Troy add enough variety to justify the purchase.

## Artwork and content quality
For fans of photo-realistic miniatures, the two figures have a classical feel with modern touches. The shading on the flesh is well done; cloaks have movement suggested by sculpts; weapons are sharp enough to look dangerous on screen but not so delicate they require protective gloves during display. Card artwork continues the medieval theme with bold fonts and clear icons. The overall presentation aligns with what you expect from Guillotine Games: high-quality pieces, easily understood rules, and a ton of character on the board.

## Practical playthrough notes
- Setup time: expect a little longer than a standard base game thanks to the new cards and tiles, but the board organization is straightforward once you get the hang of it.
- Play time: you’ll be in the zone for 60 to 90 minutes per session depending on the scenario and the number of players.
- Learning curve: minor but present; read the quick start rules; then you can dive into a shorter mission to test your new heroes.
- Table presence: two new big miniatures with dynamic poses look great on display; they draw the eye and elevate the overall vibe.

### How to integrate with your current game
If you already own Zombicide Black Plague and some expansions, Troy and Evil Troy slot in with minimal friction. You can incorporate the new cards into your existing survivor deck, integrate the tiles into the scenario board, and use the two heroes as part of the standard crew or as part of a dedicated campaign. The expansion also scales well with the base game's difficulty, giving you more control over the pace of each session. The rules clearly explain how to combine the new elements with base game mechanics, and the combination feels coherent rather than forced.

## A few caveats
- Not every table will appreciate the over-the-top heroism of Troy and the darker comedic vibe of Evil Troy. If your group prefers grim realism, this expansion might feel a bit silly. If your group loves the dramatic romance of heroes and monsters, it will feel right at home.
- The scenic components, while top-tier, add to the cognitive load a little. You might need a second or two extra to orient yourself when you draw a new card or flip a combat token.
- If you expect a huge jump in difficulty, you may be pleasantly surprised by the balanced design. If you expect a gentle jump, you might want to tweak the board or introduce a custom house rule to dial in the challenge.

## Final verdict
Zombicide Black Plague with Troy and Evil Troy is a charming addition that respects the core experience while injecting a dose of mythic flavor. It does not radically reinvent the wheel; it adds two versatile survivors with strong synergy and a narrative thread that helps hold long campaigns together. It is ideal for players who want more replayability, more flavor, and a bit of cheeky humor to lighten the grim mood of the undead apocalypse. The model quality is superb, the rulebook is friendly enough for new players, and the promised new content actually delivers real value rather than filler. If you are a dedicated Zombicide fan or a collector who wants a sealed piece to show off on a shelf, this expansion is a solid choice. If you are new to the series, you may prefer to start with the base game and later step into expansions as your group grows more confident.
