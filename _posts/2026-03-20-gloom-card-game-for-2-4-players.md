Gloom is one of those tabletop oddities that looks simple on the surface and somehow ends up consuming your life in the most theatrical way possible. If your group enjoys storytelling, light cruelty, and pretending to be morally terrible to win, you’ll probably love this little box of tragedy. In its most distilled form, Gloom is a 2- to 4-player card game where you aim to make your family members as miserable as possible while keeping your opponents’ families blissfully cheerful. It’s part card game, part improv theatre, and 100 percent mood swing capital.

## Overview: What makes Gloom tick

Gloom’s core concept is elegant in its simplicity: you want your family to suffer as much as possible while the families of your opponents suffer less. The game’s irony is that the more your characters suffer, the more happy your opponents will be—until it’s your turn to reveal the inevitable doom that makes your family the gloomiest of all. The win condition is counterintuitive: you want the lowest total happiness in all players’ families when the round ends. Therefore, you’re juggling misfortunes with a flair for storytelling, and you’re doing it with translucent, almost ghostly cards that glow when stacked properly.

The box promises up to 4 players, but the room for narrative is large enough that a fifth player can join with some house rules if you’re feeling dramatic. Setup is quick: each player selects a family and draws a number of cards to begin the tale. The rest of the deck sits as the fate deck, and players take turns playing either a positive event or a negative misfortune on their own family or on others. The storytelling element is optional in the sense that you can play by pure math, but the game quickly devolves into a performance piece, and you’ll be heckling each other with the kind of humor that makes librarians roll their eyes and then smile because you’re actually good at storytelling.

This is not a game for those who want a rigid, puzzle-like exercise in strategy. Gloom is for players who want to craft a narrative and steer it toward a satisfying, theatrical doom. If you’re the kind of player who loves dramatic reveals, you’ll be rewarded with genuine laughs and a surprising amount of strategy hiding behind the laughter.

## How to play: a quick-start guide

Gloom isn’t a long-winded rules monologue. The core loop is straightforward enough that you can start playing after a single read-through. I’ll give you the lean version, followed by a few optional rules for those who want to layer in complexity.

### Setup
- Each player chooses a family and places the base character card on the table.
- Shuffle the misfortune and boon decks separately. Draw a small starting hand from each as dictated by the number of players (the exact numbers aren’t the world’s hardest math, but you’ll know the drill).
- Place the fate deck within reach.
- Decide who goes first with a roll-off or a dramatic hair-toss. The first player is the narrator of doom for round one.

### Turn structure (simple)
- On your turn, you play one card (either a misfortune on someone’s family or a boon on yours) to change the happiness score of the affected family. Each card has a number or a mood effect that alters the final tally.
- You may also draw a card from the fate deck if you want an event that shakes the table—these are the “plot twists” that can swing the doom factor in unexpected directions.
- After playing a card, you discard appropriately and end your turn. Play passes clockwise until the doom round ends.
- The round ends when all players have had two turns (for a compact game) or when the fate deck runs out (for a longer, more elaborate session).
- At the end of the round, score the happiness values on each family. The goal is to have the lowest total happiness in your family, i.e., the person whose family is the gloomiest wins the round.

### Endgame and scoring
Gloom uses a counterintuitive scoring method: happiness is a negative thing here. You want your family’s happiness to be as low as possible. It’s a game about implied doom and the theatre of misfortune, not about the most points on a sheet. The stakes matter, but what matters more is how well you weave your story through the cards—your narration can swing the mood in tiny ways that aren’t obvious from the card text alone.

If you’ve played the game before, you know that you can run out of misfortunes or boons quickly. The design encourages you to balance bad luck with clever storytelling so that the audience (your fellow players) buys into your tragic arc. When you’re the narrator, your pronunciation of “unhappiness” and your dramatic pause can be as powerful as any card effect. You’ll learn to pace reveals, to emphasize the emotional resonance of each misfortune, and to time your boons to create laugh-out-loud moments when someone else’s tragedic irony lands at just the right moment.

### Variant rules and house rules
- Quickstart duel: If you want a tighter game for a two-player session, you can reduce the number of misfortune cards and skip some of the longer fate events.
- Cooperative doom (a fun variant): Everyone collaborates to push a single family toward doom while other players try to derail with boons. It becomes a tense, comic megafish where the table becomes a chorus of petals and thorns.
- Narrative overlay: Some groups like to write short lines of narration on the cards as they place them. The effect is a living story where players trade lines and improvise endings in the moment. It’s optional, but it adds a ton to the experience.

## Thematic humor: why Gloom lands so well at geek tables

Gloom is funny because it presumes you’ll be a terrible human being for the cause of victory, and it rewards you for it. The humor comes from two places:
- The misery cards themselves: each one describes a ridiculous misfortune that is over-the-top but surprisingly specific. A character’s “suffering” card might involve a pet dachshund who misplaces a magical ring or a banker who loses interest in money because he’s allergic to coins. The more you read, the more you realize you’re in a black comedy written by a group of enthusiastic tabletop storytellers who have a taste for the dramatic.
- The storytelling ritual: as you add misfortunes and boons, you improvise a mini dossier of your family’s fate. The act of narrating how a stuffed bear ruined a character’s life or how a grandmother’s curse led to a life of mild misfortune is the social glue that makes the gameplay feel like a performative sketch rather than a mere card exchange.

The humor in Gloom is gentle cruelty: the kind that invites everyone to join in, not to mock any particular real person, but to celebrate the ridiculousness of a fictional family’s fate. That difference matters. It keeps the game accessible to players who normally avoid grimdark vibes and gives a stage for experimental humor that doesn’t rely on punching down or heavy sarcasm. If your table likes to riff and roast, you’ll find Gloom to be a surprisingly tolerant canvas for your most absurd ideas.

## Strategy and pacing: how to win without killing the vibe

Let’s be honest: you’re not going to out-serious-score most players with pure dice luck in Gloom. It’s about optimizing the order of reveals, the timing of misfortunes, and the art of persuasive storytelling. Here are some practical tips to level up your gloomsmanship without turning the game into a numbers spreadsheet:
- Timing is everything: try not to dump a big misfortune on your own family too early. Let the narrative percolate; a delayed doom often hits harder because you’ve built up the emotional context.
- Stack for drama, not just effect: a chronology of misfortunes that builds a coherent arc for a character makes your misery feel earned rather than random. Your audience will cheer (or at least groan) when they see the inevitable last-gasp tragedy that ties all the threads together.
- Play to your table’s tastes: if your group loves slapstick, emphasize silly misfortunes with bold narration. If your group loves noir, lean into mood, mystery, and the drips of misfortune that make a tragedy feel like a noir short story.
- Use boons to misdirect: boons aren’t just for healing or happiness—they can strategically shift how others perceive your doom narrative. Bold boons used at the right moment can make a misfortune land even harder when it finally lands.
- Don’t forget the aesthetics: part of the joy of Gloom is the physical act of placing a card on a card, watching the gloom become visible. Take your time with placement, and let the table savor the moment as a character’s fate becomes visible through layers of plastic.
