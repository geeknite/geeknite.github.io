## Design and Build

The dock features a compact, rectangular footprint with a matte black shell that resists fingerprints and has enough heft to stay put on a busy desk. The port location is sensible; most users will place the dock with the HP logo facing forward, allowing easy access to the front-facing USB-A ports for a mouse or a USB drive.

### Port layout at a glance

- Upstream: Thunderbolt 4/USB-C 40 Gbps
- Power delivery: up to 120 W for the connected laptop
- Display outputs: HDMI 2.0 and DisplayPort 1.4 (or two DisplayPorts depending on model)
- USB: multiple USB-A ports (USB 3.0/3.2) and USB-C ports for fast peripherals
- Networking: RJ-45 Ethernet
- Audio: 3.5 mm combo jack
- Dock health: LED indicators to show power and data activity

The layout is designed for easy cable management. You won’t have a tangled jungle under your desk unless you bring your entire home entertainment system to the office, which is a different problem altogether.

## Setup and Compatibility

Getting started with the HP Thunderbolt Dock G4 is straightforward. Here is a quick setup checklist to get you whooshing into a functioning workspace in minutes:

1) Place the dock on your desk and plug in the 120 W power brick. Ensure you connect the power brick to the dock first so the unit boots properly.
2) Connect your laptop via the TB4 upstream cable. If your laptop does not have a TB4 port, you may need an adapter or to confirm TB3 compatibility if HP confirms backward compatibility in your packaging.
3) Connect displays via HDMI and DP as supported by your GPU. If your laptop supports two independent displays through TB4, you may daisy-chain. If not, the HDMI/DP combination will do the trick.
4) Attach peripherals via USB-A and USB-C ports. Use the faster USB-C ports for external drives and the USB-A ports for a keyboard, mouse, and other accessories.
5) Turn on the laptop and the dock, then jump into Windows or macOS settings to ensure the monitor arrangement is correct and the dock shows up in the device manager as a connected TB4 device.

In our tests, setup was quick and painless. No driver drama, no mysterious device rejections, and no long ritual of patching or rebooting. The dock plays nice with a wide range of devices, including recent Windows laptops, Macs with TB4, and Linux boxes with TB4 support. If your OS is old enough to remember floppy disks, this dock may feel like a heroic leap forward—and you deserve a halo for upgrading your life.
