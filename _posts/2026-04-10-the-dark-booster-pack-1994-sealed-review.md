### H3: The art and card quality today
Old MTG cards aren’t just old because of their age; they’re old because they were printed with different standards, different inks, and different expectations. When you purchase a sealed The Dark booster today, you’re not guaranteed pristine photographic gloss; you’re buying historical artifact that may show minor printing quirks, color shifts, or areas of edge wear that happened in the manufacturing process or in storage. That is the kind of detail that separates a “great find” from a “nice-to-have-but-be-aware.”

### H2: Grading, authenticity, and the sealed debate
Grading sealed MTG products is a tricky business. The certification economy around sealed boxes and packs is smaller than that for modern cardboard or vintage single cards, but it’s real and it matters for some buyers. A “graded” sealed booster has to demonstrate that it’s untouched by human hands since it left the factory, which is a tall order for a product aging in a world full of climate swings and shipping mishaps. In practice, many collectors rely on seller reputation, packaging integrity, and honest product descriptions over formal grading when purchasing such vintage items.

Authenticity concerns are valid here. The market sometimes sees mislabeling, counterfeit wrappers, or misrepresented pack contents. If you’re investing in a sealed The Dark booster, ask for high-resolution photos of the entire pack, consider a short video showing the wrapper and the seal, and check whether the seller offers a return policy. And if you’re new, don’t gamble your mortgage on a single booster—start small, learn the lay of the land, then pursue larger asks with caution.

### H2: Storage and long-term care
Sealed or opened, long-term care is the difference between a collectible that ages like wine and one that ages like a cosmic meme. For sealed packs, store them away from sunlight, moisture, and temperature fluctuations.
