## How to Play (A Quick Rehash)
If you’ve played Codenames before, skim this and feel smug; if you haven’t, here’s the gist:

- Split into two teams: red and blue. One player from each team becomes the Spymaster; the rest are field operatives.
- A 5x5 grid of word cards is laid out. Each card represents a word that could be an enemy, a friend, or worse: a trap word.
- The Spymaster gives a one-word clue plus a number, e.g., “Animal 3,” indicating that there are three words on the grid related to that clue. The clue’s word must be unambiguous enough to steer your team away from the assassin and neutral words.
- Operatives discuss and guess the cards they think match the clue. If they pick a card of their own color, the turn continues; if they hit a neutral word or the opposing team’s word, the turn ends (or worse, they hit the assassin).
- The first team to guess all of their words wins. The other team boos a little, but then you do a rematch, maybe with a new score sheet, and the couch becomes a strategic planning pod again.

The fun lies in the tension: a well-placed clue can unlock a cascade of correct guesses; a terrible clue can derail a night and turn your living room into a tiny chaos engine. The Czech Edition’s polish helps keep this tension at a pleasant, story-driven level rather than a bored, “we’ve been sitting here for an hour” plateau.

## Setup, Setup, Setup: Getting to the Card Shake Fast
A good game night is half setup and half chaos. The Czech Edition makes setup easy:

- Shuffle the 25 card words and lay them face-up in a tidy 5x5 grid. The rest of the deck becomes the arena you’ll dip into for future rounds.
- Determine which team goes first and mark the Spymaster role accordingly. In a party setting, you might rotate Spymaster every round to keep things fresh and give everyone a chance to misinterpret a clue with style.
- The Spymaster draws the key card and places it where everyone can study the distribution (without peeking at the dead center, that would spoil the game). The distribution tells you how many words belong to red/blue and which ones are neutral or the assassin.

Pro tip for party nights: preface rounds with a goofy, themed clue or a silly restriction (for example, “All clues must be related to space opera clichés” or “No verbs allowed”). The vibe of the night improves dramatically when you throw in a small rule-bender to get the group laughing early.

## Strategy Notes: Clue-Giving and Guesses
A good clue in Codenames is a blend of precision and risk management. The “one word, N cards” formula becomes a small strategic puzzle about how far you’re willing to push the envelope. A few tips to up your game-night performance:

- Start with a broad clue if you see several red words that can be plausibly tied together. The risk is that your team might interpret the clue too broadly, but you’ll often land multiple hits in one go.
- When you’re behind, a riskier clue can pay off. If you sense your team is hungry for a clean sweep, you might push a bold angle that unifies disparate words under a single concept.
- Avoid classic pitfalls like clueing an assassin. The line between a brilliant clue and a disastrous one is often a hair’s breadth, and your Spymaster nerves will be tested accordingly.
- Use collaborative decoding: the power of a team is in the discussion. Embrace the conversation—every guess is a story, and the right line of reasoning can feel like solving a mini-mystery together.

The Czech Edition’s design helps minimize misreads by ensuring font clarity and word legibility, which translates into fewer mistakes caused by glare or fuzzy print. In a loud room, that counts for a lot, because your brain juice is precious and should be allocated to guessing, not squinting at tiny text.
