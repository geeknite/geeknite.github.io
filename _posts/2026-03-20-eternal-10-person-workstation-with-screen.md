## Setup Tips and Best Practices
- Start with a pilot before committing the entire office. A two‑week trial helps you identify interference patterns, cable spaghetti risks, and whether your team’s energy lines up with the central screen’s brightness levels.
- Create a seating matrix. Not everyone needs equal screen share; some roles benefit from prism-like views, others from direct proximity to the coffee pot. A one‑page seating plan can save dozens of post‑its later.
- Establish etiquette for the central display. Decide how you handle side discussions, who controls the screen during demos, and how to normalize the use of annotation tools so you don’t end up erasing your own work during a heated critique.
- Invest in good chairs. A ten‑seat table is only as good as the chairs supporting it. Ergonomics wins fights against fatigue when people stay productive long enough to finish a feature and not just scroll through notifications.
- Plan for maintenance. A desk this ambitious will require occasional TLC—dusting the vents, tightening a few screws, and updating the display drivers. It’s not a glamorous job, but it keeps the show running.
