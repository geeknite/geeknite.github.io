## How to Play

### Setup

- Lay out the magnetic grid on a flat surface. Make sure there is room to breathe, because you are about to move a dozen magnetized blocks around like you are playing tactile Tetris in zero gravity.
- Shuffle the puzzle cards and place them face down. You will draw one card to reveal the target configuration.
- The timer, if used, should be set to your chosen difficulty. Beginners may start with a longer timer, while veterans can pretend they are on a competition floor.

### Objective

The objective changes with each card. Some cards require you to assemble a specified shape using all pieces, other cards require you to place magnets to create symmetry or to route a path from start to finish. The magnet grid acts as a playground for spatial reasoning, while the pieces behave like tiny physics experiments that beg you to understand how magnets attract, repel, and occasionally behave like stubborn coworkers.

### Rules and flow

- You may reposition pieces freely as long as you do not detach magnets from the grid during a single turn. This guideline keeps your brain from turning into a pretzel labeled with a warning sign.
- If the pieces snap into the target configuration, you call out solved and move to the next card. If not, you can use hints, which will gradually reveal the next portion of the solution, a feature that makes Mag-Nif friendly for those late night puzzle sessions when your mind refuses to cooperate.
- If you are playing solo, the game becomes a mental marathon. If you are playing with friends or family, it shifts into a cooperative puzzle room challenge where people shout ideas like a chaotic but well-meaning committee of puzzle enthusiasts.

### If you get stuck

The built in hints help avoid the deep dive into frustration, which in our opinion is a critical feature for anything marketed as a brain teaser. There is a joy to gradually revealing a hint and realizing that even a tiny adjustment reveals the next piece of the puzzle. If you ever get truly stuck, step away for a few minutes and return with a new angle. Sometimes the grid just needs a fresh vantage point, like realizing your cat has taken over your chair during a very important Zoom call.

## Tips and Tricks from the Field

- Start slow and let the magnets guide your intuition. Do not fight gravity entirely; instead, use it to your advantage to reveal the solution path.

- Try explaining your approach out loud. Dictating your thought process helps you spot gaps and communicate strategies to others in your group.
- Keep the pieces on a soft mat to avoid losing tiny magnets under the couch crater or the mysterious black hole behind your bookshelf.
