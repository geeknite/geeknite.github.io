## Design and materials

In the Baraja Espanola, the suits are oros, copas, espadas, and bastos. The numbers 1 through 7 appear in each suit, along with face cards 10, 11, and 12. The artwork tends to be bold, with clean lines and a nod to historical card designs rather than modern minimalism. When two decks are in play, you may notice tiny differences between the two copies, especially if the printing batches differ. That is not a bug; it is a feature that lets you tell one deck from the other by feel alone, which is great for pranks and competitive shuffling.

Back designs on two-deck sets can be symmetrical, sometimes with a heraldic motif or a generic geometric pattern. The important thing is readability from a couple of feet away; you want the pips to be easy to count without needing a magnifying glass or a detective badge.

## How to shuffle two decks properly
Shuffling 80 cards is not rocket science, but it does require a method. Here is a practical approach that keeps the cards square, the curious players calm, and the chaos at bay.

1. Separate the two decks for a moment. Let them breathe; this is their time to shine.
2. Stack the two decks into one heap, aligning the edges for a neat pile of 80 cards. If you want to be fancy, you can perform a bridge-like overhand shuffle to bring the decks closer and reduce misfires.
3. Give the pile a solid shuffle. A few riffles followed by a few runs through the hand will do. Do not perform a single two-second shuffle and pretend you have a PhD in probability. You need a bit of chaos to break any stubborn order.
4. Cut the deck. You can have a volunteer cut the deck or simply perform a quick cut with two fingers. This step is a ritual that signals to the pack that the rules have changed.
5. Optional but recommended: a quick double cut by the dealer to ensure fairness for everyone. If you are playing with friends who cheat at rock paper scissors, this step becomes essential.
6. Deal with confidence. One thing to note is that dealing from an 80-card deck increases the chance of encountering duplicates in the opening hands. If that bothers you, consider removing duplicates by removing one of each pair before the game starts, or simply embrace the chaos and let the duplicates become a feature.

Two-deck shuffles can create a tiny shock at first, especially if you are used to smaller decks. The first time you deal with 80 cards, you might feel like a magician trying to conjure a rabbit out of a towel. With practice, you will be dealing hands faster than a vending machine can spit out a snack.

For those who enjoy precision, there is also the option to do a Faro-style shuffle on two decks. This requires more skill and space, but it yields a perfectly ordered deck that is still 80 cards. If you want to show off at a party, a well-executed Faro is a crowd-pleaser that earns you a few points of gift-giving status.

Baraja Espanola is famous for a handful of games that adapt beautifully to 2 decks. Here are a few popular options to consider.

- Brisca: a traditional Spanish card game typically played with 40 cards. Two decks allow for larger tables or longer sessions. Brisca uses trumps and clever play to accumulate points across rounds.

- Tute: a classic game with quick rounds and a focus on taking tricks. With two decks, you can run a Tute session with 4 to 6 players and enjoy a more dynamic dynamic.

- Rummy-style games: you can adapt classic Rummy games to 80 cards. Create two piles as a buffer and deal as in a standard Rummy. The duplication helps to create interesting melds and strategic hold.

- Big family games: two decks are great for parties. You can run a team-based game of memory or a simple find two matching cards mode to encourage conversation and laughter.
