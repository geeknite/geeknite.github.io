## Introduction
Saboteur is the kind of game that makes you realize you can be both a mastermind and a disaster within the span of a single whispered suggestion. At its core, Saboteur is a compact card game about dwarves tunneling toward treasure. Some players are miners, determined to carve a safe path from the start card to the treasure cards. Others are saboteurs, quietly plotting blockages, detours, and the occasional structural collapse. The twist that makes Saboteur sing is that you don’t know who’s who for most of the round. The only signpost you have is the way people talk, the speed at which they draw cards, and the suspicious smile when a Map card is played. It’s silly, it’s tense, and it’s exactly the kind of social chaos that makes game night unforgettable.

In Geeknite terms, Saboteur is not a heavy strategy title. It’s a social engine with a few dicey rules and a lot of room for improvisation. It rewards quick thinking, bluffing, and soft manipulation. It also rewards good hosting: keep the rounds moving, tease out the jokes, and don’t let one person monopolize the plot twists for too long. The result is a game night that feels like a mini sitcom—a group of friends improvising a heist together, complete with accidental missteps and triumphant laughter.

## What's in the box
The Saboteur box is compact, but it feels like a miniature warehouse of chaos. You’ll typically find:
- A deck of cards that forms the core of the game: path cards (straight segments, corner pieces, and branching tunnels), treasure cards, and various action cards that influence the board or players’ hands.
- Role cards that declare Miner or Saboteur. The exact number of each depends on player count, and the game is designed to keep you in the dark until the end.
- Gold nugget tokens that count toward winning points for the Miner team.
- The start card, which is the base from which all tunnels grow.
- A few indicators to help track the flow of the round.

The art direction is bright and playful, designed to keep the mood light even when someone tries to derail the tunnel system. The components are stout enough for repeated sessions, though you’ll likely sleeve your cards if you take the game seriously.

Visual note: sample cards show tunnels with doors and branching paths that beg to be opened—or sabotaged. The variety in the card art adds a lot to the mood, making Saboteur more than a simple engine of luck.

## How to play: Setup, flow, and a sample round
### Setup
1) Shuffle the role cards and deal one to each player, face down. Everyone peeks, then tucks the information away like a secret mint in a candy wrapper. The balance of miners and saboteurs shifts with player count, keeping each session fresh.
2) Shuffle the deck of path, treasure, and action cards into a single stack. Place the start card face up on the table.
3) Each player draws a starting hand. The draw-and-play rhythm begins here, and the table becomes a map of potential futures.

### Turn flow and playing a round
- On your turn, you draw a card, then play exactly one card from your hand. If you can place a path card that connects to the already laid-out tunnels and brings you closer to treasure, you do so. If not, you can drop an action card that causes mischief or delay.
- Path cards are the skeleton of the tunnel, while treasure cards are the heart of the objective. When a miner finally lays a complete route from the start to a treasure, they claim the treasure and the round ends with a chorus of cheers, some groans, and perhaps a triumphant dwarf beard-toss.
- Saboteurs have a slightly different objective: derail the route and create detours until the end of the round or until the miners give up and concede. Saboteurs win by sabotaging the route rather than maximizing treasure—though sometimes the two ends up overlapping in glorious chaos.

### End of round and scoring
A round ends when a path is completed from start to treasure, or the deck runs out. Miners tally the gold won, while saboteurs score points for proper sabotage that disrupted the miners’ plan. The exact scoring can vary by group, but the payoff is always the same: a satisfying sense of mischief and a table full of stories to tell later.

### Thematic flavor on the table
Saboteur’s call-and-response of trust and doubt makes a great host-friendly experience. It’s not about outsmarting a board; it’s about reading people with a sly grin and learning to forgive a bad path card because, hey, you can always pivot to another tunnel next turn.

## Mechanics: What makes Saboteur sing
Saboteur blends hidden roles with light spatial puzzle mechanics. You’re faced with a choice: invest in a path that might lead to gold or use an action to sabotage the route and create doubt about everyone else’s choices. The thrill comes from negotiating with players you barely know and discovering how little you can trust your own best hunch.

- Hidden roles create social tension: you know your own goal, but you don’t know who among your friends is conspiring against you.
- Simple, fast loops: rounds last around 20–40 minutes, which is just enough time to get a satisfying arc without wearing out the room.
- A strong party-game micro-structure: Saboteur works well as a warm-up or a cool-down title during longer game nights.

## Strategy and tips: How to stay sharp without losing your cool
- For miners: avoid committing to a single path too early. Versatility is your friend here; a flexible approach allows you to pivot when a saboteur drops a detour card or a map reveals a curveball. Prioritize routes that can branch toward multiple treasures so you’re not stuck following a doomed line.
- For saboteurs: plant seeds of doubt early. A bold early detour can cause others to question not just the route, but each other’s trustworthiness. The best saboteurs act like consultants for chaos: they look helpful, offer a plausible road, then nudge the party toward a dead end.
- Reading the table: watch for tells that aren’t obvious. A player who seems overly eager to toss a map card might be the one who wants you to believe their path is the right one. A quiet player who lingers on a corner card could be biding their time for a perfect moment to pistol-whip the route with a rockfall.
- Endgame awareness: as you approach the end, consider whether a move will reveal the saboteur or expose everyone’s plan to move in tandem. The last few rounds are where the real drama erupts, so don’t be afraid to go all-in with a bold bluff or a surgical detour that changes the entire table’s momentum.
