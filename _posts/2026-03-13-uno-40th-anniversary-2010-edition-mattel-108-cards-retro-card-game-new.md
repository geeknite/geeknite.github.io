## How to actually play with 108 cards

### Basics with a bigger deck

UNO is famously simple: match color or number, play special cards to disrupt opponents, and be the first to get rid of all cards. The 108 card edition keeps these core rules but increases variety. You will still shuffle, deal seven cards per player, and take turns in clockwise order. When your turn arrives, you can either place a matching color or number card or play a Wild to switch things up. The extra cards give you longer games and more opportunities to feel like a tactical mastermind before someone slams a Draw Two on you and ruins your day in two cards flat.

### Common house rules and how the 2010 edition handles them

The UNO ecosystem is famous for house rules, and this edition is no exception. People love calling out miscounts, disputes about color matching, and debates about whether a Wild counts as wild enough to override a misspoken color. The 2010 edition is friendly to these rules because its larger deck accommodates longer draws and more mid-game color shifts. If you want a quick rule refresher, here is a compact cheat sheet:

- Match color or number to play a card
- When you have one card left, you must declare UNO or draw a card if you forget
- Wild cards let you change color on your terms
- Draw Two cards punish the next player with two cards; stacking is a common house rule
- The more players and the larger deck, the longer the chain of events can get

Pro tip for new players: if you are teaching kids, pair this deck with a short rule explanation and a parental veto that you can invoke if someone tries to bend the rules like a pretzel at a gaming con.

### Strategy tips for a retro deck that actually feels modern

- Use the extra Draw cards to set up tribal color wars on the table. If you can force a color lane on three players in a row, you might tilt the table's mood in your favor.
- Save Wilds for crucial turns when you are down to your last two cards. This keeps you flexible and prevents your rivals from locking in a color you cannot follow.
- Pay attention to color distribution. In larger groups, some colors may disappear from circulation faster. If you notice a color is running thin, pivot to it earlier rather than late in the game.
