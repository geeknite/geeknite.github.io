### Steel: 7Cr13MoV
7Cr13MoV is the latticework of budget steel: decent hardness, good corrosion resistance, and a willingness to take an edge without too much drama. It’s the kind of steel that makes you believe you can handle a weekend project without a chimera of specialized tools. It won’t keep an edge for weeks on end under heavy use, but it will take and hold an edge long enough for most camping tasks, food prep, and general kit duty. For a lot of buyers, this is the practical sweet spot: affordable, repairable in the field, and easy to sharpen back to shaving readiness with basic stones or a ceramic rod.

If you’re curious about the chemistry, 7Cr13MoV forms a good balance of chromium for corrosion resistance, vanadium for hardness, and adequate carbon content for edge stability. ## Maintenance and Care
Maintenance matters more with budget steels than you might expect. 7Cr13MoV tolerates moisture and quick tasks well, but you should still wipe the blade after use, dry the knife, and apply a light coat of oil to protect the metal. Sharpening is accessible with common sharpening stones; you will likely find that a mid-grit stone followed by a fine stone or strop is enough to bring the edge back to a usable state without needing exotic sharpening rigs. The handle scales, if made of resin or G-10-type composite, respond well to a wipe-down and lightly oiling the metal fittings to prevent oxidation.

Store the knife in its sheath in a dry place, away from direct moisture and humidity, especially if you’re in a coastal or rainy environment. The last thing you want is a blade that looks bold in a photo but dull in a field test because of corrosion. A little care goes a long way with budget knives like this; treat it as a reliable tool rather than a prized antique, and you’ll get a lot of use out of it without worrying about every scratch.
