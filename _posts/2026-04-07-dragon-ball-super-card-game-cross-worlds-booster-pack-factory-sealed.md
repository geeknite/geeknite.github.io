## What's in the Factory Sealed Box
The term factory sealed means exactly what it sounds like: intact, untouched, and likely to be re-sealed by your own enthusiasm. In the Dragon Ball Super Card Game cosmos, a factory sealed booster pack usually refers to a boxed case that contains a full run of booster packs, each one jam-packed with 12 cards and one guaranteed rarity higher than common. The Cross Worlds line tweaks the usual distribution with new characters, new mechanics, and the occasional card that makes your eyes pop in a way that only foil can achieve.

In a typical Cross Worlds factory sealed scenario, you can expect:

- A sturdy outer carton that looks a little too glossy for its own good, as if all the holographic foil needed a summer tanning session.
- A slipcover or inner tray that keeps everything tidy while your heart does a small cardio routine every time you breathe in the packaging line.
- A set of booster packs, usually around 24 packs per box, each with 12 cards including a mix of commons, uncommons, rares, and one or more potential ultra rares or secret rares per box depending on your luck and how many times Bandai has told you to look at a rainbow foil.
- A few chases that collectors obsess over, which may include signature cards, alternate art variants, or special promo cards that show up in a way that feels very boss-like when you finally pull them.

From a packaging and presentation standpoint, Cross Worlds keeps things clean and visually striking. The box art tends to feature a dynamic collage of fighters from the set, some weaponry that looks suspiciously overpowered, and color palettes that scream you should prepaid-ship a full deck to your tournament buddy. It’s flashy, sure, but it also signals that you are about to commit to a hobby that rewards patience and mildly questionable impulse buys at 2 AM.

### Art and Card Design: Is It New, or Just Glossier?
Cross Worlds leans into the aesthetic we’ve come to love from Dragon Ball: bold characters, explosive energy effects, and foil embellishments that catch light the way a disco ball catches crowds. ## The Pull Rate Reality: Rarities, Foils, and Chase Cards
The real thrill of a factory sealed box is not the average pull; it’s the possibility of a holy grail card poking its head out from a sea of ordinary. Cross Worlds continues the tradition of having common cards that are just good enough to feel satisfying when you pull them and rares that make you consider redecorating your table to showcase that one card like a small, shiny trophy.

- Common cards: your bread and butter. These are the ones you’ll be trading away to fix your binder or to complete a playset for a budget deck.
- Uncommons: a step up, often with slight stat improvements or utility effects that enable interesting combos.
- Rares: the point where you feel the fragrance of potential meta-defining power. A few of these will slot nicely into midrange decks or competitive builds.
- Holographics and foils: the sparkle that distracts your gaming group from the fact that you forgot to bring dice. Foils add that neon energy that makes each card feel special, even if it’s not the one you were hunting.
- Secret and prism cards: the mythical beasts of a booster box. They exist to prove that you don’t need to be a magician to conjure something that makes you curse softly in delight.

Pull rates vary by distribution and box, but the sheer excitement of peeling back pack after pack and discovering a card that makes your collection feel complete is the heartbeat of this hobby. If your aim is a flawless tournament deck, you’ll want to pair your pulling with strategic deck-building knowledge and a bit of market-savvy budgeting. If you’re in it for the art and the vibe, you’ll enjoy the collect-them-all journey, even if you only end up with a binder full of beautiful foil boilerplate.

For those who want a more technical breakdown: we’ve seen Cross Worlds boxes deliver a balanced mix of the expected rarities across multiple boosters, with a few boxes skewing toward a handful of higher rarity pulls and a few underwhelming pulls here and there. This is the nature of booster distribution, and the fun of it is that you never truly know what you’ll get until you crack that seal. It’s chaos with a dash of order, and that’s basically life in a collector’s hobby.

## Assembling the Collection: Practical Advice for New Buyers
If you are new to the scene, you might be overwhelmed by the packaging, the jargon, and the sheer number of variants. Here are some practical tips to help you get started without surrendering your rent money:

- Start with one sealed box to sample the system. It gives you a sense of the pull rate, the feel of the card stock, and the vibe of the set when you’re building around a core mechanic.
- Join a local or online community. Trade cards with fellow players to complete playsets. The community aspect often adds to the enjoyment far more than any single card in your collection.
- Keep a rough budget. Cards that appeal visually can tempt you to chase the wrong type of rarity. Decide how much you want to invest per month and stick to it so you don’t wake up in 6 months with a wall of holo foil hiding your parking ticket collection.
- Document your pulls. A small binder checklist or a card-tracking app can help you see value over time and adjust your deck-building priorities.
