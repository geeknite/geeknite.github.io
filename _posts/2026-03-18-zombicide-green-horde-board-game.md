## Overview
Green Horde is a standalone Zombicide experience, meaning you can dive straight into this fantasy landscape without needing the base game or any expansions. The core idea remains the same: you are a group of survivors with unique abilities, navigating a map while waves of the undead—except here they are goblins, orcs, trolls, and various moss-covered nuisances—stomp their way toward you. The twist is that instead of zombies, you’re facing a bestiary that feels like a dungeon crawl exploded into a swamp party. The result is cooperative tactical combat with a story-forward flavor, where loot feels meaningful and missteps feel cinematic (and loudly dramatic).

Green Horde blends two big genres you often see fighting for shelf space: cooperative tactical combat and story-driven adventure. The board becomes a living map of paths, doors, barricades, and loot tokens that whisper, loudly, about your impending doom. The game balances tension with humor; you’ll be cursing your luck one moment and laughing as a goblin trips over an axe lodged in a crate the next. Chaos is the feature, not a bug, and you’ll be better for it.

### The twist that matters
Fantasy elements infiltrate the classic Zombicide rhythm. Expect new classes, equipment, and enemy types that behave differently than your typical walkers. Ranged goblins using cover, orcs delivering brick-laden blows, trolls with more hit points than your last fantasy football roster—these foes demand different tactics and a few more swear words per session. The core dice-and-armor mechanics remain familiar, but the flavor text and special abilities encourage experimentation and clever positioning. The result is a game that invites ambition without demanding a full RPG toolkit to play.

Green Horde uses its standalone premise to deliver a satisfying arc across scenarios, adding replayability without turning into a legacy slog.

## How to play: setup and rules at a glance
Zombicide’s core rhythm is still present here, but the fantasy setting nudges the pacing in interesting directions. Setup can be a tad meticulous if you insist on perfection, and you’ll want your game night to start with a few cheap jokes about goblin fashion before the map actually matters. The standalone nature keeps the learning curve friendly, allowing your group to dive into action without hunting for a base game box or a specific expansion.

The typical turn structure remains recognizable:
- Action Phase: Each survivor gets a set number of actions per turn (usually four). You can move, search, open doors, pick up items, or attack depending on your character and the loot appetite of the room.
- Spawn Phase: Enemies appear according to the current scenario’s spawn rules. Sometimes they arrive politely; other times they stage a surprise goblin party that ends with you tripping over a trigger token and the whole party laughing in the next room.
- Cleanup Phase: You check injuries, apply loot, and assess whether the horde is about to overrun you. The doom clock isn’t a stress toy; it’s a ticking reminder that you’re trying not to become a team of cautionary tales told in the tavern later.

What makes Green Horde approachable is the balance between simplicity and depth. The dice are manageable, the board readable, and the escalation gradual enough to keep the tension high without turning into a brain-scorching puzzle fortress. The mechanics reward planning and teamwork, but there’s still enough luck to keep every turn exciting—and occasionally horrifying in the best possible way.

The synergy shines when you realize that your rogue can exploit a trap set by your cleric while your fighter holds a doorway like a tired bouncer who refuses to yield. The joy isn’t just in killing goblins; it’s in orchestrating a little dance of moves, where a setup becomes a combo and the next room finally clears the way to loot glory.

## Strategy and tips for success
Here are practical notes to help your next session feel less chaotic and more epic:
- Prioritize visibility: Use terrain strategically. Narrow corridors and choke points turn goblins into speed bumps and give your ranged specialists time to contribute without being endlessly swarmed.
- Diversify your team: A balanced crew with melee, ranged, and support beats the wave more reliably than a single powerhouse build that forgets about positioning.
- Manage loot wisely: Not every loot token is worth a full-blown scavenger hunt. Some items shine later; others unlock critical combos for the next room. Plan your stash like a dwarven accountant who finally learned how to enjoy loot responsibly.
- Don’t ignore the spawns: Some scenarios punish you for ignoring spawn zones. Keep an eye on the map’s edges where goblins like to stage surprise parties.
- Communicate and coordinate: The best runs come from surgical teamwork—only to crumble spectacularly when the dice audition for a comedy roast.

### Common mistakes to avoid
- Overcommitting to one location: Tempting corridor, yes, but it becomes a trap if you ignore the rest of the map. A little split-pushing keeps the horde guessing.
- Hoarding gear with no plan: Pockets full of potions are great until you can’t move due to a 40-item inventory. Plan for a mid-game unload that actually helps.
- Ignoring terrain: The green horde loves terrain tricks. The board is not a backdrop; it’s a tool—learn to swing it in your favor.
- Underestimating the AI: The horde isn’t dumb; it’s unpredictable. Don’t assume safety until the dice confirm it.
