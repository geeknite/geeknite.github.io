- NM/M: This stands for Near Mint / Mint. In card collecting terms, you’re aiming for cards that show little to no edge wear, minimal surface flaws, and no creases. Most rare lots include pivotal support cards—things that enable your deck’s core engine. These cards often become the glue of a deck, enabling you to reach your game plan reliably even if you don’t pull the one “must-have” boss card.

Rares sometimes include boss-level characters or finisher cards that define the late game. In a non-foil lot, these cards can be crucial for skirmish viability but can be environment-dependent in value depending on whether your local meta supports them.

- Value consideration: Boss-level rares can be worth more if they fit into popular archetypes or enable widely played cards. If your lot contains even one or two strong boss rareshas, you’ve got a potential price anchor when evaluating total value.

### Rares with dual-use or multi-deck applicability

## Practical value and how to maximize the lot
Now that you have a sense of the categories, let’s talk about practical steps to maximize value and enjoyment from your 27-rare NM/M non-foil lot.

### Sorting strategy
- Separate by rarity and set origin: Keep track of which expansion each card came from. If you’re collecting, you’ll want to preserve the historical arc of the game’s evolution.
- Identify duplicates and potential upgrades: If you find multiple copies of a staple rare, you can compile a deck-ready subset or trade off extras.
- Check for misprints or condition quirks: Non-foil rares are forgiving, but you’ll still want to sort out any edge wear, whitening, corner bends, or surface scratches that might impact valuation.

### Storage and protection
- Sleeves and binders: For NM/M cards, use high-quality sleeves and archival binders. Consider a two-layer approach: inner Ultra Pro sleeves for protection, outer dragon-scale sleeves for theme and color coordination.
- Binders vs. boxes: If you’re a player, binders that lay-flat store well; if you’re a collector/trader, a labeled storage box with a card-count for each set helps organization.
- Temperature and humidity: DBS cards are plastic; they aren’t gold bars. Keep them in a cool, dry place to avoid warping or humidity-related issues.

### Grading and valuation basics
- Grading scales: Most casual collectors use a simple Near Mint (NM) to Lightly Played (LP) scale for non-foil rares. In a bulk lot, you’ll likely see a mix; separate margins for players and collectors will help with pricing.
- Market value: Non-foil rarities tend to hold value closer to the bulk buy price unless they’re widely playable or part of a sought-after set. Do a quick scan for any “must-have” synergy cards or staples that could drive demand.
- Documentation: If you’re reselling, provide clear photos, show the card on a white background for edges, and indicate your NM/M assessment honestly. Transparency sells more in the long run than a glossy but smug misrepresentation.
