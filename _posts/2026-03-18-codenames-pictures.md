Codenames: Pictures is the photogenic cousin of the spy-thriller that loves to get loud, colorful, and unexpectedly philosophical about a bunch of staring eyes and abstract shapes. If the original Codenames is a battle of wables and words, Pictures gives you a gallery wall and asks you to shout at it in a hopeful, slightly stressed way. For fans of the original who want more brains-per-minute and fewer SFW word choices, this game is a gift. For newcomers who think a deck of word cards is a sign of civilization, Pictures may feel like stepping into a museum where every painting eats your clues for lunch. Either way, it is impossible to ignore the gleeful chaos that emerges when people try to interpret a cartoon sheep in a circus tent as a clue to a robber in a trench coat.

Before we dive in, a reminder: visuals matter. In Codenames: Pictures, the images are the language, and your clue choices are syntax. Mastery comes from building shared meanings with your team and learning when to play the room more than the board. Now, onward to the review.

A look at the box, and you will instantly notice something different from the word version: the art is bold, punchy, and a little chaotic in a way that makes you feel like you’re about to walk into a gallery opening where stroke order is a life choice. The components are simple: two teams, a Spymaster per team, a grid of picture cards, and a set of clue cards with little hints and icons to jog the memory. There are no word banks to torment the English majors in the room; instead, you have a curated pool of images that range from cute to cryptic, from obvious to “is that a snail or a spaceship?” The deck has a good mix of domestic scenes, fantasy stuff, sci-fi nods, and a handful of chimeric creatures that look like they came from a time-traveling art class.

Notes on components:

- Picture grid: 5x5 cards, each with a distinct image. Some are straightforward; some are ambiguous enough to cause a minor existential crisis.
- Clue cards: Each card has a color-coded back (red or blue) and a number, just like in the word version. The number indicates how many images in the grid the clue applies to.
- Card backs and separators: The old familiar silhouette of tension sits in the air, because counting is a grand sport.
- Rule book: Short, punchy, and friendly to new players. It explains not only how to play but how to break the ice when your team insists that the sheep looks suspiciously like a ninja.

For those who crave a quick-start guide: set up a 5x5 grid, choose your color teams, pick your Spymasters, shuffle the clue deck, and decide who will go first. The rest is more or less the same as the original game with a lot more “what does this even mean?” moments in the room.

Where this game shines is in the social contract it creates. The picture cards require interpretation, and interpretation requires a shared cultural language. The more you know about your teammates, the easier it gets to thread those clues together. But here is the rub: the visual language is also the source of miscommunication. A single misinterpreted image can lead to a cascade of wrong guesses that turns a clean victory into a chaotic, cinematic moment—the kind of moment that makes everyone laugh so hard they forget the scoreboard exists.

Gameplay: how it flows and why it feels different

Codenames: Pictures follows the same high-level flow as its wordy cousin. Two teams, one Spymaster per team, a grid of 25 image cards, and a deck of clue cards. The Spymasters give clues that reference one or more images. The goal remains to find all your team’s images before the other team does. The twist is that clues must be numbers, and the numbers correspond to how many images on the board are being referenced. This is where the visual language comes into play. The Spymaster must craft clues that reliably point to multiple cards while avoiding inadvertently guiding the other team to their own cards. It’s a balancing act between precision and group psychology.

A typical round begins with the Spymaster peeking at their team’s color-mandated grid of images and selecting a clue. The clue could be literal or abstract, funny or serious, and often hinges on a shared past experience. The number on the clue card tells the teammates how many cards to look for. The team then debates, points, and guesses. If they reveal a card that belongs to their own color, it’s a success and the turn continues. If they hit an opponent’s card, that ends their turn and gives the other team a potential swing of momentum. If they reveal a neutral card, the turn ends but without the catastrophic consequences of hitting the wrong side. If they reveal the assassin card, the game ends immediately—because no one wants to be the person who accidentally ends the round by guessing the wrong image.

The fun comes from the tension between the Spymaster’s intent and the team’s interpretation. The best moments are when a clue ties together two or three images in a way that feels almost obvious—until it isn’t. Then the room erupts in discussion, laughter, and a series of humorous “oh, that’s what you meant” revelations. And yes, there will be rounds where a single ambiguous image becomes an entire arc in a dramatic story told aloud by the team as they guess, with wild hand gestures and a soundtrack of internal monologues.

The humor is not low-stakes; it’s the currency of party games. People will misinterpret, misremember, and mispronounce, all in the name of speed and hilarity. Codenames: Pictures rewards teams that can pivot quickly when a clue flops and can pivot even faster when a clue unexpectedly nails five images in one go. It’s a game that thrives on misdirection and collaborative interpretation, the same elements that have made the original a modern classic, but repackaged into a visual language that makes some players feel like they’ve entered a gallery where every caption is an opportunity for a pun.

Clue design and strategy: threads to pull without tearing the room apart

Like any game in the Codenames family, the art of clue giving in Pictures lives on the edge of cleverness and risk. Some players rely on broad cultural tropes—movies, books, and memes—to hit multiple cards with a single clue. Others prefer precise, image-specific cues that point to close relationships between scenes. Neither approach guarantees success, but both generate momentum and flow. Here are some practical ideas to expand your toolbox:

- Build a visual story: Instead of aiming for multiple exact matches, think in terms of a narrative that stitches a few cards together. A clue like “Escape” might pair a space pirate, a runaway train, and a cartoon calendar—if your team can connect those images, you’re in business.
- Use non-obvious connections: The best clues in Pictures often ride on a lens that makes players think of a category rather than a single object. Think “Dreams,” “Nightlife,” or “Adventure.” The trick is to ensure your team can follow the thread.
- Mind your boundaries: The assassin card is a universal calamity. While it can be a tempting risk in high-stakes rounds, it’s usually better to avoid a trap that kills the round unless you’re confident you can thread a perfect needle.
- Respect the room’s tempo: If your group enjoys quick turns with rapid-fire guesses, you’ll want snappier, simpler clues. If your gang loves extended debates and deep dives, more complex clues are welcome, but be mindful of the session’s time budget.

The dynamic is further enriched by the fact that Pictures, despite being visually rich, still rewards sober communication. The Spymaster’s job is to select a clue that reduces ambiguity, while the team’s job is to interpret that clue with a shared grammar of images. The more you play, the more you’ll discover how your own circle communicates. It’s both a party game and a memory exercise, a social experiment and a light strategy game all at once.

Image-driven play and accessibility: who this is for and who it isn’t

Codenames: Pictures leans into universal human experiences: recognition, memory, and the capacity to draw connections. The images are easily comprehensible to most players, and even those with limited vocabulary can contribute meaningful guesses through shared cultural anchors. However, the game is not immune to accessibility concerns: some images may be culturally specific, and a certain cohort may struggle to identify a reference that others take for granted. If you have a mixed group with varied backgrounds, it helps to rotate Spymaster assignments and to mix in images that are more universal with those that are more niche. The best sessions tend to emerge when players acknowledge these differences with good humor rather than frustration.

In the end, Codenames: Pictures is a social experience above all else. It rewards teamwork, quick thinking, and the willingness to laugh at yourself when your clue turns into a mammoth inside joke. The pictures give you a shared language, and the room gives you a shared memory. The more you invest in that shared language, the more your team will rise to the challenge—and the more entertained your audience (the bystanders who aren’t actively guessing) will be.

The art of clueing: a few design notes and tips

Codenames: Pictures is at its best when the Spymaster plays with a clear, discernible strategy that translates across the group. Here are a few practical takeaways for your next session:

- Start with broad but not reckless clues. A clue like Visual 3 invites players to scan for a trio of related images rather than chasing a single image that might be wrongly interpreted.
- Use categories that the room shares. Pop culture, space, animals, or motion themes tend to perform well because they trigger a common repository of mental associations.
- Embrace the chaotic flexibility. The board changes every round, and with it your clues must adapt. You’ll be surprised at how a clue that felt perfect on turn two becomes a liability if a new image is drawn on turn four that makes your reference ambiguous.
- Keep track of near-misses. If your team frequently misinterprets a particular image, you can adjust future clues to steer away from that line of thinking or reframe your approach.
- Respect the assassin. The risk of accidentally guiding your own team into a trap is a central tension in any Codenames game. There are moments where a calculated risk pays off; there are moments where it becomes a cautionary tale your group will retell with gusto for the next few weeks.

Comparisons: Pictures vs the original code names word game

Both games share DNA: a Spymaster, a deck of clues, a grid of cards, and the pressure to avoid the assassin. The differences are more than cosmetic:

- Visual language vs word language: The original’s core is word associations; Pictures leans on image recognition and memory. This shifts some cognitive load from vocabulary to mental imagery.
- Ambiguity distribution: Word clues depend on common vocabulary that’s widely understood but can be misheard or misinterpreted in translation; image clues rely on shared experiences and the players’ cultural literacy. The balance of this ambiguity can either catalyze laughter or spark debates about whether a clue was fair.
- Accessibility for non-native speakers: Pictures can be friendlier for players who aren’t fluent in the host language, since images are often more universally recognizable than words. That said, cultural references can still create a barrier, so the same approach of inclusive prompts and patient sampling applies here, too.
- Replay value: Both games thrive on the human component—the players learning one another’s thinking styles. The difference here is that the image deck is large enough that you can rotate Scenarios quickly without seeing the same set of pictures too often. It helps freshen the game with new relationships and new laughs.
