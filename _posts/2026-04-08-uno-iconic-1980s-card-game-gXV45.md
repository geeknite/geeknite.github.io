The standard deck contains number cards in four colors, plus a handful of action cards such as Skip, Reverse, and Draw Two, with Wild and Wild Draw Four cards adding a dash of strategic mischief. The GXV45 edition preserves these, but the edition you remember might have a few twists tucked into its corners depending on the print run.

## Variants, House Rules, and The Joy of Tampering With Fate
One of UNO’s enduring gifts is the space it leaves for house rules. People love to customize the rules, sometimes to keep the game moving, sometimes to stir the pot with extra mischief. The GXV45 edition invited a few not-quite-official rules into the mix and then let players claim them with a shrug and a smile. Here are some of the most common variants that keep UNO fresh even for seasoned players:

- Progressive Draw: Instead of stacking Draw Two, when a Draw Two is played, the next person can either draw two or place a Draw Two themselves, creating a cascade magic that can turn a slow burn into an epic sprint to the end.
- Mix and Match Colors: Allow players to change color more than once in a single turn, as long as they pass the UNO call. This can transform a calm game into a fast-paced color carnival.
- Seven-O: A classic variant where if a 7 is played, the player to the left must trade hands; if a 0 is played, hands pass to the right. It creates a social training arc where players learn to pass the burden quickly.
- Stacking Wilds: Some groups allow stacking Wild cards to force the color change multiple times in a single round. This can lead to spectacular chaos, and also to a short but memorable display of shouting and eye-rolling in equal measure.

As with any rules hack, the joy lies in the shared consent of the table. UNO shines when players agree to a common set of twists and then commit to the silliness with gusto rather than rules-lawyer cynicism.
