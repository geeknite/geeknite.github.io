If you missed the original Dixit, Odyssey is the big sibling that says, “Yes, you can make this more ridiculous, and yes, we still expect you to win by persuading your friends to vote for your card.” Spoiler: you will.

## What is Dixit Odyssey?

Dixit Odyssey is a social deduction and storytelling game built around dreamlike, surreal art cards. On each turn, one player becomes the storyteller. They pick a clue—this can be a phrase, a line from a song, a memory, a painting-inspired metaphor, or even a joke that would make sense only to a very specific subset of the group. The goal is to craft a clue that will lead some, but not all, players to select the storyteller’s card from a hand of beautifully illustrated cards. If all players or none of the players guess the storyteller’s card, the storyteller loses points; if some players guess correctly, the storyteller earns points along with the players who guessed correctly. It’s a delicate balancing act of creativity, misdirection, and a willingness to look at a card that looks like a cloud-shaped cat and think, “Yes, that’s a cursor for a story about a cat and a comet.”

Dixit Odyssey expands on the original by introducing a board where players keep track of the scoring, a few new tokens, and a slightly different flow that helps groups glide from round to round with fewer hiccups. The artwork is the star of the show here: lush, whimsical, and intentionally open to interpretation. It’s not about “is this technically correct?”; it’s about “what story does this evoke, and can you sell it convincingly enough to your friends?” That’s storytelling with a tabletop brain twist.

## How to Play (The Quick Version)

If you’ve played Dixit before, you know the rhythm. If you haven’t, prepare to enter a world where you describe, guess, and occasionally argue about why your clue isn’t “that.” Here’s the basic flow, tuned for Odyssey’s slightly more modern design:

1) The storyteller looks at their hand of cards and secretly picks one that inspires a clue. The clue can be a word, a sentence, a memory, a smell—anything that evokes the card’s essence. They utter the clue aloud.
2) Each non-storyteller selects one of their cards that best matches the clue and passes it face down to the storyteller.
3) The storyteller shuffles the submitted cards and lays them out for everyone to see.
4) Players (including the storyteller) secretly vote for the card they think is the storyteller’s. The voting happens simultaneously, so there’s no telegraphing the answer with smirks or glances.
5) The cards are revealed. Points are awarded: the storyteller earns points if at least one person but not everyone guessed their card; the players who guessed correctly also earn points. If no one or everyone guesses the storyteller’s card, the storyteller earns fewer points.
6) A new round begins with a new storyteller. Rounds loop until you’ve exhausted the deck or the group declares, “Let’s do something else that sounds like this but with a different art style.”

Odyssey’s variant rules are gentle but meaningful. The scoring track is visible and intuitive, reducing the likelihood of “we’ve been playing for an hour and I don’t know who’s winning” moments. The art and the clues combine to create a surprisingly broad range of player experiences: you can lean into poetic, cryptic clues; you can rely on inside jokes; you can tell a simple, obvious story if you feel like it—and you can do all of this while the rest of the table debates whether your clue’s interpretation was art or chaos.

## The Fine Art of Clue Crafting

This is where Dixit Odyssey earns its keep. The storytelling mechanic is not about accuracy so much as persuasion. The best clues are not the most literal or the most clever; they’re the ones that strike a delicate balance between shared context and personal whimsy. The player who can thread the needle between “familiar enough to be guessed” and “eccentric enough to be questionable” usually wins.

- Prospective clues: single-word descriptors, abstract phrases, tiny personal anecdotes, a punny twist, or a cultural reference that everyone in the group will interpret differently.
- Pitfalls: clues that are too obvious, too obscure, or too niche for the table’s collective memory. The trick is to read the room—know which players are likely to vote for what kind of card and adjust your clue accordingly.

In a typical session, you’ll see a spectrum of clue quality. Some rounds feel like masterful storytelling performances; others devolve into a flurry of, “Is that a dragon or a teapot?” It’s all part of the charm. The beauty of Odyssey lies in turning misdirection into social glue. You’ll find yourself laughing at the excuses others come up with as to why your clue was “obviously mine,” even when you can barely justify your own reasoning.

## A Round-by-Round Walkthrough (Sample Round)

To give you a feel for the pacing and the vibe, here’s how a typical round might unfold in a 5-player game:

- Storyteller sits, surveys a hand of six cards, and chooses a card that captures a clue they want to share. They speak a clue aloud, something evocative like, “A dream of a lighthouse and a fox.”
- Each other player scours their own hand for a card that could fit the clue. They pick it and pass it face down to the storyteller.
- The storyteller shuffles the submitted cards, then lays them out face up for the table to view.
- All players secretly vote for the card they believe is the storyteller’s. The votes are revealed, and the storyteller’s card is identified.
- Scoring happens. If the majority is wrong, the storyteller earns points for the difficulty of the clue; players who guessed correctly share points. If too many players guessed correctly or no one did, the storyteller’s rewards adjust accordingly.
- A new round begins with the next storyteller.

In practice, this sequence creates moments of suspense, laughter, and occasional friendly debate about whether a clue was “clearly perfect” or “delightfully obscure.” The social dynamics—how we interpret art, how we persuade, how we gently bully the host into conceding a clever interpretation—are the beating heart of the experience.
