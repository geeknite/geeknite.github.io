Introduction
-----------
Gloom, at its core, is a love letter to melodrama, misfortune, and the joy of watching a character’s happiness drain away like a faulty coffee maker on Monday morning. The Deluxe Edition ratchets up the ambience: tinted translucent cards that look suspiciously like stained glass windows, a sturdier box that could double as a small weapon if you drop it on your toe, and enough flourish to make your table feel like it’s hosting a Regency-era séance for tabletop gamers.

If you’re new to Gloom, here’s the elevator pitch: each player controls a family of miserably tragic characters. Your goal is to accumulate as much gloom on your own family as possible while keeping your opponents' families as cheerful as possible. At the end of the game, the person with the least gloom on their characters (i.e., the most happiness) loses. It’s dark humor, it’s storytelling, and it’s surprisingly satisfying when you finally code-switch your in-law’s backstory into something delightfully dreadful.

Gameplay: How the Cards Tell a Story
-----------------------------------
Gloom revolves around two things: gloom cards and family cards. Gloom cards include both misfortune events and modifiers, and they’re played to the characters you control, increasing their gloom until the end of the game. The twist is that you want your family to be as gloomy as possible and other players’ families to be as happy as possible—paradoxically making your own die-hard gloom become a victory condition.

During a turn, you play gloom cards for your own family or for others, potentially in combination with twist cards that cause new rules to apply or chain reactions to occur. The stacked-faced cards create a little theatre of calamity. When you place gloom on a character, you narrate a short piece of melodrama to justify the misfortune: “Sir Percival’s teacup was cursed by a teetotaler ghost who forgot to RSVP to the parade.” The humor often comes from over-the-top storytelling and the way players try to outdo each other with pun-laden misfortunes.

One of the Deluxe Edition’s advantages is how the translucent gloom cards look when stacked: a rainbow of tragedy forming a silhouette around a pale family portrait. It’s an aesthetic you’ll want to show off to the group at least once; it becomes a bragging-rights moment that isn’t about numbers, but about the runaway theatricality of the night.

Strategy and Replayability
---------------------------
Let’s talk strategy in a game that’s as much about story as it is about scoring. The main trick is to balance short-term dramatic gains with long-term gloom accumulation. You don’t want to dump all gloom on your own family in the first round and then be stuck with nothing to do in the late game. The Deluxe Edition makes this a little easier with clearer art and better card visibility, helping you plan your turns and track how much gloom remains to accumulate.

- Early game: You’re testing waters. The thrill is in seeing how your opponents’ stories intersect with yours.
- Mid-game: The twists start to matter more. You begin to play for forced coincidences and narrative payoffs that maximize gloom without tipping the scale too early.
- Late game: It’s all about the denouement. This is when you orchestrate a final tableau that makes the audience (your fellow players) groan in theatrical frustration and chuckle at the same time.

If you want to study how to coax the most laughs and the most gloom, observe how other players layer gloom cards with restraint. The Deluxe Edition rewards listeners who let a story breathe, who wait for the perfect moment to reveal the card that flips a character’s fate, and who know when to nudge a rival’s character toward a comic tragedy that’s just too perfect to ignore.

If you’re a fan of “sit around the table and tell outlandish vignettes” rather than “rites of resource management,” Gloom: Deluxe Edition hits the sweet spot.

Installment: A Sample Round
---------------------------
Let’s walk through a short, illustrative sample to showcase the rhythm.
- Setup: Each player has a family of two to three characters. You deal gloom and twist cards and place your families in front of you.
- Turn 1: Player A draws a gloom card that says, “Cursed by the playlist of a haunted organ,” and attaches it to one of their characters. They narrate a short scene: the organist’s ghost travels through the manor, stealing the character’s favorite hat.
- Turn 2: Player B counters with a twist card that changes how gloom accrues—perhaps a card that makes gloom score double for any gloom placed on a character who wears a hat. They narrate a scene where hats become the symbol of doom in their own family, prompting a comedic exchange.
- Turn 3: Player C adds a gloom card that stacks with an existing one, creating a chain of misfortune that feels like a domino effect at a vintage music hall.
- End of round: Everyone compares their totals. The room erupts with groans and laughter; the room’s mood shifts because a table of adults just publicly acknowledged how ridiculous their characters have become.

The key is to enjoy the act of storytelling as much as the scoring itself. The Deluxe Edition’s visuals help you pull off more dramatic reveals, turning a simple card flip into a moment of theater that the whole table will remember—and retell—with increasing hyperbole in future sessions.
