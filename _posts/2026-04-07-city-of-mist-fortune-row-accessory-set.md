## Introduction
City of Mist has always thrived on mood over mechanics, on the scent of rain-washed neon and the whisper of a myth masquerading as a person. If you’ve played a session that begins with a pool cue clearly betraying a truth about a character, you know the city has a weird way of sneaking into your tabletop rhythm. The Fortune Row Accessory Set is the latest drop designed to dial up that noir-luxe atmosphere without overhauling your rulebooks. It’s not a full campaign supplement, and it’s not a random loot bin masquerading as a tabletop treasure. It’s a mood kit—props, prompts, and micro-scenes you can drop into your City of Mist table to pivot from “okay” to “oh wow, we’re in a mythic alleyway with a taste for trouble.”

To put it bluntly: Fortune Row is the kind of accessory that makes you want to run a one-shot tonight, then a longer arc next week, then a spin-off about a doomed gambler who can bend luck—just a little—before the city swallows him whole. If your table has ever whispered, hey, what if the city had a back alley with mood lighting, this set is your visual reference for that idea.

## What’s in the Box?
The Fortune Row Accessory Set is designed as a compact, self-contained mood engine. Here’s what you get when you crack the lid:

- 12 Fortune Cards: Each card is a compact prompt that can tilt a scene in new directions. They’re not “boss fights in a box”; they’re scene catalysts that push players toward truth reveals, new allegiances, or a twist in the myth behind a character.
- 20 Gambler’s Chips: Tokens that function as catalysts for social bargaining, clues you can spend, or glamour counters that track how close a myth is to being fully revealed.
- 1 Fold-out Fortune Row Map: A stylized, neon-tinted map of the district around Fortune Row, with a few landmarks that can anchor a session fast.
- 4 NPC Profiles: Quick-appearing NPCs with motivation, hooks, and micro-stat blocks to speed up GM prep.
- 6 Mood Tokens: They represent emotional atmospheres in the scene—Hope, Temptation, Panic, Resolve, Morality, and Awe—handy for signaling tonal shifts in the room.
- 2 Artifact Cards: Small macguffins that pose a bargain or a choice, and that can become central in a scene if players decide to chase a mythic price.
- 1 Quick-Start Rules Sheet: A one-page reminder for GMs on how to slot these elements into the core City of Mist rules without thumbing through the core book.

The content here is deliberately modular. It’s designed so you can pull any piece out for a quick scene or weave the whole thing into a longer arc. The design language is consistent with City of Mist’s mood-first approach: no need to fight through a mountain of new mechanics; instead, you get sharper prompts, richer atmosphere, and a handful of tools you can deploy with a GM’s instinct and a player’s curiosity.

## Design and Aesthetic: The Look That Pulls You In
Fortune Row leans hard into noir glamour with a neon twist. The art direction is a love letter to rainy streets reflecting a thousand lies and a single truth. The Fortune Cards use a typographic vibe that matches the City of Mist’s brand—clean lines, bold typography, and a color palette that feels like a neon heartbeat in the rain. The fold-out map is not merely decorative; it’s functional enough to anchor a scene or a chase. The NPC profiles carry just enough flavor to spark conversation without forcing a single path on the GM. The mood tokens are tactile, satisfying to slide around the table, and they psychologically reinforce the emotional color of each scene.

In short: Fortune Row looks and feels like a product made by people who love the game, know what the table needs to stay immersed, and want to help you skip the “what do we do now?” lurch and get straight to what matters—story, character, and the city’s mythopoetic oddness.

## How Fortune Row Fits in with City of Mist Mechanics
City of Mist isn’t a typical dice-heavy system; it’s built on the interplay of Mythos and Logos. Fortunes Row adds a dedicated spark to that engine without introducing heavy new rules. The 12 Fortune Cards are best treated as micro-scene prompts. When a card is drawn or revealed by a GM, it invites a GM-led complication that can nudge a scene toward a deeper truth or a reveal about a character link in Fortune Row. They can also push the session into a social or moral arena—exactly the kind of moment the game handles with narrative grace.

The Gambler’s Chips function as a flexible resource, which you can use for glamour counters, bargaining leverage, or as a currency that unlocks side-quests. The quick-start sheet includes a simple arc template (Scene Hook, Complication, Climax, Epilogue) that you can insert into a five-to-seven scene mini-arc. It’s not a replacement for the core rules, but it’s a superb accelerator for a tight session that wants to feel cinematic rather than procedural.

The map is the thing you’ll actually point at during play. It gives players a sense of place—the neon-lit, rain-soaked labyrinth that is Fortune Row—without forcing you to flip pages or read a dozen different sections. This is a specific tool for storytelling rather than a general-purpose puzzle box. The NPC profiles are handy anchors when a scene needs a human (or a myth) in play to bounce questions off of. And the Mood Tokens? They’re a subtle but effective reminder that City of Mist thrives on emotional stakes; the devices inside Fortune Row give you a way to track those stakes visually as the scene unfolds.

## Story Seeds: Quick-start Hook Ideas
Fortune Row isn’t designed to be a single, long arc; it’s a toolkit for quick, mood-forward storytelling. Here are three seeds you can lift directly into a session:

1) The Laughing Bell at Fortunes Row: A fortune-card prompt reveals that a key location holds a mythic debt—the Laughing Bell that rings only when someone lies to themselves. The players must decide whether to tell the truth and risk the city’s reaction, or to lie and risk waking a truth that won’t stay buried.

2) The Velvet Gargoyle’s Gaze: A statue in the map glows under the moon and reveals a secret—yet each reveal costs a Mood Token. The more players reveal truths, the more the row’s guardian becomes a living, shadowy partner in the investigation, guiding or guiding them wrong depending on the players’ choices.

3) The Price of a Wish: The Artifact Cards allow players to bargain with the Row’s guardian—everyone who wishes for something must pay a price in a scene that tests their values. It’s not a straightforward exchange: the price is emotionally or morally costly, not always financially. The set gives you a built-in structure for negotiating with a deity-like locale, turning a simple wish into a moral test.

These seeds can be used as stand-alone sessions or threaded into a longer arc. They demonstrate Fortune Row’s core strength: you can drop it into a campaign and immediately feel a stronger sense of place and mood, while maintaining the flexibility to let players carve their own paths through the myth.

## GM Tips and Best Practices: Running Fortune Row Smoothly
- Use Fortune Cards as scene accelerants. If a scene stalls, flip a card and watch the table pivot toward a new possibility. The cards are designed to prompt responses from players and to invite the GM to reveal a Truth—not simply to escalate a fight but to deepen a character’s mythic layer.
- Treat Gambler’s Chips as leverage. A single chip can compel a Truth reveal, a risky bargain, or a dramatic reveal when stakes are high. It’s a simple, tactile mechanic that reinforces the game’s negotiation-heavy tone.
- Word-of-mouth for mood: the Mood Tokens are a powerful storytelling cue. When you slide a token across the table, you’re signaling a tonal shift—the players pick that up quickly and adjust their approach. It creates a shared, physical language for mood and truth.
- Use the map for spatial pacing. A neat trick is to assign locations on the Fortune Row map to specific player questions. If someone asks where the next clue should be, point to a location on the map and tie it to a Fortune Card. The session becomes a dance of space and revelation, not a chase through abstract abstractions.
- NPCs as conversational hooks: Use the four NPC profiles as quick-start anchors for social scenes. They give you a concrete reason for each encounter and a natural way to escalate or resolve the scene through dialogue rather than combat.
