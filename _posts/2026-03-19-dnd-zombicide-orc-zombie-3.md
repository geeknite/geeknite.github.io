## Design and Aesthetics

### The Sculpt: Character in Hand
The Orc Zombie 3 is a character that refuses to quietly fade into the swampy undergrowth of the undead. The sculptex is alive with asymmetry: torn clothing, gnarly teeth, and a posture that suggests a zombie shrug—like it’s constantly saying, “I guess I’ll lurch this way.” The stance is mid-stride, which makes it a good candidate for dynamic on-table action. If you like a figure that telegraphs menace without needing a heroic pose, this one nails it. The Orc’s shoulder armor bears the dull gleam of rust rather than a glossy, heroic shine, which is a welcome change from many fantasy miniatures that look like they’ve just stepped out of a beauty salon.

From a scale perspective, the model aligns nicely with other 28mm figures you’d use in a D&D party. It doesn’t feel comically oversized, nor does it appear stubby next to a human fighter. This matters: if you’re building a murder squad or a single “boss” encounter, you want a model that sits at the same table as your carefully chosen PCs and NPCs without looking like a rumor in a chess game. The texture work—flesh, leather, and chainmail—reads well under a standard tabletop lighting setup and responds nicely to washes and edge-highlighting when you’re painting.

### Paintability and Material Quality
The material quality sits in that sweet spot where the plastic is durable but not overly brittle. That’s good news for hobbyists who like to put a lot of rough, sharp detail on a figure and then wash away some of the rough edges with a delightful corner of a brush. The armor plates and the tattered tunic present opportunities for shading techniques that can yield pleasing, cinematic results. If you’re into a darker palette, you can push the model toward a grimy, battle-worn look with a couple of glazes and some dry brushing. If you’re more into vibrant fantasy, you can still pull off a fierce, colored aesthetic that fits a hero strike with a bit of color blocking.

The sculpt contains enough micro-details to reward careful paint application, but not so much that it becomes a slog. The hood and cloak areas offer some playful shadow play that you can use to your advantage when you’re painting, especially if you enjoy a quick baseline layer then a heavy dry-brush on the edges to display grit and weathering.

### Base and Presentation
The base on this Orc Zombie is utilitarian, which is to say it does its job without stealing the show. It’s large enough to accommodate a scenic touch if you want to add rubble or a bone shard or two—perfect for a hangout spot on your dungeon floor. If you’re planning to use it on a grid map, you’ll be happy with a stable footprint that doesn’t wobble when you jot down damage numbers or roll for initiative. The design supports a fearsome presence without demanding you to commit to a heroic pose, which is exactly what you want if you’re leaning into a “horde of undead” vibe rather than a single scary boss.

### Painting Pathways: Quick Start Guide
- Basecoat: A desaturated green or a muddy brown will anchor the zombie’s skin tone and the armor’s weathering. If you’re going for a classic “undead orc” look, lean into green-grey tones for the skin with a cooler wash.
- Wash: A blue-grey or brown wash will carve out the cloth folds and armor rivets. A light glaze on the exposed flesh adds depth without muddying the highlights.
- Highlight: Edge-highlight raised areas with a pale alabaster or bone color. For armor plates, a cool silver or steel highlight will pop against a grimy base.
- Detailing: Paint the eyes with a blood-red or pale yellow to give that “watcher of catacombs” vibe. Add dark brown to the base to sell a ground scene that tells a story.
- Varnish: A matte varnish will preserve the gritty look without giving away too much gloss on bone and metal textures.

If you’re new to painting, this figure provides a good balance of texture and detail that won’t overwhelm your brush skills. If you’re a veteran, the model offers the right amount of challenge to test a new technique without driving you to the brink of despair.

### Painting Tips: Quick Techniques for Great Results
- Layering and glazing: Build a mid-tone on skin and armor, then glaze with a slightly darker shade to emphasize recesses.
- Leather textures: Use a dry brush on raised areas of leather straps to simulate worn, sun-bleached leather. A light edge highlight can sell the effect further.
- Cloth weathering: A very light stipple of brown over the cloak can simulate dirt and grime without over-darkening the piece.
- Bone and teeth: A bone color with a thin black wash can create the appearance of ancient gnashers with a hint of decay.
- Blood and gore: A subtle red wash on the wounds or torn tunic edges adds drama without going overboard.
