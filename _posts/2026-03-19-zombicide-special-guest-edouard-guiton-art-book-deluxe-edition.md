Guiton’s art rests on three pillars: silhouette readability, texture depth, and a sense of danger that feels immediate yet cinematic. Inside the pages you’ll find portraits that feel like they could speak and environments that push the boundary between ruin and illumination. Lighting choices matter: daylight shafts slice through broken skylights, while heavy shadows carve out a theater of menace. It’s film noir in a city overrun by the undead, which is exactly the vibe we crave on a Monday when the coffee is weak and the undead are not.

The palette tilts toward desaturated blues, ash-brown neutrals, and the occasional jolt of crimson or sickly green to flag danger. It is a language that communicates apocalypse with elegance, not just grim seriousness. And yes, the zombies deserve a credit sequence: hulking brutes, stitched coats, eyes that glow with a backstory of their own. Edouard’s creatures whisper a quiet dare: make me part of your world, and I’ll prove I belong on your table.
