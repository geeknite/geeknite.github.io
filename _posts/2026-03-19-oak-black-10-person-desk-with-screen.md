## Screen integration: what “with Screen” actually means

The Screen component is the most ambitious part of this desk, and it’s where a lot of open questions arise. There are two broad directions a buyer can take:

1) Screen as privacy/partition panels: These are acoustic or translucent panels that sit between the work zones to dampen chatter and reduce glare from central projectors or screens. They’re especially handy if your space doubles as a collaboration hub where people need to focus without being assaulted by a chorus of side conversations.

2) Screen as an integrated monitoring/merging hub: In higher-end configurations, you can opt for a central or per-station display arrangement—think a shared 55–65 inch screen in the middle, or a set of monitor arms that give each workstation a 24–32 inch display. This mode is excellent for teams that present demos, run standups with screen-sharing, and want a central hub for dashboards without collapsing into a tangle of laptop screens.

In practice, the best approach is to pick one or the other based on your team’s workflow. If you’re a software squad that loves to push builds and dashboards, go for the monitor-arm variant. If you’re more about deep work and collaboration in bursts, privacy panels can improve focus without sacrificing the room’s open feel.

We tested both flavors in our lab, and here are our impressions:

- Privacy panels: They cut ambient noise well and create a sense of personal space in a dense setup. Be prepared to rearrange if your team grows or shrinks, as the panels are modular but not disposable.
- Central/shared display: It’s convenient for quick demos and triage sessions, but you’ll want a robust HDMI/USB-C switch and a decent docking setup to keep cables tidy and accessible. If you rely on wireless presentation tools, you’ll still want a wired backup to avoid the “presenter’s screen black-out” moment.

## Ergonomics, comfort, and day-to-day usability

Ten people share a giant workspace, so ergonomic considerations aren’t optional putty jobs: they’re mission-critical. Here’s what we looked for:

- Height adjustability: The base is adjustable, which is crucial when different team members share the same desk. We found that a mid-range height (roughly 105 cm) worked well for mixed-stature teams. But the real win is the ability to set a comfortable minimum and maximum height and forget about it—until someone cryptically asks for a new height profile for a standing meeting.
- Keyboard and mouse accessibility: The surface is large enough to comfortably house keyboards, mice, and a few notepads, but you’ll want to ensure your keyboard trays or mats don’t cause interference with the main surface. We used a low-profile keyboard deck that slides in easily and avoids the common pitfall of banging shins on a protruding tray.
- Chair synergy: Because you’ll be sitting in a row next to nine co-workers, chair alignment matters. A uniform chair height helps maintain line-of-sight for those using any shared screen and ensures no one’s head is in front of a monitor, creating an accidental mullet-cam in your Zoom call.
- Cable management: The built-in troughs are helpful, but you’ll still have a tangle if you don’t lay out a plan for cables before you commit to the wiring. We recommend labeling every splitter and using color-coded zip ties to avoid a future “Who moved the HDMI switch now?” moment.
- Personal space: It’s tempting to treat this as a single long table, but the real magic happens when you create micro-zones for teams to huddle, brainstorm, and then step back for the daily standup. The result is a better balance between collaboration and concentration.

## Setup, assembly, and initial configuration

We tested two assembly windows:
- Baseline assembly without screen components: about 3–5 hours with two people. This includes aligning modules, fastening the frame, installing cable channels, and running a basic test of stability.
- Full configuration with screen panels and monitor arms: add another 1–2 hours for mounting monitors, calibrating arms, and wiring the central display. If you’re not comfortable with a drill in your hand, hire an experienced installer to avoid the “gnome on a ladders” moment.

Key assembly tips:
- Have a clean workspace: a 16-foot desk is a long surface, and you don’t want EDID trying to mirror your coffee stains.
- Use the right tools: besides the included hex keys, a magnetic screwdriver and a level are your friends. A partner can be indispensable. We cannot emphasize this enough.
- Plan the cable routes first: map your power, data, and display cables before you start fastening modules. It saves time and prevents the “spaghetti volcano” later.

Once assembled, the desk feels surprisingly solid for its size. The legs and cross‑members lock in with a satisfying click, and the cable channels do their best to disappear from view. It’s not a magic trick, but it’s close enough to make you feel like a productivity wizard when you finally press the Power button and nothing smokes (at least, not right away).

## Maintenance, care, and long-term durability

Oak veneer is beautiful but not invincible. Here are a few maintenance notes we gathered through the test:

- Cleaning: wipe with a soft microfiber cloth and a mild wood-safe cleaner. Avoid harsh solvents that could dull the veneer. A weekly light dusting helps keep the finish looking fresh.
- Scratch resistance: the surface can show light scratches if you’re reckless with your keyboard or if you allow metal items to slide across the surface in a hurry. Use a protective desk mat in heavy-use areas.
- Cable channels: keep them free of dust and lint; a quick microfiber wipe through the channels every month keeps them looking tidy and prevents snagging during reconfiguration.
- Monitors and arms: clean monitor arms and ensure the mounts are tightened periodically. A loose monitor is a disaster waiting to happen when someone leans into the screen during a debate about the best pizza topping.
