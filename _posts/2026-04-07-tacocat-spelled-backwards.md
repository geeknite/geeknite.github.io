## Setup and Flow

Setting up Tacocat Spelled Backwards is simple enough that you can teach a new player in under two minutes. Here is the quick breakdown:

1. Shuffle the deck and deal a starting hand to each player. The number of cards per hand depends on the player count, but you typically aim for a handful that allows a few misdirections before someone utters the wrong backwards word and triggers a chain of events.
2. Place the draw pile in the center and flip the top card to start the discard pile. The discard pile becomes a living archive of the mistakes and near misses that have occurred during the game.
3. Decide a starting player. In most groups, the organizer gets to start and the rest of you pretend this is a fair system that was decided by the ancient gods of board games.
4. The first round begins with a bid to either spell the word backwards or to draw a card and hope you survive the round with your dignity intact.

The flow of the game is a constant push and pull. You want to draw cards when your brain is already on tilt because that creates opportunities for others to misplay in spectacular fashion. Conversely, you want to play helpful spell cards when you need to stall or stall someone else, which is a delicate balance between heroism and petty sabotage. The pacing is quick; rounds typically last 10 to 20 minutes depending on the number of players and how dramatic the table wants to be with their spelling performances. This is not a game for precision strategists who demand a perfect plan; it is a game for chaos cultivators who thrive on social energy and the occasional groan from a word that should not exist in any language.
