The whole display assembly looks and feels premium without screaming luxury tax at you every time you unlock the machine.

## Display: Black WTE Screen

The focal point here is the Black WTE Screen. First rule of the WTE: don't ask what the acronym stands for; let the screen do the talking. The panel is described as deep-contrast, color-accurate, and capable of HDR if your content ecosystem can actually deliver it. In practice, you will notice the following:

- Deep blacks and a strong midtone range, which makes grayscale a joy and helps vector shading in 3D apps pop without resorting to a magnifying glass.
- Color accuracy that isn't just marketing; factory calibrations report Delta E values that stay impressively low across multiple regions. This means less grading fudge when you are pushing film-grade textures or color-correcting gameplay footage.
- A refresh rate that happily hustles at 120 Hz for those who need buttery motion and don't want to blink during a cutscene in your pipeline.
- HDR support, with Dolby Vision style tone-mapping potential and DisplayHDR-like peak brightness performance. For designers and video people, this matters because you are not showing just okay highlights; you are showing cinematic light. For gamers, it means scenes can be immersive without sacrificing clarity.

A quick note: while the Black WTE screen is ready for HDR content, you will want a properly graded pipeline and a PC with enough bandwidth to feed it. If you are rendering at 4K/120 with a heavy scene, a few bottlenecks will appear in the peripheral components before you finish your final frame.

## Hardware, Performance, and Upgrades

This is where the Infinite 10 earns its stripes, and perhaps a few scars from the upgrade path you will inevitably take.

- CPU options: The system ships with workstation-grade CPUs—think 12 to 64-core options, with configurations that skew toward Threadripper Pro or Xeon-grade silicon. Multithreaded workloads love this class of processor, rendering several frames and compiling large codebases while you sip a well earned cup of something caffeinated.
- GPU options: A range from high-end professional GPUs to multiple discrete GPUs for heavy CAD, render, or AI tasks. If you are doing any serious GPU compute, you will appreciate the performance headroom here. Just do not forget to enable ABP Always Be Plugging when you are cranking out frames at 8K in 32-bit color depth.
- Memory: Expect generous RAM capacity, often up to 512 GB ECC in the heavier configurations. If you are into scientific computing or AI training, this is where you will thank your past self for buying the extra DIMMs.
- Storage: NVMe-based storage with multi-terabyte options, plus an emphasis on fast PCIe 4.0/5.0 lanes. The rig supports RAID configurations if you are into data redundancy or throughput experiments, which is sensible for production pipelines.
- Cooling: A robust cooling system with liquid cooling options and smart fan curves that adjust to workload. In practice, the machine stays cool under load, and the acoustic footprint remains modest enough to keep your neighbors from staging a noise complaint during a long rendering pass.

Upgradeability is built into the product design in a Geeknite friendly way: tool-less access to memory slots, straightforward access to storage, and modular GPU mounting that won't require you to do plumbing level cable surgery. This isn't a system you buy and forget; it is a platform you tinker with as your needs evolve (and as your software asks for ever more GPU memory).

## Software, OS, and Ecosystem

The Infinite 10 ships with a stable, workstation-focused operating system stack. Expect Windows 11 Pro in a configuration that prioritizes virtualization and development tools, plus strong Linux compatibility for developers who want open-source playtime in the same day. The BIOS and firmware offer a sensible UX for power users who want to tune memory timings, PCIe lanes, and PCIe slot assignments without getting cards stuck in a black-hole of experimental settings.

There is a tidy software layer for fan control, thermals, and performance profiles. The Performance mode leans into CPU and GPU turbo, while the Quiet mode prioritizes acoustics and energy efficiency. The OS-level virtualization features are solid enough for containerized dev work and some light VM testing, which is precisely the kind of thing you would expect in a high end workstation build.

If you are into automation, you will appreciate the pre-baked scripts and the ability to orchestrate tasks across your drives and GPUs. It is not magic, but it is the kind of practical convenience that makes you whisper, Why did I wait so long to set this up?

## Connectivity, Ports, and Desk Compatibility

The Infinite 10 isn’t shy about ports. It is designed for real-world workstations where you might connect a router, a DAS, a VR headset, and a legacy monitor all at once and still have room for a snack. Here is the baseline I tested:

- USB-C / Thunderbolt 4: High-speed external storage? Check. External GPU enclosure? Check. Thunderbolt networking? Also check.
- USB-A Gen 3.x: For older peripherals that stubbornly refuse to die.
- HDMI 2.1 and DP 2.1: For the display pipeline to your Black WTE Screen and an auxiliary monitor (if you’re into multi-monitor wizardry).
- Ethernet: A reliable 2.5G connection for server-like workflows or large data transports.

Ergonomics and desk integration are well-executed. The chassis supports a wide range of monitor heights and offers a comfortable tilt range for long sessions of deep focus and occasional cat-herding of project files. If you have ever had your neck complain after a late-night compile, the adjustability here is a small relief that pays dividends after a week of use.

No review would be honest without a beat-by-beat on noise and heat.
