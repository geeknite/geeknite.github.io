### Safety basics
Always treat any katana as a potential hazard. Keep the blade sheathed when not in use, store it away from children, and perform routine checks for loose fittings. If you plan to do stage combat or cosplay battles, use proper protective gear and coordinate with other participants. A good practice is to keep a dedicated maintenance kit on hand, including a soft cloth, light oil, and a small sharpening stone or ceramic rod for quick touchups. The goal is to preserve the blade’s edge and finish while avoiding anything that might cause micro-chips or corrosion. Safety first, style second, but if you can combine both, you win twice.

## Care and Maintenance
### Basic upkeep routine
To keep the Honshu Boshin Katana looking sharp and performing reliably, a light maintenance routine goes a long way. After handling the blade, wipe it down with a soft cloth to remove fingerprints and moisture. For storage, apply a light coat of mineral oil or a dedicated sword oil to the blade to prevent rust. Do not rely on household oils that can attract dust or degrade the finish over time. The idea is to maintain a low but consistent level of protection for the blade while keeping the edge ready for use should the moment call for action.

### Cleaning the scabbard and fittings
The scabbard should be kept clean and dry as well. Wipe it down periodically and avoid exposing it to excessive moisture or direct heat, which can warp the wood or finish. If the fittings feel slightly loose after transport, a careful tightening with the right tools is recommended, but avoid overtightening, which can crack enamel or wood components. A steady hand, a touch of patience, and an eye for details are the trifecta here.

### Sharpening and edge care
A light bevel touch up with a ceramic rod or a fine sharpening stone is sufficient for routine maintenance. Do not aggressively grind or alter the temper while the blade is mounted, and always follow the manufacturer recommendations for sharpening angles and techniques. If you are new to sharpening, consider practicing on a less valuable blade first to get a feel for how the steel responds to pressure and angle changes. Safety is key, but that does not mean you cannot enjoy the process—the ritual of sharpening can be meditative when done with care.
