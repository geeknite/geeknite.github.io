## What is Phase 10, anyway?

Phase 10 is a classic card game designed by Kenneth Johnson and first released in the 1980s. It’s produced by Fundex, which later got acquired, rebranded, and quietly evolved into a product your aunt would gift you with that comes in a plastic wrapper with a picture of people smiling too hard. The core mechanic is deceptively simple: you must complete ten distinct phases in order. Each phase requires different card sets, typically involving runs, sets, or a combination. Once you complete a phase, you advance to the next one. The first player to complete all ten phases wins the game.

The deck consists of numbered cards from 1 to 12 in four colors, plus wild cards and skip cards. The chaos comes from the fact that a phase in one round might require two sets and a run, while the next phase might demand a different configuration entirely. It’s the card-game version of a scavenger hunt where your teammates have to interpret your garbled hints and still applaud your occasional brilliance.

### Getting started

1. Shuffle and deal 10 cards to each player. The rest become a draw pile; the top card flips to start a discard pile. Yes, you will fight over who gets to hand out the first draw.
2. Each player takes a look at their hand and prepares to complete Phase 1. Phase 1 typically asks for two sets of three, or something along those lines depending on the edition. The exact wording can be a bit… flexible, but your table should settle on a shared interpretation before anyone reaches for the wild cards.
3. Take turns drawing a card and discarding one. When you think you can complete your current phase, you lay down the meld in front of you. If you complete the phase, you advance to Phase 2 and pick up a fresh meld sheet.
4. The game ends when someone completes Phase 10. The winner is the one with the lowest total score after the game ends, or the first to finish, depending on how heroic you’re feeling that night.

If your group loves gadgets, you can use a timer for each turn to speed things up and add a sprinkle of stress-testing your relationships. If you’re a purist, you’ll enjoy the ritual of flipping a card to the discard pile with a dramatic sigh, then glancing around like you didn’t just derail the entire plan for a clown car of a round.

## How to play: a quick overview

Phase 10 is built on the idea of rounds and phases. Each round, players try to complete the current phase first and then move on to the next. The round continues until someone completes their phase and empties their hand, triggering a phase advancement and potentially a clever chain of plays that makes your friends bow down to your card-crafting genius (or your ability to draw the right card just as the discard pile turns evil).

Key elements:
- Runs: A straight sequence of cards in the same color (e.g., 4-5-6 in red).
- Sets: A group of cards with the same number (e.g., 7-7-7).
- Wild cards: Flexible; can substitute for any card in a meld, though they may have limits depending on edition.
- Skip cards: Cause opponents to lose a turn, adding a little spice to the strategy stew.

The phases typically progress from simpler to more complex. Phase 1 might be two sets of three, Phase 2 could be a set of four and a run of four, and Phase 10 might demand multiple intricate melds plus a few strategic holds. The exact phrasing in your rulebook may vary between editions, but the spirit remains the same: you must sequence the cards into precise patterns to advance.

### The strategy, in plain terms

- Look for lightweight patterns early, but keep one eye on what you’re discarding. You don’t want to open a doorway for someone to steal your most-wanted card that would have unlocked two rounds later.
- Use wild cards strategically. They’re valuable, but not infallible. If you save them for a potential late-phase finish, you’ll reward yourself with a triumphant moment—hopefully, after you’ve teased your friends for missing out on the same opportunity.
- Pay attention to what others are collecting. Phase 10 is a social psychology lab disguised as a card game: you’ll learn who quietly accelerates when they’re on a tear and who shuffles nervously when the discard pile looks too juicy.
- Don’t be afraid to adjust. If your family has a habit of arguing about how strict the rules are, consider a quick house-rule in which a misdeal or a swap triggers a playful penalty rather than a full-blown controversy. The goal is laughter, not lawsuits.

## Variants and house rules

Phase 10 has inspired a handful of variants and house rules that can breathe new life into a tired game night. A few popular options include:
- Time-limited rounds: Set a timer for each turn or for the round. It adds urgency and reduces “one more turn” paralysis.
- Progressive penalties: If you miss a phase by one card, you receive a light penalty rather than a full points hit. The stakes stay fun and low-stress.
- Cooperative mode: In some homes, players team up against the deck to complete all ten phases before the deck runs out. It’s like a friendly heist, but with more math and less suspense.
- Trading and draft rules: Allow players to trade cards at certain points, which can create hilarious trade negotiations that remind you of middle-school lunch periods.
