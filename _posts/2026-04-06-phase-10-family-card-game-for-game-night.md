## What is Phase 10, anyway?

Phase 10 is a classic card game designed by Kenneth Johnson and first released in the 1980s. It’s now part of a lineage of editions and rebrandings that sometimes feels like a family photo album that won’t sit still. The core mechanic is deceptively simple: you must complete ten distinct phases in order. Each phase requires different card sets, typically involving runs, sets, or a combination. Once you complete a phase, you advance to the next one. The first player to complete all ten phases wins the game.

The deck consists of numbered cards from 1 to 12 in four colors, plus wild cards and skip cards. The chaos comes from the fact that a phase in one round might require two sets and a run, while the next phase might demand a different configuration entirely. It’s basically a scavenger hunt with a deck of cards and a playlist of dramatic sighs.

### Getting started

1. Shuffle and deal 10 cards to each player. The rest form a draw pile; flip the top card to start the discard pile.
2. Each player surveys their hand and starts planning Phase 1. Phase 1 commonly demands two sets of three, but your edition might phrase it a touch differently. Agree on interpretation before anyone dumps a wild card like you’re playing a prank on your future self.
3. On your turn, draw a card and discard one. When you think you can complete your current phase, lay down the meld in front of you. If you complete the phase, you advance to the next one and refill your meld sheet (because nothing says victory like paperwork).
4. The game ends when someone completes Phase 10. The winner is typically the one with the lowest score after everyone has seen their fate, or the first to finish if you’re feeling especially heroic.

If your group loves gadgets and high-pressure moments, you can drop a timer in for a sense of speed. If you’re a purist, you’ll enjoy the old-school drama of flipping a card to the discard pile with a sigh that sounds suspiciously like a plot twist.

## How to play: a quick overview

Phase 10 runs in rounds, with players chasing the current phase and aiming to clear their hands. The moment someone completes Phase 10, the round ends and players tally points (or mood points, depending on your party vibe) and move to Phase 9, Phase 8, and so forth? No—this is where we keep the logic. In most rules variants, you play through the ten phases in order once per life; the game ends when someone completes Phase 10 for the last time, and scoring can determine the ultimate winner.

Key elements:
- Runs: A straight sequence of cards in the same color (for example, 4-5-6 in red)
- Sets: A group of cards with the same number (like 7-7-7)
- Wild cards: Flexible; can substitute for any card in a meld, with some edition-specific limitations
- Skip cards: Force opponents to lose a turn, adding spice to the strategic flavor

The phases progress from simpler to more complex. Phase 1 might be two sets of three, Phase 2 could be a set of four plus a run of four, Phase 3 might require a run or two, and Phase 10 can push you toward multiple mangled melds plus measured holds. The exact wording depends on your edition, but the spirit is: craft precise patterns to advance without leaking your secrets.

### The strategy, in plain terms

- Look for lightweight patterns early, but keep one eye on what you discard. You don’t want to open a doorway for someone to steal your most-wanted card that would unlock a late phase.
- Use wild cards strategically. They’re valuable but not invincible. If you save them for the final flourish, you’ll earn a chorus of groans and cheers when you pull off a phase with precision.
- Read the room. Phase 10 is a social experiment disguised as a card game: you’ll learn who secretly plans a long con and who will panic when the discard pile looks too tasty.
- Don’t be afraid to adjust. If your crew has a flexible interpretation of the rules, a quick house-rule adjustment that adds a light penalty for misdeals can keep the vibe positive.

## Variants and house rules

Phase 10 has spawned a handful of variants and house rules you can sprinkle into your night for extra spice:
- Time-limited rounds: A timer per turn or per round adds urgency and reduces the dreaded one-more-turn impasse.
- Progressive penalties: Miss a phase by one card? A light penalty, not a full points hit, keeps the mood friendly.
- Cooperative mode: Teams against the deck to complete all ten phases before the deck runs out. It’s like a family-friendly heist with less danger and more spreadsheets.
- Trading and draft rules: Allow players to trade cards at certain points for hilariously questionable acquisitions, like trading your last good card for a single spicy wildcard.
