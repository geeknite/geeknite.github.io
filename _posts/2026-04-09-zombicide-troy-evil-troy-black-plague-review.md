Zombicide is a love letter to improvise your own disaster movie while pretending you actually had a plan all along. Guillotine Games and CMON keep tossing new heroes, villains, and reanimated chaos into the mix, and the Troy and Evil Troy set for Zombicide Black Plague is no exception. This review is for the box that arrives with two new hero minis, a couple of new cards, and the age old question that gnaws at every gamer who loves medieval mayhem: do two versions of a hero justify dual mini rules, or is this just a clever way to sell paint jobs and a few tokens?

If you want to skip straight to the verdict, jump to the final recommendation, but if you are here for the long haul, buckle up. We are going to talk about the minis, the cards, the balance in a Black Plague context, and the kind of chaos that only a pair of knights from a mythic era can conjure when plopped down in a kitchen-sink zombie apocalypse that somehow smells of thyme and dragonfire.


## Unboxing and first impressions

First things first, the box looks better in person than in most photos. The Troy and Evil Troy miniatures come on their own sprues and are solid, well-sculpted knights with enough personality to tell them apart on the table without squinting. The Evil version has that dark, corrupted edge that Zombicide always nails when you want to depict a hero footnote that becomes an accidental villain after a bad dice roll and a worse mood. The packaging includes two unique character cards, a couple of token dockets, and a small stack of gear cards you can scout for once you roll the dice and deploy your squad.

The minies themselves have excellent posing. Troy looks like a seasoned warrior with a shield that seems just a hair too heavy for his torso, which is not a complaint. Evil Troy, by contrast, has a silhouette that practically screams midlife crisis in chainmail: a darker brow, ragged cape, and eyes that look like they could will a zombie horde into existence by sheer stubbornness. If you paint, you will have a field day. If you don’t, these two will still pop out of the terrain and glare at you with the confidence of two knights who just discovered that undead peasants taste better with a little garlic and planning.

The standees and dial system in Zombicide still feels a step above the old school metal setups. The mini cards for Troy and Evil Troy are clear, with a geeky but readable font that won’t cause eye strain after a six-hour siege. One tiny quality-of-life win is the color-coding on their bases. You will not confuse Troy with Evil Troy at 2 AM when you are trying to recall which one has the better shield bash. Small improvements like this keep the gameplay smooth during critical moments, which is the whole point of a good expansion box.


## The minis and design language

Zombicide Black Plague lives in a middle-ages fantasy universe where knights, necromancy, and ravaging hordes collide with a surprisingly high level of detail. Troy and Evil Troy fit that aesthetic perfectly. The details on the armor plates, the engravings on their shields, and the faces on the miniatures carry the same sharp edge you see on the rest of the line. The sculpts are clean enough that you can pick out their gear from across the board, which matters in larger games where you are swapping tiles and your brain is trying to keep track of a dozen moving pieces at once.

The Evil Troy model has some extra menace quality baked in. You can imagine a scenario where this figure slips into your party as a morally flexible ally or a questionable liability, depending on how the mission cards line up. It is a good reminder that even in a co op where the goal is to slay undead abominations, the human element can be the spice that makes each session feel like you are playing a soap opera with swords and sullen gazes.

As for components beyond the minis, the two hero cards include distinct abilities that feel meaningful rather than ornamental. The skills lean slightly toward crowd control, survivability, and a bit of surge power when the undead breath gets hot. In Black Plague, that mix matters. The core game has you balancing resource management and dice luck; when you add Troy and Evil Troy, you want to ensure their powers don’t outshine the base game’s tension, but still provide that satisfying moment when you chain a clever move that wipes a room clean while your teammates cheer through their muffled Viking-breathing masks.


## Rules and mechanics: what changes with this pack

Zombicide is famous for stacking small rules bits that feel deceptively simple until you realize the pacing depends on them. The Troy duo adds two new character cards with a handful of actions and one or two unique item interactions. The exact balance plays out like this: you can expect stronger melee options and a little extra survivability with Troy, plus a more aggressive, risk-tolerant kit with Evil Troy. The duality is not just a flavor gimmick; it creates a natural tension in team leadership and mission choices.

What this means on the table:
- You will find yourself evaluating whether to push Evil Troy into the front line or keep him slightly in reserve for a critical moment. That moment usually involves a room full of zombies that would make lesser captains crumble.
- Troy usually ships with abilities that reward precise positioning and shield work. That means you can build a defensive corridor that buys you time to deploy your more explosive powers, which is a theme that plays well with the base Black Plague deck that sometimes punishes reckless heroism.
- The new gear cards let you tailor each hero to your preferred playstyle. You do not get a dozen options to choose from, but you get enough diversity to feel like you are not playing exactly the same build you always default to. That is the sweet spot in a game that already has a heavy rhythm of deck cycling and posture adjustments.

If you are concerned about game length, the Troy set does add a few decisions that slow you down just enough to feel cinematic but not enough to derail a campaign night. The most important thing is that the new card text is clear and the interaction with standard black plague rules remains intuitive. There is no rule about activating both Troy and Evil Troy on the same turn, which would have been a nightmare to track and would have leaned hard into power fantasy rather than cooperative chaos. The designers avoided that trap, and this is a big win for the table.


## Thematic notes and narrative punch

Zombicide has always danced around a silly, sometimes campy theme with a surprisingly serious spine. The Troy and Evil Troy duo amplifies the tension between order and corruption that is the backbone of many knightly myths. Troy is depicted as a principled warrior who has seen plenty of battles and still believes in protecting his fellows. Evil Troy embodies a cursed mirror image, a character who represents the temptations and the easy road to vengeance that often lead to poor decisions in a zombie apocalypse. The clash between these two is not just flavor; it can shape how your team executes a scenario because you can choose to lean into duality or to aggressively separate responsibilities.

The art direction stays within the established Black Plague palette: earthy stone, iron, and warm ambient lighting. The paint jobs on the two minis reflect a narrative choice as well: Troy is bright and metal-polished, while Evil Troy is darker, with subtle weathering that suggests longer nights and more blood on the blade. If you enjoy conversions, this set invites some fun painting sessions where you can try to tell their story across a handful of games. The result is not a cinematic epic on your dining-room table, but it feels like a micro-epic every time you roll to save the city from a literal tide of undead.


## Integration with Black Plague: balance and table presence

The Black Plague core set has a rhythm: move, attack, survive, hope you don’t trigger the wrong zombie chain, and pray your plan survives the next chaos card. Troy and Evil Troy slot into that rhythm without breaking it. The powers are potent enough to be satisfying, yet they won’t break the game or reduce the need for careful planning. If anything, they encourage you to think a step ahead and coordinate with your fellow survivors to set up a room-clearing sequence that uses the best of both heroes in a complementary way.

From a balance perspective, this pack lands in a sweet spot where it adds flavor and strategic choices without creating a power imbalance that erodes the challenge. It is not the kind of set that makes you skip the rest of the team to chase a broken combo. It is a well-judged addition that could potentially shift the late game tension slightly in your favor if you lean into Evil Troy a bit more, but it won’t ruin the cooperative vibe if you decide to treat him more as a wildcard than a crutch.


## Content quality and value

Aside from the minis and cards, the set includes tokens and small accessories that align with the rest of the line. The production quality is solid. The components feel sturdy, and the artwork remains sharp, with consistent iconography across the entire Black Plague line. In terms of value, you are looking at two heroes that can see multiple campaigns if you rotate them through the roster of scenarios in your collection. They are not a one-night wonder. If you are a long-term Zombicide player who is expanding the kitchen-sink of your campaign nights, Troy and Evil Troy can become reliable go-to options when you want a slightly different feel without risking the balance of your table.

If you compare to other hero packs in Zombicide, this one sits in the middle of the pack for pure novelty but nudges ahead in narrative payoff. You will get more mileage out of a pair of companions with a built-in internal conflict than from a handful of generic heroes who feel similar to the base roster. The two new cards give you enough of a peek into their mechanics to justify the price, while the minis deliver the eye candy for those painting sessions that so many players use to unwind after a long week of work.


## Scenarios and replay value

The kit does not come with a complete campaign arc on its own; you will still rely on your existing Black Plague scenario library to slot Troy and Evil Troy into new or old missions. The presence of these two figures encourages you to re-run rooms that could benefit from a more aggressive closing push, or to test whether your survivors can outsmart two front-line powerhouses at once. The replay value hinges on your table’s appetite for experimentation with new hero dynamics, but there is enough juice here to justify at least a handful of play sessions beyond the initial novelty phase.

If you enjoy comparing the two knights across multiple games, you can watch their synergy evolve as you upgrade equipment and adjust your team’s formation. The result is a small but pleasant layer of meta in a game that is already full of micro-decisions and surprising outcomes.

## Setup and playtime impact

Setup remains straightforward. You will merely need to extract the two hero miniatures, shuffle their cards into the corresponding decks, and set up any mission-specific tokens as described. The actual gameplay impact is a little more involved than dropping in two knights and calling it a day. You will experience a mild uptick in decision points per turn, especially when you start leveraging Troy for defense or Evil Troy for a backline push. The playtime impact is modest; you may find that your nights stretch into a second or third hour if your table gets really into the scenario design and you are trying to maximize the synergy of your team.


## Final thoughts and recommendations

Zombicide Black Plague remains a robust, accessible co op that rewards planning, coordination, and a dash of luck. Troy and Evil Troy fit perfectly into this cadence, adding a pair of personality-driven heroes who feel meaningful without upsetting established balance. If you are actively expanding your Black Plague library, the two new heroes provide you with a pair of dynamic playstyles that can be blended with existing survivors to create fresh room-clearing tactics. The narrative flavor is the cherry on top, the sort of thing that makes you pause mid-game to nod at the miniature storytelling you just orchestrated while a zombie grasp tries to ruin your party cake.

If you are deciding between this expansion and another, consider how important personal story arcs and cinematic flavor are to your group. If you crave a little more drama and a dash of mythic content in your zombie apocalypse, Troy and Evil Troy deliver. In the end, this is a solid addition that respects the base game and adds a couple of memorable moments to your table.
