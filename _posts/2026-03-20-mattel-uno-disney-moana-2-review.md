### Core UNO rules you already know
In standard UNO, you match a card by color or number, play a Wild, manage your hand, shout UNO when you’re down to one card, and hope your friends aren’t strategic enough to foretell your doom. The MOANA 2 edition maintains those bones but adds two spice pills in the form of Special Cards and a couple of rule tweaks. If you’re upgrading from classic UNO or another Disney edition, you’ll recognize the baseline while noticing a few changes that feel like a fresh breeze on a tropical afternoon.

### The two Special Cards: a quick walkthrough
- Special Card A: This card triggers a condition that reshuffles the play order or forces a specific outcome for the next player. It’s designed to give the turn-takers a brief window to reset the rhythm, but it also provides a psychological tool—pressure, misdirection, and the occasional taunt about whether the next player’s color choice will be a strategic masterstroke or a meltdown.
- Special Card B: This one interacts with color choice or draw effects in a slightly different way. It’s the “wild card of the moment” that can swing a near-win situation into a dramatic, improvised victory or, conversely, blow up in the user’s face when misused.

### Variants and house-rule suggestions
If you like to experiment, here are a few house-rule ideas that fit the MOANA 2 vibe without breaking the spirit:
- Island Time Rule: If a player draws seven cards, they can discard two on the same turn if they also shout “Islands ahead!” with sufficient enthusiasm. It’s silly, but it creates a more dynamic rhythm for casual play.
- Maui’s Choice: If someone plays Special Card B, the next player must pick a color that matches a Moana character’s color palette (red for Moana, teal for ocean, gold for Maui, etc.). This adds a tiny thematic constraint and a dash of mischief.
- Te Fiti Blessing: If you can go out exactly on a Wild Draw Four, the player who declared UNO gets a temporary shield from challenges for one turn. It’s a cosmetic, flavor-filled benefit but it can matter in a tight game.
Remember: the point of house rules is to preserve fun, not to ruin friendships. Start small, test the waters, and escalate only if everyone agrees.
