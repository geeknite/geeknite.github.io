## Card Overview: What is No. 044 Gloom?
### Card identity and rarity
Gloom in this Japanese 1996 edition is a card that many refer to with a mixture of reverence and mild panic—the kind of card that makes a binder feel like a living museum. The No. 044 designation places it in the early wave of the Pocket Monster Card Game, when art was still learning how to scream “collectible” without shouting “price gouge.” In this print, Gloom is typically cataloged as a Very Rare card, a rarity tag that speaks to scarcity, not to the occasional sneeze you get when you open a fresh booster pack in an unairconditioned basement. The “Very Rare” label is not just about supply; it also hints at print-era quirks, batch run sizes, and the fact that these cards didn’t exactly roll off the assembly line with industrial efficiency. It is the kind of rarity that makes a card feel like a secret handshake among veterans.
### Artwork and typography
The art on this Gloom is where the romantic tragedy of the 1990s begins: bold color choices, a slightly glossy finish, and a background that looks like someone attempted to paint a sunset with ink and hurriedly called it “art.” The nosey observers among us notice the fat teardrop shapes around Gloom’s face, a color palette that both pops and threatens to be a lot for the unprepared retina. The typography—tiny yet confident—shows off the old-school Japanese syllabary styling, which makes the card feel almost like a token from a different era of design. If you’re into “art prints you could trade for a light beer,” this card is a gem, because it looks like it came from a time when card art didn’t have to scream “premium rare” with 4K gloss and 12 panels of foil.

## Visual Design and Condition Factors
### Printing quality and cardstock
If you’re scanning a No. 044 for the first time, you’ll notice how the cardstock feels: sturdy enough to survive being shuffled in high school hallways and soft enough to faintly bend if you’re unlucky with a backpack storm. Japanese 1996 print quality varies, and the Very Rare designation often correlates with a subset of print sheets that have deeper ink saturation and a more resilient surface that resisted scuffing a little better than some other cards from the era.
### Grading reality and expectations
Grading vintage Pokemon cards that are several decades old is a delicate art involving centering, corner wear, surface scratches, and print lines. For No. 044 Gloom, the rare-stuff factor compounds: if you find a copy with crisp corners and minimal whitening, you’re in a zone where PSA 9 or BGS 9.5 starts to look realistic—assuming you’re willing to pay the gatekeeping price. For many buyers, a PSA 8 still feels like a victory because it signals you’ve earned a “battle-tested” badge of honor—like a sword scratched by dragons in a fire-lit cave, but still sharp enough to cut the tension in a trade war.
### The card text and mechanics then vs now
Gloom’s original text in the Japanese 1996 set is a window into the game’s early design philosophy. The attacks were simpler, the energy costs straightforward, and the flavor text arguably more poetic than today’s engines of churn. The card’s effect is a reminder that the TCG of yesteryear wasn’t just about numbers; it was about mood, the aura of the Pokémon, and how that aura could tilt a game’s momentum even before decks were fully understood by the players. In modern collectibility, that mood translates into the ability to tell a story about your card between rounds or across a trade negotiation—the art, the rarity, the memory, and the little crinkles on the corner that whisper “I survived your careless shuffle.”

## Collecting Tips: How to Approach No. 044 Gloom
### Spotting authentic prints vs. fakes
In today’s market, where counterfeiters can mimic gloss and print quality, there are a few telltale signs to check for:
- Print alignment: Look at the border and the image alignment; authentic copies often have consistent centering, whereas some fakes show slight misalignment.
- Surface texture: Real cards from the era have a certain card stock feel and a distinctive glossy finish that doesn’t look plasticky.
- Font and text placement: The Japanese characters and numbers should be crisp and evenly spaced; if you see blurring or inconsistent kerning, it’s a red flag.
- Backing pattern: If you’ve got access to more than one card of the same set, compare the back patterns. Genuine Japanese prints from the era typically share a uniform back design.
### Grading windows and price expectations
As with any Vintage 1990s card, grades matter—and they matter a lot. If you’re chasing PSA 9 or BGS 9.5, you’re not just paying for a card; you’re paying for a graded piece of time-sculpted nostalgia. Expect prices that scale with the grade, the surface wear, and whether the card has any visible centering issues. For many collectors, the joy is not purely monetary: it’s about owning a physical artifact that represents a childhood memory or a long-in-the-toy closet dream.
### Display and storage considerations
Gloom No. 044 deserves a display that doesn’t rely solely on the heart’s goodwill. If you’re serious about shelving a Very Rare item, consider high-gloss display cases with UV protection and acid-free sleeves to slow down yellowing and surface micro-scratches. The correct display will make your Gloom feel like a small, solemn shrine—a sultry glass case with a tiny ode to the 1990s energy cap. 

## Value, Market Trends, and Practical Advice
### The price trajectory for vintage Japanese No. 044
Prices for No. 044 vary widely depending on grade, edition (first print vs later reprints), and the presence of any holo or alternate art effects. While some buyers chase top-tier PSA 9+ copies, others see the card as a piece of history that doesn’t require a six-figure pledge to own. If you’re a new collector, set a budget and give yourself permission to start with a well-preserved, mid-grade copy. If you’re a veteran, you’ll know the thrill of discovering a near-mint version that still retains the glow of youth—like a high school diary that somehow survived the gym locker and the climate of adolescence.
### Practical buying tips
- Buy from trusted sources with return policies, and ask for high-resolution photos to inspect centering, corner wear, and surface gloss.
- Consider the long-term: vintage cards can be a slow burn in terms of appreciation. The most important thing is the story you’ll be able to tell about your No. 044 copy when you’re old and wrinkly in your collector corner.
- Don’t rely solely on price as a barometer of value. Rarity is not the same as desirability; sometimes a well-loved copy with character can be more desirable than a pristine slab that looks clinical.

## Card Variants and Print Runs: What You Might Encounter
### Common variants vs. rare variants in the Japanese market
Japanese 1996 print runs occasionally feature subtle shifts—border color, font weight, or even the texture of the card stock. The No. 044 Gloom may appear in different print runs that collectors can distinguish by micro-ops like minor color shifts or the presence (or absence) of specific markings on the back. These differences are tiny but meaningful to enthusiasts who want to build a near-complete No. 044 set or who chase the rare variant that proves they’ve read the print like a cryptic novel.
### Holo vs non-holo confusion
Some of the most prized copies of Gloom in the 1996 Japanese line may include holofoil variants. The holo treatment amplifies the card’s visual impact, transforming Gloom from a quiet character into a gleaming emblem of rarity. Non-holo copies, while still valuable, carry a different aura—more subtle, perhaps more versatile for casual display or budget-minded collectors who want to dip their toe into vintage while keeping the wallet comfortable.
