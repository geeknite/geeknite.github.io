## Brewing method: getting the most flavor with minimal chaos

Brewing Meadow Green Tea is the kind of routine that rewards consistency. Here is a simple, geek-friendly method that yields reliable results without turning your kitchen into a chemistry lab:

### Step 1: Water chemistry and temperature

- Ideal water temperature: 75 to 80 degrees Celsius (167 to 176 Fahrenheit).
- Water quality: filtered or spring water if you can swing it; chlorine will mute the aroma faster than a bad code review.

Why this range matters: green tea is delicate. Overheating extracts bitter compounds quickly, and you lose the gentle meadow aroma to a hot, exasperated sigh from the cup. Underheating, and you miss the full flavor spectrum. Think of 75–80 C as the Goldilocks zone that a small, caffeine-sipping bear would approve of.

### Step 2: Tea quantity and vessel

- Tea amount: about one teaspoon per 8 ounces of water for loose leaf. If you are using sachets, one sachet per cup is plenty.
- Vessel: a standard teapot or infuser mug works best. The key is water agitation and steep time, not a dramatic ritual that slows you down while your interruption wheel spins.

### Step 3: Steeping time

- Steep for 2 to 3 minutes. If you are using a larger leaf particle or a higher quality leaf, you may push to 3.5 minutes, but no more. Green tea is a marathon sprinter; you want the finish line to be sweet, not crunchy.

### Step 4: Enjoy and adjust

- After steeping, strain and taste. If you want a touch more brightness, a tiny splash of honey or a whisper of lemon rind can brighten the cup without muting the delicate notes.
- If you brew a little too long, you will know. It will be a tad more astringent and less meadow-y. Not a disaster, just a redacted version of your potential tea future.

### Step 5: Repeat for the lab notebook

For those who like to measure, you can keep a little tea log: date, water temperature, tea amount, steep time, and your subjective tasting notes. This is not just for nerds; this is for people who want to fine-tune their palate as if they were calibrating a color profiler for screen brightness.

## Practical tips for getting the best out of Meadow Green Tea

- Store in a cool, dry place away from strong odors. Tea leaves absorb environment flavors, and you want meadow to stay meadow.
- Use a clean infuser. Residual flavors from other teas can muddy the crisp quality Meadow brings.
- Avoid oversteeping. If you want a stronger profile, increase leaf amount slightly rather than lengthening steep time by large margins.
- Experiment with temperature. If you like a brighter cup, move toward 82 C; if you prefer softer notes, aim for 75 C and a shorter steep.
