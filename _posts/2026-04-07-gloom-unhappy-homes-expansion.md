## What’s in the box?

The Unhappy Homes expansion is not a massive add-on; it’s a well-curated tranche of ideas that slot neatly into the base game’s tempo. Here’s what you’ll typically find when you crack the shrink wrap (and yes, I spilled coffee on mine and pretended it’s a feature):

- A fresh set of House Cards: These are the new “homefronts” for your families, a handful of houses that carry their own thematic quirks and a few mechanics of their own. Some houses amplify gloom faster; others encourage you to take more negative cards with the hope of an outsized payoff later on. It’s like moving from a single-enchilada menu to a mini-taqueria—still messy, but somehow more flavorful.
- New Family Cards: The expansion adds a handful of new characters who will live, laugh, and cry under the roof of your new unhappy mansions. Each family has its own starting gloom and a handful of special abilities that interact with the new houses. They’re not all doom and gloom; some are gloom with a wink and a grin, which is exactly the mood we’re chasing here.
- Event and Fateful Cards: A set of cards that trigger under certain conditions—think of them as the house’s little personality quirks, like a stubborn dishwasher that refuses to clean up its own catastrophes or a haunted piano that plays itself just to spite your strategy.
- Tokens and counters: A few cardboard bits for tracking momentum, house-specific effects, and the occasional “mismatch in the marriage contract” token that’s more dramatic than it sounds. These tokens exist to remind you that, yes, your family can suffer more efficiently if you manage resources well.
- Quick rules addendum: A tidy one-page cheat sheet that tells you how the new houses and events interact with the base game. It’s designed to be read at the speed of a grocery-store checkout line, which is to say, not at all if you’re terrified of spoilers.

In short: the expansion doesn’t reinvent the wheel. It polishes the spokes and adds a few new creaks in the floorboards so you can stage more melodramatic tableaux. The humor remains intact, the tone remains sinisterly cheerful, and the overall feel is a natural extension rather than a kitchen-sink add-on.

As a note for collectors and table organizers, the art and graphic design stay consistent with the base game—dark, slightly gauche humor with a dash of whimsy. If you liked the aesthetic before, you’ll like it even more now. If you were on the fence about the base game’s humor, the expansion won’t push you off the ledge, but it will hand you a scarf and say, “Maybe you’ll enjoy this winter of misery.”

## How it changes the game

When you slot Unhappy Homes into your Gloom sessions, you’ll notice three broad shifts: pacing, choice density, and narrative texture.

- Pacing: The new houses introduce micro-arc moments. Instead of a single, big doom card per round, you’ll have several smaller, thematically tied triggers. The pace can feel a touch more frantic, which is exactly what you want if you’re trying to out-emo your opponents without turning the table into a wailing wall.
- Choice Density: You’ll have more cards to juggle, and some of the new houses reward riskier plays. If you enjoy a “do I take the risk now for a bigger payoff later” dilemma, this expansion gives you plenty of shiny, dangerous options. The decision points multiply like a family portrait montage—happy, sad, and a couple of awkward smiles in between.
- Narrative Texture: The new families each bring a different voice to your table’s ongoing soap opera. They’re a little more character-driven than the base set’s broad strokes, which means you can craft fun, theme-heavy gloms of misery that still feel cohesive with the world you’ve built across games.

For players who love to GM the mood as much as the cards, this expansion is a welcome tool. It invites you to lean into storytelling, to choreograph a chorus of misfortune that lands exactly where you want it—without taking away the base game’s core mechanic of trying to maximize your own gloom while sabotaging your opponents’ happiness.

If you’re worried about balance, breathe easy: Unhappy Homes is designed to slot into a normal game with minimal tweaking. It’s a modular addition, not a wholesale rework. You can add one house per round, or go full chaos mode and throw in three, if your group’s metaphoric curtains need to be drawn on a bigger tragedy.


### Setup and play flow

1) Prepare as you would for a regular Gloom session. Shuffle the gloom deck and lay out the tableau. Decide whether you want to target a shorter play session or a longer marathon of melodrama. The base set’s rules still apply, so the objective remains the same: the lowest total gloom wins.
2) Introduce the Unhappy Homes expansion: add the new House Cards to the central House deck and mix in the new Family Cards with the rest. If you’re playing a shorter game night, start with one new house and one new family to avoid overwhelming the table.
3) Determine any house rules for the night. Some groups like to make each house “activate” once per round, others prefer a “first-come, first-served” activation. It’s your table, your misery. The cheat sheet in the box makes these choices easy, and if you’re feeling fancy, you can build your own house-rule board on a napkin just as gods of despair intended.
4) Start dealing: each player draws a hand of characters as usual. The new houses and families provide a few starting bonuses; use them, or don’t—though you’ll probably want to, because this is a mood piece and you’re the director of tragedy.
5) Play rounds as usual, letting the gloom counters accumulate. The new cards’ triggers will nudge the doom track in new ways, so keep an eye on the house-specific effects; they’re the real spice of the box.

Pro tip: if the table braces for a long night, consider running a “house party” variant where every time a house triggers, that house also gives a tiny joke reward (like a forced pun or a one-liner) to the player who activated it. It’s not strictly necessary, but it does wonders for morale in the after-dinner gloom fog.


### A note on art, components, and feels

The Unhappy Homes expansion keeps the aesthetic you know and love: slightly macabre whimsy, thick black humor, and art that lands with a soft “crack” rather than a loud gong. The new cards don’t feel tacked-on; they feel like they belong in the same dim hallway of the house. The cardstock quality is consistent with the base game: sturdy enough to survive several table flips and a few accidental “I dropped a soda” moments without warping or cracking.

The new imagery emphasizes interiors—parlor rooms, musty kitchens, and stairwells that look suspiciously like they’ve witnessed a lifetime of poor choices. It’s a design language that says, “The house is a character here,” and it sells the mood without shouting. For players who get a little cinephile about their board games, this expansion behaves like a well-edited indie film—straight to the point, with subtext that’s far heavier than the budget would suggest.


## Strategy tips and playstyle notes

- Prioritize synergy: If you can pair a new family with a house that amplifies its quirks, you’ll stack the deck in your favor in an entertaining way. Look for what each family craves and what the house punishes; aim to line up those triggers for efficient doom delivery.
- Don’t overlook the tails: Some new cards have “tail” effects that show up after you’ve already passed the doom threshold. These are the moments that can swing a game from “okay” to “oh no, I’ve made a terrible mistake.” Keep an eye on late-round triggers because they’re the misdirection you’ll need when the pressure is on.
- Use the cheat sheet: The one-page addendum is not just a reminder of rules. It’s a quick-reference magician’s trick that will keep you from LTFO (losing the table to flavor overrules). It helps you keep track of what’s active and what’s resolved.
- Manage your resources: The new tokens add a layer of resource management. Don’t overcommit to a single powerful effect early on; you’ll regret it when the house demands a quiet end to your show. A measured approach, paired with a timely doom, is the best recipe for drama and victory alike.
- Thematic storytelling matters: The expansion thrives on how you narrate the misery. A well-timed line about a haunted coffee mug or a parlor that always seems to smell faintly of burnt toast can be more valuable than any card in your hand. Your opponents will hate you for it in the moment, and that’s exactly the vibe you’re after.


## A quick comparative glance: Unhappy Homes vs the rest of the gloom family

- Base Gloom vs Unhappy Homes: The base game nails the “pile the gloom high and yet keep it charming” vibe. Unhappy Homes adds density, a few new engines, and more narrative hooks without removing the core simplicity that makes the system forgiving to teach and brutal to master.
- Other expansions you might own: If you’ve played other Gloom expansions, you’ll recognize the pattern—new cards, new ways to die, and a stronger emphasis on storytelling. Unhappy Homes slots into that family lineup with a smaller footprint but a similar impact: more laughter, more groans, and more opportunities to tinker with your table’s preferred tone.

If you’re chasing a larger scale or more mechanical chaos, there are other boxes in the Gloom catalog to explore. If you want a compact, mood-forward addition that actually respects pace while adding flavor, Unhappy Homes is a reliable pick.


## Thematic notes

Let’s be honest: the hallmark of Gloom is making you feel like you’re at a family reunion where everyone lied about their feelings to look good in photos. Unhappy Homes nudges that core experience forward by supplying interior drama—more rooms, more secrets, and more opportunities to discover that your in-laws are not just annoying, they’re conceptually tragic. The humor remains dark but accessible, leaning into familial melodrama rather than horror-in-guise. The tone stays playful enough to keep a smile on your face while you cry into your cards, which is precisely the high-wire act Gloom has always performed.

If you’re a writer-at-table or a storyteller who wants your campaigns to feel cinematic, this expansion is a boon. It invites you to craft micro-sagas around each house—episodes of misfortune with recurring motifs and callbacks. The payoff isn’t just in the points but in the memory you build across a sequence of turns. And yes, it’s chaotic in the best way when a house triggers twice in one round and suddenly you’re juggling three doom tracks in a single moment. Delightfully ridiculous, and absolutely Gloom.


## Verdict and recommendation

- Accessibility: Easy to integrate with your existing Gloom games. The rules are familiar, and the new components don’t require a law degree to interpret.
- Replayability: Solid. The new Houses and Families create fresh narrative arcs that reward replaying with different combinations. You’ll find yourself chasing a particular “story arc” across multiple sessions.
- Theme and flavor: Strong. If you love the melancholy humor and gothic, soap-opera vibes of Gloom, Unhappy Homes doubles down on those elements without tipping into self-parody. It’s a natural extension that respects the original tone.
- Value for money: Reasonable. It’s not a giant expansion, but it doesn’t pretend to be either. You’ll get a nice bump in flavor for a modest price, and that’s a fair trade for more storytelling at the table.

Bottom line: If you already adore Gloom and you want to push your table’s storytelling muscles while preserving the core gameplay, Unhappy Homes is a no-brainer. It adds character, mood, and a few brilliant moments that will become cherished horror-comedy anecdotes in your group’s lore. It’s not a replacement for the base game; it’s a richer, gloomier, and more theatrical version of the same old party trope: bad things happening to people you pretend to love.


## Final thoughts and recommended play setup

For a standard evening: add one new house and one new family to start, then gauge how your group handles the pacing. If you crave more chaos after two or three rounds, bring in an additional house or two and watch the table bend under the weight of your collective storytelling impulses. If you want to run a “story night” rather than a quick session, consider building a three-act arc where each act explores a different interior of the same house, culminating in a dramatic reveal you’ll remember long after the pall of gloom lifts.

If you’re still deciding whether this expansion belongs on your shelf, here is a practical rule of thumb: if you enjoy the base game’s writing and want more opportunities to riff on misfortune through character-driven stories, you will likely adore Unhappy Homes. If you prefer minimalist, almost mechanical add-ons that merely extend playtime, you might want to skim this one and save your dollars for a future box with more crunchy rules.
