## Condition, Wear, and Authenticity
When assessing any vintage deck, your first questions are practical: does it still shuffle without getting stuck? Are the numbers legible? Do the colors pop in a natural, non-faded way? For a 1986 edition, you’re looking at a few typical markers:
- Surface wear: expect some light scratches and minor scuffs, particularly on the faces that see the most shuffling and dealing. As long as no card is illegible or dangerously bent, it’s within the expected range.
- Edge fray: a sign of frequent use or rough handling. The edges might show wear, which doesn’t necessarily decrease value if the deck retains its print integrity.
- Color integrity: the 1980s print runs tended to use more saturated blocks of color. If you see heavy fading, it might indicate prolonged sun exposure or suboptimal storage.
- Back design: the issuance’s back design can be a quick authenticity check. If the back art shows a different print style or color hue from the earlier production runs, that’s a potential indicator of a non-authentic or later reprint.

To verify authenticity, collectors often compare a few traits: the exact font weight of numbers, the color blocking of the suit areas, and the presence (or absence) of certain tiny branding cues unique to the 1986 International Games run. A reputable listing will note these details; a dubious seller might try to inflate value with vague “vintage vibes” language. When in doubt, request high-resolution scans of several cards, especially if you’re eyeing the 161-card claim—that can tell you quickly whether a card is missing or simply not photographed in the listing.
