## Zombicide Special Guest Lot: Lucio Parrillo, Edouard Guiton, Miguel Coimbra

Zombicide has long treated its audience to a steady diet of undead plastic and clever gameplay, but every once in a while the kitchen throws in a little spice… or in this case, a lot of blood-red pigment and signature brushwork. The Special Guest Lot featuring Lucio Parrillo, Edouard Guiton, and Miguel Coimbra is CMON's cheeky way of saying, We love you, collectors, and also please buy more stuff. The result is a product that is as much art book as board game expansion, a coffee-table centerpiece that also doubles as a functional set of miniatures. And yes, it is as chaotic as it sounds.

### A quick primer: what is the Special Guest Lot?
The Special Guest Lot is CMON's annual tradition of inviting celebrated artists to reinterpret parts of the Zombicide universe. The idea is simple: take characters or zombies from the base game, render them through a distinctive artistic lens, and fold those designs into a limited run box. In practice, you get bigger, flashier box art, higher quality sculpts, alternate color schemes, and a handful of mission cards that lean into the guest's stylistic fingerprints. This year’s trio—Parrillo, Guiton, Coimbra—reads like a cross between a dream team and a gallery opening that somehow also sells you a sealed deck of zombie heads.

If you want to skip to the money shot, here are the quick stats:
- Artists: Lucio Parrillo, Edouard Guiton, Miguel Coimbra
- Format: Limited edition miniatures with alternate sculpts and paint schemes
- Contents: A set of guest miniatures, new tokens, and a few mission cards (NIS)
- Availability: Very limited; likely to sell out fast

For fans, this is a no-brainer if you collect Zombicide artifacts. For casual players, it’s a beautiful but expensive detour from the core experience. For the rest of us, it’s a chance to admire some truly impastato brushwork and wonder if the hobby will ever learn to stop tempting us with more plastic.

### The artists: three styles, one undead buffet
In this section, we’ll dissect how each artist’s fingerprint shows up in the pieces. Let’s start with Parrillo, whose fantasy horror backgrounds make him a natural fit for the grimmer corners of the Zombicide universe.

#### Lucio Parrillo: anatomy, drama, and saturated red
Parrillo’s approach to the figures is all about storytelling with the minimum number of brushstrokes. The Zombicide figures by Parrillo don’t just stand there; they brood. The muscles are exaggerated in a way that feels almost comic-book dynamic, and the color palette leans heavy on crimson and burnt umber to signal danger before you even roll dice. If you’ve seen his other fantasy paintings, you’ll recognize the same sense of epic pose—like a necromancer just remembered he left the oven on.

In practice, Parrillo’s miniatures read as cinematic stills more than board-game minis. The faces carry a weathered look, as if each zombie has clocked a few thousand hours in the apocalypse and can still hit you with a sneer that would melt steel. The painting over the manual-level shading gives the impression of a diorama you’d want to frame rather than a unit you’d shove into a game tray. You’ll note how the pores, the subtle bruising on knuckles, and the slight glimmer in eyes are all about intent—the intent to bug your brain into thinking these are not just plastic soldiers but revenants with a story to tell.

#### Edouard Guiton: motion, texture, and a lighter touch
Guiton injects movement where Parrillo brings weight. His zombies come across as slippery in the best possible way: you can almost hear the snick of their joints as they shamble forward, and you can sense sweat on a brow that’s been pressed by a thousand hours of paintbrush discipline. The texturing on Guiton’s models is tactile—pustules, rips, and threadbare cloth that looks like it would crumble if you whisper “surface tension.” The color schemes are often a touch more surreal, leaning into teal greens or electric magentas that pop against the more natural tones. The effect is nightlife in a graveyard: the undead become characters you can almost gamble with, swerving from gray hope to neon despair in a single gaze from the figure’s glassy eye.

#### Miguel Coimbra: narrative density and shadow play
Coimbra handles the silhouettes and negative space like a noir photographer with a flashlight. His pieces often sport leaner forms, with longer limbs that read as more menacing in the board-game scale. The storytelling here is less about frenzy and more about ominous posture: a zombie that seems to be the lead-in to a trap, a hunter who looks online expensive, ready to strike. He’s not afraid to let the shadows swallow details, which paradoxically makes the pieces more legible at tabletop distance: you see the silhouette, you read the menace, and you know this is not your friendly farm-humor zombie but a cunning predator.

### The Not In Stock label (NIS): what does it mean?
NIS, or Not In Stock, is a small but important note for collectors. In the context of a Special Guest Lot, NIS might indicate that certain stretch goals, limited-run accessories, or extra alt-prints are not included in the standard edition shipped to retailers. The result is a box that looks truly deluxe on the shelf but can feel stingy if you expected every last creative morsel to be edible inside. In practical terms, NIS means you should temper your expectations about extra packs or extrascales that would have rounded out the package if they weren’t constrained by supply chain wizardry or licensing quirks. If you are chasing the complete museum experience, keep an eye out for any reprints, reissues, or online exclusives that CMON occasionally sneaks out after a hot preorder.

### Packaging, presentation, and the feeling of unboxing
The unboxing experience is a big part of why people chase Special Guest Lot releases. The box art by Parrillo immediately signals a shift from the functional to the aspirational. You’re not just opening a game; you’re stepping into a curated moment where the undead are not just threats but canvases for a celebrated artist to express their darker fantasies. ### A brief note on playability and in-game impact
From a strictly mechanical standpoint, Zombicide’s Special Guest Lot does not reinvent the wheel. It adds new character models with unique base stats, sometimes a special ability tied to the guest’s theme (for example, a zombie that resists fear tests or a survivor with a limited-use special action). The core grid of the game remains intact: you roll dice, you draw cards, you scuttle between cover tokens. The guest pieces are designed to feel unique on the board, which means they’ll entice you to create mini-sub-roles for your team, like a painter-scout who uses parries to hold a doorway while the others carve a path through the horde. If you enjoy thematic synergy—matching aesthetics with play styles—these pieces offer a little extra flavor to a game that is, at its heart, about resource management and cooperative chaos.

Of course, if you’re a strictly “WYSIWYG” player, you’ll want to keep a few things in mind: the base game’s balance remains the baseline. The guest pieces are not overpowered, but they are designed to be a little more cinematic than strictly optimized. It’s a design choice that suits the hobbyist’s dream: play a few missions, marvel at the art, then seek out painting challenges that test your brush control and patience more than your dice luck. For many players, that’s the point of a Special Guest Lot: to blend competition with aesthetic indulgence.

### The design process: how the magic happens behind the scenes
If you ever wondered how a guest-driven set makes it from concept to table, here’s a peek behind the curtain. CMON typically starts with a concept brief sent to the artist, who then routes the moodboard to the art director. The brief will want to balance three pillars: recognizable Zombicide design language, personal artistic voice, and practical constraints of miniature production. The result is a triad of sculpts where the guest artist’s signature styling must still sit in the same game box as the base pieces without creating a visual discord that could confuse new players.

The painting phase is where things get magical—and also a little terrifying for fans of tight budgets. The artists deliver painted prototypes, which are then used for photography, marketing, and renders. QC teams check for mold-flow issues, dark spots in the sculpt, and whether a model’s pose communicates the intended gameplay role—whether it’s a fast striker or a tank zombie. The whole process is a reminder that with CMON, artistry and engineering walk hand in hand, even when the march is a little chaotic.

### Painting tips for hobbyists who want to imitate the guests
If you’re one of those hobbyists who buys a big box and then immediately considers repainting everything, here are a few pointers inspired by the three artists:
- Parrillo-inspired technique: emphasize bold highlights along bone angles, with deep, saturated shadows in recesses. Use a limited palette of red tones to keep that dramatic, cinematic look. Thin your paints for smooth transitions yet keep some plate-like brush strokes to mimic their painterly texture.
- Guiton-inspired technique: focus on texture with stippling and edge highlights. Use slightly cooler mid-tones to push the sense of motion; a raincoat or torn cloth benefits from micro-shcading for that wet, glossy feel.
- Coimbra-inspired technique: lean into soft shadows and silhouette clarity. Keep details crisp at tabletop distance by designing strong negative space around a figure’s silhouette; a clever underpainting can help you achieve that moody noir vibe without sacrificing readability.

### Real-world experience: painting, storage, and display
A lot of the joy in Special Guest Lot ownership is display value. The box art is a magnet for your shelf, and the multi-artist approach gives you three tones you can “curate” in your display area. Some hobbyists pair a Parrillo piece with a darker, red-tinted light to accent the drama, while others rotate Coimbra pieces into a “story mode”—perhaps a short campaign where you narrate the undead’s ascent from shadow to stage light. Storage is straightforward thanks to CMON’s consistent miniatures packaging, but if you’re a serious painter, consider upgrading to a high-contrast lightbox to better appreciate the subtle gradient work each artist brought to the table.

### The Not In Stock label revisited: value, scarcity, and resale
NIS isn’t just a bureaucratic footnote; it affects perceived value. Some buyers interpret NIS as potential future add-ons that could become collectible separately, which can be a selling point. Others view it as a reminder that limited editions are, by design, finite and often come with trade-offs. If you’re the type who resents missing out, you might want to enter the market with a plan: decide what you’re willing to pay for a complete aesthetic experience and set a ceiling. If a future release adds exactly what you felt was missing, you’ll be happier knowing you can opt for the non-NIS version rather than pay a premium for something that was never intended to exist in your kit.

### Final verdict: should you get this set?
The Zombicide Special Guest Lot stands out as a premium collectible that also delivers solid gameplay. It offers an art-forward redesign of three characters that feel both fresh and faithful, an important balance for a game that thrives on your ability to recognize the undead’s threat at a glance. The presence of three distinct aesthetic voices—Parrillo’s bold, dramatic forms; Guiton’s motion-forward texture; Coimbra’s narrative density—gives this box its unique personality. The “NIS” flag isn’t a catastrophe; it’s a reminder that this release respects the realities of production and licensing while pushing the boundaries of what a board game expansion can feel like.

If you’re already invested in Zombicide and you want a collector’s piece that will keep you company during paint sessions and weekend-long gaming marathons, this is a no-regrets addition. If you’re on the fence, consider the joy of owning something that feels more like a gallery piece and less like a widget you assemble in twenty minutes and forget by Tuesday. The Set will have its champions and its skeptics, and both camps will have valid points.

### Final recommendation
For fans of Zombicide who crave a little extra atmosphere and a lot of art, the Special Guest Lot is something you should consider adding to your collection. It’s not a game-changer in terms of core mechanics, but it is a beautiful, collectible package that turns a table into a tiny museum for a night. If you love the artists, buy it. If you love the game, buy it anyway. If you love both, you’ll want a spare copy to show friends who visit your shelf and say, “Is that real?” Yes, it is.
