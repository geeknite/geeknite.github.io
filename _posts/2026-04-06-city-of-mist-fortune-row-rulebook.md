If you asked me to summarize City of Mist: Fortune Row in a single blender-optimized sentence, I’d say: it is what happens when myths pull up to a neon speakeasy, order a latte, and try to negotiate with a street magician who moonlights as a data broker. Fortune Row is a rulebook expansion that wants you to lean into the vibe of a city block where legends run on espresso, bravado, and a surprisingly well-tuned dice system. It doesn’t pretend the world is simple; it multiplies the complexity like a party trick that refuses to stop dazzling you. If the original City of Mist is a noir flowering in a mythic garden, Fortune Row is the security guard who knows all the entrances and has a spare set of keys for every door. And yes, it brings new rules to the table—but they’re not just there to show off elegance; they’re there to turbocharge both GM creativity and player identity. So pour yourself a tall glass of something with ice, park yourself in a chair that squeaks just right, and let’s dive into what Fortune Row adds—and what it asks you to invest in.

## Overview: what Fortune Row is and why it exists
Fortune Row is effectively a district-focused rule supplement for City of Mist (the pop-noir RPG where players embody legends, myths, and archetypes within a contemporary city). Where the core book gives you the toolset for masks, mythos, and a city-wide magic-meets-crime mood, Fortune Row zooms in on a single, shimmering, morally gray zone and distributes breadcrumbs for GMs and players to use during long-form campaigns. In practical terms, it introduces:

- A localized setting skeleton for a distinct neighborhood—Fortune Row—complete with background factions, encounters, and thematic hooks.
- New mechanics that emphasize the role of luck, fate, and the messy, interlocking plans of powerful patrons, double-crossing syndicates, and street-level operatives.
- Expanded character options tailored to Fortune Row’s vibe: archetypes, signature tools, and Event-Driven Paths that feel both mythic and intimate.
- GMs tools for driving campaigns that move from “one-off mystery” to “city-scale intrigue anchored in myth and consequence.”

If you’re already sold on the idea of mythic power colliding with everyday life in a bustling metropolis, Fortune Row is your new favorite sandbox. If you preferred the core rulebook’s global feel and your campaigns live and die by the city-wide phenomenon level, Fortune Row still offers enough texture to justify its purchase, but you should be mindful of how much you want to tighten the lens around one neighborhood.

### Aesthetic and production choices
Fortune Row leans heavily into the neon-noir aesthetic that City of Mist fans adore. The art direction preserves the smoky, rain-slicked mood and piles on more locale-specific flavor: storefront signs that drip character, alley murals that hold whispered legends, and street-tiers of casual incantation that feel both modern and ancient. The layout respects readability while letting the art do a lot of the storytelling work. If you’re the type who wallpaper-scouts a manual for ambient inspiration, you’ll find Fortune Row generous in the mood department without ever feeling like it’s trying to be a coffee-table book. And yes, the book includes a couple of ready-to-go maps and diagrams—the funky kind of maps that tell you where to stand and where your mythic shadow is lurking.

## Content and mechanics: what’s inside and how it changes the game
If the core rulebook is the “rules of the road,” Fortune Row is the “rules of the road that you would paint on the side of the bus.” It doesn’t reinvent the wheel; it adds new hubs, tools, and verbs to keep the wheels from squealing at every turn.

### New mechanics and what they feel like in play
Fortune Row introduces a handful of mechanics that echo the core themes of City of Mist—myth and money, masks and misdirection—while giving you a concentrated toolkit for ongoing campaigns.

- Fortune currency: a resource that players and the GM can tap to bend outcomes, secure favors, or unlock a critical clue. It’s not just about “more dice” or “better rolls.” It’s about narrative leverage. The book threads Fortune into scene-building, letting the players feel the weight of seams in the city’s fabric—the moments when luck feels imminent and then collapses into a new possibility.
- Fate and Serendipity mechanics: a set of micro-opportunities that appear at the table, nudging players toward a thematic beat. One moment your investigator stumbles upon a password, the next a rooftop chase forces them to improvise with a mythic toolkit in a practical way. The luck-driven features encourage players to talk less about numbers and more about the story they’re collaboratively producing.
- Foreshadowing and Prognostication: Fortune Row encourages GMs to plant seeds that take a season to sprout. The book suggests modular scenes and plot hooks you can thread into multiple sessions without the risk of “themed fatigue.” It respects the players’ time and your own campaign’s arc by offering both short-term payoffs and long-tail consequences.
- Masks and factions in a micro-neighborhood: the Masks concept (the mythic roles players inhabit) gets refinements that feel more tactile when you’re running a localized arc. Expect new masks tailored to Fortune Row’s vibe—individuals who are famous in the wrong circles, those who can flip a fortune-forged outcome with a single whisper, and others who trade in secrets like currency.

Do not go in expecting a wholesale ruleset revolution; Fortune Row is a refinement, a sharpening of the game’s edge for a more personal, location-centric experience. It’s about turning a block into a character—the streetlamp your ally, the graffiti your codex, the corner shop your oracle.

### Character creation and advancement in Fortune Row
Fortune Row doesn’t throw away the old character archetypes; it enriches them with neighborhood-specific flavor. You’ll find new starting packs that feel like micro-dossiers rather than mere stat blocks. These include:

- Fortune Row Scions: characters who carry the myth of a neighborhood in their blood, their power tied to a lineage of local legends and the ability to navigate Fortune Row’s social circuits with ease.
- Street-runner operatives: scrappy investigators who live by wit and improvisation, using the local myths as a toolbox rather than a crutch. They’re built for fast play and quick escalation.
- Velvet operants: enigmatic manipulators who excel at negotiation, misdirection, and the art of turning a minor setback into a major opportunity.

Each starter pack comes with tailored gear, a few signature moves, and a short “row-side quest” to get the party hooked into Fortune Row’s rhythms quickly. If you like the option to lean into a character’s mythic identity while remaining grounded in a neighborhood’s everyday drama, these templates deliver the goods with a wink and a nod toward long-term storytelling.

### The GM toolkit: encounters, locales, and long-game hooks
One of Fortune Row’s strengths is its practical GM support. It doesn’t leave you with “here’s how you run a scene” and nothing else; it gives you:

- A curated roster of locales that feel distinct yet interconnected: a pawnshop that’s also a data hub, a bodega that’s a front for a syndicate, a church that houses a cryptic reliquary, and more. Each location comes with encounter seeds, narrative hooks, and a suggested progression that respects both the players’ curiosity and the city’s broader mythic machinery.
- Faction schematics: who runs Fortune Row, who owes whom, and what the power balance looks like in a neighborhood where every decision costs something. The factions are not faceless antagonists; they’re players in an evolving drama with real consequences.
- Arc scaffolding: prebuilt mini-arcs that can be dropped into a campaign with minimal prep. You can slice these into a single session, or knit them into a larger season for a full-blown City of Mist experience.

The results feel like a well-curated heist weekend that never stops having consequences; the neighbors notice, the myths remember, and the players feel the delicious ache of decisions that echo beyond the current scene.

### World-building and tone: how Fortune Row handles mood
Fortune Row leans into the city-as-character idea with gusto. The tone is a blend of retro-futurist glamour and screwball noir—think a speakeasy that knows who you are and what you owe, set against a skyline that refuses to commit to one aesthetic. The writing nudges you toward empathy for the characters who live in gray areas—the folks who trade in secrets, the dreamers who still believe in legends, and the cynics who know the system inside and out and still choose to fight anyway.

That said, Fortune Row doesn’t sugarcoat the consequences of mythic power. It leans into moral ambiguity, making sure players can feel the weight of decisions that ripple through a block where every pawnshop receipt is a potential clue, and every rooftop line of sight could become a trapdoor into a new conspiracy. If you enjoy stories where the hero’s choices are meaningful and the city answers with a chorus of tiny, sharp, almost cruel twists, you’ll feel right at home.

## Layout, art, and accessibility: is Fortune Row easy to use?
Fortune Row is friendly to both veteran City of Mist players and curious newcomers who want a doorway into the system without committing to a city-wide campaign first. The book’s layout carefully balances functional pages with evocative art. You’ll find step-by-step instructions for quick-start play, plus expanded sections for longer arcs. The font size and white space feel chosen for extended sessions—no cramped blocks of text that induce eye strain after the third page.

### Maps and visuals
The included maps and locale drawings are well-integrated with the text. They’re not just pretty; they’re practical. You’ll be able to hand a player a map and say, “This is your corridor to the next clue,” or use the illustrations to play “spot the myth.” The maps also hold up in a virtual session when you’re running remote play; you can share a clean copy or embed them directly into online roll20-like tools.

### Accessibility considerations
Fortune Row’s tone remains accessible; the mechanics are explained with the same breezy, punchy clarity that fans appreciate in the core book. There are micro-entries that explain how to adjust power scales for players who want a lighter or heavier experience, and a helpful sidebar about representing culturally diverse mythologies in a respectful and non-stereotypical manner. If you value inclusivity and clear play guidance, Fortune Row does not disappoint in those departments.

## Play experience: what it feels like at the table
Players who are in the mood for a mood will find Fortune Row delightful. The emphasis on atmosphere translates into session flow: you’ll have scenes that feel like cinematic set pieces, punctuated by smaller, character-driven beats that let players show off their Masks’ personality. The dice system is responsive, the luck mechanics create satisfying narrative tweaks, and the new gears for Fate and Fortune give players meaningful levers that don’t grind the game to a halt after a bad roll.

A typical session pattern might look like this:
- Scene setup in Fortune Row’s micro-neighborhood; players decide which Mask or Mythos to lean into.
- A Fortune moment shakes out—an ally betrays a patron, a clue opens a new path, or a risky bargain is struck.
- The GM rolls for a consequence; the players respond with narrative judo—their choices shape the city’s reaction and reveal more about their character.
- A closing beat that ties back to a larger arc that the GM is weaving, with a tease for the next game night.

If you enjoy a mix of investigative play, social intrigue, and occasional action-set-pieces, Fortune Row provides a satisfying cadence that invites long-form storytelling without feeling bloated.

## How it stacks up against the core rulebook
There are two big things Fortune Row does well:

- It localizes myth and power into a neighborhood-centric lens. This makes it easier to run campaigns with a consistent tone and a clear sense of place without having to build a city-wide mythology from scratch.
- It gives GMs a flexible toolkit for long-term arcs. You can weave a few local mysteries into a season that still lands on big, mythic milestones.

That said, Fortune Row isn’t a substitute for the core rules. If you’re starting fresh and you want broad-strokes mechanics, the core book remains essential. Fortune Row shines as a companion piece that adds texture, mood, and a set of practical tools for campaign construction in a single micro-sandbox.

## Replayability and campaign longevity
Fortune Row offers a meaningful increase in replayability by virtue of its modular scene seeds and region-specific hooks. The neighborhood’s factions and locales don’t feel like placeholders; they feel alive and evolving. The same players can approach Fortune Row from different angles across multiple campaigns: some will lean into the political intrigue, others toward street-level detective work, and a few will chase the mythic bargains that keep the city’s legends turning in new directions.

If you’re worried about how the book handles long-form campaigns, fear not: the design encourages arc progression without forcing meta-knowledge. The GM toolkit is built to stand up to weeks or months of play, with the potential to interweave Fortune Row’s micro-arcs with broader City of Mist plots. In practice, that means you can run a “Fortune Row: Year One” arc and then snap it into a larger, city-wide narrative without major rewrites.

## Who should buy Fortune Row?
- GMs looking for a compact, high-mood sandbox to anchor a campaign in a single city block.
- Players who want a strong sense of place and a mythic identity that feels personal rather than panoramic.
- Teams that enjoy the blend of investigative noir with magical mayhem and a little street-level politics.

If you crave a game where a single alley can pivot a city’s fate and where your character’s Mask isn’t just cosmetic but a lever for opportunity (and risk), Fortune Row delivers. If your table prefers epic-scale cosmic conspiracies over neighborhood-level schemes, you’ll still get a lot of value from the atmosphere and tools—but you might find some sections narrower than your dreams require.

## Quick-start tips for running Fortune Row at your table
- Start with a local hook that ties a faction to a concrete goal in Fortune Row. Make the players decide which faction’s plan to disrupt, support, or co-opt.
- Use the Fortune currency early to show players that luck isn’t a passive phenomenon; it requires risk, negotiation, and a willingness to embrace the consequences.
- Introduce a recurring NPC who embodies the neighborhood’s paradox: a figure who can grant information or power, but always with a price that reveals a piece of the city’s mythic fabric.
- Run a sequence of three to five scenes that escalate in personal stakes for the PCs. Each session should end with a cliffhanger that draws the party into the next chapter of Fortune Row’s arc.
- Reserve a session for “cleanup” where the party consolidates power, assesses the moral cost of their choices, and sets up a broader investigation that ties Fortune Row into the city-wide mythos.

## Final recommendation
If you’re a die-hard City of Mist fan who wants a more intimate sandbox—one that hums with the energy of a single, living neighborhood—Fortune Row is a sensible, stylish choice. It doesn’t replace the core system; it enriches it with a concentrated blueprint for mood, momentum, and modular storytelling. The writing is confident, the ideas are practical, and the tonal balance is exquisitely tuned for campaigns where every decision matters as much as the myth behind it.

If you prefer your RPGs templated for “blockbusters” rather than “block by block” city-building, you may not need Fortune Row to scratch that itch. But if you enjoy micro-drama, social maneuvering, and mythic flavor woven into a tangible urban fabric, this rulebook will feel like coming home to a favorite, well-loved city block—one that still manages to surprise you with a fresh alleyway and a new legend.

In short: Fortune Row is a strong addition for City of Mist enthusiasts who crave a tighter, more personal micro-world with a lot of room to grow. It’s not a full replacement for the core system, but it’s a delightful extension that pays huge dividends in mood, atmosphere, and narrative control. If you’re planning a season-long arc or a campaign that sits in one neighborhood’s heartbeat, Fortune Row should be on your shelf.
