## What exactly is in this 118-card lot?
This isn’t a pristine, factory-sealed starter set. It’s a well-loved pile that looks like it survived a few weekend garage sales with a cape and a cape-shaped shadow. The 118 cards are a kaleidoscopic mix of hero cards, mission cards, and a handful of supporting cards that gave old OverPower its push. Here’s what you’ll typically expect in a lot of this size, with some practical notes on handling and use:

- Hero cards: The backbone of any OverPower play. These are your marquee names—Spider-Man, Wolverine, Iron Man, Captain Marvel, and friends—each with a few lines of power and combat ability. Expect duplicates and some heroes with unique team-ups; in a late 90s set, a common cardboard hero might still shout, _you shall not pass_ with a smile.
- Mission cards: The objective packets. These cards push the players toward specific goals, like defeating a villain before a clock runs out or surviving a gauntlet of encounters. Depending on the print run, these can offer varied objectives or alternate win conditions, which is where the nostalgia really kicks in.
- Power and support cards: These are the crunchy bits that add flavor to battles—special abilities, equipment, or temporary boosts. You’ll find a mix of both useful effects and those “fun flavor” moves that let your Hulk smash a little differently while cracking a joke about grammar in all caps.
- Duplicates and fillers: It’s not unusual to see a handful of duplicate cards, especially for popular heroes. That means you can mock up a quick two-player game or assemble a proxy deck that captures the vibe without hunting down every rare variant.

The total distribution varies from lot to lot, but with 118 cards you’ve got enough to spin up a pretty authentic hero-vs-mission vibe. If you’re curious about how to separate the wheat from the chaff, we’ll cover condition and valuation in the next section.

## Assessing condition and authenticity: what to look for
Condition is king when you’re dealing with any vintage CCG lot. Here’s a practical checklist to help you triage this 118-card bundle without turning it into a treasure hunt that ends in existential dread:

- Card stock and edges: Look for smooth corners, minimal whitening, and no major creases. The 1990s print quality was decent, but the corners can take hits when a lot has seen the backseat of a car on a road trip. If most cards look crisp with light edge wear, you’re in a good ballpark.
- Color and print consistency: OverPower art is vivid and bold. If you see heavy fading or blotches, that’s a sign of heavy sun exposure or moisture. In a garage sale scenario, humidity can do more damage than a comic-book villain with a misunderstanding of physics.
- Authenticity markers: The set has a very specific font, layout, and card back design. If you’re the type who enjoys the thrill of a scavenger hunt, verify that the hero and mission cards share the same back and corner rounding as the rest. In a worst-case scenario, you might encounter repackaging or misprints; that can be a feature, not a bug, for a collector who loves oddities—but you’ll want to disclose it clearly in your listing.
- Damage indicators: Water stains, creases, and surface wear that breaks art into tiny, unplayable patches are signs you should price downward. If a card looks more like modern abstract art than a game piece, it belongs in a thrift store’s art wall, not in a playable deck.

Pro-tip: separate the lot into three piles for your own sanity: keepers (mint to near-mint heroes you actually want), potential playables (useful for a casual re-creation), and pass-alongs (the duplicates and the rough-looking cards you still want to color-coded in a price tag). The goal is to avoid turning a fun nostalgia piece into a scrappy scavenger hunt that drains your emotional energy and your wallet.

## How to price and sell a vintage OverPower lot without losing your mind
Pricing is partly science, mostly vibes, and a dash of “what the market will bear on a given weekend.” Here’s a practical approach you can use to maximize returns while staying honest with buyers:

- Start with a baseline: If you’ve got a complete, near-mint-to-mint pile with a few rare or sought-after cards, you can set a modest premium over the sum of the individual card values. If you’re missing a chunk of the core heroes, you’ll want to discount accordingly.
- Account for variety: A lot with a nice spread of popular heroes and a balanced mix of mission cards tends to fetch more than a pile heavy on duplicates. Buyers love a balanced pool they can actually build from.
- Highlight condition specifics: Buyers love transparency. Photograph edges, fronts, and backs. A short, clear description that lists common surface wear and any noticeable misprints helps build trust and reduces back-and-forth haggling.
- Consider bundling: Some buyers are collectors looking for a “playable” deck while others want a shelf display. Offer two options: (1) the complete lot as-is for a single purchase and (2) separated mini-lots (e.g., 60 hero cards + 40 mission cards + 18 supporting cards). Bundling can improve perceived value and speed up the sale.
- Price bands by condition: Mint to near-mint cards are the unicorns. If most of your lot is good-to-average, price it accordingly and point out the optimistic aspects (e.g., a couple of near-mint heroes, a rare mission card with bright print).
- Be mindful of market inflation: The vintage card market moves on impulse. A lot might fetch a surprising amount on a good weekend, or drop a bit when the weather is poor and attention is elsewhere. Don’t inflate too aggressively; the key currency is trust.

Document everything in your listing. A short video showing you flipping through the cards, coupled with crisp close-ups, can convert “I’m not sure” into a confident buyer in under a minute. ## Care, storage, and display: keeping the magic pristine
Collectibles in a pile are only as good as their storage. Here’s a practical transport-and-tire plan to keep this 118-card lot in good shape while you figure out what to do with it:

- Sleeves and top-loaders: Start with semi-rigid sleeves or top-loaders for hero and mission cards you value. These protect corners during handling and shipping. If you worry about bending during grading or sale photos, a soft mat and a rigid box can do wonders.
- Storage boxes: Use dividers to separate hero cards, mission cards, and power cards. This not only makes listing easier but also preserves the experience of fanning through a well-kept deck at show-and-tell with friends.
- Environment: Keep cards away from direct sunlight and humidity. The old stock is durable but can fade or warp with sun and moisture. A simple climate-controlled corner is enough to maintain the vibe without turning your living room into a science lab.
- Documentation: If you’re listing the lot, provide a simple card-count breakdown and a few close-ups of edge wear. It goes a long way toward trust and faster sales.

Illustrating the care angle, here’s a sample photo setup idea that yields great listing visuals: a clean white background, a soft cloth to avoid glare, and a gentle “fanned out” display of hero cards in the foreground with mission cards layered behind. The human eye loves a clear focal point; your listing will too.
