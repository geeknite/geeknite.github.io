## What is Phase 10?
### The concept
Phase 10 is a card-based puzzle that players solve in ten stages. Each phase is a different combination; you progress only after you can lay down the cards for the current phase. The kicker is that you must complete your phase to advance. If you draw the right card, you can lay down a whole phase in one go; if not, you keep at it until you do.

### The components
108 cards in four colors (red, blue, green, yellow) with numbers 1 through 12, plus four skip cards and eight wild cards. The deck is known for its bright, often neon look. Wilds substitute for any card in a phase, while skip cards skip the next player''s turn. The physical cards feel sturdy enough to survive a decade of family nights. If you like keepsakes, the box art is a time capsule of that era and a reminder of how far board game aesthetics have come.

### Setup and initial deal
Each player begins with ten cards; the rest forms the draw pile with one card turned over to start the discard pile. The aim is to complete your first phase, go out, and then chase the remaining phases while minimizing cards in hand at round end. ## How to Play: The Basics
### Turn order
Play moves clockwise. On your turn, draw one card from the draw pile or take the top card from the discard pile; then either lay down your current phase if you have the required cards or simply discard one card to end your turn. If you lay down a phase, you still get to keep your turn and then discard as usual.

### Action cards: Wild and Skip
Wild cards can represent any card to help complete a phase; they are the lifelines of your run. Skip cards, on the other hand, skip the next player''s turn, giving you a chance to adjust your hand and maybe stack a master plan while others scramble.

### Laying down phases and going out
When you have the exact cards for your current phase, you can lay them down in front of you. You must complete your phase before moving to the next. After laying down your phase, you keep your turn and discard. The moment you go out (no cards left after discarding), you''ve finished the round. If you go out with wilds in your hand, they still count toward completing your phase. The round ends when everyone has either gone out or passed their last turn.

## The Ten Phases in Detail
### Phase 1: 2 sets of 3
Two separate sets of three cards of the same value. Example: 3-3-3 and 7-7-7. This is the easy starter phase that separates the casuals from the determined. It feels almost too tame, which is what makes it dangerous: a false sense of security.

### Phase 2: 1 set of 3 + 1 run of 4
One triplet plus a sequence of four consecutive cards in any color combination. Example: 5-5-5 and 4-5-6-7. The run adds a new color dimension to the puzzle, forcing you to balance numbers with order.

### Phase 3: 1 set of 4 + 1 run of 4
A four-of-a-kind plus a four-card run. This phase rewards a bit more hand manipulation and value distribution across your deck.

### Phase 4: Run of 7
A straight of seven cards, not necessarily starting at the same point for every hand, but you need seven in a row. This phase tests your ability to navigate gaps and bridge them with wilds.

### Phase 5: Run of 8
A longer run, now eight in a row. The longer run raises the probability of dead cards in your hand, so you shift from speed to precision.

### Phase 6: Run of 9
A nine-card sprint. At this point, you start to feel the round length and the pressure to go out before chaos truly erupts.

### Phase 7: 2 sets of 4
Two four-of-a-kinds. The complexity increases as you juggle two large sets that must sit happily in your hand at the same time.

### Phase 8: 7 cards of one color
Seven cards all same color. This phase flips the usual numbers-first approach to color-first, which can throw off opponents who rely on color patterns for their own phase planning.

### Phase 9: 1 set of 5 + 1 set of 2
A five-of-a-kind plus a pair. It is a test of your ability to create a big set while keeping a usable second pairing alive.

### Phase 10: 1 set of 5 + 1 set of 3
The final gauntlet: a five-of-a-kind plus a three-of-a-kind. The round closes with a bang as players realize who can finalize the chain and who will be stuck with a stubborn hand.

## Strategy and Tactics
### Core principles
- Start with speed on early phases, but never abandon defense. If you rush to Phase 4 while everyone else stars at Phase 2, the late game can become chaos very quickly.
- Wilds are gold. Use them thoughtfully to bridge gaps that would otherwise derail your phase progression.
- Read the room. Watch discarded cards; they reveal what opponents are chasing and what they fear you might pick up next.
- Don''t pin yourself to a single strategy. If you see a better path by switching goals mid-round, go with it, but be mindful of tipping your hand to others.

### Advanced tactics
- The double-draw mindset: if you can draw two cards on a turn, do it only when it accelerates your phase progression or when it denies a critical card to an opponent.
- Timing wilds with runs: when you are chasing a long run, wilds become rescue devices; saving them until you absolutely need them can keep you in the hunt longer.
- Safe discards: avoid giving away a card you know an opponent needs unless it can hurt them more than it helps you.

### House rules that work well
- Make starting hands seven or eight cards to shorten the night and keep energy high.
- Allow a single optional redraw per round for players who accidentally discard a critical card early.
- Introduce a timer for each turn to keep the pace brisk and the jokes flowing.
- Use a single extra wild at the start to ramp up chaos and drama without extending the round too much.
