## Unboxing and First Impressions

The moment the mailman drops the box on your doormat, you know this is not just a regular stationery affair. Zombicide Black Plague is a world where medieval knights and zombies collide in a symphony of dice, plastic, and bright red meeples. Troy and Evil Troy are two variants of the same legendary hero archetype turned to nightmarish politics of the undead. Inside the sealed wrap you will find the following core components: two miniatures for the new characters, a handful of zombie miniatures that align with the Black Plague aesthetic, a set of scenario tiles, a handful of event and objective cards, and a rulebook that pretends to be complex but is, in reality, a well-curated set of guidelines for your next gaming session.

The miniatures themselves look crisp, with clean lines and excellent detail that makes you go, yes, I will paint these because how could you resist the temptation of repainting a cardboard dream into something that looks like you actually spent a weekend sculpting in a workshop:

![Miniature close-up](/assets/images/zombicide-troy-evil-troy-miniatures.jpg)

The Troy figure is the kind of hero that could have existed in a history book, if history included more teeth, more grimace, and a decent cape that will inevitably get torn during a dramatic reveal on the tabletop. Evil Troy is the darker mirror, with a sculpt that screams dramatic lighting and questionable moral choices. If there’s a moral to the story, it’s that even in a zombie apocalypse, there’s a bit of vanity in the hero’s pose.

The Black Plague kit itself brings the classic atmosphere: gleaming gold accents in the citadel art, a dark palette for the undead, and the occasional splash of red to remind you that yes, people will die here and you will be the one deciding how. The box exterior promises hours of cooperative chaos, while the interior delivers enough little plastic morsels to convince even the most stoic gamer that this is the moment to dig in and pretend you know how to manage a siege while also keeping a stable of zombies from overrunning your kitchen table.

## Box Contents and What It Means for Setup

The standard Zombicide Black Plague core set has a rhythm. You lay out the board, you assemble a handful of boards, you deploy zombies in a cinematic pace, and you watch as your crew of survivors either becomes legendary or becomes a casualty of miscommunication and dice luck. Troy and Evil Troy slot in as an interesting twist, providing a new dynamic to the existing survivor and zombie matrix. They aren’t simply extra figures; they are catalysts for new strategies, encouraging players to rethink the typical approach and to consider how a single powerful character can alter player cooperation and tension.

Inside the new sealed box you should expect the following as a baseline (subject to minor regional packaging variations):

- 2 sculpted hero miniatures: Troy and Evil Troy
- 2 double-sided hero stat cards with unique abilities that reflect their thematic identity
- 1 token sheet for abilities and status markers
- 2 double-sided terrain boards or tiles corresponding to new scenarios in the Black Plague setting
- 12 to 16 zombie miniatures that align with the Black Plague color palette and a few special infection tokens
- a deck of scenario and objective cards that unlock new play modes
- a small rulebook or an addendum that clarifies how Troy and Evil Troy interact with the rest of the survivors and the undead
- two dice that feel like they have seen both better days and even more chaotic nights

The unboxing ritual also includes the satisfying snap of components sliding into place and the almost ceremonial rigidity of the plastic trays. Nothing will fall out during a dramatic game end, which is a blessing when you’re trying to pack up for a midnight session without losing track of a single tile in a house full of children who are also playing a game where there is no safe zone except the safe zone you designate by your own stubbornness.

## Theme, Flavor, and Rules in Practice

Zombicide Black Plague has always thrived on a fusion of medieval fantasy and fast-paced cooperative chaos. The core rules are approachable: use your survivors to gather weapons, clear zones of undead, and herd the living and the dead into a set of objectives that typically involve grabbing an artifact, surviving a round, or escaping an area under siege. Troy and Evil Troy introduce a flavor layer that spices up the board without turning the entire experience into a monologue about one supremely powerful character who stomps across the map.

From a design perspective, Troy is a classic bold hero: a unit with a strong melee profile, reliable survivability, and a handful of special abilities that allow him to lead charges and draw the attention of the horde. Evil Troy, in contrast, demonstrates the seductive pull of power: perhaps a slightly higher risk-reward profile, with abilities that tempt players to take on riskier gambits. In practical terms, this means you’ll have to communicate with your teammates more deliberately, as Evil Troy’s choices can create tension that is both thrilling and potentially destructive to the team’s overall plan.

The interaction with the core rules is where the fun begins. If you’ve played Black Plague before, you know the scale: a board covered in zone tiles, with zombies moving in a wave that can overwhelm even the best planned route. The Troy duo adds a twist: their actions can influence how many zombies reach your survivors, how quickly objectives are completed, and how you allocate your limited resources. It’s a reminder that in a zombie apocalypse, leadership matters, but so does the willingness to improvise when a perfect plan instantly becomes a bit more complicated because a new hero has arrived on a mount of blood and charisma.

If you want to see how Troy and Evil Troy change the risk calculus on the board, you’ll want to try a few scenarios that highlight their strengths and weaknesses. For instance, a scenario that requires a quick sweep through multiple zones benefits from Troy’s leadership and mobility, while Evil Troy’s more aggressive abilities are best when you’re chasing a gauntlet of objectives that require a bolder approach. The net effect is a pacing difference: a game that can slip into a cautious crawl becomes a sprint if you misread the mood or you simply adopt a plan that makes Evil Troy feel like a magnet for trouble and glory at the same time.

## Components Quality and Aesthetics

Guillotine Games has a track record for delivering components that are sturdy and visually appealing. The Black Plague line is a demonstration of this, with a palette that feels cohesive across all elements while maintaining enough variety to keep the eye engaged during long sessions. The Troy and Evil Troy minis are well-sculpted and stand up to the scrutiny of close painting sessions. The sculpts strike a balance between heroic silhouette and fantasy grotesque—the kind of design language that says heroism and horror can share the same square inch of tabletop without collapsing into neon cartoonishness.

Two things deserve special mention: the bases and the tokens. The bases are stable and the little zombie pawns are well-defined enough to identify in the middle of a heated round, even if you’re trying to read the rulebook by the glow of a single desk lamp. The tokens are crisp and readable, which matters when you’re juggling a hand of item cards and event triggers under the table while a zombie horde claws toward your survivors’ last fortress of safety. If there is a knock on the production quality, it’s that some folks may wish for thicker card stock on the event decks; for most, the balance is perfectly serviceable and the tactile satisfaction remains high.

When it comes to the packaging, the sealed box is a promise of freshness that you can savor. The outer wrap may crackle in your hands, but the inner tray remains tight and organized, a microcosm of how a well-run siege can feel like a well-run pantry. In the end, you get the sense that Guillotine Games is aware of the mania that surrounds a new edition: they give you enough shiny stuff to keep you engaged, while not so much that you’re overwhelmed by a mountain of filler that will collect dust in your attic like a trophy for forgotten promotions.

## Gameplay Experience: Lighting Up Your Night

If you’ve played Zombicide before, you know the core loop: you move, you search, you craft, you fight, you fall asleep thinking about that last critical dice roll that didn’t come through. Bring Troy and Evil Troy into a Black Plague session, and you’ll discover a different energy. The interplay between these two heroes and your group is a reminder that cooperative play is not just about coordinating moves; it’s about reading the table’s mood as a living thing. There are moments of dramatic tension when you realize a single misstep could cascade into a chain of failures, and other moments of ridiculous triumph when a well-timed hero ability saves the day and makes your table erupt in that cathartic chorus of cheers that only a board game could generate.

A practical note: the added complexity from the Troy duo is not a barrier for new players, but it does require a bit more attention to alignment of objectives and the sequence of actions. The rulebook addition is clear enough for a first-timer to pick up in a single session, while veterans will relish the nuanced decisions that emerge when you factor in Evil Troy’s unpredictable tendencies. The result is a game that remains approachable but offers a depth that rewards thoughtful planning and a willingness to roll the dice with gusto.

In terms of balance, Troy shines as a reliable anchor for your team, providing a consistent baseline from which your crew can organize tactics. Evil Troy introduces an optional hazard-lite mechanic that can spice up a session without tipping into ridiculous, ‘laughably overpowered’ territory. The two figures together create a dynamic where teamwork and adaptation become the star players rather than a single hero stealing the show. If your gaming group enjoys the occasional curveball and a dash of thematic drama, you will likely find that this expansion is a hit.

With Zombicide, the sealed box has the dual role of preserving the pristine miniatures and cards while also ensuring you have a future shopping trip to your local game store or online retailer to actually play the thing. The value proposition rests on two pillars: the accessories and the replayability. The sets are not just “more zombies and a new hero;” they are a narrative nudge that invites you to create new stories around your existing Black Plague campaigns.

If you’re considering the sealed copy as an investment piece, a few things are worth noting. First, the market for Zombicide is robust: new expansions appear and vanish with surprising frequency as players discover the joy of mixing and matching various campaigns. Second, the physical quality remains high enough to justify a premium for a set that you intend to display and occasionally bring to life for game nights. Finally, there is the value of the experience: the social capital of getting friends together, sharing a meal, and laughing in the face of the living dead as you debate strategy and mock dice that fail you at precisely the wrong moment.

## Final Verdict and Recommendation

So, is the new Troy and Evil Troy expansion in Zombicide Black Plague worth your time and money if you love fast-paced co-op with a heavy dose of fantasy undead? The short answer is: yes, with caveats. If you crave fresh mechanics that alter how your team approaches a scenario and you enjoy a narrative layer that lets your hero and his dark mirror take the spotlight for a moment, this expansion will scratch that itch beautifully. If, on the other hand, you prefer to keep your Zombicide to a lean core set and you don’t want to disrupt a familiar balance, this expansion might feel like a big pastry in a party where everyone only wanted a small bite.

For most players who already own Black Plague and enjoy adding new characters and tactical considerations, Troy and Evil Troy offer a compelling reason to open the box and dive into a weekend of planning sessions and silly, heroic frustration. It’s not a revolution in how Zombicide plays, but it is a reminder of why people keep coming back: the promise that with enough planning, courage, and a little bit of luck, you can outsmart the undead and still keep a little humanity intact as you celebrate a hard-won victory.

In other words, if you want a fresh narrative thread woven into your Black Plague nights, if you want to test new team dynamics, and if you want to own a sealed copy that will one day be a nostalgic souvenir of the early 2020s resurgence of co-op board games, this is a delightful addition to your shelf. It might not be the cheapest piece of plastic you’ve ever bought, but it’s an investment in the kind of evenings that make friends count the days to the next patch, the next scenario, and the next heroic misstep.
