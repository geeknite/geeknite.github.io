## What is Herd Mentality?
Herd Mentality is a social deduction-ish party game with a twist: you don’t need to be ‘right’ to win; you need to be aligned with the majority. Each round presents a prompt—typically a question that invites a creative or humorous answer. Players privately write their answer and then reveal them all at once. The catch? Everyone secretly votes on which answer they think the majority would choose. If you picked the majority answer, you score points; if your answer becomes the majority, you also score. It’s a delicate dance between self-expression and people-reading. The acrylics of the game are simple: a deck of prompt cards, a bunch of answer boards, score markers, and a color-coded system that keeps the chaos clean enough to still be family-friendly.

The genius of Herd Mentality lies in the social math: you don’t need to be clever in the classic sense; you need to anticipate what your friends and family will consider “the herd.” If you’ve got a knack for spotting group psychology (or if you’re simply convenient at agreeing with everyone else to avoid the awkward silence), you’ll shine. If you’re the lone contrarian who insists on a wildly individualistic answer, you’ll get moments of glory… and maybe a gentle ribbing about your rebellious culinary choices or your obsession with obscure sci-fi trivia. Either way, the table becomes a stage, and you’re the performer who both leads and follows in perfect, hilarious synchrony.

## How to play: a quick rhythm guide
Herd Mentality can feel deceptively fast once you get the hang of it. Here’s a practical, no-fluff walkthrough to get you from “First round jitters” to “We all know what grandma will pick.” The cadence is what makes it sing at family gatherings: short rounds, big laughs, and the ongoing humor of misreading a crowd.

### Setup (5 minutes or less)
- Gather players and choose a score target (e.g., first to 15 points wins).
- Shuffle the prompts deck and place it within easy reach.
- Each player takes an answer sheet and a voting marker.
- Decide on the herd mechanism for this session: some groups use “majority wins” by tallying votes, others add a special twist card to spice things up.

### Round flow (about 10–15 minutes per round, depending on人数)
1) A prompt card is revealed and read aloud by a designated player. Prompts are designed to elicit a range of plausible answers and, occasionally, hilariously weird ones.
2) Each player writes down an answer privately. No peeking at others’ responses; keep those eyes focused on your own sheet and the snack you’re pretending is part of your strategy.
3) All answers are revealed at once. Players discuss and interpret what the herd might think, but there’s a catch: you’re not voting on which answer you like best; you’re guessing which answer is most likely to be chosen by the majority.
4) Players secretly vote on the answer they think is the herd’s pick. The scoring happens after votes are revealed: you score if your answer is chosen by the herd, or if your choice is the herd’s chosen answer.
5) Tally points and rotate the role of prompt reader. The round ends with a sense of sibling rivalry that’s never truly mean-spirited—just competitive enough to spark banter.

Pro-tip: keep your answers punchy and memorable. Short, witty lines tend to stick in the herd’s mind and get picked more often than over-thought, verbose responses. You’re not writing a novella; you’re tapping into group memory and social signals.

### Variants and house rules
Every group has its own quirks. A few house rules that often boost the energy without breaking the game:
- Speed Round: reduce writing time to 20 seconds to quicken the pace and force bolder choices.
- No-Repeat Rule: if multiple players write the same answer, it can count as additional herd votes if you’re the only one to guess it correctly. This adds pressure to think like a crowd while still maintaining your own voice.
- The Mixed Herd: include a “wild card” prompt that can be answered in any direction, pushing players to either align with a crowd or boldly risk something unique.
- Family-Friendly Filter: pre-select prompts that are safe for kids by marking certain cards with a “G-rated” sticker. This keeps the game accessible for younger players without dulling the humor.

House rules aren’t about bending the game into an extreme version; they’re about shaping the night into something your group will remember. We’ve found that a light, well-judged rule tweak often makes the difference between a memorable night and a meh night.

## Why Herd Mentality lands (or misses) for family nights
Here’s the measuring stick we use at Geeknite for family-night suitability: accessibility, humor that lands across ages, and the kind of engagement that doesn’t single out players, but invites everyone to participate. Herd Mentality tends to score well in these areas for several reasons.

## Strategy and best practices
You don’t need to be a social psychologist to win at Herd Mentality, but a little awareness helps. Here are practical tips to dominate the herd—or at least not get left behind by it.

- Read the room early, but don’t reveal your hand too soon. In the moments before everyone writes, try to guess what seems to dominate the conversation. The trick is to avoid locking in too early; the herd might shift as new answers pop up.
- Write concise but sharp answers. One-liners tend to travel faster and resonate more than a long-winded paragraph. Your goal is to be memorable enough to be considered by the herd.
- Listen to the table’s energy. If a vibe emerges toward silly chaos, lean into that. If the group is going for a clever, dry humor, invert your approach in a way that still feels authentic to you.
- Use social memory, not just social proof. If your group tends to converge on a theme (like pop culture references or kitchen-related prompts), you can ride that wave while still injecting your unique twist.
- Don’t fear the tie-breaker. In many rounds, two or more answers might share the herd’s favor. Ties are not a dead end; they’re an opportunity to show you’re in sync with the crowd, or to reveal your own bold streak.

The real skill is balancing comfort with risk. If you’re the meme-obsessed one, you can push for a meme-y answer that you think others will replicate. If you’re the quiet strategist, you can aim for a more grounded, widely acceptable response and save your wittiest ideas for future rounds.

Herd Mentality is at its best when all players feel included. The prompts are broad enough to invite diverse perspectives, and the game generally avoids content that would alienate younger players or less talkative participants. That said, as with any social activity, you’ll want to gauge your group’s comfort level with humor and personal references. A well-timed self-deprecating joke about your own fashion choices, for example, can be a safe but highly effective way to set the tone.

For families with younger players, it helps to keep prompts within a certain age band and actively monitor the room to ensure no one feels sidelined. A rotation system where adults explicitly take turns reading prompts can be a light-yet-effective way to maintain pace and equity.

## The pros and cons in a nutshell
Pros:
- Easy entry, quick setup, and fast rounds keep everyone engaged.
- Strong social dynamics; great for breaking the ice with new players.
- Colorful, friendly components that invite a broad age range to participate.
- Flexible house-rule support; you can tailor it to fit your group’s vibe.

Cons:
- It’s not a deep strategy experience; if you want intense tactical play, this isn’t your game.
- Some rounds can feel repetitive if your group is very familiar with the prompts.
- People who are naturally contrarian may dominate by forcing unusual answers that others won’t pick, which can derail the herd a bit.

If you’re in the “family-night as a ritual” camp, the pros tend to far outweigh the cons. If you’re chasing a designer board game with heavy mechanics, you might want to keep Herd Mentality as a lighter complement rather than your main game of the night.

## Comparisons: Herd Mentality vs. other social titles
If you’ve played Codenames, Just One, or Telestrations, you’ll recognize some shared DNA: social reading, collective humor, and a touch of pressure to perform. Herd Mentality has its own flavor because the core puzzle is not about word association or drawing; it’s about anticipating a group’s shared instinct. Here’s a quick breakdown:

- vs. Codenames: Herd Mentality is more about social inference and less about word clues and team-based deduction. It’s more approachable for younger players and casual groups.
- vs. Just One: Just One is individual creativity with a limited set of constraints; Herd Mentality emphasizes the herd’s willingness to conform or diverge, which yields different kinds of laughs.
- vs. Telestrations: Telestrations is visually driven and chaotic in a fun way. Herd Mentality relies on verbal humor and the social reading of a crowd, offering a different flavor of group bonding.

If you’re picking a single party-game to anchor a few family nights, you’ll want to weigh how much you value speed, social inference, and a shared experience with a broad age range. Herd Mentality ticks those boxes for many households, especially when you’re seeking something that’s easy to pick up, stress-free to run, and wildly entertaining.
