## Setup and components: getting ready to rumble

### Quick setup for ten players

The Party Pack prides itself on brevity. In a typical living room scenario you should be able to get ten players into a round within a few minutes. Here’s a nutshell guide to speed-running your setup:

1) Shuffle the deck and deal a starting hand to each player. The exact hand size isn’t volcanic, but you’ll generally want enough cards so players feel the risk from round to round without feeling overwhelmed.
2) Place any Defuse cards accessible to everyone or distribute them as the game’s rules specify. Defuse cards are the lifelines that keep players in the game after a near-miss with an Exploding Kitten.
3) Identify the first dealer and rotate clockwise. In a ten-player game, the dealer rotation ensures everyone gets a fair taste of the chaos in a predictable rhythm.
4) Separate the Exploding Kitten cards, if needed, and place them in the draw pile as directed by the quick-start guide. The rest of the deck goes face down as the draw pile.
5) Clarify any house rules and remind players that the goal is to be the last one standing. And yes, water breaks are allowed—hydration is a safety win when you’re laughing with friends for two hours straight.

## How to play: a bat of the eye and you’re in

### Gameplay snapshot

Exploding Kittens works on a simple principle: draw cards until someone pulls an Exploding Kitten and is then out of the round unless they can Defuse it. The Party Pack, designed for up to ten players, preserves this core loop but broadens the social space. You’ll see more interplay, more misdirection, and more moments where a single card played at the right time completely flips the table’s mood.

Here’s a quick walkthrough of a typical turn:

- On your turn, you draw one card from the draw pile.
- Before drawing, you may play an action card that alters the draw, protects you, or harms another player. Action cards include things like peeking at cards, forcing someone to take an extra card, or swapping hands with another player. The exact actions vary by the Party Pack deck, but the intent remains the same: you’re looking for clever ways to dodge the big red button.
- If you draw an Exploding Kitten and you do not have a Defuse card, you’re out for that round. If you do have a Defuse, you get to defuse the kitten and place it somewhere in the draw pile—usually with a sly placement that makes your next opponents sweat it out.
- The round operates with elimination, until a single player remains. In a party setting, you’ll experience several rounds back-to-back, which makes the session feel like a wild carnival ride rather than a slow chess match.

### The 10-player factor

Ten players aren’t just a crowd; they’re a social machine. The more players, the higher the chance you’ll get a dramatic reversal, a misread bluff, or a heart-stopping near-miss where someone narrowly escapes a string of bad luck. The Party Pack shines in this space because the pacing of turns becomes more erratic and more dramatic as the group expands. You’ll get to see people you haven’t seen since last year’s family reunion, all leaning in with a new take on “I swear I didn’t peek at your hand.”

That said, there are caveats. With ten players, rounds can feel long if no one is willing to take the risk on a game-changing move. The best parties embrace that tension: players lean into safe plays early, and the table erupts when someone finally gambles on a chain of action cards that cascades into a table-wide reaction shot. If your group loves he-he-haha social dynamics, the Party Pack will become your go-to box for a long, loud evening. If you’re seeking ultra-precise strategy with peer-level competition, you might find the ten-player version more chaotic than you’d prefer.

## Strategy and tips: maximizing fun without losing friends

### General tips for large groups

- Read the room: In big groups, energy is contagious. If the table is riding a high tide of enthusiasm, lean into the chaos with bolder plays. If the mood is more reserved, consider slower plays and a few well-timed jokey moves to keep the vibe up.
- Bluffs aren’t just for poker; they’re for life: A well-timed misdirection card can set up multiple round wins, but don’t overcommit. The group loves big moments, but they don’t enjoy being dumped on with a string of bad luck.
- Juggle defuses: Defuse cards aren’t just insurance; they’re strategic power-ups. If someone is near elimination, a defuse that saves them can be worth more than a flashy play that eliminates another player. You’re buying time, not just avoiding a loss.
- Keep rules light in your head: If you’re playing with a lot of players, rely on the quick-start guide rather than flipping through the core rulebook every other turn. The quicker you can validate a rule, the faster you’ll keep the energy high.

### Tips tuned to ten players

- Early middle-ground plays work best: In a ten-player room, you will have a lot of late-stage drama. Early games often weed out weaker hands quickly if players push the envelope with the first few action cards.
- Use the table as a resource: With more players, there are more defusing opportunities. Use others as your shield or as a reason to take a calculated risk—someone might save you with a misplayed card or a misread intention.
- Be a good sport about near-eliminations: The social aspect matters more than any single win in a party setting. If someone comically wails after a near-elimination, acknowledge the moment and move on. The laughter will help bond the table more than any victory can.

## House rules and variants: making it yours

No two households play the same way, and that’s part of the charm here. If you want to tailor the experience for a ten-person setup, here are a few ideas that won’t derail the core rules but will keep things fresh:

- The Rule Card Toss: After a round ends, players who performed a bold, crowd-pleasing move get to toss a joke line into the discussion before the next round begins. This adds a social reward beyond winning that keeps energy high.
- Shot Clock Riffs: Give players 20 seconds to describe their intended move before they act. If they stall too long, the next player can nudge them with a playful prod. This keeps turns snappy and the room engaged.
- Team Play Variants: For very large groups, you can pair players into teams of two. Team members share a hand and coordinate their plays. It changes the dynamic from single-point elimination to cooperative chaos, which can be surprisingly satisfying in a family gathering.

Remember: the beauty of party games is the social contract. If you and your crew agree on a few light rules that speed things up and maximize laughter, you’ll probably have a better time than sticking with the baseline rules that occasionally invite argument instead of amusement.
