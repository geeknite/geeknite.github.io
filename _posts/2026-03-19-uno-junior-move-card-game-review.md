## How to play: rules, rhythm, and the Move mechanic
### Setup
- Shuffle the UNO Junior Move deck.
- Each player draws a starting hand of five cards (adjust as needed for younger players).
- Place the move board in the center and put the tokens on their starting space.
- Decide who goes first and announce the victor as soon as someone realizes they have the perfect card combo to call uno.

### Turn structure
On your turn, you typically do the following:
1) Play a card that matches color or number. If you can’t match, you draw a card from the deck.
2) If you played a Move card or a card that triggers a Move action, you move your token along the board accordingly. The movement is usually small — one or two spaces — but it’s enough to add a little drama and a reason to cheer when you squeak past a rival token.
3) If you have one card left, you must shout UNO to alert other players. Failure to do so can spark a playful penalty, which is often more fun than the penalty itself.

This is where UNO Junior Move differentiates itself from classic UNO: the Move action injects a physical element that’s manageable for kids. You’re not forced into a long multi-step turn; you’re nudged into a quick, kinetic moment that keeps kids engaged and moving. The result is a game that feels dynamic rather than static, even for players who are still learning their colors and numbers.

### Move cards and tokens
The Move cards are the star of the show. They tell you how many spaces to move, and sometimes there are twists like “move forward two if you have this color” or “move backward one if your last card is a number under three.” It sounds like chaos, but it’s the good kind of chaos: the kind that makes your kids giggle and want another round.

The tokens are adorable little creatures or cars that invite imaginative play. The physical act of moving a token adds a tactile layer to a game that’s often purely visual. Young players who are still mastering fine motor control benefit from the repetition of sliding, tapping, and counting as they advance their meeple around the tiny track. For parents, the movement mechanic is a blessing because it reduces the tension around “who has the best card” and replaces it with “who will make it around the track first.”

### Rule tweaks for toddlers
If your toddler wants in, you’re going to adapt the rules anyway—because that’s parenting in a nutshell. UNO Junior Move is flexible enough to accommodate those adjustments. Some families disable or simplify certain Move card effects, allow more lenient color matching, or reduce starting hand sizes to keep turns snappy. The general principle is to keep the pace high and the smiles high-er. It’s not about perfect rules; it’s about shared laughter, a little learning, and teaching kids how to take turns without turning the kitchen into a small lava field of chaos.
