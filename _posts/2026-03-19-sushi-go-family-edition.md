## How to play a typical round, with extra dad-jokes
The core round structure remains faithful to Sushi Go: each player is dealt a hand of cards, you pick one to play face down, you pass the rest around, and the round ends when all cards are played. Then you reveal and tally points for the hands you collected. But we’ll expand with some practical flavor for family nights:

- Step 1: Deal a hand to every player. The number of cards in the hand is chosen to ensure quick rounds. In this edition, the distribution is tuned so that even younger players are likely to be involved in at least two decisions per round. Think of it as two chances to pretend you know what you’re doing.
- Step 2: Everyone selects a card in secret and places it face down on the table. The secret-keeping aspect gets extra drama when you quietly declare, “I’m stealing your tuna… with a smile.”
- Step 3: When everyone is ready, all players reveal their chosen cards simultaneously. Then you pass the hand to the left (or right, depending on the round’s turn). The table erupts in friendly gasps as someone grabs the maki roll you secretly needed.
- Step 4: The process repeats until all cards in the deck are exhausted.

## Strategy and playstyle for different ages, expanded
Strategic planning is still part of the game, but the family edition nudges you toward accessible heuristics rather than heavy calculation. You will still want to track which sushi types have already appeared in the round and which ones would best complement the cards you are likely to keep. However, you won’t need to be a math PhD to have fun, because the scoring can be traced with simple tallies rather than complex multipliers.

- For younger players (ages 6-9): Keep things simple. Focus on collecting single types that are worth straightforward points, such as the oldest, the biggest handful of maki rounds, or the biggest nigiri combos. The art and the iconic shapes give cues about which cards pack a bigger punch, making it easier to decide without reading every sentence on the card. Turn decisions into quick, color-based cues.
- For parents and older siblings: Embrace the occasional bluff and the subtle tempo shifts. You can still achieve some strategic depth by sequencing your card plays in clever ways and watching your opponents’ patterns, but do not force yourself into a mental puzzle that slows the table down. The goal here is to have fun while still feeling a touch of cleverness.
- For multi-generational tables: The family edition scales quite well across ages. The simpler scoring means you can bring in a 9-year-old and a grandparent without one side getting bored or overwhelmed. The real joy is showing that strategy can be accessible to everyone, even if you just want to win by the sheer power of a well-timed sashimi dash.

## Practical tips for hosting a family game night, extended
- Start with a micro-tutorial: a 2-minute run-through of the rules plus a demonstration of the simplest scoring scenario. Let the kids see an example of scoring so they feel confident about the process.
- Use the large scoring reference: place it in the center so everyone can check the points as rounds progress.
- Keep snacks on the table: sushi-themed snacks help maintain the mood; it’s easier to remember the theme when you’re enjoying actual sushi-shaped crackers or rice crackers or even something silly like fruit slices arranged like nigiri.
- Rotate primary players: If you have a multi-generational table, consider rotating the organization of the rounds so that everyone has a turn to be the starting player. This ensures everyone remains invested in the flow.
- Abracadabra rule: if the table is running slower, pause and do a quick halt for a kid to reveal their chosen card aloud; this reinforces engagement and reduces confusion during the reveal.
- Build a memory-outcome: after a few games, revisit which cards you kept and why. It’s a light memory exercise that doesn’t feel like a test and often yields a few laughs about “remember that tuna from last round?”
- Have a dedicated “parents’ corner”: one or two adults can casually keep score and help translate any confusing iconography, allowing everyone to stay in the moment and keep the pace brisk.
