## How to play a typical round, with extra dad-jokes
If you’re already familiar with Sushi Go, you’ll slide into this edition with the ease of a sushi chef sliding a knife across a perfectly chilled countertop. If you’re new, fear not—the game is designed to teach itself through play. Here’s a practical breakdown with some family-night flavor:

- Step 1: Deal a hand to every player. The number of cards is tuned to keep rounds brisk while still giving everyone something to weigh. You’ll often have two meaningful decisions per round, which is perfect for kids learning to value patience without feeling overwhelmed.
- Step 2: Everyone secretly selects a card and places it face down. The moment of reveal becomes a little dramatic moment when someone declares, “I’m stealing your tuna… with a smile.” The giggles that follow are half the fun and a reminder that mischief and strategy can share the same plate.
- Step 3: Reveal simultaneously, then pass the cards around. Depending on the round order, pass left or right. Gasps ripple through the table as someone snatches the maki you secretly wanted, followed by a chorus of “aw” or “aha!” depending on how many shuffles have happened.
- Step 4: Repeat until the deck is exhausted and every table has declared its love for an eel or two. Tally scores for the hands collected and prepare for the next mini-sushi adventure.

## Practical tips for hosting a family game night, extended
A handful of practical tips will help you keep the energy high and the table friendly:
- Start with a micro-tutorial: a two-minute walkthrough of the rules plus a demonstration of the simplest scoring scenario. Let the kids see scoring in action; it builds confidence quickly.
- Use the large scoring reference in the center: a constant reminder that scoring is approachable and not a mystery to be solved with a scientist’s toolkit.
- Keep snacks on the table: sushi-themed snacks reinforce the vibe and reduce cognitive load by delivering immediate thematic cues—think sushi-shaped crackers, rice crackers, or fruit slices arranged like nigiri.
- Rotate primary players: If you have a multi-generational table, rotate who starts so everyone has a chance to influence the early flow.
- Use the “abracadabra” rule to speed things up: when the pace slows, pause for a kid to reveal their card aloud. It keeps engagement high and reduces “dead time.”
- Build memory through rounds: revisit why you kept certain cards after a few games. It’s a gentle memory exercise that still feels like play.
- Create a parents’ corner: one or two adults can help translate iconography or handle scoring, letting everyone stay present in the moment without stalling the night.
