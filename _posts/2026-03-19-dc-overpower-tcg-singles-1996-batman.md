## Introduction
The year is 1996, and the air tastes faintly of ozone and bubblegum cards. ## What is Overpower?
Overpower is a collectible card game that tried to translate the feel of a comic book fight into mechanical decisions on a table. Each card had a hero or villain, a suite of combat values, and sometimes special abilities that triggered when certain conditions were met. The DC edition, released in the mid 1990s, brought heroes like Batman, Superman, Wonder Woman, and the rogues gallery into a card format that encouraged speed, cunning, and a pinch of silliness. The "singles" aspect means you could chase down individual cards, not just entire sets; you could buy a single Batman card, or a foil rare, or a misprint that makes your inner grammar police cry with joy.

### The dream of modular combat
The thrill of Overpower lies in how simple parts can create surprisingly deep sequences. You draw a card, you compare your hero's stats to your opponent's, you consider terrain or equipment, and you resolve the numbers on the card. The game invites you to build a little engine around a hero: the faster your hero hits, the more you can control the flow of the battle. The Batman entry is especially delightful because DC’s caped crusader is not just a fist with a cape; he is a toolbox.

## The Look and Feel: Cards, Foil, and the 1990s Aesthetic
Let’s talk about the physical experience. The 1996 DC Overpower cards were on stiff cardstock with glossy finishes that sometimes wore down your fingertips from repeated shuffles. The Batman card would feature a dramatic pose, a splash of blue and gray, and a caption that might read something like a one-liner from a late-night animated series.

A lot of the charm comes from the production quirks of the era. Card stock was thick enough to survive a kid’s backpack, but not so thick that you’d mistake it for a trading card confessional. Foil variants exist, and misprints can become a collector’s holy grail. If you ever find a Batman card that looks slightly off-center or with a glint that doesn’t align with the card art, congratulations: you might have a rare gem that makes your 12-year-old self salute you from the future.

- Gadget and prep: Focus on equipment and field effects that boost his stats or grant him extra actions. Your deck includes cards that simulate the utility belt’s offerings: grappling lines, batarangs, and mini gadgets that can turn a bad turn into a clever pivot.
- Detective mode: If the game has card interactions around revealing or countering information, Batman can use those to outplay the opponent’s plan by anticipating what they’ll do next.
- Stealth and counterplay: Batman can threaten the enemy’s plan by punishing reveals or setups that rely on brash direct attacks. The trick is to keep your deck lean but full of options that can respond to multiple threats.

In practice, a Batman hero can carry a core set of supporting cards that improve his survivability and out-of-phase actions. You would look for allies who sync well with his abilities, perhaps a Field Commander or a tech-savvy partner who can fill in the gaps when Batman’s toolkit isn’t enough.

## The Market Then and Now
Back in the day, DC Overpower singles were sold in hobby shops, newsstands, and mail-order catalogs. The price was reasonable for a child with a stack of lunch money and a dream. Today, the market has shifted toward nostalgia. There’s value in the experience of collecting the cards as a physical artifact from a different era. Singles let you pick the exact pieces you want without the risk of opening packs that may or may not contain the treasure you seek.

For new collectors, it’s a discipline. The world of 1990s card games is a mosaic of different print runs, packaging quirks, and distribution anomalies. The single-card approach makes it possible to chase Batman without needing a mortgage for a booster box. It also makes the hobby more accessible to casual players who want to enjoy the story more than the math.

## How to Find Your Batman Card: Tips for Hunters
- Check the card stock quality: Do the colors pop or do they look a little worn? A well-preserved Batman card stands up to shelf life and sunlight.
- Look for border consistency: Centering is a minor detail, but it matters when the card’s aesthetic is part of the charm.
- Pay attention to the artwork: Look for the classic silhouette and the cape’s dynamic linework.
- Condition matters: A near-mint Batman can be a centerpiece of a collection.

If you plan to display your Batman card, consider framing it behind UV-protective glass. The art deserves an audience and a break from the glare of fluorescent lights.

## Variants and Misprints: The Fun Layer of Collecting
