## Introduction
Saboteur is a social deduction card game about dwarves digging for gold. The twist is that some players are saboteurs who quietly sabotage the tunnel while others are loyal miners trying to reach the gold through a series of path cards. It’s a high-chaos, high-glory experience where deception is encouraged, storytelling is mandatory, and everyone gets to pretend they’re a heroic pickaxe-wielding engineer even when their true goal is to derail the entire operation.

The 20th Anniversary Edition marks a milestone for a game that has traveled through many a gaming group’s Christmas party and edited their group chat for the next-day memes. Amigo has rebalanced components, refreshed artwork, and offered a packaging glow-up that makes the base Saboteur look like it parked in a showroom to pick up a trophy. The result? A game that feels familiar enough to scratch that nostalgic itch while offering enough polish to impress even the most jaded tabletop snob.

## What’s in the Box (Component Quality and Setup)
When you crack open Saboteur 20th Anniversary, the first impression is the tactile one: the cards feel sturdy, the art has been reimagined, and the box design exudes a glossy confidence. The box art leans more into the dungeon-crawler vibe—think dwarves with jaunty helmets, gem shimmer, and a mood board that says, Let’s mine… responsibly? Or not.

- Path cards: These are the core scaffolding of the game. Each card shows a segment of tunnel that must be laid out to create a route from the starting point to a gold nugget. The 20th Anniversary edition uses slightly thicker stock, which helps the tunnels hold their shape during heated negotiations and accidental shuffles.
- Action cards: Saboteur thrives on chaos. Action cards give players the power to break a card, peek at someone else’s role, or throw a banana peel into a potential path—metaphorically speaking, since there are no actual banana peels, but the effect is the same: surprise and delight.
- Role cards: A reminder that not everyone is who they pretend to be. In a 2-12 player game, you’ll have miners and saboteurs entering the dance of deception. The balance between roles tends to scale nicely with player count, which is a strength of Saboteur.
- Gold nugget cards: These are your victory bells; collecting enough will decide who gets the gold. In the 20th Anniversary Edition, the gold pile has a satisfying heft to it—literally and figuratively.
- Tokens and map cards: Map cards add a twist, offering glimpses of potential treasure locations and the possibility to alter your route strategy mid-game. The Anniversary edition tweaks some of these for readability and balance.
- Player aids: Quick-reference sheets help new players get up to speed without consulting the rulebook every two minutes, which is crucial for keeping the game’s pacing brisk.

Setup is intentionally fast. Shuffle the role cards and deal one to each player, then lay out the path cards to form a gallery of potential tunnels. The map cards are shuffled into their own stack, the gold piles are prepared, and suddenly you’re staring at a table that looks like a heist planning board from a low-budget sci-fi flick.

The delight here is how a simple box of cards becomes a stage for social dynamics. You don’t need a sprawling board or a thousand components to create tension; you need just enough pieces to remind people that every choice might be a lie, and every lie could be a path to glory—or a dead-end cave-in caused by your own miscalculation.

## How to Play: A Quick Recap (In Plain-ish English)
Saboteur operates on a simple premise with wildly entertaining consequences: your goal is to get a tunnel from the start to a gold nugget, but not all players share that goal. The miner crew cooperates to lay a tunnel that reaches the gold; saboteurs secretly sabotage this plan. The catch is that you don’t know who’s who, and you can’t rely on anyone but your own wily instincts and the occasional bluff.

- Start: Each player receives a role card face-down. The miners know the general objective, but saboteurs know the plan and will attempt to derail it without revealing themselves.
- Turn order: Players play path cards to connect the tunnel from the start to gold. They can talk, negotiate, bluff, and use action cards to influence the tunnel’s direction or to foil opponents.
- Sabotage: Saboteurs can break connections, force errors, or ruin someone else’s tunnel by playing the wrong cards at the wrong times. The strategy is to create plausible misdirection while quietly pushing for a route that leads to your own favored outcome.
- Endgame: When a path is completed to a gold nugget, the round ends. All players reveal their roles; miners count gold collected by successful routes; saboteurs win if the miners fail to reach the gold or sabotage the route effectively.
- Scoring: The precise scoring can depend on the variant you play (the Anniversary edition includes a couple of optional rules), but the baseline idea remains: miners win when the route reaches treasure; saboteurs win when they thwart the miners’ attempts or extract a cheeky victory through misdirection.

The flow is fast, the back-and-forth is sharp, and the social bluffing is where the real joy lives. It’s a game about convincing others that your mine is the true path while masking your true intentions, which, in a group setting, means you’ll often hear more white lies than a family reunion after a particularly spicy casserole recipe.

## Strategy and Role Analysis: Tips for Surviving (and Thriving) in the Mine
- For miners: Build a plausible route that looks cooperative to outside observers but doesn’t reveal your own critical chokepoints. Don’t overcommit to a single path too early; keep a few candidate routes alive so you can adapt if a saboteur starts breaking connections. Coordinate with trust; the trick is to create a shared illusion of teamwork while keeping a few back channels for last-minute pivots.
- For saboteurs: Bluff with confidence. A well-timed misdirection can derail a route long enough for you to slip in a sabotage card later on. Use map cards to misreport the location of the gold or to force others into suboptimal paths. Your best tool is inconsistency: look uncertain, talk decisively, and always have a fallback plan in case your primary lie is torn apart.
- For mixed groups: The beauty of Saboteur is that you can lean into roles as much as you want. Some groups enjoy heavier deception; others prefer light, almost playful misdirection. The Anniversary edition accommodates both by keeping mechanical clarity while letting storytelling fill the room.

A practical approach is to run a few practice rounds where players rotate roles between rounds. This gives everyone a taste of both sides of the table and makes for better post-game debriefs as you unpack who lied about what and why the “path” you believed in was actually a path to the expired snack drawer.

## Variants, House Rules, and Longevity
One of Saboteur’s strengths is its adaptability. The Anniversary edition supports optional house rules that let you adjust the balance, length, or tension. Some common variants include:
- Taller tunnels: requiring more path cards to reach the treasure, increasing the number of opportunities for sabotage and negotiation.
- Hidden treasure: using map cards to conceal the gold location more aggressively, which ramps up the pressure on miners to deduce the correct route.
- Co-op ending: in some groups, you can introduce a cooperative ending where all players help ensure a specific path reaches the treasure, but only at the cost of losing some of the deception-based fun.

Longevity comes from the simple truth that Saboteur scales with table size. The 2-player variant is a different beast from a 12-player chaos ball, but the core tension remains—trust is a mutable resource, and misdirection can be both cunning and hilarious. The Anniversary edition helps by presenting a cleaner baseline that makes it easier to adopt one or a dozen variants without a rules headache.

Compared to a vintage Saboteur box that survived a move and a few roommates’ snack experiments, the Anniversary edition feels like a curated collectible gift with practical improvements.
