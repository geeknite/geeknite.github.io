## Great Condition: What It Means for This Card
“Great condition” in MTG terms means Mint/Near-Mint for a non-foil print or Near-Mint/Mint for a foil. The differences matter because the card’s value is driven by both its utility and its physical state. Some critical indicators of great condition you’ll want to verify include:

- Centering and edge wear: A perfectly centered card with sharp edges and minimal whitening around the borders is the gold standard. Iconic Masters cards sometimes show slight edge wear due to the paper stock, but a great-condition Mana Drain should have clean edges and no major scratches at the holofoil surface if foil.
- Surface gloss: For non-foil copies, you want a smooth surface with no scratches that catch the light. Foil versions should have a pristine reflective surface without water spots or tarnish on the foil pattern.
- Print alignment: A legendary reprint like Mana Drain in Iconic Masters benefits from cleanly cut corners and accurate printing alignment. Warped or miscut copies are rare but can crop up; these notes matter when you’re assessing the true condition.
- Scuff-free card back and front: There should be no deep scrapes on the back or front, and the card should be free of signs of re-housing in a binder with moisture damage or heavy handling.
- Staining and whitening: Some older reprints carry a light aging edge. A great-condition Mana Drain will have minimal whitening and no obvious staining that affects readability or the card’s aesthetic.

If you’re buying sight unseen, consider a reputable grading service or return policy. For a two-decade-old card with iconic status, investors often accept minor imperfections in exchange for the card’s power and nostalgia—but great condition is where the premium lives.

We’ll also touch on how condition influences price in a moment, but the short version is: great condition adds significant value, particularly for foil or near-mint copies in Iconic Masters. It’s the difference between “great for gaming” and “great for display.”
