## Installation

### Step 1: Secure Your Bike

First, make sure your bike is stable. I recommend a bike stand, but if you don't have one, a willing friend or a stack of old textbooks will do the trick.

### Step 2: Remove the Old Pads

Remove the wheel and then the old brake pads. Be sure to keep track of any screws or clips—you don't want to end up with a handful of parts and zero memory of where they go.

### Step 3: Install the New Pads

Insert the new Shimano 4-piston organic brake pads. Make sure they’re properly aligned and secure. Think of this step like putting on a new pair of shoes—if they're misaligned, you’re in for an awkward ride.

### Step 4: Test Them Out

Put the wheel back on and take your bike for a test ride to ensure the pads are working correctly. If they aren’t, repeat the previous steps while muttering under your breath about “modern technology.”

## Pros and Cons

### Pros

- **Quiet Operation**: These pads are as silent as a mime convention.
- **Smooth Braking**: Your stops will feel like butter—assuming butter could stop a speeding bike.

### Cons

- **Faster Wear**: Organic pads tend to wear out quicker than metallic pads. Think of them as the single-use plastic of brake pads.
- **Less Effective in Wet Conditions**: They’re not huge fans of the rain—like cats, but less dramatic.
