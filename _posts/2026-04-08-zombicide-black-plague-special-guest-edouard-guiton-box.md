In the sprawling labyrinth of tiny plastic apocalypse, a package arrived on my doorstep that felt less like a game accessory and more like a backstage pass to a celebrity artist's studio. The box announced a Special Guest Edouard Guiton, and suddenly my coffee table was elevated to a prime-time stage where sculpted heroes, gothic zombies, and glossy art prints mingle like undercooked marshmallows at a campfire of doom. Yes, Zombicide Black Plague already has a reputation for hordes of multi-coloured undead and enough plastic to fill a small museum, but this particular edition promised something a bit more... curated. A designer's slice of the apocalypse, if you will. And, for better or worse, I was ready to judge.

What’s in the Box
- Two deluxe survivor miniatures with heroic swords and capes that would make a peacock jealous.
- A set of Edouard Guiton designed hero cards bearing a signature style that looks somewhere between a fantasy tattoo parlor and a stained glass window of the undead.
- A new zombie type that sprints like a caffeinated librarian and moans in a voice that could only come from a dedicated typographer with a dramatic sigh.
- A glossy art book of concept sketches and color studies, because sometimes we all want to know what the undead look like when they are not undead at all.
- A handful of tokens and a new double-sided board tile that changes up the baseline map, adding fresh angles for ambush and escape.
- A small rules insert clarifying how Edouard’s art influences certain encounter cards; think of it as flavor with a tiny weighty footnote.

Unboxing Experience
The packaging feels premium without going full spaceship. The inner cardboard is sturdy enough to survive a shelf life longer than my gaming group’s attention span, and the pastel ink on the cover hints at a designer who understands that horror and beauty can co-exist without winking at each other. The miniatures come in plastic clutches that don’t require a degree in engineering to pop into their bases. The paint job? Clean, precise, and not afraid to use a little shading to give depth to a wooden club or a blood-slicked machete. Some reveals require a steady hand, others benefit from a deft thumb; either way, the build quality is a step above your average boxed expansion.

Artwork and Theme
Edouard Guiton’s fingerprints are all over this box in a way that feels respectful rather than pushy. His style tends toward dramatic lighting, high-contrast silhouettes, and a sense of movement frozen in a moment of danger. The survivors look weathered in a way that implies they have seen a few too many kitchens while searching for a snack, but have not yet surrendered their sense of humor. The zombies get a makeover that makes them feel both terrifying and oddly fashionable—like undead gentlemen tiptoeing through a catwalk of ruined cathedrals. It is not just pretty pictures; it is a vibe, a mood, a story told in gradients of gray and blood red.

Miniatures and Components
The two special edition survivors stand out on the board, not only because their sculpts are more ornate than your average citizen of the apocalypse but also because they seem to come with a personality. One could be the quiet scholar who mutters about arcane sigils while swinging a staff, while the other is the brash hunter who literally yells “Let’s go!” mid-ambush. The zombie sculpts have more personality than your typical wave of undead pushers; you can practically assign them names and grievances. The extra tile creates new chokepoints and line-of-sight drama, which is always a gift for anyone who loves a good tactical puzzle.

Rules and Card Design
Edouard’s involvement isn’t just cosmetic. The card work introduces a handful of new mechanics that fit within the Black Plague system and are clean enough to pick up quickly but deep enough to reward careful play. There are new encounter cards that twist the board into architectural mazes and new items that feel thematically appropriate—like relics from a cathedral of tattered banners or a lantern that flickers with a demon’s breath. If you are a stickler for rules, you’ll appreciate the explicit clarifications in the insert, which prevent minor disputes from spiraling into a full-blown zombie parliament.

Gameplay Experience
If you’ve played Black Plague before, you know the rhythm: glorious dice, heroic saves, and a chorus of groans when you roll a critical fail. The Special Guest Edouard Guiton Box doesn’t reinvent the wheel, but it tunes the wheel to a more theatrical tempo. The new survivor cards introduce small but meaningful choices. Do you deploy the quiet scholar to map out a safe line, or risk the brash hunter to sprint through a corridor and trigger a generator of chaos? The new tile layout tends to push players toward riskier but more satisfying enclosures, where one perfect reveal can swing a round from “we’re doomed” to “we might actually pull this off.”

Table Talk and Social Fun
There’s a social element to this edition that you don’t notice until you are mid-game, elbow-deep in a conversation about what Edouard would draw in his own dream zombie apocalypse. The art adds to the storytelling; the survivors are friendlier to players who like to narrate their cool escapes as if they are the knight-chasing bard of a battle-ruin anthology. It’s not just about hitting the right die; it’s about delivering a quip at the right moment, the same way you would behind a mic during a late-night improv set about a bishop and a zombie in a cathedral.

Behind the Scenes: Concept to Card
The design arc behind the special guest box is a neat little case study in cross-medium collaboration. You can feel the concept art translate into tabletop assets, and you can read between the lines of the encounter cards to sense how Edouard’s aesthetic informs the pacing and zone control. It’s not a full artistic overhaul, but the process is evident: mood boards, color studies, critique sessions, and a handful of prototypes that gradually align with the core game’s rhythm. If you are into game production or design appreciation, you will enjoy tracing this path from the first pencil sketch to the final printed card. It’s like watching a good movie trailer: you know you’re in for a bigger experience when the art starts to tell you the story before you even roll a dice.

Gameplay Nuance: Tactical Tips
- Use the new tile to funnel zombies into a choke point that your survivors can hold with a disciplined volley. It rewards timing more than brute force.
- Pair the new survivor cards with environment cards to create a corner where one survivor acts as bait while the other delivers the finishing blow.
- Don’t be afraid to spend a few extra actions to reposition for a better line of sight on the next wave; the new cards reward careful planning.
- If your table has a painterly temperament, let the art inspire your positioning; the visuals are not just pretty, they are practical.

Thematic and Practical Value
The combination of Edouard’s art with Zombicide’s core design creates a product that is enjoyable on both the table and the display shelf. The minis are robust and not fragile; they can stand up to a fairly enthusiastic playing style, including the occasional accidental nudge of the board by someone who forgot the water bottle was on the corner. The art prints and card design elevate the product to a collector’s item without sacrificing playability. You can show off the miniatures after a session and still be confident they’ll survive the next game night.

Value and Longevity
For players who love a well-curated box and want something that stands out on the shelf, this is a solid addition that remains relevant after multiple sessions. For those who prioritize raw number-crunching and huge expansions, it may feel a little light, but that is not the target here. The extra gameplay hooks and narrative pieces provide enduring value because they alter how you think about the board, not just how you roll dice.

Comparisons with Other Expansions
In the pantheon of Zombicide expansions, this Special Guest box sits somewhere between a cosmetic upgrade and a substantive add-on. It doesn’t overhaul the core mechanics in the way that a full-blown new edition would, but it does inject a layer of atmosphere that is often missing from standard releases. If you’re familiar with other major expansions, you’ll recognize the balancing philosophy: more choices, more tension, more replayability, but still anchored to the same core loop of turn-based zombie management.
