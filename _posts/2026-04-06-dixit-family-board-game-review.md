## What Dixit Is, Exactly, and How It Plays
### The Core Mechanic: Storytelling as a Card Game
Dixit is a storytelling game that uses image cards instead of words. One player becomes the storyteller for the round. They select a card from their hand and propose a clue—a phrase, a sentence, a memory, or a small poem—that relates to the card without giving it away. The other players choose a card from their own hands that best matches the storyteller’s clue. All chosen cards are shuffled and revealed, and players secretly vote on which card they think belongs to the storyteller. If no one guesses correctly, the storyteller is in trouble; if everyone guesses correctly, the storyteller also loses points. The sweet spot? You want just enough ambiguity that some players get it right and others don’t. The chaos of interpretation is where the magic lives.

This base mechanic is the reason Dixit remains a favorite at family gatherings and friendly game nights. It encourages creativity, observation, and playful misdirection, while staying light on the strategy side so your non-gamer uncle won’t feel out of his depth. The family version of the box usually scales well to kids around 8 years old and up, with the right amount of whimsy to keep older players amused as well.

## Playing the Game: A Step-by-Step Walkthrough
### Setup and Turn Order
Setup is quick. Shuffle the 84-card deck, deal a hand to each player (usually six cards to begin, though variations exist), place the voting tokens in reach, and give the storyteller marker to someone. The turn order rotates as the storyteller role passes around the table, so everyone gets a chance to exercise their inner poet and inner mischievous art director.

### Rounds: Storytelling, Choosing, and Voting
- The current storyteller selects one card from their hand and gives a clue that relates to it.
- Each non-storyteller selects a card from their own hand that they think best matches the clue, and hands it face-down to the table.
- The cards are shuffled and revealed. Players vote on which card they think is the storyteller’s, using their voting tokens.
- Points are awarded: the storyteller wants some but not all to guess their card; other players gain points if they guessed the storyteller’s card, or for the storyteller’s card’s popularity.
- The round ends; players draw back up to the hand size, and the next storyteller takes the turn.

This cycle continues until someone reaches the target score or the deck runs out. In most 가족( family ) games nights, you’ll hit a natural rhythm after a few rounds, and the table will hum with a delightful mix of giggles and groans when a particularly odd card surfaces.

### Why It Works for Mixed Groups
Dixit thrives on mixed groups because interpretation is inherently subjective. The joke that lands with the kids might leave the adults in stitches in a completely different way. The game's open-ended nature means your table isn’t chasing a single solution; instead, you’re chasing shared moments created by image prompts. It’s, paradoxically, both cooperative and competitive in the same breath. That duality is why people come back to it: even when you’re losing points, you’re still winning at the art of storytelling.
