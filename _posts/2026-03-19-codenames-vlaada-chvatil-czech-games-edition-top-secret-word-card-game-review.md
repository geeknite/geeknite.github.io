## Overview

Codenames is a word association game for two teams. Each team has a spymaster who gives a one word clue accompanied by a number. That clue is meant to guide team members toward several words displayed on a 5x5 grid. The catch is that you want to avoid choosing words that belong to the other team, neutral words, and especially the assassin word. The team that reveals all of its agents first wins, but the assassin word can snipe a round instantly and force a dramatic change in momentum, which is exactly where the drama lies.

## The core mechanics in practice

Codenames is about pairing language with group context. The spymaster must distill a tricky set of target words into single word clues that reach multiple target words but avoid leading the other team toward their own targets or the assassin. The number paired with the clue lets you gauge the risk versus reward: high numbers offer a chance to cover more words, but also increase the chance of hitting an enemy word or the assassin if your clue is too ambitious.

That tension is the heartbeat of the game. A well chosen clue can turn a near miss into a five or six word cascade that reveals a cluster of your team words in a single decisive moment. A misread clue, however, can create a cascade of near misses and a table full of discussion about what each word actually means. And that is where the social glue happens: players negotiate, justify, and laugh over the connections that feel obvious to one side and opaque to the other.

A good round is a blend of strategy and luck. Strategy comes from choosing a clue that can link multiple target words in a meaningful way without crossing into your opponents territory. Luck enters when your team lands on a couple of words that fit your theme, but also when hints align with the actual words on the board. The balance between calculated moves and the unpredictable flavor of human interpretation is what makes Codenames so replayable.

## Setup and turn structure in detail

### Setup

- Arrange 25 word cards in a neat 5x5 grid on the center of the table.
- The team sides are assigned, and the starting team is determined by the key card or by a quick toss up.
- The spymasters take the key card that reveals which words belong to which team, which words are neutral, and where the assassin sits in the grid. The other players do not see this key card.
- The remaining players gather around the table, ready to discuss, guess, and vote on the clues that will define the night.

### The turn

- The spymaster issues a clue word and a number. The clue should be a real word or a proper noun that is accessible to the group, and the number signals how many grid words the clue could potentially relate to.
- The field operatives discuss among themselves and begin guessing words one by one. Each guess is announced aloud. If the guess is one of their own words, that word is marked with their color and the team continues guessing up to the number indicated by the clue.
- If a guess hits an opponent word, the turn ends and control passes to the other team, who now has a chance to push their own agenda on the board. If the guess hits a neutral word, the turn also ends but with a quieter swing in board control. If the assassin word is guessed, the game ends immediately with a loss for the guessing team.

The flow is snappy, and a single spymaster can see the entire table as a chessboard of potential moves. The tempo is what makes or breaks the session. A calm, confident spymaster who can ride the wave of misdirections will often win more rounds than a player who clings to a single narrow interpretation of the board.

## Strategies for different players

### For new spymasters

- Start with clues that tie to multiple straightforward target words rather than risking a long string of tricky ones. You want to build confidence and momentum to keep the team engaged.
- Use familiar categories to guide your group. If most players are into a particular genre, use clues that subtly connect familiar vocabulary to those grid words.
- Leave at least one safe neutral word before you close out a round to avoid a harsh misstep that ends with an assassin pulling the rug out from under you.

### For field operatives

- Create a group memory of commonly linked words. It helps to discuss potential word relationships before the spymaster drops the clue so you pick up on the context quickly.
- Avoid second guessing every clue. If a clue seems plausible and you can connect the hidden logic with several of your words, go for it. The natural chaos of the table often yields the best moments.
- Be mindful of your teammates and the possibility that strong personalities can derail the flow. Help by clarifying what is a safe guess and what is a cautionary guess.
