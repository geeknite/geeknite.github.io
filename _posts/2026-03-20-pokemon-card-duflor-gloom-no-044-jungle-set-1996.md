## What is Duflor/Gloom, and why does the language matter?

Duflor is the French name for Gloom, a Pokémon that has haunted late-90s card fans with its ambiguous aroma and peculiar flavor text. The bilingual nature of the Pokémon TCG means many cards appeared in multiple languages during the height of international releases. For some fans, this linguistic twist is a feature; for others, it’s catalog-worthy trivia that adds to the card’s mystique. Gloom’s presence in the Jungle Set—No. 044 in the numerical order—offers a snapshot of that era when card design, artwork, and flavor text were all tugging in slightly different directions while still coalescing into a single, collectible package.

From a storytelling standpoint, Duflor/Gloom embodies a quirky balance: a dark, drooping plant perched on a single pedestal of hope (its move names, like Absorb or Mega Drain, often sounded like battlefield puns rather than lyrical poetry). The French name adds a playful layer for collectors who chase cross-language variants or who simply enjoy the cultural mosaic of the TCG’s early days. In other words: the card isn’t just a wooden block with stats; it’s a passport stamp from a time when the hobby was expanding faster than a Charmander on a hot day.

## Jungle Set context: where this card fits in the wild canopy

The Jungle Set is the rocky, leafy cousin of Base Set. It introduced a handful of new Pokémon to early collectors and reimagined some familiar faces with new art and slightly different card text across languages. Jungle carried the vibe of a rainforest trading floor—hazy with optimism, sweat, and the occasional accidental misprint that becomes a collector’s dream. If you’ve spent any time leafing through a binder full of jungle-green corners and red-cheeked holographics, you know that the Jungle era is where the hobby’s sense of discovery really started to hum.

No. 044 sits comfortably in this ecosystem as a common, non-holo staple for many early binders. From a collecting standpoint, Jungle Set Gloom is a great example of how set composition influences long-term value. No. 044 falls into the latter camp for many casual collectors: it’s a recognizable piece, easy to complete a Jungle Set run, and it comes with the added flavor of Duflor’s bilingual lineage. If you’re chasing a “complete Jungle” binder, this card is a dependable, approachable staple that doesn’t require you to mortgage your left sock drawer.

The card text itself is where the practicality of a 1990s TCG card remains accessible. Gloom’s moves were designed to encourage energy management and situational play rather than the high-velocity combos modern sets push. In the Jungle era, you’d often build for a steady tempo: poke with small numbers, stack a few Energy cards, and hope the opponent misjudges the timing of a Mega Drain or Absorb. This pacing is a different rhythm from today’s powerhouse archetypes, but there’s something refreshing about it. It invites a slower, more thoughtful game—one that rewards patience, planning, and a little luck when the coin flip finally lands in your favor.

Flavor text (when present) and in-set flavor cues in Jungle sets served as narrative seasoning rather than a strict gameplay instruction. ## Rarity, value, and the practical economics of a Jungle staple

Let’s talk the market, a topic that makes even the most stoic Collector’s Corner regulars perk up with a shuffle and a sigh. For No. 044 Gloom in Jungle, rarity is somewhere in the middle of the pack—a card you can reasonably find in a decent-condition binder without needing to sell a kidney, especially if you’re chasing non-first-edition copies. However, there are a few levers that can shift price and desirability:

- Edition and stamp: First Edition Jungle cards exist, and they carry a premium due to limited print runs. If your copy bears the “First Edition” stamp, you’ve got a potential upgrade path that can thrill any hunt. If not, you’re still riding a strong nostalgia wave.
- Condition: As with most vintage cards, condition matters a lot. A well-preserved No. 044 with sharp edges, clean surface, and centered artwork will outperform a creased, scratched version in most buyers’ eyes. Grading can add value, especially if you have a near-mint example with minimal whitening and crisp corners.
- Language variant: Some collectors chase language variants as a category unto themselves. A French “Duflor” variant can be both a conversation piece and a marketable item for folks building a multilingual collection.
- Market cycles: Vintage Pokemon cards, particularly sets from the late 1990s, ride waves of interest driven by nostalgia and pop culture moments. If a new collection box or a reprint set funnels attention toward Jungle-era cards, prices can shift a bit in favorable ways for buyers and sellers alike.

If you’re assembling a Jungle Set collection, No. 044 is the kind of card that helps you tell a complete story without overpaying. ## Practical tips for collectors: caring for Duflor in the digital-analog age

- Sleeves and top loaders: Use semi-rigid sleeves and acid-free top loaders when shipping or storing. Jungle-era cards sometimes show wear along the edges; a proper sleeve reduces friction and keeps the colors from fading under fluorescent room lights.
- Storage: Keep your cards away from direct sunlight, heat sources, and humidity. A cool, dry closet with a handful of silica gel packs does wonders for longevity.
- Handling: Minimize direct handling with clean hands. Oils and residues can micro-mark the surface, which becomes visible under bright light when you’re curating a display.
- Grading: If you’re curious about resale or display value, consider professional grading. A well-graded Jungle-era card, especially in a First Edition batch, tends to attract attention from both collectors and boutiques that specialize in vintage sets.
- Display and display cases: If you have a No. 044 in pristine condition, consider a display case that highlights the card’s artwork without clutter. The Jungle set’s artwork is part of the experience; give it a stage.
