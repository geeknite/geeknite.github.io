## What is Sushi Go?
Sushi Go is a card-drafting game designed for 2–5 players (some editions accommodate more with the party version, but the core game shines with four players at a standard table). Each turn you receive a hand of 7 cards (in a 2–3 player game the number can adjust slightly), pick one, and pass the rest to your neighbor. The chosen card goes into your personal score sheet stack, and the rest continue around the table until all cards are claimed. Then you tally points for different sushi sets, rare items, and the occasional x-factor card that gives you a little extra boost.

The objective is to accumulate the most points through careful card selection. The catch is that you don’t just choose for yourself—you must anticipate what the table might do on the next pass. You’ll often be torn between grabbing a sweet tuna nigiri that doubles your points for a single card or snagging a sashimi set that rewards you for collecting three cards of the same type. You’ll have to balance greed with patience, and sometimes you’ll learn the hard way that getting greedy can cause you to miss a perfectly timed scoring window.

Thematically, the game leans into a traditional Japanese sushi dining experience, stylized in bold colors and crisp icons. The scoring cards are grouped by type with clear icons: tuna nigiri, salmon nigiri, egg nigiri, maki rolls, tamago, dumplings, and a few quirky “special” cards like wasabi or pudding in some editions. The special cards provide small synergies that can surprise you at the end of the round, keeping even the most methodical players on their toes.

For many players, Sushi Go becomes a comforting ritual rather than a grind. It’s a game you can pick up in under a minute, teach in under five, and enjoy with a glass of something bubbly or a bowl of ramen if you’re feeling thematic. It’s not trying to be a grand epic; it’s trying to be a friendly, flavorful distraction that scratches the strategic itch without turning your gaming night into a census-taking exercise.

The genius of Sushi Go lies in its elegant constraints. You don’t have to master a hundred card interactions or memorize a ten-page rulebook. You’ll experience ephemeral table dynamics: the timing of passes, the pressure of the person who is always one card ahead of you, and the occasional “oops, I misread that a nine-POINT tuna” moment that makes the table howl.

The game rewards players who think in sets and curves: you want to be collecting three different items (like a mini-portfolio of sushi) while also planning for a symmetrical score from nigiri. The more you play, the more you discover how a small blunder—like ignoring the dumplings when you’re ahead—can flip the table’s mood and swing the prize. It’s not about brute force; it’s about managing risk and reading the table with a playful eye. That balance is what makes Sushi Go not just a filler title but a recurring favorite for many game nights.

## How to Play: The Core Loop
### The Draft and Pass Cycle
The game unfolds in a series of hands. Each hand begins with a new set of 7 cards dealt to every player. On your turn, you pick one card from your hand and place it face-down in your personal eating area (the score track). Then you pass the remaining cards to the left. The cycle continues until you’ve exhausted all the cards in your hand. The tempo is brisk; the rounds rarely drag, which keeps players engaged and prevents stall-induced yawns.

### Scoring Basics
At the end of each round, you tally points from your collected cards. There are several scoring themes, including:
- Nigiri cards: basic, but with higher value if you collect more. Tuna, salmon, and squid (if present) each have distinct values.
- Makizushi (sushi rolls): generally score based on how many of a particular roll you have. Three rolls might grant a particular point value; more might yield even more, depending on the edition.
- Tamago and other minor cards: small points but can be the difference between second place and a tie for first.
- Dumplings and Wasabi: special interactions that reward you for stacking certain types or pairing with other cards.
The key is balancing short-term gains with potential long-term combos. The scoring is designed so that even a late misstep (or a late dramatic comeback) remains plausible—no single decision should feel like it’s priced out of reach.

Let’s imagine a round: you’re eyeing a nigiri-heavy line and a couple of maki options. Your hand includes tuna nigiri, tamago, sashimi, and a set of maki. You pick tuna nigiri for a reliable single-card payoff and pass the rest. On the next player’s turn, they take tamago from your deck and pass you something you don’t want to see: another nigiri set that could outshine your own plan. The tension rises as passes continue. When the round ends, you count your points and realize you narrowly beat your neighbor because you clung to that single nigiri that turned out to be the catalyst for a bigger chain later in the game. The satisfaction meter spikes in those small moments; that’s where Sushi Go earns its keeps.

### Timing, Luck, and Table Feel
The luck element is present but light. You can’t control what passes, but you can shape your strategy to maximize your chances with the cards you expect to see. The social dynamic—watching peers fold under the pressure of a well-timed sashimi set or a sneaky wasabi card—adds a layer of playful psychology to the game. It’s not a poker face challenge, but it does reward good reads and quick math, which are perfect for bridging players with different skill levels.

## Strategy: Short Advice for Short Rounds
- Focus on balance: don’t chase a single type too aggressively early on. A well-timed mix of nigiri and maki is often more valuable than a single tall tower.
- Remember the passes: the player to your left is your echo chamber. If they take a card you wanted, you’ll need to pivot quickly on the next draw.
- Watch endgame timing: many players score heavy with combos that require multiple cards. If you can set yourself up to complete a set efficiently, you’ll tend to outpace a table that’s just farming single cards.
- Don’t ignore the little cards: sometimes a pudding-like card or a wasabi can swing the endgame in surprising ways, even if their base points look modest.

For players who want to go deeper, consider the following two-path approach:
- The Conservative Path: aim for consistent, safe scoring with a modest risk profile. It’s steady and reliable and can beat hyper-aggressive tables that fizzle out at the end.
- The Ambush Path: stack for a late surge by prioritizing cards that enable a big endgame swing. This is riskier but incredibly satisfying when you pull it off.
