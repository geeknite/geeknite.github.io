## Specs and Theoretical Speeds: What You See Is Theoretically Real

Let’s talk numbers, because numbers are the grown-up version of “trust me.” The 500GB SSD in review supports USB 3.2 Gen 2 with a theoretical maximum bandwidth of up to 10Gbps. That translates to about 1.25GB/s in ideal conditions. Real-world numbers, of course, rarely hit the ceiling because file types, CPU overhead, cable quality, and the alignment of the planets all matter. Expect sequential reads around 900–1100 MB/s and writes in the 800–1000 MB/s range for large, well-optimized blocks. Smaller files typically don’t hit the peak; your mic clip collection will copy a touch slower, and that’s okay—variety is the spice of backups. In practice, you’ll notice snappy OS drive-like responsiveness when opening applications that live on the drive, and transfers that feel instantaneous compared to older USB 3.0 externals.

For those who love the science of feature claims, here are a few clarifications:
- The 10Gbps name is under best-case electrical conditions with a direct USB-C connection. USB-C cables and hubs can degrade speed; if you’re using a cheap 2-meter USB-C cable, your speed will likely take a grayscale stroll rather than sprint. Use a certified cable for best results.
- The device uses a modern NVMe-based internal SSD, not a SATA-based anchor. NVMe is the reason you get those impressive read/write speeds rather than a nostalgic click of a mechanical drive.
- Real-world sustained performance depends on the host controller, the file system, and what you’re transferring. If you’re moving a 4K video library, you’ll likely see consistent performance; if you’re copying thousands of tiny files, speeds will drop due to metadata overhead. It’s the internet’s fault, not the SSD’s.

To give you some real-world context: when you’re hammering large media files from a laptop with PCIe lanes to spare, you can feel the data being transported in a manner that’s almost cinematic. When you’re juggling random small files, you might need to be a tad more patient, like waiting for the next season of your favorite streaming service—but with less drama and more cybernetic satisfaction.

The drive is generally plug-and-play on Windows and macOS. For macOS, you might want to format to APFS if you plan to use it as a fast scratch disk or time machine equivalent external, while Windows users typically format to NTFS for compatibility with large file transfers. Linux users: you can use the drive as-is after a quick mkfs.xfs or mkfs.ext4 depending on your workflow. It’s not the sexiest part of using a portable SSD, but it’s the part that keeps your data from wandering into the void.
