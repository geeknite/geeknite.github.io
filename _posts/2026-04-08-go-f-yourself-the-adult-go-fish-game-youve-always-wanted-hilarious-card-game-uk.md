## How to play: The basics with a British twist
Go Fish has a core mechanic: collect sets of four cards of the same rank by asking other players for cards you think they’ve got. In this adult edition, that mechanic gets bumped up a notch with prompts that test boundaries, dares that tempt fate, and penalties that make you question every life choice you’ve ever made.

Here’s the simplified flow, with UK flavor added:

1) Each player gets a hand of cards. The goal is to collect complete sets of four cards that correspond to the same rank (e.g., four Fish cards, four Dare cards, four… you get the idea).
2) On your turn, you ask another player for cards of a given rank. If they have any, they must hand them over; if not, tell them to go fish, drawing from the draw pile.
3) When you collect a set, you lay it down and announce it with a flourish, because in this edition, flair counts more than statistics.
4) Some cards trigger special effects. These could be “Dare” cards, which require you to perform a silly challenge; or “Pun” cards, which demand you tell a pun so cringe that even your own embarrassment becomes a weapon.
5) The first player to finish all their sets wins. The rest of you can argue about the ethics of victory while pretending not to care.

To help you navigate the more awkward moments, the rules include house-rule suggestions. They range from the mildly cheeky to the outright ridiculous: think “no handshakes after a failed dare,” or “everyone must wear a silly hat when someone reads a pun out loud.” It’s goofy, it’s communal, and it’s very British in spirit—a reminder that sportsmanship can be politely chaotic.

If you want a quick reference: the core mechanic resembles the classic Go Fish you’d teach to a child, but the content and consequences are designed for adults who remember what it felt like to have no responsibilities. The game happily embraces that duality, offering a nostalgic card game experience with a sharp, modern edge.

## Rule variations and house rules: making it your own
One of the joys of party games is the ability to tailor them to your group. Go F Yourself invites you to experiment. Here are a few house rules you can consider:

- The Double Dare Rule: If you draw a Dare card, you can either perform the dare or swap it with another player’s card pool for a light penalty. Either way, the energy stays high.
- The Compliment Round: When someone finishes a set, everyone must give a sincere (or mock-sincere) compliment to the player who completed it. It sounds fluffy, but the giggles are guaranteed.
- The Elevator Pitch: Before reading a Pun card, the player must deliver a two-sentence elevator pitch on why their pun is the best. This can be excruciating in the best possible way.
- The Tea Break Rule: If a player calls “go fish” twice in a row in the same round without success, everyone takes a sip of their beverage. It’s a softener that keeps the table from devolving into a shouting match.

House rules help you tailor the tone to your crowd. If you’re playing with a group of friends who love improv, lean into the dares and the quick-fire puns. If you’ve got younger siblings who still occasionally steal the chips, keep the content a notch milder while preserving the cheeky vibe. The point is to keep the energy high and the laughter loud without sparing anyone from light-hearted embarrassment.
