## Unboxing and first impressions

Unboxing is the moment when a product earns or loses a lot of the psychological value points. In this Special Guest Box, the packaging is sturdy but not pretentious, letting the miniatures breathe on their little tampered-into bases. The art direction leans into Edouard Guiton style, which means a mix of moody shadows and bold splash colors. If you are into the sort of art that makes you nod and then whisper holy heck at the color palette, you will feel seen. The first thing that hits you is the sense that this box was designed by someone who plays with dolls and then decides to fight a horde of undead in the living room, which is basically what many of us do at 2 a.m. with coffee and a stubborn sense of humor.

The contents list is generous without being obscene. You get a cadre of survivor miniatures, two branded guest miniatures representing Doud and Yomiko, a set of Event and Action cards tailored to this guest theme, and a board overlay that hints at new mission vibes. There is also a guest booklet that shines a light on Edouard Guiton's artist contributions, and a small insert that explains how Doud and Yomiko fit into the Zombicide mythos without stepping on the toes of canonical survivors from other boxes. ## The guest artist: Edouard Guiton

Edouard Guiton has a reputation for turning mundane scenes into cinematic micro-dramas. In this Zombicide Special Guest Box, his influence is most obvious in the packaging, the border art on the mission cards, and the presentation of Doud and Yomiko. It is the kind of collaboration that makes you go from casual gamer to interpretive theorist in the space of a single card flip. The guest booklet includes short notes that read like a love letter to the craft and a wink to the players who know their way around a paintbrush and a vice grip weapon handle.

Gameplay-wise, Edouard’s presence shows through the visual motifs more than any mechanical overhaul. You’ll see cross-hatching in the card borders, mood lighting cues on the survivor portrait cards, and a refined color pairing that makes objectives feel like cinematic set pieces rather than blunt scavenger hunts. If you are the sort of person who frames your game nights as small-scale theater, you will be delighted. If you are the type who only cares about dice math and table presence, you will still enjoy the sense of occasion. The guest box brings a narrative flourish that reminds you that board games can be as much about story as they are about killing zombies with creative flair.

## The survivors: Doud and Yomiko

Doud and Yomiko are not just names on a card. They carry a vibe that blends street-smart survival with a dash of anime-esque determination. Doud is the practical one: bolt-cutters in hand, a weathered backpack full of improvised improvers, and a stubborn optimism that refuses to be stripped away by a zombie downpour. Yomiko, on the other hand, brings a more cerebral approach: careful pacing, a measured move when crowds surge, and a keen sense of when to break the line for a rescue run. In the box, their miniature sculpts are full of character — you can tell who is likely to shoot first and who will linger back to trigger a combined action later.

The cards for these survivors lean into their personalities. Doud’s abilities reward quick, high-impact actions that clear space and create lanes; Yomiko’s kit tends toward protection and tactical repositioning. The synergy between the two is not revolutionary, but it is satisfying. It encourages players to think in terms of team choreography rather than one-person heroics. It is also a reminder that Zombicide’s strength lies in the interplay between character design and the board’s evolving threats. Doud and Yomiko feel unique enough to differentiate team compositions, yet familiar enough for fans of the franchise to integrate into existing strategies.

Two miniatures, two distinct vibes. In a game that can sometimes lean toward chaotic mass play, having two survivors who clearly represent different roles gives you a reason to plan a few safe zones and a couple of trap-clearing combos. It is not a radical reimagining of the core mechanics, but it is a well-executed tone shift that rewards thoughtful play rather than brute force alone.

## Components and build quality

Zombicide is famous for its plastic miniatures, thick cards, and sturdy boards. The Special Guest Box keeps that standard while giving a bit more polish on the aesthetic side. The miniatures for Doud and Yomiko have fine details on their gear. There is some light air resistance to the molding lines, but nothing that would ruin a tabletop base coat. If you are a painter, there is enough detail to justify a layered approach without committing to over-ambitious shading. If you are not a painter, the miniatures still look good on the table after a quick wash, and they read well under standard lighting.

The card stock quality is typical Zombicide: durable, with a nice finish that stands up to repeated shuffles and wipe-downs after coffee spills — not that we test those, but you know this hobby hazard is a thing. The mission cards with Edouard Guiton’s borders are a pleasant touch, giving a sense of cohesion across the guest theme while still feeling integrated into the baseline game. The board overlay pieces fit cleanly and lay flat, avoiding the wonky warps that sometimes plague smaller print runs.

One nice surprise is the packaging itself. It is a little more thoughtful than you would expect for a standard add-on: there are dedicated little slots and a couple of compartments to hold tokens and blocks. It is not a full-on upgrade set, but it is the kind of attention that makes a bit of a difference during setup and teardown. If you are a tidy hobbyist, you will appreciate the small details that reduce the time spent searching for the right resource during a tense moment of a scenario.

For those who care about aesthetics, the interplay of the guest art with the existing Zombicide line feels natural. It is not a radical departure, more of a refined remix that respects the core design while offering a new flavor. The result is a box that looks good on the shelf and plays well on the kitchen table — a combination that is essential for a product in this cost tier.

## Gameplay impressions: balance, pacing, and the guest twist

If you have played Zombicide before, you know what you are in for: co-op survival with a heavy dose of dice, some clever card-driven twists, and the ever-present pressure of a closing clock faction. The Special Guest Box does not reinvent the wheel. Instead, it nudges the wheel a bit to ride smoother on certain terrain. The mission scenarios included in the Edouard Guiton package tend to favor positioning and planned exchanges rather than pure brute force. You will find that the board layout and event cards promote a more deliberate early game, reserving the late-game intensity for that thrilling final push.

The guest theme also brings new flavor to the scenarios. The event cards riff on cinematic beats rather than random chaos, so you get moments of reprieve followed by sudden escalation. It is the sort of pacing that makes you lean in, whisper your plan to your teammates, and then watch the horde roll in with a chorus of groans and excited laughter. The two survivors, Doud and Yomiko, naturally push players toward cooperative tactics: one player can focus on securing escape routes while the other manages crowd control. This dynamic can lead to smoother wound management and fewer unfortunate one-turn wipeouts caused by isolation.

In terms of difficulty, the box sits in the middle of the spectrum for experienced players. It does not dramatically flatten or steepen the curve; rather, it reshapes the terrain in favorable ways for teams that communicate well. If you are new to Zombicide, the new mission design can still be approachable, but you might want to start with a couple of practice rounds on easier maps to get your footing with Doud and Yomiko’s tools. If you are a veteran who likes to sprint through a map with reckless abandon, you may find the pace a touch restrained but still satisfying due to the narrative weight the guest box provides.

Either approach works, which is a sign of thoughtful design.

## Comparisons with other special guest boxes

If you have followed Zombicide releases, you know there are several Special Guest Boxes on the market. The Edouard Guiton package stands out for its stronger tie-in to actual painterly art and its two-squad survivor dynamic, which adds a layer of coordinated strategy absent in some other guest boxes. Some older guest expansions leaned heavily on unique mechanics that could feel tangential to the base game. This latest entry tends to weave its guest elements into the core experience more organically — a plus for players who want a more seamless sense of cohesion during play.

That said, the value proposition is similar: you are paying for a combination of new minis, compatible cards, and a handful of aesthetic upgrades. If you are chasing novelty, there are more flamboyant editions elsewhere. If you are chasing a refined, thematically cohesive add-on that respects the original design while offering new flavor, this one is a solid choice.

## Practical tips for players

- Start with a quick read of the Doud and Yomiko survivor cards to understand their pairing bonuses. You will thank yourself later when the plan actually comes together.
- Use the new board overlays to create choke points and safe zones. Positioning matters more than brute force with this batch of scenarios.
- When painting minis, consider a basic three-step approach: base coat, wash, and selective dry brushing to bring out the gear without losing the silhouette at table lighting. The sculpts read well under standard lighting without needing a full atelier setup.
- For first-time players, pair this box with a couple of baseline Zombicide scenarios to build confidence before tackling the new guest missions. The mix of new art and familiar play patterns makes it a good bridging set.
- If you use a playmat or a modular board, align the guest mission with your layout so that the cinematic moment you envision becomes a playable turn that actually happens.
