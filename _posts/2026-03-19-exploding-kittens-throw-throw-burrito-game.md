## What is Throw Throw Burrito? A Quick Primer
Throw Throw Burrito is a party/dexterity card game where players collect matching burrito cards while trying to avoid getting hit by a thrown burrito. The burrito in question isn’t a real burrito from the local taqueria; it’s a plush, soft, equally dangerous-looking burrito that you literally fling at other players during the chaotic moments of the game. The core mechanics combine set collection (three of a kind, etc.) with dodgeball-adjacent action, where the act of grabbing a card often triggers a “throw moment” where someone adjacent to you will lob a burrito—or pretend to, depending on how hydrated their arms are.

If you’re familiar with Exploding Kittens, you’ll recognize the building blocks: a quirky theme, a fast tempo, and a suitcase-sized dose of chaos. Throw Throw Burrito takes the kitty-engine humor and translates it into physical mayhem, requiring players to be quick on their feet, quick on their reflexes, and quick to laugh at any and all misfortunes. It’s designed for 2-5 players (some house rules push it to 6 or more), with each round typically clocking in around 20-30 minutes. It’s breezy enough for newcomers and spicy enough for veteran table-flippers alike.

For context, the game ships with a handful of burrito-propelled disasters and a deck of cards featuring kitschy art, silly names, and enough pop-culture gags to make a 12-year-old giggle while a 40-year-old nods in nostalgic approval. The humor lands soft rather than sharp, making it accessible to mixed-age groups and those who enjoy cartoons wearing sunglasses at night.

## How to Play: Core Mechanics and Flow
Throw Throw Burrito uses a few simple ideas that feel bigger when you’re in the middle of a chaotic round:
- Set Collection: Players aim to collect sets of burrito cards (e.g., three identical burrito cards) in order to score points or trigger effects.
- Dodge and Throw: When a card instructs you to, you and nearby players may throw the burrito at each other or at specific targets. The aim is not to hit someone in the face—usually—but to complete objectives and avoid losing health on the deck.
- Traps and Zaps: Some cards trigger mischief: trapping a burrito, zapping a target, or forcing exchanges that scramble who has what cards. These are the social engines of the game, generating laughter and occasional chaos-induced rage-quits.
- End of Round: The round ends when certain conditions are met (e.g., a set is collected or a burrito is thrown in a particular way). Scoring is simple: keep track of your successful sets and avoid penalties.

The turn sequence is deliberately straightforward, which is a blessing for groups with varying familiarity with board games. On your turn, you draw cards, form sets, and if you trigger an action that involves throwing the burrito, you do so in the spirit of good-natured chaos. It’s less about strategy and more about timing, social awareness, and who’s willing to duck or dodge with style.

## Strategy and Tactics: Tips for Elevating Your Game (Without Losing Your Sanity)
- Focus on tempo: Round speed is essential. Don’t wait for the perfect card combo; sometimes the best move is yelling, “Throw!” while you randomly pull a set together just to shift momentum.
- Chip away at the burrito targets: If a card lets you steal a set or redirect a throw, do it. The more you shift control, the less certain your opponents feel at the table.
- Positioning matters: If you can dodge with style, you’ll reduce your risk of getting splashed by the burrito that someone inevitably launches in your direction.
- Read your group: Some players want to be the star show of the night; others want to quietly collect sets. Adapt when necessary and don’t be afraid to take a playful shortcut to get everyone involved.
- Endgame awareness: As decks deplete, the game can swing quickly. Be prepared to switch from “gadget-collecting” to “throw efficacy” mode if the table start shouting “Final round!” in a way that sounds suspiciously like a challenge.

Remember: this is a social title first and a game second. The best strategies maximize group participation, keep the energy high, and ensure that everyone, including the kid who just learned to swear a little, has a memorable experience.
