## What is Throw Throw Burrito?

Throw Throw Burrito is a fast-paced, dexterity-driven card game where you lure opponents into combat using burrito-themed cards, while two plush burritos act as the physical weapons in this utterly ridiculous sport. The core gameplay loop involves drawing and playing cards to trigger throws, dodges, and counters. The objective is to accumulate points by successfully hitting players and by completing specific card-based objectives before the round ends.

If you’re worried this is all chaotic fluff, fear not: there’s a surprisingly robust scoring system, with bonuses for streaks and clever plays. The humor lands through exaggerated trash-talk, dramatic hits, and a shared understanding that no one looks cool while wearing a burrito as a helmet—yet somehow you’ll all look gloriously ridiculous together.

## The Setup and How It Plays

### Setup basics

- Shuffle the deck and deal a starting hand (the exact number varies by player count, but the box usually suggests something workable for casual rounds).
- Place the burritos within reach of everyone. A central burrito line is a great idea if you want to enforce a no-cheat zone (which is recommended for the best laughs).
- Each player’s objective is to accumulate points by hitting opponents with burritos or by triggering card-based objectives. The details vary with rounds and card combos, but the chaos remains constant.

### Turn structure

On your turn, you’ll typically draw a card and then play one or more cards according to the rules of the current phase. The most exciting part is when a card says something like “Throw Burrito at the target” or “Dodge this!” You’ll require quick reflexes, a good sense of humor, and the ability to dodge compliments as well as actual burrito throws.

The core mechanic is the ability to throw a burrito at other players using the card actions. When a burrito is thrown, players must dodge, shield, or counter with a Dodge card. If you’re charged with a hit, you might lose points or risk a temporary setback depending on the round’s rules. The whole table morphs into a chaotic ballet: limbs flail, laughter erupts, and someone inevitably becomes a burrito casualty (which, in this analogy, is both a badge of honor and a cautionary tale).

### Cards and burrito interactions

Cards come in various flavors: throw cards, dodge cards, counter cards, and event cards that alter the flow of combat. There’s often a strategic underbelly to the chaos: do you risk a big throw to knock out a rival’s potential points, or do you hold your cards to survive a round’s unpredictable twists? The balance between risk and reward feels very “game-night hero meets chaotic roommate.” It’s messy, it’s loud, and it’s gloriously entertaining when you pull off a well-timed hit while your friend is mid-chant about their next big move.

### Etiquette and safety

Because this is a physical game with plush burritos involved, table etiquette is essential. Clear a little space, remind players that the burritos are soft (and perhaps washable), and consider a mat or cloth to soften any inevitable burrito-laugh-falls from accidental collisions with coffee mugs. The game leans into wild energy; the best sessions are the ones where everyone commits to the chaos, agrees to friendly fire, and laughs until their cheeks hurt.

Officially, Throw Throw Burrito is marketed for ages 7 and up with 2-6 players. In practice, families with younger kids and older relatives can enjoy it if a few rules are modified for safety or pace. The plush burritos make the physical interactions feel safer than a real dodgeball match, but you should still set ground rules about space, avoid throwing near fragile items, and ensure there’s clear communication about “now”s and “stop”s. Accessibility-wise, the game is simple to learn, and the humor helps bring a wide range of players into the fun with minimal friction.

## Strategy and Tips to Maximize Fun (and Points)

### General advice
- Don’t overthink the first few rounds. The early chaos is where you build a bank of stories and learn who’s the best target and who’s the best decoy.
- Use dodge cards strategically. They’re not just protection; they’re ammunition for later turns when you want to set up a spectacular hit on someone who has been annoying you all night.
- Communicate with your group about safety and space. A well-timed “Dodge!” can save someone from looking ridiculous but ends up saving your furniture from a burrito’s surprise wrap.

### Maneuvering the chaos
- Be mindful of table dynamics. If you’re playing with a rowdy group, expect louder cheers and more dramatic resignations (in the most friendly sense). If you have quieter players, give them a little space and reduce the number of hits per round to keep the mood light.
- Target wisely. If someone keeps landing hits on you, switch targets and watch your own score climb while everyone else recalibrates their strategy.
- Use event cards to reset momentum. The game loves momentum shifts; use those moments to redeploy your burrito projectiles with surgical precision (or reckless abandon—both work here).

## Comparisons: How It Stacks Up

### Throw Throw Burrito vs. Exploding Kittens (classic)
Both are built on humor, quick play, and a willingness to push boundaries with silly mechanics. Exploding Kittens leans more toward card-dense mischief with a dark-humor vibe, while Throw Throw Burrito amps up the physical, chaotic, dodge-and-dodge dynamic. If you like the “cheeky humor” of Exploding Kittens but crave more action and fewer long turns, Burrito offers a satisfying alternate path.

### Other party games worth your attention
- Unstable Unicorns: more strategic chaos, less physical slapstick.
- Funemployed: social deduction with improv energy, great for larger groups.
- Codenames: a non-physical team-based word game that contrasts Burrito’s loud, silly vibe.

While the game foregrounds physical humor, it remains gentle enough for most players and is not overly punishing. As with any game featuring physical elements for a group, safety and consent are key. If someone is uncomfortable with being hit, even with plush burritos, consider a variant that reduces throws or uses verbal “hits” instead of actual throws. The core experience—laughter, fast decisions, and social energy—still shines through in any adjusted variant.
