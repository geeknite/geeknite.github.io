## How to Play: The Quick and the Quirky
### Setup
Herd Mentality arrives with a handful of components designed to minimize setup friction—a crucial feature for party games that pretend to be brainy but mainly want to run on autopilot.

- A deck of prompt cards with questions or prompts (e.g., What is something you’d hide from your boss? or Name a famous work of fiction that features a talking animal).
- A supply of answer cards or scrap paper and pencils for players to write down their responses.
- A herd board or a simple scoring tracker (your table surface can stand in if you’re feeling minimalist).
- A timer (optional but recommended for keeping the vibe brisk).

To start, shuffle the prompts and deal each player a small pad or sticky notes. Each round, a “Prompter” (a rotating role) draws a card and reads the prompt aloud while others privately scribble their answers. The Prompter sets the timer, and the herd votes using their written answers when the round ends.

If you’ve played party games before, the mood will feel familiar: light nerve, heavy punchlines, and a chorus of “Oh, I totally said that too!” as someone reveals something hilariously on-brand with their personality and life choices.

### Rounds and Rules: The Herd at Work
Every round follows a simple rhythm:
- The Prompter reads a prompt that invites a variety of plausible answers.
- Each player privately writes down an answer that they think the majority (the herd) would also pick. The goal is to predict the group’s favorite answer rather than ground truth reality.
- All answers are revealed simultaneously. The herd then discusses and votes on which answer is the most popular or which ones best capture the spirit of the prompt.
- Points are awarded for aligning with the herd. If your answer is the one chosen by the majority, you score; if you’re close but not the top pick, you may still gain some points depending on your house rules.

The clever bit here is social alignment. This is not about “what’s correct” but about “what would most people say in this moment.” It’s a kind of low-stakes psychology experiment with snacks and giggles. The prompts are designed to provoke introspection, memory recalls, and the occasional existential crisis about whether you’re a trendsetter or a follower who’s going to end up on a viral meme because your answer was perfectly average.

### Scoring: The Herd vs. You
In the vanilla mode, the goal is simple: be the player whose answer matches the top vote-getter. In practice, the herd can swing wildly; sometimes the most popular answer is hilariously boring, like “air” or “water,” and other times you’ll witness the kind of elevated, niche humor that makes you realize your group is basically a collective stand-up organization.

There are a few common scoring variants you’ll see in the wild:
- Majority wins: The most popular answer scores for everyone who chose it.
- All-match jackpot: If multiple players choose the top answer, they all score higher. This encourages risky, think-aloud strategies.
- Wildcard bonus: Some prompts reward the wild card – a quirky answer that unexpectedly mirrors group sentiment — with extra points.

If your crew tends toward the hyper-competitive side of life, you can amp up tension by introducing a timer and a “hot take” round where players defend their answer with the zeal of a late-night debate team.

### Variants and House Rules: Make It Yours
One of the most delightful things about Herd Mentality is how easily you can adapt it. Here are a few house rules you might try:
- The Silent Herd: No discussion after answers. Points are decided purely by majority, which accelerates pace and suppresses debate-cycling. Great for quick plays.
- The Expert Herd: Each player has to justify their answer in a 15-20 second pitch before voting. This adds a performative layer that is perfect for theater majors and birthday crowds.
- The Nerd Herd: Use pop culture prompts and require references to specific works or characters. Bonus points for citations such as “as seen in X episode.”
- The Cooperative Herd: Teams of two or three work together to write a shared answer, encouraging collaboration and ritualistic high-fives when they guess correctly.

If you’re organizing a larger event, you can split into multiple mini-herds that play simultaneously and then converge for a final face-off round. It’s like a social experiment in micro-mocap (micro cooperative gameplay) with more snacks and less lab equipment.

## Social Dynamics: Who Should Game This With?
Herd Mentality scales gracefully from intimate 2-3 player gatherings to louder, friend-circle blowouts of 6-10 players. The sociability is the feature, not a bug. Here’s how different groups tend to vibe with this title:
- Casual friends: A perfect icebreaker that loosens the lips and builds inside jokes faster than you can say “that one time at the party.”
- Family game nights: Enjoyable with adults and older kids who appreciate gentle humor and shared nostalgia. You might even coax a reluctant aunt or cousin into a candid confession about their favorite cringe playlist.
- Gaming groups: Expect the occasional “this is too wholesome” moment. If your crew loves heavy strategy, you might need to inject a pinch of tempo into the rounds to maintain the adrenaline of a true Eurogame sprint.
- Corporate or mixed groups: Great for morale and culture-building exercises with the right prompts. It’s the kind of activity that slides into a corporate retreat with minimal friction because it asks people to reveal a little bit about themselves in a non-threatening context.

A word about dynamics: the heart of Herd Mentality is peer perception. It asks you to read your friends, predict their behavior, and sometimes deliberately bow to the most obvious—but not always the most accurate—answer. The result is more comedic therapy than stress relief, but oddly satisfying nonetheless.

If you’re worried about the “meta game fatigue,” consider alternating with games that emphasize different skills (deduction, speed, trivia, etc.) to keep the night dynamic and inclusive.

## Replay Value: Do Expansions Make It Better?
The core Herd Mentality is solid, but like any party game designed for casual nights, you’ll want fresh prompts to keep the party vibe alive. Many players buy expansions or create homebrew prompt cards, which is exactly what a geeks-with-adhesive-notepads love to do. The modular nature of the game makes this easy: swap in new prompts, add house rules, or create a rotating cast of prompts that engage specific communities—film buffs, tech enthusiasts, or students who survive on memes and ramen.

Pro tip for the long haul: curate your own prompt stash ahead of game night by collecting interesting questions from real life experiences or pulling from pop culture trivia. Your future self (and your guests) will thank you when you don’t have to dig through the same prompts for the tenth time.

## How Herd Mentality Stacks Up Against Similar Games
Herd Mentality sits in the social party-game lane with a number of peers that share the same DNA of light strategy, social interaction, and quick rounds. It’s worth comparing to a few well-known patterns:
