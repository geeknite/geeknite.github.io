## Overview and first impressions

When a Kickstarter campaign arrives with Season 1 in the title, you expect a certain level of ambition, and perhaps a few stretch goals that end up as resin spoons or cardboard cutouts of everyone’s favorite survivor. Zombicide’s Eva plus Zombivor pack hits you with a packaging that’s mostly efficient, with a dash of flair. Eva the Survivor is described as a nimble, resourceful character who can slip through the undead clump and avoid becoming a snack—though in Zombicide, even the nimble get a chance to try head-to-head with a well-placed bite. The Zombivor figure—the corpse-turned-savant of the husk—reads as a perfect thematic counterpoint to Eva’s mobility: you may end up spending more time looking at the Zombivor than at Eva if you choose to display the hero and the haunt side-by-side.

### The box and components

- A standard Season 1 box with Eva, the Zombivor figure, and a handful of associated cards and tokens. The insert is designed to minimize rattling; there is nothing more exhilarating than hearing a handful of zombie heads rattle at 3 AM while you try to sleep after a long gaming session.
- Card deck quality: the Survivor and Zombivor cards sit nicely in the standard Zombicide layout, with clear symbols and sharp typography. The iconography feels consistent with the rest of the line, which is important if you’re building a cathedral of cardboard minis on the table.
- Miniatures: Eva’s sculpt exudes character without sacrificing practicality; the Zombivor figure has a spindly silhouette that reads very well under Kickstarter lighting. We appreciate the micro-finetuning on the zombie’s ribs and the weathering on the survivor’s jacket—details that will pop with a clean, careful paint job.
- Dice and tokens: standard Zombicide dice, a handful of red-blood tokens, and unique decoration to distinguish Eva’s abilities from the rest of the cast. The tokens feel sturdy enough for repeated use, which is essential for a system that encourages replays and expansions.

### A note on the paint and assembly

If you’re assembling these minis straight out of the box, you’re in for a clean, almost magical experience. Eva’s model features clean lines and minimal mold lines, which reduces your time in the hobby chair and increases play-time. The Zombivor is a little more slender; you may want a fine-file or a hobby knife to tackle any minor flash. We recommend a careful cleaning and gentle priming to let the details breathe. For the paint squad, we suggest a mix of base colors that match Eva’s color palette in the artwork. You can lean into a grim palette with muted browns and olive greens or go for a more dramatic look with high-contrast shading around the fabrics. Either way, remember: you are playing with zombies now, so you can go wild with blood splashes and gore if you want to.

## Eva the Survivor: capabilities and gameplay impact

Eva is designed to be a high-mobility survivor who can slip through the undead clump and reach critical objectives. In practice, that means you should expect to use Eva to dive into clusters of zombies and pull off powerful chain activations. The Season 1 Kickstarter cards push Eva toward riskier plays, rewarding aggressive tactics with strong payoffs, but also punishing you when the dice decide to go full chaos on your plan. The balance of Eva’s kit is not just about speed; she has a couple of signature moves that interact with the Zombivor figure in interesting ways, turning a potential disappointment into a highlight of tactical play.

### Eva’s talent tree

- Move and strike: Eva’s core ability allows you to reposition quickly, enabling you to cut through crowds and trigger alleys where you can avoid line-of-sight from a dozen zombies. This is particularly satisfying when you manage to rescue a survivor from a corner and slip out with a minimal ping of zombie gore.
- Evade and orchestrate: The kit includes a small set of tricks to slow down or misdirect zombies. With proper timing, Eva becomes a moving engine of objective control, letting you orchestrate multi-turn plays that look like something out of a spy thriller—if your spy thriller features a corridor of hungry undead.
- Risk vs reward: The Season 1 cards lean into dramatic flips—if you push too far, you risk a desperate failure; if you hit the sweet spot, you can pull off a sequence that feels cinematic, with Eva dancing through a line of gnarled limbs and slipping away into the shadows.

### Balance concerns and fan perspective

In our playtests, Eva’s mobility was both a gift and a hazard. The card deck occasionally punished aggressive plays with a spike in bad luck. This is not a flaw so much as a design fingerprint: Zombicide’s risk curve thrives on that unpredictable heartbeat that makes each game feel fresh. Some players argued that Eva’s power needs to be tempered when combined with other Season 1 survivors; others argued that the synergy between Eva and the Zombivor adds a new dimension to board control. The truth is somewhere in the middle: Eva works brilliantly as a catalyst for dynamic play when you maintain situational awareness and manage risk. If you’re a fan of tactical moves, Eva might become your default go-to in the early game.

## The Zombivor figure: a microcosm of the undead’s charm

Zombivor is a concept that has lived through multiple Zombicide editions: turning a zombie into a miniature heroic figure with certain control features. The Season 1 Zombivor figure in this set steps into the role with a lot of character: a wounded look, an unusual posture, and enough detail to justify painting experiments (and a lot of gnashing fun when you post pictures to the community). The Zombivor model offers a unique high-contrast silhouette that reads well on the table, especially in dim lighting when your brains-at-2AM hobby session looks more like a midnight art project than a board game. For players who like to display their minis, the Zombivor is a showpiece that draws eyes and invites questions like, Are you sure you want to put that zombie in the display cabinet? Yes, yes we do.

### How does the Zombivor interact with Eva’s kit?

The interaction is more thematic than mechanical, but it matters. Zombivor acts as a mirror to Eva; it shows what can happen when survivors become the thing you fight, albeit with far more personality. The training wheels are off with the Zombivor: it is a reminder that the line between ally and threat can blur—an idea that resonates with seasoned players who have spent nights debating the ethics of clearing a floor filled with undead while a single survivor stands in the fizzing line of consent from a horde. On the gaming table, the Zombivor provides a visually arresting element that helps draw attention to the moral of the story: survival is messy, even when your monsters look stylish.

## Painting tips and display ideas

If you’re looking for practical painting ideas to bring Eva and the Zombivor to life, here are some suggestions:

- Prime in black to make the details pop, then dry brush with grays and browns to create a rugged, worn look for Eva’s jacket.
- Use a darker wash on Eva’s pants to emphasize creases; highlight the edges with a lighter gray, following the natural lines of fabric folds.
- For the Zombivor, consider a more decayed palette: bones, long-decayed skin tones, and maybe a hint of mold green on the clothing. A red wash on the exposed bone areas can provide a chilling look.
- If you enjoy basing minis, add a micro-detail: a tiny bit of broken glass on the zombivor’s base, a stray bullet casing near Eva’s foot, or a small piece of urban debris to set the scene.
