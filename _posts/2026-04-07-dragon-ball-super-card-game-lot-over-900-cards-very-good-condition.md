### A note on condition and authenticity
Very good condition can mean different things depending on who you ask. Some sellers use a range like lightly played to near-mint. In a bulk lot, I typically expect:
- Edges and corners with minimal chipping, notches, or whitening
- Card fronts with clean centering and no major creases or water damage
- Color-accurate prints, with no fading or smearing on intense colors
- No suspicious replays or counterfeit alerts (which are rarer in DBS but possible in very large groups of cards)

What you need is a good light and a careful eye. If you’re planning to flip the lot piece by piece, authenticity checks and a quick feel test for weight and stock quality can save you from a misfit NFT-level disappointment. For that, we’ll look at a few practical tests later in the guide.

## How to verify condition in bulk buys
When you have 900 cards, you’ll want a scalable method to verify condition without turning your rug into a confetti parade. Here are practical steps to keep things sane:

1) Quick surface check: Do a random sampling. Pick 30 cards across the lot and check for edge wear, whitening, and corner damage. If more than 5% show significant wear, you might reassess the lot's value as a bulk buy.
2) Batch testing for durability: Look at a few cards with high color contrast (reds, blues) and check for color bleed or toner smudges. A small amount of color bleed can indicate heavy handling but isn’t a deal-breaker; widespread issues could indicate storage problems.
3) Centering sanity: In many card games, centering affects both aesthetics and value. If you’re evaluating the top subset for resale, note cards with good centers rather than trying to perfect every single one. A few centered bets can carry a surprising portion of the value.
4) Surface and gloss: Run your finger across the surface to feel for micro-scratches or dullness. Glossy fronts should feel smooth; dull surfaces can indicate wear, but not always a disaster for non-foil cards.
5) Back design and misprints: Pay attention to the card backs. If a misprint or miscut exists, it can be a novelty, but it also can harm resale value if the buyer expects standard printing.

Document your results. A simple spreadsheet with columns for card name, rarity, condition, and approximate value helps you track what you’ve got and what you might want to keep or trade.


## Sorting strategy: a practical, scalable approach
Sorting is the heart of turning a bulk lot into a coherent, playable, or sellable collection. Here’s a simple, repeatable method that won’t turn you into a spreadsheet zombie:

- Step 1: Reset the scene. Clear workspace, set up trays labeled Common, Uncommon, Rare, Super Rare, Foils, and Misc (promos, misprints, non-DBS cards). A white light table makes color differences pop.
- Step 2: First pass by art style. Group cards by sets or art variants that you know you’ll want to keep together. This helps you avoid inadvertently shelving a rare behind a common card.
- Step 3: Secondary pass by rarity. Move cards from the “Common” pile that are actually rare or foil-like into a temporary Rare/SR pile. You’ll be surprised how many cards are mis-sorted by memory when you’re tired.
- Step 4: Condition curation. Flag cards for potential grading or professional assessment. In many cases, a handful of near-mint or mint cards can fetch strong prices.
- Step 5: Playables vs. collectors. Create a “Playables” stack for deck-building and casual play, and a “Collectors” stack for high-value or foil-centric trades.
- Step 6: Foils and promos. This is your glitter pile. Not all foils are high value, but some are worth a lot due to rarity or print run. Do not forget to check for commons with foil variants—they can be surprisingly collectible in the right circles.

You’ll get faster with practice. After a few dozen cards, you’ll instinctively know where to place a card based on a quick glance, which saves hours and saves your wrists from repetitive strain.

## Storage, care, and long-term preservation
Now that you’ve sorted a bit and you’ve dreamed about building the perfect super combo, you’ll want to store your collection properly. Card care is not just about keeping things pristine for an Instagram shot; it’s about protecting your future value and the joy of flipping through your binder years later.

- Use proper storage sleeves and “double-sleeve” strategies for valuable cards. This protects against dirt, moisture, and accidental thumb smears during trades.
- Pick binders with acid-free pages and robust rings. A good binder reduces the risk of warping and makes it easier to flip through a giant pile without losing your mind.
- Control humidity and temperature. Dragon Ball characters aren’t the only ones who like to stay cool under pressure; the stock and borders of cards also perform better when not exposed to extreme heat or humidity.
- Organize with labels and a catalog. A simple spreadsheet or database helps you remember what you’ve got, what you’re chasing, and where to find it in the binder when a buddy asks for a specific card during a game night.
