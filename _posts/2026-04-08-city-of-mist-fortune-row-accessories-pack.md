## What’s in the Fortune Row Accessories Pack

The Fortune Row pack is a curated set rather than a “game-in-a-box” expansion. It’s designed to slot into your existing City of Mist sessions without forcing a new campaign arc on your players. Here’s the vibe, itemized like a glamorous inventory sheet that a noir detective would keep in a cigarette-smell wallet:

- 20 fortune cards, each printed on glossy stock with bold noir art and a single-sentence prompt. These aren’t campaign seeds so much as mood anchors—snappy lines that a GM can drop during a tense moment or when a character’s fate hangs on a coin toss.
- 12 character prompts on heavyweight cards. These aren’t full character sheets; they’re flavor portraits meant to jolt a party into new directions. They work best when you pair them with a mask flip or a memory-recollection mechanic.
- 8 status overlays for the GM screen. Think “Investigating,” “Undercover,” “Wanted,” and the like—quick visual cues to narrate shifts without page-flipping. They’re printed on slightly translucent stock that looks classy when backlit by a dim lamp.
- A fold-out fortune map. It’s a stylized city map of Fortune Row with highlighted districts, alleys, and a couple of “mystery intersections” to drop into scenes. The map is generous enough to use as a table cover or a dry-run prop for a one-shot.
- 5 token-style props. Small enamel-like coins, a badge, a micro-prop pistol silhouette, a “street-runner” key, and a slip of paper with a cryptic code. These are your tactile flavor—the kind of props players love claiming as “my token.”
- 1 GM screen insert with prompts. It’s nothing heavy like a thousand-page GM guide; instead, it’s a neat cheatsheet for scene beats, villain misdirection, and clue sequencing.
- 1 adventure hook sheet. A single-page hook to weave into a Fortune Row-flavored arc—great for one-shots or a quick infusion into a longer campaign.
- “Lore slips” for players. Short, flavorful backstory bits that players can pick up when the mood strikes, helping new players get into the thick of it without a heavy exposition dump.
- A sticky-note style coffer for notes. The sheet is designed to hold your post-it reminders in one place, if you’re into real-world GM efficiency.

Overall, the kit leans into the aesthetic that makes City of Mist memorable: a city full of masks, secrets, and metaphors, delivered through objects that feel tangible rather than purely textual. You’re not getting a whole new system. You’re getting a toy box to heighten the sensory experience of the game night.

## Design quality and Components

The Fortune Row pack does not try to reinvent the wheel; it polishes the wheel so it gleams under a neon skylight. The art direction leans into the smoky blues, gold accents, and red vinyl vibes that fans recognize from the game’s imagery. The fortune cards have a sturdy, glossy finish that resists creases during enthusiastic card shuffles and GM-scanner misreads. The overlays are a smart weight—thin enough to be portable but sturdy enough to survive a couple of game sessions without warping.

The fold-out fortune map is a standout. It’s size-friendly for most tables, and the color keys are legible even under dim lighting. If you’re using a lightbox or a backlit monitor, you’ll appreciate the glow of the neon districts on the map. It’s not a pure battlefield map; it’s a scene setter—a canvas for micro-dramas to unfold.

The token props feel like they belong in a museum exhibit about modern noir. They’re convincing enough to spark player imagination and cheap enough to not induce anxiety when a table spring retrieves a coin that someone swears has a cursed fate. The GM insert and the lore slips both do a good job of giving you ready-to-use content without forcing you into a scripted experience.

Aesthetics aside, the physical quality matters because a lot of City of Mist sessions hinge on small moments: a coin dropped on a map, a badge pinned to a coat, a note slipped to a player’s hand. The pack respects those moments by giving you sturdy props that survive coffee, snacks, and the occasional accidental launch attempt by a rogue d20.

## Flavor, mood, and how it fits the City of Mist experience

Fortune Row is more about mood than mechanical power. The game rewards players for leaning into metaphor and narrative misdirection, and this accessories pack is designed to support those choices. The fortune cards act as prompts that can flip scenes in surprising ways; you can deploy them to push a character toward a revelation, or to escalate a conflict by hinting that someone nearby is wearing a second mask underneath the first one.

The “lore slips” are particularly effective for onboarding new players or for sessions where you want to accelerate the “city at night” ambiance. They give players a taste of the world’s texture—enough to spark curiosity without bogging down the group in endless lore dumps.

If you’re already a City of Mist player who loves mood and aesthetics, this pack is a no-brainer. If your table is more combat-focused and you prefer heavy rules crunch, you’ll likely appreciate the pack’s utility but may find it complements rather than supersedes your existing toolkit.

## Gameplay impact: does it change the game?

Let’s set expectations: this is not a rules expansion. It won’t add new dice, new powers, or new masks. What it does do is elevate the storytelling toolset. The fortune cards can be used to prompt a scene, reveal a truth in a dramatic way, or shift the balance of power in a tense moment. The overlays act as quick-state indicators, helping the GM describe shifts in tension without a lot of verbal gymnastics. The map and tokens provide tactile anchors for players, which helps with immersion in sessions that dance on the edge of surreal noir.

In practical terms, here’s how it might play out at the table:

- The GM draws a fortune card mid-scene, which reframes a suspect’s motive with a single line. Players pick up on the new frame instantly, and the scene pivots in a fresh direction.
- A player flips a lore slip during a quiet moment, triggering a flashback that unlocks a memory fragment relevant to the current mystery. The narrative becomes personal and cinematic rather than procedural.
- The overlays get slotted into the GM screen during fast-paced moments (chases, interrogations, betrayals). They act as visual shorthand, helping players keep track of who’s in control and who’s bluffing.
- The fortune map anchors the location-based scenes. You can lay out a sequence of encounters across Fortune Row’s districts, turning a generic urban-night backdrop into a character in its own right.

All of this translates into smoother sessions with fewer missed cues and more opportunities for spectacular reveals. It’s not about adding more rules; it’s about making the existing rules sing with flavor.

## How to use this pack in a typical session

If you’re a typical GM running a multi-session arc in City of Mist, here are practical uses:

- Start a session by dropping a fortune card that reframes the current mystery. The players’ assumptions are now up for grabs, and the session’s tempo shifts immediately.
- Use the lore slips as a quick onboarding tool for new players or for a jump to a flashback sequence. A single slip can unlock a memory fragment that ties directly into a clue or a motive.
- Bring out the map for a mid-session map reveal in a tense stakeout or chase scene. The map gives players a sense of scope and space, turning what could be a vague chase into a choreographed set-piece.
- Reserve a few overlays for pivotal moments, like a reveal or a betrayal. They act as “on-screen” cues—like a director’s cut that your table can read at a glance.

If you’re a GM who loves improvisation, Fortune Row gives you more levers to pull without requiring any heavy pre-planning. If you’re a meticulous planner who wants every beat scripted, you’ll still find the pack useful as a source of ready-made texture you can drop into a scene with minimal adjustment.
