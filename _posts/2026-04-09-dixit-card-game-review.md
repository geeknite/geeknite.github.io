## What is Dixit?

Dixit is a contemporary classic from Libellud that first appeared on tables in 2008. It is a game of storytelling and interpretation built around a deck of gorgeously illustrated cards. Each card is a tiny window into a surreal scene, a moment captured in color and whimsy. The core mechanic is wonderfully simple: on a turn, one player acts as the storyteller. That storyteller selects one card from their hand and gives a clue—this clue can be a phrase, a word, a sound, or even a cryptic reference. The other players choose a card from their own hands that best matches the clue, and all the selected cards are shuffled and revealed. Players then guess which card was the storyteller’s. Points are awarded if you guessed correctly or if your card was chosen by others when the storyteller’s clue was a bit too obscure. The result is a kaleidoscope of interpretation, collaboration, and a pinch of competitive cunning.

A key design flourish is that the art is not a hidden secret to be deciphered like a cryptogram; it is the star of the show. Each illustration invites a different interpretation, and the best clues usually lean on shared cultural memories, personal quirks, or a dash of absurdity. The game encourages players to be as creative as possible, and it respects the collective imagination of the group. The scoring system rewards creativity and cleverness more than sheer accuracy, which makes Dixit feel like a party game that earned its PhD in storytelling.

The zest comes from watching how different players weave their stories and how the same card can be interpreted by three players as three entirely different ideas.

## Components and Setup

Dixit includes a deck of 84 dreamy illustrated cards, a scoring board along a track, and a handful of tokens for keeping score. Setup is quick. Each player receives six cards, the storyteller is chosen for the first round, and a few clue cards are drawn from the deck to be used with certain variants. The rules encourage a calm and cozy table, not a battlefield of cutthroat competition. This is one of Dixit’s strengths: you can jump in without a rules-heavy learning curve, and the group can ease into the gameplay rhythm with a few sample rounds.

## How the Gameplay Really Works (The Quick Rules, In Plain Geek-n-Bites)

If you want the short version, here it is in a few crisp bullets. If you want to savor the details, scroll down; we go deeper like a well-loved recipe:

- Each round, one player acts as the storyteller. They look at a card in their hand and say a clue that describes that card without giving it away. The clue can be a word, a phrase, a song lyric, or even a mischievous omen of doom; the only requirement is that it should not be a direct description of the image.
- The other players pick one card from their own hand that best matches that clue. They submit their chosen cards face down.
- All chosen cards are shuffled and revealed. Players then bet on which card belongs to the storyteller. Everyone secretly votes for the card they think is the storyteller card.
- If at least one other player chooses the storyteller card but not all players do, the storyteller scores points for being appropriately evocative. If no one picks the storyteller card or everyone picks it, the storyteller loses points because their clue was either too obscure or too obvious.
- The winning path is to craft clues that are neither too easy nor too cryptic. The art does the heavy lifting; your clue should act as a hint, not a spoiler. The other players get rewarded for creative interpretation and clever guessing.
- After scoring, the next player becomes the storyteller and the cycle continues.

The vocabulary matters as well. Phrases like a clue that hints at shared experiences or inside jokes tend to do well in friendly groups. A truly stellar clue might be something like a line from a favorite childhood show, a memory from a rainy afternoon, or a cultural reference that only your circle knows. The beauty is that you can tailor the experience to your group’s sensibilities. If your friends are into sci-fi, a clue about a starship or a warp drive can become a running inside joke that fuels multiple rounds.

## Strategy and Party Etiquette: When to Lead, When to Listen

Dixit is a game about communication, but it also has a subtle game of social dynamics. If you want to win consistently, you should learn to tune your clues to your table’s sense of humor and shared references. In practice, that means:

- Build clues that are evocative but not too revealing. A clue that points too directly to the exact card is a guaranteed failure for the storyteller because everyone will pick it. Conversely, a clue that is so vague that no one can connect it to any card will yield a lazy failure for the storyteller. The sweet spot is a clue that resonates with a few players while perplexing others just enough to spark conversation.
- Read the room. If your table tends to be more literal, lean into concrete, slightly cryptic references. If your group likes cultural memes, frame clues with those in mind. The best storytellers are masters at reading the emotional temperature of the table and adjusting mid-round if needed.
- Embrace misdirection and humor. A good clue often relies on a small twist—an odd pairing of concepts or a playful comparison. A clever misdirection can make a card feel like a perfect fit even when it seems far away on the surface.
- Respect the art. The cards are a shared resource. If someone chooses a card that simply describes the artwork literally, it can feel like a missed opportunity. The test of a great storyteller is to coax a few players toward a card they would otherwise overlook, creating moments of delightful realization when someone shouts, That was the one! And yes, that shout is the exact sound you want to hear in a friendly game night.

In short, Dixit rewards social intelligence and a willingness to be a little ridiculous. It is not about calculating the perfect clue every time; it is about building a social moment that everyone will remember with affection and mild embarrassment in the morning.

## Teaching and Running a Session: A Quick How-To for New Groups

Teaching Dixit is a breeze, which is exactly what you want when you are the host who forgot to print the quick-start guide on the back of a napkin. Here is a simple blueprint you can use to run a session without needing a three-hour workshop:

1) Shuffle the deck and deal six cards to each player (or the number your group agrees on).
2) Choose a storyteller for the first round and present a clue that describes the card silently or verbally.
3) Each other player chooses a card from their hand that matches the clue and submits face down.
4) Reveal all submitted cards and let players vote on which card is the storyteller card. Points are awarded according to the standard rules.
5) The storyteller changes, and you repeat. Keep score on the board so the table knows who is the reigning dream-weaver.

To help teach, consider showing a few example clues and cards before you start. This reduces hesitation and invites players to dive into the playful atmosphere. A well-structured demo can turn skeptical friends into enthusiastic participants who will unexpectedly debut their own quirky clues in round two.

## Dixit vs Other Party Games: A Quick Comparo

- Dixit vs Concept: Concept relies on icons and deduction, while Dixit leans on narrative and subjectivity. If you love decoding visual cues, Concept will scratch that itch; if you crave stories that meander, Dixit is your friend.
- Dixit vs Mysterium: Both are heavy on storytelling and art, but Mysterium uses a ghostly theme with clues delivered via abstract visions. Dixit is lighter, more playful, and less likely to turn someone into a convinced psychic skeptic.
- Dixit vs Werewords: Werewords controls competition through word-guessing mechanics and a traitor dynamic. Dixit is more about shared storytelling and group consensus. Choose based on whether you want paranoia or whimsy.
