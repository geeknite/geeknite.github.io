## How to Play and What It Feels Like
Dixit plays in rounds where one player is the storyteller for that round. They select a card from their hand and provide a clue—this can be a word, a sentence, a phrase, or something more abstract. The other players pick a card from their hands that best matches the clue, and all chosen cards are revealed face up. Everyone—including the storyteller—votes on which card they think was the storyteller’s card. Scoring is where the game sometimes reveals the best and worst of human nature: you want to score points for your card being chosen by some, but not all, of the other players. If everyone or no one guesses the storyteller’s card, the storyteller loses points. If at least one person but not all guess your card, you gain points.

The Collection enhances this basic loop by giving you more cards to choose from, which means more potential misdirections and more opportunities to blossom a story that somehow makes sense only to your table at 11 pm. The fresh decks keep the game feeling new longer, so you aren’t constantly recycling the same prompts in the exact same order. That said, more cards also means more time spent in the draft phase. If you are packaging a family game night into a single hour, you might hit a snag where the storytelling takes longer than expected. The upside is that you get richer stories per round, not just quicker laughs. It is a trade-off worth making if you want the experience to feel as dynamic as your favorite animated film after midnight.

A typical session with the Dixit Collection goes something like this: a quiet storyteller sets the mood with a soft clue, the room fills with a chorus of imaginative guesses, and someone inevitably references a pop culture moment no one else saw coming. The laughter from misinterpretations is often louder than the triumph of a well-placed card. That is the charm here: you don’t need a victory margin; you need a memory. And if you are playing with people who have a habit of taking turns describing a scene that everyone simply cannot interpret, you will bond in the shared bewilderment, which is a core part of the Dixit experience.

### Setup and play length
Setup is approachable: lay out the play area, shuffle the collection deck, deal cards to each player, and center the voting tokens. This is the kind of game that is easily taught to newcomers without long setup times and can scale to bigger groups. Play length tends to center around 20 to 40 minutes per session depending on how many players you have and how elaborate the stories become. If you have a room full of raconteurs and casual players, you can push a session longer, but you might also want to fold in a shorter round or two to keep momentum up. The key is to embrace the storytelling pace. If someone goes too technical about why a card implies geometry in the real world, gently remind them that Dixit is a dream while awake, not a whiteboard exercise.

## Practical Tips for Getting the Most Out of the Collection
- Start with shorter prompts your first few rounds to warm up the room. Let people ease into the vibe before you attempt a truly weird clue about a fox on a bicycle.
- Use a timer if your group runs long on storytelling. A gentle nudge keeps momentum and prevents the game from devolving into a long poetry slam about a teapot.
- Swap players often so everyone gets a turn at being the storyteller. The wind of influence shifts with each storyteller, and the room tends to feel more inclusive when everyone contributes to the narrative tapestry.
- Consider pairing the Dixit Collection with a small art show corner. Have a few micro prints or zines on a table to inspire pre-rounds and post-round discussions. It makes the event feel like a mini gallery visit rather than a board game night and adds to the immersion.
- For families, enforce kid-friendly prompts or shared storytelling themes. Dixit works beautifully when kids feel heard and their imaginations are encouraged rather than corrected.
