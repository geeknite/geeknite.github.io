## What is Sushi Go!?

Sushi Go! is a light-weight, family-friendly card drafting game designed for 2-5 players (though there are variants and expansions that expand the party to even more chaos). In each round, you’re dealt a hand of small, cute sushi cards. You pick one card to keep and pass the rest to the next player. Your goal is to assemble the tastiest menu by collecting specific sets of cards that score points at the end of each round. It’s like watching a bustling conveyor belt of edible ambitions, except you’re the one doing the grabbing, not the kitchen staff.

The core appeal is the speed and the ease of teaching the rules. You don’t need a whiteboard to explain what a Nigiri does, what a Sashimi set is worth, or how Wasabi doubles the next Nigiri you play. The game does all of that with crisp iconography and a pinch of whimsy in each illustration. In a geeky sense, it’s the gateway drug to more serious drafting games without forcing your friends to consult the Mayo Clinic for the proper balance of risk and reward.

## How to play: the quickstart guide

### Setup

- Shuffle the deck and deal a hand of cards to each player. In the base game, that hand is small enough to fit on a crowded subway tray table without triggering a small conflict.
- Each player keeps their set of cards hidden for a moment, then you all reveal your chosen card and pass the rest to the left. The ritual repeats until all cards in your hand have been picked.
- Track scores after each round. The round ends after everyone has passed their final card, and you tally up the points earned for that round’s cards. Rounds typically last about 10-20 minutes depending on number of players and how aggressively you chase those Dumplings.

### Turn structure
In Sushi Go!, your move is simple but deliciously layered:
- Look at your current hand and consider what you might want to collect this round.
- Pick one card to keep and pass the rest to the neighbor on your left.
- Repeat until everyone has played all their cards.
- End of round: tally the points for the cards you’ve collected this round and reset for the next one with a fresh hand.

The genius of the drafting mechanic is that you’re always reacting to other players’ choices while trying to predict future passes. It’s a clever light-game approach to risk management: you want certain sets, but you don’t want to open the door for someone else to swoop in and steal your treasured Nigiri with Wasabi sauce on a dare.

### Scoring basics (the quick cheat sheet)
- Nigiri: Each Nigiri card has a numeric value. Collect more high-valued Nigiri for big points, but watch out—wasabi will double the value of your next Nigiri in the same round.
- Sashimi: Three Sashimi cards score a flat jackpot of points; two will not get you there. It’s all about the three-for-one special, folks.
- Tempura: If you grab a pair of Tempura cards in a single round, you score points; otherwise you leave empty plates on the table. It’s the culinary version of a “two correct answers” test.
- Dumplings: The more you collect, the more points you get, scaling in a funny way that makes you root for gluttony.
- Makis: The player who has the most Makis gets bonus points, with second place getting a smaller bonus. It’s the gentle reminder that being first is not always the same as being the most efficient.
- Wasabi: The Wasabi card doesn’t score on its own, but it creates a seismic shift by doubling the value of your next Nigiri card. It’s the game’s sly nudge toward “front-loading” your luck.

This scoring system, despite its simplicity, rewards careful planning and sharp memory. You’re constantly weighing whether to chase a coveted set now or gamble on what you’ll get in the next pass. It’s the reason your brain goes from “I can totally remember these 9 cards” to “I can barely remember my own lunch order.”

## Strategy and quick tips

- Play the long game: Don’t chase a single high-value card if it risks giving away your entire round. Sushi Go! rewards a balanced approach—snagging a few mid-range cards can outperform a single blockbuster, especially when you factor in Wasabi doublings.
- Watch the table: A large fraction of your success relies on predicting others’ moves. If someone has been aggressively hoarding Nigiri, you might want to pivot to Makis or Dumplings instead of clinging to your favorite Nigiri card.
- The Wasabi trap: Wasabi is a sneaky accelerant. If you pick Wasabi early and then can’t land a high-value Nigiri next, you’ll feel the sting of wasted potential. Use Wasabi as a finisher or a strategic counter-move to bait other players.
- Endgame awareness: Because rounds are short, you’ll often see a “final sprint” where everyone tries to grab Marvellous Makis or triple-Dumplings. Don’t panic; a calm, measured approach beats frantic grabbing when time is almost up.
- Adaptability matters: With 2-3 players you’ll often see different pass dynamics than in a 5-player game. Adapt your strategy to the number of opponents and how aggressive they are on the first passes.

## Replayability and expansions

Base Sushi Go! offers a surprising amount of variety for a light drafting game. The different card types—Nigiri, Sashimi, Tempura, Dumplings, Wasabi, Makis—create a mosaic of scoring opportunities that can feel fresh round after round. The short play time means you can chain several sessions in one evening, which is a great way to test different strategies.

Expansions and variants exist to scale the chaos: Sushi Go Party! is the most famous, swapping out the fixed menu for customizable menu cards that change each game. If you’re the kind of player who wants to reconfigure the experience, this is where you’ll find it. For most groups, starting with the base game, then sliding into the party version if you love the concept, is a sane trajectory.

From a design perspective, the base game’s strength is its elegance. There aren’t a million rules to memorize, and there isn’t a mountain of components to juggle. The “brand-new” status simply means the counters and cards feel like they were cut yesterday, and you can trust the integrity of the deck to behave the same way it did when it first rolled out of the factory.

## Accessibility and audience

Sushi Go! is accessible to a wide range of players. Kids can pick up the rules very quickly (and will enjoy the cute artwork and the idea of “choosing one thing to keep”). Adults who want a quick filler before dinner or a light party game for a crowd will also find it engaging. The game scales nicely from 2 players (a speedier duel) to 5 players (more bluffing and more passes to read). The instructions are short and the gameplay is intuitive, which makes it a great teaching tool for introducing drafting concepts to newcomers without overwhelming them with jargon.

There are moments of tension when you realize a fellow player has locked in a valuable set and your choices become an exercise in social mischief—without becoming hostile. It’s precisely the sweet spot many party games aim for: high fun with low conflict.
