## Overview: what is Codenames: Pictures?

### What you’re holding in your hands
Codenames: Pictures is a two-team word-game-meets-illustration party game released as a companion to the original. Instead of 25 word cards laid out in a grid, you’ll reveal a grid of 25 image cards, each one with a unique illustration. The goal remains the same: be the first team to guess all of your agents (the green team vs. the blue team, or whatever color your table chooses) using one-word clues given by your spymaster. The twist is that clues must connect multiple images—without accidentally pointing to an opposing agent or, worse, the assassin card.

The assassin card in this version is also illustrated, which adds a layer of whimsy to the risk. If your clue makes the team guess the assassin, you not only fail the round but also risk losing the game. It’s the same high-stakes vibe, just with more doodles and a little less gloom.

### Visual puzzle mechanics in disguise
The core mechanic remains familiar: one player gives a one-word clue plus a number, indicating how many cards on the table are associated with that clue. Your teammates then discuss and select cards they believe fit the clue. In Pictures, you’ll often be dealing with overlapping themes—things that could be animals, machines, mythological beings, or random objects that share a vibe. The art style of each card matters: some cards are lush and detailed, others are minimalist, and a few are downright surreal. The art helps or hinders your guesses, depending on how well you read your fellow teammates’ tastes and your spymaster’s hints.

### How it feels at the table
This version rewards visual literacy, lateral thinking, and a shared sense of humor. It’s a game about inference through imagery, so you’ll often find yourselves laughing at corporate logos that look more like creatures than brands, or trying to justify a clue like “transportation” for a card that clearly features a “spaceship.” The group dynamic is everything here: a good spymaster can thread a needle between obvious and offbeat, while a bad spymaster can cause an avalanche of wrong guesses with a single vague word.

## Components and setup

### What’s in the box?
- 200 illustrated image cards (plus a few spare ones for modular play)
- 40 agent cards (20 per team)
- 1 blue spy card, 1 red spy card, 1 assassin card, and several innocent bystander cards
- 2 reference cards for clue-giving and guessing rules
- 1 timer (not strictly mandatory, but highly recommended for that added push of panic and hilarity)
- A handy rulebook that explains everything in a way that makes even non-gamers nod along

The art direction on the image cards deserves a shout-out. The illustrations are varied enough to avoid monotony across a full game night, yet cohesive enough that you don’t spend half the round arguing whether card #37 qualifies as a “robot” or a “robotic goose.” It’s also helpful for accessibility: clearer images with high contrast help players who may have visual impairments or color-blindness. That’s not a bonus; that’s a win for inclusivity.

### Setup time: how long does it take to get rolling?
Like its wordy cousin, setup is quick. Shuffle the image cards, lay out 25 in a 5x5 grid, place the agent cards to designate teams, and decide who will be spymaster first. In a standard night, you’ll be playing within five minutes, which means less setup anxiety and more table-talk about which image is the most suspicious-looking toaster you’ve ever seen.

Here’s a quick pro-tip: before you start, agree on a theme or a goofy running gag for your team names. It will become a ritual, and rituals make games feel epic even when you’re playing with a generic pizza box as a table.

## How to play: the rules, simplified
### Turn structure
- Spymaster gives a one-word clue and a number. Example: Clue: “Aviation, 3” if three image cards relate to airplanes, helicopters, or pilots.
- Teammates discuss and select cards one by one, aiming to identify all of their agents first.
- If a teammate taps the assassin card, the round ends immediately with a loss for that team. If they pick an opposing agent card, that card is flipped and the turn ends.
- The first team to reveal all of their agents wins. If both teams complete their tasks, the game can go into tiebreaker rounds, or you can declare a friendly stalemate and go for seconds—ghost pepper challenge optional.

### Clue-giving with pictures: tricky, but satisfying
The biggest learning curve in Pictures is how you translate a word into an image-based clue. The one-word clue must connect multiple cards, but you must avoid inadvertently touching the assassin or the opponent’s agents. This is where a good spymaster shines: they read the room, calibrate risk, and craft a one-word signal that only their teammates could unpack with confidence. It’s a dance of ambiguity, creativity, and sometimes pure chaos—i.e., the best part of any party game.

### Example round (sanitized)
Imagine a spymaster says: Clue “Nocturne, 2.” The team huddles, scanning the grid for images that evoke night scenes, bats, musicians, or related motifs. They pick two cards that clearly connect to that clue. The other team groans because one of the chosen cards also looks like a bat, the other looks like a flute, and suddenly you’re debating whether “Nocturne” is a music reference or a bedtime story. The timer blinks, the room erupts in laughter, and you realize that the art direction achieved exactly what it should: creates delightful speculation rather than boring deductive pathways.

## Strategy and tactics: turning chaos into wins
### Clue-giving heuristics
- Read the room: tailor your clue to what your teammates understand, not what you think would impress the internet with obscure associations.
- Use thematic clues carefully: if your team has a shared interest (e.g., space, cinema, animals), lean into that. The more your team vibe aligns with your clue, the more guessing will feel like a collaborative moment rather than a stress test.
- Manage risk: balance the number you shout with the safety net of the cards you’re certain about. A safe 1-2 clue may win the round as quickly as a bold 3-4 that could blow up in your face.
- Accents and cross-overs: images with multiple possible valid interpretations are both a blessing and a curse. They invite discussion and memory recalls; they can also derail a guess line if you misread your team’s taste.

### Guessing strategy: what to look for on the table
- Look for clusters: cards that share a common theme appear to be the intended targets. If one card screams “futuristic vehicle” and several others share that vibe, a cluster guess becomes plausible.
- Read the relationships: sometimes you’ll see a strong connection that’s thematic rather than iconographic (e.g., all images in a row relate to a single movie or myth). Use that to guide your next guess.
- Don’t overspecify: if your clue is “Nocturnal, 3,” you may not want to push for a fourth card unless you’re certain. The risk of hitting the assassin becomes real when you chase a flashy fourth option.

### Team dynamics: roles matter
A great spymaster is patient, concise, and willing to pivot. A good clue is like a well-timed joke: it lands when your audience is in the right mood. A bad clue will derail the round with an avalanche of questions. Players who love wordplay may be tempted to overthink, while players who are visual thinkers will quickly map images to potential clues. The best teams blend these styles, trading turns between prying for detail and trusting the others’ intuitive leaps.

## Variants, expansions, and house rules
### The standard game versus longer formats
If you’ve got a bigger group, you can play with multiple teams or introduce a “double-agent” mechanic, where two agents from the same team appear on the grid to complicate the guessing process. You can also extend rounds by reducing the number of cards or increasing the number of images per row. The rules are modular enough to accommodate casual play or a tournament-night vibe.

### Expansions and additional art packs
Some editions come with extra image packs that introduce new motifs—mythology, sci-fi, or surrealist imagery—to keep things fresh after you’ve exhausted your first dozen rounds. If you’re buying additional packs, look for art that’s bold and recognizable from a distance. Busy images can be visually overwhelming and slow down guessing, which defeats the purpose of a fast party game.

### House rules that actually work
- Timed rounds: a 60-second window to guess all your cards adds a fun pressure that mirrors the original game’s vibe.
- Thematic chains: once you guess a card, your team must circle back to discuss the relationship between the new cards and the previous clue; this encourages on-table storytelling and keeps players engaged.
- Multi-language mode: for multilingual groups, a single clue can be translated into multiple languages for an inclusive experience—and also to hilariously misinterpret a clue because of translation quirks.

## How it stacks up against Codenames: Words
### Similarities you’ll recognize
- Team-based deduction with a single clue giver and multiple guessers
- A race to find all your agents before the other team
- A ticking clock or the pressure of a round limit to keep energy high
- The thrill of a risky guess that pays off, or a disastrous misread that becomes legendary

### Differences that matter
- The primary vehicle is imagery, not words. This shifts the cognitive load from vocabulary and word associations to visual interpretation and common cultural references.
- The assassin card remains, but its identity is rendered through art, not text. It can be trickier to identify visually, which increases tension and humor.
- The table’s dynamic shifts from deciphering language to decoding a group’s shared mental library of images and references.

### When to choose Pictures over Words
- If you’ve got mixed company—some word-game veterans, some non-gamers—Pictures is often more approachable.
- If you love collaborative storytelling and visual humor, Pictures provides a playground where you can riff on the same image with different clues.
- If your group tends to stall on language-based clues or if you want a quicker mental reset between rounds, Pictures can reduce boredom and speed up decision-making.
