## How Skip-Bo Works (In Short, For the Curious Time Travelers)
Skip-Bo is, at its core, a race to get rid of all your stock cards. The name suggests nothing and everything at once, like a mysterious vending machine that only dispenses chaos. Each player starts with a stack of face-down cards. On your turn, you reveal and play cards in ascending order, from 1 to 12, or reveal a Skip-Bo wild card that can stand in for any number. You draw up to five cards, hoping your sequence grows taller than your opponent’s confidence. The first person to empty their stock pile wins. It sounds simple, and it is—until the table decides to turn it into a dramatic negotiation involving wild card diplomacy, clever card placement, and the subtle art of snatching victory just before your cousin claims bragging rights for the next 14 hours.

The sealed 1986 edition sticks to the skeleton of this formula but adds the charm of a bygone era where players wore their homework as badges of honor and believed that a good game night could replace therapy. The rules on the sheet are straightforward: use your cards in order, keep your stock piles hidden, and be kind to the rules, except when being cheeky is more fun. It’s not a deep strategic trench warfare; it’s a sprint with occasional detours that require careful reading of the rules and a decent memory for which pile is your own and which pile is your neighbor’s. The result is a fast-paced, surprisingly forgiving experience that still rewards clever sequencing and a dash of mischief.
