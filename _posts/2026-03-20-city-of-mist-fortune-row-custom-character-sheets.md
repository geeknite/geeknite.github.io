## Why custom sheets for Fortune Row matter

### Thematic alignment
Fortune Row is where glamour meets grit and where luck feels like a character with a backstory and a grudge. Custom sheets let you embed narrative prompts directly into the sheet, so players are nudged toward the mood you want at the table. A sheet can remind players that in Fortune Row, favors are bought with a smile, and a smile can cost you a long, winding fate.

### Clue and relationship orchestration
City of Mist runs on clues and relationships as a social engine. Custom sheets can provide dedicated spaces for Clues in hand, People of Interest, and Faction ties that matter in Fortune Row. If your GM wants the table to track who owes whom a debt or which masked powerhouse is pulling the true strings, a sheet that makes those relationships visually obvious is worth its weight in noir dust.

### Clarity amid complexity
The Mist is a big, sprawling concept with Power, Myth, and Mystery swirling around in the same room. A bespoke sheet can declutter the macro mechanics and present the micro drama on a page: who the Mask sees as ally, which Phantom is clinging to the backstory, and what the current open questions are. It is a small sheet, but it packs a big punch when your night is a little too exciting for a single glance.

### Accessibility and pace
Custom sheets can be designed for quick play or for slow, drama-fueled sessions. They can be one-page wonders for quick-start nights or two-page epics for players who want to map a sprawling noir saga. Fortune Row demands pace: the neon hum of the street, the whisper of a wager, the click of a switchblade. A well designed sheet can nudge the pace forward without stealing the spotlight from the story.

## Design principles for Fortune Row sheets

### Clear masks and personas
City of Mist uses masks as a narrative lens. A Fortune Row sheet should highlight the two masks the character carries, and possibly a third one as a nod to the city’s dual nature — the public persona and the hidden godhead beneath. The sheet should present a clean split between Public Persona and Hidden Motive, with quick access to the associated moves and the typical boolean states of how the Mask interacts with the city.

### Relationship wires
Fortune Row thrives on the relationships that connect players to NPCs and factions. Create a dedicated space for Relationships and Faction Ties. It should be easy to add new lines, reorder them, and visualize how a single decision can ripple through the web of ties. This is where your sheet becomes a living board for social mechanics rather than a static ledger.

### Clues and clues in reserve
A Fortune Row character lives in a sea of clues. A well crafted sheet dedicates a grid or bullet list for Clues, Holdings, and Side Quests that are tied to the Fortune Row setting. The best sheets give you a reason to hold onto a clue, a reason to trade it, and a reason to risk something precious to unlock a reveal in the next scene.

### Nimble aesthetics
Fortune Row is luxury and decay in a single frame. The visual language of the sheet should evoke neon, velvet, and rain-soaked sidewalks. Use color-coding that aligns with moods (get your noir blues and gold luck tones in there) and a clean, readable typography so players don’t need a magnifying glass to read their own backstories.

## Building a Fortune Row character sheet from scratch

### Core attributes and masks
A typical City of Mist character sheet has sections for the two primary masks that power the character, plus core attributes such as Level, Power, and Edge. In Fortune Row, you can adapt this by giving each mask a dedicated column or card on the sheet, with space for a short text description, a most-likely tag, and a few quick moves that embody the character in that mask. This keeps the ethos of masks intact while letting Fortune Row flavor flow in.

### Clues, investigations, and turns of luck
Design a Clues area that can hold the most current lead, the best suspect’s alias, and the emotional stakes attached to the clue. You might want a separate table for Turn of Luck: what the character risks to gain a lead, what the city owes them, and what a bad roll would reveal about a deeper conspiracy in Fortune Row. The intent is to create a steady rhythm: obtain a clue, push the plot, and see what the city rewards or punishes.

### Relationships and faction links
Create a Relationship table where each line captures the NPC name, the nature of the relationship (ally, acquaintance, rival, rival turned ally), and a short memory prompt that can spark roleplay. Also include a Faction/Ties section for Fortune Row specific groups such as a deck of card sharp patrons, a local mystic guild, or a crime syndicate with a red carpet and a short list of simple, trackable consequences.

### The look and feel of the sheet
Decide early whether your sheet will be text heavy or graphic-heavy. A one-page sheet is perfect for quick sessions and con games; a two-page sheet is ideal for full micro-arcs and a deep dive into Fortune Row lore. If you want to mix digital and analog play, consider a clean printable PDF and a companion Notion or Google Sheets version for grid-based tracking. The digital version is particularly nice for players who like to click through relationships and update Clues with the snap of a browser tab.

## One page vs two pages: templates you can copy

### One page quick play sheet
This template is meant for speed. It includes the following sections on a single page: Mask 1, Mask 2, Core Stats, Clues, Relationships, and a small Narrative Prompt box. The layout should be grid-based so players can scan and fill in values in moments between scenes. The content should be legible at a distance and not require a magnifying glass. The one-page sheet is for nights when Fortune Row is throwing a parade while your brain is juggling the last clue and a mysterious debt.

### Two page narrative sheet
This template allocates two pages to long-term character development. Page one covers Masks, Clues, and Powers; page two is Narrative Anatomy: Backstory, Key Relationships, and a short list of Narrative Prompts that coax the player into scene acting rather than mechanical ticking. The two-page sheet is where you really lean into the noir and mythic vibes. The extra real estate invites you to describe the texture of Fortune Row, the way a streetlight flickers, and how a small choice can turn into a legend with a single dice roll.

## Tools and techniques for crafting sheets

### Color coding for mood and function
Assign a color to each mask and another color to each relationship type. Use these colors on the sheet for quick scanning. If a player is wearing two masks in a scene, the color cues help the GM know which mood is driving the action. For Fortune Row, you can also color-code by scene location: glitzy casinos in blue, rain-soaked alleys in purple, backroom deals in gold. A quick palette helps everyone stay in the vibe, even when the plot is twisting like a pretzel on a rainy night.

### Digital templates and ease of use
If you plan to run a hybrid table or a fully digital game, consider making a Google Sheet or Airtable template that mirrors your printable sheet. Include drop-down menus for Clues and a simple relation-mapping tool for Relationships. Link the digital sheet to your Notion page with a clean embed so players can update their Progress and Promises as the story unfolds. The key is to keep the magic flowing without turning the game into a spreadsheet apocalypse.

### Accessibility considerations
Make sure fonts are readable, contrasts high enough for dim basement lighting, and that the sheet remains legible when printed at common sizes. Provide high-contrast versions and large print options for players who need them. Accessibility is a form of hospitality in the Mist: you want everyone at the table to feel like a myth has opened the door for them, not that the page requires a magnifying glass and a PhD in typography.

## Playtesting your Fortune Row sheets

Playtesting is not optional; it is a rite of passage for any table nerd who wants a better experience. Start with a quick session zero where players fill out the template and talk through how their Masks interact with the city. Then run a session with a reduced encounter arc to test Clues and Relationships. Finally, run a one-shot where you throw in a mystery that ties to Fortune Row landmarks. After each playthrough, collect notes on readability, pacing, and how useful the sheet felt in action. Are the Clues too nebulous, or too obvious? Are the Relationships fun to reference during scene beats, or do they just clutter the page? Use this feedback to refine the templates and print another batch.

## Practical tips for GM and players

### For GMs
- Emphasize the hookup between Fortune Row and the player character backstories. The city loves a personal angle, so nudge players to tie a Clue or a Relationship to a personal memory from the character sheet.
- Use the sheet as a table companion, not a barrier. If a question arises in play that the sheet cannot answer quickly, allow a quick improvisation and then update the sheet post-session.
- Build micro-puzzles around Fortune Row landmarks. A sheet that points players toward a landmark with a short prompt invites dynamic scene-building without slowing down the action.

### For players
- Don’t be afraid to annotate your sheet with doodles or tiny icons. A little visual shorthand speeds up a busy combat or chase sequence and adds personality to the character.
- Treat clues as a deck you draw from gradually. Don’t reveal everything at once; let the sheet guide you toward a reveal that recontextualizes earlier choices.
- Use the Narrative Prompt space for ongoing character growth. A prompt that nudges a personal goal helps the GM weave the player into Fortune Row in meaningful ways.
