Sushi Go! 10th Anniversary Edition arrives not as a squeaky-clean reprint but as a little party you bring to the table. It remembers where the game started, and then slides a few new gears into the drafting engine to keep everyone on their toes without turning the whole thing into a costumed parade. If Sushi Go! was the pocket-sized starter kit for modern drafting, this edition is the deluxe carry-on bag, complete with a six-player expansion, upgraded stock, and a gloss that catches the eye without forcing you to squint at every card. If you own the base game, this edition is a nudge toward a nicer experience. If you don’t, it’s a strong invitation to see why quick drafting with friends can feel like a micro-adventure.

## Overview: what the edition does differently
The core loop remains the same: draft a card from a passing hand, then pass the rest to the left, and chase the best little combos in a single round for score. The 10th Anniversary Edition does not reinvent the wheel; it polishes it. The changes are tactile, readable, and polite enough to make you think, This is how a 10th anniversary should feel. The six-player expansion is tucked in, so you can open a night that easily becomes a sushi symposium with a few extra players. There are improved score sheets and optional player mats to help you visualize your scoring vectors during the chaos. And yes, there are revised illustrations sprinkled through the deck—mostly to lean into the lighthearted flavor while keeping the familiar silhouettes intact so you and your tablemates don’t need a cheat sheet to recognize a tuna nigiri at a glance.

## Gameplay and pacing: does the draft feel faster, smoother, or shinier?
Sushi Go! thrives on a simple motor: draft, pass, score. The Anniversary Edition keeps that motor humming but tunes the car for smoother turns. Card stock is less prone to warping after a few rounds, which means less fiddling with the table and more proactive plotting. The six-player mode indeed spices up the decision-making—more players mean more passing loops, more chance for someone to steal your carefully aligned plan, and more opportunities to chase the cake that is pudding.

In practice, six players creates a micro-ecosystem where the pudding pile becomes a strategic pivot. Some players don’t care about pudding; others consider it a secondary currency—worth chasing when the right set is in reach, but easy to overlook when your eyes are on those perfectly formed nigiri clusters. The mode also makes table talk livelier. Even in a tight circle, the energy grows as more players announce near-misses and delightfully dubious plans to sabotage someone else’s dumplings with a late-passing maki. For a party game, it’s refreshingly breezy: no heavy rules, no meta decks, just quick decisions and quick laughs.

## Strategy and table talk: what to actually think about during the draft
The core strategic idea remains: identify which combos are open to you in the moment, decide whether to commit to a path, and be mindful of what you’re passing. What changes in 10th Anniversary mode is the tempo and the visibility. With more players, you’ll see more signals from others about what they want on their next turn, which helps when you’re trying to time your dumplings just so you can grab the last big score and also grab a laugh from someone else’s misread on the pudding tsar.

A few practical tips to maximize success without overthinking the chaos:
- Dumplings scale nicely, but don’t overvalue them early. You want to set up a future round where you can either complete a large dumpling set or pivot toward a sashimi or maki in the next hand.
- Sushi combos reward timing: holding onto a card when the near-term hand would enable better scoring for the table can backfire if someone else snaps a strong short-term set. Balance your own lead with a sense of shared momentum.
- Don’t ignore pudding, but don’t worship it either. In six-player rounds, pudding can swing late-game scores, but it’s rarely worth fighting tooth and nail for if you’re behind on other points.

For newcomers, the rules stay delightfully approachable: you can learn the entire flow in under five minutes, and then you’re free to spend the rest of the night arguing about whether tuna nigiri is the best or whether the salmon has secretly undermined your plan. For veterans, the Edition offers enough polish to keep you returning for a quick bout before dinner or after a long match of something heavier.
