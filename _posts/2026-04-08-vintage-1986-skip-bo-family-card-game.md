## No instructions? Here is a playful guide to get you started
Suppose you open the box with the confidence of someone who has played many board games, only to realize that the instruction pamphlet has vanished into the ether. What now? You do what every competent gamer does: you create a set of rules on the fly that feels like it could have been a real game if the designers had a few more weeks of coffee fueled iteration.

First, establish the goal. In classic Skip-Bo, you want to be the first to empty your stock piles by building up sequences from 1 to 12. In UNO, the objective is to be the last player with cards in hand. A reasonable hybrid for this vintage deck: build up to a target sequence in color, while using wild cards to switch the color or to skip the opponent who is about to celebrate. The absence of instructions invites house rules the kind of rules you tell your kids will be the official rules only if the kids survive the first round without staging a mutiny.

Second, define your turn structure. A simple house rule for chaos: draw three cards, then play as many as you can to start a stack, and at the end of your turn, draw until you reach five cards. If you land on a wild, declare a color that will be in effect for the next player's turn. If you can't play, you draw. If you draw until you have to start a new deck, congratulations you have invented a brand new variant.

Third, decide how to score. You might simply count the number of cards left in each player's stock piles. The fewer left, the better. Or you could implement bragging rights points, where players earn prestige for dramatic mistakes, such as miscounting by two cards and discovering that your mistake has become the new house rule.

Fourth, define the fate of the wilds. Do wild cards let you choose the color? Do they skip turns? Do they let you swap piles? Invent your own. The deck rewards a little ruthless creativity; consider it a sandbox for your inner game designer who forgot to eat lunch and has a vivid memory of arcade cabinets.
