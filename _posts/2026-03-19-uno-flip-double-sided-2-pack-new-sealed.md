## How to play with the two pack setup
### Setup and first turns
Like classic UNO, each player starts with seven cards. The top card of the draw pile is flipped to begin the discard pile. The flip rule introduces a moment of cinematic suspense: if a flip card is played or drawn due to a special action, the current side might switch, which is part of the fun. If you play the oposite color on the flip side, you should keep track of the color you are using and avoid overthinking, because this is a game that rewards memory, quick pattern recognition, and a willingness to bluff yourself into thinking you know what side is currently in play.

### Turn structure and flip timing
On your turn, you must either play a matching card by color or number or play a wild card that can redirect to any color. The unique twist in UNO Flip comes when you flip to the opposite color side via a flip card or as a consequence of a special action. When the flip happens, the new color and actions become active, creating a sudden shift that is part strategy, part theatrics. Players must stay alert for the moment of reveal—the flip card can dramatically alter who has the advantage and who is suddenly on the back foot.

### Strategies worth exploring
- Read the table and anticipate who is about to declare UNO. If you see someone holding a lot of high cards, it may be worth not chasing the color you currently need, because the flip could hand them a bailout and hand you a crushing set of penalties.
- Use wild cards to force color changes that benefit you while sabotaging your opponents. This is especially effective if you know the blue side is dominant for a while or if the red side has punishing penalties ready to unleash.
- Pay attention to card distribution. Double-sided decks can alter the frequency of flip opportunities. You can sometimes predict when a flip may come by keeping an eye on who has to draw due to consecutive penalty cards.
- House rules can tailor the pace. If you like a quicker, more chaotic game, implement a rule that every flip changes the color automatically. If you prefer longer, more tactical matches, add a house rule to slow the alternation down and force more deliberate play.
