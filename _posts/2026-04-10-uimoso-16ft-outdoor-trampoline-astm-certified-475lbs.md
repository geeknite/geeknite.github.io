### Assembly: how long, how painful, and how fun
Let’s be real: any 16-foot trampoline is not a one-person, 15-minute job. The official manual suggests 2-4 hours for a first-timer, but seasoned backyard engineers can bring that down with a good playlist. Here are the essential steps we observed:
1. Unpack and inventory: confirm you have all rails, springs, and the net. Pro tip: lay everything out in a large work area so you don’t lose a bolt to the sonic void under the shed.
2. Frame assembly: connect the frame rails into a circular base. Use the included bolts, washers, and the Allen key. If you’re a wizard at wrenching, you’ll have a satisfying click when the frame forms. If not, you’ll end up doing a dramatic sigh while your dog watches.
3. Attach the springs: this is where the trampoline starts looking like a spring-a-renaissance. Some folks use a t-shirt trick (slip the spring through the frame and the mat) to speed this up. The number of springs matters for bounce uniformity; don’t skip this step.
4. Attach the jumping mat: this is the moment you hold your breath and hope gravity doesn’t betray you with a misalignment.
5. Install the enclosure: run the netting posts around the frame and secure the net with the zips or clips. Ensure the entry is accessible and the zipper or entrance isn’t a trapdoor to doom.
6. Pad installation: place the protective pads over the springs and frame. They should be snug against the frame so your shins aren’t flirting with raw metal.
7. Final checks: test bounce gently at low height, then gradually increase. Check for loose bolts, wobbles, or springs that are misaligned.

Pro-tip: Have a friend hold the pads while you bolt the frame; it saves cursing, and you might avoid hitting your own toes with a wrench.

### Maintenance and longevity: keeping this beast bouncing
- Inspect springs every few months for signs of wear or rust. Replace any suspect springs promptly to avoid uneven bounce or detachment.
- Check bolts and connectors for tightness. The vibrations of jumping can shake things loose.
- Clean the mat and net occasionally to remove dirt and debris. A gentle hose-down can prevent residue buildup, but avoid direct high-pressure spray on the springs.
- Keep the area around the trampoline clear from obstacles. A safe circumference of at least 6-8 feet is recommended to prevent unwanted collisions with trees or garden gnomes.
- Store away during extreme weather, if possible. In areas with heavy snow or storms, consider removal to avoid damage.
