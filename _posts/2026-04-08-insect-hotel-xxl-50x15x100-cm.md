## Unpacking and Setup: A Tiny-Home Tour

Receiving the XXL feels like being handed an architectural blueprint for the insect world. It’s large, it’s wood, and it’s ready to be mounted in whatever corner of your garden can support a TED Talk about life’s small wonders. Here’s a practical setup guide:

1) Inspect the unit. Look for loose fittings, ensure there’s no pest in transit, and confirm that all layers are securely connected. If you’re worried about splinters, wear gloves; if you’re worried about stability, you may want two hands. 
2) Pick a location. The goal is sun for warmth without turning the interior into a sweatbox. Afternoon shade helps in hotter climates; morning sun with afternoon shade works well in many temperate zones. A sheltered spot protects against rain while still allowing air to circulate.
3) Mounting method. Wall brackets are great for a fixed position, while a freestanding post or fence attachment gives you flexibility. Use stainless steel screws to resist rust, and pre-drill to avoid splitting. If you live in a windy area, consider a brace or two to prevent sideways swaying.
4) Orientation and accessibility. Position cavities to be reachable and visible for quick check-ins. South-facing cavities tend to warm faster but avoid direct sun on the most delicate materials.
5) Fill with habitat material. A mix of bamboo tubes, drilled blocks, pinecones, bark, straw, and cork pieces creates a buffet for different insects. The goal is to offer varied hole sizes (3-8 mm for mason bees, 6-10 mm for others, etc.) and to stagger materials to improve ventilation.

### Placement considerations
- East-facing walls give morning warmth, which is helpful for early rising bees.
- Avoid placing it on damp ground or under eaves where splashback might dampen the interior during rain.
- Keep some distance from heavy foot traffic to reduce disturbances for nesting insects.

## Fillers and Habitats: A Microhabitat Menu

What you put inside the hotel matters almost as much as the dimensions themselves. The XXL’s generous footprint accommodates a curated selection of fillers that create overlapping realms for different species:
- Bamboo tubes (3-8 mm diameter) emulate hollow stems where mason bees love to nest.
- Drill-block cavities (3-8 mm) provide guaranteed nesting spaces for various solitary bees.
- Hollow stems or reed sections add vertical variation that some beetles and lacewings enjoy.
- Bark chunks and cork pieces create crevices and micro-rooms for shelter.
- Straw and pine needles lock in warmth while offering nibble-sized nooks for overwintering.

Moisture management is key. The last thing you want is a mold party taking place inside your hotel. Ensure bottom drainage and avoid sealing the entire unit with heavy paint or varnish; the insects need to breathe. If you want to protect the interiors during heavy rain, a light overhang or a wooden canopy helps.

## Maintenance: Ongoing Tenant Management

The insect hotel is not a static sculpture; it’s a dynamic ecosystem that requires occasional maintenance. Here are practical tips to keep occupancy steady:
- Check cavities every few months for moisture, mold, or weather-induced wear. If you see damp wood, you can prune back the plant cover or adjust shading to reduce humidity.
- Refill as necessary. Old materials can degrade and attract unwanted pests. Replace with fresh, clean fillings to maintain a healthy microhabitat.
- Clean out dead matter after active seasons. A quick brush or gentle vacuum removes dust and detritus that can accumulate and deter visitors. Avoid heavy cleaning during peak nesting periods to minimize disturbance.
- Inspect mounting hardware for rust and tighten screws as needed. If you notice wobble or movement in the structure, add bracing or re-anchor to ensure functionality for the next season.
