## How to play in 2-player mode
This is where the two-player variant shines. If the original Bananagrams is a roaring river, Duel is a fast mountain stream—quick, refreshing, and occasionally splashing you with a stray vowel that forces a creative pivot.

### Setup
- Each player takes a rack and a pile of tiles from the bag or a shared tile pool. The aim is to have enough letters to start quickly but not so many that the game devolves into a dictionary debate between rounds.
- Decide who goes first. The ceremonial moment is part strategy, part bravado, and part wondering if your opponent has a secret seven-letter word up their sleeve that will ruin your day.

### Rules and flow
- When the timer starts, both players race to build a connected grid of valid words using all their tiles. The faster you assemble, the better your bragging rights—though in practice you’ll be more focused on not dropping your rack onto the floor and discovering your neighbor has learned a new word that begins with Q and ends with Z.
- Words must be valid according to standard dictionary conventions. This is Scrabble-adjacent territory, so a little improvisation is allowed so long as it stays within reason. The goal is to weave a series of intersecting words that share tiles in a way that looks like a micro crossword cluster rather than a chaotic jumble.
- If a player uses all their tiles, they shout Bananagrams and bask in a moment of triumph—perhaps while the other player groans and tries to salvage dignity with a well-timed pun. If both players finish, the round ends in a quick adjudication, usually decided by who can assemble the longest, most coherent network of words first.
- If you get stuck, you can swap tiles for a small penalty and keep the pace moving. The swap mechanic helps avoid long stalls that would kill the momentum and the fun.

### Pacing and round length
Duel sessions are short and sweet, typically 5 to 15 minutes per round depending on how fast you think and how hungry you are for cheese-level puns. It’s designed for multiple quick rounds, so you can string together an entire night of word battles without needing a sit-down feast of rules and expansions.

## Things that could be improved
- Material quality: while the tiles are durable, the plastic case could be a touch sturdier. If you’re the kind of gamer who treats games like armor, you might want to handle it with a touch more care or consider a protective sleeve for travel.
- Scale for long campaigns: the two-player setup shines in short bursts, but if you love sprawling word-building marathons, you might crave a larger board or an expanded grid option to extend the playtime beyond a handful of rounds.
- Tile distribution variance: in some rounds you’ll chase long, vowel-rich words that leave a cluster of consonants behind. That can slow things down briefly and require a creative pivot to salvage a good strategic flow.

## Strategy, tips, and common pitfalls
- Prioritize vowels early: because vowels unlock many word possibilities, securing a flexible vowel pool can help you pivot when your initial word choices crash into your opponent’s strategy. Don’t hoard vowels to the point you can’t form a first word; instead aim for a balanced rack that can morph into multiple word pathways.
- Read your opponent: in a two-player duel, observe your opponent’s rack and anticipate their potential moves. If you see they are chasing long words, you might favor quick, high-utility words that block their access to valuable tiles.
- Use crosswords strategically: building a word network that antagonizes your opponent’s space can be as effective as creating long terms. It’s about finding the right balance between offense and defense in a compact grid.
- Kill the tempo deliberately when needed: a short pause to assess the board can pay off. The game rewards precision as much as speed; don’t be afraid to slow down for a critical strategic insight.
- Practice with dictionary awareness: a light familiarity with common word lists helps, but remember that the spirit of the game is fun and fast, not a vocabulary pop quiz. Short, valid words are often the most effective when speed is of the essence.
