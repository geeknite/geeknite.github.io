## Unboxing and assembly

Unboxing is the moment of truth for any trampoline. If your box looks like a treasure chest, you know you will be in for a journey. If it looks like a regular cardboard box that previously contained a microwave, well, you are in for a headache. The model we tested ships with a ladder, enclosure net, frame rails, springs, pads, anchors, and a detailed instruction manual that reads like a choose your own adventure for geometry lovers.

### Assembly walk-through

- Start with the frame: Lay out the frame sections and connect them using the provided hardware. The frame is heavy enough to require two adults to position it, but not so heavy you need a forklift. It is a good sign of durability when you can assemble and move it without needing a crane.
- Attach the legs: The legs are typically eight to twelve per side depending on your model. Ensure each leg is firmly seated and locked before moving on. Wobble is the enemy here; you want statue-like stability, not a shopping cart on two wheels.
- Install the enclosure: The net attaches to the frame with stitched channels and clips. The goal is to have a snug fit that not only keeps kids inside but also prevents the net from becoming a wind sail on a breezy day.
- Attach padding and springs: The safety pads go over the springs to lessen pinch points. Secure them with Velcro or straps as provided. Do not skip this step. It is not optional decor; it is a safety feature.
- Mount the ladder and gate: The ladder attaches to the frame near the enclosure gate. Make sure the gate closes securely and the zipper is fully functional. A mistimed zipper is not a fashion statement; it is a safety risk.
- Final inspection: Check for loose hardware, test the gate, and bounce lightly to confirm the tension on the springs. If you can feel a squeak or a wobble, you probably missed a bolt. Revisit the manual and fix it before you sign off on the assembly.

## Maintenance and weather care

Outdoor gear deserves a bit of seasonal love. A few routines can extend the life of your trampoline and keep it looking sharp year after year.

### Seasonal checks

- At the start of spring and end of fall, inspect the frame for rust and the nets for tears. Replace anything suspicious before the first big bounce.
- Check padding for cracks or sun-bleached edges. If the padding is thin and the springs are exposed, replace the pads to protect feet and legs.
- Tighten any loose screws; vibration from jumping can gradually loosen hardware over time.

### Cleaning and storage tips

- Clean the surface with mild soap and water to remove grime. Avoid harsh chemicals that could degrade the padding or the netting.
- If you live in a region with heavy snow, consider removing the spring pads and net during winter to reduce wind load, or cover it with a heavy-duty trampoline cover. If you leave it up year round, ensure the base is anchored and the net is taut.
- Use anchor kits to hold the trampoline down in windy climates. A gust can turn a trampoline into a runaway accessory, which is not part of the plan for an afternoon of family fun.
