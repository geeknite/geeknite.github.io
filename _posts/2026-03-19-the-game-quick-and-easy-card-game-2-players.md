## How to play in 60 seconds: the core loop
If you can count to five reliably, you’re already halfway there. The official rules are intentionally light, and that is a feature, not a bug. Here’s the gist:

- Setup: shuffle the deck and deal five cards to each player. Place the remaining deck in the center; this is your “stream of fate.” Each player also gets a small score tracker on their board.
- Objective: be the first to reach a target score (the base version uses 25 points as a round target, but you can adjust to 30 or 40 depending on how much time you want to stretch the drama). The moment someone hits or exceeds the target, the round ends, and you crown a winner.
- On your turn: you play one card face-down from your hand. Then both players reveal simultaneously. The card’s effect resolves immediately.
- Card types: numbers are straightforward, but there are action cards that can shift scores, force redraws, or steal a point from your opponent. These are the spice; use them creatively to outsmart your buddy.
- End of round: after resolution, you adjust scores. The first player to reach the target wins the round. Best of three is a good default; best of five makes you consider retirement and retirement bonuses.
- Refill your hand: draw back up to five at the end of your turn unless the rule set says otherwise.

The experience is designed to feel fast but with enough variance to avoid predictability. You’ll often walk into a round with a plan, only to discover your plan has been outflanked by a clever use of a single action card that flips the entire board state.

To help illustrate, here is a quick playthrough sketch:

- Turn 1: You lay down a mid-range number card and an defensive action card plays to lock the score just a bit. Your opponent counters with a high-value number, triggering a blitz of point movement.
- Turn 2: A surprise swap card shifts your score by a small margin, while your opponent tries to pin you down with a “skip” effect, forcing you to reveal your card first.
- Turn 3: Endgame pressure mounts. You choose to push your luck with a riskier card that doubles as a bluff. The opponent calls your bluff, you reveal a low card and maintain parity—just enough to keep the drama alive.

That’s the rhythm: bluff, counter, respond, reset. It’s not one more tweet, but it is one more turn, please, with a smile.

## Variant play and expansions (two-minute quickie)
One of the nicest things about this game is how forgiving it is for variant play. A few simple house rules can take you from classic quick duel to something that feels fresh again without turning your room into a homework assignment:
- Increase the target score to 30 or 40 for longer sessions.
- Introduce a bomb card that erases a portion of the opponent’s score (sparingly, to avoid blowouts).
- Add a secret-keeper rule where one card’s effects are hidden until revealed, creating misdirection and comedic reveals.

The game can accommodate these tweaks without losing its core identity, which is essential for a title that exists in the quick and easy lane.
