## Design and Build

### The blade and steel discussion

The Honshu Fighter uses stainless steel, which is a broad umbrella term that includes many alloys with varying performance characteristics. The upside of stainless steel is corrosion resistance and easier maintenance—unlike high-carbon steels that crave your attention, stainless tends to forgive neglect with a forgiving smile. The downshot, as any blade nerd will confirm, is edge retention can be a little lighter than some premium steels. In practice, you’ll likely be able to sharpen it to a razor edge, but you may need to touch it up a bit more often than you would with a higher-end steel. That’s a trade-off many folks accept for a blade that’s easier to live with.

From a geometry standpoint, this dagger follows a long-blade design that favors thrusting and slicing actions common to dagger tasks. ## Maintenance and Longevity

### Caring for stainless steel blades

Stainless steel is relatively forgiving, but it isn’t a set-it-and-forget-it material. Regular maintenance is a smart idea if you want to keep the edge sharp and the finish intact. A quick wipe-down after use, a light honing session, and occasional oiling of the blade and leather sheath will go a long way toward preventing corrosion, rust spots, and leather dryness.

As with any weapon-grade blade, keep away from extreme heat and harsh chemical cleaners. A mild soap and water rinse followed by thorough drying is usually sufficient for most field-use scenarios. If you happen to be that person who loves to store a blade in a sheath for long periods, consider a light coat of protective oil to minimize moisture interaction during the off-season.

### Sharpening and edge maintenance

For sharpening, you’ll want a routine that respects the blade’s geometry. A subtle curvature and a consistent bevel help maintain the edge without overdoing the steel. A basic sharpening kit with a fine-grit stone or a ceramic honing rod should be adequate for routine upkeep. Remember: practice patience. Dagger blades aren’t kitchen knives; their geometry rewards methodical, careful maintenance rather than quick, aggressive passes.
