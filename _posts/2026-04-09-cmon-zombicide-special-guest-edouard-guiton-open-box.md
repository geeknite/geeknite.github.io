## The Open Box Moment

Cracking the seal on the box is a tactile reminder that boxes are more than containers; they are a ritual. You peel back the outer wrap and reveal a spine of plastic miniatures that squeak softly in the way only new miniatures do—the kind of sound that says, “Yes, you bought this to become a hero, but first you’ll have to become a box-zombie yourself while you find the simulator of the apocalypse.”

The box itself is heavy enough to bench-press a 1/72 scale tank, yet the content prefers to be rearranged into orderly chaos. The insert holds the core set like a mother hen protecting its hatchlings: a tight, protective nest that promises a clean setup with a dash of chaos if you’re unlucky (or very ambitious). The color scheme—muted gray for the zombies, a splash of blood-red for the city-splashes—strikes a balance between “grimdark undertones” and “you’re playing a party game with swords that squeak.”

Inside, you’ll find the core box content: a rulebook that looks like it’s auditioning for a role in a high-stakes thriller, spiffy character cards, load-bearing miniatures, and all the chits and tokens you never realized you needed for survival but now you absolutely do. And yes, there are sauce packets of flavor—little tokens that promise to unlock special events during play, because nothing says “this is a zombie apocalypse” like a shiny token with a symbol that vaguely resembles a sandwich.

Presented with Edouard Guiton on the couch (virtual or real—it’s a video call, okay?), the unboxing becomes more than a mechanical inventory. It becomes a conversation about tension: tension in the design, tension in the balance between chaos and control, and tension in whether you should have a separate box for every expansion. The guest adds context: a designer who has run the numbers on countless encounter decks and has opinions about balance that may not align with your house rules. Expect ideas about how the horde spawns, how much chaos is too much, and whether the survivor knives actually need 3D sprues or if two-dimensional tokens would suffice for the dramatic effect.

## The Special Guest: Edouard Guiton

Edouard Guiton is a name you may recognize from the world of CMON and Zombicide design. He’s not just the guy who signs off on color palettes; he’s the person who asks the hard questions: Can we make the survivors feel distinct without turning each into a tiny hero in a soap opera? How do we make a zombie feel like a credible menace without sinking the game into the abyss of “we rolled 12 zombies this turn and nothing dies”?

In this Open Box session, Edouard is chatty but precise. He talks through the decisions behind the sculpts—the way different zombie types are silhouettes first and characters second, how the survivors’ outfits nod to the theme without becoming a fashion show for the apocalypse. He explains why the board layout favors linear movement in certain scenarios and why the “ mood lighting” on the board is less about photography and more about guiding you gently toward the right path to doom. If you’re into game designer folklore, you’ll appreciate the little anecdotes about early prototypes and the moment a balance number was moved by a single point—“the difference between a feint and a frenzy,” as he might call it.

We’re not asking for personal legends here, but we do get a peek into the process. The intent is to make a game that feels both editorially sharp and casually dangerous. The zombies are there to create tension, not to overwhelm with a mechanical algorithm that makes you cry in the car after your third defeat to the same encounter card. Edouard’s approach seems to be: give the players clear agency, but never let that agency overshadow the narrative of a city under siege.

## Components and Artwork

### Miniatures and Plastic

Zombicide’s miniatures are the lifeblood of the open box. They’re not just pewter-ish sentiments; they’re the faces through which you’ll interpret the story of your table. The sculpts in this edition have been improved to emphasize motion lines—the way a zombie’s limb drags across the ground, the way a tuning fork of a weapon catches the light. The paint job on a lot of these plastics makes them pop without requiring you to break out a magnifying glass and a passport to the local hobby shop.

If you’re one of those players who spends more time washing models than playing the game, you’ll be pleased by the level of detail that pops under proper lighting. If you’re a “no extra work” purist, you’ll still get a satisfying silhouette that communicates threat without needing to study a cheat sheet.

### Boards and Cards

The boards carry the usual Zombicide atmosphere: urban ruins, dangerous intersections, and enough break lines to remind you that every corner hides a potential ambush. The tiles snap together cleanly, with a satisfying “click” that says you’ve built not just a map, but a battlefield with actual stakes. Card art continues the theme: you’ll see character references, mission objectives, and a steady stream of icons that require a quick habit of interpretation rather than a full-on cryptography degree.

### The Rulebook and Scenery

The rulebook in this box is dense but approachable, a hallmark of CMON’s engineering ethic. It is fondly thorough, with examples that help you step through the first few turns without needing a simultaneous degree in cryptography and urban planning. The scenery pieces—like barricades, walls, and debris—are robust enough to survive several rounds of enthusiastic players who aren’t sure if they want to arch the board or break the legs of the zombie horde. The 3D pieces mix with flat tokens for variety, ensuring your table resembles a living diorama rather than a simple board game.

## Gameplay Impressions

### Core Mechanics

Zombicide remains a cooperative co-op where players side with survivors fighting off undead waves. If you’re new to the series, the core mechanic is straightforward: you take turns performing actions such as moving, attacking, and using special items. The twist is the way the undead move and attack, and the way that each survivor has strengths and weaknesses that can either supplement your team or cause delicious chaos in the form of miscommunication.

The open box edition keeps the core mechanics intact but with a polish that makes setup a little less painful than dragging a sleeping dragon out of a cave. The dice are crisp, the stats are clear, and the action deck—if you’re including it in your scenario—adds a dash of unpredictability that keeps everyone on their toes.

### Solo and Cooperative Modes

If you’re into solo experiences, Zombicide has scenarios built around playing as a lone survivor with limited resources. You’ll feel the burden of memory—what you kept, what you used, and what you left behind as you sprint toward the last door to freedom. For groups, the cooperative aspect shines in that moment when someone lands the perfect combination of moves to survive the turn, and the rest of the table erupts in a chorus of “nice move.”

Edouard’s input helps design balance for solo and co-op modes, ensuring there’s enough challenge to keep you honest but not so punishing that you abandon the table after three turns. The guidance here is to encourage collaboration, with a nod to improvisation: sometimes the best plan is to scrap your original plan and improvise on the fly as the horde swarms in.

### Replayability and Scenario Variability

One of the selling points of Zombicide is the ability to mix and match scenarios, encounters, and expansions to create near-infinite combinations. The open box brings enough content to justify many play sessions before you need to purchase the next expansion. The scenario design tends to reward creative thinking—whether you’re decoding the objective cards or choosing routes that maximize the survivability of your crew. The guest’s perspective: he championed the idea that you should feel like you’re orchestrating a tense, cinematic sequence rather than moving tokens around a map in a vacuum. The result is a game that plays differently every time you assemble the board.

## Design Notes and Special Guest Insights

Edouard’s remarks are a highlight of this experience. He talks about the balancing act between “challenge” and “fun,” noting that the goal is to deliver a sense of pressure without erasing the sense of victory when you overcome a tough encounter. There’s a healthy respect for the players’ agency: you want players to feel clever when they solve a scenario, not punished for a single error. He also underscores the importance of the unit design—the way a survivor’s silhouette communicates who they are on the battlefield, and how the zombie’s silhouette telegraphs threat even when you haven’t yet rolled the dice.

We hear about the evolution of features across Zombicide editions. Edouard offers anecdotes about prototypes where certain mechanics were tested and then trimmed for clarity, while others were retained precisely because they created satisfying “aha” moments during play. The takeaway is that CMON aims to craft a coherent system—the interplay between players and the game’s world—where choices feel consequential and meaningful, not arbitrary or random.

The conversation veers into topics like art direction and color psychology. Why certain zombie tropes are kept and others replaced, how the survivors’ outfits minimize confusion on crowded boards, and how to balance a scenario’s narrative through mission text and objective design. The guest’s perspective adds texture to the unboxing experience, reminding us that a game isn’t just a set of rules but a crafted experience designed to be shared around a table—ideally with a mix of laughter, strategy, and occasional groaning at the inevitability of a bad spawn roll.

## Value, Price, and Replayability

We must talk numbers, even if numbers aren’t as fun as the minis. The value proposition for a box like this is anchored in component quality, the richness of the board and card art, and the sheer potential for dozens of play sessions before you start to feel like you’ve seen it all. Zombicide has always thrived on its ability to present new tactical challenges while staying faithful to its core identity. The open box edition with Edouard’s design sensibilities lands squarely in that tradition, offering polish without removing the bite.

Replayability is a function of scenario variety, the degree to which you can mix and match objectives with different survivor setups, and the degree to which you find new synergy between co-op teammates. If you’re the sort who loves long campaigns, think of this as the core game with a ready-made playground for the next dozen sessions—plus any expansions you decide to fold into the mix.

Price-wise, you’ll pay a premium for CMON’s presentation, the miniature quality, and the durable components. If you’re a completionist who loves the Zombicide line, the open box may be a necessary purchase in your collection. If you’re more of a casual player, you’ll want to weigh the cost against how often you expect to gather a group for a big night.

## Pros and Cons

- Pros: top-tier miniatures, robust components, strong theme, accessible core mechanics, good balance between challenge and fun, Edouard’s design perspective adds depth to the unboxing experience, scalable for groups and solo play, modular path to expansions.
- Cons: price can be a barrier for casual players, rulebook density may intimidate newcomers, some players might prefer lighter zombie-narrative games, a few tokens may require a storage solution if you’re a neat freak.

## Final Verdict

If you’re a Zombicide veteran or a curious new player who wants to dip your toes into a well-supported zombie board game ecosystem, this open box edition with Edouard Guiton’s involvement is a compelling entry. It doesn’t reinvent the wheel; it polishes the wheel and adds a few clever spokes that make the ride smoother without losing the raw, chaotic joy of walking into a city that’s gone pre-collapse. The components look good, feel good, and the design conversations deliver a sense of craft that helps you appreciate the game beyond the table talk.

If you’re chasing a campaign-driven experience, you’ll probably be content with the core rules and a handful of scenarios at first (and then you’ll likely look to expansions for more). If you’re chasing a social experience—people around a table, jokes, and impossible calls—this box delivers a satisfying mix of tension and camaraderie. It’s not perfect, but it’s a game with personality, a sense of place, and a conversation starter built into the open box experience.
