## Grading, care, and what NM actually means on a 2020s JPN print
Grading is not a science; it is a ritual. For a card like Gloom SV4a Shiny Treasure ex in NM condition, you want to check four quadrants:
- Centering: ideally close to 55/45 or better on this print. Off-centering can impact how the holo reads under glass and impact your display. 
- Edges and corners: look for crisp corners with very light, if any, whitening or chipping. The Japanese stock sometimes shows delicate corners that will grade down with blunt handling, so treat them with care.
- Surface: the holo surface should be smooth, with minimal scratching and no heavy scuff marks. A micro-scratch here and there is common for foil surfaces but too many will pull it down to near mint minus or lower. 
- Registration and print quality: alignment of the foil layers with the art print can drift in older print runs; a perfectly aligned card feels like a small culinary miracle when you tilt it under the light.

If you own a Gloom SV4a with the NM stamp, you got a card that is presentable, displayable, and valuable to the right buyer. The value proposition for NM is that it sits in a comfortable tier where you can enjoy the card visually in a binder or on a stand, and you do not have to cry about heavy corner wear every time you rotate your display. The real challenge is to keep it that way. A stiff top loader, a properly cushioned binder sleeve, and a light environment can preserve the sheen. Avoid aggressive top loaders with rough interior edges; they can scratch the surface. Another trick is to ensure your binder is not hanging on a dusty hook in sunlight, which can cause UV-related fading in the long run. Yes, UV is real even for foil cards; it is not just a woo-woo claim that your grandmother would wag a finger at.
