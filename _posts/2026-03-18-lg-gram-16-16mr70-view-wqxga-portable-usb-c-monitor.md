## Connectivity and usability

### USB-C and power delivery
One of the biggest selling points here is USB-C versatility. The Gram supports USB-C power delivery so you can charge the laptop via the monitor, or power the monitor from the laptop’s USB-C PD port. Depending on your dongle situation, you might be able to power both devices from a single wall outlet, which is the holy grail of “carry light, work hard” traveler energy. If you’re a multi-monitor aficionado, you’ll appreciate the ability to run the View WXQGA as an extended display without lugging extra bricks or HDMI adapters. In practice, you’ll just plug in the Type-C cable, open Windows display settings, and voila—the second screen appears with minimal fuss.

### Peripherals and wireless options
Beyond USB-C, the Gram includes a standard array of ports for a modern ultrabook: USB-A for older mice and dongles, a microSD slot for quick photo transfers, and a headphone jack for those who still believe in 3.5mm compatibility. The monitor ties in neatly with USB-C, and you’ll rarely fumble for drivers when you pair with Windows 11 or Windows 10-era setups. If you’re a macOS user, you’ll be pleased that USB-C works, though the Windows-centric feel of the keyboard might remind you that you’re not in Apple land.

### Ergonomics and desk setup
The portable monitor’s kickstand is robust enough for gentle tilting in everyday usage. It’s not a full-fledged stand, but for a 16:9-ish panel in the field, it does the job. The Gram and the View WXQGA aren’t going to replace a proper desk with a dedicated dual-monitor setup for most serious work, but they’re fantastic for on-the-go creativity, writing sprints, and the occasional spreadsheet war. If you’re traveling, you’ll appreciate the simplicity of docking in a coffee shop chair and pretending you’ve got a real office chair that moves silently on a suction cup of ambition.

The Gram’s CPU/gPU pairing with a contemporary SSD keeps daily tasks snappy: web browsing feels smooth, code compiles at a reasonable pace, and video streaming stays snappy enough to keep you from becoming a tangle of cords in the corner of a cafe. ### Battery endurance and charging behavior
In the real world, battery life is the journalist’s headline. LG’s Gram lineup typically emphasizes long battery life, and the 16MR70 is no exception. In mixed usage—browsing, docs, a few Zoom calls—the laptop easily crosses a full workday mark with headroom for a late-evening binge-watching session or a heavy commit log scroll. The monitor, being USB-C powered, sips energy rather than guzzling it; you’ll likely not notice it eating into your precious battery after a couple of hours of work. If you’re a power user who frequently pushes the CPU on long renders, you may want to keep the charger in your bag as a reminder that you’re living life on the edge—and that edge may be a layover at an airport.

### Thermal behavior
The Gram stays cool enough for casual productivity, and while you’ll feel a warmth under heavy sustained loads, you won’t need a ceiling fan to survive. The monitor remains passive, with no extra heat or fan noise; it’s the kind of gadget that whispers, not shouts, when under load. This makes the entire setup a surprisingly quiet companion for those who work in libraries, plan to finish a novel-length draft on a flight, or simply enjoy peaceful scenery without the drone of a GPU fan.
