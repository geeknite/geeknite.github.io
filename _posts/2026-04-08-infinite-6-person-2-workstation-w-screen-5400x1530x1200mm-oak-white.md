## Setup, Assembly, and Real-World Use

Assembling a 5.4-meter system is not a weekend DIY project unless your weekend plans include a lot of Allen wrenches and a meditation on cable routing. The modular design helps—you’re not unboxing a single monolith; you’re installing several components that click into a coordinated whole. Expect a few hours for one person with decent assembly skills. If you have a team, you can split tasks: one person channels the legs, one handles the long desk panels, and another negotiates with IT about the central screen power supply. The bigger you are, the more the social dynamic matters—assembly becomes a team-building exercise in the best possible sense: you learn how to communicate under pressure, who’s good with instructions, and who’s excellent at turning flat-pack instructions into interpretive art.

Cable management, as mentioned, is not glamorous, but the payoff is real. If you’ve ever half-kneeled under a desk to unplug a broken HDMI adapter during a meeting, you know the value of proper routing. The Infinite system offers hidden channels and a modest degree of cable containment. In a room with six people, you’ll still end up with a few stray cables—this is the price of human-geometry. The trick is to pre-allocate your power sources and use a few multi-outlet hubs so you don’t end up with a jungle of cords marching toward the center like party guests at a tech expo.

For practical usage, this desk shines during collaborative sessions. The central screen and the shared surface encourage a tilt away from “I have a private laptop, I’ll just work in silence” to “we’ll brainstorm and then assign tasks in real-time.” It’s nearly impossible to plan a sprint without someone drawing on the screen, someone else tagging tasks, and the rest of the group nodding in sleepy agreement when a consensus forms. It’s not perfect; there will be moments of “whose turn is it to speak?” and “does the central screen need a moment to boot up?” but those moments are part of the human workflow and not a knock against the furniture.

If you’re seeking a purely solitary workstation, this is not your vibe. If you’re building a six-person collaboration nucleus, this has potential to become the hub of a small (or mid-sized) team. The key to success is to embrace the configuration as a living space: shift seating for different sprints, rewire the central screen for different priorities, and treat the desk as a stage for your team’s rituals rather than a single fixed stage for a single actor.
