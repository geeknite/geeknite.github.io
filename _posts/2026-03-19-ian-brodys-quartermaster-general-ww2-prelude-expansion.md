## Overview

Quartermaster General WW2 Prelude Expansion is designed to slot into the base game and push the narrative gears toward a pre-war and early-war cadence. It’s not a standalone product; it expects you already know which side of the table you’re on and how the supply chain works when the Trains of Destiny refuse to run on time. The Prelude expansion does a few key things well:

- It introduces a thematic precursor arc that teases what comes next in a full WW2 campaign.
- It rebalances pacing, offering shorter turns and more bite-sized challenges that are perfect for a couple of hours of intense, dingy-candlelight gaming.
- It expands the leader and event card ecosystems with fresh abilities and fresh hiccups, making alliances feel precarious and power shifts feel inevitable.

If you’ve played the original, you’ll notice the tonal shift. The Prelude expansion leans into a more intimate, smaller-scale conflict style—think guerrilla logistics rather than grand-scale blitzkrieg. It’s still the same underlying engine—cards, tokens, and a map that looks innocent until you realize you’ve accidentally triggered three different cascading effects on turn three—but with a spicy, pre-war flavor. And yes, there’s still the joy of dragging your logistic chain across the board while your opponent sulks because their strategy relied on a never-ending supply of “just-in-time” reinforcements.

## How It Plays: From Prelude to Punchline

Prelude isn’t a separate game; it’s a module. You mix it with the base Quartermaster General WW2 and let the gauntlet of pre-war decisions shape the rest of the campaign. Here’s what changes in practice:

- Pacing gets a kick in the shins. Turns are generally shorter and decisions feel heavier because each choice has a pre-war ripple that can bloom into a major crisis later on. It’s your brain’s way of saying, “Remember, plans rarely survive first contact with reality.”
- The map’s geometry nudges you toward different strategies. The overlays highlight supply routes and chokepoints that feel more “operational” than “glorious.” You’ll find yourself plotting a procurement corridor with the same fervor you once reserved for your best fantasy raid night.
- Event cards become both a blessing and a curse. Some events reward clever pre-war thinking, others punish you for relying on one perfect setup. It’s a reminder that war, even in a board game, is more about adaptability than brute force.
- Leaders bring thematic quirks. Some churn the supply engine in your favor, others derail it in the most entertaining way. The characterful traits invite you to role-play a tiny, stubborn general who refuses to let a single convoy miss its schedule.
- Scenario-based replayability is real. The Prelude provides a handful of micro-scenarios that you can swap in and out, allowing a “campaign-lite” feel. This is a godsend for groups that want a narrative arc without committing to a months-long quest through world history.

From a mechanical standpoint, Prelude preserves the core of Quartermaster General—card-driven actions, supply management, and a tug-of-war between offensive and defensive objectives—while rearranging the emphasis. The early-stage friction translates into a more intense midgame and a late-game crescendo that lands with the satisfying thud of a well-timed logistics coup. If you’ve played the base game and loved the tension of “can I actually pull this off with the resources I’ve got?” Prelude leans into that tension and amplifies it with a narrative spine.

## Theme, Art, and Flavor

Ian Brody has a knack for making history feel tactile without leaning into cliché. Prelude continues that tradition. The design language remains accessible—icons are legible, the color palette is cheerful enough to persuade you that you’re having a good time, and the art direction softens the harsh edges of war by focusing on the human-scale challenges of supply and timing.

If you’re a stickler for historical fidelity, Prelude is not a documentary. It’s a stylized interpretation that respects the gist of WWII logistics while leaning into gameplay-driven storytelling. You’ll feel like a strategist who’s memorized a few historical case studies and is now testing those ideas in a living-room war room. It’s the kind of experience where you nod at your own cleverness after a well-played convoy move and then immediately curse your opponent’s clever counter-move when they disrupt your transport route with a stray card draw.

Artwork remains consistent with the broader Quartermaster General line: bold, readable, and a little cartoonish in a way that keeps the mood light even when the board looks like a battle plan written in crayon. The Prelude expansion respects the tone of the base game while letting its hair down a bit—something we geeks appreciate when we’ve spent a long afternoon pretending to be logistics gods.

## Replayability and Depth

One of the strongest arguments for Prelude is how it compounds the base game’s tension without demanding a full commitment to a multi-episode campaign. You can bolt Prelude onto a single session, ticking up the intensity for a couple of hours, or you can weave it into a longer campaign arc if your group is hungry for more strategic puzzles.

- Short-form sessions shine as Prelude tightens the clock and trims the fat. You’ll be grateful for the quick-start scenarios when you just want to laugh, cry, and argue about who’s actually in charge of the artillery.
- Long-form campaigns gain new flavor. The early-war tilt introduces new decision points—some paralyze, some empower—creating memorable endgames where the final turn hinges on a single failed convoy roll or a masterstroke that redefines your entire supply network.
- Variability remains high. The mixture of new event cards and leader traits ensures that no two sessions feel identical. Even if you replay the same scenario, the table will be surprised by the card-draws and the luck of the dice, much to your opponent’s chagrin and your inner monologue about risk management.

If you’re the sort who loves seeing a game evolve through its phases, Prelude delivers. It’s not about turning Quartermaster General into a different game; it’s about adding a different lens—one that invites you to think about pre-war decisions, the fragility of supply lines, and the way small choices snowball into big strategic events.

- Fresh leader traits and event cards inject personality without breaking balance.

Bottom line: if you love the base game and want something that scratches a different itch without reinventing the wheel, Prelude is a solid, flavorful, and genuinely fun expansion.
