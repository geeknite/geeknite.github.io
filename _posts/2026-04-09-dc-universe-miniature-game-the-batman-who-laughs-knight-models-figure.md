Assembling and converting: flexibility or fragility?

The Batman Who Laughs does not present as a multi-piece chaos monster. It is a relatively clean kit with a handful of parts: torso, cape, head, several hands, and probably one or two optional accessories if you opted for alternate posing. Building this miniature should be straightforward for an intermediate hobbyist. The joints are not overly fragile, which is a blessing when you’re tempted to pose dramatically in ways that create all sorts of legal disclaimers about balance and gravity. The downside of any dynamic pose is potential balance issues on the display base; just a dab of green stuff or a pin in the base can help stabilise a dramatic stance if you plan to tilt the model toward the viewer. If you like to convert your minis for faith-based peace talks with your local park, this model will accept some mild alterations without mutiny in the resin.

Painting guide: palette and technique for the chaos grin

Base coating and priming

Prime the figure with a light gray or white primer to ensure depth, then apply a mid-tone base color for the suit—navy or charcoal works nicely, as does a deep midnight blue for extra drama. The Batman Who Laughs wears a suit that can be characterized as ‘corporate villain with extra teeth.’ The base color should reflect that duality: the suit is dark, but with reflective highlights that hint at the character’s malevolent sparkle.

Color choices

- Suit: deep blue-black or charcoal; blend to near-black for recesses to emphasize the silhouette.
- Cloak: a tinted, shadowy purple or a cold green to evoke the horror vibe. You can push toward emerald or teal if you want a more supernatural look.
- Skin and exposed accents: greenish pallor for the exposed skin or the character’s signature ghastly complexion; it can be achieved with a green wash over pale skin tones.
- Teeth and eyes: stark white or bone for teeth; eyes can be small, piercing, and bright with a thin wash to create glare.
- Details: metallics for buckles, belts, and lockpicks or little gadgetry. You want small letters or runes to read with a contrast wash.

Layering and shading

A rule of thumb: the more you lean into contrast, the more the figure will pop on the table. For the Batman Who Laughs, you can push shading in the recesses to bring out the creases and folds in the cloak. Use a moderate wash to settle the armor panels and allow the sculpted details to breathe. A dark blue glaze over the suit can help tie the figure to the cape while a green wash across the exposed skin makes the face read as ominous rather than ‘just dirty.’ For the belt and any metallic bits, a cool silver with a touch of brass highlights yields a sense of worn, once-glossy gear.

Edge highlighting and final touches

Edge highlight with a thin bright blue or cool gray along the armor edges will separate form from shadow, but don’t overdo it. The Batman Who Laughs is all about atmosphere, not chrome. A very light dry brush on the cape edges can mimic wear and tear and give the model a lived-in, villainous aura. If you like a little more gleam, you could apply a pin wash to the teeth or use a glaze to push the enamel color toward a sickly green to signal corruption without looking cartoonish.

Jekyll-and-Hyde base work: bases that tell a story

The base is not an afterthought here. You can keep it minimal with a textured black or gray, but you’ll want to add a little context. A broken glass texture underfoot, a few shattered coins, or a hint of a city backdrop in silhouette can tell a story without overwhelming the figure. If you’re into diorama-building, a small urban ruin or a cracked pavement motif would suit the Batman Who Laughs’s mood perfectly. A few tufts of static grass or chemical spill effects add texture without stealing the spotlight from the model.

Painting on a budget: tips to get the most out of a single centerpiece

If you’re working with a limited budget, treat The Batman Who Laughs as your focal point and build a small, coordinated force around it. Use a simple three-color scheme for the main figure and invest time in the base to maximize impact. One or two strategic washes, a dry brush, and a couple of edge highlights can elevate the entire piece without turning it into a weekend-long project. If you can afford a second model, try to choose a companion piece that echoes the color palette or the theme; symmetry in color schemes helps the display read as an intentional diorama rather than a random cluster of figures.

Photography and display: how to show off your work

Lighting is key. Place the figure under directional light from the side to emphasize the cloak’s folds and the face’s menacing grin. A tiny LED under the base can mimic street lighting for a Gotham vibe. If you want to keep things simple, natural daylight through a north-facing window and a neutral background still produces striking results.
