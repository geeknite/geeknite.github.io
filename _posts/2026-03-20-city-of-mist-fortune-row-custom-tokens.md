## What is Fortune Row?
Fortune Row is a tangible toolkit designed to augment the way players and GMs track the ebb and flow of the game. In City of Mist, the world is built from legends that wear human faces; Fortune Row gives you a row of tokens that act as a physical memory of those legends in motion. The tokens operate on a simple premise: you spend, gain, and transfer momentum in ways that echo the Masks and the Mythos you’re playing with. The kit typically includes a set of differently colored tokens, each color or symbol representing a function—fortune, risk, clue, intervention, and revelation—plus a few special tokens that invite micro-narrative twists. The tokens are meant to be used with the core mechanics of City of Mist, but they also invite flexible interpretation.

The design goal is that tokens should be readable at a glance, allowing players to read the room without tapping a calculator app every time someone leans in to whisper a secret. It’s a small thing, but in a game that’s all about big myths wearing trench coats, the small things matter.

## Mechanics: How it actually works at the table
Let’s get practical. Fortune Row introduces five core token types that map well to the City of Mist flow:
- Fortune tokens: currency for narrative leverage. Spend to unlock a scene beat or to coax a reveal from a tense moment.
- Row tokens: markers for story segments—things like acts or chapters in a plotline. They help players see where the story is headed in an at-a-glance way.
- Clue tokens: pieces of information that become more valuable when combined with a character’s Mask’s abilities.
- Risk tokens: when you push the clock or escalate danger, risk tokens spike. They make it feel like you’re leaning into a cliff-edge moment.
- Revelation tokens: a limited-use tool to reveal a hidden truth about the city, its legends, or a character’s backstory.

Each token has a suggested action on it, a little flavor text, and an icon that makes it legible at a quick glance. The rule-set encourages players to think about momentum as a resource that matters to character arcs rather than only to the party’s survival odds. The GM can award tokens to players as civilization-level rewards for clever narration or successful risk-taking, and players can use tokens to push for deeper disclosures or faster pacing. The net effect is a table with more storytelling latitude—and less “let’s roll again” fatigue.

The design also nudges players toward narrative payoffs that feel earned. A token spent to reveal a truth is more interesting if the truth has consequences that ripple through the rest of the session. It’s not about “getting the truth” as a grant of information; it’s about how that truth reshapes the next scene’s choices and power dynamics. If you’re skeptical about “story money,” Fortune Row’s design aims to convert the abstract tug-of-war into a tactile, emotionally legible mechanism.

## Custom tokens and house rules
One of Fortune Row’s biggest strengths is its flexibility. You don’t need to commit to every token in every campaign. Instead, pick a minimal set that serves your group’s needs, then add tokens for specific story arcs. Here are a few practical house rules to help you start:
- Limit the number of tokens per player per session. This reduces bookkeeping and keeps the focus on the story.
- Tie token use to a mask’s core trait. A token should feel like an echo of the character’s mythos rather than a generic mechanic.
- Use a simple ledger: one page per session with token types and who earned/spent them. This helps newcomers catch up quickly.

## Box contents, setup, and deployment in your campaign
A practical note: Fortune Row’s value shows when you set it up once and forget about it—until you need it. The box is fairly light on extras but strong on plan. This means you’ll want a token tray that rests near the GM’s screen and a couple of small bowls on the players’ side to keep tokens accessible. The real trick is to assign a dedicated time in the session to token spend: a brief moment when a token is consumed and its consequence is narrated. It’s a ritual that pays off by giving players something to look forward to rather than a moment to dread.

If you’re streaming or recording your game, a token-based cue can also become a visual hook for viewers. It’s not essential, but it helps create a sense of momentum that translates well to video.

## Quick-start tips for one-shots and longer campaigns
- For a one-shot, start with three Fortune tokens total, and a single row token to mark the finale. Let players earn additional tokens through clever narration rather than through random chance.
- For a longer campaign, gradually scale token availability: more tokens in the early chapters, a controlled taper as the party closes in on the city’s big reveal.
- Keep a “token refresh” moment every few sessions where you re-pack the token set for a new arc. It’s a satisfying moment for players to feel a fresh start without losing momentum from previous arcs.

## Accessibility and community feedback
Fortune Row is not a perfect fit for every table, and that’s okay. The design team has done a decent job of providing icons and color cues that read in ambient light, but some players prefer digital substitutes or alternate tactile tokens. The community has responded with a variety of adaptations, including color-coded text on the tokens themselves and printable, high-contrast variants. If you run a table that includes color-blind players, you may want to supplement the kit with Braille stickers or embossed icons so that every player can participate equally.
