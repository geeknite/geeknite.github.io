## How to play in a nutshell
If you have played Sushi Go before, you know the rhythm. If you haven’t, here is a compact refresher you can skim while you wait for your flight to board:

### Setup and pacing
- The box supports 2 to 5 players (and in some variants up to 6 with house rules). In travel mode, the rounds go fast, and the setup is basically open the box, deal the cards, and let the first player pick. You will usually see the entire game play out in under 20 minutes, even with a crowd of folks who keep saying we should be studying for a test we never studied for.
- Each round the active player will select one card from their hand and pass the rest to the next player. You will do this until all cards are claimed. The simple loop is what keeps people coming back for more, even when the table has a few distracted travelers who keep talking about snacks and weather.

### Scoring palette
- The travel edition uses the standard scoring rules: Nigiri cards earn points based on their type; Sashimi cards require sets; Tempura and Dumplings have straightforward scoring; Wasabi and Pudding add their own waves of strategic tension. The key is to identify one or two scoring engines and chase them without becoming a card hoarder who cannot decide if they want to eat first or second.
- At the end of each round, you tally points on your score pad. In the travel variant, you will still count and total, and the quick nature of the rounds means you will have a mental map of what you are chasing as the end approaches. The challenge is balancing early picks with mid-round gambits when you suspect the table is chasing a different combo entirely.

### Strategy basics
- In a two-to-four player game, a common approach is to lock in an early assessment of whether you want to chase a Wasabi-Nigiri combo or a Sashimi-Dumpling spread. With travel cards, you often feel the pressure to pivot quickly as opponents reveal their intentions by their own first choices. It is chess with chopsticks, and your opponents are literal chips of cheese in the metaphorical sushi bar.
- In practice, the best strategy is to identify a reliable engine: pick a card that blocks your neighbor from getting a high-scoring card while still advancing your own set. The travel edition makes this easier because you can focus on core combos without the distraction of additional expansion cards or board modifiers.

## Strategy tips for the travel edition
- Pick a core engine early: decide if you want to chase a simple, steady score (for example, a hearty sum from Nigiri) or a flashy, multi-card engine (Sashimi plus Dumplings or Tempura with Wasabi). Your ability to pivot mid-round is the heart of the game, and the travel edition rewards flexible thinking.
- Watch your neighbors and read the table: even though this is a compact package, the social element is still key. If you sense someone is chasing a particular combo, you can take a card to disrupt their plan while keeping your own momentum going.
- Don’t overcommit early: you will pass the cards around quickly, and early picks can backfire if the next players jump onto a stronger phase. The travel edition benefits from a light touch in the early rounds and a sharper focus as the end of the game nears.
- Practice the math: count cards in your head to estimate what remains in the deck and what your opponents are likely to scoop. A few quick mental arithmetic attempts can swing the game in your favor when you know you are a draw away from a critical set.
