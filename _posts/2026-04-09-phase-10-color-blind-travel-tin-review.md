## How to Play with Accessibility in Mind
Phase 10 isn’t a game you can explain in five seconds and be done with it; it’s a festival of small steps and bigger mistakes, all wrapped in a neon-colored bow. Here’s a streamlined overview that emphasizes accessibility without losing the heart of the game:

### The basic loop
- Each player starts by drawing a hand of ten cards from the shuffled deck. The goal? To complete ten phases across rounds. Each phase has a unique requirement, and you advance only when you’ve completed the current phase and declared, with dramatic confidence, that you are moving on.
- On your turn you draw one card, then discard one card to your discard pile. The discards form a living museum of your strategy, which all your friends can admire as your plans fall apart in glorious fashion.
- You need to finish the specific combination for the current phase. The color-blind icons on each card guide you to assemble the required sets or runs without needing to memorize color names or rely on a color-blind friend’s quick intuition alone. This reduces the “Which color is that again?” moments that often happen in a room with multiple players wearing sunglasses indoors for dramatic effect.
- When you complete the phase, you lay down the cards to show you’ve beaten the level and draw a new hand to attempt phase 2. The pace is brisk; the tension is real; the fashion of the table is immaculate because you rehearsed a victory pose in the car ride over.

### The phases, quick and friendly
The specific requirements for each phase in classic Phase 10 involve a mix of runs and sets. In this color-blind accessible version, you’ll still encounter the same general structure—sequences, groups, and a mix of timing and luck—but with the added guidance of glyphs and large numbers. The idea is that even if someone misreads a color, they can still confidently identify the card by its icon and position on the table.

### Tips for inclusive play
- Establish a quick-reference card or a shared cheat sheet for what each glyph means. This helps new players catch up without slowing down the table.
- If someone is color-blind or has reduced vision, consider enabling players to talk through their card choices more openly. This creates a social dynamic where strategy conversations happen aloud, which is often more fun than silent glances across the table.
- Rotate the dealer role. The person who deals becomes the “emcee” for that round, narrating the phase progress with escalating enthusiasm. It’s a small theatrical trick that keeps energy high and reduces any awkward silence after a tough phase.

### Example scenario
Imagine you’re in a lively game night and your phase requires two sets of four and a run of five. With the glyph system, you can quickly locate your four-card sets by the icon, then arrange the run by sequence—no one has to squint at colors. If someone accidentally grabs the wrong card, a friendly swerve of the hand and a quick wink from the dealer can salvage the moment, turning a potential faux pas into a shared laugh. The point is not perfection; it’s shared fun without sensory frustration getting in the way.
