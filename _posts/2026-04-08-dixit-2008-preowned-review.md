## What Dixit actually is
Dixit is not a game about who can count to ten or who can recall the most obscure rule. It’s a game about storytelling through imagery. Each round, one player is the storyteller. They pick a clue—a sentence, a phrase, a mood—without saying it aloud, and then secretly choose one card from their hand that best matches that clue. The other players pick a card from their own hands that they think best matches the clue, and all the chosen cards are shuffled and revealed. Everyone votes on which card they think was the storyteller’s. Points are awarded for guessing the storyteller’s card (if you voted for it) and for how many players selected the storyteller’s card. It’s a light, accessible, and often hilarious exercise in the art of reading one another’s minds—or at least guessing at what someone’s kooky brain is into this week.

The magic of Dixit lies in the balance between cleverness and ambiguity. If the clue is too obvious, everyone will vote for the storyteller’s card, and nobody gets the special reward. If the clue is too obscure, no one will vote for it, and the storyteller sinks into the seat, wondering if that metaphor about “the moon’s sherbet” was too niche for the room. The sweet spot is a clue that’s just specific enough to feel unique, but broad enough that a few players will be surprised and delighted when the correct card reappears.

## Preowned realities: wear, tear, and value proposition
Buying Dixit 2008 preowned is a calculated gamble, but one that pays off with the right booth of enthusiasm. Here’s what to look for, and why it still matters.

- Card integrity: The most important thing is comfortable handling and legibility. If the corners are nicked but the cards lie flat and the artwork is unmarred, you’re likely in for a treat. If there are sticky residues or visible warping, factor in a potential card cleaning or replacement plan (which, depending on your local store, might be a hassle or a cheap alternative).
- Box condition: A box that closes securely is a good sign. Cracks are manageable if the inner tray keeps the deck and tokens in place. A peeled lid sticker? A minor aesthetic issue. Don’t let a flaking box keep you from enjoying the artful world inside.
- Rulebook: If the pages are intact and legible, you’re fine. A few folds or highlights aren’t a deal-breaker; Dixit rules aren’t dense, but a clean reference is helpful when you’re hosting a game night and someone forgets what “Storyteller” and “guess” mean in practice.
- Tokens and dice: Dixit uses tokens to mark guesses and scores. If these are present and intact, you’re in good shape. Broken tokens can be irritating, but replacements are often easy to source or you can improvise with small coins as a stand-in for a single session.
- Overall vibe: The deciding factor with a preowned Dixit copy is whether the cards evoke delight on sight and whether you feel you’re stepping back into a warm, humorous memory of a night where phrases like “the whispering ship of pillows” sparked an entire inside joke chain. If yes, you’ve got a keeper.

The bottom line for preowned Dixit 2008 is simple: be honest about the wear you can live with, test the feel of the cards in your hand, and assess whether the art still sings to your group. If you’re buying this as a gift for someone who adores surreal imagery or for a party crew that thrives on imaginative storytelling, it’s usually worth the purchase—especially when the price is right and the condition is clearly scrutineered.

## How to play (step-by-step and rounds explained)
Dixit’ gameplay is straightforward enough for most players to grasp within a single round, but the social dynamics can take a few sessions to unlock fully. Here’s a practical walkthrough you can use with your group to minimize confusion and maximize fun.

1) Setup: Shuffle the deck and deal a hand of six cards to each player. Create the scoring tokens (or chips) for everyone. Pick a starting storyteller for the round.
2) Storyteller’s clue: The storyteller looks at their six cards and selects one that best captures the clue they want to convey. They say nothing out loud about the card. The clue must be text or spoken words? No, Dixit is about the spoken vibe. The storyteller can describe the card through a phrase, a sentence, a mood, or even a single word—whatever they feel will guide others toward or away from their chosen card.
3) Card selection by others: Each non-storyteller chooses one card from their hand that they think best matches the clue. They place the chosen card face down if you’re playing with the standard rules, or reveal together if you’re aiming for a quicker pace.
4) Reveal and vote: The chosen cards (including the storyteller’s) are revealed. Everyone (except the storyteller) secretly votes for the card they think is the storyteller’s. Players use vote tokens to point; or, in newer setups, they may simply indicate via card numbers.
5) Scoring:
- If at least one person and the storyteller voted for the storyteller’s card, the storyteller earns a base score of 3 points, plus 1 extra point for each player who guessed the storyteller’s card (but not more than the total number of players minus one).
- Any player who guessed the storyteller’s card earns 3 points.
- If no one guessed the storyteller’s card, the storyteller earns 0, and no one else gains points for that round.
6) Refill and rotate: Each player draws back up to six cards so you’re ready for the next round. The storytelling responsibility rotates to the next player clockwise.
7) Repeat: Play continues for a predetermined number of rounds or until you exhaust the deck. The player with the most points at the end wins.

The key to success is embracing the ambiguity. If you’re too literal with your clues, you’ll earn points on a round or two before it becomes a predictable slog. If you’re too obtuse, you risk a silent round where no one can guess your card, which is funny for about 60 seconds and then becomes a problem for your friend group’s tea supply.

Round example:
- Storyteller picks a card depicting a fox wearing a crown and a cape (one of the later Dixit cards). The clue they whisper is: “Ruler of rumors.”
- Other players pick cards they believe match that vibe: a card with a fox in a throne, a moonlit cityscape, a magician with cards flying, and so on.
- All cards are revealed and players vote on which card is the storyteller’s. The fox-on-throne card earns a few votes, maybe some votes for a magical crown portrait; the storyteller’s own card is chosen by exactly two players, including the storyteller if they end up telling a clever line.
- Everyone tallies points and takes new cards. Laughter erupts when someone’s extremely literal card is mistakenly chosen as the storyteller’s, proving that not everyone shares your love for “puns with woodland animals.”

This is the social fabric of Dixit: it’s as much about reading friends as it is about the artwork. The preowned nature of a copy shouldn’t affect your ability to weave stories; if anything, the shared history with the game adds a wrinkle of nostalgia that some players cherish.

## Dixit vs. the field: comparisons with other surreal party games
If you’re exploring Dixit, you’ve likely looked at its peers: Mysterium, when you want a similar vibe but with a cooperative, scenario-based twist; Codenames: Pictures, which scales up the guessing with pictorial prompts; and a handful of indie games that lean into dreamlike art and storytelling.

- Dixit vs Mysterium: Dixit centers on storytelling with one storyteller per round and a competitive scoring system. Mysterium leans cooperative with a shared detective narrative and players taking on psychically guided roles; the tension comes from solving a mystery rather than scoring. If you love art-driven storytelling but prefer a less competitive edge, Mysterium can be a great alternative.
- Dixit vs Codenames: Pictures: Codenames is a team-based word game. The art helps a lot in Dixit, but the mechanism is vocabulary-based rather than image-based. If your group adores wordplay, Codenames is a natural fit; for those who crave dreamscapes and surreal prompts, Dixit wins the mood battle.

In a preowned Dixit, you’re not getting a modern expansion with extra cards. You’re getting the core experience—clean art, clever prompts, and a template for storytelling that remains relevant regardless of the number of expansions released since 2008. The artwork still shines and continues to provoke conversation, which is exactly what you want from a party game that doubles as a conversation starter.

## Preowned buying tips and what to check before you seal the deal
- Inspect the cards’ edges: If there’s heavy chipping or bent corners, the cards may be difficult to shuffle and handle. Light edge wear is normal with age, but avoid anything that makes dealing awkward.
- Confirm all components exist: You should see the full deck of picture cards, the scoring tokens, reference sheet, and the rulebook. Missing elements can complicate a resale license, so to speak.
- Smell test: A musty or moldy odor is a red flag. The art may be enduring, but the packaging should not carry dampness or mildew signs.
- Look for water damage: Warping and staining on cards can be a deal-breaker; it’s not a game-killer, but it can impact readability.
- Card cleanliness: If you find sticky residues or grime, you can clean and sanitize gently. A quick wipe with a damp cloth (and maybe a light card-friendly cleaner) can restore readability, but do not soak the deck.
- Box integrity: A sturdy box matters more if you plan to transport the game or resell again. A broken lid is annoying, but not a disaster if the inner tray and cards remain protected.
- Price-to-condition: A well-kept preowned copy at a fair price is a steal; a bargain that’s too good to be true might be hiding a compromised deck. Compare prices with new and other used listings to gauge a realistic value.
- Language and edition: Make sure the rulebook language matches your group’s preferred language, and confirm that the edition aligns with the card art you expect; there are minor variations across print runs.
- Seller reputation: If you’re buying online, check seller reviews and ask questions about condition and any wear you should know about. A responsive seller is a sanity signal in the used-games market.

If you’re able to verify these points, you’re likely to end up with a preowned Dixit that feels like a gateway to countless nights of storytelling, without the guilt of losing your favorite pencil or the anxiety of a recent new-release price tag.
