## What is Unhappy Homes and how it changes the game

Unhappy Homes is an expansion that adds a new lens to Gloom: it focuses on the actual house and the neighborhood rather than just the family’s fate. The core mechanic stays recognizable: you want your family to end up as unhappy as possible, ideally more than your rivals, and you do that by playing cards that either increase your own gloom or meddle with others. The expansion introduces several new card types and a few new rule tweaks that change pacing without breaking the rhythm that makes Gloom so incredible.

Two big ideas drive Unhappy Homes:

- A neighborhood focus shifts the sense of scale. Instead of a single family suffering, you can now affect what happens around them, which creates more meaningful decisions about timing and target selection.
- Furniture, rooms, and house-related events provide micro-scenarios that feel thematic and funny. You get to blame a haunted dresser for a neighbor’s misfortune, and somehow that makes sense in a shared universe where cardboard is your worst nightmare.

In practice, this expansion asks you to think about not just what your family endures but where they endure it. Rooms become battlegrounds of sentiment and misfortune, and the house itself develops a kind of personality that you can try to bend to your will or, more often, lovingly sabotage with a flourish of sarcasm.

## What's in the box: components and new card types

Unhappy Homes adds a handful of cards that slot neatly into the base game’s architecture. Here is a quick tour you can use to orient yourself before the first chaotic shuffle:

- Neighborhood cards: These are new event-like cards that affect multiple players or influence the state of the entire round. They may represent a neighbor’s petty feud, a zoning permit, or a runaway bull in the cul-de-sac. They add an element of collective risk to the round that no one can ignore.
- House cards: These are the central new item types in this expansion. They represent rooms and features within a house that can either help or hinder when flipped or revealed. Expect things like creaky stairs, a haunted closet, a wobbly railing, and a basement that won’t stay closed.
- Furniture and décor cards: These cards are both flavor and function. They come with silly or grim text effects and can trigger at the wrong moment, turning a boring room into a dramatic stage for misfortune.
- Event cards: Short, sharp events that force you to make quick choices, sometimes benefiting your opponents, sometimes forcing you to save yourself at the cost of your own happiness.
- Gloom counters: A few new ways to track misery that interact with existing gloom counters in novel ways, often accelerating the endgame just when you thought you had it in the bag.

The art direction remains in the same slightly macabre-but-charming style that fans know and love. The house cards feature floor plans with jaunty angles, the furniture cards have a caricature vibe that lands the jokes, and the ambiance remains cohesive with the base game’s tone. If you have a soft spot for retro wallpaper and creaky floorboards, you will find the visual language of Unhappy Homes irresistibly funny.

For those who care about the tech specs, the expansion adds a few new archetypes but does not require any complicated change to the base rules. It is designed to slot in cleanly, so you can bring it to the table mid-campaign without needing a PhD in Gloom mechanics. The learning curve is mild, the giggles are frequent, and the misfortune is still deliciously theatrical.

## How to set up and start playing with Unhappy Homes

Starting a game with Unhappy Homes follows the familiar pattern: players choose their family characters, assign gloom counters, and begin to weave a tapestry of misfortune through cards. The expansion introduces a few setup nuances that are easy to remember once you’ve seen them once:

- Shuffle the new deck of House cards separately, then combine with the base deck according to the included guide. If you prefer a lighter session, you can incorporate a subset of House cards to keep rounds snappy.
- Place a Neighborhood card face up at the start of the round. This card’s effect is active for the duration of the turn or until the Neighborhood effect is resolved.
- When you draw a House card, you now have the option to place it in your own house or to apply it to a neighboring player’s home. This choice creates early collision and sets the tone for the round.
- Endgame timing can be influenced by a special countdown mechanic tied to the number of Neighborhood events that have occurred. This is a neat way to ensure rounds don’t stall and that the ending comes with a chorus of groans and delighted laughter.

A sample round outline with Unhappy Homes feels like a familiar loop but with more pressure, more laughs, and more opportunities to screw with the opponents at just the right moment. The round progresses through a sequence of card plays, gloom checks, and moments where the table erupts in laughter or groans depending on how cruel the house cards have decided to be in that moment.

If you want a precise setup checklist that matches your current player count, consult the box instructions or our quick reference card that we link at the end of this post. Either way, you’ll be in the gloom business within a handful of minutes, which is exactly how the fun starts.

## Strategy and tips: playing with Unhappy Homes

As with base Gloom, the core of success lies in balancing your own misery with your ability to influence others. Unhappy Homes shifts some of the emphasis toward board state and neighborhood management, so here are practical tips to help you maximize laughs and minimize agony for your own family while still achieving victory through glamorously unpleasant means:

- Be mindful of timing. Neighborhood events tend to escalate, so it is often worth delaying the use of a harsh House card until you can maximize its impact on multiple players rather than one isolated target. The trick is to plan two moves ahead and hope your neighbors don’t retaliate with a perfectly timed counterplay.
- Control the narrative. The new House cards give you opportunities to craft micro-stories about your family’s fate. Use this to your advantage by narrating your actions in a way that makes your misfortune feel inevitable and stylish rather than chaotic and random.
- Target the right rooms. Certain House cards work best when placed in or tied to rooms that others are likely to use soon. If you can coerce a neighbor into entering a creaky room mid-round, you create a genuine moment of tense hilarity.
- Leverage neighborhood momentum. The Neighborhood cards aren’t just texture; they create a dynamic environment. If you can string together a couple of neighborhood events in quick succession, you can push a round toward a late, dramatic finish where someone ends with the most miserable estate imaginable.
- Remember the endgame. In Unhappy Homes, closing the game with a well-timed neighborhood surge can be as satisfying as landing a perfect score on a gloom check. Don’t rush to end the round too early; the satisfaction of a well-timed misfortune often eclipses a prematurely ended game.

Play style matters here. If you and your group enjoy spicy banter and theater, this expansion rewards you for storytelling as much as for card management. If you prefer tight mechanical optimization, you’ll still get a lot of value from the extra targets and forced choices because they create memorable moments you’ll retell at future game nights.

## Thematic notes: humor, horror, and heartless furniture

Gloom has always walked a line between macabre and mischievous. Unhappy Homes leans into that vibe even more, by giving you a haunted hallway to wander and more jokes about bad furniture than a poorly organized IKEA showroom. The humor is gentle enough to avoid crossing into cruelty while simultaneously delivering that satisfying dose of doom that fans crave.

Thematically, the expansion shines when you lean into roleplay. It invites you to craft elaborate backstories for each room and to narrate the misfortunes like a soap opera script. The furniture cards often come with little one-liners that land if you give them the right moment, turning a simple draw into a laugh that echoes around the table for the rest of the night.

One minor caveat: if you are playing with a group that takes long turns very seriously, the Neighborhood events can feel a bit punishing. It’s not an unfair mechanic, but it does tilt the table toward more dynamic, less predictable rounds. If you want more control, you can tweak the frequency of Neighborhood cards or set house rules that cap the number of neighborhood events per round. Our recommendation is to try it as written a few times, then tailor to your group’s taste if needed.

## How it compares with other Gloom expansions

There are a few expansions already floating around in the wild, each with its own flavor. Unhappy Homes sits in a comfortable middle ground between flavor expansions that add theme without changing core mechanics and mechanical expansions that alter how you interact with gloom counters or endgame timing.

- Flavor-first expansions: These are all about atmosphere and story hooks. Unhappy Homes borrows heavily from this pole but adds enough structural changes to keep the game feeling fresh without requiring a new rulebook.
- Mechanic-heavy expansions: In those, you often get new tokens, new endgame conditions, or new ways to manipulate gloom. Unhappy Homes stays on the lighter side of this spectrum while still delivering meaningful twists that affect decision points and tempo.
- Narrative-driven expansions: Some sets push you into longer campaigns with evolving boards. Unhappy Homes is stand-alone friendly but hints at long-running campaigns if your group wants to commit to recurring neighborhood feuds.

If you own other expansions, you’ll likely find Unhappy Homes complements them nicely. It provides a different flavor of misfortune and helps you tell new stories around the same table, which is exactly what a good expansion should do.

Unhappy Homes adds enough variety to justify multiple replays. The combination of House cards, Neighborhood cards, and new events means that no two games feel identical in tone or setup. From a value perspective, the box is solid. It isn’t oversized, it isn’t under-delivering, and it provides a tangible bump to the mood and the laughs per minute you can achieve at the table. If you are building a Gloom library, Unhappy Homes is the kind of purchase that pays for itself in the first few sessions through clever plays and a few perfect misfortunes.

## Final thoughts and recommendation

Unhappy Homes takes the beloved base game and injects a dose of neighborhood-level misfortune that clicks really well with the original tempo. The new cards are flavorful, the mechanics are approachable, and the humor lands with a satisfying thud that will make you smile even as your characters suffer a wardrobe malfunction in the haunted closet. It is not a radical departure from Gloom, but it is a clear and purposeful expansion that adds depth without becoming a different game altogether. If you own Gloom and you want more round-turning misfortune and a stronger sense of place, Unhappy Homes is a strong recommendation.
