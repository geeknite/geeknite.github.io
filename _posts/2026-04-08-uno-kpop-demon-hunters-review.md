## Gameplay and player count
The game supports 2-10 players, which means you can squeeze a whole party into a living room and still have space to step away for snacks without shattering the vibe. The rule set is designed to feel approachable for newcomers who know UNO but new players who haven’t seen a 20-card chain reaction in their life. The core UNO mechanics are intact: matching either color or number, drawing from the deck when you cannot play, and aiming to be the first to discard all your cards. The new twist is the demon hunter card set, which introduces powerups that can alter the flow of play in surprising ways.

#### New cards and abilities
- Demon Echo: It mirrors the current card, but with a twist; if you chain cards you get to copy a power from another player’s demon hunter ability. This creates chaotic synergy that can force dramatic comebacks.
- Hunter’s Blessing: A one-time shield that prevents a single effect from impacting you on the next turn.
- Stage Jump: A special card that lets you jump to the next stage of the anime fight, changing up the pace and often allowing you to dodge a potential penalty.
- Idol Break: Forces the next player to skip their turn, but only when you have the right demon hunter power in play. The interplay is clever, but you’ll want to keep a quick mental map of who has what power if you’re deep into the game with several players.

### Setup and kickoff
To start, shuffle both decks together for the large group mode; deal seven cards to each player; place the draw pile in the center; flip the top card to start the discard pile. The demon hunter powers come into play when a player uses a demon power card on their turn—these can be triggered in addition to normal UNO actions, which means you’ll be juggling color matching and power strategy from the moment the first card is laid down.

## Rules in brief and learning curve
The base rule set remains the backbone. You can play a card if it matches color or number. You may place wild cards to change the current color under which everyone else must play. The two demon hunter decks introduce a modular rule layer. For instance, in a typical game you might claim that a Demon Echo must be played only when you’re down to the last three cards, or Stage Jump might require you to announce a joke in a dramatic anime voice before you can lay the card down. The exact mechanics are enumerated in the rulebook, but the gist is this: you should anticipate how your opponents are using their demon hunter powers and adjust accordingly.

### Lightning round
If you want to get into the heat of the party faster, there is a lightning round option. Shuffle the two decks together and remove the demon hunter cards with powers you think are too overpowered for a given group size. This allows you to maintain the UNO speed while dialing up or down the chaos.

## Strategy and house rules
The core strategy remains familiar to UNO players: try to unload your hand efficiently while preventing others from doing the same. The additional demon hunter powers add layers of strategic decision-making that reward careful reading of your opponents’ patterns as well as your own risk tolerance. A recommended approach is to track which power cards have been used and to decide whether you want to disrupt another player’s plan or push for a fast exit.

House rules
- Modify the power card effects depending on group size: for younger players, limit the number of simultaneous power card activations per turn.
- Use two separate piles for color cards and power cards to keep things visually simple if you’re hosting a larger party.
- Introduce a concert mode where players must chime in with a fun line or chant when Stage Jump is played. It adds flavor and helps fill the room with laughter.
