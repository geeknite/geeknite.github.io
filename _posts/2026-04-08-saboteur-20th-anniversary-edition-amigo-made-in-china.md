## What is Saboteur?

Saboteur is a social deduction game wrapped in a light, accessible mining theme. Each round, players take on one of two broad roles: miners who want to reach the treasure, and saboteurs who want to derail the tunnel and seize the gold for themselves. The twist is that players’ roles are hidden; you only learn about your neighbors’ intentions through the cards they play and the tunnels they construct or ruin. The objective is simple to describe but delicious to execute: navigate a labyrinth of path cards to reach the treasure, all while trying to deduce who among you is an ally and who is a hidden foe.

The 20th Anniversary Edition keeps this core intact but refines the boundaries and the breath between turns. The pace is brisk—typically 25–40 minutes for a full game with four to six players. It’s a game that thrives on conversation: everyone is trying to read the room, interpret the board’s shape, and store away little tells about each other’s loyalties. When the round ends and the cards are revealed, the room erupts with a mix of triumph and accusation, which is exactly what Saboteur promises—and often delivers—on a good night of gaming.

## Setup and First Impressions

Setting up Saboteur is a five-minute ritual that teaches you the value of patience and the danger of chance. Shuffle the path cards and deal them into a stack. Each player gets a secret role card (Miner or Saboteur) and a handful of action cards to alter fate, delay a tunnel, or otherwise complicate the path to the treasure. The treasure marker sits at the end of the tunnel, barely visible at the start, as if to remind you that the prize you crave occasionally sits behind the most fragile of loyalties.

The first round is always a litmus test. Do you trust your neighbor to lay a safe path, or do you prepare a counter-bluff by discarding a card that casts doubt on someone’s intentions? The 20th Edition’s clarity makes those decisions more approachable. The tokens add weight to the endgame, and the new art makes it easier to identify who might be scheming as the board grows crowded with tunnels.

## How to Play: Core Mechanics Explored

### Turn structure and round progression

On your turn, you draw a card, or you draw and play, then you pass to the next player. The main card types are path cards (to extend the tunnel) and action/role cards (to hinder others or reveal information). Your objective is to guide a tunnel to the treasure while being mindful that not everyone is rooting for the same outcome. The round ends when the treasure is reached or when the deck is exhausted and no more path cards can be played. At that point, points are awarded to the miners if the treasure is reached, or to the saboteurs if the tunnel collapses before the treasure can be claimed.

### Role dynamics and bluffing

Role concealment is the beating heart of Saboteur. The 20th Anniversary Edition doesn’t reinvent this mechanism; it preserves it with a gloss of improved readability. The excitement comes from the subtle tells and the occasional overt bluff—like a miner who’s dead certain that the path they just laid is a major misstep, but who still pushes forward because the room’s enthusiasm is contagious. The saboteurs thrive on misdirection; a well-timed block or a misplayed card can derail an entire round and turn a near-win into a glorious fiasco.

### Endgame and strategic pacing

As the tunnel nears completion, players become more calculating. Do you press forward to secure the round’s victory, or do you throw a lifeline toward a fellow miner with a risky card to sow trust that can pay off in future rounds? The 20th Anniversary Edition’s pacing fairness helps prevent stalemates; even in a no-hope moment, someone can revive momentum with the right card placement or a timely blurt of bluffing. The result is a table that breathes anticipation rather than fatigue—precisely the kind of social interaction Saboteur excels at delivering.

## Strategy and House Rules for Maximum Fun

 Saboteur rewards flexible thinking and a willingness to adapt on the fly. Here are a few strategies that tend to work well, particularly with four to six players:

- If you’re a Saboteur: aim to create short, plausible tunnels that tempt others to extend the route but aren’t long enough to guarantee you’re the only one who benefits from the eventual collapse. Use deception to steer others toward misreading your intentions. A well-timed action card can turn a round from a straightforward tunnel into a chaotic stage play.
- If you’re a Miner: prioritize discovering where the fake routes are. Don’t chase a single tunnel into a dead end; be prepared to pivot if a better opportunity emerges. Your strongest asset is the group’s perception; if you can sow doubt about the “real” path while quietly building toward another route, you can tilt the round in your favor.
- Observing tells: in a game of social deduction, small tells often reveal more than explicit declarations. Watch for who’s defending a path card too hard, who seems hesitant to commit to a tunnel’s direction, and who consistently lays cards that create partisan tension rather than a clean route. These tiny signals can help you decide when to push forward and when to step back.
