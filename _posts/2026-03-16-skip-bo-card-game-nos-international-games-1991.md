## Geeknite Nostalgia: Skip-Bo NOS 1991

Unfold the box, inhale the cardboard, and welcome to a time when games came with a sense of personality. The NOS edition from International Games Inc. released in 1991 captures a moment when the world believed that orange and teal were the peak of color theory. ### Inside the box: a quick inventory
- Deck of numbered cards 1 through 12 in multiple copies.
- A handful of wild Skip-Bo cards that serve as any number.
- Stock piles for each player, a draw pile for the center, and a few color-coded discards.

### How to play: the basics with vintage flavor
- Setup: Each player gets a stock pile with a face-down top card, a hand of 5-7 cards, and a personal set of discards. The center has four building piles that will be filled in ascending order from 1 to 12.
- Turn flow: Draw to five cards; then play any cards from your hand or stock onto building piles. You can also place cards onto your own discard piles to organize future moves.
- Wilds and skips: Skip-Bo wild cards can be used as any number to help complete sequences. Use them wisely; they are your hail mary passes when your forward momentum stalls.
- Winning: The moment you have emptied your stock pile, or you manage to complete all four building piles, you win.

### Strategy: keep your eyes on the momentum
The core strategy in Skip-Bo is tempo. You want to maximize the number of cards you can play in one turn while keeping options open for future moves. The NOS edition’s card stock makes shuffling a tactile joy, and that tactile joy encourages more deliberate play. A few ideas:
- Order awareness: Know what is likely to come off the draw pile. If you can predict what your opponent might be able to play next, you can hold back a necessary card for a critical moment.
- Use discards to shape future turns: The discard piles act as a ladder you can climb in a future turn; by ordering the cards in each discard pile, you can unlock a chain of plays with minimal effort.
- Reserve wilds for late game: In the late game, a wild can swing the board in your favor; plan to keep at least one in reserve rather than spending them all in the opening rounds.
- Read opponents: The chaotic nature of the game lends itself to a bit of bluffing and misdirection. A confident declaration can cause your opponents to misread your plans and misplay.
