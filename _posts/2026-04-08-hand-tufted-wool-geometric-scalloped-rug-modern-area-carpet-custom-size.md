## Materials & Sustainability: Wool Power with a Conscience?

Let’s talk about what makes this rug tick. Wool is inherently resilient, flame resistant, and excellent at thermal regulation—your feet stay cool in summer and toasty in winter, which is basically the opposite of what most people expect from a floor covering that lives in your living room. Wool fibers can trap air, which means the rug provides insulation while remaining breathable enough to resist that “did I wear socks to bed?” vibe.

Sustainability-wise, wool is renewable and biodegradable. If you’re the type who worries about pet hair and life choices, you’ll appreciate that wool fibers shed less easily, resist staining better than some synthetics, and can be cleaned with relatively gentle methods. Hand tufting, while labor-intensive, supports skilled craftspeople and tends to have a longer supply chain life than mass-produced machine-made options.

In terms of care, the rug benefits from regular vacuuming (using a suction-only setting or a gentle beater brush that won’t abrade the pile). Spot-cleaning is recommended for spills, with a mild detergent and cold water. Avoid harsh solvents unless you enjoy the crunching sound of a fiber’s heartbreak. The result is a durable piece that ages gracefully, acquiring a patina that says, “I have stories.”

## Care & Cleaning: Keeping It Fresh Without Mourning Your Carpet

Maintaining a hand-tufted wool rug requires a light touch. Here are practical care steps:

- Regular vacuuming with a suction-only setting maintains pile height and prevents dirt from grinding into the fibers.
- Address spills quickly. Blot—don’t rub—with a clean cloth to lift liquid from the surface. For stubborn spots, a mild wool-safe cleaner can be used according to the manufacturer’s guidelines.
- Rotate the rug every 3–6 months to promote even wear, especially if you have a sunny spot in the room that might bleach a particular area over time.
- Professional cleaning every 1–2 years isn’t a bad idea, particularly if you live in a high-dust or pet-heavy environment.

The bottom line: treat it with respect, not with a fear of mess. It’s a rug, not a gemstone, though the reverence you show it can be almost ceremonial.
