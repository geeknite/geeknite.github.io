## How to play (the quick version)

If you know UNO, you know the rhythm: match the color or number, play action cards to disrupt your friends, and sprint toward getting rid of your hand before the others. This Disney Princess edition sticks to that core loop but adds a few thematic flourishes to keep it from feeling routine.

Setup
- Each player starts with 7 cards. The rest goes into a draw pile, flipped to start the discard pile.
- The princess theme means you’ll see color-coded groups and princess portraits that correspond to the card’s face.

Turn structure
- On your turn, you either match the color or the princess motif on the top discard card to continue play, or you play an action card that changes the pace of the round.
- Action cards might include standard UNO chaos like Skip, Reverse, and Draw Two, but with a Disney twist in their art and occasional flavor text that references princess lore.

Special cards (princess twists)
- A small subset of cards carries a “Princess Power” effect. When played, these cards trigger a mild house-rule moment that’s designed to evoke storytelling rather than pure strategy. Examples could be “Switch Hands With a Princess” or “Princess Swap Color,” which injects a little chaos but remains kid-friendly.

Draw rules
- If you can’t legally play, you draw one card. If you still can’t play, your turn ends. The pace is brisk; rounds rarely last more than 15–20 minutes, which makes this ideal for a quick, repeatable play.

Winning
- The goal is to be the first player to discard all of your cards. If you’re playing with the extended house rules, you might require one final call or a confirming gesture to announce you have won.

House rules spark joy
- If you’ve ever introduced kindergarteners to Uno, you’ll recognize the potential for loud yelling and triumphant giggles. Add a princess theme and the room becomes a stage where kids feel like they’re part of a story, not just a game.

Playstyle notes
- For younger players (roughly 5–7), this edition shines as a memory and color-matching exercise disguised as a fairytale. For older players, the nostalgia factor and the slightly elevated tempo make it a good filler game or a light warm-up before more strategic titles.
