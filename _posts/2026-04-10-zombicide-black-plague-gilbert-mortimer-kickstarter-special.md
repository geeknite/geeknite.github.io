If you’re a completionist who needs a mental model for the value proposition, think of this as a miniature expansion that doubles down on the best parts of Black Plague: team-based objectives, crunchy gear management, and the endless joy of seeing a heroic swing slice through a stack of zombies like an overcized broom through a dusty closet. ## The two protagonists: Gilbert & Mortimer
Gilbert is the archetype you want when you need someone who studies the problem and then tries to prove his theory with precision. Mortimer is the wild card: fast, a little reckless, and the kind of ally who sometimes forgets to duck but somehow still saves the party by accident. Together, they create a flexibility that can adapt to several combat styles in a way that feels natural rather than forced.

From a design perspective, their cards emphasize duo synergy. Not every action in Zombicide is a solo affair; many sequences benefit from combining two players or two sets of actions that magnify each other’s effects. Gilbert tends to excel in planful, measured actions—think setting up traps, sharpening your team’s defenses, and exploiting the brainy side of the board. Mortimer, meanwhile, thrives on momentum—chain-attacking, carefully timed charges, or a reckless sprint into a zombie horde that somehow completes the objective due to sheer bravado.

The net effect is a more dynamic turn that rewards team communication and anticipatory planning. If your group loves the moment when a team rushes a window of opportunity and turns a near wipe into a successful extraction, Gilbert and Mortimer are going to feel like a natural fit.

For fans of the base game who worry that a duo might overcorrect the balance, there are guardrails. The duo is strong, but their abilities require you to coordinate—there are no free power moves here. You still need to manage supply boxes, punch through fat stacks of walking corpses, and choose between guarding your new friends or focusing on the ever-looming objective markers. They don’t break the core loop; they remix it with a complementary chemistry.

If you want a quick in-universe snapshot that sums up their vibe, you can imagine Gilbert muttering a research note under his breath while Mortimer vaults a fence, yells a corny quip, and somehow frees a path through the necromantic mess. The argument writes itself: a duo built for mischief, efficiency, and a little chaos, all in service of clearing a board full of hungry zombies.

## Gameplay and mechanical impact
The heart of Zombicide remains a cooperative assault on a modular board that changes with every mission. The Gilbert & Mortimer Kickstarter Special introduces a pair of survivor cards that unlock specific actions and incentives when used in tandem. The most noticeable impact is a shift toward cooperative sequencing: you’ll want to discuss your turn a step or two ahead so that Gilbert can set up Mortimer for a high-impact payoff on the same round.

In practical terms, you may find yourself setting up a corridor defense while Mortimer ducks around a corner to intercept a stray zombie fat stack. The push-pull between the two characters creates a natural rhythm: Gilbert lays the groundwork, Mortimer executes, and the rest of the team capitalizes on the openings. This rhythm is especially satisfying when you roll well enough to chain a couple of actions in a single turn, effectively turning one hero into a tiny victory chain that makes you feel like you are short a coffee and a life coach but long on tactical acumen.

There are also instance-specific rules that tie into the mission book that accompanies the Kickstarter pack. Some scenarios encourage you to use the duo together to unlock a special objective or to trigger a unique event. This is where you see the design team pairing them with the scenario layout to maximize the sense of discovery while preserving the core puzzle nature of Zombicide. It is not a gimmick; it is a deliberate mechanic aimed at encouraging players to think about the board as a living, evolving problem rather than a simple kill-and-collect quest.

From a balance perspective, Gilbert & Mortimer sit on a knife edge. They’re not overpowered if your table respects their duo requirements; at the same time, if you try to force a solo play or run reckless combos without coordination, you’ll feel the pain of a squad overwhelmed by specialized zombies and a particularly stubborn boss. The design’s intention is to reward teamwork and careful planning, not to enable a solo hero show that would undermine the experience for everyone else.

## Solo and replayability notes
Like many Zombicide expansions, Gilbert & Mortimer scale in interesting ways when you play solo or with smaller groups. Solo play requires you to simulate two brains, which makes the duo a natural fit. You can handle one character as your AI assistant while you perform actions for the other, turning a narrative into a one-man show with a surprisingly heavy load of planning. For groups, the duo invites more dynamic turn order decisions, since you are constantly weighing the benefits of splitting versus consolidating the team to maximize the duo synergy.

Replayability is a mixed bag for some players. If you like the idea of exploring two distinct power curves that interact, you will get a lot of mileage here. If you prefer a more linear, set-piece experience, you might find the duo a bit too open-ended. The scenarios provided with this Kickstarter set lean toward the exploratory end of the spectrum, with optional objectives that encourage you to experiment with the pairing in new ways. If you tend to optimize your way out of every encounter, Gilbert & Mortimer will reward you for creativity rather than raw efficiency, which is exactly the kind of friction a good campaign night needs.

## Tips for leveraging the duo on the table
- Communicate early and often. A good turn for Gilbert often sets up Mortimer for a punchier payoff. Use a pre-turn planning phase similar to a sports huddle to map your best sequence.
- Focus on synergy objectives. If the mission book cues you into a duo unlock, prioritize that route even if it means diverting from a straightforward objective path.
- Don’t ignore the rest of the team. Duo power is great, but the squad needs to keep the enmity managed. Assign one player as the anchor for clearing chokepoints while Gilbert and Mortimer execute their plan.
- Schedule a debrief after a round. Thematic moments tend to stick better when you pause to acknowledge the heroic failure or glorious success of your two leads.
- Keep an eye on the resource economy. The best turns often rely on a smart use of weapons and tools that must be balanced against the total zombie projection on the board.
