## Build quality and components
Let’s break down the actual hardware you’re playing with.

### Cards
Two decks mean you can run up to a two-deck game or simply have a spare deck in case someone loses a card to the couch monster. The cards are typically standard playing cards with a smooth, slightly glossy finish. The stock might be around 300-320 GSM, giving them a decent balance between shuffling ease and durability. The edges should be smooth enough to avoid cut fingers when you’re dealing in a high-pressure scenario, but sharp enough to “feel” premium when you riffle. If you’re a magician or an energy-chewer of cards (the kind who fans cards in a dramatic arc), you’ll want to test the deck for balance and edge wear after a few games.

If gloss bothers you and you prefer a more muted aesthetic, look for sets that advertise “matte” or “linen finish” cards. The difference can affect how chips slide across the table during a tense moment.

### Chips
A poker set is only as good as its chips. The chips in these kits vary in weight, typically around 11-13 grams each, with a standard set including 3-4 denominations and four-color coding. A good set uses a solid clay-composite or ceramic fill, which gives a certain heft and satisfying clack on the table. A quality set also keeps color fastness in mind; you don’t want blue chips turning purple after a few nights of humidity and enthusiastic celebrations. Check for a durable edge engraving and inlay that won’t rub off after a few dozen hands.

A nice feature to look for is built-in denomination stamping on the chip edges. Some cheaper sets rely on color alone, which looks snazzy at first and then invites confusion when your grandma insists her favorite green chip is actually worth a black-chip mortgage.

### Dice
Dice are optional in poker, but this set includes a handful for those who want to add a sprinkle of chaos to the evening. Standard D6 dice are common, and you might also see a d10 or d8 if the brand decides to push a small set of “gambling accessories.” The dice aren’t going to win you a tournament, but they’re a fun gimmick for deciding who posts the next blind or who picks the beverage of choice for the night.

### Box and storage
The box should feel sturdy—the kind of thing you’d trust to survive a move across town or a particularly enthusiastic game night with a cat and a toddler (don’t worry, we’re not endorsing household chaos). Look for a snug interior with cross-grain dividers for cards and a leatherette or felt insert for chips. A magnetic latch or a robust clasp helps prevent the lid from popping open mid-bluff. Some boxes include a small compartment for rule cards or a mini dry-erase scoreboard; others are minimalist, relying on the weight of the chips to keep content in place.

Portability matters if you’re a road warrior who travels with a gaming group. A compact wooden box with a carrying handle adds a lot of style without turning your suitcase into a poker table.

## Care and maintenance
To keep the set looking sharp for years, follow a few simple steps:

- Keep it dry. Wood and moisture don’t mix well, so avoid leaving the box in damp rooms. If you’re in a humid climate, consider a small dehumidifier in the game closet or a moisture-absorbing pack inside the box when not in use.
- Clean the cards lightly with a soft brush if they pick up dust or oils from hands. Don’t use chemical cleaners.
- Wipe the wooden exterior with a soft, dry cloth. If you want to refresh the finish, use a small amount of wood-safe oil, but test on a hidden area first.
- Inspect the interior lining for fraying, and replace the box if the lining begins to degrade. The chips will happily outlast the lining, so you’ll want the rest of the kit to look as polished as the night you’re about to host.
