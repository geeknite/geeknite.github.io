## Fatty minis in Green Horde: what you actually get
In Green Horde, the Fatty is not merely a bigger version of a zombie with extra health; it is a storytelling device that represents the industrial strength of the horde, blending ogre-like heft with zombie tenacity. The sculpt is bold, with a blocky silhouette that reads well from across a table, which matters a lot when you are pushing around tiny meeples and trying not to trip over your own initiative track. The green skin tone has a slightly swampy undertone that reads well under common gaming lights, and the armor motifs on the Fatty are more tribal than ceremonial, hinting at a civilization of raiders who learned to terabyte their way through the swamp by accident. The sculpt quality is consistent with what you would expect from Zombicide minis: crisp edges, dynamic poses, and enough texture to let a painter have a field day.

The Fatty has a presence that feels like it commands a little more space on the board, which is exactly what you want when you roll the big boss dice and declare a roadblock to the survivors. The design also leans into the fantasy theme of Green Horde by adding tomahawk-like blades, bone adornments, and a couple of wrong choices that a designer would not make for a human hero. These are not just cosmetics; they help players distinguish a Fatty from a standard zombie on a crowded board and signal to new players that this is not your average shambling trouble. The mini stands sturdy on its base, meaning you are less likely to witness the classic “Fatty wobble” that can ruin a tactical plan mid-turn.

## Miniature design and painting potential
The Fatty sculpt invites paint, shading, and a little bit of experimentation. The broad surfaces tolerate layers of dry brushing and washes without fear of losing detail, which is a win for hobbyists who like to squeeze every drop of color into their zombie apocalypse dioramas. If you are new to painting, Fatty minis offer a forgiving entry point because the large crevices allow for easy shading, while the large armor plates give you predictable planes to highlight. The risk here is that some players might be tempted to do a flashy, high-contrast look that battles with swampy greens and browns. If you prefer a more subtle approach, you can layer greens and chroma tones to build a natural patina that reads as ancient, with a hint of rot. Either way, you end up with a centerpiece that commands the table.

Here is a quick painting tip short list to get you started:

- Base coat in a deep swamp green or olive, then build up with lighter greens toward edges to create volume.
- Use a brown wash to bring out textures in armor and bone details; it makes the sculpt pop without looking cartoonish.
- A touch of bone white on teeth and bone accents can create that creepy, undead glow in low light.
- Dry brush metallic silver on armor plates for a weathered steel look, then apply a light glaze of green to simulate corrosion.

If you love painting with a story in your head, the Fatty minis are a playground. The textures on armor and bone accessories are the perfect canvas for a narrative: this Fatty was once a raider who progressed into something else, something bigger and older than the battlefield itself. Painting them becomes not only a hobby but a small piece of worldbuilding you can bring to the table.

## Assembly and scale considerations
Fatty minis typically come as a single piece or with a couple of optional assembly choices. The CG (Cool Mini or Not) standard here is moderate to easy assembly; you usually don’t need extra tools beyond plastic snips and a tiny amount of green stuff for the pinning if you want extra pose options. The Fatty stands on a standard round base, which makes it compatible with most Zombicide boards and compatible expansion terrains. If you are into customizing your own boards or want to mix Fatty minis with other Zombicide pieces, be mindful of scale differences. You might find that Green Horde Fatty is slightly more imposing than a typical walker, which is a good visual cue that this is a creature designed to disrupt the survivors’ plans, not just add another statistic to track.

## The Fatty in game terms: how it actually plays
Fatty minis are not flavor alone; they encode meaningful mechanical weight. In Green Horde, Fatty characters act as bulwarks against the survivors’ advance, with higher health thresholds and more robust movement compared with standard walkers. The Fatty’s goals on a given turn are straightforward: close the distance, threaten heavy hitting zones, and trigger fear among the party. The terrain you present on the board becomes a second hero: swamps, ruins, and dense forests all amplify the Fatty’s menace, while open ground might invite a reckless sprint that ends in a trap of your design.

From a rules perspective, Fatty mechanics shift the tempo of the game. Instead of four or five quick kills, the Fatty tests the survivability of the party by presenting multiple attack vectors in a single round. The Fatty can soak damage that would wipe out smaller zombies and still keep moving; this means players must coordinate their actions more carefully, planning moves with the Fatty as a central strain in the narrative of the round. For players who enjoy tactical depth, the Fatty adds a psychological layer: you might spend turns harassing a Fatty to buy time for weaker team members, and the Fatty’s presence makes you react to the board state rather than simply roll dice and cross your fingers.

## Painting and hobby tips: getting the most out of your Fatty minis
- Pick a base texture that resembles clay or swamp rock to give the overall hue a grounded feel.
- Layer greens in a gradient to highlight armor plates; this adds depth without making the Fatty look like a neon-green cartoon.
- Consider weathering on the armor: a brown wash with a light green glaze on the edges can make the Fatty look seasoned in battle rather than brand new.
- Use a bright white or bone color for teeth and bone accents to give a stark contrast that reads well from a distance.
- If you want to push the narrative, give your Fatty a battle scar or two with a dark paint, then dry brush a brighter color to highlight the injury.
