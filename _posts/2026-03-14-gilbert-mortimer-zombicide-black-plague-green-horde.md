## Overview

Gilbert & Mortimer are the kind of duo who can turn a quiet Sunday into a quest across a medieval kitchen, armed with nothing but a fork, a rumor, and a couple of thrift-store swords. In this review, we plunge into Zombicide: Black Plague with the Green Horde expansion, a pairing that feels like a mismatched duo in a high-stakes farce: you’ve got heroic knights, a relentless zombie threat, and an orcish wave that’s less “gore-fest” and more “chaotic high-five.” If base Zombicide was a brisk sprint through a graveyard, Green Horde is a chaotic dash through a forest that keeps rearranging itself. It adds bite-sized encounters, more tactical options, and the kind of humor that makes you forget you’re staring down a behemoth of green muscle and teeth.

For quick orientation, the official Zombicide page is a good starting point: [Zombicide: Black Plague official page](https://www.zombicide.com/).

The two new heroes, Gilbert and Mortimer, aren’t just label stickers on cardboard: they’re the engine that makes the expansion feel like a proper addition rather than a gimmick. Gilbert can anchor the frontline with durability and threat drawing power; Mortimer contributes ranged reach and utility, offering ways to keep your squad moving when the board swells with orcs and misfortune. The Green Horde’s threats breathe new life into the scenario lineup, offering moments that demand clever coordination rather than brute force. In short, it feels like a natural continuation of Black Plague’s character-driven play, now with bigger greens on the menu.

## Setup and Components

Setting up Zombicide is a ritual of dread that somehow remains comforting. You’ll lay out map tiles, shuffle in a heap of zombie miniatures, pick a scenario, distribute survivors, and then cross your fingers that the dice decide to be merciful for once. The Green Horde expansion adds a fresh corridor of complexity without reinventing the wheel: new enemies (orcs, warg-like mounts in some setups), new equipment cards, and a handful of scenario cards that lean into multi-threat dynamics. The components themselves are uniformly sturdy: plastic miniatures with decent detail, thick tokens, and tiles that are easy to distinguish even when you’re playing in a dimly lit basement with a single lamp and a cat who wants to sit on the board.

One of the highlights of the setup is how smooth the integration feels. You don’t need to overhaul your tables or your house rules; you slot the new tiles into the existing map and introduce the Orc Boss as a named encounter. The new hero sheet cards sit comfortably next to Gilbert and Mortimer’s original sheets, and the equipment deck slides into the existing card-based economy without producing a whiplash of new resource management concepts. It’s a breath of fresh air for a fan who has memorized the base game’s flowchart and wants to see a little more color on the battlefield.

From a production standpoint, the art direction remains charming and a touch cheeky. The Orcs are fierce enough to feel threatening, but they’re rendered with a cartoonish intimidation that sits well with Zombicide’s overall tone. The cards carry clear icons and readable text, which matters when you’re juggling multiple threats across a single turn. If you’re a collector of visual cues and you value a game that rewards you with satisfying “oh, I can do this” moments, Green Horde hits that sweet spot.

### The Orc Boss and Encounter Design

The spotlight in Green Horde is obviously the Orc Boss, a multi-stage threat that can alter the tide of play in the same way a strong weather front shifts a battlefield. The Boss isn’t a passive menace; it demands attention, coordinates its minions, and forces the survivors to diversify their approach. That means you’ll want to consider objective timing, line-of-sight tactics, and the risk-reward calculus of delaying an objective to whittle down the Boss’s life total or to gather a critical piece of loot that will unlock the next phase of the encounter.

This design choice—the Boss as a tempo changer—makes sense in the context of a cooperative game that thrives on escalation. It gives your team a clear antagonist to rally against and, more importantly, gives Gilbert and Mortimer a chance to shine in the kinds of roles they were built for: steady leadership and reliable support. The Boss’s presence also helps justify some of the new equipment: if you’re going to fight a creature that dwarfs your survivors in mass and ferocity, you’ll want every advantage you can squeeze from your cards and dice.

## Gameplay Rhythm: How the Green Horde Changes the Flow

If you’ve played Black Plague enough times, you know the game’s rhythm: you plan a turn, you execute, you resolve threats, you loot, you pass turn, and the table breathes as the zombie flood repositions. Green Horde doesn’t break that rhythm; it bends it. The addition of orcs ushers in a new tempo: more decisions per turn, tighter map control requirements, and a morale effect that depends on whether your team is communicating or merely coasting on a prayer and a good pair of luck-based rolls.

The expansion’s missions typically require you to juggle multiple objectives, which makes the session feel more like a campaign than a one-off skirmish. You might need to secure an item in one corner of the map while defending a corridor under siege and then pivot to confront the Orc Boss when he reveals himself from the forest. The humor of Gilbert’s antics often surfaces precisely in these moments: a misstep leads to a cascade of consequences, and the two heroes—united by their banter—must turn the tide with quick thinking and a little bit of luck.

One caveat for players who prefer a pure, straight-forward, zombie-smashing rhythm: Green Horde introduces a generosity of rules for those who want to taste more complexity. If your group is new to Zombicide, it might be wise to start with the base game or a lighter expansion before tackling the greenskins. If you’re a veteran, you’ll relish the added tactical depth and the expanded toolkit that invites creative problem solving.

## Tips for Success and Strategy with Gilbert & Mortimer

To help you convert chaos into victory, here are a few practical guidelines based on real plays and the lessons learned from many a misstep:

- Play to your strengths: Gilbert excels at taking heat and maintaining a forward presence, while Mortimer excels at enabling long-range engagements and resource management. Build your turn plan around leveraging those strengths in concert with your teammates.
- Map awareness is king: The expanded board means there are more angles for orc charges and more routes for you to be flanked. Prioritize position, and use the terrain to block lines of attack or force the enemy to funnel into kill zones you control.
- Don’t ignore loot: The Green Horde deck includes items that significantly alter outcomes. Prioritize loot that adds synergy to your chosen hero strategy, even if it costs you a round of action to secure it.
- Adapt to bosses: The Orc Boss has multiple phases, each with its own flavor. Don’t expect a single perfect plan to work; be prepared to adjust your tactics as the Boss reveals its next move.
- Communicate, then act: The best moments come when a team crafts a plan aloud and assigns roles for the upcoming round. If you’re a talkative group, you’ll feel right at home with Green Horde; if you’re quieter, use simple signals so you don’t fritter away turns dithering.

## Artwork, Materials, and Sensory Experience

Beyond the mechanics, the physical presentation deserves a mention. The miniature sculpts for the orcs are robust and characterful, offering enough variation to look dynamic on the board rather than repetitive. The sculpting for Gilbert and Mortimer remains faithful to the original design language—slightly exaggerated heroism that still reads well on a tabletop. The color choices for the orcs and their allies give the board a lively, almost kinetic energy that keeps you engaged as you navigate the escalating chaos. The cardboard pieces—tiles, tokens, and cards—are sturdy, with clean finishes and a satisfying tactile feel as you shuffle, slide, and flip through them between rounds.

Visually, Green Horde embodies the same lighthearted energy of the base game while injecting a touch more visual density. The forest tiles look moody and immersive, which helps sell the sense of being under siege by a green tide rather than simply playing another game on an empty kitchen table. The artwork does a lot of the heavy lifting in terms of narrative immersion, allowing you to imagine your group facing off against enemies that feel both fantastical and perilous.

## Thematic Fit and Replayability

Replayability remains one of Zombicide’s strongest assets, and Green Horde contributes significantly. Each scenario offers different conditions, enemy spawns, and objectives, which reduces the risk of “one and done” fatigue. The two new heroes give you fresh starting points for your builds, and the expanded card pool adds flavor and variety to your deck-building decisions. If you’re the kind of player who loves immersing your session in a coherent story, Gilbert and Mortimer are the perfect anchors for your tabletop tale. The humor and the shared narrative will keep you returning to the table even after you’ve seen the expansion a few times.

In addition, the possibility of combining Green Horde with other expansions yields a surprisingly thick array of play experiences. If you want to push a longer, more complex co-op experience, you can stitch multiple expansions together and craft an ongoing campaign. The modularity is a strong selling point for Zombicide, and Green Horde respects that design philosophy without making things feel chaotic for chaotic’s sake.

### Final Impressions

In the end, Green Horde is a solid, characterful expansion that respects the vibe of Black Plague while adding enough new material to keep things interesting. It’s neither too light to feel inconsequential nor so dense that it becomes a slog for non-hardcore players. Gilbert and Mortimer are more than just two new heroes; they’re a narrative engine that keeps the sessions lively and memorable. The new orcs and the Orc Boss provide fresh strategic challenges that reward careful planning and good teamwork. If you’re looking to elevate your Zombicide: Black Plague experience with more variety, stronger boss encounters, and the kind of humor that makes your friends grin even as the board fills with green figures, Green Horde is worth the plunge.
