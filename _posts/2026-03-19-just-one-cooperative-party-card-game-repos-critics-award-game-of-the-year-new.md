## Overview
Cooperative party games live and die by the energy in the room. Just One orchestrates that energy with a simple skeleton: one player is the reader, everyone else writes a one-word clue to help identify a secret word. Clues are then revealed, the reader must guess the word using the non-duplicated clues, and if they guess correctly, the team collectively scores a point. If the clues are all identical, or if the reader misreads the clues, potential points go poof like a magician’s rabbit on a bad stage cue. What could be a basic word game becomes a dance of misdirection, alignment, and hilarious misfires as players attempt to out-clever each other without tipping over into chaos.

The game’s premise is deceptively simple: reduce the potential pool of clues to one-word hints, ensure no duplicates slip into the final reveal, and keep everyone engaged without tipping into chaos. It’s the kind of premise that sounds almost too easy, and that is precisely why Just One shines. It respects your time, your brain, and your willingness to concede that sometimes the best clue is the most obvious one—without screaming it aloud at the wrong moment.

## How to play: a quick voyage through the rounds
### Setup and player count
Just One supports a range of player counts, typically 3–7 players. The more players you have, the funnier the collaborative misfires become, because more people have ideas that sound brilliant in a vacuum and terrible on the page. The setup is a breeze: shuffle the word cards, place them face-down, and give each player a tiny piece of paper and a pencil. A player is designated as the reader for the round, and the rest of the table are the clue-givers.

### The round in a few steps
1) The reader flips a card to reveal a secret word (e.g., “Eclipse”).
2) Each non-reader writes a single word clue on their slips of paper. Clues should be one word; multiple words look suspiciously like premeditated chaos.
3) Clues are collected and shown to the reader, but duplicated clues are removed. If two people write “solar” and “sun,” the reader will only see one of those, which creates a tiny strategic wrinkle: you want your clue to be unique but also helpful.
4) The reader reads the non-duplicate clues aloud and must try to guess the secret word. If they guess correctly, the team scores a point for that round. If not, no points, and you move on.
5) Rounds continue with a new reader. The game often ends after a pre-determined number of rounds or when you run out of patience, whichever comes first.

### The pivotal moment: when a clue lands or misses
The thrill of Just One comes down to the reader’s interpretation of the clues. A well-chosen clue can feel like a cosmic breadcrumb, leading right to the target word. A dubious clue, especially when several players have already written something that feels similar, can trigger a cascade of laughter and confusion that might derail the round—yet derail in a glorious, memorable way. The rule that duplicates are removed is tiny but vital: it forces players to think about how their clue will be perceived by someone who hasn’t had their exact line of thought. It’s a social puzzle with a shared understanding that evolves with every round.

### Player dynamics and group chemistry
With three or more players, you get a delightful mix of personalities: the strategist trying to game the system, the comedian who writes clues that are ridiculous in the best way, and the quiet observer who suddenly becomes a language maestro when their clue lands. The balance of silence and chatter is the social heartbeat of Just One. It’s not about who’s the best at puzzle-solving; it’s about how often the table can collapse into genuine, contagious laughter because someone’s clue accidentally references a pop-culture moment only three people in the room remember.

## Practical tips for hosting a Just One night
- Pick a comfortable space with good seating and visible score-tracking space. A big table helps everyone feel included and reduces the risk of someone shouting across the room that they forgot to duplicate their clue.
- Have a few optional rule tweaks in your back pocket. For example, you can allow a single two-word clue if the table agrees that it won’t derail the game into a vocabulary bee. The core rule is still: one word per clue, duplicates removed. The spirit remains cooperative and playful, not punitive.
- Prepare a rotation plan for the reader role. If someone is consistently unlucky with guessing, mix up the order to give them a few rounds of fresh clues. The goal is to keep the energy high, not drain the life force out of the table.
- Use a timer if you want a zippy experience. A 60-second limit per round keeps momentum and keeps the laughs in the room rather than the stillness of a long, drawn-out guess.
- Create a culture of compliments. When someone writes a surprisingly perfect clue, celebrate it. Part of the magic is linguistic improvisation, and positive reinforcement helps everyone feel fearless when it’s their turn to clue.
