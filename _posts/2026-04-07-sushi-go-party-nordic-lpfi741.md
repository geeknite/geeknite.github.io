## How to play in a nutshell
Sushi Go Party is a drafting game: everyone simultaneously chooses a card from their hand, then passes the rest to the neighbor. You reveal, score, then pass again, over several rounds. The menus you select for the round determine which patterns score points, so there’s strategy baked into your card-choice decisions even as you’re trying to predict what your friends will do next.

### Setup and flow
1. Pick a menu deck (with Nordic flair, of course) and shuffle. The number of menus used depends on player count; more players, more variety.
2. Each player gets a hand of cards. In a 2- to 3-player game, you’ll see the cards move a bit differently than in larger groups, but the core feels the same: draft, pass, and score.
3. On three, four, five, or more rounds (depending on the rules you’re running), you simultaneously pick a card and pass the rest. The round ends when all cards are revealed. Then you tally your points based on the menu’s scoring rules.
4. Repeat with a fresh hand and a new menu until you’ve run through all rounds.

The game’s pacing is crisp: a quick mental calculation here, a clever bluff there, and before you know it you’re at the end of a round with a new decision waiting. It’s snack-sized strategy—no long setup, no heavy calculus, just enough decision‑making to feel clever without making your brain crumble into sushi rice.

## Strategy tips for the Nordic night
- Prioritize multi-card combos: The real joy of Sushi Go Party is building tiny, well-specified point engines. If you see a menu that rewards the same pattern, you might want to chase early cards that set you up for it.
- Read your neighbors, not just the cards: Drafting is partially predictive. If your seats are filled with people who consistently go for maki, consider a different early pick that blocks or diversifies your potential gains.
- Balance risk and reward: A single strong card can swing the round, but overcommitting to one pattern can also leave you with nothing at the end. The trick is to keep a flexible plan that adapts as cards vanish from the table.
- Don’t ignore corner cases: Some menus heavily reward rare cards; others reward clean efficiency. A quick mental tally before you draft can save you a few extra points by round’s end.
- Time your passes: How you pass cards matters. If you’re in a position where your neighbor often hits you with a blocking move, you might want to pivot early rather than ride the current hand to the very end.
