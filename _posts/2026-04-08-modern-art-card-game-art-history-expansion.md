## Mechanics and Systems: What Changes, Why It Matters
The expansion doesn’t overturn the core rules; instead, it layers on depth. Here are the systems you’ll be juggling:

### Auction Reimagined
In the base game, players bid on artworks in a sequence that reveals market preferences and valuations. The Art History Expansion introduces movement-specific bidding quirks. For example, Impressionist cards might grant you bonus points if your bid aligns with a soft-tade of “informal light” that mirrors a famous Renoir effect, while Cubist cards incentivize breaking a single painting into multiple viewpoints in your auction strategy. The practical upshot: your bidding becomes a chess match about interpretation, not just value alone.

### Contextual Scoring
Context wins. In addition to the numerical score, there are context bonuses for submitting the most historically accurate interpretation of a given set. This isn’t about being pretentious; it’s about recognizing that art is a conversation across time. If you manage to align your bids with the era’s actual market mood, you’ll pick up extra tokens that can swing late-game outcomes. The catch: you’ll need to justify your alignment with a quick one-liner to the table. Silly? Sometimes. Satisfying? Always.

### Curator Actions
The expansion adds “curator” actions that let you look at face-down cards, swap positions, or even force a single bid to be re-evaluated. It’s a control mechanism that rewards long-term planning and punishes first-round bravado, which is exactly how you should feel when you realize that the gallery’s architecture favors the patient predator rather than the flash-in-the-pan seller.

### Movement Matchups
Certain art movements interact with each other in unique ways. An Impressionist boost might amplify a Realist card’s effect, while Dada disrupts a linear auction flow and forces players to improvise. It’s wonderfully chaotic in the best way: you end up legitimizing your chaotic instincts by citing an art-historical precedent. Yes, you’ll be that player, and yes, you’ll be proud of it.

## Thematic Depth: Six Movements, Six Personalities
Let’s take a quick stroll through how the six major movements are translated into gameplay personality:

### Impressionism: Light as Currency
Impressionist cards reward players who bid with sensitivity to light and atmosphere. The mechanic rewards players who anticipate mood shifts and bid slightly ahead of the crowd. It’s not about peak-value dollars; it’s about capturing the “glow” of the moment. Expect a lot of tense whispered conversations about whether a painting’s warmth actually translates to market warmth.

### Realism: Grounded Returns
Realist cards anchor the game in practical valuation. They reward straightforward bidding and honest valuations, punishing over-the-top flamboyance with a small but meaningful penalty. You’ll see players quietly recalibrating their bets to reflect the mundane but essential truth: real-world markets often reward steady risk management more than fireworks.

### Expressionism: The Emotive Spike
Expressionist cards teach you to lean into emotion and volatility. The bids swing due to perceived intensity rather than actual quality. It’s chaotic, but in a controlled way: you learn to read your table as a social canvas, not just a market snapshot.

### Cubism: Perspective Multiplication
Cubist cards split outcomes into multiple angles. This is the expansion’s strategic backbone: a single bid now has multiple potential endgames. It complicates the late-game decision tree in delicious ways and makes you consider not just what you’re bidding on, but how many ways that bid can pay off.

### Dada: Anti-Strategist Strategy
Dada’s trick is to disrupt expectations. The expansion lets Dada cards scramble score tallies, swap bid orders, or flip a wall of perception into a mirror. The result is a delightful chaos that somehow ends with players arguing about whether a dream can be priced in dollars. It’s subversive in the very best sense—funny, sharp, and surprisingly insightful.

### Surrealism: Dream Logic in Numbers
Surrealist cards blur boundaries between value and imagination. They introduce surreal thresholds where a bid might be worth more for what it represents than what it literally costs. This is where the expansion nudges players toward creative thinking: perhaps the most valuable painting is the one that changes how you think about value itself.
