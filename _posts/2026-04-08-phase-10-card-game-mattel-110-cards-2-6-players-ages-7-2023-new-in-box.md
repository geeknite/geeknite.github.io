## How to play in 60 seconds
Phase 10 is deceptively simple in its core loop, but that does not mean the game is shallow. Here is a brisk outline you can share with newcomers:

- Each player draws one card from the draw pile, then discards one card to end their turn.
- The objective is to complete the current phase on your tableau, which means forming specific sets and runs as described in the phase list.
- When you complete a phase, you lay down the cards in front of you and advance to the next phase. The first player to finish Phase 10 and have all the required components completed wins (or at least claims the most glory while your friends grumble about luck).
- Wild cards substitute for any card needed in a phase, while Skip cards disrupt your opponents by blocking their turn.
- Points score against you if you have cards left in hand when a round ends, so efficiency matters and glory should be shared only if you have no other option.

In practice, the moment-to-moment decisions around when to push forward and when to hold back are where Phase 10 shines. It is a game that rewards quick pattern recognition, a little bit of memory, and the willingness to risk a risky move for a big payoff later in the round.

## Strategy and player interaction
Phase 10 is a social game first and a puzzle game second. It thrives on interaction—especially when players start using Wilds in mid-sentence to sub in a critical card or when a well-timed Skip card shifts the entire power dynamic at the table.

- Flexibility wins: be ready to pivot your plan if a card that could help you finish a future phase lands in your hand today. Forcing a phase early is tempting, but keeping a backup path can be a lifesaver if the deck denies you your first choice.
- Save Wilds: store your Wild cards for tricky mid to late phases rather than using them at the first sign of a small gap; you will thank yourself when you face a long run or a tricky mixed-set requirement.
- Timing of Skips: you should not blow all your Skips early; a well-placed Skip to stop a rival who looks close to finishing can swing the momentum, but wasted Skips early can come back to bite you when you are the one trying to recover.
- Remember the phase ladder: while you don't need to memorize every phase list on day one, having a sense of the typical steps you might encounter helps you prioritize which cards to hold and which to discard.
