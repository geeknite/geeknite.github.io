### The Card Spec and What “Played” Condenses to in real terms

In MTG, a card labeled as “played” is not a pristine, out-of-the-box experience. It’s a card that has seen action; it’s a veteran of a few battles, possibly host to the subtle scuffs that come from shuffling, fanning, and maybe a sloppy sleeves-swallowing moment or two. For basic lands, played condition often means: there could be minor edge wear, a small scratch here or there, maybe a tiny whitening near the bevel, and—most gloriously—foil has a little more personality than you bargained for. Foil basic lands tend to show more micro-scratches than their non-foil cousins because foil makes everything more visible, including the occasional smear of initiative from a mis-tapped mana pool.

The four in this set are not pristine museum pieces. ### Photography Tips: How to List Your Foils Without Embarrassing Yourself

When you list foils in played condition, you’ll want to deliver a fair representation. Folks who buy cards online rely on photos to judge condition, especially for foils where glare can hide wear. A few practical tips:
- Use a neutral light source and shoot from multiple angles to reveal edge wear and any scratches.
- Photograph against a clean, uniform background to avoid unnecessary color shifting that makes a set look worse or better than it is.
- Include a close-up of the foil edge and the back of the card to show the card count and surface feel.
- If possible, show the four cards arranged in a row, like a tiny mana bridge, so buyers can quickly gauge uniformity.

Here’s a little meta-tip for your listing: a small, tasteful watermark that highlights your Geeknite logo can help with trust and branding, but don’t overdo it. A subtle corner stamp is enough; you don’t want to obscure the cards, you want to advertise your taste, your reliability, and your sense of humor.
