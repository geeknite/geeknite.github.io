## Painting and basing: bringing the Justicars to life

Painting Palanite Justicars is a satisfying exercise in contrast. The armor begs for a stark color palette, and that’s where the fun begins. A classic scheme for these figures is a two-tone armor with a brass or copper trim that picks up light and adds a hint of ceremonial grandeur. A few practical tips to get you from “primer and wash” to “you can take them to a tournament”:

- Start with a clean white or black undercoat, depending on your preferred shading approach. A neutral gray undercoat can also work if you want a softer transition between panels.
- The main armor can be treated with a steel or gunmetal base, then highlighted with a lighter metallic dry brush. For a more dramatic look, you can apply a dark wash (Nuln Oil or a similar shade) to bring out the recesses and the weathered metal edges.
- For the ceremonial trim, try brass or copper tones. A quick metallic wash followed by a dry brush of a brighter brass yields a striking edge that catches the eye when you glance at the squad across the table.
- The fabric sections—like sleeves and tabards—look great in deep blues, rich burgundies, or muted grays. Use a couple of thin layers of highlight to create a sense of fabric texture without overpowering the metallic armor.
- Red or amber accents on visors or insignias can give the model a distinct “authority” vibe. Keep the color choice cohesive across all models so the squad reads as a single unit rather than a mismatched ensemble.
- Bases are an opportunity to tell your narrative. If your gang operates in a corrupted city sector, think rubble, twisted pipes, and the odd bit of glowing signage. You want bases that feel alive without stealing the show from the minis themselves. A light dry brush on the grit, plus a few tufts to hint at a neglected, feral environment, does the trick.

If you need a quick painting workflow, a simple checklist works well:
- Prime
- Base coat armor and weapons
- Wash metal and recesses
- Highlight edges
- Paint trim and insignia
- Final basing and varnish

As for the base material, ordinary cork or textured paste works fine. A tiny bit of gloss varnish on lenses and armor joints can add a little pop, but be careful not to oversaturate; you don’t want your Justicars looking like chrome-drenched opera stars—though I won’t pretend that isn’t tempting.

## Assembly and conversions: what to watch out for

The Justicars are designed to be assembled with reasonable ease, but there are a few gotchas worth noting so your build doesn’t derail into a head-tilting fiasco:

- Dry-fit before gluing. This is a rule I break with the same reckless confidence I approach a new video game release, but in miniature terms it’s a good idea. Dry-fitting the torsos with shoulder pads and weapon hands helps avoid awkward gaps later.
- Check alignment with the cape/coat folds. Some pieces align quite tightly, while others demand a touch of model-stretching artistry to avoid a cape that looks like it’s about to peel away from the armor.
- Weapon swaps can change the silhouette: swapping a boltgun for a shotgun or a baton not only changes how many shots you have but how the model reads on the table. If you like your squad to announce themselves from across the room, give a couple of Justicars longer weapons and a few with close-combat options.
- Green Stuff is your friend. If you want to add a little more personality—like a unique badge, a personal symbol, or a cape bearing the insignia of a district—you’ll appreciate the opportunities to sculpt little details here and there.

Conversions can be surprisingly manageable, but you’ll want to keep a consistent narrative thread across the squad. If you convert too much, you risk losing the overall Palanite identity. The sweet spot is customizing minor elements (headgear, insignia, and optional gen-u-ine badges) while preserving the clean, disciplined look that defines the Justicars.
