Strategy and Deck-Building
When faced with a 12-card pool, your deck-building approach should be both nimble and disciplined. Start by identifying a few anchor cards that look to synergize with others. If you have a combination of units and effects that reward you for playing together, try to build around that synergy. Don’t try to do everything; instead, aim to enforce a clear lane. For example, if you have a trio of cards that punch early and then enable a mid-game push, base your deck around that tempo and fill with utility cards that protect or accelerate your chosen line. The real magic of sealed is turning a handful of pieces into a functioning engine. It’s not about assembling a winner’s toolkit from a grab bag; it’s about weaving a thread that ties your cards together into a cohesive strategy.

A few practical tips to optimize your sealed run:
- Early game power: prioritize cards that can threaten your opponent in the first three turns. A strong early presence puts pressure on the opponent to answer rather than plan their own execution.
- Tempo management: guard against running out of steam. You want options on each turn, not a single window of opportunity that passes while you search for your last piece.
- Flexibility: include at least one card that can slot into multiple roles. If you draw it early, you want it to adapt as your deck pool reveals itself.
- Resource curves: in many Dragon Ball games, energy or resource generation is crucial. Cards that accelerate your access to energy or mana-like resources can be the difference between a brisk end and a slow drip of play.
- Board presence: your best decks rely on a few dependable interactions that establish control. If you can’t win the board in one or two turns, you should at least secure a meaningful board state that punishes a misplay by your opponent.
