### The Quick Start Rules (In Plain-ish English)
- Deal seven cards to each player. The rest becomes the draw pile.
- Flip the top card to start the discard pile.
- On your turn, you must play a card that matches either color or number of the top discard. If you don’t have a match, you draw one card (in most cases; some variants in this edition allow a quick-draw option that can mix things up).
- The Express twist adds a timer. When the timer runs out, the active player gets a small penalty that accelerates the next player’s decision-making.
- Special cards (Skip, Reverse, Draw Two, Wild) behave as in classic UNO with the added speed-boost: effects are applied quickly so that rounds move along.
- The moment you have one card left, you must shout “UNO!” or risk a penalty if someone calls you out too quickly.

If you’ve played UNO before, you’ll recognize the core bones. If you haven’t, the Express edition is an approachable entry point that encourages participation and minimizes downtime, which is a nice way to say you’ll maximize your screen time-watching and minimize the post-game debate about who ‘really’ won.

## Quick Start Guide (In Case You’re Skipping to the Fun Part)
- Unpack the box and lay out the cards in a face-down draw pile.
- Shuffle them and deal seven cards to each player.
- Flip the top card to start the discard pile.
- The player to the left of the dealer goes first by matching color or number. If you can’t match, draw one card. If that draw card makes a match, you may play it immediately or end your turn depending on the variant you’re using.
- Use the timer to force time-based decisions; if you’re calling Uno, do it with enthusiasm because the social vibe matters more than the precise timing.
- When you have one card, yell “UNO!” If someone calls you out for forgetting to yell UNO, you go through the gentle ribbing and keep playing.

This quick-start outline is intentionally concise. The actual rulebook is compact, which suits the edition’s design philosophy: you can learn fast, play fast, and still walk away with a story about that time your cousin accidentally played a Wild Draw Four onto themselves.
