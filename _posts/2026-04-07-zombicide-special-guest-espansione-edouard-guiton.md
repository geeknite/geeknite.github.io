## Design and mechanics: what Edouard Guiton brings to the table

Edouard Guiton’s influence on this expansion isn’t about re-inventing the wheel; it’s about giving the wheel a fresh coat of gloss and a couple of new spokes. The architecture remains recognizably Zombicide: cooperative play, crowd control, gear scavenging, and a timer-ticking sense of peril. Where this expansion shines is in two areas: character agency and moment-to-moment tension.

### Character agency and survivability
The new survivors come with distinctive abilities that feel thematic and mechanically coherent. You’ll find a mix of “gun-and-muts” style heroes and stealthy, utility-driven characters who rely on positioning and timing rather than brute force alone. The abilities encourage you to coordinate with teammates and to deploy your skills at just the right moment. The net effect is a slightly slower early game with more meaningful bursts of late-game decisiveness—exactly the kind of arc we gamers love when we’re trying to justify the cost of a new expansion.

### Encounter design and pacing
The new zombie variants test your plan-one plan-two approach. Some walkers flood the board in tricky clusters; others require you to chase a target across multiple zones. The pacing, in turn, rewards those who hold back just enough to pivot to a more favorable engagement. It’s medical-grade balance with enough chaos to keep it interesting. If you’ve played a lot of Zombicide, you’ll recognize the DNA but appreciate the fresh flavor.

### Thematic integration
Guiton’s aesthetic shines in the theme delivery. The new survivors and enemies carry a cinematic weight that translates well to the tabletop. The artwork and design choices align with the base game’s vibe while offering a more story-forward feel in the missions. It’s a blend that will satisfy players who crave narrative tension without dragging the game away from what makes Zombicide work: fast, co-op push-your-luck gameplay.
