## What is Phase 10?
Phase 10 is a rummy-style competitive card game where players attempt to complete ten distinct phases, each requiring a different pattern of cards. The rules are deceptively simple: draw, discard, and plan ahead. The catch is that you need to complete the current phase before moving on to the next, and your progress is visible to everyone at the table. It sounds like standard family game night, but with a timer and a dash of pressure that only siblings can provide. The Fundex vintage edition from 1998 is often celebrated for its colorful artwork, chunky cards, and the way it makes you shout 'Uno? Bah!' when someone lays down a phase you thought you had locked in. If you are a collector, the NEW SEALED label carries a whisper of 'this copy is still fresh enough to pretend you just opened it'—which is priceless for a retro-stocking stuffer.

### The Rules Are Simple, the Strategy Is Not
At its core, Phase 10 rewards planning. Each phase typically demands a specific combination—runs, sets, or a mix of both. You draw cards, discard, and aim to be the first to complete the phase. Then that player advances to the next phase, and the rest attempt to catch up. The rulebook can feel like a compact encyclopedia when you are new to the game, but it’s deliberately straightforward enough that a child can grok most of it over one game night. Yet the real joy comes from the moments of tension when a crafty opponent spins a last-minute wildcard or blocks a key card by discarding something you need.

### How It Plays: A Quick Boardroom of Chaos
The game typically supports 2–6 players, though your kitchen table may politely accommodate a seventh or an eighth with some careful seat arrangements and a small, improvised “zone of control” around the coffee mug. Each round, players draw a card and discard until someone completes the phase. Then that player advances to the next phase, and the rest attempt to catch up. The pacing is a delicate dance between squeaks of delight and groans of dismay as someone nails a perfect run while you’re still collecting two pairs. The intensity comes not from sheer randomness but from your ability to anticipate, bluff (in a wholesome family sense), and adapt when your draw pile betrays you with the exact cards you avoided stocking in your hand.
