### What is Sushi Go Card Game?
Sushi Go Card Game is a fast-paced, set-collection card game that plays in about 15 minutes and supports 2-5 players. The concept is elegantly simple: each round, players pass a hand of five to seven tiny cards, pick one, and reveal simultaneously. The rest of the cards go to the left, and you repeat until all cards have been played. At the end of each round you score points based on the types of sushi you’ve collected. The game ends after a set number of rounds, and whoever has the most points wins. It’s brain-boosting fast food for your brain: you’re drafting, you’re planning, and you’re occasionally praying that your opponent doesn’t snag that sashimi you desperately need for a perfect trio.

You’ll find cards with adorable illustrations of nigiri, maki rolls, sashimi, tempura, dumplings, wasabi, and pudding. Each card has its own little set of rules and scoring quirks, which is part of the charm and part of the challenge. The artwork is cute enough to elicit a smile even from the sternest of gamers, yet not so cute that your inner strategy nerd is scared away. It’s the kind of design that says, we take our snacks seriously, but not ourselves.

### Components and Setup
The card quality is solid for a game of this scale, with crisp art and clear iconography. The deck contains a variety of sushi cards, each type offering different scoring opportunities. The Wasabi card is a sneaky little multiplier; the Nigiri cards are classic power plays; Sashimi requires you to commit to a trio; Tempura rewards you for paired cards; Dumplings scale with how many you collect; Maki rolls give you a lead in a few points without being the entire victory—you’ll feel the tug of multiple scoring hooks pulling you toward a victory sweet spot.

Setup is a breeze. Shuffle, deal (the exact number of cards per player varies by player count: in a 2-player game you’ll get fewer cards than in a 5-player game), and pass to your left. The first round begins with everyone selecting a card to keep and then passing the rest along. This goes on for several rounds, and before you know it you’re a few minutes into your lunch break with a smug grin and a potential victory on the horizon. The true gift here is the minimal downtime. There’s no long tutorials, no complex rules, just quick-fire decisions that matter.

### How to Play: The Quick-and-Quirky Draft
1. Deal out a hand to each player. The number of cards per player depends on the player count (2-5 players, general range is 7-9 cards per player per round depending on variant). 
2. Each player secretly chooses one card from their hand and places it face-down on the table. 
3. When everyone has chosen, reveal cards simultaneously. Each player then adds the chosen card to their personal tableau and passes the remaining cards to the next player. 
4. Repeat until all cards from all rounds have been played. 
5. Score based on the card types collected. 
6. For a remixed takedown, you reset and deal again until the cookbook closes: typically after a few rounds, the bigger point cards start to swing the score in dramatic fashion.

The scoring basics are straightforward, but you’ll want to pay attention to the little rules that create big swings. Nigiri cards can be worth 1-3 points (with wasabi sometimes tripling their value if you’ve stacked three or more). Sashimi requires three cards for a big reward; Tempura pays off if you have pairs; Dumplings scale up with how many you’ve collected, giving you an interesting “go big or go home” mechanic. Maki points are awarded to the player with the most Maki rolls at the end of the game (and, depending on the variant, the second-most as a consolation prize). Pudding sits in a separate track and only matters after the final round, altering the end-game balance—an elegant touch that adds a subtle strategic layer without complicating the day-to-day drafting.

If you’re curious about the exact scoring table, a quick glance at the card icons usually tells you enough to get started. The first few plays are a learning process; after that, it becomes a quick dance of risk and reward: do you chase the big sashimi trio, or do you lock in the safe point cards and call it a round?

### Strategy Notes: How to Not Be the Goose of the Table
Sushi Go is not chess, it’s a high-energy, food-themed improv session. Here are some practical tips that actually help, especially if you’re playing with kids, friends who hover at 60 FPS in League, or coworkers who call “lunch break” a social event:

- Prioritize early on a few reliable sets. If you’re chasing late-game pudding, you’ll need to hold back on some card choices and protect your pudding track. However, don’t forget that some rounds you’ll want to push for quick points with nigiri and wasabi combos.
- Balance short-term points with long-term potential. A single sashimi with a wasabi can become a massive swing if you hold the right card at the right time—but you have to survive the passing gauntlet to get there.
- Watch your opponents’ passes. Since cards get passed each round, you can infer what they’re likely to chase. If someone is consistently aiming for Maki, you might want to snag a second Maki to deny their dominance, or pivot to a different scoring strategy entirely.
- Don’t neglect the dumplings. They scale nicely and can absolutely win you a late-game spike if you’ve been slow to score elsewhere.
- The pudding twist is flavor, not a main course. Pudding is a subtle end game shakeup; it’s worth keeping an eye on, but don’t let it derail your strategy in the earlier rounds.
