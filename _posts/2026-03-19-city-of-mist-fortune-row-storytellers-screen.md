Introduction: what is Fortune Row and why would a screen exist for it?
Fortune Row is a thematic corridor in the City of Mist universe—think of it as a glamorous, dangerous lane where gossip is a weapon and every NPC is a potential clue or a mirage. The screen for this supplement aims to do a few big things at once: hide your secrets, reveal quick reference material, and gamely remind you that you are, in fact, a city-drenched god-detective with a keyboard and a clipboard. The question is not whether a GM screen is necessary for Fortune Row; the question is whether this specific screen elevates your play, or merely elevates the price of your table stakes. We’ll explore the build, the content, and the way it behaves under the pressure of a live game night.

H2: Build quality and design philosophy
The first thing you notice when you pick up the Fortune Row Storyteller’s Screen is the weight. It’s not a flimsy, cheap promo; this is a three-panel, hard-stock screen with a reinforced spine and a matte finish that begs you not to press your nose against it while you interpret an NPC’s motive at 1:00 AM. The central panel is wide enough to hide your most important notes, with two narrower flaps on the sides to keep essential quick-refs in arms’ reach. The material feels dense enough to withstand the occasional accidental spill—the sort of accident that occurs when you’re leaning into a moment of dramatic revelation and a glass of something tumbles toward the session’s hot take.

The design philosophy here appears to be: we’re not sitting in a cafe pretending to be a noir film; we are letting you stage a noir film without you having to hold every card in your brain, at least until you have had enough coffee to pretend you’re a director with a cape. The artistry across the panels is consistent with the city-at-night aesthetic: a palette of deep blues, grays, and the occasional crimson highlight to signal danger or romance. The art isn’t overly busy, which is a relief; there’s enough texture to remind players that Fortune Row isn’t a fantasy window-dressing comic strip but a living, breathing boulevard where myths do business after dark.

H2: Layout, panels, and how it helps you cheat gravity (in a good way)
The screen’s interior is where most of the magic happens. There are quick-reference blitzes for the core mechanics of City of Mist, including tags, powers, and the frequently used Armory of Clues that the usual GM might pull from the atmosphere rather than a formal “rulebook.” You’ll find a neatly organized set of prompts for Scene Structure, Niceties (NPC dialogue cues), and a condensed list of Detective Work prompts to nudge players toward the next clue without you having to endlessly narrate exposition. The design’s strength lies in the way it anticipates what a GM needs to riff on in the middle of a tense moment—the moment when a player says, “What do I know about the Climax of Fortune Row?” and you reply with, “Everything you need, watered down to a two-second glance.”

There’s a small, tactile detail worth mentioning: a soft-grip edge along the panels helps you flip the screen without leaving fingerprints open to the players’ prying eyes. We all know GM screens get smudged with coffee and the existential dread of someone asking for “one more detail” about a villain’s lair. Fortune Row’s screen resists that fate with a gentle, utilitarian elegance. In short, it’s the kind of object that makes you feel you’ve earned your noir badge the moment you lift it up and swear you’re ready to govern a city’s shadows.

H2: Content inside the screen: what you actually get
The core product includes the standard GM-screen trifecta: a durable exterior, the three-panel interior with quick-navigate references, and a page in the Fortuna Row supplement that hints at new locations, factions, and twist-ready hooks. The inside content is designed to keep you from flipping through the book while your players are doing something dramatic. It’s not a full retelling of Fortune Row on the screen; it’s more like a curated cheat-sheet for the game’s most common moments—detective work, mythic triggers, and the social-engineering duels that flavor City of Mist sessions.

H3: Screen content and quick-reference tables
The quick-ref tables include:
- Clue Flow: How clues in Fortune Row typically emerge, what kinds of clues they are, and how to escalate them without stalling the scene.
- Tag and Power shorthand: A reminder of the Mist’s tag-based system and how to translate a character’s power into narrative leverage during a scene.
- Nemesis and Faction prompts: Short prompts to jog your memory about rival crews, factions, and their interests, so you won’t forget to weave a rival’s plan into a scene’s momentum.

H3: Fortune Row-specific flavor: tables you can actually use in session
Fortune Row-specific prompts lean into the supplement’s themes: glamour, corruption, secrets, and the dance of social maneuvering. Expect a handful of flavor bullets you can drop into dialogue, plus a couple of micro-scenes you can slip into an act to remind players that Fortune Row is not merely a backdrop but a living ecosystem of mythic influence. The risk, of course, is the temptation to treat the screen as a crutch rather than a catalyst. The best usage is to treat it as a camera you point toward a city’s twisted heart while your players furnish the rest of the frame with their choices.

H2: Value in play: is this a must-have or a nice-to-have?
When you’re running City of Mist, you’re often juggling three things at once: atmosphere, momentum, and rules clarity. Fortune Row’s Storyteller’s Screen aims to be a value proposition for GMs who want that extra edge, but you’ll only get the maximum return if you actually use the content in the live table. If you’re the kind of GM who prefers to memorize every stat and has a 46-page flowchart of scene hooks permanently pinned to the wall, you might find that the screen feels redundant. However, if you value a clean, immersive table presence—the ephemeral theatre that exists when a screen blocks sightlines but clarifies action—then Fortune Row’s screen acts as engine-blocks for your scenes in a good way. It invites faster scene transitions, less rummaging, and more dramatic tempo. It’s not a replacement for the core rulebook or the supplement’s pages; it’s a gatekeeper that says, “Here, take a quick look at the map, now go make the map meaningful.”

In terms of price-to-value, you’re paying for a combination of build quality, aesthetic, and the convenience of a well-curated quick-reference. If your group loves Fortune Row’s vibe, you’ll likely feel the screen pay for itself in fewer “table-lookup” breaks and more time for the players to negotiate, scheme, and narrate alongside the cops and criminals you’re about to unleash.

H2: Aesthetics and production values: does it look the part?
The Fortune Row screen nails the noir vibe with a tasteful balance of moisture-dark blues, city-glow purples, and chrome highlights that catch the eye without distracting the eye. It’s the type of product that photographs well and reads even better in person. The typography is legible from a distance, the icons are intuitive, and the art direction feels cohesive with City of Mist’s overall brand: a world where reality wears a mask, and the mask sometimes wears your patience. The cover art is a moody, street-lit silhouette that signals to players: you’re about to step into a glamour-drenched nightmare, and you’ll be fine as long as you remember to roll a Die of Fate every now and then.

H2: Artwork and art direction: a note on style and function
Fortune Row’s visuals lean into a silhouette-heavy aesthetic. Portraits and textures that might otherwise steal focus are kept deliberately secondary on the interior panels, which is a wise design choice. You want the art to evoke mood, not to shout “look how fancy we are” every time a player asks for a quick reference. The interior art focuses on lines, silhouettes, and the city’s hidden architecture—the kind of visuals that help players interpret a location by mood rather than by minutiae.

H2: How to use Fortune Row on your table: tips for a smoother session
- Place the screen so you can see your players clearly, but your notes are absolutely not legible to the players. The goal is to maintain an aura of control without appearing controlling.
- Use the quick-reference as your “start-of-scene” cue card. When a session warms up, glance at the top rows of the quick-ref to re-anchor the current scene’s stakes and to remind yourself which tags are in play.
- Leverage Fortune Row’s Scene Structure prompts to guide players toward the act’s turning points. Don’t be afraid to drop a couple of micro-scene prompts if the players seem stuck; a well-placed prompt can transform a standstill into a dramatic beat.
- Pair the screen with a concise wall-chart showing a handful of Fortune Row’s key factions and their motives. The wall-chart is a companion piece, not a replacement for the screen—together they create a rhythm for scenes and a sense of “the city as a living thing.”
- For online games, use a virtual screen in the same layout to preserve the same experience. The quick-reference sections translate well to overlays and chat prompts, so you can maintain momentum even when you’re not cradling a cardboard shield.

H2: Mechanics and the mythic layer: how Fortune Row reinforces the city’s vibe
City of Mist uses a tag-based system for both heroes and villains, where a given trait interacts with the world’s mythic fabric to shape outcomes. Fortune Row’s screen condenses this complexity into glance-friendly reminders. It also nudges you toward narrative uses of myths and legends that are particularly thematic to Fortune Row—where glamour meets danger and the city itself becomes an active, albeit capricious, character. In practice, this means you’ll be able to guide players toward scenes that explore the tension between social power and mythic consequence without pausing the game to search for a definition of a symbol or a power that everyone already half-knows by heart.

H2: Scenarios and stretch goals: how to stretch Fortune Row beyond a one-night stand
If your group craves long arcs, Fortune Row can function as scaffolding for an ongoing campaign arc rather than a one-shot experience. The screen’s layout invites you to insert recurring elements: factions who cross the players’ paths, a recurring symbol that keeps resurfacing as a clue, and a finale that hinges on a single choose-your-path decision. The screen doesn’t write the entire arc for you, but it does help you feel the pulse of Fortune Row’s themes—identity, power, and the price of myth—in a way that is consistent from session to session. If you’re an MC who loves long, multi-act reveals with DM-only notes that don’t derail the players’ momentum, the Fortune Row screen behaves like a steady hand on the steering wheel.

Compared to standard GM screens, Fortune Row’s screen differentiates itself through its thematic alignment with the Fortune Row supplement. Other screens often focus on generic mechanic references, which is fine for many games. But this one leans into the city’s mood, and that gives it extra utility if you’re running Fortune Row. H2: Value, alternatives, and where to fit Fortune Row in your shelf
If you’re a completionist who wants the entire Fortune Row experience gathered into a single physical object, this screen is attractive. If you’re more of a “start with the core, then decide if you need more flavor” type, you can probably get away with a digital quick-ref and a printed map of Fortune Row’s key locales. The screen’s strength is convenience and atmosphere, not exhaustive content. It’s the kind of accessory you’ll reach for if you’re playing weekly and want to preserve the vibe without flipping to the book each time a scene shifts from social intrigue to a stakeout. For those who prefer maximum storytelling density per dollar, there are other screens that emphasize raw stat data and long text blocks. Fortune Row’s strength lies where the city’s mood matters most: in the moment, not in the margin.

H2: Accessibility and inclusivity notes
For players who rely on color-coded cues or iconography to parse scenes quickly, the Fortune Row screen’s design is friendly but not overbearing. The visual language is clear and minimal enough to accommodate color-blind players if you expand with additional monotone or high-contrast cues on your table. The product’s physical form is accessible to gamers with moderate space; it doesn’t require a special table, a dedicated chair, or a separate lighting setup beyond a lamp and a properly dimmed room. As with most City of Mist products, the experience is less about a rigid rule crunch and more about a mood guide and a gonzo storytelling instrument. If your group prizes readability, narrative nudges, and a screen that respects the players’ ability to read the room rather than the tiny dotted grid on your napkins, Fortune Row will deliver in spades.
