## What is Sushi Go?
Sushi Go is a fast-paced drafting card game where players assemble the best combination of sushi dishes by selecting one card from a hand and passing the rest to the next player. The rounds repeat until all cards are played. Scoring hinges on collecting special sets of cards—nigiri, sashimi, maki rolls, dumplings, and a couple of one-off dishes—and if you’ve played any light drafting games before, you’ll recognize the rhythm instantly. The goal is simple: score more points than your friends without turning the table into a quiet, hungry disaster.

The core twist that makes Sushi Go feel special is the drafting mechanism without the heavy downtime of other games. You don’t spend three minutes debating whether to take the tuna or the egg custard; you take a tiny card, pass the rest, and try not to telegraph your entire strategy with the way you lick your lips at a particularly tasty-looking card. It’s face-friendly, family-friendly, and wants to be your friend even if you’re not sure you want to like it back.

## How to Play: The Drafting Dance
The main sequence of Sushi Go unfolds in rounds, each round consisting of players selecting one card from their current hand and passing the remaining cards to the next player. The chosen card is kept in front of you to form a personal platter of potential points. After everyone has chosen, cards are revealed and the round ends. The deck is passed again, with players picking a new card from the incoming hand and continuing the ritual.

### Step-by-step rhythm
- Start with everyone holding a hand of cards (the exact number depends on player count).
- Players simultaneously choose one card to keep and place it face-down in front of them.
- The remaining cards are passed to the next player (left in the first round, then alternating directions with each subsequent round).
- Once all cards are taken, it’s time to tally the score for the round or accumulate points for ongoing sets.
- Repeat until the deck is exhausted. The total score determines the winner.

This rolling carousel of choices is the heart of the game’s charm. It’s not about who makes the biggest individual play; it’s about the collective vibe of everyone trying to maximize their buffet while minimizing the psychic damage of their neighbor’s sneaky moves. And yes, it’s a little chaotic in the same way a sushi conveyor belt is chaotic: visually delightful, a bit fast, and always leaving someone with an empty plate and a hungry grin.

### The magic of the scoring system
Sushi Go balances simple math with a dash of strategic risk-taking. The scoring is designed to reward short-term gains (like a sashimi run) and longer-term plans (such as accumulating a large number of maki rolls). The major scoring categories include:
- Nigiri: Each nigiri card has a numeric value that you add up. If you can collect multiple nigiri of different values, you’ll feel like a culinary scholar calculating the subtle differences between salmon and tuna—except the stakes are just your pride and a tiny plastic pizza of points.
- Sashimi: Three sashimi cards earn you a bundle of points; two sashimi are basically a near-miss. The joke here is that you need the perfect three-card trio to score, which makes the pursuit feel oddly film-noir and extremely snack-consumptive.
- Maki: The player with the most Maki rolls receives points at the end of the round; the second-most also gets a smaller payout. The race for Maki often becomes a hilariously petty duel where someone tries to “help” by grabbing a random card only to sabotage themselves—and somehow still come out on top.
- Dumplings: These scale incrementally; the more you collect, the more points you earn. It’s a rewarding but cunning trap: if you’re a dumpling hoarder, you’re probably a serial optimist in real life as well.
- Dessert and Wasabi: Pudding, desserts, and Wasabi cards add flavor to scoring, with Wasabi providing an optional doubling effect on your next nigiri (a neat little twist if you can time it right).

The scoring is intentionally approachable, but not brain-dead. The trick is to remember that you’re not trying to win every category; you’re trying to optimize a few while occasionally throwing a curveball to others. It’s a balancing act of math, memory, and the occasional bluff—though you won’t need a poker face to succeed, you might want one to hide the obvious blush of delight when you snag a perfect sashimi run.

## Strategy and Tips for Connoisseurs and Casual Diners Alike
If you want to maximize your Sushi Go performance without needing a PhD in Mandarin board-game theory, here are some practical tips that won’t ruin your social life:

### Tip 1: Prioritize your sighs and sigh-ables
- Early in the game, spots for high-value Nigiri and Sashimi are scarce. If you see a sashimi trio in the same vicinity, it’s worth evaluating whether you can secure at least a part of that plan without losing your short-term gains elsewhere.
- Don’t chase a single big score to the exclusion of all else. Sushi Go is about tiny successes that add up; a few small, consistent points beat one big swing that leaves you empty-handed in later rounds.

### Tip 2: Manage your appetite for Maki
- The Maki race can swing the entire table’s morale. If you’re behind, consider pushing for Maki points to claw your way back. If you’re ahead, you can throw a few cards to snipe opponents instead of chasing your own Maki glory like a ninja cat.
- Remember that the top Maki value isn’t just about the number; it’s about timing and who’s trying to catch you. If you’ve built a lead, you can sometimes block others with a strategic discard or a well-timed Wasabi play.

### Tip 3: Wasabi is a sneaky multiplier
- Wasabi cards buff your next Nigiri, so you might want to pair Wasabi with your highest Nigiri value when the moment is right. However, don’t be greedy; Wasabi is a timing mechanism, not a shield for poor planning.

### Tip 4: Endgame awareness
- In later rounds, you’ll notice a thinning of choices. Keep your eyes on the table and try to predict what your neighbors are likely to need. This isn’t mind-reading; it’s pattern recognition and a little sleight of hand (in a purely non-magical sense).
- Don’t underestimate the power of simply ending a round with a balanced, multi-item set. A few well-timed picks can salvage a round when you’re slightly behind.

### Tip 5: Player count and pacing adjustments
- With extra players, the deck stretches, and you’ll experience more variability in what you can chase. Use that to your advantage by diversifying your strategy—picking a little from multiple categories can keep you flexible.
- With fewer players, you can be a bit more aggressive in chasing a specific set. The pace speeds up; you’ll need to stay sharp and not get carried away by your own sushi fantasies.

## A Quick Read for the Nerds: Rules, Edge Cases, and House Rules
- Edge case: If you’re playing with a Chopsticks card (and yes, some editions include it), you may be allowed to take two cards in one turn by using the chopsticks to swap in a new card—use this as a surprise engine along with Wasabi timing.
- House rule ideas: If your group loves spicy decisions, allow players to declare a “kitchen special” that doubles the value of the next sashimi you collect, but only if you announce it before your pick. It’s a risk-reward toggle that adds a pinch of drama to the buffet.
- Solo mode beware: Sushi Go works best with a table full of humans who talk back. If you want a solo variant, you’ll need to rely on a puzzle or an app-made opponent, but the charm of real-time banter and playful trash-talk is part of the recipe.
