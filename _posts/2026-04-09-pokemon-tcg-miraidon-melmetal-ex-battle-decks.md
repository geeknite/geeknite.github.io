### Miraidon ex deck: speed, tempo, and big finishers

- Primary attacker: Miraidon ex (the star of the show). Expect a big HP pool, a couple of energy-intensive attacks, and the occasional ability that accelerates energy attachment or draws extra cards. Think of Miraidon ex as the game-speed player—hit fast, force the opponent to respond, and push them into a corner where you can close with a high-damage burst.
- Supporters and draw: The deck leans on a handful of draw and search cards that let you find your key pieces quickly. You’ll see the usual suspects—Professor’s Research or calculate-your-own-turns cards—paired with some utility tech that helps you fetch energy or specific Pokémon during critical moments.
- Energy structure: The Miraidon ex deck tends to run a higher energy count with a focus on Electric or Lightning-type energy. The goal is to ensure your main attacker can power up in two to three turns while maintaining the ability to threaten with a one-turn finish when the window opens.
- Trainers in play: Expect a mix of disruption and board-control tools. You’ll see tools that let you switch between attackers, stall with item cards, or disrupt your opponent’s setup. This is where Miraidon ex’s tempo plan shines: you keep your board messy enough to force suboptimal plays on your opponent while you charge up your ultimate strike.

### Melmetal ex deck: the hammer strikes back

- Primary attacker: Melmetal ex is the deck’s raw power anchor. In this build, Melmetal ex often functions as the “cleanup hitter” that can slam the opponent’s board once you’ve created a favorable energy environment. Melmetal ex’s attacks are designed to maximize metal energy efficiency, letting you convert energy attachments into devastating returns.
- Metal energy economy: This deck_ is built around efficient metal energy usage, enabling a more consistent stream of attacks without losing momentum. The energy curve tends to be smoother, with less reliance on big, single-turn bursts and more emphasis on sustained pressure.
- Support lines: You’ll see trainers that speed up metal-type energy attachments, help you fetch specific Melmetal ex-related techs, and sometimes reduce the energy cost of certain attacks. The deck’s resilience comes from being able to throw in a second Melmetal ex or a sturdy alternative attacker to keep the pressure up.

Against heavier counter-attack decks, Melmetal ex’s heavy-hitting presence provides a fallback plan if your tempo stalls. The real magic happens when you start mixing and matching, adding tech cards, and stepping up your “play the right piece at the right time” game.

## Strategy blueprint: how to pilot each deck

### Miraidon ex: tempo-first warfare

- Opening plan: Your first priority is to establish a threat that forces your opponent to respond. This means getting your opening attacker in place quickly, while your bench remains flexible enough to bring in a stronger attacker once the setup is in motion.
- Mid-game pivot: As you begin loading energy, you should consider using draw/search tools to avoid dead hands. The aim is to keep a constant threat level on the board and avoid giving your opponent a clean turn to reset.
- Finishing touch: When you’re ready to seal the game, deploy the big finish with two to three energy attachments. Your opponent should feel pressured to respond immediately or risk being knocked out before they can stabilize.
- Common mistakes: Overcommitting to the first attacker and running out of fuel before you can bring your second line online. Also, under-utilizing trainer disruption in the mid-game can give opponents the window they need to rewrite the clock on your tempo.

### Melmetal ex: slow-burn hammering

- Opening plan: Establish a sturdy board presence and accumulate energy while defending your bench with a buffer of not-too-fragile attackers. The trick is to keep Melmetal ex in reach without giving your opponent a clean target to knock out early.
- Mid-game pivot: The deck thrives on sustainable pressure. If you can maintain a heavy energy on your main attacker while still impacting your opponent’s setup with disruption and proper energy management, you’re in good shape.
- Finishing touch: A well-timed Melmetal ex finish—backed by multiple metal-energy attacks—will often overwhelm the opponent by creating a knockout you can’t easily answer without the right disruption.
- Common mistakes: Overbuilding on one attacker and forgetting to draw into your second plan; or ignoring your energy economy, leading to stalling and a slower-than-desired kill line.

## Practical play tips and tricks

- Manage your energy like a tiny, well-intentioned librarian. If you attach too many energy on the wrong turns, you’ll end up with clunky hands and not enough options when you need them most. Balance speed with sustainability.
- Don’t be afraid to reset your board state when you have a particularly clean turn. A well-timed retreat and re-entry can save you several turns of pressure, which is often the difference between winning and watching your opponents celebrate a last-minute comeback.
- Build a micro-arc: think of your game as a personal arc of momentum. The first three turns should lay the groundwork for one decisive moment—your big, energy-laden attack that ends the game.
- Card-draw is king. In many matchups, the ability to fetch a key tool or a missing energy card determines the winner. If your draw hands feel dry, consider how to shuffle more efficiently or how to re-balance your bench for a better draw pool.
