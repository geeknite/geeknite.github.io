City of Mist: The Gilded Age plunges readers and players into a rainy, gaslit corridor of a metropolis where fortune and fraud wear the same fine suit. If you thought the original City of Mist was a love letter to comic book heroes wearing trench coats, wait till you see how gilded rails, steaming machinery, and velvet-piled crime lords weave a neon-lit web through the late 19th century. This is not a rehash in fancy threads; this is a full-on expansion that leans into the era’s glamour and grime, offering a playground where the mythic and the modern collide in spectacular sparkles of misdirection and possibility.

In this post, we will explore what The Gilded Age brings to the City of Mist table, how it changes (and sometimes evolves) the core mechanics, and whether you should add it to your campaign roster. So polish those fedoras, tighten those corsets, and let’s walk the foggy corners of a city where every neon reflection might be a story calling your name.

For quick context, City of Mist is a narrative RPG that centers on detectives with mythic alter egos—the Logos and the Mythos—trying to balance heroic impulses with the constraints of a world that often wants them to stay quiet. The Gilded Age leans hard into a period where every social club has a secret, every factory floor hums with the pulse of modernity, and every alley might hide a forbidden wonder. The thematic throughline is glamour married to grit, where the player characters are not just crime solvers but living embodiments of a city that has learned to monetize secrets and monetize heroism alike.

## H2: The Gilded Age setting — tone, mood, and political webbing
### H3: A city built on copper, brass, and broken promises
City of Mist, The Gilded Age, makes the late 1800s into a playground where the “city as a character” trope hits a modern intensity. You get the opulence of gilded railcar interiors, the clamor of street markets that smell like engine oil and fresh ideas, and the shadowy backrooms where industrial titans carve up the city’s future with a keen eye for monopolies and influence. This is not a clean, romantic noir; it’s a neon-drenched pulp where technology is just as much a weapon as a symbol of prestige.

If you enjoy the vibes of a gaslamp fantasy but want the stakes to feel instant and contemporary, this setting nails it. The Gilded Age gives you a city that feels lived in, with corners that hide both murder and mercy, where reformers stand next to river pirates and union organizers share table space with stage magicians who pull more than rabbits out of hats. It’s a city where myth meets industry, and every invention seems to carry a mythic promise or a mythic price tag.

> The mood is not just about crime and romance; it’s about accountability. Heroes aren’t flawless; they’re people who need to reckon with power and with the fact that the city is listening, recording, and that someone is always watching. The Gilded Age leans into these tensions with wit, a touch of sarcasm, and a generous dose of theatrical flair.

### H3: Glamour on the rails, danger in the stairwell
Expect social intrigue with Victorian-era elegance and a capital-C Confidence. The Gilded Age is built to host high-society balls and after-hours sabotage in the same volume, which means your investigations can swing between pulp capers and social satire in the same session. The new narratives encourage players to use their mythic identities to influence crowds, venues, and the flow of money, which is perfect for groups who like a little theatre with their crime-solving.

## H2: Mechanics at a glance — how The Gilded Age plays with your mythic identity
### H3: The tag system and the feel of improvisation
City of Mist runs on a tag-based resolution system. In The Gilded Age, the tag set expands with period-appropriate options such as Steamworks, Velvet Subterfuge, Gilded Politicks, and Carnival of Secrets. Tags are adjectives or nouns that describe your Logos and Mythos—who you are and what powers or constraints you wield. When you attempt an action, you select tags that apply to the situation, roll the dice, and read the result to determine success, failure, or a twist that adds color to the scene.

What The Gilded Age does well is to nudge you toward narrative outcomes rather than strict pass/fail results. Succeeding on a roll might reveal a secret door, or it might provoke a rival to reveal a hidden motive. A failure can push your group into a richer complication, turning the scene into a melodramatic chase through a copper-lined lobby or a tense negotiation in a back-alley speakeasy that masquerades as a theater.

### H3: New tags, new tensions
The expansion introduces specialized tags tailored to an era of industrial espionage, political machinations, and public spectacle. Some of the fresh lines you’ll encounter include: gleaming technology that borders on magic, factions vying for influence, and a social economy where appearances are almost more important than power itself. Players will discover that their Mythos is not just a persona but a lens through which to interpret the city’s temptations, while their Logos acts as a practical toolkit for navigating the constraints of law, custom, and public perception.

The synergy here is strong: you get a toolkit that invites dramatic interplay between stagecraft and street-level hustle. The Gilded Age loves a good reveal, and the tag system rewards you for thinking in terms of both dramatic flair and grounded consequence.

### H3: The balance of freedom and consequence
One of the trickier aspects of any City of Mist expansion is maintaining a balance between player agency and GM stewardship. The Gilded Age leans into that balance by giving players a stronger sense of agency in gilded social spaces—the clubs, salons, and private clubs where decisions ripple outward. At the same time, it increases the consequences of those decisions, particularly for characters who reveal their Mythos in public or who exploit a crowd to achieve a goal. The result is a game that can oscillate between high moral drama and over-the-shoulder hustle with equal ease.

## H2: New character concepts and archetypes in the Gilded Age
### H3: The Magnate, The Mask, and The Muse
The Gilded Age adds a handful of archetypes that feel at home in the era’s social ecosystem. The Magnate is a Logos-forward character who channels corporate influence, leverage, and the cunning of a boardroom into investigative prowess. The Mask is a Mythos-forward alter ego who uses disguise, stagecraft, and a captivating persona to manipulate public perception and gain access to restricted places. The Muse is a hybrid who uses art, music, or performance to influence people and bend events to their benefit.

These archetypes aren’t mere skins; they come with expanded moves, new tags, and a couple of bespoke gear options that fit the era’s vibe. Think of a Magnate who can legally leverage a city’s procurement process or a Mask who pulls strings in a theater where tickets grant access to a world beyond the ordinary. The Muse offers a toolkit for shaping crowd energy, turning public spectacles into leverage against rivals and enemies.

### H3: New gear, new sanctuaries
Expect a trove of gadgets and costumes that feel period-authentic yet fantastically adaptable. You’ll find clockwork devices, steam-powered tools, and elegant wardrobe pieces that double as concealment devices or display platforms for your Mythos. The expansion also expands on lairs and sanctuaries—places where your character can recharge, plan, or reveal the next big twist to the city’s hungry audience. The balance here is to keep the gear flavorful without letting it become a crutch. The best sessions use gear as a storytelling lever rather than a solution to every problem.

## H2: Sample hooks and session ideas for your gilded campaign
### H3: Hook 1 — The Clockwork Charity Ball
A soiree hosted by a philanthropic magnate doubles as a stage for industrial espionage. Someone is siphoning funds through a philanthropic façade, and your team must identify who weaponizes generosity while avoiding public scandal that could topple a fragile reform movement. The theater, the ballroom, and the clock-towered city hall become interchangeable stages where your Mythos Identity can shine or collapse.

### H3: Hook 2 — The Steamwright’s Promise
A public works project promises a new era of prosperity through a visionary steam-powered engine. However, your team uncovers a web of sabotage, corruption, and a rival faction that would rather see the engine turned into a symbol of fear. The adventure asks players to navigate technical mysteries, political intrigue, and the moral questions that accompany a machine that promises salvation while demanding tribute.

### H3: Hook 3 — A Masquerade of Truths
An annual masquerade ball invites every faction to reveal, or to bury, a truth about the city. Some truths are dangerous to expose, others transformative. Your character’s Mythos identity may be the key to extracting the truth, or it might become a weapon other players exploit to punish you for past misdeeds. Expect dramatic reveals, fast-paced social combat, and a final twist that reshapes the city’s balance of power.

## H2: GM tips — running a gilded, gaslit campaign with style
### H3: Lean into societal microcosms
The Gilded Age offers a rich layer of social politics. Use the city’s clubs, markets, newspapers, and political salons as living maps. Let factions respond authentically to the players’ actions, creating a city that seems to react in real time to the decisions the players make. Encourage your players to think in terms of influence, audience, and the risk of public exposure when the Mythos goes visible.

### H3: Balance spectacle with consequence
The expansion makes glamour a serious tool with real consequences. When a player uses a flamboyant Mytho-identity moment to sway a crowd, ask yourself how the crowd’s support translates into tangible effects, like favorable press or protected access to a restricted area. Conversely, if a plan fails publicly, let the fallout ripple through the city’s social fabric, affecting entertainment venues, business dealings, and even legal proceedings.

### H3: Stagecraft and pacing
The Gilded Age thrives on dramatic timing. Prepare scenes with a theatrical beat, allowing players to plan, improvise, and then execute in stylish, cinematic sequences. A ball can become a stage for a rescue, a reveal, or a chase. The pacing should feel like a Broadway act but with the grit of a street-corner exchange. If you’re new to running City of Mist campaigns, consider running a short arc first to establish the tone before diving into longer campaigns.

## H2: What I liked and what gave me pause
### H3: Strengths
- The era ambiance is thick and glamorous without tipping into pastiche. The Gilded Age captures the energy and contradictions of the era with wit and flair.
- Expanded tag options invite fresh character concepts without breaking the core identity of City of Mist. It feels like a natural extension rather than a break from the system.
- The suggested hooks provide ready-made dramatic engines that scale well from one-shot to campaign arcs.
- The art direction suggestions and era-specific gear are flavorful without being gratuitous; they serve as useful storytelling prompts rather than cosmetic add-ons.

### H3: Potential drawbacks
- The emphasis on social spaces may overwhelm players who prefer grim street-level grit. If your table loves hardboiled action, you may need to modulate the social-heavy scenes.
- Some players may yearn for more explicit mechanical depth. The tag-based system remains wonderfully narrative but can be opaque for newcomers unless the GM is patient and the group is comfortable with improv-driven outcomes.
- A few new archetypes can feel slightly over-tuned if the table doesn’t lean into the mythic aspect consistently. Lean into the mythos and logos balance and you’ll avoid these issues.

## H2: Where to start if you want to run The Gilded Age
- Decide your city’s flavor: Which gilded factions rule, and which social clubs are the real power centers? Create a short list of three to five key NPCs, each representing a different facet of the era—industry, press, reform, and crime.
- Establish a “mask” ritual: What is your character’s mythic cover, and how do they reveal or hide it in public? Establish a couple of recurring motifs that appear in their scenes to reinforce the theme of myth versus man.
- Plan your first arc around a single event that connects most factions: a grand ball, a new rail line, a contested theater production, or a controversial public policy. The event should force factions to interact, while giving players a platform to reveal their Mythos and Logos in meaningful ways.
- Use the recommended pregens if you want to test-drive the system without heavy prep. This will help you calibrate pacing and tone before you commit to a full campaign.

If you want a quick checklist to prepare your first gilded session, here are a few items to consider: a) a city map with major social hubs, b) a rotating cast of NPCs with clear motives, c) a takeaway clue that ties the event to a conspiracy, and d) a dramatic reveal that redefines the city’s balance of power. You’ll be surprised how quickly a room full of actors becomes a city full of secrets.

## H2: Final verdict — should you buy City of Mist The Gilded Age?
If you crave a pulp-noir experience that blends Victorian glamour with neon-lit ambition, The Gilded Age is a welcome addition to the City of Mist family. It preserves the core freedom of the system while enriching the setting with era-appropriate texture, new archetypes, and narrative hooks that are as theatrical as they are could-be-true about the city’s glittering veneer and its dark underbelly. It’s not a complete overhaul; it’s a thoughtful enhancement that encourages larger-than-life storytelling without abandoning the grounded, character-driven investigations that make City of Mist special.

If you are new to the system, you can start with the core mechanics and then sprinkle in the Gilded Age’s delicious hints at your own pace. If you’re a long-time fan who loves the dramatic contrasts between public spectacle and private chaos, you’ll likely find The Gilded Age to be a sandbox that expands what your table can become: a stage on which heroes balance their own myths against a city hungry for both.
