Installation and compatibility
-------------------------------
This winch is designed to be installable on a broad range of vehicles with standard winch mounts, but there are a few caveats:
- Mounting pattern compatibility: Most ATV/UTV winch mounts use a universal four-bolt pattern and a symmetrical mounting plate. Ensure your mounting plate aligns with the winch’s footprint; mismatched holddown bolts can lead to unwanted vibration, which is a bummer when you’re trying to winch yourself out of mud, not have a hardware store unsettlingly close to your ride.
- Electrical load: A 12V system will supply the motor, but check your battery capacity for long pulls. If you’re using the winch repeatedly in a session, you’ll be reminded that your battery isn’t a magic power orb. Some owners pair a dedicated winch relay to manage heat and prevent battery drain. If you’re not sure, consult your vehicle manual or a competent mechanic.
- Wiring: Use proper gauge wiring (usually 6 AWG to 4 AWG) for the power leads, and keep the wiring clean and dry. Heat shrink and proper zip-ties help maintain a tidy setup that won’t threaten your wiring harness when you’re navigating brush and branches.
- Fairlead and rope routing: The included Hawse fairlead is a good default choice for a straight rope path. If your setup demands a roller fairlead, you’ll want to confirm compatibility with the drum width and the rope diameter. Avoid sharp bends that can kink the rope and undermine its strength.

Maintenance and longevity
--------------------------
The All-CARB model benefits from routine care that’s actually enjoyable if you’re a little motorhead with a soft spot for gadgets:
- Regular inspection: Check rope for frays, nicks, or broken strands. A damaged rope can fail unexpectedly at the worst moment (like when you’re halfway up a hill and realize you forgot your coffee).
- Clean and dry: After use in mud or rain, wipe down the winch housing and allow the rope to dry to prevent corrosion in metal parts around the drum. Synthetic rope handles moisture fairly well, but water and grit can accumulate in crevices if you aren’t careful.
- Trigger discipline: Don’t abuse the remote by yanking it repeatedly while the load is not moving. It wears the internal battery and can degrade its range over time.
- Heat management: Winching generates heat, especially when dealing with longer pulls. If you’re in a sticky mud situation that requires multiple short pulls, give the motor a moment to cool and avoid running it into a thermal throttle. Some units include a thermal cutout—if yours has one, you’ll notice the motor temporarily pausing to protect its internals.

Safety first, always
---------------------
Winches are powerful, and with power comes responsibility. Here are a few safety guidelines to keep your adventures from turning into a montage of awkward videos:
- Wear gloves and eye protection when dealing with rope and accessories. Synthetic rope doesn’t slice like steel, but it can still cause rope burn or snap back in a bind.
- Stand clear of the line’s angle during operation. A loaded rope coming taught can snap back with unexpected force if the load shifts.
- Use a winch dampener or blanket over the line to reduce recoil and keep yourself safer from a rebound in case of a failure somewhere down the line.
- Always lock the vehicle’s transmission, place the parking brake if possible, and use appropriate anchors (tree straps, ground anchors) to prevent the vehicle from rolling unexpectedly.
- Do not stand directly in the line of pull. It’s a simple rule, but one that saves you from a sudden rope-related surprise.
