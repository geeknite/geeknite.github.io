There are at least 15 cards described as mint gradable. What does that mean in practical terms? You’re looking for cards with centering that sits nicely within typical tolerance, crisp corners, clean edges, and minimal surface flaws. A practical approach is to request sample photos of the top five mint gradable candidates and compare them against known grading criteria. Some buyers go so far as to pre-score potential grade-readiness, using a simple rubric: centering, corners, edges, surface, back gloss. If you can validate a strong start on 3–5 cards, you’re already in a good place for a grading plan.

### NM Holos

NM holos typically show minimal surface wear, bright foiling, clean alignment, and no major creasing. In a mixed lot, you’ll see a spectrum—from the common holo cards that are nice to own but not massive value drivers, to a few rarer foils that can sparkle in a collection or even fetch a healthy resale price. ## The Grading and Value Debate
Mint gradable is a phrase used frequently in the DBS market to signal grade potential without guaranteeing a specific score. The reality is this: grading is an investment, and results vary card by card. Here’s how to think about it without losing your mind or your wallet.

- Grading costs: Submitting cards to services like PSA or CGC typically costs somewhere in the range of a few dollars to several dollars per card, depending on the service tier and speed. For a batch of 15–25 cards, you can expect a few hundred dollars in submission fees, plus shipping and insurance.
- Turnaround times: Grading can take weeks to months. If you’re chasing a quick flip, this is something to consider. If you’re building a collection, the waiting game can be part of the fun.
- Potential uplift: A high-graded hit (9.0 or higher) can significantly improve resale value and personal satisfaction. A few 9.5s or 10s could turn the lot into a tidy return, while a batch of 8.0s and 8.5s will typically result in slower, steadier gains.

The big takeaway: don’t bank on grading as the sole driver of value. Treat grading as a strategy, not a guarantee. If you’re purchasing this lot primarily for holos, you should still care about the potential play value and the visual appeal of the holo set, since these add a lot of non-monetary value to a collector’s binder.

## Buying Logistics: How to Shop Smart
Before you click the buy button, map out a simple plan to avoid buyer’s remorse. Here are practical checks to include in your due diligence checklist:

- Photo quality and card scan depth: Request clear, close-up images of the top 20 cards, especially the mint gradable set. Zoom in on centering and corners. If the seller can’t provide decent images, don’t buy blind.
- Confirm the mix: Ask for a rough breakdown of the 15+ mint hits and the 100+ holos. A vague listing is a red flag; a precise breakdown helps you estimate grading potential and holo resale value.
- Authenticity checks: Look for telltale signs of counterfeit patterns in the holo foil, font, card back, and set icon. If something seems off, ask for more examples or walk away.
- Return policy and protection: Ensure there’s a return policy if the condition isn’t as described. Tracking and insurance are essential for a bulk purchase like this.

## Practical Tips for Traders and Collectors
- Negotiate with a plan: If you can show a laser focus on grading potential and holo density, you’ll have a stronger negotiating hand. Suggest a split approach: a portion of the lot at a lower price for bulk sale, and a tiered offer for graded hits.
- Build a grading plan: If your goal is to submit, pick 15–25 of the strongest mint gradable candidates first. Consider a two-batch submit strategy to spread risk and costs.
- Track your costs: Create a simple ledger that includes the lot price, estimated grading fees, shipping, and insurance. This helps you decide whether the potential resale value justifies the upfront investment.

## Storage, Handling, and Display Tips
- Protect the holos with sleeves and top loaders. For maximum protection, keep them in a binder with acid-free pages that have individual pockets. This helps prevent edge wear and keeps the foils unblemished.
- Climate matters: store in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight. UV light can fade holo finishes over time and reduce the visual pop.
- Organization helps: separate mint gradable hits from holos, and then group by set or rarity. A clean catalog makes future grading or selling a smoother process.
