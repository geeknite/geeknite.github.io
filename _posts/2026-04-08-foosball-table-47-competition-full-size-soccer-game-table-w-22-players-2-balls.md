### Maintenance and care

Maintenance is not glamorous, but it pays off in spades when you’re mid-tournament and the ball refuses to bounce as you’d expect. Lubricate the rods sparingly with a purpose-made rod oil or silicone product designed for sports equipment. This keeps the rods moving smoothly and reduces resistance caused by friction, especially during longer sessions. Keep the playing surface clean with a soft cloth to avoid grit that could scratch the finish or alter ball bounce.

Tighten the screws and check the levelers periodically. A wobbly table is a recipe for chaos and gross underhand serves from the wrong side of the table. The casters or legs, depending on your model, may require occasional tightening to prevent drift. If you notice persistent misalignment, you may want to re-level the table and check for any structural issues in the cabinet or the legs.

If you are the kind of person who collects spare parts, consider stocking a few common replacements: extra balls, a spare net, and perhaps a couple of alternative handles in case your crew prefers a different grip. Spare parts aren’t always essential, but they save you from a lot of frustration during a party where the table suddenly refuses to cooperate just when the final buzzer sounds.

## Maintenance and Longevity (Deep Dive)

A long-lived foosball table requires a simple maintenance routine. The big-ticket items to watch are the rods, the bearings (or bushings) within the rod housing, and the ball’s bounce surfaces. Lubrication is essential but do it sparingly. A dense layer of oil can attract dust and grit, turning your smooth play into a gritty grind. A light touch every few weeks or after heavy use will keep things moving. Inspect the bearings for wear; if you notice wobbling that doesn’t respond to leveling or tightening, you may have worn bushings that need replacement. Replacement parts are typically available through your retailer or the manufacturer’s service portal.

Clean the playfield with a soft damp cloth; avoid harsh cleaners that could degrade the finish. If you play in a humid environment, check for any warping or swelling of the cabinet material. If you see signs of humidity damage, consider a dehumidifier or protective cover to preserve the surface.

Storing and transporting the table responsibly is another piece of long-term care. When moving, two or three people should handle it to avoid bending or stressing the cabinet. If you own a garage lab or a workshop that doubles as a storage area, keep the table out of extreme temperature fluctuations to extend its life.
