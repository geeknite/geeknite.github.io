## What this insert is and isn’t

This particular insert is a molded item—think MDF or polymer-based foam with plastic dividers—designed to fit inside the base Zombicide: Black Plague core box. It’s not a fidget toy. It’s not a novelty dice tower with a zombie face embossed on the side. What it is: a purpose-built storage and organization solution that aims to reduce setup time, protect minis, and keep everything in its right place so you’re not spending five minutes three-dimensionally calibrating where to put your swords and shields while a dozen undead slowly win through in your headspace.

Two quick disclaimers before we go deeper:
- It’s designed for the base game and at least some expansions, but not every expansion fits perfectly out of the box. If you’re a collector who owns every add-on ever released for Black Plague, you’ll want to check compatibility notes before purchasing.
- The aesthetic is practical rather than flashy. If you’re hoping for a display case-like centerpiece, you may want to look at something more artisan. If you want a workhorse that makes setup feel like a well-oiled heist, you’ve come to the right place.

## Build quality and materials

A good insert should feel sturdy enough to survive the occasional clumsy finger-pick during a tense moment, and lightweight enough to ship without requiring a forklift. The standard insert for Black Plague typically uses a combination of:

- High-density foam or MDF for the main trays
- Snap-fit dividers to keep components from shifting during transport
- A top lid or cover to protect minis when closed

In practice: the insert I tested did a solid job of withstanding regular play sessions. The trays click into place with a satisfying snap, and the walls of each container are thick enough to resist minor drops or enthusiastic dice throwing (you know who you are). The bottom compartments for tiles and bases feel roomy enough that you don’t have to shove everything back with the precision of a surgeon after a close defeat. The only caveat is that some parts can be a touch snug for certain miniature sizes—if you’ve got miscast or slightly larger-than-expected zombie minis from a particular expansion, you might need to remove a tray and reconfigure a bit.

One thing to note about materials: durability is good, but if you’re a traveler who tosses their game into a car trunk with reckless abandon, you’ll want to consider a protective travel case with the insert inside. The insert is not a toy chest; it’s a battlefield organizer. Treat it with reasonable care and you’ll get years of service.

## Layout and organization: what sits where

This insert shines most in its layout. It’s designed to split the box into zones that mirror how you play: core game components on one side, zombie spawns and road tiles in the middle, and player boards and markers tucked into a side pocket or two. The idea is simple: when you open the box, you can almost immediately locate the levers you need to pull to start a match instead of rummaging through the box like you’re searching for a magical relic.

- Cards and tokens: The insert includes shallow slots for stat cards, equipment cards, and certain tokens. The slots are sized to prevent bending or curling, which is important because card sleeves have a very healthy sense of entitlement.
- Miniatures: A dedicated rack or tray keeps fighters, zombies, and monsters separated by type. The result is a neat, tiered display rather than a chaotic pile-in. If you’re the kind of player who polishes minis between turns, you’ll appreciate not having to root around for the right base size.
- Terrain and tiles: A larger tray or two holds terrain tiles and board pieces. The design prevents tiles from sliding into the realm of the void—also known as the bottom of the box where tiles go to escape from reality.
- Dice and markers: A small, easily accessible pocket for dice, wound counters, and status markers prevents the dreaded “Where did that d6 vanish to?” moment mid-game.

The layout does a good job of aligning with the game’s natural flow. You pick the board, you lay out tiles, you grab the zombie bag, you roll dice, you slay wretched undead, and you do it again when the next wave shows up. If you run a multi-player campaign with lots of expansions, you may want to do a one-time reorganization to optimize for your play group, but the basic design holds up well enough that you won’t feel compelled to redesign the entire insert just to add a few extra tokens.

## Setup time and gameplay impact

One of the core reasons to invest in a good insert is to reduce setup time. The more you can pre-sort and pre-place, the faster you’re off the starting tile and into actual gameplay. In practice, the insert shaved a noticeable amount of setup time off our first playthrough, and the reduction persisted across subsequent sessions. You’ll still need to shamble around a bit when you pull new expansions or refitted tokens, but the heavy lifting—sorting through a mountain of components—is largely automated by the layout.

During our playtests, we timed a standard setup before and after installing the insert. The before time hovered around 12–15 minutes for a mid-sized scenario with two players, while the after time dropped to roughly 6–9 minutes. That’s not just faster; that’s a dramatic reduction that makes you feel like a veteran of your own dungeon-crawl empire.

The trade-off is not purely speed. Some players report that the fixed slots can make changes in expansions feel a bit restrictive. If you’re the kind of person who loves shuffling components around like a magician performing card tricks, you might find the fixed nature of the trays limiting. However, if you lean toward consistent setup and less frustration, the efficiency gains are real and measurable.

## Maintenance and long-term durability

In the world of tabletop accessories, longevity is the quiet killer. A flimsy insert might be glamorous on day one but crumble into a pile of dust after a year of giggling players and the occasional accidental drop. The insert under review holds up well to regular play, and its materials resist denting and warping under normal humidity and temperature. That said, you’ll want to treat it with a bit of respect: avoid leaving it in direct sunlight for extended periods (the zombie paint job on your miniatures will thank you) and keep sharp or heavy objects away from the edges to prevent snags.

If you live in a high-humidity environment, a microfiber cloth wipe-down every so often will keep the surface looking pristine. A little moisture control goes a long way toward preventing warping. If you’re the kind of player who worries about scratches on the tray lids, there are protective sleeves you can apply that won’t interfere with the flush fit.
