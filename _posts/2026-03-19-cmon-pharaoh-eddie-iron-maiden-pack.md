## Design and craftsmanship
### Sculpt details
Eddie as a Pharaoh is a curious but very real marriage of two worlds: the iconic Eddie design that has haunted metal fans for decades and the glamour of an ancient Egyptian pharaoh. The crown features the characteristic coil-and-snake motif that fans recognize, but there’s a deliberate CMON-ization with extra ribs on the nemes that catch highlights in a way that’s perfect for back-of-blast shots on social media. The build has a few small pegs and sockets that align the pieces with satisfying click-fit, which reduces the anxiety of “did I glue this on straight?” You didn’t, not if you check the alignment marks.

The base height adds a little elevation, letting Eddie stare down at your opponent with divine smugness. This is not a flat base with a tiny figure; it’s a proper scene, which makes it ideal for display when you’re not rolling dice.

## Assembly and painting workflow
### Tooling and fit
Dry-fitting is essential here. The scepter’s chain and the cobra’s tail need to align with the arms for a natural look. A micro file helps with slight reduction on glue seams, but you’ll want to avoid over-sculpting the gemstone inlays. A steady hand is the friend here, not brute force.

### Painting strategies
A few recommended steps:
- Prime with a neutral gray to capture both warm golds and deep blues.
- Block golds first, then shading with a dark brown wash; highlight with bright gold.
- Paint the blue sections in rich lapis with a couple of glaze layers to avoid flat color.
- Use a pale ochre wash on the base to simulate desert grime before applying a dry brush of bone to the raised stones.

### Basing and diorama finishing
Use textured sand to simulate dunes around the base, and consider tiny tufts for a desert wind effect. A thin gloss varnish on the pharaoh’s jewelry can give the impression of metal in the light.
