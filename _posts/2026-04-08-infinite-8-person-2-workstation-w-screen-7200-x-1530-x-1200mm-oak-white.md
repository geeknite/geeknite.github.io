### A note on dimensions and space planning
At 7200 mm long, this is a desk for teams that do not like to stand shoulder-to-shoulder with their neighbors. In real-world spaces, you’ll want about 1.2 to 1.5 meters of clearance behind the back row, at least as a rough measure, and ensure doorways won’t mind a ceremonial parade of chairs being rolled through on assembly day. The depth of 1530 mm provides generous knee space and allows comfortable seating for chairs with deeper seats or modest armrests. The height of 1200 mm is a standard-ish ergonomic baseline for adult office tasks; if your team has a lot of keyboard-centric work, you might want to propose adjustable chairs and monitor arms to keep screens at eye level without craning the neck excessively.

## Ergonomics and user experience: comfort is not negotiable
Ergonomics on a desk this large can make or break the experience, especially when eight bodies are moving around it and two workstations demand at least some degree of mutual space. The stack height is generous enough to permit comfortable leg movement under the surface, and the space under the desk is also well thought out for feet and rolling chairs. That is not to say it’s perfectly optimized for every body type; adjustability with chair height, monitor arms, and if possible an under-desk footrest becomes essential for long sessions.

Two workstation bays mean two different micro-environments within a single table. If one person is into the ergonomic rainbow—a monitor a bit higher, keyboard lower, chair back at 110 degrees—while the other person wants a more classic setup, you can tune them independently. The key to comfort here is to use proper monitor height and distance. The general rule of thumb remains valid: screens should be at eye level and about an arm's length away. On a long desk like this, you could pivot chairs to face the primary monitor or you could commit to a single central video wall in some configurations. Either way, you want to avoid neck strain by ensuring the screens are arranged to be readable without tilting the head constantly.

In practice, we found that two larger monitors per side were a comfortable default. If your group uses laptops, docking stations give a clean surface to maintain the minimal appearance we all secretly crave. The integrated cable management channels help keep cable chaos to a minimum, which is crucial on a desk that invites a thousand little cables to make mischief every day. A tidy desk is a productive desk, and in a space this long, even a small amount of cable clutter can feel like a daily performance piece.

## Tech integration: screens, power, and the great cable garden
The W Screen feature hints at a modern approach to a desk that expects digital work to be a team sport. The actual integration depends on the configuration you choose, but the desk ships with enough cable management to reduce the chance of tripping over a stray HDMI or USB-C cable. Central power distribution is all about reducing the number of wall outlets exposed, and that means you can reduce the number of unsightly power strips and have a cleaner look overall.

Two good practice tips for this desk: first, plan for power density. Eight users can draw a lot of power if they’re all running monitors, docking stations, and a few peripheral devices. If you can, plan for three or four outlets per side and consider a modest UPS if your space supports critical work. Second, choose a monitor arm setup that keeps screens close to the main line of sight without crowding the user’s space. This is where the W Screen concept shines—by allowing flexible positioning while maintaining a stylish, unified look.

### On maintenance for the screen components
If you opt for the screen integration, keep the display surfaces clean and avoid knocking over cables when you adjust chairs. The last thing you want is a screen that slides off its mount because of a stray chair leg or a knee sliding under the desk during a heated debate about color grading. Regular checks on cable tension and anchor points will extend the life of the system. Treat the screens as you would treat a high-end speaker or fragile glassware: with a little respect, and a lot of habit.
