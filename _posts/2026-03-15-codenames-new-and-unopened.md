## Setup: Time, Pace, and the Sensory Experience
Codenames is famously fast to set up, which tracks with the game’s DNA: a grid, two teams, and a flurry of word associations. With a brand-new copy, setup is a minute or two of shuffling, laying out the grid, and debating who will be the first to draw a truly heroic clue. The orientation of the grid often becomes a minor skirmish: which side’s words face your team, and who gets the first dramatic turn? In practice, you’ll want the color distribution oriented so that your team has the clearest view of their target words, and the other team gets the best view of theirs—because this is real life, not a chessboard.

For new buyers, here’s a quick pro-tip: run a practice round before the in-laws arrive. You’ll save yourself from the dreaded family moment where your aunt’s clue cascade becomes a legend that haunts family reunions for decades.

### The Word List and How the Clues Work
The heart of the game is the word list. Each word on the board is a clue ladder that the spymaster must carefully ascend for the team. Clues come in two parts: a one-word hint and a number indicating how many related words there are on the board. The challenge is balancing breadth with precision: a clue that ties too many words can hand victory to the other team, while a too-niche clue may yield a single lucky guess.

When a spymaster lands a solid clue, the table erupts in a chorus of whispers and careful nods: is it a sly reference to an obscure pop culture tie-in, or a sharp semantic link that anyone with a decent vocabulary can catch? The new copy’s crispness helps not only with reading the cards but also with perceiving how the clue lands across a room of different ages and backgrounds. If someone leans on pop culture, you’ll see the room tilt between admiration and strategic skepticism. That’s Codenames in a nutshell: vocabulary, social intuition, and a dash of competitive misdirection.

## Practical Tips for New Buyers
- Start with one house rule: allow one guess per clue, with a potential third guess on a safe word if the clue is especially juicy. This keeps early rounds brisk and fun without turning into a logic marathon.
- Keep the lights on. The new copy’s visuals pop, and better lighting helps you read even the more obscure words without squinting at tiny print.
- Dedicate a tidy space for cards and tokens. A clean rack reduces table chaos and keeps everyone focused on clues rather than card pile gymnastics.
- Use a timer for pace control only if your crew wants it. A 60-second martini round can be a blast, but you don’t want a velocity race that steals the social warmth.
- Rotate the spymaster role to spread the thrill and the terror of clue giving. Everyone deserves a turn at both ends of the spectrum.
