## How to play in roughly 15 minutes per round, or two hours if you get distracted by your own clever banter

Sushi Go! Party is designed to be fast, delicious chaos in a friendly-society way, and surprisingly strategic once you catch the drift of the rotating menus. The game supports up to eight players; you’ll likely spend more time groaning at a neighbor’s pick than actually savoring a bite of sushi in real life. Here’s the core rhythm:

### Setup
- Shuffle the deck and deal each player a hand of cards according to player count.
- Place the first round’s menu in the center. The menu defines how the points will be earned this round.
- Each player keeps a personal score track (or uses a notepad if you want to play the “we forgot the score app” game).

### Draft Phase
Each round plays out like this:
- Everyone selects one card from their hand and keeps it secret for the moment.
- When all players have chosen, everyone reveals their card simultaneously.
- Cards are then passed to the left; direction can vary with house rules, but standard is left-to-right.
- Repeat until all cards in the hand have been drafted.

This rhythm creates social tension without requiring a referee. You get to savor a moment of suspense as someone peeks at a card and then tries to feign innocence about their intention. It’s an exercise in polite cunning that results in big laughs when someone’s plan collapses because a rival grabbed the last gunkan wrap.

### The Reveal and Score
After all cards are drafted in a round, players reveal and score according to the menu’s instructions. The rotating menu is the star of the show; it keeps the game feeling fresh and ensures you’re always adapting rather than executing a fixed plan. Some rounds reward clear set collections (like maki groups or nigiri), while others encourage diversity or the chase for a rare card that’s easy to miss.

We won’t list every rule here—part of the charm is letting the current round’s goals guide your choices. Think of it as a graphical scavenger hunt where you assemble a tiny gallery of sushi under a time pressure that’s more playful than punitive.

#### A quick flavor of the scoring flavor
- Some menus reward the most of a single dish type. You’ll find yourself deciding between going all-in on nigiri or spreading your bets to grab small sets.
- Other menus reward sets (more maki, sashimi, or nigiri combos). This is where memory and inference come into play: who drafted what earlier, and who is likely to cut a deal by the end of the round?
- A few bonus cards or tiny penalties spice things up. The interaction level rises quickly, and the tension is reminiscent of a high-stakes round of pie-eating at a school fair.

The balance is inviting. New players latch onto the drafting idea quickly, while veterans appreciate the emergent strategy provided by the rotating menus. It’s a gentle push-pull that keeps your brain engaged without requiring a PhD in combinatorics.

## Menu cards and scoring variability: the real spice

The rotating menu is the lifeblood of Sushi Go! Party. Each round introduces new scoring aims, which means you aren’t just chasing “the most points,” you’re chasing “the right kind of points” for the current round. The menus can emphasize variety, speed, or precision; sometimes a no-duplicates rule nudges players toward diversification, while other rounds reward chasing particular combos. This dynamic approach ensures that no two plays feel identical, even if you play three times in a row.

Here’s how this design pays off in practice:
- It rewards adaptability. You will often have to pivot mid-round as the menu reveals itself.
- It scales with player count. More players means more competition for the same cards, which raises tension without inflating playtime.
- It lowers the barrier to entry. New players learn the current menu quickly and can join in the fun without a long lecture about dozens of card types.

## Strategy and tactics: what to optimize for, and what to ignore

What makes a good Sushi Go! Party player? Flexibility, social read, and a willingness to pivot on a dime. Here are some practical guidelines that help translate flavor into points:
- Prioritize the current round’s menu. Chasing a long-term plan can be a trap if the current scoring rewards variety or a particular combo that your neighbors are racing toward. Be prepared to switch gears mid-round.
- Watch your neighbors, not just your own cards. A large portion of scoring comes from what others collect. If you notice someone hoarding a dish, consider blocking them or pivoting to a path that intercepts their plan.
- Manage tempo. The drafting mechanic means you control the pace of what cards come into play. In bigger groups you can manipulate tempo to increase uncertainty and create dramatic reveals.
- The chopsticks card (in house rules) adds a twist. It’s a nice late-round tool for revision, but leverage it well; a misused chopsticks can stall your own momentum.

Experienced players will find layered complexity in the menus. A small misstep may look costly, but there are often alternative routes to victory. The thrill is that the scoring system rewards resilient, adaptable play more than brute force.
