What you get in the box (Components)
The Troy & Evil Troy promo arrives with two miniatures that match the Black Plague aesthetic—Troy, the sturdy knight, and Evil Troy, the twisted doppelganger who looks like he just watched a horror-movie montage and decided to adopt a smirk. You’ll also find two character cards with unique abilities, two stand-first base inserts so the miniatures sit perfectly on the map, and a compact promo deck that slots into your existing Zombicide equipment schedule. The promise here is simple: more personality without more book learning.

Setup and integration: the devil is in the details
If you’ve run Black Plague before, you’re basically halfway to knowing Troy & Evil Troy. The minis use the same bases and peg connections as standard survivors, so you can drop them into a game mid-session with only a brief pause to exhale dramatically. The promo deck sits with your other cards, and the two hero cards align with your standard survivor boards so you can quickly remember “what does Troy do again?” and “what does Evil Troy enjoy most: chaos or loot?” The hardware here is sturdy, the sculpts readable at a standard gaming table, and the entire bundle slots into your existing campaign smoothly enough that you’ll wonder why more promos aren’t this well-tempered.

Play and rules: how Troy and Evil Troy shake up the turn order
Troy is the straightforward, stubbornly brave type. He’s the guy who pushes forward, blocks a path, and makes a few playlists in his head about who owes him a ale later. Evil Troy is the wild card: he courts risk, triggers secondary effects, and sometimes nudges the team toward a dramatic, cinematic misstep that somehow lands just right.

In practice, the two figures diverge from the baseline. You’ll notice:
- Troy offers a leadership-like aura; he can boost nearby survivors, or soak damage to protect the group.
- Evil Troy offers risk-reward: he might double your damage output on a good turn, but he also spawns an extra zombie threat or triggers a sneaky event that complicates your plan.
- The promo deck introduces a handful of event cards that reference Troy and Evil Troy, ensuring the board state feels new without requiring the players to memorize a new ruleset.

One thing that’s especially satisfying is how this pair resonates with the cooperative tension without tipping the game into a “who rolled the best dice” lottery. It’s more about tempo, misdirection, and the occasional heroic sacrifice. You’ll find yourself plotting a sequence where Troy shepherds a pool of survivors toward safety, while Evil Troy pulls a detour that creates an epic, dice-clattering chase through the city’s blood-streaked streets.

A sample turn: a micro-encounter
Let’s stage a micro-scenario. The team is cornered near a churchyard; two zombies edge closer, and Evil Troy’s presence has teased a trap on the east gate. Troy steps forward, defending two teammates with a shield and a shout that would make a knight proud. He holds the line long enough for one survivor to move to safety and loot a critical item. Evil Troy, meanwhile, flips an ambush card that triggers a secondary gate to slam shut and spawn a wave of fast zombies from above. The result? A tense, cinematic moment where you’re choosing between saving a friend now or risking a little chaos for a bigger payoff on the next round. It’s nerve-wracking, it’s silly, and it’s absolutely Zombicide in spirit.

Thematic notes are strong here. If you’re a fan of dual-nature characters or narratives about how good intentions can collide with temptation, Troy & Evil Troy lands with a satisfying thud. The expansion doesn’t pretend to reimagine Zombicide; it enriches it. It gives players a reason to talk about “our hero” in the same breath as “the bad influence in the corner.”

Value, availability, and longevity
Two things matter in this segment: cost and how long the content remains a good idea. The Troy & Evil Troy duo is a compact promo—compact enough to slip into the English Black Plague edition but meaningful enough to alter a game night’s mood. The price is fair for hobbyists who chase limited-run minis and narrative-enhancing cards. If you’re a regular purchaser of expansions and promos, you’ll feel the value more quickly because the figures integrate naturally into your campaigns. If you’re more casual, you’ll still appreciate the comedic tone and the fresh dynamic these two bring.

Cross-compatibility and expansions
If you already own other Zombicide expansions, one of the biggest perks here is compatibility. The two new heroes don’t require you to modify your tables or adjust the zombie spawn counts in dramatic ways. They slot into your existing schedule and give you new responses to the same threats you’ve faced a hundred times before. On the other hand, mixing Troy & Evil Troy with a larger pool of characters can create some truly memorable play sessions where alliances and betrayals become part of the core game’s fabric.

For those curious about beyond-the-core play, it’s worth mixing this promo with scenario-centric campaigns. The two new minis deserve to be placed into a larger story arc: perhaps a siege of a cathedral, a rescue mission that spans two neighborhoods, or a last-stand scenario in which Troy’s leadership has to hold while Evil Troy attempts a power grab. It’s a perfect setup for your homebrew narratives.

Painting and display tips
If you’re into painting, Troy & Evil Troy are a good canvas. Here are a few practical pointers:
- Troy: warm metallics with a dash of heroism—gold highlights on the edges of his armor and a bright shield that reads as a focal point in bright lighting.
- Evil Troy: cooler tones and pale skin, with deep washes to highlight the shadowed facial features. A touch of sickly green or purple on the weapon or armor edges can underscore the “evil” vibe without crossing into cartoonish.
- Bases: a weathered stone texture or a city street from dusk helps them anchor into the Black Plague aesthetic.

Community feedback and experiences
The reception is consistently positive about the narrative energy Troy & Evil Troy bring to the table. Players enjoy the dramatic interplay between the two, and many describe the promo as a “perfect balance of drama and chaos” that doesn’t overwhelm the core mechanics. In groups that lean toward story-driven nights, the combination becomes a frequent choice for a mid-campaign special event. Some players note that Evil Troy’s powers can be punishing in solo modes or in scenarios with tight deadlines, but this is often redeemed by the moment-to-moment excitement his presence injects into the board.

Final verdict and recommendation
If you’re a Zombicide veteran who loves a healthy dose of theatrics, Troy & Evil Troy is a no-brainer. It adds two compelling character options and an extra layer of tension that can elevate even familiar scenarios. If the base game is your jam and you’re itching for new tactical puzzles, this promo is likely to become a favorite add-on in your rotation. If you’re new to Zombicide, you’ll still have a good time, but you might want to focus on mastering the core game first; these two figures will still be a blast to incorporate on your second or third campaign.
