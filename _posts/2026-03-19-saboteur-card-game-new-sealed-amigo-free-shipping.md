## What is Saboteur Anyway?
Saboteur is a card game about dwarves mining for treasure. The core tension is social deduction without the heavy reliance on hidden roles you see in games like Werewolf or One Night Ultimate Werewolf. Instead, Saboteur leans on player-driven path-building and a sprinkle of misdirection. Some players are miners who want to reach the treasure by laying down a chain of path cards. Others are saboteurs who secretly try to derail the mine so that no one can reach the goal. The unique twist is that you never quite know who is on your side—or how cunning your fellow players can be—until the game ends and the treasure is either uncovered or your path collapses into a glorious pile of cards and groans.

The Amigo edition under review is a sealed deck that preserves the classic gameplay with a refreshed presentation and, crucially for many buyers, free shipping in certain bundles. The art style tends toward cartoony dwarves with expressive faces, which makes betrayals even funnier. If you’re into quick, social, competitive experiences that don’t require a 90-minute planning meeting, Saboteur scratches that itch nicely.

The rest is history—provided your history involves suspicious glances, a muttered “I wasn’t the one who did it,” and a chorus of laughter as someone draws the wrong type of card and starts a cascade of unintended consequences.

## How It Actually Plays: The Core Loop
Saboteur is built around a simple loop that yields delightful complexity with each round:

1) Draw a card and either play it to extend a path or use an action card to influence the mine or other players.
2) Continue building a path toward the treasure while watching for cracks in your opponents’ cover.
3) When a path reaches the treasure or when the path collapses, reveal roles and tally up who benefited from the layout.

The tempo is brisk. A typical round lasts about 15 to 25 minutes, which means you can realistically bounce through multiple rounds during a game night without losing your mind or free-limbed social grace. The conditions around victory are delightfully simple: one of the paths must connect the starting entrance to the treasure with a valid route. However, saboteurs can complicate or even break the chain with traps, misdraws, or simply convincing someone else to take a risk that pays off for their own agenda.

### Card Types in Play
- Path cards: Create the mine’s route. Some paths are straightforward, some bend, and some contain a hazard or an advisor that changes the way you think about your neighbors.
- Action cards: Lanterns can reveal, wagons can block, tools can fix, and sometimes sabotage can be done in a way that looks tactical and fair—until it isn’t.
- Role cards: A mix of honest miners and crafty saboteurs. The balance of truth and deception is what gives Saboteur its flavor.
- Gold cards and bonus goals: These add optional objectives to the round and give players little incentives beyond the treasure itself.

This blend of mechanics results in a game that rewards social intuition and a bit of bluffing, but never requires you to be a mastermind of political intrigue to enjoy the experience. It’s more a warm embrace of misdirection than a cold, calculated heist.

## Strategy: When to Be a Miner, When to Be a Saboteur
One of Saboteur’s joys is that you rarely know your role for sure until the end of a round. Still, you can adopt a few mental strategies:

- Early rounds are about information gathering. Watch how others lay paths and use action cards. If someone repeatedly plays a blocking card whenever a certain player starts making progress, that’s a potential saboteur cue.
- If you’re a saboteur, you want to sow doubt without becoming the obvious target. A well-placed obstacle early on can derail one player’s path long enough for another to drift into the final blow.
- Miners should cooperate in a cautious, almost suspiciously cooperative way. Sharing information about what you can see on the board helps reduce the missteps that can cost you the treasure, but be careful—sharing too much might reveal your own guess about a saboteur’s identity.
- Resource management matters. Don’t burn your powerful action cards at the wrong moment. Saboteurs love when you waste your tools on a non-critical moment; you’ll be left with a useless crowd-pleaser when you need real sabotage later.

The fun is in the tension: a round can swing from “we might actually win this” to “wait, who sabotaged the mining plan?” in a single misdraw. And that is exactly Saboteur’s charm.
