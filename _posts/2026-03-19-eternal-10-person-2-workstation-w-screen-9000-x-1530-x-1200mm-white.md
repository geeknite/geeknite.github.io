## Assembly and Setup: It Comes in at Least Three Phases
Like any large-scale furniture project, this beast ships in components that need to be assembled with maybe a little more patience than your average bookshelf. The packaging is deliberate: modular panels, rails, feet, and an aggressive but friendly amount of hardware. If you have built IKEA at war with a CAD program, you will feel right at home. The instruction manual reads like a treasure map and is surprisingly forgiving about misaligned bolts in the second act.

We tested assembly with two technicians and one office dog named Pixel who provided moral support. The first phase is the skeleton: align the rails, bolt the frame pieces, and drop in the main crossbars. The second phase is the power: install the integrated cabling channels, route the longer power strips, and connect the W Screen’s backplane to the central hub. The final phase is fine-tuning: leveling, adjusting the screen wall tilt, and calibrating the height of the individual stations. The good news is that the instructions are clear enough for a human to follow without requiring a graduate degree in mechanical engineering. The slightly longer news is that you will want to allocate a substantial block of time—ideally half a day—if you insist on doing a flawless install with zero do-overs.

In terms of time estimates, expect roughly 4–6 hours for a small team to get the ten seats up and running with all cables neatly tidied. If you have a procrastination habit or a person who insists on rechecking every bolt’s torque for the sake of ritual, you might stretch this into two full coffee-fueled days. But hey, Rome wasn’t built in a day, and Rome also had epic boardroom battles that lasted longer than a team retrospective. The Eternal’s multi-person design is built with future changes in mind, so if you’ve got a new hire or a much larger or smaller team next quarter, the modularity should help you avoid a full rework.
