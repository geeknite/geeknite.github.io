## Setup and Rule Skeleton

The setup would be mercifully quick, designed to get you to the chaos as fast as possible:

- Unwrap the burritos and lay them at the center of the table within easy reach of all players.
- Shuffle the card deck and place it face down. Create a small discard pile for quick resolution of certain action cards.
- Distribute life tokens to each player and decide who goes first by the most ridiculous dice roll or the loudest cheer.
- Each player draws a starting hand of five cards (or two, depending on balance). The rest sits in a draw pile.

Gameplay would likely revolve around two core mechanics: drawing and playing cards on your turn, and the occasional live-action dodge round when a burrito card is revealed. Each turn might look like this:

1) Draw two cards.
2) Play one card from your hand. Effects can include forcing an opponent to dodge within a time limit, swapping hands, or initiating a mini dodge round.
3) If a burrito card lands on your turn or if the active effect triggers a dodge round, you must react quickly and try to avoid being “hit” by a burrito. Hits cost you life tokens or cards and bring you closer to elimination.
4) End of turn cleanup and pass to the next player.

The presence of a live-action dodge phase would require a strong emphasis on safety, clear boundary lines, and a robust “out of bounds” policy for any stray burrito. If you run a family-friendly event, you will want to set explicit safety guidelines and maybe appoint a designated “referee” who is not emotionally invested in a tiebreaker round.

In terms of rules clarity, you want a quick reference sheet that fits in a pocket or on the back of the rulebook page. The best hybrid designs offer a one-page cheat sheet that explains the turn flow, the types of cards, and the outcomes of common interactions. A well-made cheat sheet can save a lot of time and reduce the argument rate, which is essentially the oxygen supply of party games.

## Core Mechanics and Strategy

The heart of this hypothetical product would lie in how it blends deck play with live-action dodge drama. Here are the core mechanics and some strategy notes that would help you survive, and maybe even savor the chaos:

### Card Types and Timing
- Attack cards: These push you toward a chaos moment. They could force you to reveal a burrito throw location, or require a quick dodge mini-challenge.
- Dodge cards: These help you avoid hits. Timing matters; using a dodge card too early might be wasted; hold for a moment of high-stakes round where you can swing the momentum.
- Mischief cards: These swap hands, swap burritos, or create a mirror round where players defend their own burrito while targeting others. Mischief is where the humor blooms and the group’s coordination becomes delightful chaos.
- Burrito cards: These spark the live-action moment. They could impose penalties for missing a catch or cause a “burrito volley” that moves across players in a short window of time.

Strategy at a glance: balance aggression with defense. You want to push opponents into bad dodges, but you also want to keep enough cards to defend yourself. Positioning matters in the same way as a dance floor at a wedding: you want space to move but not so much space you lose track of the group chaos.

### The Dodge Phase and Safety
The live dodge phase is where the energy peaks and the safety rules must be crystal clear. A good Dodge Phase should be short, funny, and forgiving. Here is what to optimize:
- Clear signals for when a dodge phase starts and ends.
- Visible boundaries to prevent accidental hits on players or inanimate objects like lamps and cat trees.
- Quick, light foam burritos that won’t cause bruises but still feel satisfying when you bob and weave.
- A penalty system that emphasizes humor, not humiliation. It should be possible to recover a hit quickly or laugh it off and press reset for the next round.

If you are playing with kids, you want to scale down the speed and danger, turning the burrito toss into a gentle rhythm that provokes giggles rather than alarm. The best family-friendly chaos comes from timing and cooperation as much as from physical dexterity.
