## What is OverPower 1995 and why Lod 5 matters
OverPower began as a collectible card game by Fleer with a pedigree: Marvel licenses, glossy art, and a ruleset that rewarded clever drafting and synergy. The 1995 iteration is remembered for its bold art, heavy card stock, and the sense that every card could be both a hammer and a scalpel in the right hands. Lod 5 (Pick From List) is a flavor of power card selection that forces players to weigh choices rather than rely on brute force alone.

In plain words: instead of drawing a random power that could be great or mediocre for your hero, you get five options on the table and you choose the single one that will shape your next few turns. It’s like a mini draft within each duel. The mechanic rewards foresight, risk assessment, and a little bit of bluffing: can you misdirect your opponent into thinking your five options are all terrible, so they overcommit to a counter that isn’t actually optimal?

## The Lod 5 mechanic explained
### How it works
LOD 5 presents players with a curated list of five Power Cards. Each card represents a unique ability, buff, or effect that can alter the course of a duel. The player selects one card from the list to add to their hero’s arsenal for the upcoming turns. The rest of the cards stay out of circulation for that round, which means the selection is a valuable strategic resource rather than a throwaway choice.

The design intent is straightforward but deliciously devious: you want something that complements your current board state, your recent card draws, and your opponent’s likely plan. It also means that even if you’re behind, you can still flip a match by choosing the perfect card from the five shown to you. The five-card pool makes every decision feel weighty, every choice a potential swing.

### Why five cards and not more or less
The number five is not just a random flourish; it’s the sweet spot in game design. Too many options overwhelm the player, turning the pick into a gut-check lottery. Too few options, and you remove meaningful choices, turning the mechanic into a binary coin toss. Five cards strike a balance between variety and manageability. It’s enough to inspire strategic thinking without devolving into paralysis by analysis. Also, the aesthetic of a “five-card hand” is a comforting nod to classic card games without drowning in probability math.

### Interaction with the rest of the deck
Power Cards exist to create synergy opportunities. A Lod 5 pick might interact with your current hero’s powers, with your team’s overall plan, or with your opponent’s likely counters. A savvy player looks for triggers: cards that enhance damage when combined with a particular hero power, or effects that chain into a defensive shield when you sense an aggression window forming. The Lod 5 system rewards players who can read the table, not just their own card catalogue.

## Gameplay implications of Lod 5
### Strategic depth and player psychology
The Lod 5 mechanic adds a layer of psychology to duels. You don’t just play a card; you reveal your strategy in a micro-arc. The five-card pool becomes a window into your mind: do you lean into offense to create a quick, brutal tempo, or do you lean into defense and setup a longer grind that pays off later? ### Game balance and counterplay
From a balance perspective, Lod 5 can be opinion-dividing. Some players adore the tension and the clutch moment of choosing a perfect match card. Others gripe about variance: what if the five options all look like they mostly help your opponent? The designers typically mitigate this with card text that favors self-forgiveness and with synergy rules that reward a well-built deck. In practice, Lod 5 shines in the right hands and can feel infuriating in the wrong one—but that’s part of the romance of this era of card games.
