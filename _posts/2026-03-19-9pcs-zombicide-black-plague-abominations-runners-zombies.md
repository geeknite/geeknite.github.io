### Miniature quality and design language

The Black Plague aesthetic leans toward medieval-fantasy gore with a dash of grim humor. The Abominations are the big-bads: chunkier torsos, protruding bones, and a vibe that says, “I’ve eaten a village, and I’m looking for more.” Runners have a slick, angular silhouette designed to convey speed and menace; their long arms and raised legs suggest motion even when they’re standing still. Regular Zombies are the bread-and-butter of the game: lanky, swarmy, and not shy about multiplying across a tile.

In the 9-piece set, the sculpts typically feature clean lines, subtle feathering, and limited but effective texture work on skin, fabric, and armor. The casting quality—a common concern with mass-produced metal/ resin plastics—usually lands somewhere in the “solid but not museum-grade” tier. You’ll see mold lines, especially around joints, which is par for the course in affordable tabletop miniatures. If you’re the sort who loves a flawless display piece, you’ll want to tidy up and maybe do some green stuff sculpting. If you’re here for gameplay, the degree of detail is more than enough to read silhouette and rank on the table from a reasonable distance.

For base size, these minis align well with standard Black Plague basing. If you’re planning to magnetize or keep you figs stable on a windy gaming day, a light base pin or a dab of glue in the feet helps; otherwise, the dynamic poses tend to anchor them nicely to the surface. Speaking of bases, the included bases are uniform and unassuming—not fancy, but functional. This keeps your table looking cohesive rather than a chaotic gallery of mismatched platforms.

### Assembly: easy or exorcism?

If you’ve assembled Zombicide minis before, you’re in familiar territory: small pegs, simple joins, and the occasional line you wish you could unsee. The 9-piece set tends to be forgiving. The Abominations, being bulkier, can be slightly fiddly at the joints, but nothing that a steady hand and a dab of plastic cement can’t handle. Runners and Zombies typically have cleaner join points, thanks to their slender profiles and fewer bulky limbs.

Pro tip: wash the parts before gluing; a quick soak in warm soapy water helps reduce mold-release residue. When in doubt, dry-fit first to avoid end-state panic. If you’ve got a friend who loves green stuff, these are perfect candidates for a light conversion to make some variants that feel distinctly “your table’s own.”

### Painting: simple, accessible, satisfying

On painting, these models respond nicely to a straightforward approach. For beginners, a three-color scheme works wonders: a base coat, a wash, and a dry brush on raised edges. The Abominations look imposing with a desaturated, mossy grunge palette; the Runners benefit from a sharper contrast on the musculature and exposed bone to highlight their speed. Zombies scale well with skin tones that lean toward sickly greens or grayish corpse hues; you’ll want a darker wash to bring out the texture in the ribbing and torn fabrics on their shreds of clothing.

If you’re time-limited, you can batch-paint these using your standard zombie color recipe and get decent results in a single sitting. If you want a showroom piece, you’ll enjoy a slightly longer session focusing on dry brushing and edge highlighting to bring out the sculpted textures. One of the big joys of Zombicide minis is how they catch light during a game turn; even modest highlighting can push a piece from “stats on a stand” to “character on the board.”

### Materials and casting: what to expect

Most sets like this are a mix of resin or fine-pinish plastics. Resin can pick up detail nicely but requires careful handling to avoid chipping or bending. If you’re an airbrush enthusiast, you’ll appreciate the way the surfaces hold a crisp edge after a good primer layer. If you prefer metal, you’ll notice a density difference—these are usually lighter than old-school metal minis, which makes them easier to handle in large squads.

Cleanup is a reality check: expect a few drips of excess material and a few mold lines to sand down. A gentle sanding session makes all the difference between “just okay” and “board-ready.”
