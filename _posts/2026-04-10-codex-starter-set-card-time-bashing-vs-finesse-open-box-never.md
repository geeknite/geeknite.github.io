Core Mechanics: Card Time and Bashing vs Finesse
-----------------------------------------------
The heart of Codex is the Card Time mechanic. Each turn, players reveal from their hand and place cards on a shared timeline. The position of a card on this timeline determines when its effects resolve and how much you can influence the board state before your opponent can respond. You’re juggling tempo, order, and resource management all at once. If you like timing puzzles, you’ll feel right at home; if you prefer a pure engine-building game with constant binary choices, you’ll still enjoy the dance, just with more footnotes.

Bashing vs Finesse is where the game gets personality. Bashing is the aggressive approach: cards that advance on the timeline to hit hard, disrupt opponents, and lock down your own tempo buffers. Finesse is the patient approach: cards that slow the game’s tempo, manipulate the timeline with surgical precision, and win through a succession of controlled, incremental advantages. In practice, most games split the board into a bashing front and a finesse backline, with players alternating who claims the moment they’ll regret next turn.

Strategy Deep Dive: When to Bash, When to Finesse
------------------------------------------------
This section is where the real meat lives for players who want to win in style rather than win by accident. The early game often rewards the bold: you sprint toward a tempo lead by playing accelerators that threaten a sequence your opponent can’t easily counter. The mid-game is where you either lock in a plan or pivot away from a failing approach. The late game is where timing becomes the garnish on the victory cake: a single correct play can flip the board state and force the opponent into a suboptimal sequence.

Key rules-of-thumb for Bashing players:
- Early aggression can create a tempo advantage if your cards reliably land on time. If you’re fortunate enough to your own board to push through a strong strike, you can often snowball the momentum before your opponent recovers.
- Don’t squander your high-impact cards on early, cheap damage; you’re aiming for a window where your opponent’s actions are forced to respond to you—creating a tempo that forces mistakes.
- Use timing trickery to pull your opponent into bad sequences. If you can force their cards to resolve in less-than-ideal order, you’ll start to build a clock that marches toward you.

Key rules-of-thumb for Finesse players:
- Early game tempo is about establishing safe, incremental advantages. You’re not chasing the perfect play; you’re setting up robust future windows that your opponent can’t smash with one big swing.
- A major finesse move is often a setup that delays the leading path of your opponent, buying you the necessary cycles to land your own big effect later.
- Read the opponent’s tension: if they’re playing fast and loose, you can exploit their haste with a precise, slow burn move that locks in victory mid-to-late game.
