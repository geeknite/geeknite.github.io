## Sculpt and Art Direction
This box is essentially a celebration of two distinct art voices. Parrillo’s work is known for dramatic lighting, heavy armor textures, and a certain gothic fantasy feel. Guiton counters with motion-forward silhouettes, flamboyant drapery, and action-readiness that feels like a mid-battle still frame.

### Lucio Parrillo minis
The Parrillo piece feels like a sentinel who has stood a thousand battles and will stand a thousand more. The armor plates are busy without becoming noisy; you can pick out individual rivets, leather stitching, and the wear patterns you expect after a long siege. The face sculpt reads intensity rather than caricature, which works beautifully for a mid-campaign hero who leads the little group through a catacomb of doom. The cape or cloak material has a weight to it; the sculptor has thought about how the fabric drapes, which makes it a joy to paint because the highlights and shadows follow natural fall-lines on the model.

## Painting and Assembly Tips
To get the most out of these figures, you want to approach painting with a plan but with room for joyful experimentation. The sculpts reward careful work, but they are not so fragile that you cannot enjoy quick wins on your first session.

- Prep first, then prime. A light sanding of mold lines and a clean rinse before priming will save you a lot of frustration. A well-prepared surface makes metallics pop and skin tones read more natural.
- Establish a base color scheme. For Parrillo, a cool base with blue-tlecked metallics and bone tones in the accent areas can mimic his dramatic lighting. For Guiton, warm skin tones with bold reds or purples in the cape create a complementary counterpoint. The trick is to maintain a cohesive overall palette so the two pieces do not feel as if they belong to separate boxes.
- Layering and feathering. Use thin layers to build the color depth. For metallics, a bright metallic applied in thin layers over a dark base gives a realistic shine without overpowering the sculpt’s texture.
- Washes and contrast. A brown or sepia wash into recesses on leather and armor helps pull the textures forward. A slightly blue wash on the armor corners can add a cold, night-time vibe that works well with Parrillo’s style.
- Edge highlight technique. A bright edge on plate armor makes the figures pop at the table, but you do not want to overdo it. A light silver on the outermost rims followed by a subtle white or bone highlight reads cleanly and avoids a toy-like finish.
- Basing that tells a story. The base is a quiet stage that informs your overall presentation. Gravel, broken stones, and a few tufts of grass can anchor the scene. If you want a moodier look, a darker basing scheme works with the dramatic lighting that these artists tend to evoke in their work.

### Tips for assembly without tears
If you are new to assembling two-part or multi-piece sculpts, a few ergonomic tips help avoid frustration:
- Do a dry fit first. This helps you understand how the parts connect and whether you need a little putty to close any gaps.
- Use a methodical clipping approach. Cut away the excess plastic cleanly and save the offcuts for potential conversions if you feel adventurous.
- Apply glue with patience. Gel or thinner formulations allow you to reposition a piece if alignment is off, saving you a second try and a potential ooze of glue that could ruin surface detail.
- Allow adequate curing time. It is tempting to rush to the paint stage, but a properly cured joint will hold up better during handling and table play.
