What is Saboteur: The Lost Mines?
--------------------------------
Saboteur: The Lost Mines is a compact, push-your-luck, worker-placement-ish card game in which players take on the roles of dwarves digging for treasure. The twist is that not all dwarves are on the same team, and some players secretly want to sabotage the mine while others want to reach the temple of gold first. The Lost Mines introduces new mine locations, new treasure cards, and a set of villainous saboteur cards that force you to question every greedy grin at the table.

You’ll build a network of tunnels coming from your mining cart and you’ll need to guide the mine carts through a series of routes to reach the treasure. But beware: the saboteurs can bend the tunnel layout, re-route paths, and flip a few cards to send you off in the wrong direction. It’s a game about deception, collaboration, and moments of panicked laughter when a card you’re sure about suddenly becomes a “nope, not today” moment.

Mechanics and design: how the Lost Mines feels on the table
-----------------------------------------------------------
Saboteur games live and die by the way you bluff, deduce, and build a path without tripping over your own plans. The Lost Mines adds a specific mine-route layer to the classic formula. You’ll be laying tunnels (aka connecting paths) and discovering treasure cards as you go, all while trying not to be the unfortunate saboteur who reveals their secret role too early—or too late, depending on how much chaos you crave.

Key mechanics include:
- Hidden roles: Some players are miners seeking treasure; others are saboteurs aiming to derail progress. The revelation happens at defined points, which means there are several turns of crisp social deduction. The tension is often higher than a goblin on caffeine.
- Path-building: The core action is to lay cards to connect tunnels from your mine cart towards the treasure. You must balance optimism with risk: you might find an easy path that is too short, or a longer detour that invites a saboteur’s mischief.
- Resource management: You’ll draw cards, play action cards, and deploy tools to straighten a tunnel or sabotage a rival’s work. It’s a gentle tug-of-war that can swing dramatically on a single card draw.
- Endgame and victory: Most games end when someone completes a path to the treasure or when a certain number of coins are claimed. The exact thresholds vary by edition, but the adrenaline rush when the last tunnel is laid is universal.

The Lost Mines, compared to the base Saboteur, adds a few fresh card types and a thematic focus on “lost” mine secrets. The art direction stays playful, which keeps the mood hospitable for families and parties—though there are enough moments of misdirection to keep the most serious gamer from taking a victory for granted.

Playtime, pacing, and table dynamics
-----------------------------------
Expect a game that clocks in around 30 to 60 minutes, depending on player count and how aggressively you play the sabotage cards. If you’re playing with a table of natural-born bluffers, you’ll see a lot of “sniffing” in the conversations as people try to interpret who just laid a detour. If you’re playing with new players, you’ll likely see more cooperative moments as everyone learns the path-building rules, but you’ll also get plenty of “wait—are we sure we’re on the right track?” moments that produce laughter and occasional groans.

The table dynamics shift considerably with player count. At smaller tables (3–4 players), the sabotage cards feel like a practical joke, but everyone tends to be more directly involved in the tunnel-building. At larger tables (5–7 players), you’ll see a wider spread of strategies, more misdirection, and a stronger emphasis on what I like to call “team paranoia.” It’s glorious chaos—the kind of chaos that pairs well with snacks and a loud vacuum cleaner in the background to remind you of real life’s interruptions.

Setup and rule book notes
-------------------------
Setting up Saboteur: The Lost Mines is straightforward, which is a blessing for those who fear long rulebooks more than they fear the dark. Here are a few setup reminders that help you avoid the classic rookie mistakes:
- Shuffle the treasure cards well. You want the “best outcomes” to be a function of luck rather than your own memory of what’s left in the deck.
- Place the mine route mats and path tiles within easy reach. The moment you realize you need a junction card you forgot to set aside, you’ll understand the true meaning of “panic in the tunnel.”
- Distribute role cards according to the number of players. If you short-change the saboteurs, you’ll end up with a game where no one suspects anyone, which is technically less dramatic and more boring.
- Ensure the players know that deception is allowed, even encouraged. The best games come from players who argue with bravado about their own brilliant plans and then promptly ignore their own advice to go with a wild detour. It’s the essence of Saboteur.

Rule-writers often appreciate the crispness of the Lost Mines’ approach: short rules, quick clarifications, and a generous allowance for players to negotiate what “completed” means on the last turn. If you’re a new player, the first session will probably include a lot of “is this allowed?” and “can I do that if it seems fun?” and that’s perfectly fine. The game rewards curiosity and humor more than pedantry.

Artwork, theme, and humor
-------------------------
Saboteur’s humor is a big part of its charm. The Lost Mines leans into a slightly darker-yet-playful vibe—think dwarven memes, “danger” icons that look suspiciously like mugs of ale, and treasure maps that look like they were drawn by someone who learned geography from a pirate’s treasure chest. It’s not grimdark; it’s more like a lighthearted caper where everyone’s in on the joke except for the guy who keeps misplacing his own sword (which, in this game, is a double-edged metaphor for a misplayed card).

Thematically, the mix of mining, treasure, and sabotage tracks perfectly with a range of playstyles. Some players love the strategic depth of route planning; others enjoy the social bluffing and “nice try, I’ve seen this exact card before” banter. The Lost Mines manages to straddle both camps without welling up into complexity, which is a hard trick to pull off in a title that is, at its core, a family-friendly party game with a dash of conspiracy.

Comparisons to other Saboteur entries
--------------------------------------
If you own the base Saboteur or other expansions, you’ll notice that The Lost Mines enhances the flavor without turning the entire system into a rules labyrinth. The core decisions—whether to advance your own team’s objective or to derail someone else—remain intact. For players who love the base game’s bluffing and dice-free tension, The Lost Mines is a natural extension. If you’re curious about how the experience scales, consider these rough comparisons:
- Base Saboteur: Simpler, quicker plays, and a uniform theme of “are you the saboteur this round?” The Lost Mines keeps the core identity but adds a mine-specific flavor that can feel fresher on repeat plays.
- Other expansions: Some expansions introduce more heavy mechanics or additional goal cards; The Lost Mines tends to stay lean, providing variety through new layouts and a few extra role cards rather than a wholesale mechanical overhaul. If you’re the sort who collects expansions for variety rather than complexity, this is a forgiving and welcome inclusion.
- Co-op or semi-co-op variants: Saboteur is not a pure co-op, but there is a shared goal that creates a sense of collaboration. The Lost Mines exaggerates this by allowing players to ally briefly with others to reach a treasure while still preserving that delicious tension that comes from a hidden saboteur in your midst.

A few practical tips for pre-owned copies

- Inspect for missing rule tiles or markers. If you’re missing a piece, improvise with a small token and pretend the original was a legendary relic from the mine’s first expedition.

- Use a soft play area so the box doesn’t become scuffed during dramatic reveals. This is a game that invites a little showmanship; don’t let a bruised box ruin the moment you reveal a treasure card.
