## What is Codename Duet
Codename Duet is a cooperative variant of the beloved word game Codenames, designed specifically for two players. Instead of a large team trying to guess their own team’s agents from a shared grid, Duet places two players on the same mission. One person gives a clue that points to multiple word cards on the table, while the other person must interpret that clue to select the correct cards while avoiding the forbidden assassin card. The objective is simple in words yet devilishly tricky in practice: reveal all the agents without stepping on a red flag that ends the game prematurely.

Duet introduces a slightly different win condition and score counting that emphasizes cooperation, planning ahead, and a pinch of risk management. The grid remains the same size as the original game, but the feel is different because you and your partner share a single goal rather than racing two separate teams. The design aims to capture the tension of a shared secret mission while maintaining the approachable wordplay that fans adore.

## How the game is structured and how setup feels for two players
The setup for Codename Duet is deliberately simple, which is a big win for frustration-free evenings. You lay out a 5 by 4 grid of word cards on the table. Nearby are the agent cards: two different colored agents for you and your partner to identify, plus the assassin that can end the game abruptly in the worst possible moment. One player will serve as the clue giver, the other as the guesser. In this two player mode, there is still a rhythm you must learn together: clue, guess, check, and align.

Steps for setup generally look like this:
- Shuffle the word cards and lay them out in a grid
- Give the clue giver a cue sheet or the method to interpret the grid
- Determine who goes first and how many turns you have to reach your mission goals
- Place any scoring tokens or track progress as per the rules you are using

The box contents are designed to support a flexible play style. If you prefer a tighter session, you can play with a shorter timer and a smaller number of required guesses. If you want a longer cooperative challenge, you can extend the session with more words on the grid or additional constraints to raise the complexity. The modular nature of the rules is one of the reasons this variant remains engaging after dozens of plays.

## How to play in two player mode
The core loop in Codename Duet is straightforward but cunning:
- The clue giver selects a clue word and a number that relates to the number of cards on the table the clue touches
- The guesser interprets the clue, selecting the corresponding cards that best fit the clue
- If the guesser hits the assassin card, the game ends immediately with a loss; hitting neutral cards has no direct effect on the mission status beyond potential misaligned progress; the goal is to avoid mistakes while satisfying the mission requirements within the constraints of the grid
- After a set of successful guesses, the players reassess the grid and proceed with another clue until all required cards have been identified or the assassin makes a fatal appearance

Clues must be crafted with care. The best clues are both expansive and precise, a paradox that makes Duet so delightful to master. Word choice matters more here than in almost any other two player word game you have tried, because you need to connect multiple cards through a single concept without creating too many unintended connections. The guesser’s job is equally nuanced: you must parse the clue not by your own worldview but by interpreting your partner’s intent. This requires good communication, shared vocabulary, and a willingness to accept that you might have misheard the clue or misread a card under the pressure of a ticking clock.

Balancing risk and reward is part of the fun. You can push for a faster win by attempting more cards per clue, but the risk of hitting the assassin or incorrect cards increases with each additional guess. The game rewards thoughtful pacing; a calm approach, where you confirm each card before moving on, can yield a smoother victory over more chaotic bursts of guessing.

## Strategy and tips for two players
### Clue creation basics
- Start with broad clusters: If you see a group of 3 or 4 cards that share a common theme, a well chosen clue can touch them all. The number you pick should be at least the number of cards you are confident will be correct, plus one if you want to invite a riskier but potentially faster run. The exact math is less important than your confidence in the shared mental map you and your partner share.
- Avoid dangerous overlaps: The assassin and the opposing color cards pose real risk. If a plausible safe interpretation pops up, you should try to avoid linking that clue to cards that could lead to a fatal or counterproductive result.
- Use categories you both understand: If you and your partner share a hobby or a common reference frame (for example, a beloved sci fi universe), this shared vocabulary can dramatically improve your clue precision and reduce misinterpretation.

### Guessing tactics
- Confirm before committing: When you are unsure, take a careful step. A cautious player is often a more reliable partner. The cost of a wrong guess can be high, so a pause can be worth it.
- Use the map in your head: In two player mode, you should attempt to build a mental map of which cards you believe fit the clue. Discuss with your partner what you think the clue covers, then decide together.
- Manage escalation: Start with a modest number for your clue. If the initial guesses go well, you can escalate on the next turn with a slightly bolder clue. The rhythm of escalation helps stall and then spike the excitement at just the right times.

### Common mistakes to avoid
- Over-narrow clues that trap you into a single card: You want to connect multiple cards, not hammer a single point home.
- Ignoring your teammate’s perspective: Often a clue makes sense to you but is opaque to your partner. They may read different associations, and you should be open to adjusting midstream.
- Ignoring the assassin risk: It is easy to slip into a mode of trying to squeeze out one more card when the board has become fragile. In Duet, a single wrong move ends the mission; you must be psychologically prepared to bail out when necessary.

## Variants and how it compares to the original Codenames
The two big differences between Duet and the original team based version are the number of players and the mission oriented objective. In the main game, you are racing two teams to identify their own set of color-coded agents without triggering the assassin, and the tension runs high with multiple guessers per team. In Duet, you share a single mission with one teammate, and the tension shifts from pure speed to a blend of risk management, mutual understanding, and careful communication.

For players who love the original Codenames for its wordplay, Duet offers a refined, more intimate experience that still preserves the word play core. It is not simply a stripped down version of the original; it is an adaptation designed to leverage two players as a frictionless duo rather than a large team dynamic. If you have a friend who is up for a mental challenge that is both cozy and intense, Duet is a perfect fit. If you dislike wordplay puzzles that depend on a delicate shared mental model, you might find the two player format a bit too precarious for long sessions.
