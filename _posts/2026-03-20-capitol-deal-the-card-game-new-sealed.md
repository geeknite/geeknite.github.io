## What’s in the sealed box
### Components and quality
Inside the sealed Capitol Deal box you’ll typically find around 110 cards, a handful of plastic money cards in denominations you’ll recognize from classic banking dreams, a few reference cards, and a tiny but sturdy deck that begs for shuffles, stacks, and the occasional misdeal. The art leans bold, the color palette reads cleanly on a crowded table, and the Capitol dome iconography gives everything a cohesive theme. The card stock is decent—not premium tarot-board thick, but sturdy enough to survive a Sunday family marathon where someone inevitably drops the box on your foot.

### Card types and their roles
- Money cards: The circulating coin fleet that pays the rent, funds the pile, and sometimes pays for your rematch after a blitz of negotiations.
- Property cards: These are the real estate you’re trying to assemble into a complete set. Expect a mix of district-like cards and special properties that grant unique bonuses or protection.
- Action cards: The heart of the game’s negotiation engine. Steal, demand rent, veto votes, and a variety of “lawyer up” style plays exist to complicate everyone’s day.
- Debt cards and deals: The debt mechanic adds a simple but effective layer of risk. Debt is payable, though sometimes you can dodge it with the right argument.

The result is a deck that can support the right number of players and an assortment of strategies. The sealed box keeps everything fresh, but it also means you’ll be exploring the game with fresh eyes every time you crack it open.

### Setup and player count
Capitol Deal is designed for 2 to 6 players. In our tests, the sweet spot hovered around 3 to 4 players, where the math of negotiation remains lively without devolving into pure chaos. At 2 players, you’ll get a faster, more cutthroat duel; at 5–6 players, expect a healthy dose of talk, alliance-making, and alliances that vanish faster than a senator’s poll numbers after an umbrella scandal.

Setup takes about 3–5 minutes: shuffle the deck, deal a starting hand (usually five cards), assign a player order, and get ready to bargain your way to a complete set of property cards while keeping an eye on the debt pile that can swing back and forth like a budget committee vote.

### Theme and design notes
The Capitol Deal artwork leans into light satire rather than brutal realism. It’s colorful, cartoony in the right places, and it doesn’t feel mean-spirited toward actual politics. The cards feature friendly caricatures and abstract icons that convey function at a glance, which helps when you’re trying to teach a roomful of friends who are arguing about whose turn it is to be the “Speaker of the House” in a game about pretend money and property.

### How to play in simple terms
- On your turn, you can play money to a personal bank, put down property cards to start a set, or play an action card to alter the board state.
- You’re aiming to complete a set of property cards—three or more colors—before other players can lock you out with gimmicks, debt, and the occasional theft.
- Action cards can steal, swap, or disrupt; debt cards can force opponents to pay; and there’s usually a way to veto or push back against a too-aggressive opponent’s plan.
- The player with the most power, money, and property at the end is not exactly the winner; the winner is the first to complete their property set, but you’ll often see subplots of debt repayments, strategic trades, and last-minute bargains that feel like real-world negotiations without the legal risk.

## Gameplay depth and pacing
### The core loop
Capitol Deal thrives on a core loop: draw a card, play a card, negotiate, and plan your next move. The negotiation piece is where the game shines. It rewards players who can read the room, appeal to common interest, and casually threaten to unleash a veto or a “nudge” to move things along. The pacing can swing wildly based on player count and personal style. In a 3-player game, you’ll have rapid back-and-forth with more direct confrontations; in a 6-player game, the social dynamics become a study in micro-politics, where every trade becomes a mini-debate and every misdeal opens a tactical possibility you hadn’t considered.

### Strategy tips for newcomers
- Balance risk and reward: Don’t chase a full color set at warp speed if you’re left with debt cards that sulk on your bench. A healthy blend of cash, debt, and partial sets often wins the day.
- Play the long game: Some action cards are better used in mid-to-late game when your opponents’ plans are visible and their debt load is heavier.
- Read the room: Negotiation is a legitimate sport here. If you notice a player’s discomfort with a particular color, you can steer trades to benefit you and your ally (or your own strategic rival, if the table enjoys dramatic flips).
- Protect your core: Don’t let one steal or one misdeal derail a your path to a complete set. Use protective action cards and timely vetoes to keep your trajectory intact.

### The art of the deal
Deal-making is a big part of Capitol Deal, and it’s where a lot of the game’s personality emerges. Some exchanges feel fair, others feel cheekily skewed, and a few trades become legendary in your group as the moment when someone turned a neighbor into a temporary ally in exchange for a shield against a looming debt crisis. This is where the game earns its place on any party-level gaming night, because it rewards players who can talk their way through a trade while keeping a straight face about the monetary math.

### The fold: what works and what doesn’t
- Works: The tempo is quick, the table talk is lively, and the theme is approachable for non-gamers. The mechanics support friendly but competitive play and encourage social interaction.
- Don’t love: If you’re allergic to take-that mechanics or you dislike debt mechanics, Capitol Deal might test your patience. The game can feel a little repetitive after several rounds if you’re not in the mood for negotiation, but that’s when you can switch sides and try shelling out more cash or attempting a different color strategy.

## Getting the most out of your sealed copy
### House rules and variant tips
- Add a 2-minute rule for negotiations on a big trade to keep turns moving.
- Introduce a charitable “bipartisan” trade option where players must agree to a mutual benefit before a deal goes through.
- Try a “no debt” round to see how the game plays when debt cards are removed. It’s a different flavor and a neat way to test how much you rely on debt as a lever.
- For larger groups, consider a “city council” variant where you assign a temporary chair who can call a vote on major deals. It adds a level of cluster control that can be quite entertaining.
