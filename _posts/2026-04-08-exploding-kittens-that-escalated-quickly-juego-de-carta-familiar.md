## Overview

Exploding Kittens plays fast, looks cute, and asks you to gamble with the fate of your friends. The base game is a rapid-fire, take-that style card game for 2 to 5 players, designed to be easy to pick up for newcomers while offering enough cheeky humor for veteran gamers. The art by Matthew Inman is a major part of the appeal: charming, slightly deranged kittens doing human things, exploding in unexpected places, and a general sense that chaos is the default setting for this universe.

In the box you’ll find a deck of cat memes in card form, a handful of Defuse cards (your Swiss Army Knife against disaster), and a few types of action cards that let you peek, skip turns, shuffle, attack, or force someone else to draw. The rules are delightfully simple: draw a card; if you reveal an Exploding Kitten and you don’t have a Defuse card, you’re out of the game. If you do have a Defuse card, you play it and put the kitten back somewhere in the deck where your enemies can sigh loudly at your audacity. Simple, chaotic, and oddly tactical at times.

For our purposes in Geeknite lore, Exploding Kittens also serves as a how-not-to-approach game design: it leans heavily on humor, accessibility, and a brisk pace rather than heavy strategy. The result is a game that is incredibly social and sometimes mercilessly funny. The laughter tends to be louder than the actual decision-making, which is exactly the kind of magic you want when you gather around a coffee table and pretend you have a future in competitive mischief.

## How to play: a quick walkthrough with a wink

### Setup
1. Shuffle the Exploding Kitten cards into the deck based on the player count. More players means more potential explosions, which is the human version of a fireworks show you didn’t ask for.
2. Shuffle the Defuse cards into the deck or keep them in a separate pool if you prefer to feel fancy. Each player starts with a Defuse card or two, depending on how generous you want the chaos to feel early on.
3. Deal a starting hand to each player. The goal is simple: don’t explode, don’t explode, don’t explode… and try to stay awake during the final round.

### Turn structure
- On your turn, you can play any number of action cards from your hand. Some actions let you peek at the top of the deck, rearrange the top cards, or skip your turn entirely. Others are more diabolical, like forcing someone else to draw a card.
- After playing any number of cards, you must draw a card from the draw pile. If you draw an Exploding Kitten and you cannot Defuse it, you’re out. If you Defuse, you place the Kitten back into the deck somewhere—the suspense starts again, and the game continues.

The flow is purposely fast: rounds last minutes, not hours, and the social energy tends to surge and wane with each reveal. The best part is that even when you think you’re out, the Defuse card can turn someone else’s night into a cat-powered inferno of regret.

### Card types you should know
- Exploding Kitten. The centerpiece of the night. Draw this and you lose unless you Defuse it.
- Defuse. The lifesaver. It can save you from the apocalypse, provided you’re willing to spend a precious card to neutralize the threat.
- Attack, Skip, See the Future, Shuffle, Favor. Each card changes tempo in a different way. See the Future lets you peek, Shuffle mixes up the deck for unpredictable results, and Favor makes someone else draw, which is basically social media in card form.
- Cat Cards (like Splashing Puddles and other meme-themed beasts in the deck). These are typically played to trigger combos or to manipulate the opponent, all while keeping the group giggling.

## Strategy notes: when to push your luck and when to retreat

This is not a game where every choice is a life-or-death call, but the few decisions you do make matter. Here are some not-so-obvious tips that can help you survive long enough to brag about it.

- Balance risk and defuse resources: If you hold multiple Defuse cards, you’re in a safer zone. Use the Defuse sparingly, because the deck will eventually cycle back to you and you’ll wish you hadn’t burned them too early.
- Read the room, not just the deck: People who talk a big game often reveal their strategy through their tone and body language. If a friend keeps saying they don’t care about winning, they might be setting you up for a perfectly timed misdirection.
- Don’t chase explosions: Sometimes the best move is a quiet turn where you skip or peek rather than escalate. The table’s energy will swing back and forth, and a calm moment can be worth four chaotic bursts of laughter.
- Use Cat Cards strategically: They often serve as social tools to force someone else to draw or shuffle. Don’t waste them on players who are already on the brink; target those who are clearly panicking but still smiling.
- House rules can save you: If your group loves to bend rules, set a few fun guidelines. For example, allow a one-time “save” for a desperate moment, or permit a “do-over” after a particularly egregious misplay. The point is to keep the table lively, not to punish the table spirit.
