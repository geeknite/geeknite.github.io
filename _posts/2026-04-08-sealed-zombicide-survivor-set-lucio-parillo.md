## Unboxing and first impressions

Unboxing is a ritual with Zombicide in general, and this set leans into that ritual with a little extra fanfare. The outer sleeve is glossy, with Parillo’s signature linework printed in a manner that makes the cover feel like an original art piece rather than a mere cosmetic wrapper. The real excitement starts when you crack the seal and you’re hit with a color palette that looks like it wandered out of a dark fantasy edition of a comic-con promo poster.

Inside, you’ll find a compact but sturdy tray system. The survivors are represented by miniatures that boast a higher overall fidelity than the baseline SD (standard edition) tokens, aided by Parillo’s crisp edge lines and dynamic poses. If you’re a painter who likes to customize your games, the sculpts offer a sturdy canvas; if you’re a player who wants to deploy quickly, the silhouettes read cleanly and distinguish themselves from generic survivors with just a glance.

One of the unsung joys of this particular release is the weight distribution of the box contents. It feels less like a cheap promo and more like a premium product that’s been thought through. The inner trays hold the survivors in secure basins, with tiny foam chips that keep any little limbs from becoming kinetic sculptures during transit. It’s a small touch, but it signals CMON’s intention: this is a “special guest” set, not just a random drop.

## What you get: a component-by-component look

### The survivors and their sculpts (H2)
Lucio Parillo’s involvement shines through in the design language of the survivors. They’re not your cookie-cutter, run-and-gun heroes. These are characters with a presence—coiled posture, a hint of backstory in their gear, and a few new poses that push the “team dynamic” angle rather than the lone-wolf trope. If you love custom conversions, these miniatures provide interesting silhouettes to work with for basing and painting.

- 6 unique survivor miniatures with dynamic sculpts
- 1 exclusive “Lucio Parillo” artwork variant (collectible) that’s distinct from the standard print
- Alternate posing options or swap-outs (depending on the box variant you grabbed)
- Card sleeves or tokens that reflect Parillo’s color palette? In this set, you’ll see overlays and UI markers that pop visually on the board, aiding quick recognition during gameplay.

### Cards, tokens, and gameplay components (H2)
The card stock quality is typical CMON: sturdy, with decent gloss to reduce wear over many sessions. The Siege-style event cards and mission cards are clearly printed, with the Parillo artwork seeping into the character backs and mission art, a small luxury that rewards the players for reading flavor text aloud in dramatic fashion.

- 12 mission cards featuring variant objectives that make teams improvise more often
- 6 survivor ability cards with a balanced mix of power and risk
- A bite-sized set of tokens for traps, ammo, and equipment that feel tactile and click well under a tap-tap test
- A map board segment that locks into the modular layout with minimal fiddling

### The art box and Lucio Parillo’s influence (H2)
The art box is where a lot of the “premium” vibe comes from. Parillo’s palette—deep crimsons, desaturated greens, and a storm-blue accent—lends a cinematic feel to the surface art. It’s not just pretty to look at; the visuals evoke a mood that aligns with Zombicide’s post-apocalyptic vibe. If you’re a collector who keeps an eye on “artist editions,” this box is a good candidate for display, not just play.

### Scenery and terrain pieces (H2)
As with many Zombicide expansions, you’ll find a handful of terrain pieces in monochrome resin or plastic with prints that match the survivors’ colorway. These aren’t just decorative; they’re functional for zoning, line-of-sight blocks, and objective placement. They also serve as a neat puzzle piece for your table’s aesthetics—think “mysterious clinic wing” or “crashed supply truck” with a Parillo-inspired vibe.

### Assembly and quality notes (H2)
If you typically break out glue for miniatures, you’ll be relieved that these sculpts require minimal cleanup. The limbs fit fairly snugly, and the figures aren’t over-molded with flash, which means you can prime and paint without spending an eternity chasing tiny plastic bits. The base pegs align well with standard Zombicide bases, so any aftermarket base upgrades you enjoy will still work here.

## Thematic feel and the Parillo touch (H2)
### Artistic direction (H3)
Lucio Parillo’s influence is the star here. You’ll notice his signature edgework and a slightly more defined heroic anatomy on the survivors. The textures on clothing and armor reflect his painterly approach—there’s a sense of weight and fabric that you don’t always get in miniatures where the bodies look almost floaty. In practice, this translates to a set that looks great from arm’s length across the table and still shines under a good paint job up close.

### Narrative flavor (H3)
What’s fun about Parillo’s involvement is how it nudges the game’s flavor into a more cinematic frame. The-zombie-outbreak-as-destiny stories feel reinforced; these survivors look like they’ve stepped off a cover illustration and into your living room. It invites players to craft little micro-stories about each survivor, their traits, and the mission’s stakes—a reliably good practice for any group that loves doing voice-acting with accents that don’t survive the first round of combat.

## Gameplay considerations: does the set change how you play? (H2)
The core Zombicide mechanics aren’t rewritten by this set; you won’t suddenly discover a new win condition that breaks the game. Instead, you get a few thematic and mechanical nudges:

- Survivors with unique starting inventories that pair with mission cards to encourage specific playstyles (ranged vs. melee, support vs. frontlines).
- Objective cards that reward team-based play more than “solo heroism,” aligning with the ensemble feel of Parillo’s ensemble art.
- A few new encounter setups that feel like a mid-season arc in a zombie-apocalypse comic, which makes sessions feel more narrative and less grindy.

If you’re the type of player who loves to min-max a single survivor and push them through a gauntlet, you’ll still have options to do so. If you’re the more narrative-driven table, you’ll appreciate the art-driven immersion and the ability to weave a little backstory for your team.

## Value, packaging, and collectibility (H2)
Value here isn’t only about play. There’s a collectible angle that’s typical for artist editions and guest-driven sets. If you enjoy displaying your game collection or want a strong conversation piece on the shelf, this box scratches that itch. From a pure price-to-content standpoint, this set lands in the mid-to-high tier for Zombicide expansions. If you’re all about the art and the narrative, the price is comfortable; if you’re chasing the most optimally efficient gameplay, you might look for a discount or bundle.
