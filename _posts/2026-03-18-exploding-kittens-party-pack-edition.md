### Setup considerations
Setting up for 10 players is not a magic trick; it’s a logistics exercise. First, shuffle the deck thoroughly—no, not like you’re trying to wake up a sleepy cat; you want a fair distribution. Then deal each player a starting hand (we’ll discuss numbers later), and place the Exploding Kitten cards into the deck—one fewer than the number of players, if you want to keep it spicy, and with Defuse cards sprinkled in so your players don’t exit the table after the second round.

### The “you start” debate
In a room full of chaos, who takes the first turn matters. In the Party Pack, the order is the same as the original: the player to the left takes the first turn. But in a 10-player ring, you’ll be yelling “Nope!” at unpredictable moments and trying not to sneeze during a See the Future draw. It’s a social experiment with card sleeves.

## How to play with up to 10 players
### Core loop
On your turn, you play cards from your hand to manipulate the deck, steal turns, or skip danger. If you draw an Exploding Kitten and don’t have a Defuse, you’re out of the game. The Party Pack adds more ways to defend yourself, bluff, and accidentally draw a kitten, which is the true goal: cause chaos while pretending you’re completely calm.

### The Defuse conundrum
Defuse cards are your lifeline. They allow you to survive an Exploding Kitten. When you draw one, you use your Defuse card and then the kitten is reinserted into the deck somewhere that only your future self can predict. In a 10-player game, the probability of someone else hitting that Exploding Kitten eventually skyrockets, which is both terrifying and hilarious.

### The Nope culture
Nope cards are the lifeblood of the game’s humor. They let you interrupt other players’ actions, which often leads to dramatic standoffs and petty moral debates about who did what first. In the Party Pack, you’ll see even more use-cases for Nope: timing blocks, trolling, and optimizing your survival chances when a late-comer tries to defuse you with a clever one-liner.

### The new adds and expansions
The Party Pack includes expansions and new card types that are designed to scale up the chaos for a 10-player game. Expect more event cards, more interaction, and more moments when someone says, “Is this allowed?” Yes, yes it is.

## Strategy tips and play experience
### Early decisions
In a 10-player game, your early-turn decisions matter more than a coffee order at 9am. You want to keep a balanced hand: enough defuses for safety, but not so many that your opponents suspect you’re playing a long game. Early aggression can pay off if you catch a few players off guard, but it’s a high-wire act that can backfire when you’re the one who ends up on the sour end of a well-timed Nope.

### Mid-game chaos
As the table fills, you’ll see a rhythm emerge: a flurry of cards, a chorus of “Nope!” and a sudden, surprising end when someone defuses their way to victory after three twists of luck. The party edition rewards risk-taking, but it also punishes indecision with a cascade of skip-turns, see-the-futures, and surprising card combos that keep the table guessing.

### Late-game dynamics
When there are only a few players left, each decision becomes a micro-drama. That’s the beauty of party mode: even a simple card draw can decide who gets to hoard bragging rights for the rest of the night. The lingering scent of popcorn, the sound of someone shuffling cards like a magician, and the occasional chorus of groans when someone misreads a rule all contribute to the energy.

### House rules and house-keeping
Our group adopted a few light house rules to keep things moving: optional timer for turns (to keep the table from turning into a library), rotating dealer, and a quick table talk after key reveals to keep things friendly. We found that adding a 30-second timer per turn reduces “analysis paralysis” and makes the game feel more like a fast-paced comedy show than a courtroom drama.

## Variations and house-rule ideas
### Timer-based rounds
Impose a 60-second timer per turn. This keeps the energy high and reduces the risk of a player metastasizing into a rule-lawyer who spends more time explaining the card text than playing the card. The timer also helps to keep younger players engaged without letting the table descend into chaos that lasts longer than a sitcom.

### Bulletproof Defuse economy
If your group is new to Defuses, have a starting pool of Defuse cards equal to two per player. As the game progresses, allow players to trade Defuses with “defuse again” options if someone misplays—within reason. It adds a social contract moment: players agree to be gracious about lucky defuses and epic fails alike.

### Cat-card synergy set
Create mini-sets of Cat cards to trigger special effects when completed. This adds a layer of strategic collection to the chaotic chaos and gives players a tiny long-term goal beyond simply avoiding the exploding kitten.
