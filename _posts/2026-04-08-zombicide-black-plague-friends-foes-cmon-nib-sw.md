## Introduction
Zombicide Black Plague sits on the shelf like a dragon that forgot to learn chess and instead insists on teaching plagues how to roll dice. It is big, messy, and glorious in its own malfunctioning way. The Friends and Foes expansion arrives like a chaotic party guest who shows up with two extra limbs and a positive attitude about zombie apocalypse roleplay. In this review, we dive into the box, the rules, the shiny new minis, and the little moral dilemmas running through your head as you try to decide if you should rescue that survivor or push them toward the horde to save yourself.

The subject of this review is not a brand-new game; it is a thoughtful add-on that tries to deepen the drama without turning the co-op into a soap opera. If you are a CMON fan who can talk about the difference between a heroic zombie and an adversarial necro-human with the same enthusiasm you reserve for your favorite fantasy sorcerer, this expansion will likely scratch that itch. If you are here simply to plan your next gaming night with a group that enjoys tense decision-making and occasional accidental betrayal, you are in the right place.

### What is Friends and Foes?
The concept of Friends and Foes in Zombicide Black Plague is to push the core mechanism of rescue and risk into higher gear. The "Friends" aspect introduces some NPC-style survivors who can join your party under specific conditions, offering special abilities or loot, but they also come with demands and quirks that can complicate your plan. The "Foes" side, conversely, throws in new adversaries that change the dynamic of a typical encounter: they may require coordinated tactics, they may retreat, or they may merge into mini-swarms that behave differently from your standard walkers and riders.

In practice, you will be juggling alliance management along with zombie management. This is not a game for people who want to play heroically in a vacuum; this is a game for people who want to manage trust, panic, and resource allocation while wearing a helmet of chainmail and telling jokes that would make a lich wince.

In addition to the new characters and new antagonists, the expansion typically includes a handful of mission cards that scale the difficulty and add campaign flavor. The aim is to provide a sense of storytelling while preserving the tactile satisfaction of punching a plastic orc in the face with a plastic sword. If you like your group dynamics to resemble a space opera with swords and sorcery, you will likely feel right at home.

## Box contents and components
The box arrives in its usual CMON fashion: sturdy, generously illustrated, and slightly intimidating if you are a casual buyer who thought Zombicide was a coffee mug and a deck of cards. The miniatures are crisp, with clean sculpting that holds up under the standard basing and painting level most hobbyists gravitate toward. The plastic pieces are robust enough to survive the occasional frantic table flip without snapping, which is a relief when you roll a critical fail and accidentally catapult a plastic blood-splattered goblin across the room.

### New survivors and new foes
Friends and Foes adds a handful of new survivor tokens, each with their own backstory and unique active abilities. Some are more resilient in the face of panic; others provide buffs for your party but impose certain restrictions, creating exciting dilemmas. The Foes are not mere re-skins of existing zombies; they come with nuanced behaviors that require you to adapt your strategy on the fly. A common pattern you will encounter is a foe that can ignore line-of-sight restrictions or one that only becomes vulnerable after certain conditions are met. This encourages you to coordinate with your teammates, plan a few turns ahead, and accept that your best plan is going to require improvisation.

### New cards, tokens, and map elements
Along with new models, the expansion provides new mission cards, new loot items, and new tokens that track the Friends and Foes mechanics. The tokens are clear and easy to read, a welcome touch in a game that often rewards the rapid scanning of tiny symbols under poor light conditions. The mission cards are the real star here; they present setup variations that can keep the campaign feeling fresh across multiple play sessions.

### Set up and ease of play
If you are a seasoned Zombicide player, you will recognize the setup flow: lay out the board, place the zombie spawn tiles, allocate survivors, shuffle equipment, and start turning. The Friends and Foes expansion does not radically overhaul the base game’s setup. It adds a few new deck-building elements and some condition markers, but the core sequence remains intact. The initial setup may take a little longer than a vanilla game as you incorporate the new elements, but the payoff is a deeper, more textured co-op experience.

### How it changes the core gameplay loop
The core loop of Zombicide—explore, scavenge, fight, and rescue—remains intact. The expansion adds two critical wrinkles:
- Friends: You may recruit or ally with NPC-like survivors. They provide special skills but can react unpredictably in high-stress moments. Their presence forces you to consider moral choices, resource distribution, and potential betrayal scenarios that mirror a well-spun fantasy drama. You may need to decide if a friend is worth the risk of exposure or if they must be sacrificed for the greater good. The debates around this will amuse and frustrate your group in equal measure.
- Foes: The new enemy types introduce tactical variety. They can be faster, tougher, or cunning in ways that standard walkers are not. Some require focus-fire strategies; others reward players who split attention and coordinate. This keeps fights dynamic and prevents the box from offering a repetitive sequence of the same zombie encounter.

The rule book and learning curve
The rule book for Friends and Foes is clearly written but not necessarily short. It expects players to be comfortable with the base rules and the new modifiers. The learning curve is manageable for a mid-level group; novices might want to run a quick practice round or two before tackling the first full mission. As with most CMON expansions, there are a handful of edge cases that emerge only after several plays, but nothing so severe that it ruins the experience. If you have a player who loves the idea of a tense moral dilemma in a zombie apocalypse, you will find their head spinning with possibilities.

### Miniatures and theming
The miniatures carry the same standard as the base game: well-sculpted, with good sculpts and crisp detail. The color palette adheres to the Black Plague’s medieval fantasy vibe: deep browns, rusty reds, muted greens, and weapon highlights that pop on the table. The bases are robust, and the overall aesthetic strongly communicates that you are dealing with a world where knights, thieves, and necromancers share space with the undead. The overall feel is cohesive and consistent with the rest of the Black Plague line, which is no small feat given the volume of content CMON has produced for this franchise.

### Table presence and gameplay duration
A single session with Friends and Foes remains roughly in the 60–90 minute window per scenario, depending on the players and the map size. On the scale of epic co-op board games, this is a comfortable chunk of time for a gaming night, not a marathon. The presence of Friends and Foes does introduce occasional long-term planning, because you are managing a potential ally and a new foe at the same time. But this added complexity is what makes the experience feel more cinematic rather than a grind through a slate of identical zombie encounters.

### Strategies and tips
- Plan for the worst, hope for the best: When you have an ally who can risk exposure for a powerful effect, you might want to push for synchronized actions that maximize the ally’s advantage while minimizing risk to the rest of the team.
- Use the environment to your benefit: terrain and line-of-sight are still crucial. The new foes can take advantage of terrain features in ways that standard zombies do not. Setting up chokepoints and controlling the board’s flow remains a core skill.
- Manage morale and panic: The expansion makes morale a more salient factor. A panic-laden decision might save the group in one turn but doom it in the next if a Friend betrays or a Foe explodes into action.
- Balanced risk vs reward: It is tempting to chase the most valuable loot or the most powerful ally, but you must weigh that in light of the team’s current situation. The right risk at the wrong moment can flip a game from victory to spectacular failure.

### Campaign and replayability
Fans of Zombicide love campaigns because they let you see your group evolve over multiple sessions. The Friends and Foes expansion is designed to slot into this ongoing narrative without forcing you into a single rigid storyline. The new mission cards, when combined with the base game’s scenarios, allow for varied campaigns with different pacing and tension. You can tailor the difficulty by selecting missions that emphasize allies and foes that match the group’s preferred play style.

### Solo and co-op viability
If you prefer playing solo or with a smaller group, Friends and Foes still offers interesting options. The NPCs can be controlled by a single player as a sub-heroic device, providing a helpful layer of management that can keep a one-person board interesting. For groups looking for high tension, the expansion introduces a level of narrative that scales well with more players, but it remains accessible for a pair of players who want something more than a pure co-op slugfest.

### Quality of life and presentation
One of the underrated strengths of this expansion is its quality of components. The tokens are well-designed, the cards are readable, and the storage for the new pieces is reasonably organized. The artwork is consistent with Zombicide’s mature fantasy vibe, striking a balance between grim atmosphere and cartoonish fun. The production value remains high, and the expansion feels like a proper addition rather than a throwaway gimmick.

### The design intent and the designers’ approach
Designers in the Zombicide line have a knack for balancing scale, randomness, and narrative heft. Friends and Foes is a testament to that approach. It is not content to simply add more zombies; it seeks to create moments that feel earned rather than inevitable. When you finally pull off a coordinated rescue that requires you to trust an ally who might betray you next turn, you get a genuine thrill. It is not just about removing the square tokens on the map; it is about telling a micro-story with your friends as the cast and the undead as the chaotic backdrop.

### The best moments you might encounter
- A cunning ally saves the team at the last moment but reveals they have a ticking risk card you must manage.
- A Foe type that rushes down a corridor and collides with a group of zombies, turning a death trap into a spectacular last-ditch save.
- A mission that forces you to split up, forcing you to juggle two objectives in different corners of the map while under a looming time pressure.

### Common pitfalls and how to avoid them
- You overcommit to a single ally, leaving others exposed: Remember that social dynamics are the key here; keep a balanced approach so no one player can dominate the narrative or the board state.
- You ignore terrain: New foes may exploit it, so do not underestimate the value of a well-chosen choke point.
- You misinterpret the timing of the ally’s special ability: The tactical window can slip by if you do not coordinate with your team. Communicate clearly and assign two watchers for each critical turn.

## Final verdict

If you crave more narrative heft, tighter moral choices, and a tactical challenge that rewards careful planning, Friends and Foes delivers. The expansion respects the core co-op feel while injecting a jittery dose of unpredictability that makes each session feel like a different cinematic experience. The new mechanics feel intuitive after you play a couple of scenarios, and they scale nicely with player count. It is not a perfect expansion; there are occasional moments where the rules feel a touch overloaded, especially for newcomers, but the payoff is worth the learning curve. Overall, this is a solid upgrade for any Zombicide Black Plague owner who wants to level up the tension and the storytelling in their zombie-horror fantasy nights.
