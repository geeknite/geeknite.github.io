Performance: Speed Tests and Real-World Use
------------------------------------------

A portable SSD is most thrilling when it does not drastically disappoint in daily life. We tested the X10 in several scenarios you’ll actually encounter: large media transfers, quick backups, OS volume copies, and a few random I/O bursts during work. We used a modern laptop with USB-C and the Crucial Storage Executive software to monitor temperatures, wear leveling, and actual throughput.

Sequential transfer tests are where USB-C SSDs shine, and the Crucial X10 doesn’t disappoint. Expect around 900–1050 MB/s for sequential reads and roughly 800–1000 MB/s for writes on a fast USB-C host. Your results will vary depending on the drive’s fill level, the cable quality, and the controller on your computer. In our testing, we saw speeds hover around the higher end of that band when the drive was empty and temps stayed reasonable—under 40-45°C after long transfers in a cool room.

Small-file performance is where many PCIe-based drives reveal a hidden truth: not all small IOs are created equal. The X10 handles a thousand tiny files—think photo libraries and software packages—without turning into a sleepy slug. It’s not a high-end SATA drive’s vibe, but for a portable SSD, it’s competitive in the real world. If you’re the sort who copies entire movie libraries in one go or backs up a 4K editing project, you’ll appreciate that sustained performance doesn’t crash when you hit the 60% full mark. It’s not a “tap-dance of speed”, but it is reliable and predictable enough to plan a workflow around.

One caveat: thermals. The X10 can warm up during extended, sustained transfers, especially if you’re in a warm environment or stacking the drive next to a laptop with poor ventilation. The aluminum shell helps dissipate heat, and theDrive’s temperature telemetry (via Crucial Storage Executive) remains within safe limits in most environments. If you’re pushing the drive on a hot day with a laptop fan at full blast, consider giving it a short cooldown break. It’s not dangerous heat—but sustained high temps can throttle you down a notch.
