## Introduction: Why Audrey, Why Now?

If you’ve followed the Zombicide wave – the CMON juggernaut that turned mall walkers into miniatures with more personality than most of us in high school – you’ll know the core set is chunky, loud, and gloriously chaotic. ## What is Audrey the Bookworm?

Audrey is not your typical survivor. She’s a narrative engine disguised as a librarian who insists that knowledge is, occasionally, more lethal than a broken bottle and a good key card. In game terms, Audrey introduces a mechanic that rewards information gathering, bookish buffs, and the ability to leverage discovered lore for board control. The miniature itself carries the signature CMON polish: crisp sculpting, crisp lines, and a tiny pair of round reading glasses that splendidly catch the light as you tilt the table to admire the gloss.

### The Miniature and Paint Job

If you’re into paint-slinging, Audrey is a dream or at least a very entertaining challenge. The sculpt captures a poised posture, a notebook tucked under one arm, and that quintessential nerd chic: a cardigan with tiny elbow patches, a sweater chain of tiny beads, and a backpack that suggests she owns more fantasy novels than a small library can legally hold. The detail on her scarf, the pages of the book she holds, and even the little quill tucked behind her ear are proof that CMON didn’t cut corners to chase the exclusive hype. It’s a mini that begs for a photoshoot and a detailed close-up, preferably with a light source that emphasizes the subtle texturing of the fabric and the weathered leather bindings of the tome she guards.

In-hand feel is another strong point. Audrey feels sturdy, not fragile, which matters when you’re shoving her on a zombie-sodden board and praying she doesn’t topple over like a paperback avalanche. The NISB packaging ensures you’re not worrying about paint chipping or delicate parts shivering in the shrink. For collectors, that reliability matters as you rotate your display, whispering to yourself that you chose the option with the best shelf presence.

### Lore and Theme

Audrey’s theme leans into the classic nerd fantasy: survival by intellect. The narrative here is less about brute force and more about the quiet, stubborn confidence of someone who believes that a well-timed quote can rattle a disoriented abomination back into the creepy silence of a ruined city. The art direction matches the vibe: vintage maps, a library card faceplate as a badge of honor, and the kind of color palette that makes you feel like you accidentally wandered into a secret corridor of a ruined old-world library. It’s the sort of flavor that makes new players less anxious and long-time players more excited to explore new interaction loops with the core game rules.

The packaging of a Kickstarter exclusive matters because it’s a promise: you paid for something special, something you can’t just drop into the shopping cart later. Audrey arrives in shrink wrap (naturally; this is what NISB stands for in the wild), with the usual CMON joie de vivre printed on the back: a reminder that this isn’t just a zombie horde; it’s a nerdy, hopeful apocalypse where strategy and wit still matter.

The box insert is sensible and thoughtful. The card art maintains the same level of crisp edge and bold typography you expect from Zombicide, which is a relief because sometimes Kickstarter exclusives drift into “design by someone who forgot to bring an eraser.” Audrey manages to avoid those pitfall zones with grace.

Unboxing is a ritual, not a chore. If you value narrative compatibility, this is the kind of exclusive that earns its keep by giving players a reason to read more than the standard rulebook.

## Game Implementation: How Audrey Changes the Flow

Audrey is designed to slot into the existing Zombicide framework with a couple of clever modifiers. She brings a bookish buff bucket that triggers under specific conditions, typically when you engage with a scavenging or knowledge-based action rather than just swinging a weapon like a hero in a grindhouse flick. The net effect on play is not a radical rewrite of how Zombicide works; it’s a modular twist: you get more options, more narrative beats, and most importantly, a tasty justification for pausing the action to flip a page and mutter a quote about epistemology while a dozen zombies shuffle into formation.

From a tactical standpoint, Audrey’s rules encourage players to seek information. This doesn’t just pad your table time with flavor text; it can unlock discrete advantages that feel earned rather than handed to you on a silver platter. You’ll find yourself planning routes not only to maximize the number of zombie tags you can hit in a single turn, but also to trigger Audrey’s ability chain in a way that lets you read the situation like a battlefield novella rather than a serial-killer highlight reel.

The playtest sanity-check for Audrey is this: you’ll appreciate the pacing shift. The core Zombicide engine thrives on high-tension, high-munition, high-chaos moments. Audrey creates pockets of strategy within that chaos, giving you a chance to breathe, reflect, and use your knowledge advantage to outsmart the undead. It’s not a guaranteed win button; it’s a clever nudge toward smarter planning, which is exactly the kind of thing we geeks live for when we’re not busy arguing about whether a zombie should have a soul or just a great sense of rhythm.

## Value, Pricing, and the Exclusivity Question

Is Audrey worth the extra expense? The short answer: it depends on what you want from Zombicide and how much you enjoy the lore around your purchases. If you are a collector who loves a well-integrated theme, Audrey is the kind of exclusive that improves the shelf presence of your set and adds a thread for future narrative sessions. If you are a player who values most of your game experience from the board’s mechanical symmetry rather than the story corners, you may opt to view Audrey as an optional luxury.

From a price-performance perspective, Audrey’s value lies in three places: the mini’s physical quality, the unique mechanics tied to the exclusive, and the additional narrative weight she brings to your sessions. You don’t get a miracle gadget here; you get a thoughtfully designed character that enriches the game’s storytelling potential and offers a new way to approach the map’s layout. If that’s your jam, the cost starts to look more like an investment in recurring table time than a one-off splurge.

It’s a trade-off, as a good conspiracy novel should be.

Audrey’s design lends itself to both new players and veterans. For newcomers, she provides a thematic entry point that helps reduce the intimidation factor when first opening Zombicide’s big box. Audrey the Bookworm isn’t the loudest character in the Zombicide library, and she isn’t a revival of a classic berserker archetype. She’s something more delicate and equally powerful: a device for storytelling that quietly nudges players toward smarter decisions. If you love the idea of a post-apocalyptic city where library cards, borrowable lore, and a sharp wit can tilt the balance in your favor, Audrey is a winner.
