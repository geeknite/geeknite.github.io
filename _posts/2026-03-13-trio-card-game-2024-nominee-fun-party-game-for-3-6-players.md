### Setup: Easy as Pie (Unless You Hate Fun)

1) Shuffle the deck and deal three cards to each player. The starting player draws an additional card to seed the round.
2) Place the draw pile in the center and flip the top card to create the starting trio prompt.
3) Decide on a house rule regarding how strict you want the prompts to be. Short version: you can be as silly or as savage as the group agrees.

Pro tip: If your group includes a stack of disenchanted puzzle enthusiasts, consider setting a 60-second timer for prompts to keep momentum high. The goal is to keep the energy up, not to craft a doctoral thesis on comedic timing.

### Aesthetics and Accessibility

The cards use bright, high-contrast colors that make it easy to spot the suit and number in a glance. The art style is approachable and friendly rather than edgy, which is perfect for family game nights or streaming when you want to avoid the risk of a misfired joke becoming a scandal.

## How to Play: The Gist (with Extra Fun for the Daring)

Trio Card Game is built around three action types: Prompt, Play, and Score. At the start of a round, the prompt card reveals what kind of trio you need to assemble. It might be an answer, a physical action, or a silly statement that must tie to your own hand of three cards.

### Turn Flow

- On your turn, you may either draw a card or play a card to the central prompt. Playing is where you attempt to build or complete your trio. Each card has a role — one might add a word, another an action, a third a modifier that changes how your trio is judged.
- When you think your trio meets the prompt, you declare it and lay down your three cards. The group votes on whether your trio fits the prompt, awarding you points if the trio is validated.
- Then the next player takes a turn, and a new prompt card is revealed to keep the cycle moving.

The scoring is intentionally forgiving. Even if your trio is a little off-kilter, a sharp explanation and a confident smile can push you over the line. The goal is not to win every round with flawless setups but to keep the energy high and ensure everyone leaves the table with a story about that one ridiculous move you made.

### Examples of Prompts and Strategies

- Prompt type: Describe your ideal vacation. A common strategy is to use three cards that correspond to places, activities, and a personal anecdote. The better your story, the more points you’ll snag.
- Prompt type: Perform a three-word myth. This invites theatrical performance. The best players lean on timing and exaggeration rather than technical knowledge.
- Prompt type: Combine three items into a gadget. This tests improvisation; the most ridiculous combos often become the crowd favorite.

Pro strategy: Keep a small mental list of three quick story hooks you can pull whenever a prompt appears. For example, in a pinch you can pivot to a nautical voyage, a space mission, or a courtroom drama to unlock laughs and empathy from the table.

## Practical Tips for Cross-Table Success

- Encourage players to lean into storytelling. The more vivid your tale, the more you’ll sway opinions and wins.
- Consider using a two-round format for larger groups; round pairs tend to maximize laughter and keep momentum.
- For streams or content creation, assign roles like narrator or judge to different players to create a natural rhythm and reduce downtime.
- If you’re new to the game, keep prompts within a familiar theme (food, movies, travel) for faster buy-in.

### House Rules You Might Try
- The Silent Round: one round requires no talking; players must use gestures to convey their trio. It’s surprisingly harder than it sounds.
- The Double-Down Rule: players can spend two prompts on one turn to create a more elaborate trio, but this costs extra points to keep it fair.
