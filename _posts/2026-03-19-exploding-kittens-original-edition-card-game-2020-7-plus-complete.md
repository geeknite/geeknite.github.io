## What is Exploding Kittens Original Edition (2020)?
Exploding Kittens is a card-drafting, luck-and-guessing party game designed by Elan Lee and Matthew Inman (The Oatmeal) that places players in a tense, giggly sprint toward avoiding an “explosion” card. The Original Edition (2020 printing) maintains the same core premise as the earlier runs: you draw cards, you play cards, you try not to draw the Exploding Kitten card, and you heckle your friends with dramatic, over-the-top animations. The 2020 edition keeps the humor intact, the art alive, and the gameplay wonderfully simple to teach to newcomers.

In this edition, a typical game lasts anywhere from 15 to 30 minutes, depending on the number of players and how loudly you snort-laugh during the “Bomb Squad” moments. The deck includes a mix of attack, skip, shuffle, see-the-future, and of course, cat-themed bombs. It’s silly, it’s chaotic, and yes, it’s a perfect excuse to yell “BOOM!” while pretending you’re a high-stakes gambler running on pure luck and kitten-powered adrenaline.

### Box contents and components
The Original Edition 2020 isn’t exactly a luxury kitchen-sink experience; it’s a lean, mean, cardboard machine designed to get you from setup to explosion as fast as possible. You’ll typically find:
- A deck of Exploding Kitten cards

- Various action cards like Attack, Skip, See the Future, and Shuffle

The components aren’t premium in the traditional sense—no linen-card stock or fancy embossed finishes—but they’re sturdy enough to survive a dozen game nights and occasional table flips in the name of good vibes. The art, as with most of the license, is irreverent and silly, designed to invite laughter rather than contemplation. The cards shuffle well, the artwork remains legible, and the overall tactile feel is exactly what you’d expect from a game meant to be played with friends who don’t recognize the concept of “quiet game night.”

> Pro tip for hosts: keep a small trash bag handy for those dramatic “discard” moments, because you’ll want to reuse all the Defuse cards as props for your dramatic storytelling later on.

### How to play (in 60 seconds or less)
If you’ve played UNO, you’re halfway there. The goal is simple: avoid drawing an Exploding Kitten card. If you draw one, you’re out unless you can play a Defuse card to defuse the bomb, or somehow luck out with a mitigation card that saves your day. The action cards let you speed up, slow down, or mess with your opponents just enough to keep everyone on their toes.

Here’s the rough flow:
- Each player starts with a hand of cards. The exact number varies by edition, but you’ll be comfortable with a handful, not a ten-card shopping spree.
- On your turn, you may play one or more action cards and then you must end by drawing a card from the draw pile. If you draw the Exploding Kitten, you must use a Defuse card if you have one; otherwise, you’re out.
- The Defuse cards give you a few precious moments of mercy. After you defuse, the Exploding Kitten card goes somewhere back into the deck (often in a strategically painful spot for the next player).
- The game ends when only one player remains who hasn’t exploded. Congratulations, you’re the winner, and you’ve earned the right to brag about surviving kitten-splosion to your friends for the next week.

The rules are intentionally straightforward, which means you can learn the game in the time it takes to wait for your turn to come around. It’s not a cerebral exercise—it's a social exercise with a lot of opportunities for mischievous banter and dramatic gasps when someone refuses to skip or shuffle in a way that sabotages the table’s plans.

### The humor, theme, and why it works
Exploding Kittens thrives on comedic timing and a shared understanding that not every game needs to be about “winning” with strategic depth. The humor comes from the absurdist premise and the cat-centric, cartoonish violence (i.e., kittens with tiny bombs and exaggerated expressions). This isn’t about cruelty; it’s about communal chaos, a social contract to laugh at each other’s misfortune while keeping the mood light and silly. The kid-friendly age rating (7+) is a rough guideline; in practice, it’s best enjoyed by slightly older kids who can deploy sarcasm without getting offended.

The card art and goofy flavor text contribute to a culture around the game: you’re not just trying to win; you’re providing a show, a small performance for the table’s audience. That dynamic makes Exploding Kittens a fantastic party game, particularly for groups who want a shared, unpredictable experience rather than a tight, tactical battle. And yes, there’s a light poker-face element—some players pretend to be calm while their hands tremble as they try to decide whether to risk a Defuse or push their luck on a drawn card.

### How many players, how long, and how replayable is it?
- Typical player count: 2–5 (some editions support up to 6 with the right expansions, but the Original Edition is comfortable at 2–5).
- Gameplay time: roughly 15–30 minutes depending on player count and how much trash talk you’re willing to tolerate.
- Replayability: moderate-to-high for a party game. Every round is different due to the shuffled deck and the unpredictable plays of your friends. The Defuse cards introduce a rickety safety net, but you’ll still feel like you’re walking a tightrope between “survive” and “spark chaos.”

In short: it’s a brisk, repeatable experience that scales well to a few friends and a couple of family members who don’t mind a little messy laughter.

### Components quality and setup tips
The setup is simple, which is a blessing. Shuffle the deck, deal the initial hands, and insert the Exploding Kitten cards into the deck as prescribed by the rules (usually a few into the draw pile, depending on player count). The Defuse cards offset risk in a way that keeps the table engaged, not paralyzed by fear of impending doom. If you’re playing with kids, you’ll want to keep a closer eye on the lighter, non-violent humor and stick to the kid-friendly cards.

A few setup tips to smooth your night:
- Keep the Defuse cards accessible but not visible to everyone (mystery adds suspense).
- Place the discard pile within everyone’s sight to make the fate of cards visible and dramatic.
- Use a timer for turns if your group has a tendency to debate every single move for 20 minutes. The goal is fun, not torture.

### Strategy tips (for those who want a shred of control)
Let’s be honest: Exploding Kittens isn’t a strategic tournament game. However, you can win by reading your table. Here are a few light tips:
- When you have multiple Defuse cards, consider bluffing about your risk appetite. The table may fold and waste turns trying to outplay you.
- Use See the Future cards to peek at the next several draws. It won’t guarantee survival, but it helps you plan a safer path.
- Attack and Shuffle cards can be used to create psychological pressure. If you’re in the mood for chaos, lean into this and watch everyone scramble.

If you’re playing with newbies, don’t bore them with a minute-long explanation of probabilities. Keep it snappy, emphasize the humor, and let the cards do the talking.

### Accessibility and inclusivity considerations
Exploding Kittens is accessible in terms of rules math: it’s easy to learn, quick to teach, and enjoyable for players with varying experience levels. The visuals are bold enough to be seen by players with normal-to-good vision, and the humor translates across ages well. If you have players who prefer quieter sessions or more strategic heavy-lifting, you may want to pair this game with a more contemplative title instead of trying to squeeze overly serious vibes from a game designed for high-energy moments.
