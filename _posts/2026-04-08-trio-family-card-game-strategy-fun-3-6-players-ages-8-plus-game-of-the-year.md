## What Trio Brings to the Table

Trio aims to be accessible for younger players while still offering meaningful decisions for older ones. The core mechanic is simple: form trios by collecting three cards of the same number. But the twist is all the little ways you can disrupt your opponents and protect your own plans. Action cards let you steal a trio from someone else, swap hands with a neighbor, freeze a player while you slip in a fast trio, or redraw from the deck to re-roll your luck. The result is a game that moves quickly but rewards players who pay attention to the evolving table state.

If you have played card drafting or set-building games before, you will feel right at home. If not, don’t panic; the learning curve is gentle, and a quick teach will have your table racing toward their first trio in under five minutes.

## How to Play in a Nutshell

### Setup
Shuffle the 60 cards and deal seven to each player. The remaining deck becomes the draw pile, placed in the center. Each player starts with a small face-down pool of potential trios that you can reveal when the time is right. If you are playing with kids, you can use a simple scoring guide: form three trios and you have the lead.

### Turn Structure
On your turn you draw a card and have the option to play any trios you currently have on hand. You can also play an action card to disrupt others, swap with a neighbor, or block their progress for a short window. You must end your turn with at least four cards in hand. If a player forms a trio with their last drawn card, they may reveal it and claim the points.

### End of Round and Scoring
The round ends when the draw pile runs out or when a pre-agreed round length passes. Score triads (3 cards of the same number) with a base value, plus any bonuses from action cards. The game continues for a few rounds until a winner emerges. We recommend two or three rounds for a full night of play with a larger group; with fewer rounds you can go for a speed variant that offers intense two-minute rounds.

### Reading the Table
Logic matters more than luck here. Notice who is hoarding what, who is resisting the urge to declare a trio, and who appears to be the most eager to steal a prize. A good memory and a willingness to pivot when your initial strategy collapses will win the day more often than a single lucky draw.

## Strategy Deep Dive

### Early Round: Establish the Baseline
In the first few rounds, your aim is to build a flexible base of potential trios. Do not commit to a single plan; you want to keep several options open. If you pick up a few high-probability cards (like three copies of a number that appear frequently in the deck), you can accelerate into a trio without tipping your hand.

### Mid-Round: Disruption and Defense
As trios begin to form around the table, the real negotiation begins. Use action cards to either steal a near-finished trio from a leader or to force others into suboptimal plays. If you sense that someone is about to complete a high-value trio, a well-timed steal or swap can swing the lead. At the same time, protect your own progress with defensive moves or by keeping moderating cards in hand for the right moment.

### Endgame: The Final Flurry
In the last rounds, you will often see players jockeying for a single last trio that can push them to victory. If you can catch a player with a weak hand and a valuable trio in the same ten-card window, you can close the game by claiming a last moment score. However, if you overextend and give away your strategy, you risk watching a rival seal the win on you.

### 3- vs 6-Player Dynamics
With 3 players, you can track the deck more thoroughly and anticipate opponents’ moves with greater accuracy. You can also coordinate to form safe traps for each other. But be mindful of not appearing to collude; that will just spark hard feelings around the table. With 6 players, the game becomes a social puzzle. You will rely on your ability to improvise and to read the room. The more players, the less predictable the flow, which increases the chance that a single lucky card will break the balance. In multi-player games, the power of action cards is magnified, and misdirection becomes a legitimate strategy.

## Variants and Teaching Tools

- Speed variant: give each player two minutes to complete as many trios as possible. This is great for a high-energy game night when kids have loads of stamina and parents want to keep pace with the kids' excitement.
- Drafting variant: expose a small row of face-up cards and let players pick from that row. This adds a layer of planning and reduces wait time for players who aren’t comfortable with random draws.
- Family mode: require fewer trios per round for younger kids to reduce the cognitive load and length of play.
