## The Kit List and Concept
This project is inherently two things at once: a conversion exercise and a painting challenge. On the one hand, you have a Zombicide Black Plague zombie miniature—familiar, chunkier than your average 28mm figure, and outrageously characterful in its own undead way. On the other hand, you have a Frostgrave-inspired winter palette: stark grayscale sculpting, feather-light weathering, and pristine snow that looks like it fell yesterday but was immediately subjected to a blizzard of post-apocalyptic proportions.

The M203 aspect is primarily a conversion flourish. Frostgrave minis aren’t known for heavy weapons; Zombicide zombies sometimes arrive with improvised gear, but for this build we’ll cheat a little and graft on a grenade launcher lookalike. The result is a zombie that looks like it raided a post-apoc armory, not a fantasy coven. The aim isn’t literal accuracy; it’s narrative weight. Your table needs a character that says, I’m not just a zombie, I’m a zombie with a plan—something that is almost always better than a zombie with a limp and a blank stare.

## Unboxing, Assembly, and First Impressions
Unboxing is mostly standard-issue for Zombicide: chunky plastic that smells faintly of geometry and destiny. The Frostgrave elements come from the painting plan rather than from the kit itself; we’re borrowing a grayscale aesthetic more than swapping parts. The assembly phase is where you decide how much you want to convert. Do you graft on a tiny plastic M203 from a separate accessory sprue? Do you keep the base zombie’s arms as-is and simply fashion a makeshift launcher from bits of sprue, green stuff, and a stubborn bit of glue that somehow always resists time and gravity? The answer, as with so many of my hobby decisions, is: yes, all of the above, but with a plan.

Key tips for assembly:
- Wash thoroughly. Zombicide plastics can sting your fingers with tiny release agents; a quick soak in warm soapy water pays dividends.
- Dry-fit first. Nothing kills enthusiasm like a misaligned zombie forearm glued into an M203-shaped blob.
- Keep a spare sprue. The M203 barrel is finicky; a little reserve plastic saves hours on cleanup.
- File the base a touch. A stable, flat base makes painting easier and reduces wobble on the gaming table.

## Painting Plan: Monochrome Snow with a Familiar Twist
The core concept is grayscale with a snow overlay. Monochrome painting is one of those techniques that sounds simple until you try to pull the tonal range from pure black to near-white without relying on color cues. We’ll start with a grayscale primer, work up midtones with a careful light source, and finish with a snow layer that doesn’t collapse into comic-book white fluff. The challenge is to keep the zombie feel intact while applying winter texture that looks both realistic and cinematic.

Here’s the plan in rough order:
- Prime in a mid-gray. A neutral gray base allows you to push both black shadows and white highlights without fighting an overwhelming color bias.
- Establish a full grayscale gradient on the skin and clothing. No color—just values.
- Add snow on top with a light, dry brush technique to simulate powder; avoid masking details in the process.
- Dry-brush metallics for any metal bits on the M203 conversion. Keep the metal slightly dulled to avoid shiny sci-fi look; this is a zombie with a rifle, not a showroom display.
- Apply light weathering to simulate frostbite and ice crystals. A tiny touch of white pigment on raised edges sells the snow effect without looking cartoonish.

## Step-by-Step Painting Tutorial
The following steps are written for those with a basic brush kit but also designed to be flexible if you’re new to monochrome or snow textures.

1) Priming and base grayscale
- Prime the figure with a neutral gray spray in a well-ventilated space. If you’re into safety first, wear a mask and pretend you’re a mad scientist. The aim is to create a smooth, even canvas.
- Block in the darkest shadows with a black wash. Let it settle into recesses and nooks; we want depth, not chaos.
- Build up midtones with a mid-gray. Apply in thin layers, avoiding hard edges. The goal is to create a believable grayscale range across skin, clothes, and the improvised armor plates on the M203.
- Lift highlights with a lighter gray. Use a dry brush technique on raised surfaces to simulate light catching on rough skin and fabric texture. You’re not painting a photograph; you’re painting a rumor of light that hints at a lurking presence.

2) The monochrome skin and fabric values
- Flesh tones in a grayscale painting can feel spooky. Emphasize texture by dry-brush weathered areas and keep the edges slightly soft to mimic gnawed skin. If you want a little more drama, introduce a muted blue-gray for bruised areas in the shadows—but resist the urge to color shift. We’re staying grayscale, not mood-ring.
- For fabrics, push a tactile look by using slightly cooler midtones and then a lighter edge highlight along seams. Zombicide cloth often bears battle-worn texture; emulate that with gentle stippling and a few random specks of lighter gray for dented fabric.

3) The M203 conversion and metal bits
- The M203 piece should feel like a separate object attached to the zombie arm. Use a pin-and-sleeve approach if possible, or a sturdy glue that doesn’t melt the plastic. Paint the launcher in a steel range: base metal with a black wash, then highlight with a fine silver. The trick is to keep the weapon readable without turning it into a sci-fi blaster.
- Add bolt-heads and tiny screws with a dot of white or pale gray; it helps sell the metallic feel without going full chrome. Subtle weathering is your friend here; a few light brownish-black streaks simulate oil and grime that might accumulate on a live grenade launcher.

4) Snow texture technique
- Snow isn’t just white. Real snow has depth, shadows, and reflected light. Start with a thin white wash to brighten raised areas, then apply a powdered snow texture with a dry brush. Focus on the tops of shoulders, helmet edges, the top of the M203 sight, and any crevices where snow would naturally pile.
- Use a touch of gloss medium mixed with white for wet-looking snow on the higher edges or ice patches. Frostbite is only cool if the overall figure doesn’t look like it rolled through a laundry basket of cotton.
- Do not overdo the snow. You want a light, convincing layer. Too much snow makes the miniature look like a pastry—delicious, but not what you’re going for.

5) Weathering and final touches
- Apply a subtle graphite or dark wash along the base of the figure to ground it. This helps the monochrome scheme feel cohesive and prevents the model from floating in space.
- Weather metal bits with a touch of rust pigment. Zombies with improvised weapons should feel rugged, not pristine. The rust adds a narrative layer, suggesting the gun hasn’t seen a clean maintenance cycle in years.
- Finish with a final matte seal. Monochrome snow looks best when the surface reads as natural; a satin or matte finish reduces glare and chisels out that cinematic feel.

## The Frostgrave Twist: Why Snow Was Even Worth It
Frostgrave is a game about wizards, thieves, and the occasional dragon that gets a little too friendly with the toolkit. But when you borrow their aesthetic for a non-Frostgrave zombie, you get a striking visuals effect: grayscale shadows, crisp highlights, and the sense of a frigid battlefield where the wind gnaws at your ears more than your nerves. The monochrome snow look aligns well with Frostgrave’s cinematic vibe, but it’s the snow that ties everything together. A simple snow layer transforms a standard zombie into something you’d expect to see in a winter siege—an undead sentry that has somehow learned to tolerate subzero temperatures, if only to spite you.

If you want to push the Frostgrave vibe even more, consider adding a tiny snow cobweb of frost on the M203’s iron sights or a couple of ice crystals on the gun’s edges. Tiny touches here yield big results: the figure reads as a winter-ravaged unit rather than a generic zombie with a plastic button stuck to its arm.

## Basing, Diorama, and Table Presence
A good base is the stage where your painting comes alive. Since we’ve embraced a snow theme, the base should echo frost: a light gray stone with a powdering of white and a hint of icy blue in the crevices. Here are some base-building tips:
- Use a textured basing paste to create ground frost. Apply in patches and sprinkle with fine white sand to simulate snow drift.
- Paint the base with cool gray tones to keep the overall contrast consistent with the monochrome palette.
- Add tiny tufts of dead grass to emphasize the bleak winter setting. Make sure they’re subdued; you want the zombie as the focal point, not a field of cartoonish greenery.
- Place the model slightly off-center on the base to give a dynamic feel; a static pose quickly becomes dull, even if the grayscale is dramatic.

For diorama enthusiasts, this zombie can anchor a scene where other minis are trudging through snow or a ruined outpost. If you’re feeling extra ambitious, create a small vignette with a blue-tinted light source that mimics the glow of a distant lantern. The grayscale palette will pick up the cool light nicely and give your entire display a cohesive, cinematic arc.

## Photography Tips: Making Monochrome Snow Pop on Screen
- Lighting: Use a soft, diffused light from the side to highlight textures without creating harsh reflections on the snow. A little side-light helps reveal micro detail on the armor plates and the M203.
- Contrast: In post-processing (or your preferred shot setup), increase contrast slightly to separate the grayscale values. Snow should stay bright, but not glare white; push the shadows a tad for depth.
- Angles: Photograph from slightly above eye level to capture the snow texture on the helmet and shoulders. A lower angle can look impressive, but it may obscure the gun’s details.
- Background: A neutral gray or cool-toned sky backdrop keeps the figure readable. Avoid busy backgrounds that steal attention from the grayscale drama.

## How This Standalone Build Plays at the Table
The M203 variant is primarily a storytelling tool on the tabletop rather than a pure rules-based upgrade. You can treat it as a “leader” or “special zombie” in narrative scenarios. It isn’t a literal stat boost in most rulesets, but the visual impact matters: your opponent sees a well-painted, clearly defined model with a strong silhouette. The conversion communicates that this zombie has a backstory: it’s a scavenger that improvised a weapon from wreckage, a symbol of persistence in a winter hellscape, and a reminder that even the undead deserve a thumbs-up when the brush is applied correctly.

From a hobby perspective, this project demonstrates how far monochrome snow can push a model. If you’re teaching a new painter, this build exercises layering, edge highlighting (without color), weathering with pigments, and the delicate balance of white snow against dark shadows. It’s also a practical exercise in safety: you’ll learn how to handle small weathering powders without turning your entire workspace into a snow globe gone wrong.

## The Verdict: Do I Recommend It?
Yes, with enthusiasm tempered by practical caveats. The monochrome snow technique is an excellent way to expand your painting toolkit without jumping into full-color chaos. It trains you to use light and shadow as the primary storytellers, with snow providing a tactile texture that reads well on camera and in person. The M203 conversion, while not strictly necessary, adds a lot of character and narrative weight to the model. It’s not a “how-to” for every zombie, but for those who want a standout piece at a gaming table or in a display case, this approach delivers.

Pros:
- Strong visual contrast with grayscale values and snow texture.
- The M203 conversion adds story and tabletop presence.
- The technique teaches disciplined layering, shading, and weathering without relying on color.
- The base and diorama potential elevate the model from a tabletop token to a narrative centerpiece.

Cons:
- It can be intimidating for beginners if you try to execute every detail perfectly on the first pass.
- A monochrome scheme can look flat if you don’t maintain tonal variation and texture.
- The M203 conversion requires careful assembly; rushing can ruin the sculpture’s proportions and alignment.

If you’re on the fence, I recommend starting with a standard grayscale zombie and then adding a light snow wash as a separate project. Once you’ve mastered the tonal ladder, you can escalate to a more complex weapon conversion like the M203 and feel confident that your results will be dramatic rather than merely imposing.
