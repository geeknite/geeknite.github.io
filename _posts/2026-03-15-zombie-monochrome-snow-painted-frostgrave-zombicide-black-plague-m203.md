## Painting Plan: Monochrome Snow with a Familiar Twist
The core plan remains grayscale with a convincing snow overlay, but the expanded version leans into:
- a more deliberate tonal plan that uses mid gray film as a scale laden neutral instead of relying on black and white extremes.
- a narrative light source: imagine a flickering lantern or distant muzzle flash that creates a dynamic light angle across the torso and helmet.
- a crosshatched pattern on clothing to simulate fabric texture without committing to color.

How to think about tonal ranges:
- shadows: deep but not flat black, enriched with blue gray undertones to preserve depth in the recesses.
- midtones: broad fields of mid gray on skin and fabric to establish mass and volume.
- highlights: crisp light gray or almost white on the highest edges to simulate snow etched surfaces.

## Step by Step Painting Tutorial: Expanded and Detailed
We ll extend the original steps with more nuance and a few extra sub steps for those who want to chase perfection.

1) Priming and base grayscale
- Prime with a neutral gray spray from a slightly elevated angle to avoid pooling.
- Apply a black wash to establish deep shadow regions, but avoid flooding the eyes or mouth; keep facial details legible.
- Build midtones with a controlled gray scale, layering gradually to preserve smooth transitions.
- Lighten exposed surfaces with an even lighter gray, and test edge highlights on raised parts to check snap.

2) The monochrome skin and fabric values
- Skin: add a touch of texture by stippling rather than heavy brush strokes. Build texture by dry brushing with a granular approach.
- Clothes: emphasize seams with slightly darker midtones; a cool palette helps the armor plates look metallic without color.

3) The M203 conversion and metal bits
- The launcher should read as metal: base steel with a nominally darker wash to pick out raised detail.
- Edge highlights on the barrel and sight with a fine brush. Use a tiny amount of dry brushed light with a gentle sweep for a worn metal appearance.
- Paint minor screws and rivets with a dot of light gray or white to create tiny focal points.

4) Snow texture technique
- Build up the snow with multiple light passes; start with a white wash at the tops, then apply a powdered snow medium with a stiff brush.
- Add a glaze of blue white to simulate cold reflections in the creases; a glaze keeps the snow from looking chalky.
- Use a gloss medium sparingly to simulate wet ice patches, but keep the majority of snow matte to preserve the wintry look.

5) Weathering and final touches
- Apply subtle soot along engine greebles or the base to imply spent heat or road dust.
- Paint a micro thin ring of frost on the M203 s muzzle—this reads as a defensive measure against condensation, not a sci fi aesthetic.
- Seal with a matte or satin spray that reduces glare and locks the contrast in place.

## Photography Tips: Making Monochrome Snow Pop on Screen
- Lighting: Use a diffuse key light to minimize harsh shadows, then add a side light to reveal texture on the armor and snow.
- Contrast: Push the tonal curve in post production slightly; the snow should be bright but not glaring. The shadows should hold detail to maintain depth.
- Angles: A three quarter view from slightly above eye level works best to show off the M203 and the snow texture on raised surfaces.
- Background: Keep it a neutral gray, or use a cool toned backdrop that does not distract.
