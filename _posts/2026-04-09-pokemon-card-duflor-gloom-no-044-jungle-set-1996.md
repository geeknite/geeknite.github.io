## Introduction: Jungle Set, Duflor, and the 044 Mystery
The Jungle Set was the wild, leafy evolution of the original base set. It brought new art, new vibes, and a whole lot of cherishing for anyone who grew up trading under fluorescent store lights. Card No. 044 in that set is the Gloom card, known in some languages as Duflor. This is not just a number on a glossy rectangle; it’s a squat little badge of mid-90s optimism, when Koga’s secret poison became a collectible and your little pocket budget could feel like a treasure map.

For those of us who adore the dual identity of language and nostalgia, Duflor’s French name adds a touch of cosmopolitan whimsy. It’s like your card has a passport and an allergy to small talk. The 044 slot isn’t the flashiest of the Jungle lineup, but it’s one of those cards that quietly holds a place in the hearts of collectors who enjoy the long game: the art, the flavor text, the subtle color palette, and the stories that come with secondhand sleeves and a dusty binder spine.

In the spirit of Geeknite, we’re not here to pretend this card is the single greatest piece of cardboard ever minted. We’re here to celebrate the charm, the quirks, and the weird little tricks this card offers to people who love collecting as a form of personal archaeology.

## The Card Design: The Green, the Purple, and the Poisons
### Artwork and Aesthetic
Gloom is that purple blob with a droopy face and big, droopy leaves. Jungle’s rendition keeps the vibe from the Base Set era but grants it a more tropical backdrop and a slightly punchier color balance. The leaves look like they’ve considered chaos theory and then decided to photosynthesize instead. The art isn’t cleaning up the gym at the end of a long workout; it’s more like a mood-lit forest where the critters are suspiciously polite about your candy habit.

The French name Duflor doesn’t change the card’s personality: it’s still a Basic Pokémon with an oddly charming silhouette. The Jungle set version of Gloom captures a transitional look—part classic retro Pokemon, part late-90s experiment in showing off more vibrant greens and purples. It’s a card that could have hung out on a shelf next to a lava lamp and felt right at home.

### Rarity, Print, and Condition Cues
In Jungle, No. 044 is not a holo foil; it’s a standard non-foil glow with a clean print. Condition matters in a big way for nostalgia value. The art line is simple, which helps hide wear in ways that some more complex art cards can’t. A well-loved Gloom from the Jungle set may show light edge wear, slight whitening on the back corners, and a gloss that’s more ‘smirk’ than ‘pop.’ If you’re chasing a PSA 9 or 10, you’ll be looking for crisp corners, unblemished surface, and centering that isn’t playing tricks on you. It’s a world where a tiny speck of dust on the scanner can become a PSA0 if you’re not careful—okay maybe not that dramatic, but you get the idea.

### Language Variation: Duflor vs. Gloom
Language variants aren’t just a font choice; they’re a reminder of the global love for Pokemon. Duflor is the French iteration of Gloom, and encountering it as a collector can feel like discovering a secret handshake with the past. It’s not a separate card in terms of gameplay; it’s a different naming convention, a reminder that these little cardboard friends traveled a long way to end up cataloged in our binder pages. If you’re completing a language-themed collection, Duflor is the MVP of “edutainment via trading card.”

## Gameplay and Utility: Does a 1996 Card Still Do the Job?
In the actual TCG, Gloom is a Colorless/Grass-type—depending on the year’s errata and the card’s base printing. The Jungle version of Gloom typically arrives with a couple of classic attacks that feel a bit like a warm-up routine: something that applies a status (like Poison) and maybe a couple of Energy cards. The utility in today’s metagame is niche at best; in the world of casual joe-crafting deck-building and nostalgia-driven play, it shines as a “cool but not broken” addition that reminds players that values weren’t about power creep; they were about mood and a sense of collecting being a quest rather than a rush to the finish line.

If you’re a collector who also likes to play, Gloom in Jungle-era form is a reminder that the game’s early power balance favored simpler combos, slower pacing, and the thrill of flipping a card you’ve looked at a thousand times and going, “Yes, this little guy still brings joy.” So, whether you’re using it in a joke deck with a Duflor homing beacon or as a nostalgic nod in a casual game night, it has a place—just don’t expect it to win your local tournament unless your plan is to out-meme your opponent with cuteness power.

## The Market: Value, Vibe, and Verifiable Nostalgia
### Pricing Sketches and Grading Impact
Vintage Jungle cards like Gloom No. 044 ride a market wave that’s more about condition than pure rarity. The non-foil prints still fetch a decent premium when well-centered and pristine; a PSA 9 or 10 can command a respectable chunk, especially if the card shows minimal wear and the colors stay true to that early-90s vibe. For casual collectors, a mid-grade Jungle Gloom can be an affordable entry point into the broader Jungle Set nostalgia without forcing you to remortgage your gaming hobby. The key: be patient, watch for centering quirks, and beware of common edge wear in the bottom corners—the telltale signs of a card that’s loved and then spent years in a binder.

### Long-Term Collecting and the Jungle Niche
If you’re thinking long-term, Jungle Set cards—Gloom included—tend to hold steady or appreciate slowly with a bias toward clean copies and language variants. The fun of Jungle is the shared memory: a time when the franchise expanded beyond the original roster and started to become a world-building exercise in color, culture, and the occasional misprint rumor. If you’re building a Jungle-themed collection, you’ll want to mix in both popular and under-the-radar cards, because the story of the Jungle era isn’t just about the big hits; it’s about the quiet sleepers that, decades later, reveal their charm in the binders of grown-up kids.

## Collecting Tips: Building a Jungle Set with Doflor in Mind

- Consider graded vs ungraded: PSA 9 is often a sweet spot between cost and resale value for mid-90s cards.
