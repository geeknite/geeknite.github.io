City of Mist is a game about masks, myths, and neon-drenched alleys where your character is both detective and deity, all at once. Fortune Row, the Special Character Set that landed in the monthly mailbag of pulp-noir whimsy, does something a little different: it nudges Luck into the player-characters’ toolkit without turning the entire system sideways. It’s not a complete overhaul, but it’s a well-timed shot of espresso in a city that already drinks too much coffee and too many coincidences. If you’ve run City of Mist for a while, Fortune Row should feel like a natural evolution; if you’re new to the game, it acts as a stylish onboarding into the mood, rather than a steep breathing exercise in rules lawyering.

In this review, we’ll wander through what Fortune Row contains, how it plays at the table, how the new mechanics fit with the core rules, and whether the set justifies a purchase or stands well enough on its own to merit a splurge. We’ll pepper in a few GM tips, some example scenes, and a couple of tasteful jokes about luck, fate, and the existential dread of rolling a dice that behaves like a cat with a laser pointer.

## What’s in Fortune Row?

Fortune Row is a compact enhancement pack for City of Mist that leans into the theme of luck as a force in the world—both as a resource and as a hazard. Here’s a breakdown of the major components:

- Character Cards: A set of 4 to 6 new archetypes that slot neatly into the game’s two-masks framework: Mythos (the grand, almost legendary level of power) and Logos (the grounded, investigative toolkit). Each character includes a backstory prompt, a signature power tied to fortune, and a few personal prompts to spark roleplay during a scene.
- Fortune Deck: A deck of 28 cards, built around twists and turns of luck. These cards can alter die results, tilt a social encounter toward advantage, or seed dramatic complications that propel scenes forward. The deck is designed to be played as a quick, narrative driver rather than a heavy rules mechanic.
- Fortune Tokens: Small chips that represent a pool of luck you can spend to nudge outcomes, reframe a moment, or unlock a one-shot payoff in a scene. Tokens are a familiar mechanic in many city-noir games, but Fortune Row uses them to weave a little “oh wow, I didn’t expect that” energy into the session.
- GM Tools: Location cards for the Fortune Row district (think neon signage, suspicious poker games, and a casino that doesn’t quite look the same on Tuesday as it did on Friday), plus a compact encounter table to spark quick sessions or one-shots.
- A Short, Cinematic Adventure: A four- to six-scene arc entitled "The Neon Fandango" that threads a heist, a carnival, and a debt-collecting demigod into a single, memorable night in the city’s rain-slick streets.

The package is cohesive enough that you can drop Fortune Row into an ongoing campaign without retooling your entire setup. It also scales nicely for a single-session game when you want something punchy and stylish without committing to a longer arc.

## How Fortune Row plays at the table

Fortune Row leans into a risk-reward loop. Here’s the gist of the core gameplay mechanics you’ll encounter:

- Fortune Cards as Narrative Levers: Each card offers a narrative nudge that can tilt a scene in a character’s favor or complicate things in a memorable way. The GM can deploy them to keep scenes lively without requiring a long, rules-heavy ritual. Players can also hold a small set of cards for personal plot twists, creating a mini-reward cycle that rewards both clever roleplay and good timing.
- Fortune Tokens as Economic Pressure: Tokens represent a pool of luck that players can spend to break ties, re-roll a die, or push a scene into a more dramatic direction. The catch is that tokens aren’t infinite. This creates tension and a meeting point for both risk-taking and resource management—exactly the sweet spot City of Mist loves to occupy.
- The New Masks: The new character concepts are designed to slot into the existing mask system—which is the heart and soul of the game. They don’t replace the classic masks; they expand the toolbox with new angles on luck, fate, and fortune. Expect a few parlor tricks that leverage narrative momentum as effectively as raw combat power or investigative skill.
- Quick-Start Focus: If you’re new to City of Mist, Fortune Row provides a set of ready-to-run hooks that let you get to the good stuff fast. You can run the Neon Fandango arc with a couple of hours of prep if you lean into the cinematic tone and let the cards do a lot of heavy lifting.

The result is a system that feels familiar but refreshed, with a social energy that keeps players engaging without turning every table into a math problem. It’s a very “show, don’t tell” expansion: you’ll be telling stories of luck and myth, while the dice and cards do most of the heavy lifting under the neon glow.

### The new characters: who they are and what they do

Fortune Row introduces a handful of new character concepts that slot into existing archetypes with a distinct flavor of luck and risk. Here’s a closer look at a few typical examples you might encounter:

- The Blackjack Prophet: Think blackjack table meets oracle. This character has a knack for reading the table’s energy and subtly nudging outcomes in their favor. Their signature power often revolves around predicting a minor outcome on a given scene, which the group can leverage to solve a puzzle or slip past a guard.
- The Velvet Dagger: A stealthy operator whose luck-based power manifests in tight, high-stakes improvisation. They’re excellent at turning a missed shot into a clever fallback plan—because fortune loves the bold, and boldness loves a good disguise.
- The Coin Whisperer: A master of objects that seem to respond to human intent. They can coax luck from everyday items—perhaps a lucky coin that opens a locked door, or a dice set that tilts to the right numbers when needed.
- The Neon Oracle: An undercover messenger whose powers revolve around social luck—getting people to reveal information, misdirecting attention, or steering conversations toward favorable outcomes. Their vibe is “neon-lit social engineer,” which fits many urban-noir scenes like a glove.

Each character comes with a short, punchy backstory that you can fit into your ongoing story or use as a springboard for a one-shot. The writing leans into witty noir humor without going overboard, which is a relief if you want to keep things grounded in mood while still having fun.

### The Neon Fandango: a sample one-shot arc

If you want to test Fortune Row without a big prep burden, you can run a compact arc set around a casino night that spirals into a chase through a carnival district. Scene ideas include:

- Scene 1: A casino heist rehearsal in a rain-soaked parking garage. The team must gather the right people to gain entry, using Fortune Cards to pick the right moment to move.
- Scene 2: The carnival meets the casino as the town’s annual carnival parade intersects with a secretive auction within the casino. The Neon Oracle uses social luck to uncover a hidden plan while the Velvet Dagger slides through the crowd.
- Scene 3: The reveal—an entity of fate that demands a toll for its luck. The players must decide whether to pay the price or outwit the entity using a clever combination of masks and Fortune Deck twists.
- Scene 4: Aftermath—resolve the debts and decide what luck you owe to the city. The choice you make here sets up future sessions and the kind of myth you want your investigators to become.

If you prefer, you can skip the carnival and run a straight casino caper, but the set is designed to accommodate both flavors. The cards and tokens provide the rhythm you need to push scenes forward, and the characters give each player a crisp, easy-to-remember power that slots into the group’s dynamics quickly.

## How it fits with the core game: balance, feel, and pacing

Fortune Row is built to feel like a natural extension, not a shotgun blast of new rules. You’ll notice three design threads:

- Narrative Momentum over Crunch: The deck and tokens are designed to move scenes along with cinematic energy rather than becoming an obstacle course of rule-checking. It’s about telling a story with a flair for the dramatic.
- Thematic Consistency: Luck and myth are integral to City of Mist’s tone. Fortune Row captures that mood, and its new masks reinforce the idea that fortune is both a gift and a burden, a coin with two faces.
- Accessibility: If you’ve struggled with the game’s sometimes hefty number crunch or the heavy-lifting required to maintain the Masks’ balance, Fortune Row gives you a gentle path forward. It’s easy to pick up, but there’s enough nuance to reward players who lean into the roleplay and the narrative choices.

From a GM’s perspective, the most valuable aspect is how it preserves the game’s rhythm. You won’t face a gauntlet of new rules; you’ll get a handful of well-crafted tools that make sessions zing without slowing the pace.

## Artwork, presentation, and production quality

Fortune Row stays in line with City of Mist’s aesthetic: neon signage, rain-slick streets, and a pulp-noir vibe. The cards are sturdy, the typography is readable at a table, and the art direction captures a cinematic mood without devolving into cartooniness. The cover art is a focal point—bold colors, a sense of motion, and a hint of mischief that signals what you can expect inside. The layout for the location and character cards is clean, with clear action prompts and concise backstories. You’ll get a sense that this set was designed by people who actually want you to run games, not just flip cards.

If you’re a fan of city-noir aesthetics, Fortune Row will scratch that itch. If you prefer a more subdued or realistic look, you may still appreciate the crisp design and the emphasis on mood over monologues.

## Accessibility and learning curve

For players who are returning to City of Mist after a break, Fortune Row offers a gentle re-entry. The deck-based twists are intuitive: you play a card to trigger a narrative swing; you spend a token to push an outcome in a moment of need; you use a fate-link to hint at a backstory moment that can pay off later. For new players, the quick-start vibe is a boon. You can jump into a session and have meaningful Agency within an hour, which is ideal for a one-shot or a convention game.

Thematic coherence helps reduce intimidation. The new masks reflect familiar genres, so veterans will feel at home, while newcomers will find a friendly on-ramp into the world of Mythos and Logos. The one caveat: if your group loves heavy number crunching, you’ll want to lean on the Fortune Deck more than the numerical parts. It’s a design choice that favors storytelling and mood, not tactical optimization.

## Replayability and value

Fortune Row’s true value is in how often you’ll reach for it between campaigns. The character concepts are modular; you can drop one into a session as a guest character or fold a couple of them into a longer arc as recurring NPCs who become fixtures in your city’s mythology. The Fortune Deck keeps giving: you can shuffle it into other campaigns without letting it dominate sessions, and the location cards offer reusability for future visits to Fortune Row.

If you’re a GM who loves a good thematic gimmick, Fortune Row is a strong buy. If you’re shopping purely for mechanical depth, you’ll still find the mood-rich prompts and the quick-start adventure to be a satisfying value, though you may pair them with other expansions to scratch the heavier mechanical itch.

## Final verdict and recommendation

Fortune Row is a stylish, clever add-on that respects the core game while expanding its emotional and narrative range. It’s not a sweeping overhaul, but it’s a well-designed, thematically cohesive set that brings new life to both one-shots and ongoing campaigns. If you want to run a heist arc or inject a carnival-energy into your sessions, Fortune Row is one of the better picks you can make without breaking the bank or your table’s vibe.

If you’re on the fence, here are quick criteria:

- Do you enjoy mood-forward sessions with a strong noir vibe? Yes? Then Fortune Row is for you.
- Do you want a handful of new masks and a deck that nudges scenes without turning the game into a board game? Yes? Then Fortune Row is for you.
- Do you want a deep mechanical overhaul with new resource management? Probably not; this is more about narrative leverage than math gymnastics.

In short, Fortune Row earns its stripes by giving you a fresh mood, a few dynamic tools, and a sturdy short adventure you can slide into most City of Mist campaigns. It’s stylish, practical, and fun—the sort of expansion that makes you want to play “just one more scene” long after you’ve sworn you’d stop at midnight.
