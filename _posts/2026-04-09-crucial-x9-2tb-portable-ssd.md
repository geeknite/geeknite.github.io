## Real-world tests and impressions

I ran a few practical tests to frame the X9’s behavior in everyday scenarios. The drive is clearly optimized for speed, but the real magic comes from how consistently it performs across different tasks. Here are some of my takeaways from the lab and the field.

- Sequential transfers: Moving sizable video folders or game libraries pours through the X9 with a speed that feels like a small upgrade over internal NVMe drives, especially when the host is a modern laptop with a fast USB-C port. In my tests, sustained reads hovered near 900-1,000 MB/s and writes stayed in the 800-1,000 MB/s range, depending on file fragmentation and whether the host was on battery or plugged in.

- Large file transfers: Copying multi-gigabyte files (think 4K video projects or RAW photo sequences) is where the X9 shines. You won’t wait around for the drive to catch its breath; the transfer window remains short enough to keep your multitasking sane.

- Mixed workloads: When copying a mixed bag of small files (documents, assets, folders), the drive doesn’t stall or degrade dramatically. It maintains a cadence that feels closer to traditional SSDs rather than spinning drives. This matters if you’re backing up a whole project folder with a mix of assets and little metadata bits.

- 4K random I/O: If you’re a video editor who frequently scrubs 4K timelines or a photographer who churns through tons of RAW files, you’ll appreciate the 4K random read and write performance. It’s not the same as a PCIe NVMe internal drive, of course, but for an external, pocketable unit, the numbers are respectable and capable of smoothing out rough edges in workflows.

- Temperature and throttling: The X9 does get warm under sustained heavy load, but not alarmingly so. It’s a tiny device, after all, and the aluminum shell helps dissipate heat reasonably well. If you’re editing long 4K sessions while tethered to a laptop on a warm afternoon, you may notice some thermal throttle after a couple of dozen minutes of non-stop writes. The effect is not dramatic but worth noting if your work demands a long, continuous transfer.

- Noise and vibration: There’s no moving parts in there (obviously, it’s an SSD), so it’s quiet and fairly immune to typical jostling in a backpack. You’ll hear the occasional CPU fan from your laptop or a mild tick from the desk, but the drive itself is content to hum along.

## Compatibility and setup

The X9 is designed for broad compatibility. Plug it into a modern Windows PC, a Mac, or a Linux machine and you should be good to go without any extra software. Some workflows might benefit from the Crucial software suite, but the device remains fully usable as a standard USB mass storage device even without any extras.

If you want to squeeze every last ounce of performance or manage privacy and encryption, you can explore the optional Crucial software suite for things like secure erase and firmware updates. For those who care about encryption, you’ll find support through the OS or the provided software; if you’re transporting sensitive data, encryption is something to consider, though it may impact speed slightly depending on the algorithm and CPU overhead.

On Windows, I tested the X9 with both NTFS and exFAT formats to cover a general-use scenario. On macOS, exFAT worked well for cross-OS transfers, and I also tried a HFS+ partition for older Mac workflows, courtesy of the OS’s native compatibility. The key takeaway is that you won’t have to wrestle with drivers or complicated installs; plug-and-play remains the name of the game with the X9.

For content creators who rely on fast external storage on shoots, the X9 is a strong candidate. It’s light enough to carry in a camera bag without adding significant weight, and its speed helps reduce the time between shot collection and project backups. ### How to choose your portable SSD (the geeknite way)

When evaluating a portable SSD, there are a few knobs you’ll want to turn:

1) Capacity: Do you really need 2TB, or would 1TB suffice? If you’re dealing with 4K videos and RAW photo libraries, 2TB can provide a nice buffer.

2) Interface and protocol: USB 3.2 Gen 2 is a safe bet for speed. If you’re planning to carry around more modern devices, ensure your host supports USB-C or USB 3.2 Gen 2.

3) Form factor: If you travel a lot, a compact and light drive is worth choosing over a heavier model, even if the heavier model offers a little more throughput.

4) Durability: An aluminum chassis generally means more robust physical protection and better heat dissipation.

5) Software and encryption: If you require encryption or drive management software, confirm that the drive supports the features you need.

6) Price: Compare price per gigabyte across competitors to determine if you’re paying a reasonable premium for the convenience.
