### Rules and accessibility for kids

The core mechanic is drafting: each player starts with a hand of cards, picks one, and passes the rest to the left. Then all players reveal simultaneously and score based on the cards they've collected. The kids pick and pass, the adults occasionally pretend they are kids again, and soon everyone is shouting numbers like a hacker conference.

### Learning curve and teaching tips

Keep the rule explanation under five minutes. Use a live example you draw up on the table. Picture a salmon nigiri in your hand and a maki on the table; you'll score three points if you also have a maki. Then play a quick two-round practice to cement scoring. For younger players, consider using a timer or a gentle penalty for thoughtfulness—like a goofy sound effect when time runs out.

## Gameplay overview

Sushi Go! for Kids plays in roughly 15-20 minutes, which is perfect for nap interruptions and bedtime bravado. Here's how it flows:

- Each round, deal a hand of cards to every player.
- Everyone secretly selects one card and places it face down.
- All players reveal their chosen cards simultaneously.
- Each player passes the remaining cards to the next player.
- After a few rounds, the round ends and all players tally points.

The scoring system in the kids version emphasizes core combos:

- Nigiri cards that stack for points.
- Maki rolls that grant points if you collect the most.
- Sashimi requiring sets to achieve bonus points.
- Tempura pairs for a small bonus.
- Wasabi triples the value of the next Nigiri.
- Pudding goes into a special end-game scoring pile. In the kid edition, pudding might be optional depending on the house rules.

The scoring examples are simple. A three-card maki stack might yield three points per roll. A sashimi set of three is worth a fixed bonus. The rulebook is short and friendly, and if you have a family that loves pizza nights, you will happily slip this game into your weekly rotation.

### How to score

- Each nigiri card provides a base point value.
- Maki rolls: most rolls in front of you grants a middle-range score; second most gets a consolation prize.
- Sashimi: three cards equals a small but satisfying bonus; two or fewer means nothing.
- Tempura: a pair yields a small bonus; an orphaned card means nothing.
- Wasabi: the next nigiri card you draw is worth triple.

### Strategy tips for kids

- Prioritize partner combos that you can complete with minimal risk.
- When in doubt, take the card with the highest short-term payoff rather than trying to predict who will take it away next.
- Don’t worry about perfect play; the joy is in learning patterns and counting quickly.

## House rules and customization

If you want to tailor the experience for younger players, try slow drafting: each round, allow players to discuss their choice for a moment before revealing. For a cooperative vibe, you can set a target score that all players try to reach collectively before time runs out. Another option is rotating dealer rules, where the youngest player becomes the dealer for the first round and then passes that privilege along in future rounds. You can also experiment with different end-of-round bonuses such as a mini lottery for a random card that grants extra points.
