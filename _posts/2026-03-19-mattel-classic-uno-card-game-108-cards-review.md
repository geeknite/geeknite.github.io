## The Deck, Card Types, and How It All Works

### Card Breakdown

The 108 cards break down into a few easy-to-remember buckets, useful for teaching kids or for testing your memory when you’re losing a turn and pulling your hair out in mock frustration.

- 76 Number Cards: Four colors (red, blue, green, yellow); per color, numbers 0-9. The 0 appears once per color, while 1-9 appear twice per color. That’s 1 + 2 × 9 = 19 cards per color, and 4 colors × 19 = 76 total.
- 24 Action Cards: Skip, Reverse, and Draw Two. Each color has two of each action card, leading to a little dance of strategy as you watch someone time a Reverse when you’re about to win.
- 8 Wild Cards: 4 Wild and 4 Wild Draw Four. Wilds let you choose a color; Wild Draw Four forces the next player to draw four, while letting you dodge a questionable choice you’ve just made. The drama!

Put simply, the deck is a modular chaos system: the color-coded numeric cards give you the basics, the action cards spice up turns, and the Wilds let you throw a wrench wherever you please.

### How to Play (A Quick Refresher)

If you’re new to UNO, here’s the quick-and-dirty version that’ll get you playing in under five minutes:

- Each player starts with 7 cards. The rest becomes a draw pile, and the top card starts the discard pile.
- On your turn, you must play a card that matches either the color or the number of the top discard card, or you can play a Wild card to switch colors.
- If you can’t play, you draw one card from the draw pile. If that card is playable, you can put it down immediately; otherwise your turn ends.
- Action cards complicate the flow: Skip makes the next player miss a turn, Reverse flips the turn order, and Draw Two forces the next player to draw two cards. Wild cards let you declare the new color, and Wild Draw Four does the same while making the next player draw four cards (this one is often the source of social debates and “serious” rule-lawyering at family game night).
- When you have one card left, you must shout “UNO!” to alert others. If another player calls you out before you do, you draw two cards (or more, depending on how dramatic your group wants to be).

This quick refresher is intentionally short because UNO thrives on improvisation, mischief, and the gentle chaos that makes family gatherings memorable.
