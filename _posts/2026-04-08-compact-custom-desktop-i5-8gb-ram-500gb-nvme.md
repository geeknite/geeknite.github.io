## Upgradability and Longevity
Compact builds often raise the question of future-proofing. Here’s how this unit holds up and what you can realistically upgrade down the line:
- RAM: The system’s motherboard typically allows at least one more RAM module; upgrading to 16GB or more is a straightforward and cost-efficient upgrade that yields tangible multitasking benefits.
- Storage: The 500GB NVMe SSD is a fast bootstrap drive, but you can add extra storage via a second NVMe or SATA SSD/HDD depending on the motherboard and chassis. An external USB-C NVMe dock can be a simple workaround for expanding storage without opening the box.
- GPU: If you’re into gaming or GPU-accelerated workloads, a discrete GPU upgrade will provide a substantial uplift. The compact chassis may limit size; you’ll want to check PSU capacity and clearance for larger cards.
- CPU: In most compact platforms, upgrading the CPU is not straightforward due to motherboard compatibility and BIOS constraints. If you anticipate heavy future workloads, consider whether a bigger chassis with a newer CPU could be a better long-term investment.

Upgrade plan checklist:
- Confirm RAM slot availability and supported speeds
- Plan additional storage with a compatible interface (PCIe NVMe preferred)
- Verify GPU clearance and power requirements if you plan to add a discrete GPU
- Check BIOS/UEFI update notes for compatibility with future hardware

Internal shot and cable management tips: keep cables tidy to maximize airflow. A cleaner interior not only helps thermals but also makes future upgrades friendlier. There’s nothing heroic about a spaghetti monster inside your PC; you want a neat wiring path that even a helpful office plant can admire.
