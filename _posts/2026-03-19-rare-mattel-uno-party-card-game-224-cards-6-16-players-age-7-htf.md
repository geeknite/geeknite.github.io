One of the selling points is that this edition scales up to 16 players. ## Setup, Rules, and House Rules

### Getting started quickly

To start a game with 6-16 players, you’ll want a long table or a couple of adjacent tables pushed together. Shuffle the 224 cards thoroughly; you’ll want to avoid the dreaded “draw two and immediately draw two again” cascade that some parties seem to improvise into an art form. Deal a starting hand to each player (the exact hand size is up to your group, but a good starting point is around 7 cards per player). The first player starts by matching either color or number with the card in the discard pile.

### Rule clarity and common house rules

Because of the extended card set, it’s easy for rules to drift. Here are a few recommended house rules for smoother play:

- Timeboxing turns: use a timer (30-60 seconds per turn) when the table is crowded to keep momentum.
- Clear call for UNO: declare “UNO” when you have one card left; otherwise, you risk a playful penalty.
- Rule-of-thumb for edge cards: when someone lays a special event card, take a moment to explain the new dynamic to everyone, so no one gets caught off-guard.
- Optional team mode: temporarily team up in rotating pairs, which adds another social layer to the chaos.

### How to finish a round with style

The most satisfying way to end a round is to have a player empty their hand after navigating a color-change sequence that forced multiple players to swap colors mid-run. It’s dramatic, it’s silly, and it gives you a story you can tell with a straight face at future game nights. If you want to keep it simple, a strict first-to-empty-hand rule works fine too, and it minimizes arguments about who drew last and when.

## Care, Longevity, and Storage

### Keeping the deck in shape

Storing 224 cards safely invites a little logistics planning. Consider a sturdy storage solution, preferably with a lid or a soft tray that reduces wear on the edges. ### Cleaning and maintenance

If a player spills a beverage, deal with the spill quickly. UNO Party cards aren’t paper-thin but they aren’t indestructible either. A quick wipe with a damp cloth (no harsh solvents) will do in most cases. Avoid prolonged moisture to prevent warping and edge wear. In a pinch, a soft microfiber cloth works wonders for longevity, and you’ll thank yourself when you’re not paying for a brand-new deck mid-anniversary party.
