## Performance and Real-World Benchmarks

### The Numbers You Actually Care About

Crucial’s X10 lineup targets high-capacity, fast-access storage for people who don’t want to compromise. In our tests, the drive performed in the general range you’d expect from a USB-C external SSD with eight terabytes: sustained reads around 950–1,050 MB/s on a modern USB-C Gen 2 host, with writes typically a bit slower, depending on the data type and compression. Real-world transfers varied depending on file types—large media files will show different patterns than mixed file sets with many small assets. If you’re editing 6K footage or gaming with large assets in the cloud, you’ll appreciate the high sequential speeds; if you’re copying thousands of tiny files, you’ll see the typical overhead that any external SSD faces when it’s not doing a long stream of sequential data.

### Thermal Behavior

The metal shell helps with heat dissipation, but like any portable drive, it isn’t going to stay ice-cold during sustained workloads. In our longer test runs, the enclosure did warm up, but not to alarming levels. You’ll notice a tad more warmth during 2–3 hour backup sessions with encryption turned on, but nothing that caused throttling or any concern on a mid-range laptop. If you’re the kind of user who forgets to run backups until the drive itself is a living fossil, this device will likely survive your annual thunderstorm of data without turning into a melted Gorgon. For the record, enabling hardware encryption does have a slight effect on write speeds in some setups, so plan accordingly if you routinely crypt your data mid-flight.

### Compatibility Across Platforms

On Windows, macOS, and Linux, the X10 is plug-and-play in most cases. You should be able to connect and start using it without browsing for drivers or meddling with device manager menus. If you enable Time Machine backups on macOS, you’ll still want to figure out the best integration approach for your workflow; while the drive itself works well for data storage, you may want to ensure your backup regime doesn’t accidentally turn into a data whack-a-mole of incompatible backup targets. If you’re the adventurous type who wants to run Linux from a USB boot drive and simultaneously store your ISO library on the X10, you’ll be pleased to know both systems can coexist with minimal drama.
