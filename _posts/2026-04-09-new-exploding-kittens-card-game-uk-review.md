## How to play: Quick-start guide for busy evenings

If you want to jump into action without scribbling rules on napkins, here’s a concise run-through. The rules are intentionally light; the game rewards quick decisions, bluffing, and timing rather than perfect memory.

### Setup
- Shuffle the deck and deal a small hand (usually 7 cards per player, depending on your variant).
- Each player starts with a Defuse card or two (varies by edition) so nobody gets instantly ejected for poetic misfortune.
- Place the Exploding Kitten cards back into the deck with the rest of the cards, face down.
- Decide who goes first and let the chaos begin.

### Turn structure
On your turn you may play one or more cards from your hand according to the card types. The objective is simple: avoid drawing the Exploding Kitten. Common actions include:
- Defuse: Counter the bomb and keep playing with a new draw.
- Attack: End your turn and force the next player to draw more cards.
- Skip: End your turn without drawing.
- See the Future: Peek at the top few cards of the deck to plan your moves (or to fake planning).
- Shuffle: Jumble the deck to disrupt someone else’s plan.
- Nope: A clever interruption to counter someone else’s action (especially helpful when someone is about to thwart you).

The game flows quickly, and the excitement is often built on the suspense of the next draw. The end condition is dramatic: the last player standing (or last to draw and survive) wins.

## Strategy, timing, and house rules worth trying

If you want extra depth without sacrificing the party vibe, a few light house rules can elevate the session:

- Limit the number of times you can use See the Future during a game to keep it from becoming a chess match of perfect memory.
- Introduce a “slower mode” where players have to describe their thought process aloud before playing a card. It’s cringe-worthy, heartwarming, and incredibly funny.
- Add a “no-repeat” rule for attack cards so someone can’t simply chain attacks to force everyone into a loop. This keeps players honest and the table more unpredictable.

These tweaks don’t ruin the core experience; they give the night a little extra spark, which is ideal for longer family gatherings or a party with a crowd that enjoys a chaotic, antic energy.
