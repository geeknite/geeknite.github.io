## How Phase 10 Works: A Quick Refresher for the Time-Traveling Gamer
If you’re here for the first time, Phase 10 is essentially a race to complete ten predefined phases before your opponents do. Each phase is a specific combination of cards—things like two sets of three, one run of four, one run of four plus a set of three, etc.—and you advance to the next phase once you’ve completed the current one. The catch? Everyone starts at Phase 1 and must sequence through each phase in order, which means the game has a natural escalating tension that can fuel some surprisingly fierce banter around the table.

The official Phase 10 concept is simple on the surface: draw one card, discard one card, and attempt to finish your current phase. The round continues until someone completes Phase 10, and that player claims the round’s points, while other players tally penalties for cards in their hand. The ultimate winner is the person who racks up the fewest points over multiple rounds. This combination of “complete the phase” and “minimize penalties” creates a compelling push-pull dynamic: do you rush to finish a current phase, risking a larger hand of points if you miscalculate, or do you play conservatively, preparing for later phases but potentially handing your opponents the leg up they need?

In the Deluxe edition, you’ll find the same core structure, but with refined components that make the drawer-and-discard rhythm feel a touch more satisfying. The card stock is more pleasant to shuffle, the faces are crisper for quick recognition, and the overall tactile feel adds a hint of quality that your table will notice—particularly if you’ve played the basic edition a dozen times and are now chasing that “this box feels premium” sensation.

### Setup: How to Prepare for Phase 1
- Shuffle thoroughly. The 108-card deck (or more if you count the phase reference extras) should be well-mixed so you aren’t tempted to memorize top-order trivia about your own deck. 
- Deal ten cards to each player (depending on your house rules, some groups deal more to start). The rest forms a draw pile, with the top card flipped to start the discard pile.
- Each player should have access to the ten phase reference cards so you can track what you’re aiming for as the game unfolds. In some setups, players might prefer to hide the reference cards to avoid spoilers, but Phase 10 is a shared secret and part of the spice is knowing exactly what your opponents are aiming for with their own hands.

### Playing a Round: The Flow
1. Draw a card: from the draw pile or the discard pile. This is where the “face-off” begins—do you grab a card from the bottom of your plan, or do you snatch something your neighbor discarded that could push you toward your next run?
2. Discard one card: you end your turn by discarding one card that might help your opponents. This is where the social contract begins: you’re playing against people, not against a cardboard wall. The meta-game of reading faces, glancing at their efforts, and guessing their possible phase fuels a lot of the fun.
3. Try to complete your current phase: to move to Phase 2, you must complete Phase 1’s required combination(s). If you have it, you can lay down your hand in front of you and mark your progress. If not, you keep adding cards to your hand, hoping for the right combination.
4. Everyone continues to cycle until one player completes Phase 10 and declares victory for that round. The rest tally penalties, and a fresh round starts with the next phase sequence.

The strategy in Phase 10 is all about managing the balance between completing your current phase and thwarting your opponents’ plans. The Deluxe edition doesn’t radically alter these decision points; instead, it makes it easier to notice what you’re missing and what you’ve already locked in. You’ll feel the tactile satisfaction of laying down a complete phase with a perfectly aligned run or set, and the chaos of a late-add that knocks a rival into disarray is part of what makes this game a repeat offender for game nights.
