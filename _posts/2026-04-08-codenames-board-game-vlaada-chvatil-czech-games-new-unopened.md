## What is Codenames?
Codenames is a word game for two teams, red and blue; each team has a Spymaster who gives one word clues that connect multiple words on the table. The other players are Field Operatives who guess the words. Some words are red (your team's agents), some are blue (the opponents' agents), some are neutral bystanders, and one is the deadly assassin. The goal is to reveal all your agents before the other team. The clever part is that one simple clue can unlock many words, so good spymasters squeeze the maximum mapping from a single concept, or sometimes they bomb out with a clue that feels brilliant and sends everyone to alternate universes.

## How to Play (A Quick Start Guide)

A typical two-team session looks like this:

1) Set up a 5x5 grid of word cards face up. 
2) Assign a Spymaster for each team; the rest are Field Operatives. 
3) The Spymasters receive a grid key that marks which words belong to their team, the opponents, neutrals, and the assassin. 
4) The Spymaster gives a one word clue plus a number, indicating how many words on the grid relate to that clue. 
5) The Field Operatives race to guess which words the Spymaster meant, in any order, until they hit an incorrect guess or fear the assassin. 
6) The teams flip the revealed words to mark them as either red or blue. 
7) The game ends when one team claims all of its agents or the assassin is revealed.

Clarity matters here because a clue like Animal 3 could point to a dog, a cat, and a horse—but if your grid contains Horse, Whale, and Shark, your team might drown in a sea of confusion. The tension comes from the countdown of a single clue and the possibility that your own teammates will misinterpret you, causing the whole table to erupt in laughter and groans.

## Strategy and Tactics

- Be mindful of word diversity; avoid ambiguous clues that risk hitting multiple enemy words. 
- Teams should pick one or two safe words and then push for riskier words. 
- Spymaster s discipline matters: avoid random guesswork; you should craft clues with care. 
- The value of a good clue: not too many words, not too few; the number in the clue is the hint that keeps your team on track without gifting the opponent a shortcut to victory. 
- Always account for language drift. If your group consists of bilingual players or folks from different hobbies, the same clue might hit wildly different targets. Be ready for the occasional phrase that becomes a running joke instead of a meaningful hint.

## The Experience: Flow and Pacing

A critical factor in Codenames is pacing. The way the table interacts with the clue can determine whether your game lasts 15 minutes or 90 minutes. The Spymaster s job is to craft a clue that maps to as many of their words as possible while avoiding the assassin. The Field Operatives need to interpret the clue and decide how many of the mapped words they want to guess in one go. The sum of those decisions creates the game’s tempo: explosive bursts of activity, followed by quiet, calculating debates about what a single clue could possibly connect.

Part of the charm is the social contract: you must trust your teammates to interpret hints with good humor, to celebrate near misses as learning moments, and to rib each other when a clue backfires spectacularly. It is less a game about words and more a celebration of shared vocabulary, inside jokes, and the occasional tragic misfire when the word you guessed was, say, a dolphin, and the clue was Sea 3.
