## What is Saboteur Card Game
The premise is delightfully simple and deliciously chaotic. You play as dwarves who want to reach a treasure hidden somewhere in a mine. Most players are miners who aim to pave a path to the treasure, but a few players are saboteurs who secretly try to derail the path and tank the whole operation. The twist is that you never know who is on which team until the end of the round, which is the exact meta pressure you secretly crave on a Friday night.

The Mayfair edition keeps the core idea intact while presenting a clean, accessible version of the game. The deck holds path cards to lay out the tunnel, action cards to influence the flow, and treasure cards to crown the winner when a path reaches the gold. Roles are distributed secretly, adding a social layer that makes the table feel less like a math problem and more like a dramatic improv scene with occasional explosions of paper and laughter. The result is a party game that can be enjoyed by families and groups who adore a good bluff without needing a college degree in cryptography.

## Setup and Components

The remaining roles go into a central pile, waiting for the moment the secrecy becomes public suspect drama. Each player receives a hand of cards equal to the number dictated by your player count, and the game is ready to roll like a four-legged minecart in a cartoon sandbox.

## Gameplay Mechanics: Bluff, Build, and Bluff Again
Saboteur thrives on a simple yet flexible core loop. Each round begins with secret roles distributed to players. Most players are miners who want to pave a path to the treasure. A few players are saboteurs who secretly attempt to thwart that goal. The rest of the players will be bluffing about their loyalties in front of you while quietly harboring their own ambitions.

On your turn you play one card from your hand. Path cards extend the tunnel or branch into forks, giving you opportunities to steer the mine toward the treasure or toward a dead end that frustrates everyone. Action cards can do a variety of things: they can reveal someone else’s role, force a card draw, or disrupt the path in a way that feels both clever and cruel in a friendly way. The exact effect of the action cards varies slightly by edition, but the spirit remains the same: you have islands of control in a sea of uncertainty, and every card you play can change who looks like a genius and who looks like a fool.

The miners must connect to a treasure card by laying a continuous sequence of path cards. A dead end can sabotage progress and force everyone to recalibrate. Saboteurs win when they can derail the path long enough to prevent the treasure from being reached, or if they can force the round to end without a successful path. The tension emerges from what players know and what they suspect. A miner who seems to be quietly laying an alley of progress may trigger a saboteur to pivot, while a saboteur who acts confident may end up exposing their role through a poorly-timed misplay.

Rounds move at a brisk clip. The timing of when to reveal a role, when to lay down a powerful path card, or when to spring an action card is the real loot here. If you are a player who enjoys the thrill of misdirection and the satisfaction of a carefully crafted bluff, Saboteur is a playground for your strategic vanity. If you prefer a slower, more methodical experience, you may find your patience tested by the rapid-fire pace. Either way, the energy that emerges around the table is hard to beat.

### The turn structure in practice
1) Draw a card if you can and choose to play a card from your hand. 2) Place a path card to extend the mine or connect to an old node. 3) Play an action card to influence the board or your fellow dwarves. 4) If a treasure card becomes reachable, the round ends in a dramatic flourish as miners celebrate and saboteurs swallow their pride. 5) Score the round, reveal roles, and start anew.

The modular path construction leads to a variety of experiences each round. You might get a straight shot to the treasure with minimal drama, or you could be invited to a labyrinth of forks and dead ends that tests your ability to read the room. The Mayfair edition emphasizes speed and accessibility, which means less time spent on complex rule explanations and more time spent crafting cunning plans and cracking jokes at the table.

## Host Tips: Running a Smooth Night
- Start with a quick rule rundown. Give new players a few practice rounds with minimal stakes so they can feel the rhythm without being overwhelmed.
- Keep the energy positive. A good Saboteur session is about playful deception, not unkind accusations. If someone looks genuinely put out, remind everyone that this is a game and the dwarves will forgive you after the treasure is revealed.
- Manage pace with a timer. If rounds start to drag, gently nudge players to make faster decisions. The game remains entertaining when it moves quickly and the table stays lively.
- Use thematic props. A few tiny props like a toy pickaxe, a cardboard gold nugget, or a scroll-like treasure card can make the night feel more immersive and fun.
- Rotate roles. If you play many rounds, rotate the role cards to ensure everyone gets a chance to be the saboteur and the miner. Rotation encourages empathy for the other side and spices up the game night with fresh dynamics.
