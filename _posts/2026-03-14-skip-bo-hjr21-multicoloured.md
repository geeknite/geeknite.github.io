## How It Plays: The Core Loop and What Makes It Tick

Skip-Bo is a racing puzzle with a warehouse logistics vibe. Your goal is to empty your personal stock with a hand of stacked numbers, while the stockpile in the center of the table acts as the ever-present pressure that keeps the adrenaline up. HJR21 Multicoloured adds color-coded guidance without removing the core chaos that makes the game exciting.

### Setup and Turn Structure

- Each player starts with a personal stock pile of face-down cards and a hand of five to seven cards.
- On your turn, you draw up to five cards, then play as many as possible from your hand onto the building piles in ascending order. A pile starts at 1 and goes to 12. Skip-Bo wilds can stand in for any number, which keeps programmers and grandmothers alike shouting for joy.
- The twist here is the multicolored deck; the color helps you track potential sequences at a glance, which speeds up play and reduces the time you spend arguing about whether a red two should be a red two or a black two in your head.

The cadence of a round feels like a dance with a mischievous robot: you think you have the steps down, then a wild card comes along and flips the tempo, and you end up laughing at your own cleverness while your table mates groan in shared relief that the table is still intact.

### Strategy That Does Not Require a PhD in Telemetry

- Early rounds curb your tendencies toward heavy optimization; you learn to value simple sequences and quick dumps more than flawless, highly efficient plays.
- As you become more comfortable, you can craft longer, higher-value sequences, and the multicoloured cards help you to identify candidate moves quickly.
- The key is tempo: push to build piles when you can, but hold back when your opponents threaten a dramatic comeback. The color cue helps you feel the rhythm of the game even when your brain declares a temporary mutiny.
