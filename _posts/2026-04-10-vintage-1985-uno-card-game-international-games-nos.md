### Authenticity and condition checks when you’re buying NOS
 NOS means sealed, not necessarily immortal. If you’re shopping for a 1985 International Games UNO, you want to look at:
- The box seal: intact and free from crusty edges. A compromised seal is a signal to inspect more closely or pass on.
- The box art and typography: make sure the logo and color palette match the era; some modern forgeries imitate but miss the subtle grain on the box texture.
- The inner contents: the deck should have consistent color saturation and no obvious wear or fading on the card faces.
- The instruction sheet: a clean, legible sheet with no ink smearing or misprints. In some cases, early prints feature small typographic quirks that are charming rather than alarming, but it’s worth a look.

## Preservation, care, and long-term value
NOS items deserve proper care to preserve their condition. Here are practical tips to ensure your 1985 UNO remains as pristine as possible:
- Store in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight to minimize color fade and cardboard brittleness.
- Keep the box closed when not in use to reduce dust accumulation and moisture contact.
- Use a card sleeve or a small display case if you’re displaying the set open; this protects the faces while letting you admire the 80s design.
- Avoid excessive bending or shuffling, especially with the outermost cards. Gentle riffles work best when you’re ready to play again after years of quiet storage.

Preservation isn’t just about keeping a game pristine; it’s about preserving a moment of cultural history in cardboard form. Think of it as archiving a tiny piece of how people socialized in the 1980s, one colorful card at a time.

## Authenticity checks: quick verification tips
- Box and card stock: look for consistent print quality and a uniform color depth. Fades or mismatched hues may indicate aging or reproduction issues.
- Logos and branding: compare the International Games logo with known authentic examples from the period. Subtle differences in typeface or placement can be telling.
- Card dimensions: UNO cards are standardized, but older runs may show slight size differences due to manufacturing stations. If the cards don’t stack evenly, be cautious.
- Instruction sheet: check for language quality and formatting. A well-preserved sheet with intact margins is a good sign; a sticker or photocopy vibe around the edges is a red flag.
