## Setup and Components
Setting up Monopoly Deal is quick enough to be done while someone reads the setup rules aloud with a dramatic whisper. Here’s the gist:

- Each player starts with five cards in hand. Yes, five. It feels right because you’re balancing hand management with the pressure to build property sets—this is a card game, not a polite tea party.
- The rest of the deck becomes a draw pile. On your turn you’ll draw two cards, then you can do anything you want with up to three cards from your hand.
- You’ll need a place for two key zones: your property area (the place where you assemble sets) and your bank (where you stash money cards). The bank is where you “pay” opponents when rent or deals demand it. It’s not fancy, but it does the job with style.

What comes with two decks is mostly more variety and fewer stale combos. ### Card Types and What They Do
To understand the joy (and occasional pain) of Monopoly Deal, you need to know the card taxonomy:

- Money cards: These are the currency you use to pay rents and to bank up for expensive property sets.
- Property cards: These are the color-coded blocks that form your sets. A complete set is a win condition, so think of them as tiny victories that accumulate toward a bigger victory statement.
- Action cards: The chaos-creators. You’ve got Deal Breaker (steal a full property set from an opponent), Sly Deal (steal a single property from an opponent), Debt Collector (force a payment from another player), Pass Go (draw two extra cards), It’s My Birthday (everyone pays you), and more. Action cards are the spice that makes this game feel energized rather than a slow bank simulation.

Two decks increase the density of action cards in play, which means the tempo can swing wildly. It’s not unusual for someone to pull a huge swing and suddenly be two cards away from a victory—and the table knows it could be you next turn.

### Build, Bank, and Battle: The Turn Structure
On your turn, you do the following:
- Draw two cards from the draw pile. If you’re low on deck, you shuffle the discard pile back in. Card count never sleeps in Monopoly Deal.
- Play up to three cards from your hand. This is where you decide whether you’re going to build your property empire, shore up your finances, or threaten a rival with a surprise steal.
- You can place money into your bank, add properties to your property sets, or play action cards to disrupt your opponents or sabotage their plans.

The bank acts like your wallet in a casino—the bigger it looks, the more you can push people around with rent charges. Property sets must be built in color groups; a completed set means you’ve checked a major box on the path to victory.

### How to Win
The goal is simple in theory and chaotic in practice: collect three complete property sets. The set colors don’t have to be distinct—though in practice you’ll end up juggling color diversity to maximize your options. A single strong combination can get you there, especially if you’ve managed to thrive on a couple of high-impact action cards that let you steal or block at just the right moment.

Two sealed decks help ensure that there are enough copies of wild cards and color properties to keep the game lively. It also reduces the chance of someone getting the exact same hand twice in a row, which is the kind of symmetry that board game fans love to hate.

## Strategy and Tactics
So, you want to win without turning the game into a full-blown arbitration session. Here are some practical tips gleaned from many a night of Monopoly Deal mischief:

- Balance your hand: Don’t flood your bank with money too early if you’re not going to build property soon. The tension comes from being able to pay rents while still having enough property to complete sets.
- Use action cards thoughtfully: It’s tempting to hoard your Deal Breaker cards for a grand finale, but a timely Just Say No or a well-timed Sly Deal can derail an opponent who is about to win. Value is not only in the card you play, but in the timing of its play.
- Plan for wild cards: Wild cards are your insurance policies and wildcards. They can stand in for various colors and save you when you’re short on a color for a complete set.
- Don’t overspend early: If you go heavy on property early, you may be short on cash to pay rent later. A balanced approach—property growth with a solid bank—tends to win more games than flashy steals that backfire.
- Read the room: Monopoly Deal is as much about social dynamics as it is about card management. A player who keeps a calm, “I’ve got this” attitude may discourage others from pushing you around, which can be a powerful game strategy in itself.

With two decks, you’ll frequently deal with a broader mix of action cards and duplicates, which changes the math of the late game. It’s easy to be caught in a cycle of “I can steal your pink property” and “no you can’t” if you’re not careful about your hand management and your use of Just Say No cards.
