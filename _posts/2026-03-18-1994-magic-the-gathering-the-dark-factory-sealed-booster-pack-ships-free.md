## Factory Sealed Packs: What does it mean to be factory sealed?
Factory sealed booster packs are a snapshot of a moment when card manufacturing still felt like a wild frontier. In The Dark’s case, you’re looking at a wrapper from a period when card stock was thicker, packs were less likely to have error-laden misprints, and the protection film was a durable shield against the ravages of time. A factory sealed pack promises that the contents have not been opened or disturbed since they left the factory floor, which is a magical claim in a world full of loose packs and counterfeits.

Key points about factory sealed packs from this era:
- The inner contents are arranged in a way that minimizes the chance of obvious damage to rarer cards, though not a guarantee—these packs existed before stringent modern humidity control and advanced anti-counterfeit tech.
- Grading sealed packs is a field of its own. Some collectors value the pack wrapper and overall preservation; others care most about the perceived integrity of the contents and the potential for high-value rares to have remained untouched.
- The wrapper itself can be a collectible. The color patterns, the printing runs, and the even the minor cosmetic quirks can affect value. If you’re a wrapper-connoisseur, you’ll appreciate the little details—like whether the pack has a consistent seal strip run and whether the blister-like inner wrap shows uniformity across a full display.

From a practical standpoint, sealed packs from this era can be and are graded, but authenticators have to consider the wrapper’s age, any signs of resealing, and the likelihood that a genuine factory seal remains intact after decades. That’s not a knock; that’s just a reality check: the longer a pack has aged, the more vigilant you must be about authentication if you’re buying with an eye toward investment or resale value.

If you’re seeking a ship-free delivery, you’ll be happy to know that many listings hammer out promotions to entice buyers with free shipping, which is nice for a hobbyist on a budget. The shipping policy is often the deciding factor when you’re buying something that’s both fragile and a little magical.

## Authenticity, authentication, and how to verify value
Authenticity for vintage sealed packs is a specialized skill. Here are practical steps you can take before pulling the trigger on a purchase labeled ships free:
- Examine the listing photos. Look for consistent typography, correct color palette, and misprints that look suspiciously modern or off-kilter. The packaging should feel aged in a way that’s consistent with 1990s manufacturing rather than a glossy modern print.
- Check the wrapper’s seam lines and the inner seal. Real vintage factory seals tend to have a certain uniformity in the seal strip and edge finish. If something looks irregular or too-perfect, ask for additional photos or proof of authenticity.
- Review seller reputation. In a market where fakes exist, a credible seller with multiple verified sales and positive feedback is worth more than a slick listing with stock photos.
- Request provenance, where possible. A seller who can provide a story of where the pack came from and how it was stored adds credibility and helps you gauge risk.
- Consider professional appraisal or grading. If you’re contemplating a significant investment, professional grading and authentication can be a wise step, though it adds cost. The final decision should balance the researcher’s wallet with the would-be thrill of owning a sealed relic.
