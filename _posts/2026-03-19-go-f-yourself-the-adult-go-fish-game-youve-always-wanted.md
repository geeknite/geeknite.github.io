## How to set up and start playing
Setup is a breeze, which is exactly what you want after a long day of work and a long list of adult responsibilities. Shuffle the deck, deal a certain number of cards to each player (the guide suggests 5-7 depending on how spicy you want the night to be), and place the remaining deck in the center. The player to your left becomes the first “question master,” the human embodiment of the curious coworker who asks way too many follow-up questions at the office party. The goal is still the same as classic Go Fish: collect enough complete sets to win. The twist: you will encounter prompts that blur the line between legitimate questions and comedic opportunities to roast your friends.

An important note: if you’re playing with brand-new players or folks who don’t appreciate adult humor, you might want to set a few boundaries before the first draw. The game is designed to be provocative but not mean. A good rule of thumb is to define a few topic guardrails at the start—things that are absolutely off-limits, and others that you promise to handle with a wink rather than a shove. The value of a night out hinges on comfort and consent as much as it does on clever prompts and snappy one-liners.

Once the game starts, the action moves with the same tempo as regular Go Fish, but the prompts give you a human-level rollercoaster of reactions. You’ll ask for a card of a specific rank; if someone has it, they hand it over and you keep playing. If not, you Go Fish and reveal a prompt card when you draw. The prompt cards vary in degree of boldness, with some being mild and funny and others aggressively cheeky. It’s this range that keeps people engaged without turning the table into a debate club about political correctness.
