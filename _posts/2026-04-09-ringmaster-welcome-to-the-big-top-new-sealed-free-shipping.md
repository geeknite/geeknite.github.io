## Gameplay and Mechanics: The Ringmaster’s Controls
### Core Loop: Plan, Play, Polish
Ringmaster is built around a core loop that feels satisfying without being overbearing. Each round consists of a series of “acts,” where you’ll plan a sequence of moves using cards, deploy your meeples, and respond to events that pop up from the central deck. The objective is to maximize points by completing stunts, managing crowd mood, and cleverly timing your cards to weather the inevitable curveballs the deck throws at you.
- Act Phase: You draft or play cards to set up your rounds. The drafting aspect introduces cutthroat but friendly competition: you’re choosing to improve your own show while potentially blocking your neighbor’s best plays.
- Execution Phase: You take your actions, move meeples, and trigger events. The difficulty isn’t in the number of decisions but in balancing risk versus reward—do you risk a big stunt now or save resources for later acts when the crowd’s mood gauge is lower?
- Resolution Phase: Scoring and crowd mood adjustments determine who’s winning and who’s left holding a clown horn at the end of the night.

### Player Interaction and Table Presence
The game encourages a lively, interactive table. You’ll be watching your neighbors’ moves with a mix of “I hope they don’t steal my thunder” and “yes, please steal their thunder.” That economic pressure—knowing your actions affect others—keeps the table buzzing. It’s not a hot take to say a well-timed stunt can derail someone else’s plan in a way that feels fair and exciting rather than mean-spirited. The social dynamic is one of the strongest aspects: Ringmaster thrives when players lean into showmanship, banter, and the occasional shared groan at a misread stunt.

### Solo and Cooperative Possibilities
Ringmaster shines most in a group, but there’s a solid solo mode that scratches the itch for solo players who like puzzle-solving with a circus sheen.
