## How to Play: A Quick Tour Through the Rules
Playing two games in one box means you will be juggling two different rule sets, but fear not: they play politely with one another, like two roommates who agree to split chores but have widely divergent snacks. Here is the essential gist of how the two games operate within the same session.

### Skip-Bo quick rules
- Objective: Get rid of your stockpile by forming building piles in numerical order using the cards in your hand and on the table. First player to deplete their stockpile wins.
- Setup: Each player is dealt a certain number of cards to form their hand; the rest become a draw pile. A few building piles are laid out in the center area as targets we will aim for.
- Turn structure: On your turn, you draw to a hand size, then you attempt to play from your hand, discarding to your build piles or playing to the center. You can use wilds to fill gaps as needed. The aim is to chain as many plays as possible in a single turn.
- Strategy tips: Don’t hoard your wild cards for the end. You may need them to complete midgame builds, and using them at the right moment can swing a round in your favor. Pay attention to what others might be collecting and what cards are likely to be discarded next.

### Phase 10 quick rules
- Objective: Be the first to complete all ten phases, each with a specific card arrangement (e.g., two sets of three, one run of seven, etc.). Phase cards in hand tell you which phase you’re aiming for on a given round.
- Setup: Deal out your initial hand and lay out the phase cards that show the order for completion. You will be drawing and discarding to achieve each phase’s requirements.
- Turn structure: On your turn, you draw from the draw pile if you don’t have a playable card, then attempt to complete the current phase. If you finish a phase, you move onto the next one; if you can’t complete a phase, you keep trying in the next rounds.
- Strategy tips: The Phase 10 world rewards planning and memory. If a color or set appears in your hand, you want to protect it and think a couple of moves ahead. But don’t forget to be flexible: other players may be scoring the same combination and you may need to adjust on the fly.

## Strategy Corner: Tips for Both Games without Losing the Fun
The best part of a two-game set is learning parallel strategies that translate across both experiences. Here are some practical ideas to keep the momentum (and the laughs) going while players of all ages have a blast.

- In Skip-Bo, aim to keep your hand’s flexibility high. Don’t lock yourself into a single plan early; instead, keep several possible build sequences in your head and be ready to pivot when a Wild card appears.
- In Phase 10, prioritize early completion of phases that you can reasonably assemble with the cards you’re likely to draw from the deck. Don’t chase a rare combination if you don’t have a good shot at finishing it before others.
- Watch your opponents’ visible progress. If you notice someone is close to finishing a phase or stockpile, you may want to adjust your strategy to increase the odds they draw into a needed card during their next turn.
- Use table talk and light humor to keep the vibe friendly. A little light banter about queuing strategy or the ideal use of a Wild card can turn a tense moment into a shared laugh.
- When in doubt, lean on the “house rule” approach. If your family tends to slow down on certain rounds, you can add a timer for a few rounds to speed things up. If your family loves longer sessions, you can deliberate on pressing for longer sequences or stacking more cards for bigger sets. The key is to keep it fun and fair for everyone involved.

## Variants and House Rules: Poking a Fun Hole in the Box
Every family has its quirks, and this box is good at supporting a few light modifications that preserve core gameplay. Here are a handful of simple variants you can try to tailor the experience to your crew:
