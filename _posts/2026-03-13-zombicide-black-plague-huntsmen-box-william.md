## Overview
The Huntsmen Box isn't just another add-on; it’s a mid‑season glow-up for Zombicide: Black Plague. It brings in a new narrative thread, a bunch of fresh miniatures, and a few rules twists that are both delightful and potentially terrifying depending on how your group handles “balanced difficulty.” The box leans into the “huntsman” archetype: a hunter of the undead who treats the living as optional, and the corpses as a fantastic source of fashion inspiration. In many ways, this box is a love letter to the concept of ambushes, long-range takedowns, and the uneasy feeling that your line of sight might be compromised by a catapult’s trebuchet-level roll of the die.

Before you dive in, know that the Huntsmen Box assumes you’ve got a Black Plague core game or at least a base familiarity with the system. It’s not a standalone experience; it’s a dense, robust expansion that expects you to bring your A-game and a table large enough to host a small army and a couple of catapult-shaped anxieties. If you’re new to Zombicide, I’d recommend a slower onboarding, maybe a couple of small expansions first, then circle back to the Huntsmen Box with plenty of snacks and a backup plan for “mulligan dice.”

## What’s in the Huntsmen Box
The box is a mix of plastic, optional rulebooks, and the emotional baggage of a medieval apocalypse. Here’s the breakdown of the standout components:
- William the Huntsman (the titular survivor): a charismatic, long-range-capable icon with the ability to lay down suppression fire, cover lanes, and make your ranged choices feel like a tactical dance rather than a desperate sprint to the nearest hedge. He’s tall, he’s dangerous, and yes, he wears a cloak that looks suspiciously like a cape cut from a dragon’s wing. 
- Additional survivors: A few more characters who haven’t learned the art of being discreet around the shambling tide. They bring new abilities and a new flavor to the table, encouraging players to mix up tactics and band together in surprisingly goofy ways. 
- New zombie types and encounter cards: The Huntsmen Box expands the “predator” vibe by introducing zombies with better armor, who will test your ability to actually use line of sight, cover, and reload timing without succumbing to melodrama.
- Rules variations and campaign hooks: It isn’t a light “add-on.” You’ll find a handful of scenario tweaks, event cards designed to push players into riskier maneuvers, and a few narrative prompts that make your table feel like a roguelike dungeon crawl rather than a quiet afternoon of tile placement.

Assembling the components is a small in-game rite of passage. The minis are well-sculpted with personality—some of them look ready to pull off a heroic showdown, others look suspiciously like they’ve just realized they left the stove on in the city-of-the-dead. The quality is typical CMON: sturdy, with clean molds and lots of fine details that reward careful painting and a vigilant eye for mold lines (you know who you are, fellow hobbyists).

## The William Survivor: A Tale of Peril and Panache
William is the centerpiece—the character the box wants you to celebrate. He’s not just a stat block; he’s a narrative engine. In play, he emphasizes range, patience, and the joy of removing a target from a distance with surgical precision. His ability set encourages players to consider the map from a safer vantage point before committing to a risky dash toward the center of the fray.

The wonder of William is how he shifts the group’s tempo. If you’re a player who enjoys the pickup-and-go chaos of close-quarters fights, William may feel like a breath of fresh air, a reminder that you can influence the tide while still contributing to the group’s feelings of epic scale. If you’re a lover of heavy melee and heroic last stands, William adds a counterbalance—your team now has a reason to plan and protect a single line of sight across the board. It’s not just “shooting the zombies,” it’s “orchestrating a mini-symphony of tactical movement.”

Some tables find William’s long-range options slightly overpowering in the early game; others feel the opposite—an opportunity to introduce more risk management and multi-turn planning. Either way, the box nudges you toward a more thoughtful approach, which is a rare thing in a game that can easily degenerate into a dice-rolled brawl. If you enjoy building a narrative arc—William as the archer-king of the siege, guiding your crew through chokepoints and forested outposts—the Huntsmen Box nails that vibe with relish.

If you’re a “display-the-collection-on-a-shelf” kind of gamer, the aesthetic payoff is high—these are the sorts of minis you want to show off at a con or a friends-and-coffee session.

## Rules, Mechanics, and How Huntsmen Changes the Game
If you already know Zombicide Black Plague, you’ll recognize the familiar dance of action points, zombie spawns, and objective-driven triumphs. The Huntsmen Box spices up the rhythm in a few key ways:
- Long-range engagement becomes more viable: William’s presence incentivizes players to think beyond melee reach. You’ll be more careful about line of sight, a concept that often gets glossed over during a “rolling for everything” night.
- New zombie variants introduce tactical challenges: You’ll see walkers that reward pinning and suppressive fire, as well as more cunning undead that require you to reposition rather than simply shoot your way through. It’s a reminder that in a plague, there’s more than just numbers to consider—the terrain, the lines of vision, and the morale of your survivors all matter.
- Card economy and risk management: The new encounter cards push players toward decisions that test both luck and prudence. Do you risk a dash toward a clustered objective or hold back to protect weaker teammates? The answer often changes as the board evolves, which is exactly the kind of dynamic you want from an expansion.

The rules themselves remain approachable; there’s a learning curve, of course, but the box doesn’t rewrite the core system so much as it adds a new oversight: you’ll want to be more deliberate about where you place your figures, when you expose your archers to risk, and how you coordinate your team’s turns in a way that doesn’t feel like chaos with better lighting.

## Campaign Integration and Narrative Hooks
One of the strongest reasons to add Huntsmen to your Black Plague collection is the way it threads a narrative through your sessions. The Huntsmen Box isn’t content with solving a single scenario and calling it a day; it invites you to craft a chorus of mini-missions. William becomes the anchor for recurring themes: a siege commander who must hold a chokepoint, a hunter who tracks a “monster” in a given sector, or a protector who guides weaker survivors through a corridor of doom.

If you enjoy narrative campaigns, you’ll find that the box has enough content to sustain a handful of sessions. The new cards, combined with the existing campaign framework, allow you to weave a story where William’s long-range prowess becomes a recurring motif—each game a new chapter in his legend, each zombie spawn a reminder of how precarious a life spent in the open can be when your lines are too thin.

For players who only tangentially care about narrative and simply want more crunchy tactical decisions, you’ll still get plenty: more options on how to orchestrate the board, more opportunities to leverage the terrain, and more moments of satisfying “gotcha” plays when a well-placed shot clears a critical threat.

## Thematic Artwork and Tone
The aesthetics of the Huntsmen Box lean into a moody, wood-smoked vibe. The armor is dark, the banners bear faded heraldry, and the overall palette nails that “gloomy medieval village under siege” feeling. The art direction supports the game’s tone: you aren’t playing a bright, cheerful siege; you’re coordinating a controlled retreat toward a fortress whose gate is slightly too crooked to be healthy. There’s a subtle humor to this, too. The character designs let you imagine your group as a ragtag band of survivors who’re doing their best with a map that’s more holes than ink. It’s not slapstick; it’s gallows humor—the kind of humor you need when you’ve rolled two failed stealth checks and the zombies are now using your own rations as a snack.

## Setup Time, Storage, and Table Presence
As with most mid-tier expansions, the Huntsmen Box adds a meaningful amount to your table presence. ## Tips for Gameplay and Table Tactics
- Start with William’s long-range capabilities as your anchor, but don’t forget to pair him with a shoulder-to-shoulder melee survivor who can hold the line when reinforcements arrive.
- Use terrain to your advantage. The Huntsmen Box rewards careful positioning; use narrow corridors to force zombie clusters into chokepoints where your archers can maximize effect.
- Keep track of line of sight early. The expansion sometimes makes line of sight feel a little “award-y” if you’re not careful about your initial placements.
- Build a narrative arc. Give William a personal objective in your first campaign and let it thread into later sessions. A good recurring objective keeps the table engaged and gives the players a reason to push through tough rounds.
- Don’t forget to rotate survivors to avoid fatigue. The extra complexity is delicious, but you still want to keep the game fun and fast-paced.

If you’re looking for a compact checklist before your next session with Huntsmen, here’s a quick cheat sheet:
- Prepare the William figure and confirm his range abilities.
- Shuffle the new encounter cards with the base deck.
- Place terrain that bottlenecks zombies into narrow lanes.
- Assign a narrative objective for your group’s ongoing story.
- Decide on a quick victory condition that doesn’t punish the party too harshly for bad luck.
