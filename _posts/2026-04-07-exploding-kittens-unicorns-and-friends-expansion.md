H2: What’s in the Unicorns and Friends Expansion?

The box promises rainbows, glitter, and enough mischief to derail a quiet evening. Here’s the gist of what you’ll encounter when you crack the lid:

- A stack of Unicorn Cards that can nuke the mood or, surprisingly, save your bacon. These unicorns aren’t just pretty faces; some grant you a second chance, some swap control of turns, and a few let you pawn off exploding-kittens risk to a rival with a wag of a tail.
- A fresh cadre of Friends Cards. Because every cat needs a squad. These cards team up with you or taunt your opponents with slightly more benevolent chaos than before. Think allies that bend the rulebook in your favor—just long enough to critique your own life choices.
- Additional Action Cards with glittery twists. The game divine timing: when you think you have your enemy cornered, a Unicorn swoops in to turn the board into a glitter storm.
- Component upgrades: new art, a slightly bigger box, and cards that feel so fancy you want to show them to your cat as if they’re a tiny royal audience.
- A revised player-count range. The base game tops out around five players; the Unicorns expansion adds a bit more chaos power for six and beyond in the right social setting.

H2: Thematic Vibe and Visuals

Geeks love theming, and the Unicorns and Friends expansion leans into it with a pastel-punk aesthetic. The cards wobble between adorable and “oh no, that just happened,” with unicorn motifs, rainbows that remind you of caffeinated fountain drinks, and some cheeky captions that could only exist in a room full of people pretending to be responsible adults. If you’re a fan of the exact brand of whimsy that makes you say, “This is ridiculous and I love it,” you’ll appreciate the look. If you’re a grimdark purist who wants your kittens in noir noir, this expansion might feel like someone glued magnets to a cloud, but in a good way. H2: How It Changes the Gameplay Flow

H3: Tempo and Tension

The unicorns introduce a new tempo layer. Some unicorns skim across the rounds, giving you accelerants (extra draws, quick-pass turns) while others act as wildcards that can deflect explosions to someone else’s pile. The Friends cards inject alliance politics into a party game. If you’ve played a lot of party games, you’ll recognize the shift: the table becomes a miniature boardroom where you pretend to be benevolent while privately calculating your exit strategy.

H3: Risk versus Reward

With new cards, players will weigh the risk of shuffling into a turn that could be a “unicorn buff” for someone else. The unicorns themselves come with varying risk profiles: some make you draw more, others let you skip a lethal moment, and a few reward you for mischief. The balance, in practice, hinges on how aggressively your group leans into backstabbing cosplay. If you’re all in for chaos, you’ll enjoy the sweet, sweet rush of feeling the table’s energy spike mid-game.

H3: Player Interaction

Expect more direct interaction. Unicorns reward targeted play, and Friends cards create arguments about who deserves the moral high ground. It might get heated, but it’s the fun kind of heated: the “we’re all children trying to pretend we aren’t” kind of heated. If your gaming nights lean toward cooperative vibes, you can dial down the hostility with house rules; if you live for vicious banter, you’ll get a lot of material here.

H2: Card Breakdown (What to Expect)

H3: New Unicorn Cards

- Sparkling Salvation: play this when you’re about to explode and swap fate with a unicorn who lets you re-roll the explosion. It’s basically a glittery get-out-of-jail card but with more sass.
- Rainbow Ripple: a card that can bounce an effect to another player, changing who’s left standing with you in a single glittery moment.
- Glitter Slash: if you time it right, this card lets you peek at the top card of the deck and decide whether to draw or skip. It’s not cheating—just well-informed magic.

H3: New Friends Cards

- Buddy System: you form a temporary alliance with a neighbor; if they survive a turn you benefit, if not, you’re both in the same chaotic boat.
- Favor Exchange: swap a card with someone else. The negotiation arc alone can power a whole night.
- Group Hooray: a one-turn group buff that makes everyone draw? No problem—except for the person who’s about to explode next.

H3: New Action Cards

- Unicorn’s Wink: a stealth action that lets you look at the top card of any deck and optionally swap it with a better one you own.
- Sparkle Shield: protects you from a single explosive blast, but the catch is it requires you to wink at the table and declare your intent to sparkle responsibly.
- Infinite Jest: a card that teases future rounds; it’s basically a mood ring for the game—if you pull it, your next turn is a carnival of chaos.

H2: Setup and Playthrough Tips

H3: Setup Basics

- Shuffle the base deck and expand with Unicorns and Friends boosters.
- Remove any homebrew “too long to play” cards if your group needs a shorter session.
- Determine the number of players (2-6 is typical with Unicorns). If you’re expecting a party of eight, you might want a house rule that limits the chaos to a manageable scale.

H3: First Round Walkthrough

In the first round, you’ll want to feel out who’s fearless and who’s allergic to cat-like chaos. Expect some playful plays that bait others into bad decisions. People will comment on your strategy with the intensity of a sports referee who forgot their whistle. Don’t overthink it; the joy is in the silly misfires that follow.

H2: Strategy and House Rules

H3: Beginner Strategies

- Don’t be afraid to take a risk early if you’re behind. The unicorns give you a chance to swing momentum, even if you look ridiculous.
- Use Friends cards to create temporary safe zones and plan your explosion timing like a chaotic parade.
- Read the table energy. If someone looks like they’re accumulating power, a timely use of a unicorn buff can derail their plan in a very satisfying way.

H3: Advanced Tactics

- Synchronize unicorn effects with Friend card swings to maximize table-wide chaos while you stay relatively safe.
- If you’re playing with a full table, split your attention between what you want and what the strongest player might exploit next round. The unicorns reward cunning and punish laziness.
- Consider a “glitter pause” house rule where players must pause after a big move to note how fabulous the moment was. This keeps the mood light and the game moving.

H2: Artwork, Quality, and Production Value

The Unicorns and Friends expansion is a visual treat. The cards feel thicker, the corners less likely to chip after a week of kitchen-table gaming, and the color palette is intentionally cheerful. The unicorns are more than accessories: they’re tiny narrative prompts that nudge players to justify their ridiculous plays. It’s silly, it’s charming, and yes, it’s a bit over-the-top—in the best way possible for this franchise.

H2: How It Compares to the Base Game and Other Expansions

Compared to the base Exploding Kittens, Unicorns and Friends leans more into the social negotiation layer. If you loved the quick, sneaky jabs of the core game, you’ll appreciate the Friends cards that introduce alliances and grudging camaraderie. It’s not about “more dice” or “bigger explosions”; it’s about turning the table into a theatre of chaos—where every misplay becomes a story you’ll retell at your next game night.
