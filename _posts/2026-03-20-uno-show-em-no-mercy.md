## How to Play: Setup, Turn Order, and Mercy in Practice

### Setup
1. Shuffle the deck. Each player draws 7 cards. The remaining deck becomes the draw pile. Flip the top card to start the discard pile. If the starting card is a special card, follow the action immediately (or reshuffle, depending on your table’s tolerance for chaos).
2. Decide if you’re using Mercy tokens and Mercy cards. If you’re brand-new to the concept, start with one Mercy token per player and one Mercy card in the deck to keep things simple.
3. Establish the ruleset you’ll be using for the night. Do you want normal UNO rules with one Mercy twist? Or do you want the “whole package” which includes Mercy tokens as an additional resource? It’s your game, your rules—just communicate them clearly before the first turn.

### Turn Structure
- On your turn, you must play a card that matches either the color or the number of the top discard card, just like regular UNO. If you have no playable cards, you draw from the draw pile.
- If you play a Wild or Wild Draw Four card, you can declare a color, then the Mercy mechanic may modify the next player’s action depending on the variant you’re playing.
- Mercy cards and Mercy tokens introduce new decisions: you can use a Mercy token to reassign a penalty (for example, switch a draw-two to a skip), or you can deploy a Mercy card to alter the flow under specific conditions.
- When you’re down to one card, you must shout “UNO!” If someone forgets, the penalty is the same as in standard Uno (they must draw two cards if caught). The Mercy shaker adds a playful pressure to remember. 

### The Mercy Moment
The real magic happens when the Mercy element triggers. Here are two common interpretations:
- Mercy as a strategic shield: you can use a Mercy token to avoid drawing extra cards when you have to draw. It’s not a full pass, but it gives you a chance to survive a brutal hand.
- Mercy as a turn flipper: the Mercy card can reverse a turn in a tricky situation, letting you dodge a pile of doom cards or pivot to a winning hand when the current lead looks unstoppable.

The effect is that the game remains tense without becoming mean-spirited. It adds a light-tension mechanic that suits family gaming sessions but still feels adult enough to be interesting for mixed groups.

## Variants, House Rules, and How to Tailor the Chaos

### Variant A: Mercy Frenzy (High-Chaos Mode)
- Use Mercy tokens more aggressively. Each time a player draws two or more cards, you may offer a Mercy trade (swap tokens with another player, or force a redraw of the turn order).
- Increase the mercy board’s significance by awarding a small bonus to the person who ends a round with the most Mercy tokens spent.
- This mode is not recommended for young kids who are strongly allergic to chaos. It’s for players who enjoy a competitive banquet of chaos and giggles.

### Variant B: Co-op Mercy Madness (Co-op Night)
- For a softer experience, team up in pairs. The Mercy mechanics apply in a cooperative fashion: teammates share a Mercy token pool and coordinate when to deploy a Mercy card.
- The aim becomes a cooperative victory rather than individual wins. It’s surprisingly satisfying to clutch a win as a duo while the audience roars.

- This version is meant for the 20-minute window between airport security and boarding announcements. You know who you are.

### House Rule Considerations
