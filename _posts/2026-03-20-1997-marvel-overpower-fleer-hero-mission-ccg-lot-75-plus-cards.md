## What is OverPower and the Hero Mission subset
OverPower is a collectible card game from the mid to late 90s that tried to harness the Marvel Universe into a fast and flashy skirmish card game. If you remember the era when every game needed a foil border and a hero portrait so glossy it could survive a superhero movie premiere, OverPower fit right in. The Hero Mission subset was one of the flavors Fleer experimented with to give players a cinematic feel, where your team of heroes faced a mission with victory conditions inside the card text. In practice, you built teams, attacked villains, and used Mission cards to push the clock toward a win. The result was a chaotic, over the top experience that felt like a cross between a trading card game and a Saturday morning cartoon arc.

## Card types you’ll likely find
- Heroes: The star power. These cards show the hero with a detailed art piece, a litany of powers, and a flavor text that makes you wanna collect the next one. The hero cards define your team composition and your strategy for the mission.
- Villains: The shoulder shaking opponents who print a smug grin on your face when you topple them with creative synergy.
- Locations: Where the action happens. Locations can add bonuses, modify Mission requirements, or unlock special effects during combat.
- Events: One time or limited use cards that shake up combat or mission conditions.
- Mission Cards: The centerpiece that gives you the objective to conquer in the Hero Mission mode. Some are straightforward, others are fiendishly clever in a way that makes you think the game designers had a sense of humor about pulp fiction.

## Artwork and card design
The art style on OverPower cards in this era is unmistakably late 90s: bold colors, dynamic action scenes, and a glossy finish that still stings the fingers when you flip through a few dozen at a time. The hero portraits are dramatic, sometimes a little over the top, and always evocative of a cartoonish version of comic-book drama. If you’re a fan of Marvel property art from this period, you’ll recognize a lot of the details that show up across the set. The card frame, power icons, and text box layout reflect a period when game designers were experimenting with how much information could be packed into a single card without turning the readers into a LCD reader on a sci-fi submarine.

## How to evaluate a bulk lot: quality, quantity, and compatibility
When you buy a bulk lot like this, your main priorities shift away from individual card perfection and toward overall pool viability. Here’s what to look for:
- Card condition: In a 1997 product, edge wear and surface gloss can tell you whether a card has survived a kid’s playful but careful flings across a kitchen table or if it spent a lazy decade in a printer tray. If the lot has a lot of very good condition cards, that’s a win.
- Duplicates: A lot of duplicates is both a blessing and a curse. It’s a blessing because it gives you extra copies for deck-building options or for trading with friends. It’s a curse if you hoped to complete a specific set and find you have three copies of the same villain card that you don’t care about.
- Completeness: If the lot seems to have the majority of the Hero Mission cards, you may be able to piece together a playable deck or a fun collection without having to chase every single rare. In some cases a bulk lot can be a perfect starting point for a game night with friends who appreciate retro CCGs.

## Playing it today: rules in a nutshell
For players new to OverPower, the general framework is pretty simple: you assemble a team of heroes with powers and abilities, choose a mission, and participate in a series of turns that involve attacking, defending, and sometimes turning the tide with a well-timed mission card. You measure power by a numeric value on your hero cards and card effects. Battles flow in a battle zone with standard combat steps. The exact rules can be a bit fiddly, but a quick skim of a mission card often explains the key steps. Since this is a Hero Mission subset, you’ll also encounter mission cards with specific objectives that push you to try particular combos or reach certain thresholds before the clock runs out.

## Care and maintenance of 1990s cards
Storage matters, especially when you’re dealing with shuffling as many as 75+ cards. Use soft sleeves for individual hero or unique cards to reduce surface wear, and consider top-loaders for cards you’re actively using in a deck. A tidy storage system helps you avoid the dreaded “Where did I put the extra copy of the power booster card?” crisis. When you’re dealing with older cards, humidity can cause warping; climate-controlled storage is worth it if you’re planning to keep an appreciable portion of the collection intact.

The 75+ card lot gives you a baseline from which to build a deck, trade, or simply display the art and wonder that comes with a time capsule of comic-book nostalgia.
