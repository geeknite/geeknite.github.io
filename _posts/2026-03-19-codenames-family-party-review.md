## How to Play: A Practical Walkthrough

### The Core Loop
The game centers around two roles: the Spymaster (who gives clues) and the Field Operatives (who interpret clues and pick word cards). The goal is to responsibly connect several target words with a single clue while avoiding words that point to the opponent’s team or the dreaded “assassin” word. In Family Party, the framing is friendlier, the clues are generally shorter, and the language tends to be more universal so non-native speakers can participate without a glossary on the side.

### Setup, Turn Structure, and Timing
- The Spymaster assembles a round-colored field of word cards on the table, typically in a grid layout. The teammates line up, ready to be the eyes and ears of the operation.
- The Spymaster looks at a key card that tells which words belong to their team and which are neutral or dangerous. In Family Party, the key card is sometimes simplified to reduce confusion and speed up rounds.
- On a turn, the Spymaster gives a one-word clue followed by a number, like “Fruit 3” or “Hero 2.” The number indicates how many word cards on the table relate to that clue. Teammates then discuss and select their guesses by pointing at the word cards.
- The timer is optional, but the healthy-ish pressure of a ticking clock adds energy. If the players agree, you can use a longer or shorter timer depending on group size and patience level.
- Guesses continue until either the team feels satisfied or they hit a word that ends their turn (wrong guess or out of guesses). The next round then rotates to the other team, followed by a new Spymaster line.

The Family Party edition wires these steps together with a friendlier vibe: simpler language, shorter rounds, and clearer endgame conditions. This reduces the chance that a complicated clue causes a meltdown and a family feud that lingers for weeks. It’s not perfect—no game is—but it’s pretty close to “manageably chaotic” for households with kids and adults in equal measure.

### Example Round (Illustrative, not an official rule map)
Spymaster: One-word clue, “Space 2.”
Operatives: Point to “Moon” and “Rocket.”
Spymaster: Correct! Both words are yours. The team smiles, the clock ticks, and someone quietly writes down a victory dance in their head for later.

### What Makes a Good Clue in Family Party
- Relevance with multiple targets: A well-chosen word can link several target words without straying into the assassin’s lane.
- Ambiguity, but not confusion: You want a clue that a veteran will recognize and a younger player might ask about, leading to light teaching moments.
- Benign tie-ins: Clues tied to kid-friendly experiences (cartoons, school subjects, sports) tend to land better with mixed-age groups.

### Spymaster’s Perspective: Strategy Notes
As the Spymaster in a family setting, your job is to steer the ship with minimal chaos. A few practical tips:
- Start with safe, family-friendly word clusters: Think common nouns rather than abstract concepts.
- Favor single-word clues that reference tangible connections, not long phrases that invite misinterpretation.
- Gauge your team’s comfort level: If younger players are in, keep the clue budget tight (1-2 targets) and avoid tricky metaphor territory.
- Be mindful of language balance: If players aren’t fluent in English, choose clues that rely on widely known concepts, not obscure cultural references.

### If You’re a Field Operative
- Listen for non-obvious connections: Your Spymaster might be testing a clever bridge between two seemingly unrelated words.
- Use process-of-elimination: If you’re unsure about a word, pick one you’re more confident about to reduce risk.
- Communicate clearly and supportively: A family game night is about fun, not victory at all costs.

### Rule Tweaks for Family Accessibility
Some groups adopt house rules like “no dead ends” (if you’re stuck, pass) or “three strikes and you’re done” to keep the energy positive. If you want to speed things up, set a firm time limit per clue. If you want more safety, remove the assassin word from play entirely for younger players. The beauty of Family Party is that you can bend the rules to fit your crew without feeling like you’re breaking the spaceship you built in a weekend woodshop class.
