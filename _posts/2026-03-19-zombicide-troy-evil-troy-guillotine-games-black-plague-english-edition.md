# Zombicide: Troy & Evil Troy – Guillotine Games brings myth and mayhem to Black Plague (English Edition)

If you’ve ever wondered what happens when a ravenous horde meets a pair of mythic knights who might as well be named after a pair of stubborn twins, you’re in the right place. Zombicide: Troy & Evil Troy is Guillotine Games’ tongue-in-cheek, fan-service-laden entry into the Black Plague line that drops two uniquely themed survivors into the existing horde-busting, dice-rolling chaos that fans of the series either adore or fear with the determination of a tenacious zombie shuffling toward a level up. In short: it’s glorious, a bit ridiculous, and somehow still feels like the most dialed-in cooperative dungeon crawl around a round table where catapults and crossbows become an art form.

![Zombicide Troy & Evil Troy boxart](assets/images/zombicide-troy-evil-troy-box.jpg)

If you’re new here: Zombicide is the cooperative zombie survival series by Guillotine Games. Black Plague is their medieval-flavored spin, where you aren’t fighting modern hordes so much as undead knights, necromancers, and the occasional dragon-sized menace. Troy and Evil Troy are two characters that aren’t just cosmetic tips of the spear; their rules interact with the game’s core systems in ways that can tilt the balance toward hilarity or near-terrifying mayhem depending on how your group rolls the dice on a given night.

## What is in the box and what makes this duo tick

The box for Troy & Evil Troy is packed with the following core components:

- 2 heroic miniatures: Troy and Evil Troy, each with a distinct silhouette and an arsenal that makes them stand out on the board.
- Character cards, quick reference sheets, and stat trackers tailored to their unique abilities.
- A set of tiles and a few modular add-ons that let you slot them into standard Black Plague missions without a hitch.
- Objective cards and new encounter goals designed to highlight these two knights’ contrasting personalities.
- The usual assortment of tokens for synergy abilities, damage tracking, and the occasional “Curses of Troy” counter that reduces or magnifies certain actions—depending on how much the group likes drama.

Let’s pause for a moment to talk about the figures themselves. Troy is the stalwart, gold-armored ideal of a knightly hero—a bulwark against the undead who seems to have a plan for every turn. Evil Troy, as you might guess, brings a darker toolkit: a few devious tricks, a willingness to bend the rules, and a power set that rewards risk-taking with the potential for big payoff—if your dice gods are kind and your memory of his quirks is sharp.

The quality of the miniatures is typical Guillotine Games: sturdy plastic with clear, readable poses. They’re not the kind of miniatures you’ll treat as investment pieces for painting competitions, but they’re solid enough to hold up to tabletop abuse and look great on camera when you’re showing off a particularly glorious hatchery moment—the moment when Troy charges into a cluster of abominations and somehow survives long enough to call in a heroic aid from the heavens (or at least from a well-timed card draw).

## Thematic flavor and how it plays with the Black Plague engine

Zombicide: Black Plague is a synthesis of fantasy-medieval ambiance and accessible co-op rules. It’s not a solo hobbyist’s dream, but it is a delightful pump-action game that makes you feel like a band of knights stood up to a tide of undead. Troy & Evil Troy slot into this world with a theme that’s more than a cosmetic flourish: their powers interact with the terrain, line of sight, and the morale-based objectives in ways that create memorable, story-driven turns.

- Troy brings a shield wall vibe: he can soak up damage and provide protection for weaker comrades, which changes how you plan your safe zones. Expect to juggle aggression with defense when the board gets crowded. 
- Evil Troy adds tempo shifts: his abilities may trigger card-driven boosts, oscillating the team’s advantage between “near cornering victory” and “we might just get overwhelmed if we miscalculate this move.” The dynamic between these two is not just about which knight looks cooler; it’s about how your team choreographs a two-character symphony in the heat of a zombified siege.

## Mechanics, pacing, and how Troy & Evil Troy swing the game

If you know Black Plague, you know the core cadence: you spawn zombies, you’d better plan your clear, you roll your dice, you draw event cards, and you hope your survivors live long enough to complete the mission objectives. Troy and Evil Troy inject some fresh verbs into that sentence:

- Stance synergy: Troy’s abilities encourage you to form a defensive perimeter around his friends. This can slow the zombie horde down and create opportunities for a surgical strike later in the round.
- Risk-reward loops: Evil Troy trades long-term safety for the possibility of chain reactions that wipe out multiple enemies or yield more experience points (XP) for your team. It’s the tabletop equivalent of taking a risky pastry—delicious if it succeeds, devastating if it crumbles in your hands.
- Tile-based interplay: The terrain matters more with these two—certain floor-tiles empower or hinder their abilities. This means part of the tactical planning is reading the map like a mini-chessboard while also juggling card effects and zombie spawn patterns.

From a mechanical perspective, their presence adds an asymmetry that’s rare in co-op games. You’re not just pathing a generic squad; you’re nudging two hero archetypes through the same grid, each with different goals that don’t always line up perfectly. That tension is exactly where the game shines: you’ll make a call that benefits one knight at the expense of the other, and you’ll like the narrative that follows as you recount it around the table.

## Scenarios, campaigns, and how many times you’ll want to play this duo

This expansion slots into the existing Black Plague mission pool, and it tends to encourage one of two play styles:

- Short, punchy runs where Troy and Evil Troy are the centerpiece of a single, well-timed mission. These are great for weeknights when you want a satisfying, fast burn that still feels epic.
- Longer, campaign-like strands where you link multiple missions to tell a story arc about the knights’ uneasy alliance and the toll it takes on the party.

The scenarios are well-designed: they leverage the two Knights’ distinct toolsets to create sequencing choices that aren’t just “move, kill, repeat” but rather “how do we orchestrate the two heroes’ abilities to maximize the payoff?” It’s not always about who shoots the biggest monster; it’s about who triggers the right synergy at the right moment to unlock a chain of events that would make a dungeon boss jealous.

## Setup, play time, and the learning curve

Like most Zombicide-based products, setup is straightforward but not trivial. You’ll need a clean space to lay out the tiles, sort your zombie spawns, and position Troy and Evil Troy. The good news is that the rulebook is a faithful companion that lines up with your existing Black Plague knowledge. If you’re new to the system, you’ll enjoy the gentle push toward learning by playing with a few implied tutorials on the first two missions. If you’re a veteran, you’ll relish the micro-choices that become apparent only after half a dozen sessions.

Play time per mission tends to settle around 45-75 minutes for 2-4 players, depending on how aggressively you push for the end-game objective and how many times you test the mercy of the dice. With Troy & Evil Troy, expect a slightly longer learning curve as you adjust to their timing windows and the way their abilities escalate as you level up.

For those curious about solo or two-handed modes: Zombicide scales considerately for two players. If you’re attempting a solo run, you’ll want to control the two knights with a careful plan and a willingness to exploit board geography. The two-knight dynamic plays differently for solos than it does for a 4-player coalition, but it’s a worthwhile experiment for players who want to test their tactical mettle against a relentless undead tide.

## Strategy tips and early beacons for your first session

- Prioritize zone control early: Use Troy’s defensive synergy to anchor a safe zone around your strongest survivor. Evil Troy can then pivot to exploit a spawn point that would otherwise snowball into disaster.
- Don’t fear the risk: Evil Troy’s mechanics reward calculated risk more than reckless bravado. If your dice are hot, lean into his power to create a cascade of successful saves that earns XP for everyone.
- Manage your line of sight and tile effects: Some mission tiles boost the knights’ capabilities, while others hamper them. Read the board like a mini-map and pivot your plan as new zombie spawn orders arrive.
- Balance your XP allocation: As the party levels up, decide who gets what card to maximize the synergy between Troy and Evil Troy. It’s tempting to hoard the power cards, but sharing the wealth often unlocks the most satisfying outcomes.
- Prepare a rapid refresh plan: If things go south early, you’ll want a backup plan for retreat or regroup. The game rewards that kind of composure, and you’ll be glad you had a moment to breathe before the next swarm hits.
