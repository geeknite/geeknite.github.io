## Overview

Friends and Foes is one of those expansions that keeps the core vibe intact while injecting a bunch of personality into the game. The base Green Horde game is famously gristly with a dash of olive-green bureaucracy — the mood of the wasteland with the texture of boiled spinach. This expansion adds new survivors (the Friends) and a new category of threats (the Foes) that love to complicate your plans in delightfully antagonistic ways.

What you’re getting is not just more cardboard and dice. It’s a playground where your survivors can form fragile alliances, trade favors, and potentially punch each other in the back when you’ve got a critical move lined up. It’s goofy, tense, and surprisingly thematic. If you’re into a slightly more social, slightly more political take on a zombie apocalypse, this expansion is basically the game’s party buff that also introduces a new boss mechanic you’ll wish you hadn’t met at 2 a.m.

## What’s in the Box

The expansion box contains:

- A cadre of new survivors with distinct personalities and abilities
- New zombie types that function especially well with the Friends and Foes dynamic
- Event cards and objective cards that tilt the stakes
- A handful of new tokens and a slim rulebook section clarifying the interactions
- A small handful of cardboard chits and tokens to keep tracks tidy

The production quality remains solid for a Zombicide set: heavy cardboard you won’t mind dropping on the table, colorful art that reads well from across the room, and enough space on the map to prevent constant token wrestling with your neighbors.

If you’re chasing a compact, all-in-one-sideboard experience, remember you’ll still want the core game and at least one of the other minimal expansions to get the most out of Friends and Foes. The synergy is where the fun truly lives.

## New Survivors

The Friends in this expansion aren’t generic adventurers; they’re a cast of misfit heroes with a sense of humor about the end of civilization. Expect a mix of grizzled veterans, streetwise scavengers, and a few wildcard personalities who will probably backstab you if you’re not looking.

- Survivor A: The Negotiator — trades with the group and can avert a bad encounter with a successful dialogue check. If you like diplomacy, this is your go-to. If you don’t, it’s your easiest way to trick your friends into taking a bullet for you.
- Survivor B: The Technician — kit brawler who can repair, rewire, and repurpose gear, giving you a predictable path to the most ridiculous save-you-from-death-miracle plays.
- Survivor C: The Trickster — excels at manipulating events, causing misreads and misplays in your enemies and allies alike. Great for comedic chaos; terrible for your short-term plans.
- Survivor D: The Beast Tamer — a fearless survivalist who can coax better outcomes from certain encounters, especially when the tide of battle is turning against you.

These survivors come with your standard little punch-card of stats. You’ll want to read their abilities aloud with a dramatic flourish, ideally while holding a foam weapon and a mug of questionable coffee. The flavor adds a lot to the experience and gives everyone at the table something to latch onto beyond “smash zombie, take card,” which is, let’s be honest, the backbone of Zombicide in most groups.

For practical purposes, you’ll want to track loyalty and handoffs within your crew. Some abilities hinge on you having other survivors in your immediate vicinity. The social mechanic here is the star: it’s not just the goblins you should fear; it’s your friends’ ability to siphon your resources at the exact wrong moment.

## New Foes

The Foes section is where the expansion earns its keep. These aren’t just stronger zombies; they’re cunning, taunting, and ever so slightly disappointed in your strategic incompetence. The new zombie types often incentivize you to coordinate with your teammates in ways that feel like a heist movie rather than a straight-up dungeon crawl. Expect faster spawning, more tactical spawns, and a few “oh no you didn’t” moments that leave you laughing and cursing in equal measure.

The new enemies interact with the map in ways that the base game doesn’t always push you toward. Some will require you to pivot your plan mid-turn, others will force you to think about your bonds with the other players as much as you think about your weapon loadout. It’s a good reminder that in a zombie apocalypse, your friends can be both your strongest shield and your most dangerous liability.

## How the Expansion Changes Gameplay

If you know the rhythm of Zombicide, you’ll recognize a few patterns here, but Friends and Foes throws in enough curveballs to keep things fresh:

- Social mechanics: You’ll have opportunities to negotiate, trade, and feign loyalty to manipulate outcomes. This introduces a light layer of diplomacy and suspicion that can swing a game’s pace dramatically.
- Cooperative tension: Your decisions aren’t just about your own survival anymore. You’ll be balancing your immediate survival with the risk/reward of backing your ally’s risky plan.
- Expanded objective variety: New objectives keep the table moving and prevent the same old “clear this room, grab the loot, rinse and repeat” loop.
- Scaling and balance: The expansion scales elegantly with the base game, maintaining the heavyweight tactical feel while adding new tools for the players who enjoy longer, more story-driven runs.

If you’re a solo player or only half-jokingly play with new rules before your group sits down, you’ll want to take a few test rounds to calibrate how the new mechanics land in your table’s dynamic. Some players will adore the social gambits; others will simply want to punch more zombies and go about their day. Both camps are valid here, and that’s a win for a modern Zombicide expansion.

## Scenarios and Campaigns

Friends and Foes isn’t just flavored cards and new models; it offers scenario hooks that feel meaningful rather than tacked on. The scenarios often have you balancing loyalty factions, which creates a narrative tension that’s rare to capture in a kitchen-table game. A typical scenario might require you to escort a non-player survivor across the map while negotiating with a foe faction, all while you’re being pressured by a clock that you can feel in your bones.

The campaign mode (if your group is into serialized play) gains a richer, more personal feel with “character arcs” that reward repeated playthroughs. You’ll see survivors’ stories evolve, and you’ll feel the bite of bad luck when your favorite ally betrays you at a crucial moment. It’s very much the kind of design that makes you want to run another cycle just to see how a rival’s plan falls apart.

## Tactics and Strategy Tips

- Know your survivors, then know your table: The trick with Friends and Foes is to maximize synergy between survivors. Some combos explode into powerful turns; others are less reliable but could win if you stack the odds.
- Control the chaos: Use negotiation and loyalty cleverly. Don’t underestimate the power of forcing a helpful ally to do your bidding at the wrong moment for your opponents.
- Plan for the clock: The new scenarios often run on timers. Prioritize actions that keep you on schedule without sacrificing your longer-term survival.
- Manage expectations: The social element can tilt the table in surprising directions. If you’re not into friendly combat, set clear house rules at the start to maintain a fun vibe for everyone.

A few tactical anecdotes worth sharing: the moment you realize the Trickster can flip a scenario’s dynamic by turning an ally into a temporary foe, or when the Technician’s repairs become a critical lifeline in a late-game sprint. These are the moments that make the expansion feel like a living, breathing narrative rather than a set of card-chains and dice rolls.

## Art, Components, and Theme

The artwork stays consistent with the Green Horde line — bold, readable icons, and a color palette that makes the board feel alive without making you squint at every die roll. The new characters are distinctive enough to tell apart in a crowded table, which you’ll appreciate after an hour of zombie carnage and a shaky coffee mug full of questionable energy drink. The card stock is typical mid-grade for a board game, not the premium heft you’d expect in a deluxe edition, but perfectly serviceable for the scope of this expansion.

From a thematic standpoint, Friends and Foes nails the vibe: you’re not just surviving a zombie apocalypse; you’re navigating a makeshift society that’s trying to stay cohesive while everything falls apart. The “friends” concept introduces rough-and-tumble social dynamics without tipping into grimdark grimness, which is exactly the tone Zombicide should strike in a campaign-friendly format.

## Storage, Setup, and Longevity

- Setup time: Slightly longer than base due to the extra components and the need to layout new faction zones. If you’re hosting, factor in an extra 10–15 minutes for setup.
- Storage: The box isn’t sacrificially big, but you’ll want an extra organizer to separate survivors, zombies, and tokens. It’s not a disaster, just a small ergonomic consideration.
- Longevity: If your gaming group cycles through expansions, you’ll find Friends and Foes continues to offer fresh experiences on repeat playthroughs. It’s not a one-and-done; the scenarios and dynamic interactions give you enough variability to keep the table entertained for a while.

## Compatibility and Replay Value

One of the best things about Zombicide expansions is how they scale with the base game and other add-ons. Friends and Foes slots nicely into a variety of setups, letting you pair this expansion with the core game, or with other expansions that share a similar design philosophy. The interplay between new survivors and old survivors can lead to satisfying, table-shaking moments that make you want to replay the same scenario to explore alternate alliances and betrayals.

If you’re a collector or a completionist, the set is a strong addition. If you’re a casual player who only has time for one more thing to pack into your weekly session, you’ll still get your money’s worth by the sheer number of “gosh, wow” moments this brings to the table.

## Final Verdict

Friends and Foes lands as a confident, character-driven expansion that respects the core feel of Zombicide: Green Horde while injecting a playful, social layer that invites players to engage with each other in new ways. It’s the kind of expansion that makes you want to host a session that runs long, with a couple of in-jokes and a dramatic, overacted benediction between rounds. If you enjoy the Green Horde's slower burn, the added depth of survivor personalities, and the chaos of moral choices under zombie siege, you’ll find yourself delighted rather than frustrated with what this box adds to your table.

If you’re torn between buying this or waiting for a sale, I’ll give you a small nudge: the extra replay value is real, and the social dynamics alone are worth it. The expansion won’t reinvent Zombicide, but it will shift your game night from “nobody talks, we roll dice, we kill zombies” to “we talk, we joke, we gamble, we win some, we lose some.” And that, my friend, is exactly the kind of undead party you want in your life.
