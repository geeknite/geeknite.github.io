## Assembly and Setup

Setting up a 14 ft trampoline is not the same as assembling a bookshelf from that famous Swedish store. You will need time, space, and probably a friend with a strong back and a willingness to talk about their feelings while you tilt a giant metal frame into place.

### What you typically get in the box

- The steel frame segments along with springs
- Jumping mat
- Safety enclosure net with zipper
- Poles for the enclosure with foam padding
- A ladder
- A packet of tools and an instruction guide that may or may not look like a treasure map

### Step by step

1) Clear a wide, flat area. You want your trampoline to be on a surface that is level and with at least 6 feet of clearance from any fences, trees, or suspicious garden gnomes. 
2) Assemble the frame on the ground to the point where you can stand it up. The trick here is to align the frame segments and ensure the springs match the numbers on the mat. 
3) Attach the springs to the mat gradually, rotating around to avoid twist. Do not attempt to gain the perfect symmetry while your back complains. 
4) Attach the enclosure net and poles, then slide on the foam padding for those sharp edges. 
5) Attach the ladder, then test the entry by letting a fearless child take the first hop. 
6) Check for stabilizers or ground anchors and secure them to the ground. 

This is the part where you should start to see sweat on your brow and a sense of accomplishment that rivals finishing a 1000 piece jigsaw with a few missing edges. Expect a couple of hours for a solo setup; factoring in a helpful mate will bring it down by about 60 minutes. Always follow the leak-free instructions. We do not advise improvisation with duct tape unless you are practicing for a post-apocalyptic backyard.

### Safety checks during assembly

- Ensure the net is fully zipped and secured before use
- Inspect all springs for corrosion or gaps
- Check for wobble in the base and adjust ground anchors as necessary
- Verify the ladder is securely attached and that the feet have good grip

## Durability, weather, and maintenance

Backyard trampolines are not bulletproof; they live outdoors in the weather, which means rust, UV fade, and occasional wind gusts. A good 14 ft trampoline will feature a powder-coated steel frame that resists rust and a UV treated mat. The spring cover and enclosure padding should be weather resistant to prevent cracking and cracking jokes about your lawncare service. We recommend removing the enclosure during extreme weather and storing pieces safely if you live in a windy area.

### Routine maintenance

- Check springs for wear and replace as needed. A broken spring not only ruins your bounce but can cause injuries if it snaps unexpectedly.
- Inspect the ground anchors and ensure they are fully engaged, especially after a windy night or a storm.
- Keep the mat clean by brushing off leaves and debris; wash with mild soap and water occasionally to remove sweat and sunscreen build-up.
- Inspect the net for tears and repair or replace sections promptly to avoid a teen landing into the shrubs.

### Weather considerations

If you live in a climate with heavy snow, you may want to disassemble and store the unit for winter to avoid structural damage. If you must leave it up, consider a heavy-duty cover and snow removal strategy. Always secure the unit to the ground; wind can turn your backyard fun into a flying fortress if anchors are not properly installed. ## Safety guidelines and best practices

Safety should be the first thought, not the last thought when kids are involved. The enclosure net is a critical safety feature but it does not grant immunity from common sense. A few best practices:

- Always supervise jumping. The boundary rules apply; if you can see a potential collision, stop the session and reset.
- Ensure no jewelry or loose clothing that could get caught in the springs.
- Teach a rule of one jumper at a time in the air to avoid mid-air collisions.
- Use the ladder to access the trampoline and avoid stepping from the ground onto the mat in ways that could twist ankles.
- Regularly inspect the seams and the zip; zip closure should be secure and not forced.
- Keep the landing zone clear with a soft surface like a trampoline mat and a safety mat around the perimeter.

## Best practices for setup, usage, and storage

- Place on level ground. If your yard has a slope, consider a foundation or adjustable feet to even things out.
