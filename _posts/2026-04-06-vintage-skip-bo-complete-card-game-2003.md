## How to Play: A Nutshell Guide
Skip-Bo is deceptively simple to learn but demands a bit of strategic foresight if you want to maximize your winning chances. Here is a quick primer for players new to the 2003 edition:

- Objective: Be the first player to empty your stock piles by playing cards to the building piles in ascending order.
- Setup: Each player receives a set of stock piles. You deal from the central deck to fill those piles. The exact number of piles depends on the number of players, but the general vibe is chaos and order at the same time.
- On your turn:
  - Draw up to your hand limit.
  - Play as many valid cards as possible from your hand or from your stock piles onto the building piles.
  - Use stock leftovers to prepare for the next round of creativity, because almost anything can be a stepping stone if you think fast.
- Wild cards: Use them to replace any missing number in a building pile. They are your lifeboats when you are drowning in 2s and 7s.
- Endgame: When a player empties their stock piles, the round ends and you tally points. The more you can chain together, the more bragging rights you earn (and the more likely you are to be teased mercilessly at the next gathering).

The rules are intentionally modular. House rules abound in every family game night, and the 2003 edition embraces that spirit. Don’t be surprised if your cousin tries to stack a dozen 1s in a single move to force a dramatic finish—this is the kind of swagger Skip-Bo encourages, and honestly, it is glorious when it works and horrifying when it collapses in spectacular fashion.

## Variants and House Rules
House rules are the spice of family game night. The 2003 edition welcome them with open arms. A few popular twists include:
- The Sprint Round: Players race to empty a single stock pile within a strict time limit.
- The Hidden Stock: Players keep a stock pile face down until certain conditions are met, adding a layer of suspense.
- The Mutation Rule: Allow a wild card to act as any number not currently in use on the building piles, amplifying dramatic comebacks.

These variants shine when you want to keep things fresh without buying new expansions. The beauty of Skip-Bo is that you can tailor it to your group—spicy enough for the kids, gentle enough for the grandparents, and chaotic enough to be right at home in a dorm room.
