## How to play in 60-second bite size
If you already know UNO, you have a head start. The rules bedrock in Flip Uno remains the same: match color or number and use action cards to disrupt your opponents. The twist is the flip side, which is activated by one of the action cards or a special flip card. When a flip occurs, the entire deck flips to the dark side, with new color sets and new action cards that shift the pace from casual to caffeinated. Here’s a quick breakdown:

- Setup: Each player starts with seven cards; the rest form a draw pile; flip the top card to reveal the starting color on the light side. If the top card is an action card, you follow its instruction immediately, as in regular UNO. On Flip UNO, the flip action can be triggered by specific cards or by the general rule that you must flip on the first time the light side deck is exhausted.
- Turn order: Move clockwise. On your turn, you either play a card that matches the color or the number, or you draw a card if you cannot play. The cards on the light side behave as you expect in UNO; when a Flip card or a matching rule calls for it, you flip the entire deck to the dark side.
- Flip effect: The dark side changes the playing field. The colors might be the same, but the action cards now have new effects. For example, a normal Skip card on the light side might become a Draw 4 on the dark side, or something equally chaotic. The flip adds strategic misdirection: it is often worth it to flip early if you want to disrupt a dangerous lead or sow chaos in your rival's hand.
- Winning the round: As soon as a player has a single card left, they call UNO as usual, and play continues until someone yells UNO and then slaps down the last card. The twist: if the last card is on the dark side, the scoring mirrors the dark side rules, and that can sting a bit more.

A common concern with flip-based games is whether the dark side becomes overpowering.
