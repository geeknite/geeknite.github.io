## Common issues the guide addresses and how to apply them at the table
Even though the exact wordings vary slightly from edition to edition, there are several recurring stumbling blocks that show up in Sushi Go! rounds. Here are the big ones, with practical how-tos for your next game night.

### 1) Nigiri and Wasabi: chaining those spicy rice bites correctly
Nigiri have a simple goal: collect scoring cards that multiply with Wasabi triplicates. The replacement guide clarifies how Wasabi interacts with multiple Nigiri of different types when you have more than one Nigiri in front of you. The essence is that Wasabi triples the score of the first Nigiri placed under it during scoring, not any Nigiri card drawn afterward. In practice, this means you should plan your Wasabi best-case scenario ahead of time, then apply the guide’s clarifications when the table’s been chanting “Wasabi, Wasabi, Wasabi!” like a chorus of sushi-chanting monks.

A quick play tip: when you’re unsure, agree to a “Wasabi freeze” for that round. Slow down for a turn to confirm that Wasabi’s effect will apply to the intended Nigiri, then resume play. This prevents miscounts and keeps the table friendly rather than furious.

### 2) Maki scoring: who gets the glory when the stack is big? 
Maki rolls are the classic cases where the guide helps you settle who gets points when you have a tie for most Maki. The 2014 booklet replacement guide spells out the tie-breaking logic clearly: count total Maki symbols; if there’s a tie for first place, those tied players each receive the full Maki points, and the rest receive nothing for Maki from that round. The visual you can print out is a tiny scoreboard showing Maki distribution for that round to help players track the math without pulling out the calculator app mid-game.

Tip: keep a separate “round tally ring” near the center of the table; it’s a fun prop that reduces disputes and adds a little theater to the scoring ritual.

### 3) Pudding: endgame vibes and the last-minute twist
Pudding points at the end of the game are a notorious source of either epic triumphs or dramatic comebacks. The replacement guide clarifies how pudding is counted and who’s eligible for those last-second points when every other card has already had its moment in the sun. In many versions, pudding points don’t influence mid-game decisions, but the guide emphasizes keeping track of pudding options during play so you don’t accidentally forget one player’s tribute to the pudding gods.

Pro-tip: assign a pudding point counter at the start of the game. This avoids the “wait, who had the pudding last?” moment when the round ends and you all scramble for sticky notes and a pen. A simple token or a labeled card can do the trick, and it looks adorable on the table—the edible version, not the real pudding, obviously.

### 4) End-of-round timing and last-turn behavior
Sometimes players end a round a microsecond before the seating chart expects it; the guide steps in to provide a canonical approach to end-of-round timing. It clarifies when a round ends (after the current player completes their pick and places their cards) and ensures that all players have taken the same number of turns in that round. The idea is to prevent “one more card, just one more card” syndrome that spirals into a game-night epic saga of near-misses and near-keens.

To implement this practically: use a visible round counter and agree before the round begins that every player receives one opportunity to choose a card, then proceed to scoring. If a dispute arises, the guide’s approach is to pause, count, and score, then continue. It’s not the most exciting moment on the table, but it is the most reliable moment.

### 5) Card labeling, deck composition, and known misprints
The replacement guide often lists known misprints, such as mislabeling a card’s point value, or a misprint on a symbol that could be mistaken for another. If you stumble upon one of these, you’ll have a ready-made fix in the guide: substitute the corrected value or rename the misprinted symbol for the current game, and annotate your notes so future rounds follow the corrected convention. If you’re playing with new players, it’s worth saying “Hey, we’re using the replacement guide’s corrections for the night” so you can proceed without confusion.

In practice, you’ll print a small “errata card” or annotate the physical deck with a tiny sticker showing corrected values.
