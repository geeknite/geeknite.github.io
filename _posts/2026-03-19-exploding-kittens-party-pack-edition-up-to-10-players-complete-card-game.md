## How to play with up to 10 players

The base game is already a sprint: draw a card, play a card, try not to explode. The Party Pack edition simply inserts more people into the dance floor, without turning the rules into a mathematical nightmare. The turn order remains the same: players take turns in clockwise order, playing cards to sabotage, protect, or explode the tension. The goal is to avoid drawing an Exploding Kitten card while using Defuse cards to stay alive when the cat-shaped doom lands on someone else’s head.

Key adjustments for large groups:

- Card counts scale the longer you play, but the core loop remains simple and intuitive.
- You will have more opportunities for misdirection, bluffing, and debating whether the person across the table is about to defuse or unleash a disaster.
- With ten players, the swingy nature is amplified: a single hand can swing from hero to zero with barely a cat whisker of luck.

The Nope card remains the crowd-pleaser: a quick counterspell that makes a mockery of anticipated moves. In party mode you will see this card deployed with theatrical flair, often right after the “I was totally ready for this” moment. ## Strategy and player interaction

Do not mistake this for a deep strategy game; it is more like a playful duel of wits punctuated by raucous cat puns. Still, there are strategic elements worth noting:

- Bluffing matters. In a crowd of ten, you can overplay your hand or underplay it and still emerge victorious. Reading the room becomes a skill, not just a hobby.
- Timing is everything. A well-timed Nope can derail someone who looks like a runaway winner, while a poor Nope can waste a precious defuse later in the game. The crowd reacts to every blink and eyebrow raise as if they were a panel of suspicious jury members.
- Resource management still matters. Defuse cards are finite; you will feel the pressure to decide when to save them for the endgame or to cash them in early for a dramatic turn.

If you want a purely tactical breakdown, you may be disappointed: the charm of Exploding Kittens is its social layer, not its detailed resource optimization. In Party Pack mode, the social dynamics become a kinetic stream of jokes, glares, and the occasional furious whisper of “I was robbed!” It’s glorious to behold and slightly questionable if you are playing with colleagues who value silent professionalism over a good cat meme.

## Setup and teardown tips

- Keep the rulebook handy, but don’t worry about memorizing every edge case on the first playthrough. The first game is mostly a warm-up, a way to understand how the Nope cards interact with different card types.
- Organize the deck by color and function as you shuffle for a smoother setup. With up to 10 players, a clean shuffle translates into less table chaos and more happy chaos.

- Designate a “chaos coordinator” for the first round to guide the group through the rules and keep things light instead of turning the session into a detective thriller about rule interpretation.

## House rules and variations

For long-running game nights, consider these gentle variants to keep things fresh:

- The Buddy Defuse Rule: if two Defuse cards appear in a single hand, two players must cooperate to use both on the same round, producing a comedic mini-choreography.
- The Dice of Doom: swap a few card draw rules with a couple of light dice rolls to add unpredictability (that still keeps things fun and quick).
- Time-bound rounds: a timer keeps the round moving; this helps prevent a single tense exchange from dragging the table into existential dread in the middle of a game night.

These modifications keep the integrity of the core game while nudging it into a more dynamic social experience. They are optional but highly recommended for groups that love to tinker.
