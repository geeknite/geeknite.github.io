### Materials, casting, and edge cases
In terms of material, Fortune Row sits comfortably in the resin/plastic sphere common to modern minis. The level of detail translates well to standard 28–32mm scales. There are a few areas to pay attention to during prep:
- Small details near hands and faces can be brittle; a gentle cleanup with a sharp hobby knife will pay off when you’re doing primer.
- The more intricate textures (like lace, chainmail, or etched metal) respond well to thin layers of paint; plan your basecoat to leave room for shading.
- If you’re planning on base swaps, compatibility is generally good; the bases are well-sized for stable posing without a heavy base rig.

As with any resin or white metal kit, you’ll want to wash the parts in warm soapy water and prime before painting. A good primer, followed by thin glaze layers to build up the noir shadows, will help these miniatures deliver the “moments of truth” on your tabletop.

### Painting tips and tabletop playability
If you’re chasing a quick start, try a three-step approach:
1) Base coat: Neutral tones with a touch of desaturated color on each figure to reflect their roles (golded accents for the mastermind, warm browns for the enforcer, and steel-gray for the clockwork fellow).
2) Shadows: A soft black wash into crevices will give depth without pooling in every nook.
3) Highlights: Focus on edges along fabrics and mechanical parts to create a cinematic edge without over-saturating the paint job.

The verdict here is simple: Fortune Row paint up nicely and look great on the table. The detail holds up at a reasonable distance, so you won’t need to fancy up with a very precise brush to get a satisfying result. If you’re a player who wants their hero to look compelling in cinematic shots, these figures take well to shading and edge highlighting, and they respond nicely to a light dry brush that pops the raised textures without washing out the silhouette.

### Painting and display tips
Painting Tip 1: Use a slightly desaturated palette for the clothes to keep the noir mood intact. Bright red accents work well as focal points but avoid neon saturations that clash with the city-night vibe.
Painting Tip 2: Use edge highlights sparingly. A thin line along the weapons, jewelry, or mechanical parts will catch light in dramatic moments without looking overbearing.
Painting Tip 3: Rim shading around the bases with a cool gray can help separate the figure from the board, especially on darker gaming tables.

To display, consider a small diorama base that evokes a neon-lit alley: a few bits of clear resin for rain, dry-brushed bricks, and a subtle gloss to simulate wet surfaces. The figures love to be the hero of the shot, but the setting around them should tell the larger story you’re trying to tell.
