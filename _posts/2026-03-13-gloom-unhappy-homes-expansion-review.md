## What is Unhappy Homes?

### Thematic overview

The Unhappy Homes expansion leans further into the story aspect of Gloom. It invites you to set up more elaborate, more ridiculous households with interwoven backstories, plot twists, and house-specific effects. Think of it as the original game’s characters getting a spinoff where the houses themselves have moods, reputations, and a tendency to sabotage the happiness meters of the residents who forgot to pay their spectral bills. The houses are not just static backdrops; they’re active participants in the drama, with their own quirks and occasional curses that ripple across the table. It’s cozy, it’s chaotic, and it’s very much in the spirit of a gothic soap opera narrated by a raven that refuses to stop heckling you.

### Card types introduced

Unhappy Homes adds a broad mix of new cards layered on top of the base game’s standard families and events. You’ll encounter more house cards, more neighborhood complications, and a handful of bespoke events that only trigger when a particular home’s mood shifts. In practice, that means more ways to twist a story into a deliciously deranged arc. The expansion keeps the core mechanics intact—cards have gloom values, you want yours to be low and your opponents' to be high—but it complicates the decision tree in entertaining ways. It’s not a by-the-numbers expansion; it’s a narrative expansion that nudges you toward storytelling choices as much as it nudges you toward strategy.

## Components

If you’ve opened the base game box and stared longingly at your collection of translucent gloom markers, you’ll appreciate the expansion’s approach to components. The Unhappy Homes kit typically includes:

- A new deck of house cards with attached backstories and mood effects.
- New family cards that can be paired with the existing roster or nestled with the new homes for fresh tragic arc connections.
- A set of event cards that nudge players toward thematic mini-scenes during play.
- A small handful of tokens that track mood, misfortune, or spectral influence, depending on how dramatic you want to get.
- A dedicated rule sheet that covers the interaction between base game mechanics and these new elements, including some optional, house-specific rules for players who want to lean into a more narrative experience.

The quality feels consistent with the base game—solid card stock, clear art direction, and humor that sits somewhere between cheeky and chilling. The art is the same pulp sensibility you expect from Gloom: bold lines, exaggerated expressions, and a palette that leans into purples, creases of gloom, and the occasional flash of red to signal a family emergency. It’s not about gore or horror; it’s about atmosphere and the silly, often pun-based misery of your characters’ lives.

A small note on the artwork: the houses themselves are treated as characters. The mood of a room is conveyed visually—think creaky floorboards, cobwebs in the corners, and portraits that seem to leer as you draw a card that could shift the entire family’s fate. If you enjoy the cartoony macabre vibe of base Gloom, you’ll feel right at home here. The art doesn’t shout; it nudges. It’s the slow creep you want when you’re trying to tell a story about a family whose most heartfelt wish is for the mortgage to disappear, only it never does.

## Mechanics and Gameplay

### New features that matter in practice

Unhappy Homes introduces a few well-chosen mechanics that breathe life into the game’s already strong storytelling engine. The central idea is that homes carry moods and histories that can alter the way events unfold. Some effects target a single family, others ripple across the table, and a few are triggered only when a family is in a particularly bad or good situation. The result is a game that can tilt dramatically as players leverage the house’s mood to shape outcomes or to set up dramatic reversals for the player sitting next to you.

In play, you’ll notice the following shifts:

- House moods can rise or fall, changing the payoff of certain events or the penalties you suffer for poor life choices. Mood shifts are often triggered by events that relate to the family’s backstory or the house’s history, which means you’ll want to pay attention to the chain of cards you’ve revealed and the way they interact with the house’s temperament.
- New event cards offer scene-driven prompts. They tend to encourage a quick pivot in strategy, nudging you toward a storytelling beat that can swing the gloom meter several points in a single turn. The best results come from leaning into the prompt rather than fighting it; a little improv goes a long way here.
- The marriage of house and family creates synergies you didn’t know you needed. A house card might provide a small, perpetual penalty as long as a family remains, but a matching backstory card could dramatically reduce that penalty or turn it into a net gain if you can orchestrate the timing just so. It’s a little engine-building, a little narrative shaping, and a lot of fun if your group enjoys plots with a wink and a nudge.

### The impact on pacing and table time

The expansion adds depth but not complexity for the sake of it. You’ll notice games can take a bit longer as players weave in house-based story hooks and react to mood shifts. The pacing remains friendly to social deduction fans and storytelling enthusiasts alike; you’re still gluing pieces of a morbid story together while keeping a careful eye on the score trackers. If your table gets bogged down in rules minutiae, the included rule sheet does a decent job of outlining quick-start modes and streamlined interactions for a brisker session.

### Opening moves and mid-game arc

A recurring piece of advice with Unhappy Homes is to treat it as a storytelling accelerator. At setup, skim the houses you’re drawing and think about potential backstories that could thread into the families you’ll encounter. Early plays benefit from establishing a couple of dramatic hooks that you can reference later in the game. For instance, if you’ve got a haunted hallway and a family with a mysterious ancestor, you can set up a mini-arc about a cursed inheritance that comes to a peak in mid-game. It’s not required, but the expansion rewards conversation and collaborative storytelling as much as sharp card play.

In mid-game, the mood changes become more pronounced. A well-timed event can swing the board by several gloom points, turning a previously safe card into a liability or giving you a path to a dramatic, last-minute win. The thrill here comes from watching other players attempt to salvage a story that is increasingly tangled in misfortune. The person who can improvise the most comedic tragedy often earns bragging rights that last longer than the game’s actual score.

## Strategy and Tactics

### Teaming up with the house

The core strategic insight with Unhappy Homes is to use the house’s mood as a tool, not just a backdrop. If a particular house punishes certain storylines, counteract that by leaning into other backstories that the house makes more friendly. It’s a dance: you sacrifice a minor long-term advantage to secure a dramatic mid-game swing when the house mood aligns with your narrative beat.

### Card economy and timing

As with base gloom, you’ll want to balance the flow of cards between your hand and the table. The expansion adds more levers to pull, which means you’ll often benefit from a slower, more deliberate tempo. Don’t rush to dump your gloom cards just because you drew a big event. Sometimes holding onto a card that interacts with a house mood can set up a powerful chain of events several turns down the line.

### Endgame gambits

The last few turns in Unhappy Homes can get hilariously chaotic. A well-timed event can push a rival over the edge by exposing a tragic backstory that the house amplifies, while you quietly position a card that reduces your own gloom just enough to slip into the lead at the last moment. The key is to keep your options open and to lean into the game’s storytelling vibe. If you can conjure a ready-made scene for the final few cards, you’ll likely collect the last laughs and the last points.

## Artwork, Theme, and Tone

The Unhappy Homes expansion keeps the same tongue-in-cheek humor that characterizes Gloom. It doesn’t go darker for the sake of it; it goes darker to spark more ridiculous storytelling. The art works in tandem with the mechanics to emphasize the kind of comically tragic family melodrama you’d expect from a gothic comic. The tone remains light enough for casual play but sharp enough that your group will appreciate the witty prompts and the way a single card can turn a scene from charmingly tragic to gloriously absurd.

If you’re someone who likes to narrate a scene as you play, this expansion offers a lot of rich fuel. The house cards are effectively mood boards for your storytelling, and the event cards give you new lines to riff on as you describe a family’s most eyebrow-raising misfortune. It’s funny, it’s a touch macabre, and it’s surprisingly good at making the table engage in joint storytelling rather than simply counting gloom points.

## Interaction with the Base Game

Unhappy Homes slots neatly into a standard session of Gloom without requiring a heavy re-teach of rules. If your group already knows how to weave stories around families and misfortunes, you’ll adapt quickly. The expansion doesn’t rewrite the core mechanics; it adds new layers that you can opt into as you see fit. For players who crave more structure around storytelling, the new house and event prompts provide a gentle scaffold that guides the tale without boxing you into a single narrative path.

One nice feature is that the expansion respects the base game’s pace. You can include just a subset of its components for a quicker game, or go all-out with the full complement for a longer, more narrative night. The design philosophy here is to give you more ways to tell a good, funny, tragical story rather than to punish you with a completely different scoring scheme.

## Solo and Cooperative Play

Gloom is not primarily designed as a solo game, but the Unhappy Homes expansion does offer some fertile ground for a solo play experience if you’re the kind of gamer who wants to chase a good story while commuting on a bus. A handful of event cards translate well into solo play as you attempt to craft a personal tragedy arc against the house’s mood. For true co-op vibes, you can run a 'house vs. players' mode where a single house card acts as the antagonist, pushing gloom on everyone until someone breaks the curse. It’s not a canonical official mode, but it’s a natural extension if you enjoy experimenting with how storytelling and strategy interact.

## Replay Value and Longevity

The expansion’s value lies in its ability to re-signal familiar mechanics with fresh prompts and house synergies. Because the cards are designed to combine with the base game's families, you can run dozens of unique combinations by mixing and matching. Even if you’ve played Gloom dozens of times, Unhappy Homes invites you to discover a new set of unfortunate naps, new dialog prompts, and new dramatic reveals. Thematic storytelling is a moving target; this expansion helps you chase it from a variety of entertaining angles rather than forcing you into a single, predetermined path.

If you’re the sort of player who likes to curate a long-term table tradition around a single game, Unhappy Homes can become the centerpiece. You’ll likely end up with in-jokes, recurring house pairings, and a ritual of setting up a micro-drama for the players who crave the most colorful misfortune.

## Comparisons to Other Expansions

Gloom has a handful of expansions, and Unhappy Homes sits comfortably among the more narrative-focused entries. If you’ve tried other expansions that add more cards but not much context, you’ll appreciate how this one threads storytelling into the experience. It’s not the biggest mechanical overhaul; it’s the most theater-ready expansion so far. If you’re seeking something that nudges you toward improv and character-driven drama, this is the one to pick up. If you prefer a very strict, numbers-heavy approach to gloom, you might be better off with a lighter add-on that focuses on more cards and fewer story prompts.

If you want more ways to tell ridiculous, melodramatic stories at the table and you enjoyed base Gloom enough to crave new narrative hooks, Unhappy Homes is a slam dunk. It’s not essential for a casual night, but it is absolutely worth it for groups who like to lean hard into storytelling, theater of the absurd, and the particular joy of watching a table of friends make a neighbor’s haunted chandelier into a plot device for their own petty revenge stories.
