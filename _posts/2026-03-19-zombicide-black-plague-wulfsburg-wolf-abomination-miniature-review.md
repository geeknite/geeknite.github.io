## Assembly: tips, tricks, and common pitfalls
Assembling a big boss like this is less about “build, glue, done” and more about choreography. You’ll likely deal with several pieces (head, snout, two front legs, hind legs, torso, tail, and a couple of accessory bones or runes that hang from the armor). Here are practical steps that can save you from crying into a bottle of plastic glue:

- Clean and dry-fit first: Because this model is a predator in plastic, misalignment at the shoulder joints can create stress fractures or odd posing. Dry-fit the pieces to find a natural stance before you apply glue.
- Use a generous but tidy amount of glue on large contact surfaces: the model’s weight is downward-facing, so ensure the joints have secure contact rather than a fragile dab. Running a thin bead along the seam and pinning with a tiny clamp or a paperclip can help with long-term durability.
- Reserve the “jaw” pieces until the very end: the mouth often needs a slight adjustment once the rest of the model stands. It’s easier to pose the body first, then snap the jaw into place for a natural bite.
- Primer matters: you’ll be tempted to skip primer on such a dramatic model, but the fur texture reads best with a good primer. A black or gray primer will help you push highlights in fur more naturally than white would.
- Edge highlighting on bone accents: the Wulfsburg variant uses a lot of bone runes and armor panels. A bright edge highlight on these can help the model pop on the table during heavy light conditions.

If you own a hobby knife, consider removing any mold lines with a light touch before gluing. The goal isn’t to erase the model’s personality entirely, but to avoid a lumpy silhouette that makes the pose read as bloated rather than dynamic.

For painters who like to plan color schemes, a quick palette suggestion: start with a desaturated base for the fur (e.g., a muted gray-blue), a midtone that reflects the wolf’s natural range (stone gray or ash brown), and deep shadows in the crevices. Accent runses with bone-white or ivory highlights; use a pop color for optional armor plates if you want a narrative element (for example, a copper or brass edge on the bone runes). If you’re inclined to a more “fantasy winter” look, you could contrast the fur with a chilly teal or icy blue to evoke a glacial atmosphere as if this abomination prowls straight from a frozen river of doom.

## Painting tips: getting the most from your miniature
A few practical tips for painting this model without turning it into a survival horror scenario for your brush:

- Block base colors first: fur in cool greys or browns, bone runes in ivory, armor panels in a metallic tone. Keep your color choices cohesive so the final product looks like a single creature rather than a patchwork of parts.
- Use washes for depth: a dark wash in fur crevices will reveal texture without needing 12 layers of highlight. You can then pick highlights with a light dry brush and finish with edge highlights on runes and armor pieces.
- Layering is your friend: you don’t need a full gradient on every edge, but layering different tints on the fur can create subtle variety that reads well on the table under typical room lighting.
- Eyes matter: a simple trick is to give the wolf a piercing set of eyes (a bright amber or icy blue) to create a focal point that draws the eye and increases the dramatic effect when the model sits center stage.
- Base the stance to the board: the model’s pose should guide its narrative on the table. If you’re going for a mid-leap attack, adjust the tail and the hind legs accordingly so your display looks balanced—this isn’t a free-floating head with legs at random angles.
