## How to Play: Rules as Friendly as a Golden Retriever
Rummy basics are straightforward: form valid combinations (sets and runs) and be the first to deplete your hand. The animal variation adds a layer of whimsy rather than complexity. Here’s a practical walkthrough to get you into the rhythm without needing a graduate degree in card psychology.

### Setup
- Shuffle the animal and standard decks together (if your edition combines them) or separate them if your edition uses a dedicated animal subset. If you’re feeling thematic, place a toy otter or a rubber plant on the table for wallpaper vibes.
- Determine the dealer; deal a pre-agreed number of cards to each player (usually 7–10 depending on the house rules).
- The rest forms a draw pile; the top card goes face-up to start the discard pile. The game can begin with a little ceremonial flourish (shuffle, wink, place a parrot card on top). It’s optional but strongly recommended for mythic vibes.

### Turn Structure
- On your turn, you draw a card (from the draw pile or the discard pile) and then discard one card.
- You try to assemble your hand into valid sets (three or four of a kind, using animal cards if you like) or runs (sequences of consecutive cards in the same suit). Whitman’s twist makes you occasionally throw a curveball at the other players by using animal cards as wilds or as special scoring pieces, depending on edition.
- When you have a legal hand, you can declare a “rummy” or “go out,” depending on your group’s etiquette. The first to do so wins the round and tallies points. The animal cards often carry playful point values that can help or hinder your strategy, adding a lighthearted risk to the risk-reward calculus.

### Scoring and Ending the Game
- Traditional rummy scoring is flexible here. Some editions award points for each card left in opponents’ hands; others use a simple card-count. The animal cards typically contribute unique values that add a small layer of strategy to which cards you’re comfortable discarding or keeping.
- The game ends when a player reaches a predetermined point total or after a set number of rounds. The winner is whoever has the most “animal charm” intact at the end, i.e., the fewest points in hand. It’s silly in the best way—this is exactly the kind of game that invites jokes about “collecting more zoological misfits than your rival.”

If you’re worried about rule creep or ambiguous variants, the best move is to pick one of your favorite holographic-bellied rule sets (from the instruction sheet or a fan-made house rule) and stick to it. The goal isn’t to memorize a parchment of exceptions; it’s to savor the vibe while you shuffle, deal, and chase that satisfying “chomp” sound of a well-timed discard.
