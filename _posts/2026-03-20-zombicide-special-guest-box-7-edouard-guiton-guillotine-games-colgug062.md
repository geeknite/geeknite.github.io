Introduction
==============
When you think you’ve survived every flavor of zombie apocalypse, Guillotine Games drops a Special Guest Box that turns your table into a madhouse of plastic mold and cheeky design decisions. The COLGUG062 code marks the box in the long line of collaboratives, and if you’re here, you’re likely a Zombicide devotee or someone who enjoys the spectacle of a big zombie horde with a side of art school typography.

Aesthetics and sculpting: Edouard Guiton’s fingerprints
===================================================
If you’ve seen Edouard Guiton’s work anywhere else, you already know what to expect: a certain pop-art, slightly grotesque charm. In these Special Guest minis, the sculpts lean toward stylized proportions, with exaggerated facial features and bold, clean lines. The result is a set of survivors who look like they stepped out of a graphic novel and into a kitchen in late-night zombie apocalypse dramedy. The zombies, too, carry Guiton’s signature: silhouettes with clear silhouettes, big silhouettes, and enough personality to make you pause mid-game and say, “That one’s clearly plotting something.”

In practice, the sculpts read well on the table. They’re easy to identify at a glance, which matters when you’re juggling a dozen "white plastic friends" and trying to remember who got the extra action in the last turn. The details—the grit on clothing, the subtle batik patterns on a survivor’s jacket, the grotesque yet charmingly cartoonish zombie expressions—are crisp without feeling sterile. If you’re the kind of painter who loves to highlight a single brush stroke for dramatic effect, you’ll have a field day here. The balance between style and practicality is a tightrope walk, and the Box 7 team does not wobble; they moonwalk, and that’s kind of impressive.

New mechanics and how they affect playability
=================================================
Two things stand out in Box 7’s design: a thematic twist in the mission deck and a light-but-present shift in the action economy. The mission cards introduce a few conditional objectives that encourage players to balance defense with a bit of aggressive push, which is a refreshing change of pace if you’ve been tunneling through a standard survival plan for too long. It’s not a complete overhaul; rather, it’s a flavorful nudge toward story-driven play. If you enjoy your Zombicide rounds anchored in narrative flavor, this box provides a satisfying nudge forward.

The survivors’ abilities tilt toward cinematic moments: a character who can rescue a fallen ally more reliably, a second character with a one-turn “scare” aura that discourages fast-moving zombies, and a third option that provides a temporary boost to range-based attacks. It’s not a revolution, but it is a gentle reminder that character synergy matters. The added survivability adds a layer of cooperation that doesn’t overshadow the core game’s tactical bones. You’ll still have to juggle grid positions, line-of-sight checks, and the occasional “which zombie spawns where?” decision—only now there’s a little more theater for your group to enjoy.

Painting and hobby tips
=======================
If you plan to paint these minis, you’re in for a treat. Edouard Guiton’s characters cry out for bold color blocking, followed by selective weathering to bring out textures on clothing and armor. Here are a few practical tips:
- Base coat with a smooth skin-tone for survivors, then add a wash to bring out facial features. Don’t overdo it—Zombicide’s style benefits from clean contrasts rather than muddy gradients.
- For the zombies, keep it grotesque but legible. A subtle dry brush on the clothing can separate figures quickly during a fast-paced game.
- Use a high-gloss finish on any metallic accents (knives, bolt-action gear) to catch the light during dramatic turns.
- If you’re new to painting, start with a two-color scheme per miniature. It helps you see the character quickly during gameplay and reduces the amount of time you spend on base coats.
- Protect your work with a matte sealer, especially if you play on a board that picks up stray scuff marks. Zombicide’s chaos is dramatic enough without extra wear and tear from your paint job.

Playing with Box 7: quick-start tips
=====================================
- Start with a short session to acclimate your group to the new mechanics. Don’t jump into a full campaign until you’ve had at least one or two rounds with the new survivals and the exclusive zombie variant.
- Use the included mission cards as a shared objective for your first couple of games. It’s a good reason to coordinate strategies instead of sprinting toward individual glory.
- If you’re new to Zombicide, pair this box with a basic core set and a couple of expansion packs to keep the learning curve digestible. The Special Guest Box is a great way to introduce new players to the rhythm of the game without overwhelming them with options.
- Consider rotating who the “leader” is for each game. The new abilities in Box 7 reward collaborative planning, and a rotating leadership role helps keep everyone engaged.
