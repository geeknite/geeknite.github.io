## Authentication and preservation: how to know you’ve got the real deal
Authenticating a vintage No. 133 deck involves a few practical checks, mostly about packaging, card-back design, and paper quality:

- Packaging cues: authentic vintage sets have period-appropriate packaging with bold typography and certain color hues typical of 1987. Look for wear consistent with a box that’s been opened, not a modern reprint with glossy coating.
- Card-back insignia: the back often features the game’s branding and a consistent design language that aligns with other Second Edition assets from that era. If you see obvious inconsistencies in font styling or color balance, it could be a red flag.
- Paper weight and finish: vintage cards typically have a slightly heavier stock with a matte finish. If they feel too slick or too light, you might be dealing with a reprint or a near-copy.
- Ink bleed and edge wear: intentional aging is common in real vintage decks. If the edges look artificially distressed, you may be looking at a modern “ aged-graphic design” piece rather than the genuine thing.

Preservation-wise, the best practice is to keep cards flat, away from moisture, and away from direct sunlight. If you plan on playing them, consider protective sleeves or a dedicated card box. And whenever you handle the deck, wash your hands first—greasy fingertips and marker ink do not play well together in the long run.
