## How to Play: A Rapid Refresher and a Dose of Geekery
Skip-Bo’s core mechanic is deceptively simple: build sequential runs using numbered cards, managing a personal stockpile while trying to force your opponents into drawing more cards. The 162-card deck provides enough shuffle variety to ensure you’ll see a few “Wait, what just happened?” moments before the endgame is decided by a single bold play.

### The Quick Rundown
- Each player starts with a stockpile of face-down cards. Only the top card of your stockpile is visible.
- Players take turns drawing from the draw pile and playing cards in ascending order on Central Build Piles.
- You can also play from your hand to your own build piles or to steal a move away from another player by using wild cards.
- The first person to clear their stockpile wins. If you’re playing with extended rules, you might see a second or third winner depending on the house.

This section is intentionally short, because the joy of Skip-Bo lies not in long-winded explanations but in the moment when you realize you’ve been overthinking a two-card move for 45 seconds and your friend is already laughing at your misplay. The game invites both strategic planning and glorious accidents—the sweet spot where table camaraderie is built, one misread card at a time.

## Strategy, Tactics, and the Joy of Predictable Chaos
Skip-Bo rewards a mix of planning, memory, and willingness to risk a move that could go either way. The 162-card mix provides a rhythm where you believe you have a handle on the board, only to realize an unexpected wild card reshuffles the entire strategic landscape.

### Basic Strategies
- Prioritize clearing your own stockpile early: a quick stockpile cleanup gives you more agency in late game turns.
- Use wild cards strategically to bridge gaps in your own build piles or to hamper others when they’re within striking distance of victory.
- Balance risk and reward: sometimes it’s worth letting a mid-game opponent stumble into a big run rather than forcing a single perfectly-timed move that could backfire.

### Advanced Tactics
- Card counting, with a twist: while you don’t get a formal score-count, remembering which numbers have already appeared helps you predict what might come next. This is less about perfect recall and more about pattern spotting under table-side pressure.
- Tempo management: decide when to push a build pile aggressively and when to hold back to deny others an easy path to victory.
- Psychological warfare: a well-timed grunt, a dramatic pause, or a deliberate misread can tilt the room and force your opponents into planning errors.
