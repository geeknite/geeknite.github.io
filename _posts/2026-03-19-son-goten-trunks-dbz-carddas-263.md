### Grading and condition tips

If you are serious about your collection, consider professional grading for a No 263 print. Carddass cards across generations have different standards for what counts as a mint condition. When evaluating for grading, you should look at the following:

- Edges and corners: A sharp, crisp edge with minimal whitening or chipping is a good sign. Be mindful of hairline scratches that may not affect gameplay but will impact a grade.
- Surface: The surface should be clean with no heavy scratches that disrupt the image. Foil variants may reveal rainbow patterns upon inspection; inconsistent foiling can signal a misprint or wear.
- Centering: For many vintage cards, center alignment matters. A slight tilt in the card image might reduce the overall grade, though some collectives appreciate the character of imperfect centering.
- Backing: The texture and print on the back should be uniform. Any signs of water damage, creases, or color bleed should be carefully noted when submitting for grading.

If you plan to submit the No 263 for grading, merge your practical collecting goals with an honest appraisal from a reputable grading service. Some enthusiasts prefer to submit only their favorites; others take a risk and send multiple copies to see which ones come back with top scores.

### Display and care tips

Displaying a significant card like No 263 requires a light touch. Here are a few practical tips:

- Sleeves first: Use a high quality sleeve to protect from fingerprints and moisture. Soft inner sleeves reduce friction and reduce the risk of edge wear.
- Top-loaders: For active display, consider top-loaders to keep the card upright and safe. If you have a framed display, ensure the frame is UV-protected to minimize color fade.
- Binding and binder selection: If you prefer binder pages, choose those with adequate spacing to avoid friction against neighboring cards. Japanese cards sometimes have slightly different tolerances; ensure you do not bend or press too hard against other cards.
- Climate control: Avoid extreme humidity and temperature changes. Card damage often starts with moisture and heat, which can lead to warping or bond deterioration on the back.
