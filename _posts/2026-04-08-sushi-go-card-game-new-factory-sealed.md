## How to Play: The 1–2–3 of Sushi Go!

### The Goal
The goal is simple on the surface: collect the best combos to maximize your score. The more you align your cards with the recipes that score big, the more likely you’ll be the top chef at the end of the game. The bookkeeping remains friendly, and the scoring rules do not demand a degree in culinary meteorology to decipher. If you’ve cooked up a little math in your head before, you’ll feel right at home; if not, the game offers a robust learning curve that you’ll master after a few rounds with friends who love to tease your choices.

### The Setup
- Shuffle the deck and deal a hand to each player (the exact hand size depends on the number of players; typically, you’ll see around 7 cards per player per round in a 2–5 player setup).
- Place the rest of the deck face-down in the center as the draw pile.
- Each player looks at their hand, selects one card to keep, and passes the rest to the left. This continues until everyone has drafted all cards from their hand for that round. The passing creates a delightful push-your-luck element: what you take now might make your neighbor’s next pick worse—or better, if you’re a benevolent drafter.

### The Turn and Draft
On each turn, you choose one card from your current hand and place it face-down in front of you, then pass the remainder to the player on your left. When everyone has picked, you reveal your chosen cards and proceed to the next round. The cycle repeats for 4–5 rounds (depending on player count). The pace is brisk; players who over-think the first draft tend to pay for it with a late-round scramble. The joy is in reading the room: you want to anticipate what others want while also ensuring you aren’t left with a hand that is all Wasabi and Dumplings because you misread the table.

### The Scoring System: A Tiny Puzzle with Big Rewards
At the end of each round, you tally points from your collected sets:
- Nigiri cards grant base points, with Wasabi magnifying the next Nigiri’s value when used wisely.
- Makis award points based on who holds the most Makis in a round.
- Sashimi requires collecting three cards to score. If you only get two, you walk away with zero for that set.
- Tempura rewards paired cards; two Tempura cards give you a little boost, but you must actually own both to cash in.
- Dumplings scale with quantity; the more you grab, the higher the payoff.
- Pudding cards are counted at the end of the game for their end-game utility. They can swing a final score if you’ve been strategic elsewhere and saved a few pudding chips for late-night math.

This combination of small, quick decisions and potential big points makes every round feel like a mini-puzzle competition. It’s not about who has the biggest card, but who can assemble the most efficient, high-scoring cabinet of dishes before the rounds run out.

## Strategy Corner: From Chaos to Culinary Victory

### Early Round Etiquette: The Gentle Opera of Drafting
In the first rounds, you want to establish a baseline of options without overcommitting. A safe approach is to mix a couple of high-scoring potential runs with a few flexible cards in case your neighbors pivot to a different strategy. The general rule of thumb is: don’t over-index on one type of card in the early rounds unless you’re confident you can complete the set without giving your rivals a clean blocking path.

### The Wasabi Conundrum: When to Play It Smart
Wasabi is the sneaky multiplier: it magnifies the value of the next Nigiri you play. If you’ve drafted a few Nigiri cards and see an opportunity to maximize their value with Wasabi in subsequent rounds, you can set up a killer turn. But be mindful: using Wasabi too early can backfire if your next drawn Nigiri is a dud or if Wasabi becomes a coveted block by other players who have a plan to triple-dip Nigiri power later on.

### Sashimi as a Late-Blooming Engine
Sashimi centers on collecting three matching cards. The risk here is obvious: you commit to three specific cards, and if you don’t get the third in time, you’re left with little payoff. The cunning strategy is to hold two Sashimi and observe the drafting flow. If the table starts to diverge from your direction and you think you won’t complete the set, pivot to other scoring opportunities. The beauty of Sushi Go! is that you’ll often snag two good sets while gambling on the third, which can still yield respectable points.

### Temptations of Dumplings and Puddings
Dumplings reward you more as you collect more of them, which makes them perfect for a “fill the gap” playstyle when your other plans are stalling. Puddings, counted at the end, provide a dramatic swing if you’ve set yourself up with a strong mid-game plan. Don’t ignore pudding cards; they’re quiet, but they can tip the scales in a close final tally.

### House Rules to Elevate the Experience
- Drafting with a timer: If you want more tempo and more table talk, add a 30–45 second sand-timer per turn to keep players honest and prompt.
- Solo variant: For a light solo practice, you can draft cards against a pretend opponent by rotating the pass to an imaginary player. It’s not the same as a real game, but it helps you practice the decision-making process and can be a surprising warm-up before a real round.
- Team play: In larger groups, pair up players into teams of two. Each team drafts and discusses choices after each round, encouraging collaboration and a little friendly sabotage in a controlled environment.
