## Gameplay in a Nutshell (and in a Bento Box)
### The drafting loop
Sushi Go! is a card-drafting game. In a 2–5 player match, each round you’re dealt a hand of 7 cards (adjusted for the player count), and you secretly pick one card to keep while passing the rest to your neighbor. After everyone has chosen a card, you reveal your chosen plate and resolve its score contribution for that round. The round ends when the deck is exhausted, and you tally up scores based on bit-sized sushi combos.

In the Deluxe Edition, the drafting loop remains the same, but the improved physical components make it easier to track what’s out there. The cards are less likely to slide around, and you won’t lose track of a valuable card under a beverage coaster. It’s the little things that make a big difference when you’re scanning for a mini combo like “three tempuras” or “two nigiri with a chili kick.”

### Card interactions and strategy
The strategy in Sushi Go! is deliberately approachable. You’re not waging wars on a chessboard; you’re selecting mini-suites of cards that will score points when collected in groups. The Deluxe Edition’s surface clarity helps with quick triage decisions: can you assemble a set of rolls efficiently? Do you want to disrupt a rival’s plan by snagging a card that would otherwise complete their combo? The answers often come down to milliseconds and a good sense of timing.

- Nigiri and Wasabi: The classic combos remain the backbone of most late-game points. Wasabi cards empower your nigiri if you can set up the turn order to play Wasabi first, then Nigiri for triple damage. In practice, this feels a bit like pulling off a one-two punch in a powwow of sushi-shaped tokens.
- Tempura and Sashimi: These are the “smaller bets” that can pay off big with the right timing. The Deluxe Edition’s clearer icons help you track whether you’re on pace to complete a Tempura pair or a Sashimi triple while still paying attention to the table’s evolving mood.
- Dumplings and Cherries: The new tokens don’t drastically alter the core math, but they do make you more mindful about your eighth round decision to push for a dumpling or settle for something safer. The result is a game that feels both familiar and slightly more strategic as you anticipate how others will respond to your choices.

If you’ve played base Sushi Go! before, you’ll notice that the Deluxe Edition nudges you toward a smoother rhythm. It’s less about dramatic “aha” moments and more about the flow: quick decisions, faster reveals, and a satisfying crescendo as the last few rounds edge toward a crunchy conclusion.
