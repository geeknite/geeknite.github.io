## Design, sculpt, and paintability
### Sculpt quality and character
The Special Guest miniatures lean into character-driven storytelling. Each statue has facial expressions, small accessories, and personalized armor or clothing that communicates a backstory before you even start painting. If you enjoy the theater of gaming, this is a boon. If you just want a bunch of identical soldiers marching in lockstep, you might find yourself muttering about how these guests keep stealing the spotlight. The design team did a commendable job balancing distinctiveness with batch-friendly assembly, which means you can still convert or swap parts if you want a few bespoke survivors in your roster.

### Paintability and assembly tips
Paintability is where the set shines. The smaller scale means details can be bold enough to read from across the table, which is excellent for streams, photo games, or those who enjoy the “ooh that shading is neat” moment. The best practice is to pre-clean and prime each piece, then use a thin baseline coat to avoid obscuring raised details. Dry brushing works well on the minor textures, and washes in the recesses will bring out the depth. If you are a seasoned painter, you may want to push contrasting colors to emphasize the character traits, using a bright accent color to reflect a guest’s flamboyance or a darker hue to reflect the menace they bring into the group’s dynamic.

If you are new to painting, don’t panic. Zombicide minis are forgiving. The key is to start with a solid color plan: base coat, shade, highlight, and then the little edge highlights on armor plating. A little bit of chaos comes with the territory; embrace it as your new hobby’s quirky personality. The important thing is that you end up with a cohesive set that looks like it belongs on the same board as your other survivors rather than a cross-stitched mismatch from a thrift store diorama.

### Materials and vibe
The plastic used feels sturdy enough for tabletop use but not so brittle that you’ll be afraid to handle them.
