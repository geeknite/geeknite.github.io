## How Rummy Royal actually plays

Rummy offers a simple engine: draw, discard, meld, and score. Rummy Royal adds a dash of pomp, possibly a royal title on the memory of the deck, and a sense that you are participating in a tradition older than your first aggressive hand gesture during a tense multiplayer game night. Here is a practical, not-too-abstract playthrough based on standard Rummy conventions and the likely variants described on the sheet.

### Step-by-step playthrough
1) Setup: The dealer shuffles the deck and deals a fixed number of cards to each player. In most two-to-four player rounds, you’ll see seven cards per person; with bigger groups, you might deal ten. The remaining cards form the stock face down in a draw pile. The top card from the stock is flipped to start the discard pile.
2) Objective: The aim is to form “melds” in your hand consisting of sets (three or four cards of the same rank) or runs (three or more consecutive cards of the same suit). When you can lay down your melds, you begin to reduce your hand’s dead weight and chase a clean exit.
3) Drawing and discarding: On your turn you draw one card from either the stock or the discard pile and then discard one card to the discard pile. The discard is a tactical signal to others—do you want them to grab a particular card, or are you blockading their path to victory?
4) Melding and laying down: Once you have a valid combination, you can lay it down on the table. Some variants allow you to lay off cards onto existing melds on the table, expanding sets or runs without adding new melds of your own. The rules sheet for Rummy Royal will spell out whether you can extend a run with a card from your hand or whether you must lay down complete melds all at once.
5) The Royal twist: The name implies a flourish. Some versions include royal cards or a special wild card that acts as any other card for a hand point tally or for blocking opponents. In the Whitman sheet you hold in your hands, that twist is present but not overbearing; it is the kind of rule that makes a mid-game table whisper about becoming a royal court of card-shuffling wizards.
6) Going out and scoring: When you can reduce your hand to zero by laying down all your cards in valid melds (and possibly a final discard), you win the hand. Points are tallied by counting the pips of unused cards left in opponents’ hands. The exact scoring method may differ slightly by rule edition, so the second half of the pack often includes a quick reference for scoring.
7) End of game: You decide the end condition—either play to a fixed number of hands or play until a player hits a target score. The rules encourage a sense of friendly competition and a little bit of theatrical humility when someone shouts BINGO and reveals they just scored three points on a crucial run while the rest of the table gasps in mock disbelief.
