## What is Codenames Pictures?
Codenames Pictures is a standalone entry in the Codenames family that replaces the word clues with image clues. Instead of connecting words with a single hint like “light” or “bank,” the Spymaster crafts a clue that links several pictures on the board. Picture clues can point to creatures, objects, actions, or the occasional baffling abstract image that feels like a rorschach test after two cups of juice boxes.

The core idea remains the same: two teams race to locate all their agents on the board while avoiding the opposing team’s agents and, crucially, the assassin (the thing you don’t want anyone to touch). The Spymaster may say something like “Ocean two,” hoping their team can see a whale and a sailor and a splash as three separate picture cards. If your team can name all your pictures correctly, your team wins the round; if you pick the assassin, you’ve technically triggered a “you lose” condition the same as if you yelled into the void and hoped for a clue that results in zero wrong guesses.

The pictures style makes humor and guesswork the star players. Some cards are straightforward: a dog, a rocket, a pizza slice. Others are wacky: a person riding a goat, a strange hybrid creature with a crown, or a street performer juggling bicycles. The art style is bright, cartoony, and accessible for all ages, with enough strange juxtapositions to fuel ridiculous clue discussions for hours—or at least until someone asks, “Can we just use the old game?”

If you’re curious about the vibe, the art quality, and whether the game truly scratches the “family-friendly party game” itch, you’ll find the answer both in practice and in the post-game conversations about “what exactly is this card?”


### What makes it different from the original?
- Images instead of words: the clues reference pictures rather than vocabulary.
- Similar round structure: two teams, Spymaster vs. Field Operatives, with an assassin spanning a single shared risk.
- Easier for some players: visual cues can be easier for kids and non-native speakers to latch onto; harder in the sense that artistic ambiguity can break even the best clue-givers.
- Faster or slower depending on the group: the image-based approach can sometimes speed up correct guesses, but it can also descend into a chaotic flurry of “Is that a penguin or a mime?”

## How to Play (In Plain English and a Few Laughs)
Codenames Pictures keeps the flow of the original game, but with a visual spin. Here’s a practical walkthrough that won’t require a secret decoder ring.

### Set Up
1) Shuffle the 25 image cards and lay them out in a 5x5 grid. 2) Determine which team will go first. 3) Give each team’s Spymaster a marker of which color they’re playing and a stack of clue cards. 4) Place the assassin card where everyone can see it. 5) The Field Operatives gather their tokens; let the guessing begin.

### The Spymaster’s Turn
- The Spymaster gives a one-word or short phrase clue and a number, indicating how many image cards on the board relate to the clue. For example: “Ocean, 3” might lead players to pick a whale, a sailor, and a boat (if those cards exist on the board) and hope they align with the clue.
- The clue must connect to at least one image on the board; giving a clue that is irrelevant is a strategic blunder and a waste of everyone’s time, like ordering tea at a hardware store.
- After the clue is given, the Field Operatives discuss and choose cards they believe match the clue. They reveal the chosen cards one at a time, stopping after the guessed number if they’re confident or continuing until they’re out of picks or they hit a wrong card.
- If they pick a blue agent card (assuming they’re Blue’s team), the turn ends and the other team begins their turn. If they pick a red agent card when it’s Blue’s turn, that’s not good—they’ve just honored the other team with a free point. If they pick a neutral card, the turn ends with a neutral kiss of luck no points awarded. If they pick the assassin, the game ends immediately in favor of the other team.

### The Field Operatives’ Turn
- They discuss as a group, weigh the clue’s intent, and try to identify all the cards their Spymaster hints at. This is where the “spies” in both Victorians and memes comes to life: a single well-chosen clue can unlock multiple correct cards and burn the turn of the other team.
- They continue guessing cards one at a time, with the goal of hitting all their team’s agents first.

### End of Round
- The round ends when either team completes their set of agents or hits the assassin. The game is won by the first team to correctly identify all of their agents.

The game rewards teams that can build a shared vocabulary for the board’s images—an informal code that becomes a form of shorthand during later rounds.


## Spymaster Strategies (Because Someone Has to Carry the Clue Lamp)
Being a Spymaster in Codenames Pictures is a bit like being a tour guide for a gallery you secretly hate because you have to herd your group toward the right painting while dodging the “Is this the same painting as last turn?” questions.

- Look for multi-link clues: If you notice several images share a common theme (e.g., “space,” “animals,” “food”), craft a clue that hints at multiple cards. The trick is ensuring those cards actually exist on the board and belong to your color.
- Be mindful of the picture drift: Some pictures are ambiguous; pick clues that minimize cross-team confusion. If your clue is too broad, you risk guiding the other team to the right cards—and you’ll hear about it around the snack table later.
- Use negative clues sparingly (if at all): Some players try negative clues like “not empty” or “not water.” In a pictures game, negative clues can become an overcomplicated scavenger hunt. If your group can handle it, go for it, but know you might get asked to explain yourself more than once.
- Plan for a “block” approach: If possible, guide your team toward a subset of cards that are likely to be theirs, while avoiding ones that could land the other side in a mess. This is the art of tactical fun rather than brute force guessing.
- Adapt your voice and pace: A live, enthusiastic Spymaster can turn a tense moment into a laugh-filled memory. A flat monotone “Ocean two” can be comedic in a tragic way. The best Spymasters blend clarity with a little theatrical spice.


### Spymaster Examples (Safe, Non-Spoily)
- If you notice a pirate, a ship, and a map on the board, you might say “Sea Adventure, 3” to connect the idea of exploration, danger, and treasure. The goal is to create a cluster of cards that match the clue while not accidentally pointing to the assassin or the opponent’s images.
- If you see a dog, a bone, and a crown, you might go with “King’s Best Friend, 3.” It’s a playful way to tie royal imagery and canine loyalty into a single line of thought.

## Replay Value and Longevity
Codenames Pictures has a lot of life in it for a couple of reasons:
- Randomized boards: With 25 image cards per game and a large pool of possible clue combinations, no two games feel identical. This is crucial for keeping a party game feeling fresh rather than rote.
- Flexible player count: The base game supports typical party sizes well; you can scale up or down without losing the core mechanic. If you want to stretch it for a bigger crowd, you can run multiple tables with shared clues and a tournament-like vibe, or simply mix in a couple of short “relay rounds.”
- Clue creativity as a limiter: The most interesting sessions tend to be the ones where players push the limits of what a single clue can connect. That means a healthy dose of humor, some wild misinterpretations, and a handful of “Please explain how that connects.”
- Replayability with variants: If you get bored, you can toss in alternative scoring rules, or switch to “no repeating clues” for a round to switch up the strategy. It’s not a gateway into forever, but it’s a solid ladder of laughs that can keep a group engaged for several game nights.


## Codenames Pictures vs. Other Variants
- Original Codenames: The classic word-based version demands a sharper vocabulary and a different kind of brain gymnastics. Some groups prefer the wordplay, others prefer the whimsy of images. Pictures bridges that gap for folks who want a lighter, more visual approach.
- Codenames Duet: The cooperative two-player variant is ideal for couples or close friends who want to work as a single team. Pictures can be adapted for two players as well, though you’ll miss the “team versus team” competitive spark.
- Other image-based party games: There are other gallery-style or image-matching games out there, but Codenames Pictures leverages the familiar SPIY Master dynamic and the tension of a race to finish, which is a powerful recipe for fun at many ages.

## Design Notes and Practical Tips for Your Next Game Night
- Rotate the Spymaster role: To keep things fair and fun, rotate who gets to be the Spymaster. The person who is best at tossing out surprisingly apt connections one round might be less handy the next, and that’s what makes it entertaining.
- Use the pictures to your advantage: Images that spark strong associations will often trigger bigger clue cascades. If you can tie three or four cards into a single clue, you’ll surprise everyone (including yourself) with your cognitive gymnast skills.
- Keep it light: Some players take these games a touch too seriously. Remind everyone that the goal is fun first, bragging rights second. If someone’s clue leads to chaos, you’ve probably achieved the opposite of a bad clue.
- Create family-friendly house rules: A “gag guess” or a silly bonus for guessing a particularly tough card can keep energy high without derailing the game’s structure.
