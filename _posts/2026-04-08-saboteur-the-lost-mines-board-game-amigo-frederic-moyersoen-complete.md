Saboteur: The Lost Mines is not your grandma’s mining simulator, unless your grandma is into sneaky dwarves, dramatic sabotages, and the occasional accidental avalanche of misplays. This 2020s-era reimagining of a classic card-game-turned-board-game hybrid from Amigo, designed by Frederic Moyersoen, proceeds with a brisk wink: imagine a dwarven treasure hunt where half the crew wants to mine gold, and the other half forgot to read the map and is pretty sure the map is a prank by goblins. And somehow, the game works. It works hard. It works so well that you forget you’re arguing with your friends about which path card to lay next, and you remember you’re having a fantastic time with people you love to loathe in a friendly, competitive way.

If you’re new here at Geeknite, we don’t do hot takes that disappear with the next patch note. We want systems that feel clever on week one and still spark joy on week 52. Saboteur: The Lost Mines aims to be that kind of evergreen: a compact box, a handful of mechanics, and a whole lot of social deduction that doesn’t require you to cosplay as a dragon to have fun. The result is a game that can feel like a chaotic sprint one moment and a carefully staged heist the next. It’s not perfect, but it is very, very entertaining—enough to justify the loud noises you’ll make when a well-timed play flips the entire table’s plan on its head.

What you get in the box reads like a love letter to both German-engineering and goblin engineering. The art is punchy, the components sturdy, and the rulebook—bless its laminated heart—attempts to forecast every possible meltdown at the table with a calm, slightly sardonic tone. We’ll break down whether this edition lands its dramatic heist, and where it leaves you emotionally if you’re the competitive type who secretly wants to become a mine-dwelling tyrant for a night.

H2: What is Saboteur: The Lost Mines, exactly?

Saboteur: The Lost Mines is a compact, downtempo-competitive board game built around a secret-role dynamic, where players are dwarves tasked with locating and extracting treasure from a labyrinthine mine. Some players are miners who want to reach the Lost Vault; others are saboteurs who would rather see the shaft crumble into a spectacularly cinematic failure. The original Saboteur concept—which is primarily a card game about building routes and thwarting others—gets a board-game expansion that reorganizes the loot, the stage directions, and the drama. The goal is simple to say, slightly more difficult to execute with grace: reach the treasure before the saboteurs make sure there is no treasure worth reaching.

The Lost Mines adds a modular board, resource tokens, and a handful of new action and path cards that create a sense of “choose-your-own-damned-path” with a chorus of parodies under the table. It’s not a betrayal-by-default game; it’s more like a cooperative rickroll: you’re compelled to work together, but the moment a single misplayed card reveals your true allegiance, it’s game-on. The mood stays playful—this is not a grimdark dungeon crawler; it’s a Heist-In-Grandma’s-Old-Subway-Tunnel, with a bit of glitter and a lot of cardboard.

H2: Components and build quality

The box feels like a sincere lunchbox for dwarves: sturdy, compact, and a little heavier than you expect. Inside, you’ll find a modular board that folds into a respectable map of twists and dead ends. The Lost Mines introduces new board tiles that can slot together to form different “routes” in every game, which is neat for replayability. The treasure chest token, the gold nuggets, and the mining lanterns all look stylish and carry a tactile satisfaction—the kind of heft you notice when you handle them and think, “Yes, I am the cunning one who controls the fate of this little mine.”

The card stock is decent enough to survive multiple plays without warping in heat or rain; the artwork is cartoonish yet elegant, with the dwarves looking determined, slightly annoyed, and (crucially) very expressive. The rulebook is compact, with a helpful quick-start section and a longer elaboration that attempts to cover edge cases without turning into a legal brief. If you’ve played other Amigo games, you’ll recognize the crisp layout and a sense that the components exist to support gameplay rather than to dazzle you with collector’s edition glory.

H2: How to set up the game (and not instantly forget your own name)

The setup is intentionally straightforward. Shuffle the modular board tiles to create a new mine layout each game. Place the treasure chest at a random reachable location, then distribute role cards face down to each player. In Saboteur: The Lost Mines, you’ll be dealing with several roles: miners who want to reach the treasure, saboteurs who want to foil the plan, and occasionally “wildcard” roles that introduce a special twist for a round or two. Each player receives a set of action cards, which include tools to fix a broken path, cards to redirect the shaft, and, of course, sneaky options to block an ally’s progress. The key setup idea is: you want to know enough to plan, but you don’t want to know everything. That’s where the joy begins.

H2: Core mechanics: how the mine actually gets mined

Saboteur: The Lost Mines is rooted in a few familiar ideas:
- Path-building: Players place tiles to build a route from the mine entrance toward the treasure. Each tile shows a segment of the tunnel that must align with adjacent tiles. Mistakes are costly, and there’s always a risk of creating dead ends due to your own poor memory and/or your neighbor’s cunning mind.
- Hidden roles: Your allies are not guaranteed. Some players secretly sabotage the route, blocking or re-routing with the help of action cards. The social tension is the engine here: you pretend to be nice to your neighbor while quietly counting how many “shoring” cards they’ve discarded to sabotage a later turn.
- Limited information: You don’t know who is who until a reveal or a critical moment. That uncertainty creates a delicious pressure that is the lifeblood of the experience.
- Endgame tension: A few rounds in, you’ll see a shift in strategy as everyone realizes that the path to treasure is fragile, and the person who seems the most trustworthy may be the one who triggers the final disaster.

The combination of these elements yields a game that plays quickly—typically 30 to 60 minutes for a standard 4- to 6-player session—and maintains momentum with enough clever decisions to keep the table engaged. The Lost Mines introduces some new twists, like a “resource ladder” mechanic that forces players to plan for sustainment: do you invest in longer routes now, or trade resources for a faster, riskier path? The answer depends on the table’s temperament and the kind of laughter you want to spark.

H2: Gameplay cadence and room for bluffing

In practice, Saboteur: The Lost Mines is a rhythm game of sorts. The first few turns feel like scouting; players explore the available tiles and talk in whispers about the best places to lay a path. By mid-game, you’re juggling two or three parallel considerations: is the route viable? Should I save my key action cards for defense or offense? Can I trust the only person I’ve never questioned, who is now suggesting a peculiar diagonal route that would be perfect if everyone else cooperates? And all the while, the saboteur pickpockets a crucial advantage by quietly removing an obstacle that will later bite someone else in the back—often not themselves, which is the delightful irony.

The game thrives on misdirection and social friction, but it doesn’t demand a cutthroat personality to enjoy. If you’re the type who enjoys a cheeky “you sly dog” moment when a neighbor’s plan backfires, you’ll find it easy to lean into Saboteur’s vibe. If you’re the type who dislikes social deduction in any form, you’ll want to set the table with a clear round-robin style and emphasize the cooperative component of a few routes that eventually culminate in a shared sense of victory or, at least, mutual embarrassment.

H2: Strategy notes: when to push and when to pretend you’re not pushing

- Early game: establish options, don’t commit fully to a single path. You want flexibility. In this phase, it pays to hold back a couple of your best action cards for late-stage drama. You’ll use them to either cement your own route or to foil a rival’s ambitious plan.
- Mid game: pivot with the information you collect. If you notice a few players consistently playing defensively, you can start to push for a more direct route, forcing others to adapt. This is also the moment to consider whether you want to reveal a role or keep it secret—revealing can be a double-edged sword; it may earn you a friend or a fatal rivalry.
- Late game: do what you can to ensure the treasure room isn’t sabotaged by a last-minute play. That means thinning your deck, discarding underpowered cards, and using your strongest moves at just the right moment. The timing is everything; you’ll learn to feel the weight of a decided turn as if holding a heavy, gleaming coin that could change everything.

H2: Thematic integration and art direction

The Lost Mines leans into a playful, tongue-in-cheek dwarven vibe. The dwarves look comically earnest, as if they’ve misplaced their beards in the rush to mine, and the set pieces—the lanterns, the crumbling timbers, the glint of gold on a misdrawn card—don’t shy away from campy charm. The humor lands most in the card flavor text and in the occasional card that acts as a snarky aside to the table. It’s not a grim fantasy epic; it’s a light, zippy, “we’re all in this subway tunnel together, now quit quarrelling” kind of mood that works surprisingly well for family game night or a late-night gaming session with friends who treat gaming nights like a gym for social skills.

H2: Replayability, expansions, and how it scales with your group

The modular board is the star here for replayability. Each game can feel distinct because the minor changes to route layout alter the tension dramatically. If you enjoy puzzling out a route, you’ll return again and again to see whether you can outsmart the room or whether your tablemate has a trick up their sleeve that you simply didn’t anticipate.

If you’re hungry for more, there are expansion options (both official and third-party) that add new roles, new action cards, and new board tiles. The base game is thick with potential; expansions feel like a season pass for a show you already adore, delivering fresh misunderstandings and new opportunities for glorious misdirection. The downside is that with more players comes more complexity and a longer setup; you’ll want a dedicated session of 60–90 minutes if you roll in with eight people or more. That’s not a knock against Saboteur: The Lost Mines—it’s merely the reality of energy distribution when you add more brains into a mine that might collapse at any moment.

H2: Comparisons: Saboteur card game vs. Saboteur: The Lost Mines board game

If you’re coming from the original Saboteur card game, you’ll recognize the DNA: secret roles, route-building, and a healthy dose of misdirection. The Lost Mines translates the essence of that DNA into a tactile, board-driven experience with a few new bones attached. The board adds spatial puzzles where you can be forced to consider diagonals, blockages, and the geometry of tunnels in a way the card game never could. The social dynamics remain intact: you still have to decide who to trust, how to bluff, and when to push for a critical victory.

But there’s a distinction in pacing. The card game is fast, in-the-weeds, and excellent for a high-energy, quick-fire session. The Lost Mines, with its board, can create a longer tail and a more theatrical arc if your group is in the mood for a heist with a cast of colorful dwarves. If you crave a snappy, social deduction experience with a strong push-your-late-game-luck factor, the card game might be your jam. If you want a more substantial, tactile, route-building experience with “one more turn” magnetism, the board game is your ticket.

H2: Pros and cons (quick take)

Pros:
- Strong social deduction flavor with clever route-building.
- Modular board increases replayability and keeps things fresh.
- Solid component quality; the cards and tiles feel durable enough for regular play.
- Accessible for mixed-ability groups; it has depth without requiring an augury-level strategy.
- Thematic charm and light-hearted humor that fits a casual gaming night.

Cons:
- Longer playtime with larger groups can test patience if you’re not in the mood for multi-turn scheming.
- Some players may find the hidden-role mechanic a little opaque at first; the quick-start helps, but you’ll want a second game to settle into the groove.
- If you’re a pure strategy gamer who dislikes social deduction, you may feel a pinch of friction between the game’s social layer and its puzzle-like core.

H2: Who should buy Saboteur: The Lost Mines?

- Groups who enjoy light-to-medium weight social deduction with a puzzle twist.
- Players who want a fresh take on route-building with a cooperative tilt and some misdirection.
- Families and friends who like themed, characterful games that don’t require long, heavy rulebooks to get started.
- Players who appreciate a strong table presence and a game that looks good on the shelf and in photos for social media bragging rights.

H2: Final thoughts and recommendations

Saboteur: The Lost Mines is a delightful package for the right crowd. It nails that chaotic, joyful vibe you crave when you want a night that feels cinematic but is actually a clever negotiation between humans with varying risk thresholds. If you go into it expecting a hardcore strategy contest, you’ll be a little surprised at the laughter-driven energy and the pace of play. If you go in expecting a light social deduction with a puzzle edge, you’ll walk away convinced you found a solid evergreen title for your game library.

The Lost Mines shines when you use it as a social catalyst rather than a sole source of strategic victory. It invites players to bluff, to cooperate, to gently sabotage, and to celebrate the tiny wins that come from clever board-tile placement and well-timed card plays. It’s a game that respects your time by keeping rounds concise, but it rewards careful planning in those precious moments when the “one more turn” impulse hits. It’s not a perfect staple for every group, but it is a near-constant crowd-pleaser for many, especially those who enjoy a good-natured rivalry with a side of dwarven sarcasm.

If you want one more reason to add this to your collection: it scales nicely with different group sizes, offers enough variety to stay interesting after a dozen plays, and has that satisfying “aha” moment when everything lines up and you realize you were looking at the wrong tunnel from the start. The Lost Mines doesn’t pretend to reinvent the wheel; it polishes it, adds a few new grooves, and gives it a more adventurous sound in the process.

H2: Closing notes on design and vibe

The designers at Amigo and Frederic Moyersoen have a knack for turning “secret plans and misdirection” into something approachable for a broad audience. Saboteur: The Lost Mines captures the spirit of a tense, heist-themed night and translates it into a play experience that is accessible, engaging, and polished. The art direction, the component quality, and the pacing all contribute to a product that you’ll likely reach for again when you want a game-night that feels a touch cinematic without requiring a PhD in tabletop math.

If you’re into this kind of game, you’ll find yourself revisiting the Lost Mines not just because it’s entertaining, but because it’s an excellent vehicle for storytelling and group dynamics. It’s easy to teach, quick to run, and hard to master in the best possible way: there are always a few new cunning routes to explore, and someone else will inevitably pull off a surprising twist that makes everyone laugh, groan, and declare a rematch.

H2: Final recommendation (TL;DR)

- If you want a fast-paced, social, and clever game with a moderate bit of bluffing and a lot of fun, buy Saboteur: The Lost Mines.
- If you prefer heavy strategy or solo play with rigid optimization, skip this one for now and try something with fewer social variables.
- If you like light-hearted dwarves, dramatic mine-capes, and a box that begs to be opened during a game night, go ahead and grab it. It’s the kind of game that builds memories around a table more effectively than most “serious” games can manage in one sitting.

Final note: the players-first design approach here means you’ll get more laughs per minute than you might expect, and you’ll find yourself replaying the same scenario just to chase that “aha” moment when a subtle misplay becomes a masterstroke.
