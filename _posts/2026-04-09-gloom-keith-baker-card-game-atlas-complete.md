## Introduction

Designed by Keith Baker and published by Atlas Games, this is the game that turns every family gathering into a bittersweet performance of misfortune. In Gloom, you don’t win by making your characters happy; you win by making them dead first, all while your rivals’ families suffer fabulously longer, and more spectacularly morose than yours. It is a card game about gloom, delivered with a sense of humor sharp enough to cut a candle. The mood is Victorian macabre meets black comedy club, and somehow it works better than it has any right to.

## What is Gloom?
Gloom is a multiplayer card game about turning your family’s lives into a narrative tapestry of wretched misfortune, while attempting to keep rival families’ fates as cheerful as possible. The central mechanic hinges on transparent, plastic gloom cards that you stack on your family members. These cards physically sit on top of a character card, and since they’re transparent, you can stack gloom on one character while also letting others peek through to see how things are stacking up elsewhere. The beauty of this is twofold: you can literally see the drama unfolding, and you can rearrange gloom with all the flair of a tabletop playwright.

The ultimate objective is to accumulate negative mood points on your own family and then reveal a hand-death card for the opposing side that ends their line. The catch is that you want to die last, ideally while your rivals are expiring in a stack of sorrow. The winning condition is a polite opposite of a traditional victory: you’re striving to have the happiest funeral ever while your opponents are buried beneath layers of gloom. It’s silly, macabre, and a little Shakespearean in its pettiness. The rules never take themselves too seriously, but the stakes—aka the score track—are serious enough to drive real strategy, especially when you start selecting which family members to keep alive before the final curtain falls.

If you’re curious about the designers and the publisher behind the mood, Keith Baker is the name to trust. He crafted a game that embraces storytelling and tactical card placement with a wink. Atlas Games published the original edition, and the brand has continued to nurture Gloom with a series of expansions and reissues that keep the mood fresh without losing the core mechanic that made the game a modern classic. The result is a product that feels both collectible and infinitely playable, a paradox that only well-designed card games can truly pull off.

## Components and Design: A Close Look
Gloom ships with a modest, well-thought-out lineup that feels consistently deliberate rather than flimsy or gimmicky. Here’s what you get in the base box:

- A character board per player featuring a family tree with multiple slots for gloom and a place for a death card. Each family member has a little portrait that becomes a living ledger of misfortune.
- A deck of opaque gain cards and a separate deck of gloom cards. The gloom cards are the star of the show: translucent plastic with black ink that looks wonderful on the table and against light, which means you can casually admire your own terrible planning while villainizing your friends’ characters.
- Death cards and condition cards that alter how gloom stacks, or how points are calculated as the game progresses. These cards add moral flexibility to the puzzle, because sometimes the sob story isn’t a simple arithmetic problem; it’s a narrative beat you want to hit.
- Score tokens and a simple score track so you can watch each family dip into the red as the round unfolds.
- A little rulebook that’s both compact and surprisingly readable, which is essential for a game that invites a lot of storytelling and improvisation.

The aesthetic is a deliberate homage to Victorian and early 20th-century gloom. The art is moody but not too grim for a casual night; the components are sturdy, and the card sleeves you might want to use practically beg to be purchased as a separate accessory because you will ask yourself: how many gloom cards can I fit on one family before the rules force a reset? The answer is more than you’d expect, and that is the whole point.

If you’re the kind of gamer who loves to line up components like you’re building a dungeon, you’ll appreciate how Gloom rewards thoughtful placement. Since gloom cards are transparent, you can deliberately create cascading effects: a gloom card on one family makes it harder to avoid calamity, which means you’ll want to balance the deck across players to maximize dramatic tension. The design rewards careful timing, not just reckless card throwing. And that’s a refreshing change of pace in a landscape full of take-that shenanigans and dice-rolling chaos.

## How to Play: Setup, Turn Structure, and Tactics
Gloom’s flow is simple in principle, but the storytelling layer is where the game shines. Here’s a concise walkthrough that will let you start playing within a few minutes, and still have plenty to think about for the rest of the night.

### Setup
- Each player picks a color and takes a family board, a starting line of two or three family members, and the corresponding cards for their family. You’ll populate a small set of gloom cards for each family member to begin with, though the exact number isn’t rigid—the game encourages you to morph your setup as you like.
- Shuffle the gloom deck and the non-gloom deck separately. Place the stacks within reach so everyone can feign gleeful malevolence as needed.
- Determine the starting player by a flip of a coin, a die roll, or whatever ritualistic method your table approves. The game typically plays 2–4 players, and with more players you get more dramatic misfortune to juggle.
- The basic rule is that you want to end with the most gloom on your own family but with daddy’s death card still not revealed until you’re ready to score the round. It’s a balancing act of storytelling and mild arithmetic.

### Turn Structure
Each turn, you have a few quick options to manipulate gloom on your characters, either by adding gloom cards from your hand or by playing special effect cards that move gloom between players. The structure goes something like this:
- Draw a gloom or action card (depending on the version you’re playing, you might draw from one or both decks). The ability to pick up a card that gives you a new way to narrate misfortune adds depth to decisions that would otherwise be rote.
- Add gloom to your own family or to other players’ families. The transparent cards stack visually, and you can see the “domino effect” you’re trying to set up as other players respond in kind.
- Play any action or condition cards that alter how gloom works, or that force opponents into dicey moral choices. This is the heart of the game’s charm: each turn, you’re both a shaper of fate and a stage director trying to coax the best possible tragedy from your fellow players.
- Pass the turn to the next player. The order matters because you want to ensure that your own gloom remains devastating while you disrupt others’ potential for a quick, cheerful scrape to the finish line.

### End of Round and Scoring
When a round ends, you reveal all death cards and tally gloom on each family. The scoring is straightforward, but the implications are clever: your aim is to have the most gloom on your own family while ensuring that your opponents have fewer gloom points when they die. The game uses a simple set of scoring tokens and a track, so the math stays approachable, even as your storytelling grows more elaborate.

### Variants and House Rules
Gloom invites player-driven storytelling. Some tables introduce a shared narrative objective, while others keep it strictly competitive in the conventional sense. You can add house rules like limiting the number of gloom cards per turn or introducing a timer to keep the pace brisk. The game’s flexible structure supports both a quiet, simmering gloom and a roaring melodrama, depending on your table’s energy.

### Strengths in Play
- The translucent gloom cards are an absolute delight to manipulate; seeing a family descend into gloom in real time gives you a tangible sense of impact.
- The emphasis on storytelling encourages players to improvise, making each session feel unique. It’s not just about who wins, but who crafts the most entertaining tragedy.
- The humor is well-tuned: dark but not depressing, silly but not silly to the point of failing to suspend disbelief.
- The design is compact. You can teach a new player in minutes, and you’ll be surprised by how much depth emerges from relatively simple rules.

### Common Pitfalls
- The humor can be a bit morbid for sensitive players. It’s worth setting expectations before the first draw if your table has a mixed comfort level with dark jokes.
- With more players, tables can drift into longer narrative interludes. If you want a tighter experience, limit the number of potential gloom cards a player can hold at once.
- The art style is not for everyone. If you prefer bright, family-friendly themes, Gloom’s aesthetic might feel a little too noir for your taste. Still, the stylized gloom tends to charm most players once the game gets rolling.

## Theme, Tone, and Accessibility
Gloom’s tone is the star here. It leans heavily into dark humor and affectionate satire of tragic storytelling. The paper-pulp vibe, the Victorian gloom, and the theatrical nature of the card reveals make it feel like a live-action RPG night where the dice are replaced by wit and judgment. The accessibility is strong: you don’t need an English degree to play, and you don’t need a PhD in math either. The learning curve is gentle, and the humor helps keep the pace lively so that players don’t get stuck in a rules quagmire.

If you enjoy comparing your own family’s misfortunes against those of fictional rival families, Gloom rewards both creative storytelling and cold-blooded tilting of the narrative. It’s a game that thrives on shared banter, playful scheming, and the occasional melodramatic gasp when a gloom card interacts with a death card in the most unexpected way. In a world of heavy euro mechanisms and crunchy combat, this one is a welcome palate cleanser for people who want something lighter on the head but heavier on the heart (in a good way).

## Who Should Play Gloom? Who Should Skip It?
- Best for groups of 3–5 players who enjoy a healthy dose of storytelling and a fondness for theater. Two players can still play, but the social dynamic and narrative tension are at their strongest with more participants.
- Great for nerdy personalities who like to craft mini narratives on the fly, and for players who appreciate a game that doubles as performance art.
- A superb choice for a game night where people want to laugh and groan in equal measure, all within the bounds of a light, approachable ruleset.

Who should avoid it? If you detest dark humor, you want a battle-rich strategy game with heavy randomization, or you’re seeking a long, heavy, euro-style experience, Gloom might not be your best match. Also, if you’re playing with a table that dislikes the concept of proud misfortune inflicted upon pseudo-family members, you’ll want to choose a different vibe to start the evening.

## Strategy and Tactics: Reading the Room and the Cards
The strategic core of Gloom is not about numerical dominance; it’s about narrative leverage. You leverage gloom to shape other players’ decisions while maintaining a sense of control over your own fate. Here are some practical tips for getting more drama out of your turns without tipping into chaos:
- Plan your long arc while you’re still assessing how the other players plan to respond. The translucent gloom cards create a visual map of who is likely to cry first, who will survive until the last curtain, and who will be blamed for the orchestra of misfortune.
- Don’t overcommit early. By keeping a few key gloom cards in reserve, you can react to other players’ plans with a flourish, while still leaving room for a dramatic reversal.
- Use death cards strategically. Revealing a death card at the right moment can swing the emotional pendulum in your favor and force opponents to scramble for last-minute changes to their plans.
- Embrace collaboration and competition in equal measure. You can maneuver your opponents into positions where their own gloom cards backfire on them—a satisfying payoff that never gets old.
- Remember that story beats are the currency of Gloom. The best players aren’t always the ones who cause the most gloom; they’re the ones who weave an entertaining, cohesive tragedy that everyone at the table will remember.

## Expansions and Longevity: Does Gloom Age Well?
Gloom has aged rather well in the sense that it remains a compact, approachable game that scales nicely with player count. There are expansions that add new families, locales, and gloom types, expanding the narrative possibilities without bloating the core experience. Unhappy Homes and the deluxe reissues refresh the look and feel while preserving the essential mechanics that make the base game click. The expansions do a proficient job of injecting new death cards, new scoring twists, and new ways to manipulate the gloom grid, which keeps the experience from going stale after a handful of sessions.

The beauty of the expansions is that they don’t demand a redesign of the entire engine. You can drop in a couple of new characters and watch the drama evolve in fresh ways. For a table that loves running campaigns or episodic sessions, this keeps the storytelling dynamic without turning the rules into a labyrinth. There’s a balance to strike here: more gloom gives you more narrative options, but it can also complicate how you teach and manage the table. If you’re starting out, the base game plus a single expansion is a smart path; if you’re a veteran, you’ll enjoy mixing and matching to keep the mood novel.

For completeness, here are a few real-world touchpoints you might consider when deciding on expansions: the typical session length doesn’t balloon; you can tailor gloom density to the group’s appetite for drama; and the art direction continues to be a consistent anchor that helps players stay immersed in the misfortune.
