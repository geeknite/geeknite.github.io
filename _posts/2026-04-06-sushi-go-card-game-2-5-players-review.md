## The quick snapshot

Sushi Go! Card Game is a drafting game with a simple mechanic that somehow manages to be both elegant and sneaky. Each round, players pick a card from their hand and pass the rest to the next player. The chosen card is kept, while the rest keep moving around the table in a loop that stops only when every card has found a home on a score sheet or behind a napkin. The goal is to assemble tiny sushi combos that score points in small, sharp paragraphs of delight. It is a game about observation, prediction, and what you think your friends will do before you know what you will do yourself. The short rounds keep the mood buoyant and help you avoid the dreaded table flop when someone rolls their eyes because another player has gone for the silly Maki roll for the third time this week.

## How to play in a word or two

This is a drafting game. Each round, you pick a card from your hand and pass the rest to your neighbor. You do this until everyone in the circle has picked a card. The tackle is simple: you want to assemble sets that maximize your points, but you also want to read your rivals a little to guess what they might be chasing. The scoring comes in tidy little packages for each card type introduced on the board. Tempura is worth a set; Sashimi requires a triple; Nigiri cards score depending on their type; Wasabi doubles the first Nigiri that you place under it; and Maki tokens are all about who has the most. The rest is history, but the gist is that the whole thing wraps up in roughly 15 minutes per play, which means you can run a full tournament during a rainy afternoon and still have time to order pizza in the middle of it all. A typical session can be two or three rounds, with the score tally feeling like a fast, goofy sprint rather than a marathon of calculation.

## The gameplay loop in practice

Every round, every player starts with a hand of five cards. You pick one card to keep and pass the rest to the left. This continues until all cards are played. After you have collected your five cards, you tally your score for that round and pass the stack to the next player and start again with a new hand. The tension comes from predicting what the person to your left will take on their turn, which cards they have already collected, and what gaps their table might reveal when you finally see a full layout of cards for scoring. The Wasabi card is a little spicy in that it multiplies the next Nigiri you place. Maki tokens are a race to see who has the most at the end of the round, and if you time your Tempura and Sashimi combos correctly, you might punch out some extra points that feel like a cheeky wink from the game itself.

What makes Sushi Go! feel clever is that it remains accessible without becoming trivial. The rules are easy to explain in under 60 seconds, but there is nuance in the decisions you make when you look at the five cards in front of you and try to predict what your neighbor will do next. The result is a loop of small, satisfying choices that can stack up into a surprisingly strategic experience if you lean into it, while still staying friendly to casual players who just want a fast, fun round and a story about how your team did not realize the wasabi would be multiplied by the very next nigiri they placed.

## Strategy notes you can actually use without a professor present

- In early rounds, keep an eye on the current board status and the likelihood that certain card types will run out soon. If a key card type is running low, you might be better off grabbing it rather than waiting for a perfect combo that may not arrive.
- Wasabi is a sneaky multiplier. If you receive a Wasabi in a round, plan the next Nigiri you place under it in a way that yields the maximum points without giving away your entire strategy to the table. This is where anticipation pays off more than brute force.
- Tempura and Sashimi combos require patience. If you chase one, you risk letting other players assemble a competing set that denies you the points you hoped for. The best move is to balance chase with opportunistic grabs that deny critical points to others.
- Maki tokens create a separate micro-objective that can swing a round even if your main card strategy is not perfect. If you can grab enough Maki in a round, you can win a chunk of the points and keep your total competitive even if your Sushi Roll collection is a little sleepy that day.
- Track what has already gone out of the table and what is likely to come around again. Short rounds reward confident players who can read patterns and adjust plans on the fly.
