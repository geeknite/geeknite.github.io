# 1995 Fleer Marvel Overpower Card Game Singles: M-Z You Pick

If you were a kid in the mid-90s, you probably owned a handful of trading card games that promised glory, domination, and the chance to prove you could finally draw a decent hand without shuffling like a caffeinated octopus. Among them, Fleer’s Marvel Overpower was the loudest, flashiest, and most unapologetically superhero-y of them all. It was a speed-run through the Marvel Universe, where heroes and villains collided not on a cinema screen but on a table crowded with character cards, energy tokens, and the occasional stale pizza slice that doubled as a peace treaty between alliances.

This post focuses on the “Singles M-Z You Pick” portion of the Overpower catalog—the part where you choose your favorites from Magneto to Zemo and argue with your friend about who has the most dramatic cape. If you’re reading this as a long-lost treasure hunter or as a nostalgic reader who once tried to convince a sibling that Mystique should be your team’s MVP, you’re in the right place. We’re going to break down the lineup, talk about why these letters were sometimes the best and weirdest part of the set, sprinkle in some goofy anecdotes, and end with a practical guide for tracking down M-Z singles without selling your kidney on eBay.[^1]

## The M-Z Roster: A Quick Primer
The M-Z segment of Overpower singles is where the game gets a little wilder, a lot brighter, and often more tongue-in-cheek. The characters span the breadth of Marvel’s rogues and rogues-adjacent heroes: from tireless moral compasses to characters whose backstories include a surprising amount of cosmic electricity and moody capes. What makes M-Z so fun to talk about is not just the standout cards but the wild, sometimes questionable balance that came with a 1990s card game economy—where power creep happened and no one admitted it, and foil variants existed to justify any strange purchase you felt at 2 a.m.

In this section, we’ll cover some representative picks from M-Z, highlight the vibe of each character on card, and offer a few pro-tips on building a fun, thematically coherent roster—whether you’re playing a casual 2-player game at a kitchen table or organizing a mini-collection with the same level of enthusiasm you reserve for a cosplay parade.

## M: Magneto, Mister Fantastic, Moon Knight, Morbius, Mole Man, Mystique (and More)
- Magneto: The aura of Magneto is an obvious starting point for M. He’s not just about brute force; he’s the archetype of a villain-turned-antihero archetype who can flip the board with the right power synergy. If your strategy leans into disruption and control, Magneto is your go-to. In the game, Magneto often shines when he can manipulate the environment (metal, in this case) and create lanes where ordinary heroes get stuck dealing with his magnetic shenanigans.
- Mister Fantastic: The ultimate Tinkerer-in-chief of the Marvel U. You want tactical placement, flexible abilities, and a hero who doesn’t panic when the board gets weird? Mister Fantastic is your friend. He can stretch the limits of “what counts as a move” and help your other cards stack up for big, dramatic plays.
- Moon Knight: The midnight avenger with a vibe problem that somehow translates into a surprisingly consistent card in Overpower. Moon Knight’s value lies in multi-edged utility—he can be a wall, a charger, or a punchline when the game gets too serious. Expect the occasional “moon-landing” pun in your game commentary.
- Morbius: The living vampire’s inclusion is a reminder that Overpower enjoyed leaning into the atmospheric corners of the Marvel cosmos. Morbius can offer niche interactions that punish over-aggressive playstyles; he also makes a great “should I trust this card?” moment when you’re handling a life-or-death decision about who attacks whom.
- Mole Man: If you want a quirky, terrain-taming pick, Mole Man is your character. He’s not the flashiest option, but he does well in decks that hinge on environmental manipulation and layering effects that feel cheekily rebellions against the “one-big-hit” meta that plagued many players in the era.
- Mystique: This is where the strategy gets spicy—morphing, misdirection, and a chameleon approach to combat. Mystique cards usually reward players who enjoy deception and synergy with a team built around misdirection and counterplay.

What do these picks have in common? They reward players who value flexibility and “reading the board” more than “picking the biggest puncher.” If you’re building a M-zone roster for casual play, mix gritty powerhouses with a few sneaky tacticians, and you’ll avoid the match-to-match fatigue that comes from every game turning into a slugfest.

## N: Namor, Nova, Nick Fury, Nightcrawler, (and Friends)
- Namor: The Sub-Mariner is all about being hard to pin down and capable on multiple vectors—on land and sea (in game terms, this translates to board control, mobility, and some surprising resilience). If your meta includes lots of ranged options, Namor can lurk on the edges and pick off targets when you least expect it.
- Nova: The cosmic-powered, star-born protector has a certain “orbit” feel to his power profile. Nova adds some space-age vibes to your roster—great for players who like to leverage energy-based mechanics and long-range pressure.
- Nick Fury: The strategic brains behind the operation. Fury cards typically team up well with a squad that likes to coordinate multi-card plays and leverage tactical benefits—think of him as the field general who steadies a chaotic front line.
- Nightcrawler: The teleporting acrobat brings flanking opportunities and surprising angles of attack. Nightcrawler cards shine when you want to beat a stubborn frontline with a quick strike and a retreat plan that would make a gymnast jealous.

Why include these in M-Z? Because Namor through Nightcrawler often balance raw power with positioning, giving players a satisfying palette of tactical options that reward patience and planning as much as raw might.

## O: Odin, Omega Red, Red Skull (and Other Big Guns)
- Odin: The Asgardian All-Father. Odin cards tend to escalate the epic scale of matches, introducing thunderous moments where you wonder if you should bow to Valhalla or simply slam the card on the table and declare victory.
- Omega Red: The ominous track-cycler of the roster, Omega Red provides a grim, kinetic energy to the game’s rhythm. Expect Enter-the-Dragon style moments where your opponent’s board witnesses a sudden, eerie threat that only the most careful planner can contend with.
- Red Skull: The classic villain with a cane and a villainous smirk. Red Skull cards can destabilize a plan with cunning tricks and set-up plays that punish reckless aggression.

The takeaway here is that O-characters tend to tilt the match toward “the story matters” rather than pure numerical dominance. They’re perfect for players who want dramatic moments, cinematic reversals, and a sense that the Marvel Universe itself is watching—like a judge with a cape and a rulebook.

## P: Punisher, Phoenix, Psylocke, Professor X (and the Psionics Parade)
- Punisher: The no-nonsense icon of aimed justice. In a game of cards, Punisher brings a ruthless efficiency—great for players who like clean, surgical plays and a little grim humor about vigilante ethics.
- Phoenix (Jean Grey): Power, responsibility, and a little cosmic chaos. Phoenix cards usually offer high-impact, game-changing plays that can swing the entire board in a single, blazing arc. Perfect for climactic moments.
- Psylocke: The telepath’s edge. Psylocke cards lean on mental powers and precision—control the battlefield with psyche-shocks and well-timed countermeasures.
- Professor X: The strategist’s dream—cornerstones of board control, ability to enable allied cards, and a knack for turning a temporary lull into a full-blown comeback.

Together, these Ps form a theme: mental prowess, strategic depth, and narrative weight. If you want your M-Z roster to feel like a compact X-Men arc, these picks deliver the vibe without needing a yearlong tabletop campaign to understand the rules.

## Q: Quicksilver, Quasar (and Quiet Precision)
- Quicksilver: The speedster’s edge—quick strikes, surprising angles, and a tempo that can push opponents into mistake territory. He’s great in decks that like to punch first and ask questions later.
- Quasar: Cosmic energy with a flair. A Quasar card can give you big hits when you need them most and pages of energy synergy to lean on in late-game stages.

In the M-Z spectrum, Qs are about speed, precision, and the thrill of pulling off a move you didn’t think would fit into a casual game at the kitchen table. They’re also a reminder that even a single Q card can turn a slower build into a fast, cinematic sprint.

## R: Rogue, Reed Richards, Rhino, Red Skull (Reprise)
- Rogue: The risk-reward character who can steal power from opponents and turn it to her advantage. Rogue embodies the thrill of “outplay” as a core mechanic—timing, patience, and a bit of swagger.
- Reed Richards (Mr. Fantastic): The scientist’s brain behind a sustainable, scalable build. Reed adds synergy and multi-card combos that feel like a well-orchestrated heist.
- Rhino: The brawler with the boulders-for-hands energy. Rhino’s presence is all about front-line pressure and a stubborn approach to defense that forces you to rethink your plan.

Rogue-to-Reed forms a bridge between street-level grit and blue-sky genius. They’re a nice anchor for decks that want both creativity and resilience.

## S: Spider-Man, Spider-Woman, Scarlet Witch, Storm, Silver Surfer, Star-Lord (and the Cosmic Web)
- Spider-Man: The everyman hero who somehow makes everything work with a mix of wit and web-slinging versatility. Spider-Man cards are a reminder that you can still save the day with a clever trick or two.
- Spider-Woman: A solid blend of mechanical reliability and occasional flair. She’s the middle ground—not too flashy, but reliable in the long game.
- Scarlet Witch: Reality-warping vibes for days. Her cards often enable creative combos and, in the right hands, can alter the board’s entire topology.
- Storm: Weather and power as a package. Storm cards reward players who love timing and rhythm—the classic “you don’t control the sky, you ride it.”
- Silver Surfer: Cosmic scale, existential stakes, and big energies. The Surfer’s presence elevates a deck to “epic showdown” territory and makes you feel like you’re piloting a board that arcs through galaxies.
- Star-Lord: If you’re into a roguish, adventuresome energy, Star-Lord brings a fun, risk-friendly dynamic. He’s the social captain of your squad—great for players who enjoy a bit of charisma on the battlefield.

The S cohort is where the game truly sings. It blends iconic heroes with cosmic grandeur and practical deck-building that doesn’t require a PhD in physics to understand. If you want a roster that reads like a Marvel movie poster, this is your lane.

## T: Thor, Thorista… No, Just Thor; Thing; Taskmaster
- Thor: The thunderous pillar of heroism. A Thor card is about dramatic impact—the kind that says, “We’re doing this right now, and the storm will remember.” If you want raw spectacle with some magical gadgetry, Thor is your pick.
- Thing: The unstoppable force with a tough exterior and a heart of gold. Thing’s presence anchors your front line and gives you a reliable, high-durability option that can withstand a punishing assault.
- Taskmaster: The mimic, strategist, and anti-counterpunch. Taskmaster rewards careful study of your opponent’s moves and turning those insights into counter-play that makes your opponent wish they played rock, paper, scissors instead.

T-characters deliver a mix of spectacle and reliability. They’re ideal if you want a roster that feels like a superhero encyclopedia come to life on a tabletop battlefield.

## U: Ultron, Ulterior Plans, Uranium… Just Ultron
- Ultron: The metal-manipulating, almost-eternal machine. Ultron cards typically excel at relentless pressure and consistent pressure from multiple angles. If your deck loves cycles and swarm-like effects, Ultron feels right at home.

U is a compact but mighty letter in Overpower; Ultron’s clones-and-shells vibe fits players who like to build up a boss fight over several turns rather than deliver a one-turn KO.

## V: Vision, Venom
- Vision: The synthezoid with a human vibe. Vision cards are about balance—soft power, strong defense, and unique synergy with others that makes your team feel almost like a well-tuned AI squad. Vision’s playstyle is often about utility and the quiet strength that keeps you breathing through the late game.
- Venom: The symbiotic, unpredictable force. Venom cards bring raw aggression and a capacity to surprise your opponent with the occasional burst that reminds you why you started playing a card game in the first place.

The V pair is all about a mix of control and raw aggression—a good pairing if you want a duel where you’re never quite sure which way the scale will tilt until the last turn.

## W: Wolverine, War Machine, Wonder Man, Winter Soldier
- Wolverine: The go-to “top-tier casual flex” option. Wolverine is the fan favorite who can grind through a fight and make big moments feel personal. He embodies the grit of a lot of 90s comics—the relentless, no-surrender attitude you want in a game around a kitchen table.
- War Machine: The machine-hero with jets and gadgets. War Machine brings a balanced package of firepower and tech-soaked utility and is especially satisfying when you want your roster to feel like a modern military-meets-sci-fi armory.
- Wonder Man: The swagger-meets-sunbeam hero who is often good at turning the late-game into a victory lap. Wonder Man cards can capture that “the crowd is cheering” moment you crave in a casual match.
- Winter Soldier: The tactical assassin of the piece. Winter Soldier offers strategic plays, digging for the perfect two-step path to victory, and that cool, cold, precise vibe that fits perfectly with a cunning, control-focused deck.

W-series cards are the backbone of many players’ “reliable but stylish” rosters. If you want your team to feel like a capstone on a long, entertaining arc, this is your neighborhood.

## X: Xavier and the X-Comm (X-Men Vibes)
- Professor X (Charles Xavier): He’s the quintessential field general for a hero team, crafting synergy, communication, and long-term planning. In Overpower terms, Professor X is about “make it work” and enabling other cards to shine.
- X-characters in Overpower are often the rare birds—whether it’s the omega-level vibes or the classic X-Men ensemble. If you’re hunting for a hero who changes the rhythm of a match, Xavier-adjacent options provide that cerebral punch that keeps the game interesting.

X may feel like a quiet letter, but it can anchor a roster with smart pairing and unexpected reach across the board.

## Y: Yellowjacket, Yellowjacket Redux, and Other Y-Notes
- Yellowjacket: The underdog with a sting. In a lineup, Yellowjacket can punch above his weight if your deck values misdirection and getting the most out of limited resources.
- Other Y-Notes: The 90s Marvel roster sometimes leaned into plausible-y, unusual picks starting with Y, often as a wink to collectors who chased niche variants. If you’re building a themed roster around “street-level heroes with occasional cosmic detours,” Y cards can slot in as flavorful supports.

Y is the wink in the alphabet: small but clever, and often crucial for a balanced 60-card rotation.

## Z: Zemo, Zola, and the Zany Side of the Universe
- Baron Zemo and Arnim Zola: The classic villain duo that includes a mix of cunning, technology, and the occasional trap card that forces you to re-evaluate your entire strategy. Zemo and Zola cards are a reminder that the greatest matches aren’t always about who hits hardest—they’re about who can survive the longest with the most dramatic flair.
- Other Z-nods: Depending on print runs, there are a few Z-characters that show up as cameo antagonists or rare board-shapers. If you like the idea of ending a match with a mind-bending twist, Z cards are where you want to look.

Z remains the punctuation mark on a roster that loves to end on a dramatic note. It’s the cherry on top of a cake that was already stacked with neon energy and dramatic thunderclaps.

## Mechanic Spotlight: How M-Z Singles Play the Game
If you’re new to Overpower, the core loop is relatively simple but richly satisfying: you build a team from your pool of character cards, you pay energy to activate their powers, and you resolve combat that combines attack strength, defense, and a dash of luck from card draws. The M-Z subset tends to showcase a few recurring themes:

- Versatility over raw power: Many M-Z picks reward flexible playstyles, encouraging you to adapt to your opponent rather than force a single plan.
- Thematic cohesion: A lot of players who lean into M-Z rosters enjoy cards that fit a certain vibe—magnetism, mystique, cosmic energy, and the occasional street-level grit. It’s a great way to tell a story with your deck.
- Strategic tempo: With less obvious “one-turn knockouts” than some higher-powered lines, these characters reward board control, timing, and clever sequencing more than brute force spamming.
- Risk and reward: The era’s design encouraged risk—mismatches in power can be offset by a clever trick, a misdirection moment, or a well-timed boost.

In practical terms, building M-Z teams is about picking a few anchor cards (say Magneto or Spider-Man) and surrounding them with tacticians, control specialists, and a couple of high-tempo curveballs (like Phoenix or Ultron). The emphasis is on variety and readability of your plan—players could tell a story as they played, and that storytelling energy is a big part of Overpower’s charm.

## Collectibility, Value, and The Hunt for M-Z Singles
If you’re reading this as a collector rather than a player, you’re probably thinking about the chase more than the game itself. Here are a few reality checks and tips to help you chase M-Z singles without turning your life into a scavenger hunt that ends in a dark corner of the internet:

- Rarity varies by print run: Early runs tended to be more generous with foil variants and misprints; later runs adjusted to market demand and production realities. Be mindful that some M-Z cards exist in limited quantities, while others were churned out with fewer surprises.
- Condition matters: The 1990s card stock isn’t as hardy as modern releases, and a lot of wear is visible on older frames. If you’re chasing a glossy, high-contrast look, you’ll want to seek lightly played or near-mint examples.
- Seek bundles and promos: The best way to build an M-Z collection is often through bundles that include multiple characters, or through promos that tie into magazines, conventions, or store events. Those promos hold a nostalgic premium and are fun to own as conversation starters.
- Grading is a ritual: If you’re serious about your collection, you’ll encounter the same questions as modern card games—grading, authentication, and a market that loves to debate whether a crease is a “character-defining battle scar” or a fatal flaw. Approach grading with a mix of humor and seriousness, and you’ll avoid buyer’s remorse.

If you’re ready to start your hunt, here are some practical avenues to explore. These aren’t affiliate plugs or “get-rich-quick” schemes; they’re real, human ways to enjoy chasing the cards you love.

- Cross-collection swaps: If you find yourself with duplicates of common M-Z cards, consider trading with someone who has their own rare wants. It’s a low-friction way to refresh your lineup and make a friend in the hobby.
