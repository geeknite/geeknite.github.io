Greetings, fellow geeks, shambling bags of plastic and cardboard, and anyone who thinks a well-painted miniature is a better life partner than a real one. Today on Geeknite, we dive into the CMON Zombicide open box featuring a very special guest: Edouard Guiton. ## The Box: What Slid Out of the Crate of Destiny

Opening a Zombicide box is a ritual. The cardboard sings, the plastic whispers, and your inner child squeaks, “Yes, more miniatures, please.” The new open box presents a glossy lid, a deck of rulebooks that probably contains the secret to turning every dinner into a post-apocalyptic scavenger hunt, and an army of miniatures that look like they stepped straight out of a 3D printer’s daydream.

- The minis arrive with a sparkle that makes you reconsider your entire life choices—likely including that gym membership you bought for reasons way back when.
- The plastic is sturdy, with a satisfying click when you snap them into their bases. It’s the tabletop equivalent of finding a seat on a crowded subway: relief, followed by a sudden fear that you might be sitting on a small army of undead.
- Tokens and boards feel premium enough to justify the price tag, yet the practical part of your brain whispers, “We’ll see if the coordi-nate tokens actually survive your coffee spills.” Spoiler: they probably will not, but the rage will look nice on a shelf.

Aesthetically, Zombicide’s new edition shines. The color palette is punchy without being cartoonish, and the sculpts have that “you could paint this while listening to a podcast about quantum computing” vibe. Edouard’s presence, even as a guest, adds a layer of genuine industry insight—the kind that makes you realize you’ve been unboxing blindfolded, while he’s been unboxing with a plan and a bin labeled “maneuver tokens.”

## Meet the Special Guest: Edouard Guiton in the House

Edouard Guiton, for those who don’t keep a running list of board-game luminaries in their brain’s contact list, is the designer behind some of CMON’s most beloved tabletop boons. His unboxing etiquette is a sport: he manages to be both precise and delightfully chaotic at the same time. Watching him dig into a box is like watching a barista craft latte art on a rocket: you’re impressed, slightly confused, and craving a little more caffeine.

In this session, Edouard brings a few unbox-specific tips:
- He sizes up the board first, because weight distribution matters on a table, especially if you’re playing in a coffee shop that doubles as a generator of anxiety about your posture.
- He checks miniature tolerances as a form of art critique; if a zombie fig has a seam you can shave, he does not pretend otherwise, which is both noble and terrifying for the casual hobbyist who thought “battle-ready” meant “superglue ready.”
- He shares a few quick design anecdotes about how a single square on the grid can influence an entire turn sequence. Turns out, the square you never thought about is the square you’ll be cursing for the next hour.

If you’re hoping for a backstage confession about the exact color recipes used on the “survivor” sculpts, you’ll walk away with a few glimpses of process and a stronger urge to rearrange your painting station into something that resembles a spaceship’s command module.

## Components: Quality, Quantity, and Quiet Whispers of Plastic

Zombicide is known for its abundance, and this open box doesn’t disappoint. The components feel like CMON’s answer to “free frosting on every cupcake”—more frosting than you know what to do with, and somehow you still want more. The minis are sharp, the sculpts are detailed, and the bases give you that satisfying click when you slot them into the standing pose.

- Miniatures: Heavy on the walkers and the survivors who look like they’ve seen their fair share of late-night board game sessions. The sculpts strike a balance between heroic posture and “I’ve definitely panicked and dropped this somewhere in the last round.” Edouard notes the importance of dynamic poses for visibility at table height, which is the kind of designer insight that makes you re-check your own painting technique and then hide in the corner with a cup of tea.
- Tokens and cards: The card stock feels robust, the fonts are legible, and the color-coding helps your eyes do the math after a long day of Zoom calls. The tokens stack neatly, which is a small victory when your cat decides that cardboard is a perfectly acceptable toy.
- Board and tiles: The layout is logical, with a few clever flow ideas you’ll only appreciate after your third playthrough. The grid squares are generous enough to avoid the “one inch of freedom” problem but tight enough to ensure tension when you’ve cornered yourself into a corner you didn’t realize existed.

One note here: if you’re a color-therapy believer, this set’s palette provides a bit of emotional comfort amid the chaos. It’s not pastel vibes, but it’s not neon apocalyptic either; it lands in a sweet spot that’s pleasing to the eye and not headache-inducing after a 90-minute session. Edouard jokes that the best design choice is choosing the right color for the “dead” look—too gray and you’re lifeless; too blue and you’re feeling adventurous. It’s a careful balance, and the box shows the brush of a seasoned designer at work.

## Setup and Learning Curve: Quick Start or Quick Frustration?

Unboxing is just the warm-up; setup is where the real party begins. Zombicide has a reputation for rewarding players who actually read the rulebook, which is a sentiment I could quote on a poster and hang above my painting table. The new open box edition doesn’t reinvent the wheel here; it refines it. The rulebook is organized, with an index that doesn’t require a decoder ring, and the quickstart guide is genuinely helpful for first-timers.

- Quick start: You can get a playable setup in under 15 minutes if you ignore the part where you pretend you’re not going to need a separate box for the “special guest” miniatures. The first game might not be a perfect victory, but it will be a glorious, chaotic learning curve where you discover you can actually remember the turn order without writing it on sticky notes.
- Learning curve: For seasoned Zombicide players, there are new modifiers and mission types to explore. For newcomers, there’s a friendly onboarding flow that introduces core concepts (bitten tokens, survivor concealment, zombie spawns) without turning you into a spreadsheet-wielding monster. The balance here feels like a well-curated training montage rather than a boot camp—productive, but not traumatizing.

Edouard’s presence adds a practical flavor to the rules explanation: he points out where players tend to stumble and how to phrase a rule explanation without sounding like you’ve swallowed a lexicon of bureaucratic jargon. If you’re the “rule lawyer at the table,” this is a precious gift: you can still win, but you won’t need to ruthlessly correct every misread on day one.

## The Open-Box Experience: First Turns and First Triumphs

Now, the moment you’ve been waiting for: the first actual turns in the game. The initial turns are a joy of pivoting strategies and muttered curses as the first wave of walkers appears in increasingly inconvenient places. The open box experience—unboxed, organized, and ready for a session—feels like stepping into a playset that invites you to improvise with purpose.

- Turn tempo: The game flows well, with a rhythm that alternates between “plan your move” and “oops, forgot about that special zombie ability.” The tension comes not from randomization alone but from the combative interplay of your planned moves against the inevitable curveball the zombies throw at you. Spoiler: the curveball usually involves you realizing you placed survivors in a room with a stray zombie patrolling the exit like a tiny, error-prone gatekeeper.
- Cooperative vibe: It remains a solid co-op experience, requiring a bit of coordination without devolving into a strict “sit down and fill out a spreadsheet” mode. The players who enjoy synergy-heavy play will find the new mission structures comfortable and engaging, and there’s enough variability to keep replay value high without becoming a pure “roll-and-move” slog.

Edouard’s input on play balance is a treat here. He notes the design intent behind certain mission objectives and how small tweaks to spawn points or objective cards can dramatically alter the pacing. If you’ve ever wondered how to wring a few extra hours of meaningful tension from a game session, this part is your new favorite guilt-free cheat code.

## Rulebook, Reference Cards, and The Real-Life Help You Need

The rulebook of this edition isn’t a doorstop; it’s a cheat code. It includes a well-structured layout, quick reference cards, and a “how to read the board” section that actually helps you avoid that awkward moment where everyone suddenly forgets what color token represents which zombie type.

- Reference cards: These stay close to the action, letting you verify rules without flipping to the back page in the middle of a tense moment. They’re a smart addition that reduces table chatter and increases game speed.
- Index and glossary: The clarity here helps newcomers avoid the classic “it says X, but we must do Y” rabbit hole. If you’ve ever yelled at a translate-y rule with five hyphenated clauses, you’ll appreciate the no-nonsense approach.
