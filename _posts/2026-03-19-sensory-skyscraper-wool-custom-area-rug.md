## Size Options: Custom or 8x10

### The 8x10 Standard: Why It’s a Classic

The 8x10 option is the standard, go-to choice for many spaces. ### Custom Sizing: Because Your Room Loves Uniqueness

The big selling point here is customization. The rug can be ordered in a non-standard size, which is perfect for oddly shaped rooms, large open kinesthetic play zones (aka living rooms that double as mini gyms), or if you simply want a rug that will make your interior designer say, Huh, that’s bold. Custom sizing is ideal when you’re chasing a precise alignment with furniture clusters or you’re creating a dedicated gaming hutch where every fiber under the chair has to behave. The caveat: custom sizes may come with longer lead times and a higher price tag, since you’re not buying a mass-produced cookie-cutter sesame street. Or, in rug terms, you’re not buying a cookie. You’re buying a very expensive, wooly bespoke pastry.

### Practical Sizing Tips

- Measure your space twice, tell your cat once. If your space has an unusually shaped living room or an architectural feature you need to navigate, consider a rug that’s slightly larger than the main furniture edge and let the rest of the room define itself. A rug that’s too small has a sad existential crisis about its purpose; one that’s too big can make your furniture float in a sea of carpet. Aim for about 1 to 1.5 feet of exposed floor around the edges for a balanced look.
- If you’re using under a coffee table, ensure there’s at least 2 feet of rug beyond the table’s legs on all sides. This gives you space to pull chairs out, scoot in, and avoid the dreaded chair leg snag on an edge warp.
- For open-plan spaces, use multiple area rugs with a shared color story to create defined zones without sacrificing flow.

Wool is known for its natural insulating properties. In a chilly apartment, stepping onto this rug can feel like a warm hug from a benevolent woolen deity. Wool can be a mixed bag for allergy-sensitive folks. If you’re prone to lanolin sensitivity or wool allergies, you’ll want to consider a different fiber. If you’re not allergic but sensitive to dust, you’ll appreciate wool’s natural tendency to shed less dust than some synthetic fibers over time, though a good vacuum routine remains essential. We recommend regular but gentle cleaning to preserve the texture and fiber integrity.


## Maintenance and Durability

### Cleaning Protocols

- Vacuum regularly with a beater bar on low power to avoid fiber fraying. For delicate areas or high-traffic zones, use a suction-only vacuum to minimize wear. A rug under a coffee table might need less aggressive cleaning than a rug under a kid’s play zone, but you still want to keep the weave looking crisp.
- Spills should be blotted immediately with a clean cloth. Avoid rubbing, which can push liquid deeper into the fibers. For stubborn stains, a mild wool-safe cleaner is recommended. Always test on a small, inconspicuous area first.
- Deep cleaning should be considered only if you’re confident in wool care, or you’re willing to pay for professional cleaning. In most households, a yearly professional cleaning is enough to refresh the scent and vitality of wool without turning it into a damp sponge experiment.

### Durability Notes

Wool is inherently durable, with a natural resistance to crushing from foot traffic. The rug’s weave contributes to this durability, and the edges are typically reinforced to prevent unraveling. In our test environment, a few weeks of heavy lounging, coffee spills, and a feline wrestling match did not result in catastrophic fiber loss or dramatic pilling. If you’re looking for a rug that will survive a climate of chaotic life events (children, pets, and late-night pizza runs), the Sensory Skyscraper passes many practical tests with flying colors.
