## What’s in the Starter Set
The Starter Set is marketed as a “launchpad” for new players while delivering enough content for a few nights of play with friends who know your face but not your legend. Here’s a practical breakdown of what you’ll actually find when you crack open the box:

### The core books: quick-start rules and the adventure
- A streamlined rulebook that focuses on the mechanics most players need to begin: how to make your character, how to resolve actions with a focus on narrative outcomes, and how to weave myth and reality into scenes without getting tangled in a spreadsheet of numbers. It’s not a full-core rulebook, but it’s enough to get you rolling and then hook you into the longer rules when you’re ready to dive deeper.
- A pre-written adventure designed to showcase both investigative play and mythic escalation. It starts with a puzzle in a city that loves to pretend it’s ordinary, and then gradually peels back the skin to reveal the legends underneath. The pacing is calibrated to give new players a taste of the setting without requiring you to map out the entire city on day one.

### Character sheets and mythic identity cards
- A handful of pre-rolled templates: your Mythic Identity, your Everyday Identity, and a bundle of Tags that describe what you can do, what you cannot do, and how you’re likely to trip over your own cape when you blink. The idea is simple: you’re both the hero and the person who forgot to take out the trash—simultaneously.
- Visual cues and prompts for players to quickly define why their Legend matters and how their Real World ties into the scene you’re about to play. It’s a surprisingly effective mental switch—you can pivot from a coffee shop debate about a city ordinance to a rooftop fight with a mythic creature in a single breath, which is exactly the kind of tonal swing City of Mist excels at.

### Tokens, dice, and the right vibe
- The Starter Set includes a modest set of tokens and dice that are enough to run one or two tables. The tactile feel matters here; rolling a handful of dice to illustrate a “tag” being used is a small but effective cue that says, “This isn’t a math problem; it’s a story moment.”
- A GM screen and quick-reference sheets that keep the game’s rhythm snappy. These aren’t just for keeps—the visual reminders help reduce “rule drain” at the table when you’re introducing new players to the rhythms of myth and reality colliding.

### Artwork and components
- The art direction favors moody neon, rain-slick streets, and close-ups of character faces that tell you more about their legend than any monologue could. It’s a vibe-driven aesthetic that leans into style rather than ultra-high fidelity realism. If you’re the type who keeps a poster of a noir-lit cityscape above your gaming desk, you’ll feel right at home.
- Jekyll-and-Hyde style iconography is embedded into the components: the two identities, the two worlds, and the tension between them. It’s a design choice that pays off in scenes where your character tries to act human when their inner myth wants to cameo in every scene you’re in.

## How the Starter Set handles the setting and tone
City of Mist operates on a strong “myth held in the city” premise. In practice, that means the city is routinely doing two things at once: pretending to be normal and refusing to stay quiet about its legends. The Starter Set leans into that duality with scenes that feel like a crime-noir story and a mythic fable wearing a trench coat. The result is play that can be funny, tense, and surprisingly moving all within the span of a single scene.

### The mythic identity as a narrative tool
The core idea is that every character has a myth to live up to, but the scorecard of who they are in the real world is equally important. This dual identity is not just world-building fluff; it’s the engine that powers scenes. When you lean into your myth, you push for moments that feel larger-than-life. When you lean into your real-world persona, you ground the scene, which makes the mythic beats all the more impactful when they surface.

This tension is what elevates City of Mist above other “superhero in the city” games. It’s not about whether you can bend reality with a single action; it’s about whether your legend can survive the wear and tear of daily life and still matter to the people around you.

### Themes you’ll likely explore
- Identity vs. expectations: How do you reconcile who you are with who you’re supposed to be?
- Power in plain sight: The city has hidden legends lurking beneath ordinary routines—what happens when they intersect with your job, your family, or your favorite barista?
- Consequences and repairing the myth: If your choices fracture the city’s myths, how do you mend them without erasing everybody’s stories in the process?

## Mechanics: a quick tour for newcomers
The Starter Set isn’t here to bake you a complex pastry of rules. It’s here to get you through the first bites without getting your teeth stuck on a pastry bag of exceptions. Here’s what you need to know if you’re new to the system:

### Tags, dice, and narrative outcomes
- Tags are the primary way you define what you can do in a scene. They represent your capabilities, your flaws, and your moments of luck. When a character attempts a task, you look at the relevant tags and decide how many you want to apply to the action. The more tags you use, the more likely the action will trend toward success—but also the more likely you’ll invite trouble or reveal more about your legend.
- The dice mechanic favors narrative momentum. It’s designed so a single successful moment can lead into a narrative beat that feels cinematic rather than squarely “won.” This is where flashbacks and truth-bombs become your friends rather than roadblocks.
- Consequences matter. Outcomes aren’t simply “success” or “failure.” The system emphasizes how outcomes ripple through the city and into your legend. It’s that ripple effect that makes City of Mist feel like a living, breathing urban fable rather than a rigid board game.

### Character creation: quick-start, deepening later
Creating your character in the Starter Set is a gateway. You pick a Mythic Identity (your legend), an Everyday Identity (your real-world persona), and a handful of Tags that connect the two. You’ll probably spend more time chatting about your legend’s backstory than rolling dice on the first night, which is exactly what you want. The aim is to spark collaboration: two players discover their legends are entangled; a third player tries to piece together a clue from a city map; and the GM threads it all into a scene that feels inevitable in the best way possible.

## Practical tips for running your first session
If you’re GMing your first session, here are a few practical tricks to keep things moving without losing the vibe:

- Start with a “human moment” before unleashing a mythic beat. Give players a scene in which their Everyday Identities interact in ordinary ways. That makes the sudden shift into legend land land with impact.
- Use the city as a character. The setting should feel alive and opinionated. A street vendor might offer a cryptic clue; a district manager could embody a local myth in a mundane policy fight. The more you treat the city as a living co-conspirator, the more players invest in the story.
- Encourage player collaboration. The best scenes tend to bloom when players notice their legends are intersecting or opposing. Don’t be afraid to let a scene devolve into a flashback-heavy sequence; flashbacks can reveal critical truths without derailing present-mased tension.
- Keep the pace human. If a scene stalls, pivot to a smaller moment—maybe a chat in a back room or a quiet reveal in a hallway. City of Mist isn’t just about big reveals; it’s about the quiet choices that shape legends.
- Use quick prompts for players. The Starter Set includes prompts that help you describe the city and your legend in a few lines. If you’re drawing a blank, read the prompts aloud and let players riff. The best ideas often come from group improvisation rather than a single narrator hogging the mic.

## How it compares to the core book and other kits
The Starter Set is deliberately slim but faithful. It provides enough material to play a few sessions without drowning you in rules that you’ll promptly forget. If you’re a player who wants to see the system’s bones and you want to upgrade later, you’ll likely want to pick up the core rulebook. The core book expands options for Legends, deeper mechanics for tag interplay, and a wider array of tools for running longer campaigns.

For folks deciding between a Starter Set and a different indie option, consider your group’s appetite for story focus versus mechanical complexity. City of Mist is built for collaboration and drama; it expects players to contribute to the narrative as much as the GM does. If that’s your jam, you’re already halfway to a great night.


## Who should buy the Starter Set?
- Newcomers to City of Mist who want a guided, approachable entry point without needing a full-rulebomb on day one.
- Groups that love a strong mood and roleplay focus but aren’t looking for heavy tactical crunch.
- Players who enjoy “two identities” storytelling—the thrill of balancing a legend with a grounded real-life persona and watching how they influence each other.
- GMs who want a ready-to-run packaged adventure to test-drive the tone with friends new to indie RPGs.

On the flip side, if you’re looking for a game that expects you to master a sprawling rule set before you sit at the table, the Starter Set might feel a little too much of a friendly deterrent. In that case, you might prefer starting with a more traditional fantasy or sci-fi system and then dipping into City of Mist as a palate cleanser later on.


## Potential drawbacks and things to watch for
No game is perfect, and the Starter Set reflects a design choice: focus the experience on narrative flow rather than table-accumulated crunch. Some potential drawbacks to be aware of:

- Rule-lite can be frustrating for players who crave a deeply explicit mechanical framework to govern every decision. If that’s you, plan a long pre-session where you set expectations about the level of crunch you want.
- The mood can be hard to sustain for players who don’t vibe with noir aesthetics or mythic tropes. The Starter Set excels at a certain tone; if your group leans more toward bright, comedic fantasy or grimdark realism, you may want to adjust the emphasis accordingly.
- The two-identity model is conceptually amazing but can feel abstract at first. It might take one or two sessions before players feel completely comfortable weaving myth and reality together in a satisfying way. Be ready to offer prompts and gentle coaching.
