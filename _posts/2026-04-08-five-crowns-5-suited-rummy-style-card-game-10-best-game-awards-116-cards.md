## What is Five Crowns, and why should you care?

Five Crowns is a light-to-midweight card game designed for 1–7 players, though your mileage may vary depending on how aggressively your family negotiates the rules at the table. The core concept is delightfully simple: use a meld system to lay down sets and runs across five suits, in as many rounds as there are cards in a given hand. There are 11 rounds in total, and the number of cards you are dealt grows as the rounds progress. The deck is a behemoth at 116 cards, because five suits demand more cards and slightly more chaos.

If you’re reading this and your brain just rang a small alarm bell about “It sounds like Rummikub, but with cards,” you’re not wrong. Five Crowns scratches the same itch as Rummy-style games—clear goals, a little luck, and enough decision points to make you feel like you didn’t waste your Friday night learning a new language—but with the extra spice of five suits and a handful of jokers acting as wilds. The result is a game that’s easy to teach, quick to play, and oddly satisfying when you set down a flawless meld that makes your table neighbors nod, concede, and mutter, “Okay, that was a nice run.”

## Setup and rules, without the snooty pretensions

### Setup
- Decide on the player count. Five Crowns scales reasonably well from 1 to 7 players, though light rules abuse can occur in the extremes (two players will run the game faster than a crowded table).
- Shuffle the 116-card five-suit deck. In some editions, jokers are included as wilds; in others, they’re omitted. If your copy has jokers, keep them handy; they’ll earn their keep.
- Deal the appropriate number of cards for the round. There are 11 rounds; the number of cards per hand increases with the rounds, typically starting around three and climbing toward thirteen cards for the later rounds. The precise math isn’t the point; the point is: more cards, more opportunities to craft glorious melds, more chances to feel like a tiny genius.
- The rest of the deck becomes a draw pile, with the top card flipped to begin the discard pile. Play proceeds clockwise.

### Objective and melds
The core mechanic is melding. A meld is either:
- A run: consecutive cards of the same suit (for example, Red 7-8-9 in a single suit).
- A set: multiple cards of the same rank across different suits (for example, three Queens across three suits).
- Jokers (if you’re playing with them) can represent any card, turning a tricky hand into a possible win at a moment’s notice.

Your goal each round is to “go down” or lay down your melds to meet the round’s requirements. Once you lay down, you may increase or adjust your melds via subsequent turns, but your hand will be scored at the end of the round with values for cards left unmelded. The title of the game—Five Crowns—becomes humorously literal as the table grins at your five-suited attempts to create harmony out of chaos.

### Rounds and flow
Rounds progress with increasing card counts, and with a few policy quirks that keep things lively. You’ll draw a card, decide whether to keep it or discard it, then lay down melds when you can, and then discard to end your turn. The jokers, if in play, can be used to fill gaps in your melds and then replaced by the corresponding actual card as needed. The round ends when a player goes out, and then everyone tallies their unmelded cards. The round’s penalties accumulate toward the final score; the player with the lowest total at the end of 11 rounds is the winner. If it matters to you, the order of play can be used strategically to influence which melds are easier to complete first, though luck still plays a pretty significant role.

### Scoring (the safe, non-dramatic version)
In most editions, each unmelded card carries a value that contributes to your round total. The exact values for face cards, aces, jokers, and number cards can vary slightly by edition, but the spirit is consistent: you want to minimize lost points. The jokers, if used, typically carry a higher value, making a clever play with wilds potentially devastating to your opponents’ plans. The endgame rule of thumb is simple: you want to keep your hand’s point value as low as possible, and you want to do it for 11 rounds while your friends slowly realize you are not as innocent as you look.

### Variants worth considering
- Joker-enabled variant: If your copy has jokers, agree on their point values and how they interact with runs and sets before you start. This can swing the moral of a round from “I’m almost there” to “How did that joker just win the round for you?” in a matter of seconds.
- Speed variant: For a fast-paced game night, you can shorten the number of rounds or reduce the number of cards per round to cut down on setup and length. Great for younger players or when you’re trying to conquer a busy schedule.
- Solo practice: If you’re learning the ropes, try a two-handed solo practice mode against the clock and a memory-tricky AI-like scoring sheet. It’s a silly idea, but it can be surprisingly satisfying when you pencil your way through a difficult meld sequence.

## Strategy notes: play smarter, not harder
- Early rounds reward modest, flexible melds. Don’t chase a perfect hand too soon; instead, look for opportunities to set up future rounds by leaving a few flexible cards in hand.
- Use jokers strategically. If you have a joker, think about what it can anchor later runs or sets, then plan to “lock it in” when the window of opportunity is about to close.
- Pay attention to discarded cards. If a specific run or set keeps making appearances in your opponents’ hands, consider whether it’s worth blocking that path or whether you should pivot to something else entirely.
- Manage your endgame. As the rounds approach the thrilling (and terrifying) tail end, you’ll want to minimize danger by keeping a lean hand and avoiding wasteful deadweight melds.
- Adapt to player count. With more players, rounds tend to feel more dynamic and chaotic. In smaller groups, careful planning and bluff-free information sharing can give you an edge.
