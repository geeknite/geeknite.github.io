Ports and connectivity: the heart of a dock

Let’s get to the meat and potatoes: what’s actually on this thing and what you can plug into it. The HP Thunderbolt Dock 120W G4 leans heavily on USB-C Thunderbolt for everything it does. The exact port configuration can vary by revision, but typical specs include:
- 2 x DisplayPort 1.4 outputs (for multiple high-res displays)
- 1 x HDMI (for a third display or a general-purpose HDMI connection)
- 2 x USB-C (often USB-C 3.2 Gen 2 or 3.1; one might support power delivery)
- 4 x USB-A (USB 3.0/3.2 Gen 1 or Gen 2 depending on the batch)
- 1 x Gigabit Ethernet
- 1 x 3.5mm audio jack
- 1 x 4K display chain or alternate mode with DP/HDMI combos
- 120W Power Delivery (PD) pass-through to charge the host laptop or other devices

In practical terms, the dock handles daily workloads with ease: a 4K external monitor (or two 4K monitors if your GPU and laptop’s own video lanes cooperate), a keyboard, a mouse, and a handful of USB peripherals without breaking a sweat. If you’re juggling video conferences, streaming, and a few spreadsheets, this dock won’t drop a beat. The DP 1.4 outputs can deliver 4K60 on capable laptops; the HDMI port adds flexibility, particularly when you’re connecting to legacy displays or a big-screen living room setup as a secondary display. The inclusion of Ethernet is a nocturnal ally for stability: Wi-Fi is great, but nothing says “professional environment” like a reliable hard-wired connection for video calls and large file transfers.

Power delivery and performance

120W PD sounds generous, but it’s not a magical battery charger that can rescue a dead gaming laptop mid-tunage. The dock is intended to power and charge the host notebook while providing peripherals. If your notebook requires more than 100W to run at full tilt, you’ll want to check your laptop’s power draw. Most business laptops cap at around 65-100W, which means the 120W PD should have enough headroom to charge the laptop while running a display and a keyboard. However, if you’re running a desktop-grade workstation or a high-end gaming laptop that routinely hunts for 180W, the power delivery would mostly maintain or slowly decrement the battery while under heavy graphics load. In short: 120W is plenty for most everyday productivity, but if you’re that rare animal who maxes out both CPU and GPU on laptops for hours, manage expectations and consider your device’s power envelope.

It behaves politely, not like a diva demanding red M&Ms and a private elevator.

Software, drivers, and setup

HP’s dock is mostly plug-and-play. You connect the dock to a Thunderbolt 4-enabled laptop or PC, attach the power brick, and the host should recognize it as a docking device with hot-plug support. There isn’t much to install beyond standard USB drivers and possibly a quick firmware update if HP provides one for your batch. We found the experience to be straightforward, with a minimum of fiddling. The important thing is to ensure your host machine has TB4 support and that you’re using compliant cables—preferably the ones that come in the box or proven third-party options with the right power rating. Cables that sag beneath the desk like lazy snake-boys are half the problem; the other half is you forgetting to connect the dock to the power brick. Yes, that happened in a lab somewhere, and no, we didn’t judge the investigator who did it.

Compatibility: notebook vs desktop PC

The “notebook and desktop PC” tagline isn’t just a marketing flourish. The 120W G4 is designed to be flexible, which means you can use it with a notebook that supports TB4 for desk-mode efficiency, and with a desktop PC (if you happen to have a TB4-enabled workstation or a TB4-equipped motherboard with a Thunderbolt header). For notebooks, you get the usual benefits: a single-cable monitor setup, external drives, and a stable charging source that lets you avoid the eternal laptop battery anxiety. For desktops, you can still leverage the dock for its ports and its 120W PD to drive a smaller laptop as a temporary workstation or an on-call device. The portability argument is, of course, subjective; if your goal is to create a portable 1-cable solution, the dock does a reliable job. If you’re content to use your desktop’s built-in IO and you only need occasional TB4 expansion, the dock remains useful as a centralized hub for peripherals.

Display support and video chain

Two DisplayPort 1.4 outputs and an HDMI 2.0/2.1 (depending on revision) provide a robust video chain. If your laptop supports DisplayPort Alt Mode and MST (multi-stream transport), you can drive two 4K monitors at 60 Hz, or a single 5K monitor if your host supports the bandwidth. In practice, two 4K60 displays are a sweet spot for professionals who require generous screen real estate for code, content creation, or datasheets while keeping a third display for chat and email. For gamers who also do spreadsheets, this is a reasonable compromise: you can have your visual bliss and your productivity simultaneously—though you’ll probably notice a drop in frame rates when you push the GPU to the redline while streaming.

External storage, USB devices, and performance

The USB ports are plentiful enough for a day at the office. The dock’s USB-A and USB-C connections are suitable for external SSDs, mechanical keyboards, mice, and the occasional USB fan for those hot coding sessions. We tested a handful of portable NVMe drives and an external SSD and saw solid performance that matched typical PCIe-based external storage. The Ethernet port is a quiet hero for those times when Wi-Fi is a little… dramatic. In all our tests, the dock kept devices well-behaved, produced minimal heat, and didn’t require a rearranged living space to function correctly.

Tips for setup and long-term use

- Place the dock in a stable position with easy access to power. A gentle cable management system goes a long way toward desk serenity.
- Use compatible TB4 cables; not all USB-C cables are created equal. If the cable is too long and cheap, you might see degraded display output or occasional disconnects.
- Update firmware if HP provides a dock firmware update. It can improve performance and stability, although the dock is generally robust out of the box.
- For multi-monitor setups, align your monitor order logically in your OS to avoid confusion: primary display to your left or center, secondary displays to the right. This reduces the “which monitor is which?” headaches during intense spreadsheet sessions.
- If you’re running a desktop PC that’s primarily a gaming rig, consider the nuances of PCIe bandwidth and how many TB4 lanes you’re crowding. In practice, you’ll probably be fine, but it’s worth noting for those chasing the perfect dream machine.
