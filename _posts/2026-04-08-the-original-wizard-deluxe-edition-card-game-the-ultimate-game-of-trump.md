## How to Play: Flow, Phases, and Flavor
This is where the Wizard Deluxe Edition earns its keep. The rules are designed to keep things quick and chaotic without devolving into a mutiny of rulebooks. The core loop blends set collection with bluffing, with a side of “everyone talk at once and try to look strategic.” Here is the skeleton, with enough color to keep you from nodding off.

### Setup and Turn Structure
- Gather players (recommended 3 to 6, but 2 can work in a pinch if you enjoy nightmares). Each player selects a wizard avatar. The start player is determined by a whimsical roll of the dice or by the person who last ate a spicy noodle.
- Shuffle the spell deck and deal each player a starting hand. The remaining deck becomes the draw pile. The event deck goes face-down for later reveal.
- On your turn, you perform three steps in order: draw, play, and resolve. The draw step replenishes your hand; the play step lets you cast a spell or deploy a trick; the resolve step tallies effects, checks for wins, and passes the turn.
- The aim is to accumulate influence points by winning tricks, completing strategic goals, and cynically dragging your opponents into your schemes.

### Card Types and Roles
- Spell Cards: These are the spicy meatball of the game. They let you flip the board, steal a card, force an exchange, or conjure a temporary rule that benefits your position. Some spells are protective, some are offensive, and a few are just there to confuse your friends in the most entertaining way.
- Role Cards: Each round, players might encounter a temporary alignment, like a Councilor of Mirth or a Grand Sorcerer of Silly. These roles grant special abilities that can dramatically alter the balance of power. Knowing when to play a role card is as important as having good cards in hand.
- Event Cards: When drawn, events shake up the room. They can trigger a misdirection wave, chaos on the board, or a sudden political plot twist worthy of a late-night stand-up bit. Events are a reminder that the game thrives on surprises more than predictability.

### Scoring and Endgame
The scoring system is intentionally approachable, with a few twists that keep experienced players on their toes. You earn points by winning tricks, fulfilling secret objectives, and occasionally by misdirecting others with a clever use of your spells. The end condition triggers when the draw pile runs out or after a pre-agreed number of rounds, at which point the player with the most influence points is crowned the Grand Wizard of the Realm. The crown here is symbolic, but the bragging rights are real; your friends will remember this game for the exact moment you conjure a perfect pun while everyone else pretends to be offended.

### Strategy Tips for a Strong Start
- Learn when to burn a trump card and when to save it for a late surge. The Deluxe Edition rewards patience as much as bold moves.
- Pay attention to opponent tells. In a game about politics, the ability to read a table so you know who is bluffing is often more valuable than a good spell.
- Build a personal narrative. The best players weave a small story about their wizard and the roles they assume, which makes the entire night more immersive and entertaining.
- Balance offense and defense. Some rounds you want to force others into the corner, but other rounds you need to shield your own position from a sudden cascade of counterspells.
- Don’t forget the table talk. A well-timed joke or a witty insult can be worth the same as a well-played card. The Wizards are there to entertain, after all.

To illustrate a typical round, imagine this sequence: you draw a strong spell on turn one, you bluff with a fake misdirection on turn two, you unleash a devastating effect on turn three that changes who can win that trick, and you close with a diplomatic flourish that leaves two players plotting about your next move. This is the rhythm the game thrives on: fast, funny, and surprisingly strategic if you lean into the chaos.
