## How to Play: Flow, Phases, and Flavor
This is where the Wizard Deluxe Edition earns its keep. The rules are designed to be quick and chaotic without mutinying into a full-blown ruleset. The core loop blends set collection with bluffing, with a side of everyone talking at once while trying to look strategic. Here is the skeleton, with enough color to keep you awake between rounds.

### Setup and Turn Structure
- Gather players (recommended 3 to 6, though 2 can work in a pinch if you enjoy nightmares). Each player selects a wizard avatar. The start player is determined by whimsical dice rolls or by the person who last ate a spicy noodle.
- Shuffle the spell deck and deal each player a starting hand. The remaining deck becomes the draw pile. The event deck goes face-down for later reveal.
- On your turn, you perform three steps in order: draw, play, and resolve. The draw step replenishes your hand; the play step lets you cast a spell or deploy a trick; the resolve step tallies effects, checks for wins, and passes the turn.
- The aim is to accumulate influence points by winning tricks, completing strategic goals, and cynically dragging your opponents into your schemes.

### Card Types and Roles
- Spell Cards: The spicy meatball of the game. They let you flip the board, steal a card, force an exchange, or conjure a temporary rule that benefits your position. Some spells protect, some attack, and a few are simply there to confuse your friends in the most entertaining way.
- Role Cards: Each round, players might encounter temporary alignments like a Councilor of Mirth or a Grand Sorcerer of Silly. These roles grant special abilities that can dramatically alter the balance of power. Knowing when to play a role card is as important as having good cards in hand.
- Event Cards: Drawn during gameplay, events shake things up. They trigger misdirections, chaos on the board, or a sudden political plot twist worthy of a late-night stand-up bit. Events remind you that the game thrives on surprises more than predictability.

### Scoring and Endgame
The scoring system is approachable with a few twists that keep seasoned players sharp. You earn points by winning tricks, fulfilling secret objectives, and misdirecting others with clever spell use. The end condition triggers when the draw pile runs out or after a pre-agreed number of rounds, and the player with the most influence points is crowned the Grand Wizard of the Realm. The crown is symbolic, but the bragging rights are real; you will remember the exact moment you conjure a perfect pun while everyone pretends to be offended.

### Strategy Tips for a Strong Start
- Learn when to burn a trump card and when to save it for a late surge. The Deluxe Edition rewards patience as much as bold moves.
- Pay attention to tells at the table. In a game about politics, reading a table for bluffing is often more valuable than a powerful spell.
- Build a personal narrative. The best players weave a small story about their wizard and the roles they assume, which makes the night more immersive and entertaining.
- Balance offense and defense. Some rounds you want to force others into the corner, while others you shield your position from counterspells.
- Don’t forget the table talk. A well-timed joke or witty insult can be worth as much as a great card. The Wizards are there to entertain, after all.

To illustrate a typical round, imagine this sequence: you draw a strong spell on turn one, bluff with a fake misdirection on turn two, unleash a devastating effect on turn three that changes who can win the trick, and finish with a diplomatic flourish that leaves two players plotting your next move. This rhythm—fast, funny, and surprisingly strategic when you lean into the chaos—defines the play experience.
