### What you’re getting into
The Infinity 10 Person Workstation with Screens is not a single desk. It’s a modular, scalable platform that arranges ten individual workstations around a central hub—think office-centric Velcro for people who pretend they’re in a cockpit. Each seat gets a dedicated screen, with the option to daisy-chain additional displays for non-stop multi-monitor glory. The system claims to offer ergonomic nirvana, end-to-end cable management, integrated power distribution, and a software suite meant to coordinate display layouts across seats. In practice, you’ll get a combination of sleek, premium hardware and a set of decisions that will either streamline your workflow or require a PhD in cable taxonomy to understand.

Let us be clear: this is not a “put it in a corner and forget it” product. It’s a platform designed for teams who actually need to work together at scale without knocking over a fragile lamp or fiddling with 18 adapters to connect all the monitors. It wants to be the Swiss Army knife of collaborative workspaces, packing in screen sharing, remote control, and physical separation when needed. But does it deliver on the dream? Read on.

## Unboxing and Setup
### What you get in the box
The Infinity 10 arrives in a stack of crates that looks like it could double as a small coffee shop. Inside, you’ll find ten modular desk modules, each with its own monitor arm system, a central hub with power distribution, a tangle of cables that would make a wizard cry, and a set of assembly instructions that could double as a small novel. The instruction manual is not just necessary—it’s a rite of passage. We recommend a team of two people who know the basics of furniture assembly and one person who can tell a good joke while holding a crate in place to keep the thing from sliding off the table during installation.

The hardware components are premium: brushed aluminum frames, tempered glass work surfaces, and a cable raceway that looks like it could survive a small typhoon. The screens come pre-paired with a docking system, which is good, because if you had to wire ten monitors by hand, you’d deserve a medal, or at least a very stiff drink after hours.

### Assembly time and tools
If you’re assembling with a crew of engineers and a couple of office managers who know how to read a level, you’re looking at roughly 4-6 hours for the full setup, assuming zero dramatic surprises. Our team hit a few snags: one monitor arm required a callback to tech support for a “non-standard bolt,” two power rails refused to align due to a minor manufacturing variance, and there was one dramatic moment where the central hub decided to throw an error code that looked suspiciously like it was trolling us. You’ll want the following on hand: a metric allen key set (provided), a power drill with a torque setting, a spirit level, cable ties, and a sense of humor.

The assembly experience is part of the vibe: serious, methodical, with occasional bursts of laughter when someone realizes they’ve just built a 10-person cockpit of productivity. The first boot is both exciting and terrifying. Will the screens turn on? Will the central hub hum like a friendly drone or a miniature jet engine? Spoilers: there is a gentle hum and a blue glow that says everything is ready to roll.

## Design and Build
### Materials and aesthetics
Infinity 10 leans heavily into premium materials: aluminum frames, tempered glass, and a thick, rugged tabletop that can survive a spreadsheet sprint and a coffee spill without showing its trauma. The aesthetic is clean, minimal, and vaguely sci-fi, which is exactly the vibe you want in a “team has arrived” environment. It’s not cheap-looking by any standard; it oozes a kind of understated luxury that says, we mean business, but we also know your screens aren’t decoration, they are your lifeblood.

The modular design is the real star here. Each seat is independent yet visually connected, thanks to the shared cable raceway that snakes around the central hub like a metallic nervous system. When you walk into a room with Infinity 10 in place, you don’t just see a workstation—you see a platform for collaboration. And with the optional color accents, you can tailor the vibe to your corporate brand or your own inner nerd who wants neon lines tracing the perimeter of the desk at night.

### Ergonomics and comfort
Comfort is a big claim here. Each workstation has adjustable arms, tilt-rotation on the screens, and a slightly curved design that tries to keep everyone within screen eye-line without banging elbows. The chairs are the standard office fare—comfortable enough for long sessions,—but the real ergonomic hero is the height-adjustable surfaces and the screen arms that can sit at eye level for every seat. You’ll appreciate that if you have multiple people of wildly different heights sharing the same workspace, there’s a good chance you won’t have to scream across the table to check a KPI because someone’s monitor is wrong. The downside is that with 10 seats, you’ll spend some time dialing in the exact heights so that no one is looking at a screen from a 20-degree angle while someone else complains about neck strain.

### Display array and layout options
A key feature here is the display configuration. The Infinity 10 supports up to 10 screens per bench, with a central controller that can sync resolutions across all displays for a “wall of clarity” effect. You can opt for uniform panel sizes (e.g., ten 27-inch 4K monitors) or mix sizes for a more eclectic, “creative studio” vibe. The system can also drive additional external monitors via a daisy-chain, but we found that once you hit beyond eight displays per seat (and yes, some seats can host two displays on extended arms), you’re entering the world of GPU memory planning. The practical setup we tested used a primary 27-inch 4K per seat, plus a shared central display for dashboards and a few reference materials. The result is a robust multi-display environment that makes pair programming feel like you’re piloting a starship bridge.

## Cable Management and Power
If there’s one thing Infinity 10 does not skimp on, it’s cable management. The 10-seat system ships with a robust cable raceway that snakes from each seat into a central spine, then into a master power strip with surge protection and a UPS bump for safety. The raceway is wide enough to swallow multiple power cables, HDMI/DisplayPort runs, USB-C dongles, and a surprising number of coffee-stained USB cables you thought you’d thrown away last year.

We tested the system in a real-world office environment: two laptops, three desktop PCs, a time-lapse camera streaming in, six HDMI cables, and a handful of external drives. The result? It all stayed relatively neat. The cable channels are modular, so you can reconfigure quickly if someone reorgs the seating or you decide to swap seats with the marketing team because, you know, synergy.

However, there are caveats. Even with the best cable management, you’ll want to train your team to coil and label. We found that when new folks join, their impulse is to jam everything into the nearest hollow space, which leads to a small bundling disaster. A quick onboarding session about labeling conventions and color-coded cables saved us a lot of headaches and an emotional rollercoaster of “Who moved my HDMI?” moments.

For those who care about energy efficiency, Infinity 10 ships with smart power strips that can dim lighting and put unused displays into sleep mode when a conference is running or when you’re on mute and pretending to be busy. It’s not a magic wand, but it’s a nice touch that reduces heat and power draw when the office is quiet.

## Performance and Collaboration on a 10-Seat Scale
### How does this perform in real life?
We put Infinity 10 through three scenarios that a modern team actually faces:
- The daily grind with eight-to-ten people working on different tasks across multiple screens.
- A design sprint with real-time collaboration, whiteboarding, and rapid sharing of screens.
- A remote/onsite hybrid meeting where attendees join via video and require synchronized screen layouts.

In all cases, the experience was smooth, provided the network bandwidth was sufficient and the GPUs driving the displays were not total mid-range chaos. The built-in display management software makes it easy to assign layouts, switch primary displays per seat, and push a global layout to all screens with a single command. We appreciated the ability to set a unified “presentation mode” that expands a single screen or a subset of displays for everyone to share content during a sprint review. It felt like the product was designed for team rituals rather than individual solo sessions, which is exactly what you want if you’re investing in a 10-person configuration.

Where it shines is collaboration. Hand-offs between developers and designers happen visually, not verbally. A team lead can pop up a dashboard on the central screen, annotate a screenshot on the fly, and push changes to everyone’s workspace in seconds. It’s not flawless—latency can creep in if you’re streaming 4K video across all seats or running heavy virtualization workloads on remote machines—but in general, it makes collective work feel like a well-choreographed improv session rather than a chaotic sprint.

### Ergonomic quirks and small annoyances
No product is perfect, and Infinity 10 has its share of quirks:
- The central hub can produce a gentle but audible purr during heavy GPU use. It’s not loud, but it’s noticeable if you’re sitting close and not wearing headphones.
- The included chairs are decent but not revolutionary. If your team does 8+ hours in a chair, you may want to upgrade to premium ergonomic seating; the desk’s height range is excellent, but long sessions still take their toll.
- Cable count climbs quickly as you add more monitors and devices. We recommend standardizing on USB-C docks for simplicity. If you’re into minimalist setups, you may want to go full-dock and keep the host devices lean.

## Practicality for Teams
### Ideal use cases
Infinity 10 is at its best in environments where multiple people need to consume, annotate, and respond to information simultaneously. Here are some prime scenarios:
- Software development teams performing code reviews, pair programming, and sprint demos with live dashboards visible to all.
- Data analytics teams that juggle notebooks, dashboards, and data pipelines across several monitors.
- Creative studios where brainstorms turn into visual stories across a wall of screens, with a shared canvas and reference feed in the hub.
- Remote/hybrid teams that want a consistent physical shared workspace by day and a clean remote show-and-tell by night.

### When it’s not the right fit
If your team mostly works in one-on-one sessions or you have a constraint-based office with limited space, Infinity 10 can feel excessive. It’s a behemoth in a conference room and will consume floor space that could accommodate several regular desks. If your team is small and you don’t foresee a future uptick in collaboration demand, there are more compact solutions that offer a better cost-to-productivity ratio.
