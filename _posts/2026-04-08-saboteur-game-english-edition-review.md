## What is Saboteur? An Overview
Saboteur is a push-your-luck, hidden-role card game for 3–10 players. At its core, it's a race to lay down a path from the dwarves’ mine entrance to a treasure-troweled shaft. The catch? You might be a gold-digging dwarf—or, more interestingly, a saboteur who wants to stop the path and steal the treasure for yourself or other saboteur teammates. The English Edition keeps the core idea intact while updating the artwork and refining the rule-book to be more accessible for players who speak English as a second language or for table groups that want clearer iconography on cards.

### Components
- A deck of path cards, with straight, bent, and cross paths, along with a handful of hole cards.
- Role cards: miners and saboteurs.
- Resource card types that allow you to forge paths or derail them.
- A set of gold nugget tokens and a few special action cards.
- Player aids that explain the action queue and the victory conditions.

In the English edition, you’ll notice improved typography and panel artwork that reduces confusion during late-night, caffeine-fueled gaming sessions. The art style stays faithful to the original’s lighthearted dwarven vibe—think gold sparkles, cartoonish beards, and a dramatic looming miner pick that seems to say, You’re either a genius or a disaster, let’s find out together.

## Setup and First Impressions
Setting up Saboteur is like assembling a tiny, chaotic orchestra. Shuffle the role cards and deal one to each player face down. You’ll keep your role hidden from others, except for a couple of public signals that confirm who is on which side when necessary. Next, deal each player a handful of path cards and action cards. The rest forms the draw pile. The English edition may feature improved text on back of cards and clearer icons to help non-native speakers parse the rules quickly—no more squinting at the images while you pretend to be calculating a multi-step strategy.

Display a couple of the cards so players can see the possible paths. The goal is to connect the entrance of the mine to a treasure path with exactly four or more tiles, depending on the variant you’re playing. The mine’s entrance is marked, and that is the only fixed standard; everything else is a wild guess and a clever bluff.

## How to Play: A Quick Rundown
Saboteur is divided into turns, and a lot of the action happens in the negotiating, bluffing, and path-placing moments that feel like a tense board game version of a group chat where everyone’s pretending to be reasonable.

1) Start of Turn: The active player draws a card and may play a card, or discard, or pass. If you draw or play a card that ends a path, you might be rewarded or punished depending on your role. The “curse of the miner” is that you’ll sometimes reveal a role to others if you use a particular action, creating a dramatic moment of gasped astonishment.
2) Path Building: The main action is to lay a path card in a way that connects to your existing trail from the entrance toward the treasure shaft. Some cards include tunnels or dead-ends that can trap or protect your colleagues depending on your plan.
3) Role-Playing and Bluffing: If you’re a miner, you want to lay a path that reaches the treasure; if you’re a saboteur, you want to break it. The English edition’s clearer iconography helps keep track of who is who as the tension foils become increasingly comedic.
4) End of Round and Scoring: When a path reaches the treasure or the deck runs out, you reveal roles and score. Miners who completed the path share the loot; saboteurs try to derail and claim control or block. The round-based system means there’s always a few cycles of shifting alliances and last-second gambits that can flip a game on its head.

Gameplay in practice feels like a well-timed improv exercise. You’ll defend a plan, accuse your neighbor of treachery, and then realize you yourself might be the traitor—only in the comedic sense, not the fate-of-the-kingdom sense. The English edition’s streamlined rules help avoid during-game confusion, allowing more focus on the social deduction and misdirection that Saboteur thrives on.

### Secret Roles and Strategy
- Miners: They’re chasing the treasure; their goal is to complete a continuous treasure path to the end.
- Saboteurs: They want to derail that path or mislead other players through forged paths and misdirections. The fun of Saboteur is how transparent this can feel—until someone twists the situation in a glorious way.

A little strategic note: you don’t always want to reveal you’re a saboteur immediately. Timing is everything. Creating a false sense of security around a particular path can coax miners to invest cards into the wrong branch. The English edition clarifies what each card does, so you’ll spend less time muttering, “Wait, does this card break a path, or only continue it?” and more time scheming and laughing when a plan goes sideways but in a glorious way.
