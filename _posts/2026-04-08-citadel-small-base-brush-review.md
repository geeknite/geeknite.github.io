## What is the Citadel Small Base Brush
The Small Base Brush is a synthetic fiber brush designed for base coating large flat areas on miniatures with a balance of control and speed. In the world of miniature painting, base coats are the foundation you build upon. If you lay down a sloppy base coat, everything else tends to crumble like a bad joke at a convention. The brush aims to deliver a nice, even coat without shedding bristles and without turning your paint into a full-blown abstract expressionist canvas. In practice, that means a tip supple enough for smooth coverage but stiff enough to push paint into recesses without wiggling around like a jellyfish on a hot plate.

Citadel branding promises retention, tip shape stability, and a damp-friendly ferrule. The base brush size is one of those unsung heroes – not flashy like a specialty brush, but essential for layers, base coats, and consistent color coverage. This is not the brush for tiny details or shimmering highlights; this is the chassis that gets your model from bare plastic to paintable stage with a single heroic sweep.

## Painting Techniques and Tips with the Small Base Brush
Here are some practical techniques to maximize the potential of the Small Base Brush while keeping your sanity intact:

- Base coat density matters: thinning your base paints slightly helps this brush lay down a smooth surface. It’s not a magic wand for thinned paint; it’s a helper that ensures even coverage with less brushwork resistance.
- Use flat strokes for base coverage: long, consistent strokes across flat surfaces yield a uniform tone and smoother transitions into recessed areas.
- Edge control: use the tip for sharper edges on larger panels. If you need a truly crisp edge on a curved surface, a little practice with lip-of-brush technique goes a long way.
- Avoid heavy pooling: keep the paint load moderate and avoid painting with excessive paint on the bristles. The Small Base Brush isn’t a soak brush; it is a precise tool disguised as a small base coat ace.
- Use with multiple layers: after applying the base coat, skim with a lighter shade to create subtle depth. The balance of the brush helps with the initial coat and prepares the surface for the first wash or shade.
- Cleaning strategy: rinse after each color, then give the bristles a light soap wash. The more you rinse and clean, the longer the brush lasts in your collection of tiny warlords.

## Cleaning and Maintenance
Maintenance is the unsung hero of any good brush review. A brush that is easy to clean, dries quickly, and maintains its shape after drying is worth its weight in paint pots. Here is a practical routine that keeps your Small Base Brush performing without turning your workspace into a science experiment:

- Rinse immediately after use with lukewarm water. Don’t let the paint dry in the bristles; it is a miniature version of a nightmare when you try to rehydrate it later.
- Gently work paint out with your fingers or a soft brush to avoid bristle splaying. This is not a battle, it is a polite conversation with bristles about their life choices.
- Use mild soap or a dedicated brush cleaner. Avoid harsh solvents that can degrade synthetic bristles over time.
- Shape and dry: after rinsing, reshape the bristles with minimal pressure and lay the brush bristle-side down to dry. Avoid standing bristles up while wet; water can seep into the ferrule and cause loosening or rust in metal parts.
- Storage matters: keep the brush in a protective case or at least in a stand that prevents bristle damage. The Small Base Brush is a workhorse, not a decorative wand — treat it like the tool it is.

A quick anecdote: I once tried to dry a brush by sticking it in a mug of coffee to help the handle stay warm. Do not do this. The brush did not magically paint better; it smelled like a latte that never quite finished. Lesson learned: coffee is for drinking, not for drying brushes.

## Comparisons: How It Stacks Against Other Citadel Brushes
- Small Base vs Layer Brush: The Layer Brush is designed for mid-to-thin coats and glazing. The Small Base is the base workhorse — it deposits color broadly but still keeps a sharp edge if you use it carefully. Pair them together for a smooth pipeline: base with Small Base, then layer for subtle tonal shifts. 
- Small Base vs Small Brush: The Small Brush is for fine work. The Small Base offers more coverage and is less fussy about paint load. If you want to keep a crisp boundary on large panels, you might reach for this one first before switching to smaller tools.
- Synthetic vs Sable (in Citadel line): many hobbyists swear by sable for feathering and fine lines, but synthetic bristles in the Small Base Brush are perfectly adequate for base coats and speed painting. Budget-conscious painters often appreciate the durability of synthetic fibers when base coating squads or terrain where a lot of surface is involved.

The goal of these comparisons is to emphasize use case. There is no moral victory in the brush you own, only in the results you achieve. If you are building a starter kit, this brush is a strong base option; if you are chasing the perfect glaze, you might supplement with other specialized brushes.
