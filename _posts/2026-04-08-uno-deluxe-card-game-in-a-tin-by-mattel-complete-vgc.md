### Cards and Print Quality
The UNO cards themselves are standard in size and shape and use glossy, high-contrast colors. The print quality holds up under typical use—no obvious ink bleed around the numbers, and the action symbols pop nicely against the colored backgrounds. The font size on the numbers is readable for adults and kids alike, which is crucial if you’re playing with a crowd that sometimes misreads the deck because the table is too bright or someone forgot their glasses. The stock feels reasonably durable; it’s not the absolutely thickest card stock you’ll ever encounter in a dedicated game, but it’s sturdy enough for repeated shuffles and the occasional accidental drop onto a carpet tile. For a family game that gets tossed into backpacks and hauled to friend gatherings, this is a sensible compromise between weight and longevity.

The Wild and Draw Four cards are the stars of the show in terms of impact on gameplay; their print quality is crisp, making wins and misdirections visually legible at a glance. When you’re managing a distance-initials style sprint across color sets and you throw down a Wild, you want to be able to look at the card quickly and know that your chosen color is the one your friends won’t see coming.
