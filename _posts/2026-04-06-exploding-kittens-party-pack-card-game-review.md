## Gameplay and rules in practice

The Party Pack is built around the same core loop as the original game, but with improvements that make it friendlier for larger groups. The objective remains simple: avoid drawing an Exploding Kitten. If you draw one, you must use a Defuse card (if you’ve got one) to survive. If you don’t, well, the kitten wins and the table erupts in laughter, mock outrage, and the occasional \"we’re totally stealing that card\" conspiracy.

### Turn structure

On your turn, you generally do one of two things: play an action card to influence the draw, or pass the turn by using a Skip or similar utility to force someone else to draw. Then you draw a card from the deck, hoping your next card isn’t the catastrophic Exploding Kitten. The Party Pack extends the strategic palette by introducing more options for non-solo play, allowing you to coordinate with others without turning the game into a math exam.

### Handling Exploding Kittens and Defuses

Defuse cards are limited resources. They’re your lifeline when luck tilts toward kitten doom. Managing Defuses becomes a social challenge: who should you protect, who’s likely to keep track of who has what, and when should you pressure someone into giving you a Defuse if you’re the one facing a near-catastrophe? The social mechanics here are as important as the card text itself.

### The social energy

The Party Pack shines when you’re playing with a larger group. The table talk becomes more chaotic, the bets on who will explode grow louder, and the jokes get a little more inventive. It’s a party game in the most literal sense: the party is the game, and the game is the party.

## House rules and variants

In the spirit of Geeknite, you’re encouraged to tailor the rules to your crew. Here are a few common variants that work well with the Party Pack:

- Team chaos: Split into two teams for a few rounds. Team members can confer on Defuse usage or target choices, but each turn is still individual—saving the social tension for the final showdown.
- Quick-start variant: Start with a smaller hand, say four cards per player, to speed up the early chaos and get to the 
  “who’s still alive?” moments faster.
- Nope stacking: If your group enjoys rule-scrambling, allow multiple players to chain Nope cards to cancel artist attacks, which can lead to delightful chaos.
- See the Future spree: If you add more “See the Future” cards or equivalents, use them to build a predictive narrative about the upcoming draws. The more you know, the more you can bait and mislead others.
- Custom deck rotation: Use one or two of the Party Pack’s new decks in rotation with the base game’s deck to create a more varied play experience without learning a lot of new rules.
