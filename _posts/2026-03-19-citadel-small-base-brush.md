## Design and Ergonomics

### Grip, Weight, and Comfort

The Small Base Brush is not a full-on ergonomic nightmare, but it isn’t trying to be a torque-friendly monstrosity either. The handle has a modest diameter with just enough curvature to fit the fingers naturally without forcing you to contort your grip. It sits comfortably in the palm and doesn’t slip when your hands get slightly sweaty after a long painting session. If you are someone who tends to plan seven micro-steps in your head while you brush, you will appreciate the balance this brush offers. It feels like a tool that understands the mission: keep control, avoid fatigue, and let you focus on the base coat rather than the grip itself.

### Brush Hair Quality

The bristles are synthetic, which is typical for this class of brush and a sane choice for base coats. They hold a decent amount of paint without soaking up the entire pool and spilling into adjacent areas you swore you would keep clean. The tip holds a reasonably sharp edge for a base brush, letting you edge against panels with a solid crispness. It does not pretend to be a dedicated detail brush, but it is precise enough to create clean boundaries without tugging or leaving random stray bristles in the paint. If you rely on a very fine edge for a strict horizontal line or a crisp panel edge, you may want to pair the Small Base Brush with a dedicated detail brush for the final pass. For large surface areas, this brush shines because the bristles deliver a controlled amount of paint with a light touch.

## Performance in Practice

### Paint Flow and Control

Base coats want to behave; they want to be even, they want to be fast, and they want to not litter your model with brush strokes that look like a toddler’s attempt at abstract art. The Small Base Brush gives you a nice, reliable flow. It loads enough paint to cover a broad area without dragging. The bristle stiffness helps prevent too much pooling in the recesses, which is a common problem with slick surfaces and glossy enamel paints. The brush’s tip is forgiving enough to blend along the main surfaces, so you don’t feel forced to rely on heavy-handed side loading or back-and-forth scrubbing that makes panel lines go to war with your patience.

A common move when base coating is to move quickly around the model and then come back with a second pass to ensure full coverage. This brush performs well in that scenario, letting you keep a consistent pressure so that you don’t accidentally saturate the mold lines. If you aim to minimize brush marks, you will benefit from a light touch and consistent speed. The brush is not magic; it is a tool, and tool discipline matters here.

### Base Coating vs Detail Work

The Small Base Brush is designed for base coats, not for the final edge highlighting or micro-detailing that a true detail brush handles. With base coats, you want a broad, even swath of color across the main surfaces, and this brush does that well. It’s not a chameleon that will turn into a detail brush on the fly, but it does the base coat job with a sense of reliability that you can count on when you are painting squads. If your project involves quick undercoats or large glossy panels that need a uniform finish, this brush is a smart pick. For tiny shoulder pads or tiny rivets where you want crystal clean lines, you will probably want to switch to a smaller, more precise brush after the base coat has established the color foundation.

## Maintenance, Cleaning, and Longevity

Maintenance stores the secret to long-term brush happiness. The Small Base Brush should be cleaned immediately after use, especially if you have been working with acrylics that tend to dry fast in the bristles. A quick rinse with lukewarm water and a mild brush soap will help keep bristles from becoming brittle or stiffer over time. Gently pat dry with a lint-free cloth and resettle the bristles into their natural shape. A few collectors of hobby gear might tell you that letting the brush dry bristles-down can guarantee longer life, while others insist bristles-up is better to avoid water creeping into the ferrule. The practical approach is to dry with the bristles horizontal and supported so the tip doesn’t deform into a weird pointy pancake. Don’t leave the brush soaking in water or cleaning solutions for long periods; your bristles deserve a spa day, not an aquarium day.

If you do proper maintenance, you will be rewarded with a brush that keeps its shape and continues to deliver consistent performance over many games of painting sessions. Expect to replace this brush after a long dammed amount of base coats, or if you accidentally drop it onto a hard surface and the bristle tip goes flat. It happens to the best of us; just remember that brushes are not immortal like your favorite space marine, but with care they can outlast a dozen undercoats and a couple of paint pots.

### Cleaning and Storage Tips

- Rinse immediately after use to prevent paint from hardening in the bristles.
- Use a gentle brush soap or mild dish soap; harsh cleaners can degrade bristle integrity.
- Gently squeeze and reshape the bristles after washing; avoid twisting which can loosen the ferrule.
- Dry flat or with bristles supported to preserve the tip shape.
- Store in a brush holder or cup aligned upright to avoid bristle splaying.

## Comparisons: Citadel Small Base Brush vs Other Brands

### Citadel vs Army Painter

When stacking Citadel against Army Painter base brushes, the first thing you notice is the stiffness and spring of the bristles. Citadel tends to hold a stable line with a slightly stiffer feel, whereas Army Painter brushes may feel a touch softer and more forgiving on curved surfaces. For base coats on larger models, both perform quite well; the Citadel brush offers a robust cornering ability that helps when you want to keep paint off the negative space without micro-managing every edge. The Army Painter option is excellent if you want a softer base that blends a touch more naturally into the recesses without too much technique, but if you prefer crisp edges on the main surfaces, Citadel often wins in terms of control.

### Citadel Small Base Brush vs Detail Brushes

The comparison with dedicated detail brushes is inevitable. A small base brush is your base coat engine, while a detail brush is your final polish. If you are trying to cover broad surfaces quickly, the base brush is the winner; it pays for its larger tip with speed. But for crisp edges and micro-lines, you’ll reach for a finer point eventually. The trick is to use each tool for its intended purpose and recognize when to switch tools rather than forcing one brush to do everything. This is especially true in a time-crunched painting session where you want to get a clean, presentable model onto the tabletop without spending an eternity on one figure.


## Practical Tips and Techniques

- Load technique matters. A small amount of paint on the tip and a light pressure can yield a smooth, even base coat. If you are dipping too deeply into the pot, you risk pooling and a blotchy finish.
- Angle the brush slightly and use long, even strokes across the surface. Short, choppy strokes tend to leave brush marks that this brush would rather avoid.
- For larger flat surfaces, rotate the model as you move the brush to keep the edge of the bristles in contact with the surface, smoothing out the coat.
- If you need a quick base in a darker tone, apply an even coat and then build up with a second pass. Layering is your friend here, and the Small Base Brush plays nice with multiple passes.
- Pair with a larger base brush for big swaths and switch to the Small Base for tight corners and edges. This pairing is the dynamic duo of efficient base coating.

## Value, Longevity, and What to Expect

Price is a factor, of course, and Citadel products are often in the mid-to-upper range for hobby brushes. The Small Base Brush is not the cheapest option on the market, but it is reasonably priced for what you get in terms of control, durability, and a more comfortable grip than some budget options. If you are a casual painter who wants a reliable tool for base coats and some light edge work, it is a sensible purchase. If your budget is tight and you only want the absolute cheapest brush you can find, you will likely outgrow this tool quickly or you will end up compensating with more passes and frustration. The decision comes down to how often you paint and how much you value consistent control over a long-term tool.

Durability is also decent. The brush tends to hold its shape after a few uses and does not shed excessively, provided you take care of it and avoid aggressive cleaning methods. If you treat your brushes like disposable props, you will get similar results; if you treat them as investments, you should see this one last a good while with proper care.


## Final Verdict

The Citadel Small Base Brush is a solid addition to your painting arsenal if base coats are a large part of your workflow. It is not a miracle worker that will magically transform a rough model into a flawless masterpiece overnight, but it does offer reliable performance, excellent control, and a level of convenience that makes it a joy to use for the right tasks. It shines on base coats for panels and large shapes while providing enough precision for small adjustments around edges and seams. If your routine involves painting squads, vehicles, or terrain pieces that require crisp base coats without sacrificing speed, this brush earns a place on your workbench.

If you are still not sure, consider pairing it with a dedicated detail brush for final edge work and micro-detailing. The synergy between a base brush and a fine detail brush is where you get the smooth, professional finish that tabletop players admire.

- Great for base coats on medium to large surfaces
- Good control for edges and panels

- Not a substitute for true detail work
