### The Bonus Card Holders: what they actually do

The “Bonus Card Holders” are not a myth or a meme. They’re plastic or acrylic (depending on production batch) organizers designed to house those extra decks—armor, risk, sabotage, or encounter decks—that accumulate as your game grows. In practice, you’ll notice:

- Improved accessibility: you don’t rummage through a storm of cards the way you rummage through a bag of snacks at a campground. You actually get to flip to the card you need without throwing your back out.
- Reduced wear: fewer fingers pinching the corner of a card and bending its edge. If you’re a stickler for card longevity, this is a small mercy.
- Aesthetics: the holders come in a few colorways or clear options that can be customized to match your table’s vibe. It’s not essential, but it does push the “look at this table” factor from “mid-tier board gamer” to “quite fancy, actually.”

We’ll revisit durability and value in a dedicated section, but suffice to say: if you own a Black Plague table and you have a campaign that uses lots of encounter decks, the holders are a nice-to-have that doesn’t shout “rip out your wallet” at you every time you open the box.

## Loose vs Complete: the storage debate

Here’s where some tabletop philosopher in your playgroup might take the stage and declare: “Are we buying a game or a lifestyle?” The answer, as always in Geeknite land, is a little of both. The Huntsman Pack exists in two states:

- Complete: everything you’d expect from a boxed expansion, including the card holders and the full accessory bundle. You’re paying for convenience, a tidier table, and fewer moments of card chaos that lead to slow-mo replays of failed tactics.
- Loose: the parts that come without the neat packaging, sometimes with a few items missing. This is the version you will encounter if you bought it second-hand, found a clearance sale, or simply enjoy the scavenger-hunt vibe: what do you actually need to play today, and what can you improvise with from your own array of tokens and drink coasters?

From a gameplay standpoint, your table will function with either variant. But the experience differs: with the complete set, the vibe screams “this is a serious night”—everything clicks, the card holders glide out when you need them, and you can pretend you’re a wizard who can conjure space in the box without shuffling. With the loose set, you’ll be improvising more. You’ll probably end up using card sleeves or scraps of notebook paper to mark decks, which is charming in a post-apocalyptic-industrial-recycling kind of way.

We tested both routes during multiple sessions, with players of varied experience levels. The conclusions: the complete Huntsman Pack tends to shorten setup time and reduce argument potential; the loose set invites memory games about which card belongs to which deck and how many encounter cards you actually pulled last game. If you’re a purist who wants a perfectly curated experience every time, go complete; if you love the thrift-store thrill and don’t mind a little chaos, the loose route is fine—especially if you’re playing with casual groups or streaming your ineptitude for laughs.

For those who love a technical breakdown of storage, we tried out a few organizing schemes. If you’re attracted to camera-angles and “tabletop aesthetics,” consider lining up the holders like a tiny, dignified cathedral of cardboard. In short: build quality lands in “solid enough for weekly gaming and occasional demo nights with friends” rather than “premier display piece.” If you’re someone who likes all the bells and whistles, you’ll appreciate the holders; if you’re budget-conscious, you’ll enjoy the practical gains without the premium tag attached to every micro-feature.

## Storage and organization tips

If there’s one lesson to leave with you today, it’s this: organization equals speed, and speed equals fewer “zom-bro” jokes about your table manners. Card holders save you a lot of tedium, but you still need a plan. Here are some practical tips:

- Use the included holders for encounter cards and the main deck. Keep objective and equipment cards in a smaller binder or a separate box. The separation reduces rummaging time and helps you explain rules without the “is this the right deck?” chorus.
- Label everything. Even if you think you’ll remember, five minutes into the game you’ll forget that “Loot A” belongs to the southern fortress or whatever. Small sticky labels or erasable markers can save you a lot of squinting and guesswork.
- Consider sleeves if you’re using a lot of cards. Sleeves help preserve cards from players who like to shuffle aggressively or flip past a deck like it’s a trading card duel. Just ensure the sleeves aren’t so thick that your holders won’t close.
- Create a quick setup card. A laminated sheet that shows where each deck and holder goes can shave minutes off setup time and reduce the “assembly line chaos” that sometimes happens when the box is left open during a multi-day campaign.
