### The Bonus Card Holders: what they actually do

The “Bonus Card Holders” aren’t a myth or a meme. They’re plastic or acrylic organizers designed to house those extra decks—armor, risk, sabotage, or encounter decks—that accumulate as your game grows. In practice, you’ll notice:

- Improved accessibility: you don’t rummage through a storm of cards the way you rummage through a bag of snacks at a campground. You actually get to flip to the card you need without hurting your back.
- Reduced wear: fewer fingers pinching the corner of a card and bending edges. If you’re a stickler for card longevity, this is a small mercy.
- Aesthetics: the holders come in a few colorways or clear options that can be customized to match your table’s vibe. It’s not essential, but it does push the “table wow” factor from “mid-tier board gamer” to “quite fancy, actually.”

We’ll revisit durability and value in a dedicated section, but suffice to say: if you own a Black Plague table and you run campaigns with lots of encounter decks, the holders are a nice-to-have that doesn’t scream “rip out your wallet” at you every time you open the box.

## Loose vs Complete: the storage debate

Here’s where a tabletop philosopher in your playgroup may step up on a chair and declare: Are we buying a game or a lifestyle? The answer, as always in Geeknite land, is a little of both. The Huntsman Pack exists in two states:

- Complete: everything you’d expect from a boxed expansion, including the card holders and the full accessory bundle. You’re paying for convenience, a tidier table, and fewer moments of card chaos that slow down late-game tempo.
- Loose: parts that come without neat packaging, sometimes with a few items missing. This is the version you encounter if you bought it second-hand, found a clearance sale, or simply enjoy the scavenger-hunt vibe: what do you actually need to play today, and what can you improvise with from your own array of tokens and coasters?

From a gameplay standpoint, your table will function with either variant. The experience, however, differs: with the complete set, the vibe shouts “this is a serious night”—cards appear where they should, holders glide out when you need them, and you can pretend you’re a wizard who conjures space in the box without shuffling. In the loose route, you’ll be improvising more, perhaps using sleeves or scraps of notebook paper to mark decks. It’s charming in a post-apocalyptic-industrial-recycling kind of way.

We tested both routes across multiple sessions, with players of varied experience levels. The verdict: the complete Huntsman Pack tends to shorten setup time and reduce argument potential; the loose set invites memory games about which card belongs to which deck and how many encounter cards you pulled last game. If you’re a purist who wants a perfectly curated experience every time, go complete; if you love the thrift-store thrill and don’t mind a little chaos, the loose route is perfectly fine—especially if you’re playing with casual groups or streaming your ineptitude for laughs.

For those who love a technical breakdown of storage, we tried a few organizing schemes. One recommended approach is to use the card holders for the encounter decks and the main mission deck, while keeping reference cards in the rulebook’s spare pages or a dedicated binder. If you’re attracted to camera angles and table-top aesthetics, consider lining up the holders like a tiny, dignified cathedral of cardboard. ## Build quality and materials

Quality matters in any expansion—no one wants a brittle token snapping in half during a tense late-game moment. The Huntsman Pack is a mixed bag here:

- Miniatures and tokens: The sculpts are solid, with enough detail to justify a quick wash or dry brush if you’re into painting. They’re not museum-quality, but they don’t crumble under the breath of a zombie horde either. They fit well into standard Zombicide bases and do not look comically oversized on a crowded board.
- Card holders: The holders are sturdy enough for a few sessions without showing signs of fatigue. If you’re going to move them around a lot, you’ll want to ensure you don’t over-tighten any spring-loaded components that might wear out over time. In practice, the holders do not feel cheap, but you should avoid forcing them into corners you know will be stubborn. They’re not Olympic-grade polymers, but they get the job done with style.
- Card stock and sleeves compatibility: The card backs and thickness are within normal ranges for Zombicide cards. If you sleeve your decks (and we always encourage sleeves for longevity and mint condition), make sure to double-check the thickness before you clamp the holder shut. A few sleeves can make the fit snug, but it’s a good problem to have if you hate bent corners.

In short: build quality lands in “solid enough for weekly gaming and occasional demo nights with friends” rather than “premier display piece.” If you’re someone who likes all the bells and whistles, you’ll appreciate the holders; if you’re budget-conscious, you’ll enjoy the practical gains without the premium tag attached to every micro-feature.

## Storage and organization tips

If there’s one lesson to leave with you today, it’s this: organization equals speed, and speed equals fewer zom-bro jokes about table manners. Card holders save you a lot of tedium, but you still need a plan. Here are some practical tips:
- Use the included holders for encounter cards and the main deck. Keep objective and equipment cards in a smaller binder or a separate box. The separation reduces rummaging time and helps you explain rules without the chorus of is this the right deck?
- Label everything. Even if you think you’ll remember, five minutes into the game you’ll forget that Loot A belongs to the southern fortress or whatever. Small sticky labels or erasable markers can save you a lot of squinting and guessing.
- Consider sleeves if you’re using a lot of cards. Sleeves help preserve cards from players who like to shuffle aggressively or flip past a deck like it’s a trading-card duel. Just ensure the sleeves aren’t so thick that your holders won’t close.
- Create a quick setup card. A laminated sheet that shows where each deck and holder goes can shave minutes off setup time and reduce the assembly line chaos that sometimes happens when the box is left open during a multi-day campaign.
