## Artwork and Artist Spotlight

Keiji Kinebuchi is a name that might not be as famous as the big cover artists, but in the realm of early Pokemon card art, his work on Gloom stands as a little jewel. The 1996 print era had artists who were still figuring out how far you could push the pencil before a kid with a wallet could ruin your day by buying the entire print run. Kinebuchi’s Gloom is not about dynamic action; it’s about a mood. The droopy petals around Gloom’s head resemble a living1960s poster vibe, a soft caricature rather than a hyper-realistic creature. The pose is deliberately static, which works perfectly for a card whose stat line demands you lean into defense and patience rather than blitz and bravado.

What makes this Gloom pop aesthetically is the interplay of soft shading with sharper line work along the edges of its petals. It’s a design that invites light to bounce around the card’s surface, creating a sense of depth that isn’t necessarily there in a single glance. If you tilt the card just right in a binder light, you’ll notice the subtle glints along the outline—little details that remind you someone cared about the texture, not just the silhouette.

For fans of the artist, this card is a little trophy: a reminder that early Pokemon art was often more about quirky personality than hyper-polished realism. It’s the kind of card you’d display on a shelf with a tiny fan lamp aimed at it, so the Gloom’s face smiles at you in the late afternoon light and you pretend you’re a collector who values feelings over forging the next tournament win.

## Card Text and In-Game Implications

Back in 1996, the Pokemon TCG was a relatively new arena where players learned to balance risk with reward. Gloom, with HP 60, sits in the early midrange of the grass-type spectrum. Its moves are simple by modern standards, but they offer a nostalgic reminder of card design philosophy: keep it lean, keep it reliable, and never forget the aroma jokes.

The practical takeaway for gameplay is simple: a HP 60 padding means you’re not going to burn a hole in your opponent’s plan with raw numbers. It’s a card that asks you to use patience, to build a deck around gradually whittling your opponent down while Gloom supplies a reliable front-line presence. The lack of heavy power is not a deficiency in the context of its time; it reflects the era when the game emphasized strategy, resource management, and the fun of collecting more than the thrill of one-shot kOs.

If you are a purist who collects for historical balance, this print is a wonderful reminder of how a card can be both a collectible object and a playable piece, depending on your mood and your playgroup. It’s not the kind of card you would slam into a modern competitive deck and expect to top a tournament, but it is the kind of card that yields after-action glory stories when you recount your first loss due to a misplayed energy attachment and still smile because it happened in the best possible way—with a card whose art could charm your grandmother.

## Collectibility and Market Presence

In the world of Pokemon card collecting, 1996 Japanese prints hold a special place. The early print runs, particularly the non holo base prints, are cherished more for their nostalgia than for raw investment yield. Gloom No 044 sits in that category: widely accessible to collectors who want a first-gen Japanese piece without breaking the bank. Condition matters, of course. A near-mint copy with clean edges and crisp colors can fetch a respectable price in the collector scene, whereas a well-loved copy with edge wear or surface scratches will be more of a charming display item than a serious investment.

To many collectors, the value isn’t strictly monetary. It’s about the thrill of owning a small artifact from an era when the card game was still a curious new thing in toy aisles across Japan. The 1996 Japanese Gloom serves as a tangible reminder that the franchise began with a handful of cards that felt less like a casino of competitive power and more like a mysterious hobby that could spark a lifelong obsession.

Pricing is, of course, baselined by condition and market demand. As of now, a clean, ungraded copy might fetch a price that ranges in the low tens to the low hundreds depending on the collector’s market at the time and the overall reputation of the seller. A heavily played card, while not worthless, appeals more to players who want a budget piece for a nostalgic deck rather than to pure collectors chasing perfect corners. Grading can elevate a card’s value, but the 1996 Japanese print rarely attains the same kind of top-tier value you see on holo or rare first edition prints. Still, a well-preserved No 044 Gloom is a badge of honor for any display case and a conversation starter at card meetups.

If you’re new to the scene, here’s a quick guide to estimating value without hiring a professional evaluator: check the corners for whitening, assess surface gloss, scan for crease lines, and consider the rarity of the edition. Japanese base set prints often have a distinctive gloss and color saturation that can help identify whether a card has lived a gentle life in a binder or has endured some rough handling. The Gloom No 044 will usually show minor signs of wear if it has been played, but in many collections, even a lightly played example maintains a certain vintage charm that modern reprints simply can’t replicate.

## Design Variations and Collectors' Notes

One of the fun aspects of early Pokemon cards is the subtle differences that exist between language editions and print runs. The 1996 Japanese Gloom is the product of a time when design iterations were iterative, not revolutionary. While the English base set quickly moved toward holo variants and new frame treatments, the Japanese prints stuck closer to straightforward, unembellished layouts that highlight the artwork and the card’s silhouette. The No 044 Gloom has a calm, stable feel—a card that looks good under a desk lamp and even better in a proper display case.

Versus later Gloom prints, the 1996 edition lacks modern touches like energy cost text blocks that overshadow the art. It’s a reminder that the game was still in its infancy, experimenting with how to balance readability, aesthetics, and playability. Some collectors enjoy chasing small differences such as the exact shade of green used in the grass type icon or the font used for the card name. These tiny differences are often what separate a casual dip into nostalgia from a serious, year-long quest for a complete Japanese base set collection.

## Display, Storage, and Preservation Tips

If you plan to keep this Gloom for decades, here are some practical tips that can help preserve both the art and its value:

- Use soft-sleeves and a rigid top loader when stacking or displaying.
- Keep away from direct sunlight to prevent color fade and yellowing of the card stock.
- Store in a cool, dry place to minimize moisture damage and odor buildup in the room where you display it.
- Consider a binder page designed for standard size trading cards to organize with other Japanese base set prints for a tasteful display.
- If you want to compare with later reprints, keep a separate, clearly labeled set for those comparisons to avoid confusion.
