## The missing cards conundrum
Missing cards are not rare in the wild world of board games, but they feel personal when you’ve bought a brand-new Adventures Edition and your box refuses to behave like a complete, well-oiled machine. The typical culprits fall into a few buckets:

- Print-run quirks: Sometimes a subset of cards is slotted into the wrong deck or mispacked into the wrong box. The result: a complete board with a partial life story.
- Packaging mistakes: A card might be stuffed into a different sleeve, or even worse, tucked under a spacer that makes you think it’s part of the rulebook rather than a card you actually need to play.
- Used-copy realities: If you’re buying secondhand, missing cards are a common reality. The cards you expect might be out of the box, left at a friend’s house, or simply replaced by a post-apocalyptic version thrown into the heap by a game-night deity who enjoys chaos.
- Variant print runs: In some markets, the Adventures deck you get may differ slightly from the one pictured on the box. If you’re hunting for a complete experience, that mismatch can feel like a cosmic joke.

What’s at stake when you’re missing cards is not just “the experience” — it’s the pacing, the risk-reward cadence, and the narrative arc you hoped to ride. Adventure Cards are the lifeblood of the edition; remove them and you’ve got a hearty dinner without dessert: still tasty, but where’s the spark?

## How to check if your copy is complete
If you suspect you’re missing content, the following steps give you a clear, structured way to verify and decide your next move.

- Count and compare: A quick deck audit will tell you how many Adventure, Event, and Treasure Cards you’re supposed to have. If one deck is markedly short, that’s a red flag.
- Inspect the backs and borders: Incomplete decks often reveal a mismatch in art style or card back design, hinting at a misprint or mispack. If the backs don’t match across decks, you’ve got a clue to follow up with the publisher.
- Check for near-identical substitutes: Some cards have lookalikes from other expansions or earlier print runs. If you’re thinking, That card exists but with different art, you may be looking at a variant; you’re not crazy, you’re a collector. But you’ll want to confirm.
- Reference images from credible sources: The official Hasbro product page and fan-compiled references like BoardGameGeek’s gallery offer a visual checklist. If your deck is missing a card in a category, you’ll likely feel it when you compare to these references.
- Confirm packaging and SKU: If you bought new, the SKU on the box should line up with the published listing. If you bought used, ask for a photo of the contents list printed on the inside of the lid or the bottom of the box. A photo beats a memory any day of the week.

If your checks reveal a genuine discrepancy, you’re not alone. Our reader community is dotted with stories where someone found themselves short one or two cards from the Adventures, but with a little patience and a few replacements, the experience remained joyful. The key is to stay proactive and not let missing cards derail your game-night energy.

## Replacement options and how to proceed
Here are the practical routes you can take to fix the missing-card issue. Pick the path that fits your vibe: official, community-driven, or DIY.

- Official replacement route: Hasbro’s customer-care channels are typically the most reliable route. You’ll want to gather your purchase proof, box contents list, and product code. Submit a replacement request for the missing cards. The upside is you’ll likely receive exact-match cards that fit the deck perfectly and restore your planned game flow. The downside is sometimes the process takes a little longer than a casual game-night session, so you’ll need to plan accordingly.
- Community-driven replacements: The board-game community is full of generous folks who swap, sell, or share printable PDFs of missing cards. If you go this route, check the credibility of the source and ensure that the artwork matches the edition you own. This approach can be faster than official channels, especially if Hasbro has backorders or stockouts. Always prioritize the original art for a consistent aesthetic.
- DIY proxies and homebrew: If you’re into home-rule creativity, you can craft your own proxies for the missing cards. If you go this route, keep the proxies clearly labeled and separate from the official deck to avoid legal/ethical issues or confusion in the middle of a game-night. A little note card explaining that a card is a homebrew addition can save a lot of headaches.
- Digital or revised print runs: Some publishers release digital companions or updated print runs to replace missing components. While this is not common for every edition, it’s a possibility worth checking. If you see a digital or mixed-media option that includes a replacement deck, it can be a good compromise—especially if you’re more comfortable with digital assets.

In all cases, communication is key. If you choose to contact Hasbro, be precise but polite. If you go to the community forums, be constructive. A positive tone will often yield faster and more helpful responses from fellow players who have dealt with the same issue.

## The VGC angle: what does Very Good Condition have to do with it?
In the hobby market, VGC stands for Very Good Condition. It’s a useful descriptor when you’re buying or selling used copies. If you’re evaluating a used Adventures Edition with a handful of missing cards, you’ll want to note what is present and what is absent, and annotate the card count publicly. Missing cards can dramatically reduce value, but if the rest of the components are pristine, a buyer who’s willing to DIY replacements may still find a good deal. The practical takeaway: if you’re chasing a complete pristine set, you might want to upgrade to a newer print run or seek a seller who can confirm a complete deck before you hand over the cash. If you’re more flexible, a well-maintained rest of the components can still deliver a game-night winner, even if the card count isn’t perfect.
