## Setup and compatibility

### Getting started with a used unit
The setup for a used HP TB4 G4 dock is mostly plug-and-play, with a few caveats. First, ensure you have a TB4-capable laptop or a dock-compatible solution (some Macs can be fussy about TB4 docks even though support exists). Connect the dock to your laptop with the included TB4 cable, then connect your displays to the appropriate ports, attach USB peripherals, and boot up. If everything is in good shape, you’ll be rewarded with a desktop-like experience with a single cable—provided you’ve got a monitor setup that your laptop agrees to support.

Compatibility is a mixed bag on older devices. Windows generally plays nicely with TB4 docks, offering hot-swappable USB devices and reliable ethernet when the drivers cooperate. macOS tends to do well with docks that present DisplayPort or HDMI output cleanly and don’t demand a coffee-augmented ritual to get the drivers right. If your laptop is from 2020 and supports TB4, you’ll probably be fine. If you have a curious “MacBook Pro from the stone age” and a wall of adapters, you may need to tinker a bit with display arrangement and color profiles. The important thing is to verify the up-to-date driver package from HP or the laptop manufacturer—because even the best docks sometimes require a quiet moment with a firmware update or a restart.

### First boot and test plan
A practical test plan includes:
- Confirming power delivery by plugging in a laptop and watching the battery indicator. A good dock should charge your device without needing a separate charger, or at least provide a robust pass-through with a little headroom for battery-hungry machines.
- Extending or mirroring displays across multiple ports. If you have two external monitors, check that both are recognized and aligned as you expect. If you’re into aggressive multitasking, test with a third party USB-C monitor or a USB-C docked monitor to verify the bandwidth remains sane.
- Testing USB peripherals: keyboard, mouse, a USB audio adapter, and a fast USB drive. If you run a lot of external peripherals, you’ll want at least a couple of USB-A 3.0 ports with enough bandwidth to handle data-intensive devices.
- Network reliability: wired Ethernet is a big win for remote work and gaming; test download speeds and latency to ensure the dock isn’t bottlenecking your connection.
