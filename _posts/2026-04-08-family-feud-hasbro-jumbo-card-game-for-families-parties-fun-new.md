## How to Play: Setup, Turn Structure, and Timing
Let’s get to the meat and potatoes, or, if you want a more culinary metaphor, let’s get to the chips and salsa of this experience. The game is designed to be accessible to newcomers while still offering something for the die-hard party gamer who loves to shout answers before the “survey says” drumbeat finishes.

### Setup
- Split players into two or more teams. The exact number doesn’t matter as long as you can assign at least a captain on each side who won’t forget the rule where you may not shout the answer aloud before a prompt is revealed.
- Shuffle the jumbo cards and place them face-down within reach of all players. A central area should exist for the timer, pens, and any snacks that will inevitably become part of the gameplay’s other half—snack diplomacy.
- Prepare the score sheets and choose a first captain. The captain will be the one who reads the question aloud (or at least attempts to pronounce the question without tripping over their own enthusiasm).

### The core mechanic
Each round, the designated captain draws a card and reads the prompt aloud to their team. Think along the lines of “Name a movie character who would not complain about a long taxi ride,” or “Name something you’d find in a geek’s backpack.” The opposing team then shouts or writes down a guess that could plausibly be in the top five surveyed responses. The card deck includes multiple prompts, each designed to elicit a variety of common or culturally recognizable answers. The goal is to collect points based on how many people in the survey would have given that answer.

The tension comes from timing. The timer is used to pace responses, which is essential. It’s not about who is the fastest; it’s about who can combine boldness with a plausible answer. That delicate balance is where the party magic happens. If you’re dealing with a rowdy group, the timer becomes a kind of referee with a sense of humor, blowing a tiny whistle in the back of your brain every time someone blurts out something so outrageous that the crowd roars with laughter.

### Validating answers
Once an answer is declared, the captain checks the board against the top survey responses listed on the card. If the answer matches one of the top answers, the team earns points equal to the number of people who gave that answer in the survey. If not, play passes and the opposing team gets a shot. It can feel a little like a game show, except the studio audience is your family’s living room and the prize is either bragging rights, a plate of leftovers, or a chorus of groans when the final scores are tallied.

### Scoring and rounds
The scoring is straightforward, designed to avoid math phobias. A few good rounds might swing the game into a light, joyous triumph for your chosen team. A few bad rounds might result in heated discussions about whether “chair” is a valid top five answer for “Name something you sit on during a long conference call.” The beauty of it is that even misreads and miscommunications become part of the entertainment—the kind of chaos that makes communal laughter feel like an indulgence rather than a burden.

### Endgame and victory
After a pre-determined number of rounds or a target score, the game crowns a winner. The celebration is often more about the shared experience rather than the trophy. There’s something heartwarming about seeing family members high-five when a surprisingly correct answer lands on the board, or watching cousins do a ridiculous victory dance that somehow qualifies as a family tradition. These moments are what keep people coming back for more, even if they vowed to never again mention their aunt’s “unique” interpretation of the word “bucket.”
