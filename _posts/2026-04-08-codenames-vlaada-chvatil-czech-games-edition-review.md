## How to play in a nutshell

Codenames is played in teams, typically red and blue, with one spymaster per team and a squad of field operatives. The spymaster looks at a key card that designates which words on the board are red agents, which are blue agents, which are bystanders, and which single word is the assassin. The spymaster then gives a single word clue followed by a number. The clue word should connect to several of the target words on the board; the number tells how many words on the board relate to that clue. The operatives discuss and pick the words they think match the clue, stepping closer to victory as they identify their agents. The challenge is to avoid selecting the opposing team’s words, the dangerous bystanders, and especially the assassin. If a team hits the assassin, they immediately lose the round. If they identify all their agents first, they win. If they hit a bystander, their turn ends with the reveal of a non agent word, and the other team gets a chance to steal the round. It is a simple mechanism that scales with the people present and the creativity of your clues.

The core thrill comes from the tension of guessing. You can feel the group shift when a clue starts to click or when a clue that seems elegant turns into a misfire and a cascade of bad choices. The balance between a great clue that connects multiple words and a vague clue that leaves everyone arguing for minutes is what makes Codenames replayable and, frankly, endlessly entertaining in the right circle.

## How to host a Codenames night

Hosting is where Codenames shines. Here are practical tips from our experience at Geeknite:

- Pick a capable spymaster for each team. A good spymaster is more important than a brilliant guesser because a poor clue can derail an entire round.
- Create a word grid in a well-lit area where everyone can see the words clearly. This eliminates misreadings and arguments about letter shapes.
- Encourage playful misdirection without cruelty. The best clues entice connection without becoming personal attacks on a teammate. The goal is shared laughter, not social warfare.
- Start with a standard 25 card grid and a single assassin. Once players are comfortable, consider adding a second assassin for a harsh but entertaining twist in larger groups.
- Maintain a fast pace. The longer a round drags on, the more the tension drains and the more people lose track of their vocabulary. Keep rounds snappy and energetic.
