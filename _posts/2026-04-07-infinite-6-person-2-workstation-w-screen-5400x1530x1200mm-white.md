Hidden within the two major zones are privacy panels that reach monitor height, delivering a feeling of personal space without isolating teams. From a design perspective, the white color and the long lines give the desk a sense of calm and focus—perfect for teams that want to appear organized even when their digital hoarding says otherwise. ## Build quality and assembly
When you’re dealing with a 5.4-meter length desk designed to seat six people, assembly is no small feat. It’s not furniture that you haul home in a sedan; imagine two refrigerator-sized boxes, maybe three, and a lot of instruction pages that read like a treasure map. The manual is straightforward if you read it aloud in a pirate voice; otherwise, it’s a well-organized set of steps. The key is to have a second person, ideally a friend who understands the concept of “don’t drop the crossbar near the cat.”

Two main assembly phases emerge. Phase one is the frame: you align the long rails with the end caps, bolt the crossbars in, and thread the power channel along the underside. Phase two is the top: you lay down the white surface, apply edge trim, and attach the privacy panels. It’s heavy, precise work, but the pieces click together with the kind of satisfying feel you only get from a model kit that’s designed to outlast your keyboard habit.

One helpful tip: start at one end and move in a straight line; stray off path and the long edges will remind you why metric measurements exist. If you’re using it in a busy space, allow yourself a long afternoon; it’s not a weekend project, but it’s the kind of furniture upgrade that makes your team feel like they’ve moved into a real office instead of a coworking club.

## Ergonomics and usability
A six-person workstation has two fundamental design constraints: legroom and the proper height relative to chairs. The Infinite 6 Person provides substantial legroom with a 5400 mm main surface and a standard 70-75 cm seat-to-table ratio when you account for the two printed privacy panels acting as subtle barriers rather than brick walls between seats. I tested with a mix of mid-range chairs (think ergonomic, not plush thrones) and found the arrangement comfortable for sessions that lasted well into the afternoon.

With privacy screens, you also gain improved focus. The screens themselves add a sense of personal space that reduces the urge to test colleagues' patience with loud keyboard clacks. The desk surface tolerates notebooks, external keyboards, and a healthy amount of coffee mugs. The cable management trough is more than a hobbyist’s dream; it can swallow power strips and docking stations with room to spare for future USB-C PD hubs. The water bottle test—when you knock your bottle and spill a tiny geyser of water across the trough—sends a helpful reminder to secure your drinks.

We also considered accessibility. The desk height is compatible with most office chairs, but the sheer width means you’ll want a rolling chair that can glide from one zone to another without fighting the edge of the surface. If you’ve got people who need frequent access to the screen between workstations, you’ll appreciate the ability to slide the privacy panels aside or reposition the monitor arms that attach to the frame. It’s not a one-size-fits-all vibe; it’s a modular, community-first approach to desk design.

### Monitor arms and peripherals
The two work zones support standard dual-monitor setups. If you’re planning to push the limits, you’ll want to invest in monitor arms with good weight ratings. For keyboards and mice, a central mat helps unify the area and reduce wrist strain across the whole line of seats. A shared printer corner or a whiteboard stand can live near the end of the desk if you want to maximize collaboration without creating a cluttered center mass.
