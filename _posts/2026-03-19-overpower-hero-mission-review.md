## Contents, Condition, and Sorting the Gold from the Gum
That 75+ estimate can cover a wide range of actual contents. In practice, expect a mix that includes:
- A handful of rarer or more visually striking hero/villain cards from the Hero Mission wave
- Plenty of common-to-uncommon cards that are perfect for budget trades or filling out a deck
- A smaller subset of potentially “hot” cards that collectors covet for their art or nostalgia factor

Condition is everything in this space. The 1990s print runs weren’t exactly rigorously sealed in protective stages, and many players kept cards in mixed conditions: some pristine, some glossy but with edge wear, some that show signs of humidity or storage in less-than-perfect climate conditions. Here’s how to sort effectively:
- Examine corners: rounded corners aren’t a deal-breaker for casual play, but if you’re planning to grade, pristine corners are the gold standard.
- Check surface gloss: older cards often have micro-scratches or surface gloss wear—this doesn’t doom a lot, but it affects grading potential.
- Look for physical damage: creases, bends, or water stains can be deal-breakers for high-grade pieces but might still be acceptable for a bulk collection intended for play or display.

Sorting strategies:
- Group by hero/villain and then by rarity if you can identify it. Odds are some cards will be difficult to classify without a collector’s guide, but you’ll learn a lot just by comparing art and text cues.
- Separate event/power-up cards from character cards. The latter are typically what players use to construct a deck, while the former often act as round-defining gadgets that spice battles.
- Keep a “potential trade” pile for cards that you suspect others might seek, either for play or display value.

Remember: condition is king when it comes to value, but for a 75+ card lot that’s primarily about collection or casual playing, the vibe and completeness frequently trump perfect archival status. You can still extract substantial enjoyment and value by organizing the pile into neat stacks, sleeve-protecting the most-visible figures, and setting aside any obvious misprints you might encounter.

## Storage, Care, and Display: Keeping the Magic Fresh
Card collecting is as much about storage as it is about discovery. For a 75+ OverPower Hero Mission batch, you’ll want to optimize both use and preservation:
- Sleeving: Use semi-rigid sleeves or standard card sleeves for display purposes. Consider in-binders with divider tabs for hero-vs-villain categories. This makes it easier to flip through the collection without creating glare on the shiny surfaces.
- Sleeved-in-boarding: Place sleeved cards in a slim binder or in archival-safe toploaders if you want to present the collection as a feature piece at a convention or a meet-up.
- Protection: Keep away from direct sunlight and humidity. Plastic storage boxes with silica gel packs can help maintain dryness and prevent warping or color fade. If you do plan to keep them in a box under a bed, consider using desiccant packs and a moisture indicator to monitor the environment.
- Organization tips: Create a simple catalog or spreadsheet noting the card name, rarity (if you can identify it), approximate year, and your condition estimate. A little database work makes people far more likely to trade and helps you track potential flips or sales later on.

Display options exist as a spectrum—from a simple binder of hero cards to a table-top diorama of a faux “superhero skirmish” using a few stand-in miniatures. If you’re into photography or video review, setting up a small staged scene with a couple of hero and villain cards can yield crisp content for social channels and your blog, delivering both nostalgia and aesthetic that resonates with friends who collect or just enjoy Marvel’s visual language.
