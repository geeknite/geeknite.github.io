## Colorblind accessibility in practice: how readable is readable enough?

Colorblind-friendly design is more than bold hues; it’s about readability and differentiating cards at a glance. In our extended testing, numerals are large and legible, and color blocks are sharply defined. Some cards feature subtle back patterns as a third differentiator for those with common color-vision challenges, reducing confusion when multiple players hold similar palettes. Action cards (Skip, Reverse, Draw Two) rely on clear symbols and shapes as quick cues. Wild cards sport a distinct icon or border to help colorblind players spot them quickly in a crowd.

That said, no product is perfect for all color vision types. Some players still lean on verbal reminders or a quick glance at a neighbor’s hand to confirm what they’re reading. The designers lean into UNO’s signature chaos while preserving legibility for critical information. The larger real estate can complicate shuffling, and the table presence is a major factor in social pacing. We tested both a casual family night and a longer, more strategic session to see how the colorblind-friendly features hold up under different play styles.

UNO is flexible, and the giant deck invites even more room for experimentation. We tried a few house rules to tailor the chaos:
