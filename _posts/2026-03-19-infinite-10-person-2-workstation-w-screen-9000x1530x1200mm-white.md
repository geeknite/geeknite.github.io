Specs at a glance
This mammoth is listed with dimensions you can measure a small planet by: 9000 mm in length, 1530 mm in width, and 1200 mm in height. That translates roughly to a 9-meter long island of productivity that can be split into two modules, each designed to cradle five seats with a shared central media spine. The colors are strictly white, with a matte finish that resists fingerprints but not personality. In practice, you won’t hide this under a cloth when the boss visits; you’ll want to show it off like a trophy, preferably after you’ve learned to hide the mess behind the heavy guillotine of a back cover.

The W Screen system is the star of the show. No need to micromanage monitors on each desk; the central spine hosts a continuous display rail that sweeps along the length of the workstation. It’s not a single giant monitor, but a modular array of screens and projection surfaces that can be configured to display code, dashboards, design boards, or cinematic team introductions. It can be configured as a large shared canvas or as separate face time panels for each group. The W Screen is quiet, low-friction, and surprisingly forgiving of wall-warts and coffee stains—though you should still treat it like a delicate instrument rather than a chew toy for your desk plant.

The engineering is a blend of industrial and thoughtful. The frame is powder-coated steel with rigid cross-bracing to resist the kind of flex you would expect from a 9-meter loom of furniture. The work surfaces are high-density melamine with a subtle wipe-down finish, edge-banded to keep your wrists happy and your interruptions to a minimum. There’s cable management aplenty: a top rail for power modules, trunking paths under the surface, and floor channels that pretend to be tidy while hosting a small ecosystem of chargers and dongles. The whole thing weighs enough to deter accidental rearrangements but not so heavy that you’ll need a small crane to move it around.

Unboxing and assembly: the reality vs the brochure
If you crave the “aha” moment when you slice through boxes and reveal the magic, the Infinite workstation delivers, but with caveats. The two modules arrive in heavy, carefully labeled crates that scream quality and slightly threaten your back if you try to lift alone. The assembly manual is well-illustrated and written with a dash of humor that is appreciated in a long weekend furniture project. Expect a two-person job for the initial module and a second crew to join for the final alignment and cable routing. The full assembly time will depend on your space, your tool kit, and whether you’ve pre-cleaned the room of rogue desk plants. In a typical office, plan for a half-day to a full day if you’re on a tight timeline and you want the wiring to be buttoned up rather than “approximately tidy.”

The two-module approach is the right idea here. It makes handling easier and gives you the option to reconfigure into a different floor plan if your team expands or contracts. The joints between modules are designed to look clean after installation, but you’ll want to run a flat-edge ruler along the seam to confirm there’s no lift or twist. The finish on the white surfaces is not a miracle—it will show scuffs if you aggressively relocate furniture with bare hands—but it feels durable enough to survive the daily rigors of a busy office.

Ergonomics, comfort, and daily use
Ergonomics is where the Infinite system kind of earns its keep. The bench’s long depth, generous legroom, and adjustable monitor height options on the central W Screen rail let you tailor the setup for a room of ten different body types. The chairs are not included, which is a shame if your HR department insists on a matched set of chairs that exude both ergonomics and style. However, the desk heights and the central display system are designed with typical office chairs in mind, which makes the seating plan work with standard furniture. If you’re a hardware hacker who loves to tinker, you’ll be pleased by the modularity allowing you to adjust the spacing between seats in real time as your team grows or shrinks.

Most teams will appreciate the under-surface clearance. The middle console provides a discreet channel for power cables, USB-C passes, and the occasional Ethernet cable, should you still run a local network rather than cloud-based workflows. The integrated power rails deliver a reasonable amount of grunt for typical office devices. You’ll still need to add a separate IT closet for server hardware or high-wattage displays, but you’ll be thankful the main desks aren’t fighting for power in a crowded room.

Screen integration and media spine

It supports a mix of display sizes, from wide panels for team dashboards to smaller, touch-friendly surfaces for quick prototyping in stand-up sessions. In practice, you can route the team’s dashboards to the full-length display while privately assigned screens at each seat can show personal tasks or chat windows. The result is a balanced workflow: a shared reality for the meeting and a personal reality for the tasks. For large-scale workshops or hackathons, the screen rail can display a shared agenda, live code, or a whiteboard capture—depending on the app you’re using, your Wi-Fi coverage, and the patience of your IT department.

Maintenance, care, and durability
Like any good sci-fi hero, the Infinite workstation requires care and a sense of ritual. Cleaning the white melamine surface with a mild microfiber cloth is enough for day-to-day maintenance. Avoid abrasive cleaners that can dull the finish or leave streaks. The frame’s powder coat is durable, but it’s not invincible—if you intend to treat it like a gladiator arena, you’ll want to keep a protective barrier around the most physically active zones. Cable channels should be checked periodically for wear and tear; a small snag can turn a neat cable-tray into a tangle you’ll regret on a Monday morning.

Two words of caution: geometry and space. The 9-meter footprint means you’ll need a suitable room to install it. If your workplace is a boutique space or a former meeting room that hasn’t seen a proper furniture audit in years, moving the unit into position could require careful planning, a couple of floor mats, and maybe one of those fancy felt pads to protect your new floor. If you’re dealing with a ceiling grid, you’ll also need to verify clearance for any overhead lighting or sprinklers. In short, you’re buying furniture that acts like a portable office, not a nook for casual coffee breaks.

Setup checklist and quick-start guide
Pre-installation survey: measure entryways, door widths, and floor load. Confirm you have a path from the loading dock to the installation site that can accommodate the 700–750 kg total weight with all accessories.
Tools and crew: you’ll want at least two strong teammates, a level, a screwdriver kit, and a cable-tie management kit. Bring a helper with a good memory for labels.
Space planning: determine whether you want a single long line or a mirrored layout facing a central screen. Check sightlines and ensure the central display is visible from all seats.
Post-installation testing: run a quick power-on test, check cable routing for pinch points, and ensure the screen spine responds to driver changes. Do a test meeting to verify legroom and comfort for at least three hours.
