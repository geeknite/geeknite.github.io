Introduction
------------
If you ever wanted to turn your sacred couch into a flying fortress of productivity, the LG Gram +View 3rd Gen Plus View Portable Monitor 17MT70 is here to grant your wish with a faint whirr of a fan and a dramatic sigh of a thousand tabs. In Geeknite fashion, I approached this sub-monitor the way I approach a new game expansion: with caffeine, skepticism, and a willingness to pretend I need one more screen so I can finally be blah blah. The 17MT70 is pitched as a companion for the Gram 17, a monitor that aims to disappear into your workflow so you can pretend you only own one laptop even when you own multiple coffee mugs that all say productivity on them.

What you get in the box is not just a screen on a stick. You get a modular second display that can live on the desk or fold into a slim profile for travel. The form factor matters here because the whole point of a portable monitor is to feel like you are transporting a superpower rather than a gadget that will be forgotten under a pile of cables. The 17MT70 tries to balance two opposing vibes: a sturdy, professional panel and the kind of featherweight design that makes you look cool while commuting to the coworking space that doubles as a gym.

Unboxing and first impressions
-------------------------------
The packaging is minimal but thoughtful. You get the monitor panel, an adjustable stand, a USB-C cable, a power dongle for when the laptop cannot provide enough juice, and a neat little sleeve that makes you feel like you are opening a premium pastry box rather than a tech accessory. The build quality is where LG typically shines: rigidity without the rattle, a hinge that feels precise, and a finish that resists fingerprints like a tiny force field. The chassis is light enough to not feel like a medieval shield but sturdy enough to survive the occasional desk dive during a frantic productivity sprint.

Design and ergonomics
---------------------
The 17MT70 is a 17-inch beast with the sort of presence you expect from a Gram accessory. It tucks into your setup with a slim profile and a smart stand that allows tilt adjustments for comfort during long code sprints or spreadsheet benders. The bezels are slim but not aggressively slim, which is fine because we know what happens when bezels go on a diet: they start flirting with the pixel grid, and no one wants a monitor with a diet plan that fails at 4K. The color accuracy is decent out of the box, with enough saturation to keep photos vibrant and enough neutrality to keep documents readable. It is not a professional color workstation, but it does not pretend to be one either. If you live and die by color-critical work, you will likely calibrate the display with a colorimeter, which is same old story: you get what you need, not what you wish for.

An important note for Gram users: the 17MT70 is designed to play nicely with the Gram 17's internal ecosystem. The second display slots into your workflow without stealing the spotlight. The hardware is compatible with USB-C power delivery and display, which means you can power both the laptop and the monitor from a single charger in many scenarios. In practice, this makes the setup cleaner than your average desk clutter, and cleaner desks are a gateway drug to more focused code writing, or so the myth goes.

Display and performance
-----------------------
The 17MT70 uses an IPS panel with decent viewing angles and a brightness that is usable in a sunny apartment, which is the modern equivalent of a battlefield win. Colors pop enough for casual content creation and light photo editing, while text remains sharp enough for long reading sessions. The 60 Hz refresh rate is standard for portable monitors in this category, which means you should not expect heroic gaming frames. If you are a gamer who measures success by frame rate rather than by the number of tabs you can juggle, you may consider this as a secondary display for light indie titles rather than a primary gaming monitor. It is absolutely usable for gaming, just not the kind that shoots for eSports glory.

Color accuracy: a practical baseline. If you are a Photoshop ninja or a Lightroom enthusiast, you will want to run your own color calibration. But for 99% of productivity tasks, the 17MT70 offers a clean image with respectable contrast. The anti-glare coating is a nice touch for coffee shop usage, reducing the glare from that one neon sign that seems to be casting a cosmic glare on your tabs. Watching videos is comfortable, with sufficient brightness and decent handling of color shifts when you move around the screen while multitasking.

Connectivity and power
----------------------
Ports matter, especially when you want to keep your life cable-free. The 17MT70 leans into USB-C for both power and video, which means you can run a tidy setup from a single USB-C-Cable solution in many scenarios. There is also a power passthrough option to keep the monitor running when you demand the Gram to push through long days of spreadsheet sorcery or late-night meme reviews. The setup usually goes like this: connect the USB-C from the Gram, select the monitor as a secondary display in your OS, and you are ready to go. No drama, no drama, just productivity with a dash of neon keyboard glow in the dark desk shot you will inevitably post to a late-night forum.

One caveat here is that if your Gram is not providing enough power, you may need to rely on the included power dongle. This is not a failure of the monitor but a reminder that power is a finite resource, and even the best second screen is a river that must be fed. In practice, during typical office usage with the Gram connected, the dongle is rarely needed, and you can keep cable clutter to a level that would make Swiss engineers proud.

Setup and daily usage
---------------------
Pairing the 17MT70 with a Gram 17 is straightforward. You connect via USB-C, ensure the display is selected, and you are in business. The OS-level recognition is smooth on Windows and macOS alike, though the latter is not officially supported by LG on every portable monitor. In geek terms, you get plug-and-play with a friendly interface and a responsive on-screen display for fine-tuning brightness, contrast, and color balance. The included stand is sturdy enough for desk use, and if you want to go more ergonomic, you can set the monitor at a comfortable height and tilt. The goal is to create a dual-display environment that does not feel like a compromise but rather a well-tuned machine with extra mental real estate.

Usage scenarios
----------------
Workstation mode: You are not just a person with a laptop; you are a person with a mission. The 17MT70 gives you extra screen real estate for research, writing, or coding. It is ideal for documentation on one screen and code on the other, or for tab managing in your favorite browser while a word processor stares at your face with judgment.

Gaming and entertainment: Don’t expect triple-A gaming glory, but you can enjoy light, casual titles or streaming while you work. The 60 Hz refresh rate is enough for casual play, though you will feel the difference if you try to push for high frame rates in fast-action titles. It is not designed to be your primary gaming rig; think second screen with a fun side quest to keep you company during long compile times.

Coding and multitasking: This is where the LG monitor shines. For a coder who enjoys splitting a screen into panels for syntax, docs, and a live preview, the 17MT70 becomes your sidekick. It is especially useful when you want to keep references and notes visible while you type on the Gram. The increased workspace reduces the cognitive load that comes with tab-juggling and window-snapping. If you are a terminal whisperer who likes to see logs while you write, this is your product.

OS compatibility and driver support
----------------------------------
Windows users will likely be happier due to robust hot-plug support and straightforward display settings. macOS users might need a tad more fiddling, especially if using target display mode or fancy color profiles, but the end result is workable. No dramatic driver drama, just a few small adjustments in display settings to ensure your color profiles look honest and not angry.

Display tuning and color management
-----------------------------------
The on-screen display menu is accessible and responsive, allowing you to tweak brightness, contrast, and color temperature. If you have a color-critical job, a quick calibration with a colorimeter can yield better results. The default color profile is fine for most everyday tasks, with enough warmth and clarity to keep long sessions from turning into a grayscale nightmare. If you enjoy warm visuals, you can push toward warmer tones; if you are more of a cool-tech person, you can lean into cooler whites. The middle ground is perfectly acceptable for working hours, and that is what most people will value in a portable monitor.

Comparisons
-----------
Against other 17 inch portable monitors, the 17MT70 stacks up well in terms of portability and ease of use. It is not the brightest, the most color-accurate, or the fastest at response times, but it strikes a favorable balance for folks who want a second screen that feels like a natural extension of their Gram laptop rather than a separate gadget with its own ecosystem. When placed beside non-LG portable monitors in the same price bracket, the 17MT70 tends to win on weight and build quality, loses a bit on color calibration, and wins again in overall usability due to polished software and seamless USB-C connectivity.

Value and pricing
------------------
Pricing for portable monitors fluctuates based on sales, region, and whether LG runs a promotion featuring a free stand or a bundled USB-C cable. The 17MT70 sits in a sweet spot for people who value portability but still want a large screen to work on. If your workflow relies on a second screen daily and you move between coffee shops and office desks, the value proposition becomes compelling. The monitor earns its keep by boosting your productivity, not by pretending to be the main event. It is a modest investment for a meaningful upgrade to your desk setup.

Tips and tricks
----------------
- Use the monitor with a single USB-C cable when possible to minimize clutter.
- Calibrate color if you need accuracy for design tasks, but don’t expect a pro-grade color workflow without an external professional display.
- Keep a dedicated stand or a travel case to protect the screen during transport.
- If you experience a faint PWM flicker, try increasing brightness and enabling the OS eye care features to reduce perceived flicker during long work sessions.
