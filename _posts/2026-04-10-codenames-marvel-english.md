## How to play in a sprint (the quick version)

If you already know Codenames, you’ll glide through Marvel without major detours. If you’re new to Codenames, you’ll feel like you’ve crashed into a galaxy where every word wants to be your friend, even if it turns out to be a villain in disguise. Here’s the rough skeleton:

1) Set up the board: lay out 25 word cards in a 5x5 grid. Pick a Marvel color team (red or blue) as the Spymaster, and give your teammates the corresponding key card. The key card shows which 9 words belong to your team (or sometimes a mix, depending on the layout). The remaining words include bystanders and one assassin.
2) Give a clue: the Spymaster must give a one-word clue that links multiple words on the table to a single theme. The clue must be followed by a number indicating how many words on the board relate to that clue. For example, Spymaster might say “Webslinger, 2” to hint at Spider-Man and possibly something like Web or Spider if those words appear on the board.
3) Guessing phase: teammates discuss, debate, and point to words they think match the clue. They can keep guessing until they either hit all correct words or accidentally hit a red herring (a bystander or the assassin). The goal is to identify all your team’s words before the other team does, while avoiding the assassin.
4) End of turn: once a team guesses too many words (or reveals the assassin) the turn passes to the other side (or the proper alternation, depending on variant). The Spymaster reads the next clue, and the dance continues.

The Marvel twist layers in hero-named words and thematic associations that vary from deck to deck. It’s not about deep canon comprehension; it’s about fast thinking, quick association, and occasionally merging a few adjacent Marvel memes into a single clever clue. In practice, you’ll get a steady rhythm of “we can almost solve this” followed by a jolly scramble as someone realizes the clue was a stretch but still kind of works because you can see it in the movie adaptation in your head.

## Thematic notes: why Marvel fits (and where it stumbles)

Marvel cards aren’t just window dressing; they seed the associations you’ll leverage. If your team is heavy on MCU content, you’ll get a lot of valuable crosslinks: you’ll notice that Cap, Spider-Man, and Hulk feel distinctly different from a word like Genius or Super. That helps you connect a broader set of terms, which can be both a blessing and a curse because some word combos feel forced once you run out of obvious Marvel connections.

Where Marvel mining shines is social: friends who love comics, films, or memes get a grin from the theme. The images on the cards are dynamic and expressive, so even older players feel their eyes draw to a hero’s silhouette and a familiar pose. The downside is that you can get trapped in a cycle where everyone searches for the next pop culture breadcrumb rather than focusing on pure wordplay. If the table leans heavy into superhero trivia, you’ll have a ball; if someone at your table doesn’t know their Thor from their Thanos, you might need a quick refresher or documentary-night fallback to keep the mood light.

## Strategy and tips from the field

- Start with a high-velocity clue lineup: your best opening clue is one that ties two or three words together with a clear Marvel tie. The more your team can connect with a single number, the faster you’ll push forward.
- Don’t over-index on one hero: a clue that’s too specific to Spider-Man, for example, may leave you with two or three nonconforming words that could derail you later. Balance specificity with breadth.
- Read the room, not the text: sometimes the team’s best bet is to sniff out a few safe words and then go for a bigger leap later. Don’t force two or three words if your team is unsure about a single one.
- Use the assassin as a mirror: the assassin is a nightmare of a card, but in some rounds you can use the fear factor to push your teammates toward safer plays. If your team is risk-averse, you might be better off letting the other team risk the assassin’s trap.
- Wordplay synergy beats theme-only play: the best moments come when you link two or three seemingly unrelated words with a clever one-word clue that somehow ties them to a Marvel-related concept. This is where the game sings and your table erupts in laughter.

Tips aside, the critical thing to remember is that probability and fun must dance together. Marvel flavor should elevate the experience, not force a cram session on pop culture bursts. The perfect turn is when your clue hits two or three words cleanly and the rest of the table roars with the “aha” moment—followed by a group chuckle at how close you were to the assassin and how you still saved the round.

## Variants and house rules you can try

- Timed rounds: add a 60-second sand timer for each clue to quicken the pace and turn the night into a breathless sprint. This makes the experience more chaotic but also more cinematic.
- Double clue rounds: for a dramatic twist, allow a two-word clue for a single turn with a cap on how many words you can link. It’s a cool risk-reward mechanic that matches a Marvel team’s unpredictable strategy.
- Blind guess adjustments: allow a guess after every clue, not just after the number is announced. It’s a more conversational and chaotic approach that works well with a loud group.
- The agents’ swap night: switch roles mid-game if you’re playing a long session. It can feel like a page turn in a Marvel cross-over where the hero becomes the mastermind and the mastermind forgets his plans for a moment of comic relief.
