Unboxing and assembly journey:
The package arrives flat-packed, like a modern art sculpture that has decided to become furniture. Step-by-step assembly tips:
- Clear the play area: you’ll want plenty of room to maneuver the two long panels and the cable tray. If your space is tight, consider moving furniture away and giving yourself a temporary staging area.
- Align the panels: start by aligning the two large tops so you’ll know if the holes line up. A helper makes this much easier and reduces the risk of cross-threading.
- Start with the frame: attach the legs with the cross-brace; tighten with a standard Allen key until it feels sturdy but not a drill-sergeant tight. Avoid overtightening; you want the wood to seat, not crack.
- Install the cable tray: slide the tray into place along the length. Some models have the tray fixed; others give you a little adjustable range so you can tune for your power strips and cable heights.
- Tighten, check, and test: once you’ve got everything secured, gently shake the desk to test rigidity. If you hear creaks, re-check the fasteners and ensure the tray is fully seated.

Cable management and cable layout:
The cable tray is the desk’s best trick. It runs nearly the entire length, giving space for power strips, data cables, and the occasional VR headset cable you forgot you had. We used color-coded zip ties and measured out paths for each device. The result? A surface that invites you to place a keyboard and mouse without the cords turning into a jungle gym. A few tips:
- Label main lines and sub-lines to avoid the old “which cord goes where?” moment during a midnight software update.
- Use a modest number of vertical cable clips to guide cables from devices to the tray, but don’t crowd them; you want easy access for changes.
- If you’re planning to add big power adapters, measure clearance height under the desk before choosing your monitor stands.

Ergonomics and real-world use:
 Ergonomics aren’t just marketing talk; they influence day-long comfort. With a two-person desk this size, each user should be able to sit with their elbows at roughly 90 degrees and their wrists straight when typing. The height of most desks in this category sits around 72-75 cm, meaning most chairs will fit well, albeit with some tweaking needed for taller or shorter folks. Monitor placement is essential: avoid crowding your monitor arms so they collide with the other user’s monitor when you pivot to reach the keyboard.

In our test, two adults used the desk for a week with laptops, external monitors, and some occasional writing. We found the center cable tray made it easier for both people to route and access their cables without climbing under the desk or negotiating a phalanx of power bricks on the surface. The top wood finish held up well against daily use and occasional coffee mishaps; a few surface marks appeared but nothing unfixable or unsightly.
