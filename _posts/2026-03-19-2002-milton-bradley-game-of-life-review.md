## Setup and rules
### Getting started without a time machine
Setup for Life is straightforward: lay out the board, assemble your peg cars, place money stacks somewhere accessible, and decide who goes first. In the Milton Bradley 2002 edition, the rulebook is minimal but forgiving. You spin to move, and the path you take influences a cascade of decisions—career choice, marriage (or not), kids (or not), and the inevitable retirement destination. The game doesn’t punish you for taking detours; it rewards you with a gentle, sometimes exaggerated, reminder that life is a roller coaster you can’t predict but can enjoy pretending to steer.

One of the joys of this edition is how quickly you can get a family of four into the action. It’s not a hyper-strategic game by any means; it’s a social experience: laugh track included, and the occasional groan when you land on the “Pay Day” space and realize you owe more taxes than you earned this round.

## Playthrough experience
### The spinner and money system
The spinner behaves like a trusted old friend: not too fast, not too slow, just enough suspense to make you believe your destiny is a result of the wheel’s capricious glow. Money is in the form of chunky bills that feel substantial in your hand, a tactile reminder of the “fake wealth” you’re accumulating. You’ll exchange money for cars, houses, and, crucially in the game, life decisions that will shape your family’s future and your own bragging rights at game night three years later when someone inevitably shouts, “I won the game but I still owe my mom $50 in life taxes.”

The careers and salaries add a playful layer of role-play: you can be a doctor, a lawyer, or a professional YouTuber before that career path becomes a meme before your own retirement. The balance between luck (spin results) and choice (career, path, and spouse status) creates a satisfying tension that keeps players engaged without becoming a math-heavy nightmare. In a table full of friends who may not remember birthdays but will remember who ruined the mortgage bill, this game becomes a delightful excuse to pretend you have your life together while clearly not having your life together.

### Cars, pegs, and the box art payoff
The little cars are more than decorative; they serve as mobile indicator of your board presence. Each player’s car carries pegs that symbolize family or personal milestones. Pegs are a tactile cue that you’re accumulating “life currency”—things you’ll later justify with a retirement fund you’ll pretend to understand. The process of adding pegs is oddly satisfying; it’s a tiny, plastic, kid-friendly ritual that mirrors real-life milestones in a way that’s charming and a little terrifying.

The board’s layout makes it easy for everyone to jump in and out of the action. The path is readable, the spaces are clearly labeled, and you won’t spend minutes squinting at tiny icons while someone else is waiting to take their turn, which is exactly the spirit of a well-designed family game.

## Maintenance and storage
### Keeping a 2002 relic in prime form
Maintenance mostly involves keeping the box dry, avoiding direct sunlight that can yellow cardboard, and checking the spinner for smooth action. Wipe down surfaces with a microfiber cloth to avoid scratches, especially if you’re displayed as a “museum of board game history” on a shelf. Storage can be simple: keep everything in the original box or a sturdy bin with dividers to prevent the cars and pegs from migrating into a permanent “lost & found” state in your living room furniture.

If the game has been played plenty, you might notice the money stacks compressing a bit or the cards curling at the edges. A light press under a flat object can help re-flat any mildly curled cards. The key is gentle care; you want this to last long enough to prank your future self into thinking you had a more organized life back in 2002 than you actually did.
