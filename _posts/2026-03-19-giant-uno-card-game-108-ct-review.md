## How to play: setup and basic rules for 2-10 players

Giant UNO follows the same core rules as classic UNO with a few practical upgrades for the oversized format. The goal remains simple: be the first player to discard all your cards. If you’re not familiar with UNO, the game is a ballet of color matching, number matching, and sometimes performing dramatic oohs at the right moment. Here’s the gist for this jumbo edition:

### Setup
- Shuffle the 108 cards thoroughly. Yes, all 108 cards are in play here. That means more potential for color chaos and less chance of boredom while waiting for your turn.
- Each player draws a hand of seven cards. In a 2-player game, expect the pace to be brisk and the strategy to be fierce; in a 10-player game, the chaos level shoots through the roof and you’ll experience a social experiment disguised as a card game.
- The remaining deck becomes the draw pile, placed face down in reach of everyone. The top card is flipped to start the discard pile.

### Playing a turn
- On your turn, you must play a card that matches either the color or the value of the top discard card. If you can’t play, you draw one card from the draw pile. Easy, right? Except for the megaphone you’ll need to shout like a villager in a medieval festival to be heard across the table.
- Special cards work as usual: Skip makes the next player lose their turn; Reverse flips the direction of play; Draw Two forces the next player to draw two cards; Wild lets you change color; Wild Draw Four forces the next player to draw four and declares a new color (but watch out for challenges in some house rules).
- When a player has one card left, they must yell UNO. In the Giant edition, this moment is epic in scope and often accompanied by a giddier chorus of cheers or mock outrage if someone forgets.

The jumbo format means you’ll see dramatic table moments: a perfect “UNO” shout will echo, a misread color triggers a cascade of challenges, and a player who just realized they can Turn the Tables will suddenly become the life of the party for five minutes flat.

### Strategy at scale
With 2-10 players, strategy shifts from “how do I minimize my draws” to “how do I maximize chaos and timing.” Here are a few tactical tips that survive the glow of the living-room light:
- Hold your Wild and Wild Draw Four for moments when you can change the color to maximize your chances of finishing next turn. The giant cards give you more room to maneuver—literally, more surface area to wave those wilds with flair.
- If you’re playing with kids, be mindful of keeping track of color flow. Sometimes a misplay can give younger players a win that feels magical. Let them bask; it’s a family night after all.
- In a full-party setup, designate a “ref” to keep track of UNO calls and challenges. A light referee helps avoid arguments about whether a card did, in fact, match the color or if the deck was shuffled correctly.

If you want to add some spice, you can lean into house rules such as stacking Draw Twos or allowing multi-card wilds. Just remember: the bigger the cards, the bigger the energy when someone calls UNO and you realize you forgot to shout it yourself.

## Variants and house rules to try

The great thing about UNO is that rules are flexible enough to support your own family traditions. For Giant UNO, you can introduce a few optional twists to match your crew’s rhythm:
- Color-changer night: add a rule that after a Wild happens, the color must be announced in a dramatic fashion. Extra points for a chorus of “UNO!” shoutoffs.
- Team play: pair up players and allow teams to coordinate color choices. This can reduce chaos for younger players while still delivering big laughs.
- Time-limited turns: if your group wants a faster pace, set a timer for each turn. The timer adds pressure and keeps the energy high.
- Skill bets: before a tricky move, players can wager a small, playful prize (like choosing the next family movie or controlling the remote for a round).

These variants keep things fresh and give you a reason to revisit the Giant UNO deck across multiple family nights.
