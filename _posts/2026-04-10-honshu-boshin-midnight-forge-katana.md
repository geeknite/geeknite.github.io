### Materials and Construction: Where the Budget Shows and Where It Shines

- Blade: Carbon steel means you should oil it, dry it, and maybe whisper a short prayer to the rust gods after you’ve used it in humidity. Carbon steel blades can hold a decent edge, but they also rust more easily if neglected. The Midnight Forge blade vibe is one of workable sharpness that will shave a sheet of paper if you’ve got the patience and a steady hand.
- Heat treatment: This category typically uses a basic heat-treat to create a hamon or simulated hamon visible along the blade. The effect here is mostly cosmetic; you’ll see a line that looks dramatic under shop lighting, more so than something that would scare a dojo instructor.
- Tang and fittings: On budget katanas, the tang—if it’s full tang at all—often gets a pass. Expect a half- to mid-tang with a simpler handle construction. The menuki (decorative ornaments under the wrap) might be minimal, but the overall finish on the guard and wrap can still look presentable in a display case or cosplay rack.
- Scabbard: Wood with a finish that’s reasonably robust for display and gentle practice. It isn’t designed to survive heavy outdoor cutting on bamboo or tatami; this is a sword you buy for the vibe, not for battlefield-grade efficiency.

The trick with budget blades is to appreciate the design language while recognizing the trade-offs. You’re buying a sword that looks the part and can handle constructive contact for light practice, but it isn’t a gym-grade training blade or a museum piece wearing a suit of armor. If you’re hoping for a workhorse that will stand up to serious cutting, you’ll likely need to step up to a more expensive model. ## Maintenance and Care: Keeping Midnight Forge in Good Shape

Maintenance is where a carbon steel blade earns its keep and also its potential hazard. Here are practical tips to ensure your sword remains a showpiece and a functional tool:
- Oil lightly after use and store in a dry environment. A tiny film of mineral oil or gun oil works well to slow down rust formation.
- Wipe the blade clean and dry before sheathing. Water and carbon steel don’t mix well, even in a cosplay photo shoot.
- Inspect the scaffold wrap (tsuka-ito) periodically for looseness. If you notice any seam gaps, tighten gradually without over-tightening, so you don’t crack the wrap.
- Clean the scabbard exterior to avoid moisture build-up that can transfer to the blade during sheathing.

Maintenance is not fireworks; it’s a quiet ritual that respects the blade’s role in your collection and your creative projects.
