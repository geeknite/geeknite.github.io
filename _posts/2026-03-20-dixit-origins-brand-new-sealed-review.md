## The Rules, Revisited
If you’re already a Dixit veteran, Origins will feel familiar. The basic flow remains: one player becomes the storyteller and provides a clue in the form of a sentence or phrase connected to one of the 84 images. The rest of the players select a card from their own hand that best matches the clue and submit it face-down. Cards are revealed and each player (except the storyteller) votes for which card they think matches the storyteller's clue. The storyteller reveals their card, and points are awarded: the storyteller gets points if at least one but not all players pick their card; other players get points if they guessed the storyteller's card or if they used their vote wisely and scored more points overall. The pressure to balance enough specificity to avoid universal猜ing with enough ambiguity to obfuscate the correct card is the heart of the mechanic.

Origins introduces a few subtle refinements to that rhythm. The prompts tend to lean toward origin myths and dream logic, which can push your clues from simple to poetic without crossing into nonsensical territory. You’ll enjoy this if you relish writing a two-liner that somehow connects a myth to a mischievous fox or a lighthouse that drinks the rain. If your group has a poet present (or a friend who can improvise in iambic pentameter after two sips of soda), you’re in for a treat. If not, you’ll still manage a fun ride by leaning into the humor of misdirection and the joy of when someone finally nails the card you never intended anyone to guess.

For those worried about “rules bloat” or overcomplication, Origins keeps things brisk. There is no app integration, no heavy scoring spreadsheets, just the simple joy of a story told with one line and a set of vivid images. The jury is still out on whether this is better with a smaller or larger group, but in practice a 4- to 6-player table is near-ideal. A single round can feel like a short story in a coffee break; a few rounds will feel like a micro-season of improv theater.

## How to Play: A Quick Start Guide
- Setup: Shuffle the 84-image deck, give each player a hand of cards, and place the storyteller board in the middle. You’ll need six voting tokens (one per player in a six-player game).
- Turn structure: The storyteller studies their hand, picks a card, and gives a clue that ties to that card. Other players select a card from their hand that could match the clue, then all chosen cards are revealed.
- Voting: Everyone (except the storyteller) privately votes for the card they think matches the clue. The storyteller reveals the actual card, and points are issued.
- Scoring: The storyteller scores if at least one but not all players choose their card. Voters score if they guessed the storyteller’s card or if their own card earned votes from others. The exact scoring thresholds can depend on your house rules, but the core is a mix of deduction and creative persuasion.
- End condition: The game ends after a set number of rounds or when players hit a target score. The winner is the one who tied their imagination to a story so effectively that others willed their card to win.

Tip for first-timers: Start with a simple, concrete clue. As you get the hang of the vibe, you can branch into more cryptic clues that make players sift through the dream logic of the cards. The real joy is watching someone articulate a clue that makes everyone pause, then burst into laughter when the card finally pops up in the reveal.
