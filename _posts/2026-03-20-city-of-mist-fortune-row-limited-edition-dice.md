## Build quality and aesthetics: a closer look

Fortune Row leans into the tactile, with dice that feel balanced and satisfyingly weighty without being brick-like. The edges are crisp, not sharp—there’s a deliberate rounding that makes handling comfortable over long sessions. The color palette is where the set earns its noir cred: smoky blues, emerald greens with copper flecks, and a few accents in antique-gold that hint at gilded signage you’d expect to see on a retro-futurist city block.

Texture-wise, the dice have a smooth polish that is pleasant on the fingers, with a subtle marble or swirl pattern visible when you tilt them in the light. The font for the numerals (or symbols, depending on the die type) is legible but unobtrusive, which is exactly what you want when you’re trying to narrate a complicated scene without having to squint at your results.

If you’re using dice as a storytelling device, this set makes the act of rolling feel meaningful. We’ve seen a few artisan sets that look good but clamor for attention when they collide with a critical moment; Fortune Row manages to stay elegant without slipping into ostentation.

## Packaging, presentation, and long-term care

Packaging quality matters more than it might seem because it affects long-term storage. Fortune Row does a good job with tactile packaging that supports display, portability, and protection. The dice can scratch if jumbled with metal minis, so the included pouch or foam cradle helps prevent that. For long-term care, keep the dice away from direct sunlight to preserve color fidelity; resin can fade under harsh UV exposure, and we’d hate to see your emeralds go sun-bleached after a year of hero shots.

If you’re curious about maintenance, the basic rule is: don’t soak them in solvents and avoid abrasive cleaners. A soft microfiber cloth lightly dampened with water is usually enough to keep them looking like new after a long campaign where you rolled them into the most dramatic cliffhangers you could conceive.
