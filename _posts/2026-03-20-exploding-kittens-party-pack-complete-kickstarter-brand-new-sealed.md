## How to Play: The Fast, Cheeky Rules of Cat-astrophic Delight

### Basic flow and turn structure

Players take turns drawing cards from the deck and playing cards that affect the draw order, the progress of the game, and who is most likely to scream when a kitten pounces (figuratively, of course). The main goal remains the same: avoid drawing an exploding kitten card. If you draw one, you must play a defuse card to neutralize it, and your defuse cards can be a limited resource that makes you question your life choices as you watch your competitors squirm.

On your turn, you can play cards with varying effects: some let you skip your draw, some force opponents to draw more than one card, and some simply mock the person who drew the exploding kitten with cartoonish cruelty that’s primarily for comedic effect.

The party nature of this pack means you’ll be dealing with more interactions per round. More cards, more ways to poke at each other, more opportunities for someone to win by wild accident or to lose by overthinking a simple turn order. It’s a game that rewards social timing as much as it rewards counting cards in your hand.

### Strategy: when to push the risk and when to fold

Strategy in Exploding Kittens is not about deep math or perfect probability sheets. It’s about reading the table, noticing patterns, and knowing when to gamble with a “See the Future” flip, or to bait someone into a poor decision with a strategically timed Attack card. In the Party Pack, you’ll want to balance:

- Card economy: how many defuse cards do you hold versus how many you might lose in a single risky draw?
- Player psychology: who in your circle is the worst at resisting a playful taunt? That person is your prime target to draw extra cards from.
- Tempo: does your group benefit from rapid-fire turns, or do you want to slow down the pace so that each move feels like a dramatic infomercial where the stakes are “will someone survive the next two cards?”

### House rules and fun variants

A staple of party games is the willingness to bend rules for a laugh. The Party Pack plays nicely with simple house rules. Some groups enjoy adding a “king’s ransom” for every explosion that lands on a player’s lap (figuratively, not literally). Others prefer to double down on the See the Future card, letting players peek several upcoming draws and then selectively pass the doom around in a dramatic, improv-heavy moment. A few veteran players even add a “dead man’s hand” variant where the last surviving player earns a tiny victory dance at the end of the game. It’s silly, but it’s your table, your chaos, and your story—so lean into it.

## Practical Tips for a Smooth Game Night

- Start with a quick rules recap and a practice round. It helps your group calibrate who’s comfortable with the pace.
- Nominally track who has defuse cards in their hand, but not obsessively. Part of the fun is to take the surprise out of the Bag of Fate gradually, rather than spiking everyone’s anxiety with an onslaught of “you should have seen that coming.”
- Have a backup activity ready for players who don’t vibe with high-chaos formats. A lightweight filler game or a snack break helps prevent fatigue.
- Use a neutral, humorous table script. If someone draws the exploding kitten, respond with a playful line that fits the room rather than hurling blame. The vibe matters more than precise outcomes.
