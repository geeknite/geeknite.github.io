## In the wild: installation and setup

Installing a winch is not quite rocket science, but there is a dance to do with power, control, and respect for physics. Here is a practical, do‑it‑yourself friendly guide, focusing on the Stealth 4500 and the most common bumper setups.

### Step by step install sanity check
1) Prep your mounting surface: clean, dry, and free of protrusions that might snag the rope. A quick coat of rust protection on metal edges is optional but polite to your winter coat of dust.
2) Mount the winch along the bumper’s centerline where possible. Align the bolt pattern with care and hand-tighten the first pass to ensure everything lines up before you torque.
3) Attach the control solenoid and route the power cables from the battery fuse block to the winch controller. Use proper gauge wire and secure the connections to prevent corrosion from a rogue shower of mud.
4) Feed the synthetic rope onto the drum with a clean start. Make sure the rope is wound evenly; an uneven start leads to rope bird‑caging and drama during the first recovery attempt.
5) Connect the remote and test the operation in a clear area. Always wear gloves when handling the rope; synthetic fiber can cut if you’re not careful.
6) Add the damper plate or bag to the pull area for safety. In a worst case scenario, this helps to absorb energy and reduce the risk of forward recoil if the line snaps.
7) Do a low-load test pull on flat ground to make sure the rotation, winching direction, and braking engage properly. Then go for a gentle incline test, increasing slowly to feel how the unit handles heat and load.

### Safety first
Winching is not a spectator sport. Always establish a clear recovery plan with one person focused on line tension and another person operating the remote. Never stand in line with the rope or the load. The static energy stored in a loaded winch can surprise you in all the wrong ways. If you plan to recover a heavy load near a trailer hitch or under a vehicle, consider a line dampener and a rated recovery strap that can handle the anticipated force without turning into a whip. ### Synthetic rope: is it worth the hype
Synthetic rope benefits include lighter weight, no memory, easier handling, and drastically reduced risk of severe rope injury compared with steel cable. The tradeoffs are cost and care. Synthetic rope can be damaged by abrasion, UV exposure, and sharp edges, so it needs protective wear and occasional inspection. For a typical ATV recovery scenario, synthetic rope is a clear winner if you exercise standard safety practices and self‑care around the gear. ## Maintenance and care easy wins

A winch is not a set-and-forget device. A little routine maintenance goes a long way towards longevity and reliability:

- Inspect the rope for frays and snags after every heavy use. Replace if you see worn fibers or flat spots. With synthetic rope, you will notice if the rope looks bead‑like or has flattening near the drum.
- Clean the winch housing after exposure to mud and water. Wipe down with a dry cloth and avoid harsh solvents that can degrade seals.
- Test run the motor periodically to ensure smooth operation and respond to any odd noises or stuttering. If you hear unusual grinding, it may be time to service or replace components.
- Inspect electrical connections for corrosion. Clean and reseal as needed to avoid voltage drop during operation and to keep performance stable across weather conditions.
