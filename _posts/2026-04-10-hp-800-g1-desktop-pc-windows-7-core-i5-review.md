- GPU: Integrated graphics; not a gaming rig but perfectly adequate for productivity, light video playback, and the occasional retro emulation experiment.

- OS: Windows 7 Professional, a friend to old business apps and a foe to the modern web’s ever-deepening security requirements.
- Form factor: Desktop tower with room to breathe and slot in upgrades when your inner tinkerer wakes up.
- Ports: USB 2.0/3.0 mix, video outputs (VGA and sometimes DisplayPort or HDMI depending on the exact model), audio jacks, and Ethernet.

## Performance and user experience
What happens when we marry Haswell-era silicon with a Windows 7 bedrock in 2026? It’s not going to win any speed contests at a sprint, but it does deliver a dependable, predictable routine that can feel surprisingly modern if you tune your expectations and content with moderate upgrades.

- Boot behavior and everyday pace: Expect a traditional boot sequence, likely in the 25–40 second range on a healthy HDD. Swap in a SATA SSD, and you’ll shave those seconds off like a barber snipping a few lazy hairs off a shaggy memory of time.
- Multitasking reality: With 8 GB of RAM, you can juggle a handful of browser tabs, a document editor, and maybe a media player in the background. If you push into virtualization or heavy photo editing, you’ll want more RAM or a lighter workload.
- Office productivity: Word processors, spreadsheets, and presentation software glide along nicely. The core i5 with turbo helps with compilation tasks or larger spreadsheets where the CPU gets to stretch its legs. It’s not a speed demon by modern standards, but it’s a steady, predictable workhorse.
- Media and light gaming: It’s more than enough for streaming, photo slideshows, or older titles that don’t demand the latest GPU horsepower. Don’t expect to play the latest open-world epics; think retro gaming and classic emulation with a side of nostalgia.
- Security and modern workflow caveats: Windows 7 Pro is past its prime in the security department. If you must use it, isolate this PC from sensitive networks, and keep offline tasks away from the open web as much as possible. Consider a modern, secure host to handle sensitive data while letting the 800 G1 run as a dedicated legacy apps box or a testbed for old software.

For readers who want an extra chuckle, picture this: a bureaucratic hero in a micro-tower chassis brimming with memories of times when coffee was perhaps the most dangerous thing you could spill on a keyboard. ### Upgrade paths that actually matter
Upgradeability is where the 800 G1 earns its keep. It’s not the yolo hackathon of modern rigs, but it invites you to tinker in a rewarding, low-risk way.

- RAM upgrades: If you find the 8 GB a little stingy for today’s browser wars, you can add more memory. DDR3 RAM is cheap and plentiful on the used market, and a bump to 16 GB is a solid, noticeable improvement for multitasking.
- Storage upgrades: The 250 GB HDD is a natural target for a modern SSD upgrade. A 512 GB or 1 TB SATA SSD is a practical upgrade that makes the system feel snappier, especially when loading Windows and your older apps. If you’re feeling extra ambitious, a small NVMe drive can be used as a cache with a proper PCIe adapter, but compatibility checks are essential.
- WiFi options: If the unit didn’t come with WiFi, a PCIe card or a USB WiFi dongle is a simple fix. For best performance on a retro box, look for 802.11n or newer adapters that still fit the legacy PCIe slots.
- External peripherals: A vintage keyboard and mouse are wonderful, especially if you want the full retro vibe. USB hubs help you attach more devices without creating a cable forest.

### Windows 7 Pro reality check
Running Windows 7 on a PC from this era is glorious and a little reckless. The classic Start Menu returns with the comfort of a familiar old friend, but security updates are no longer guaranteed. We recommend using this box as a dedicated legacy-app workstation or a testbed for software you know won’t require the newest security patches. If you need to access the modern internet regularly, consider a separate, modern PC for daily tasks, with the 800 G1 strictly serving offline or sandboxed roles.

## Connectivity and peripherals explained
The 800 G1’s connectivity is straightforward and practical. It doesn’t brag about 10 Gbps LAN or next-gen fiber optic ports; instead it focuses on what most small offices needed a decade ago: stable wired connectivity, decently flexible video output options, and enough USB headroom for keyboards, mice, and a postcard-sized stack of USB sticks you definitely should not keep on the desk.

- WiFi and ethernet: If your SKU includes built-in WiFi, you’re good to go. If not, USB adapters are a quick fix. A wired Ethernet connection remains the most reliable for office productivity.
- Video outputs: Expect VGA as a standard on many SKUs, with DisplayPort or HDMI as upgrades on others. You can connect to an older monitor with VGA and pair it with a modern display via DisplayPort or HDMI. It’s a bit of a cable circus, but a flexible circus.
- USB ports: A healthy mix of USB 2.0 and USB 3.0 ports gives you room for peripherals and external storage without needing a USB port rescue mission.
- Audio: Onboard audio for calls and media; you’ll get the job done with basic speakers or a nice pair of headphones.
