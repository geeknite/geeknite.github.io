Introduction

City of Mist has always been the kind of game that wears a trench coat and winks from across a neon-drenched alley. It promises a world where ordinary people become extraordinary by embracing their legends, a tabletop RPG that blends gumshoe noir with comic-book mythos. The Shadows of the Past is the latest expansion (or supplement, depending on how you like your slogans) that leans into memory, identity, and the sometimes inconvenient truths our pasts insist on shoving into the here and now. If the base City of Mist was about discovering your true potential by accepting your mythic alter ego, this expansion shifts the lens to the shadows we drag behind us—our 'shadows' not as a metaphor, but as a literal, narratively potent mechanic that can make a session feel like a slow-burn thriller and a character study at the same time.

If you’re here for a quick, bullet-point takedown, you’ve come to the wrong neon-lit crossroads. If you’re here for a long-form, geeky dive into how this thing plays, what it does well, and where it sometimes trips over its own cape, strap in. We’re going to ride this fog machine until the truth leaks out in a little ooh, aah, and maybe a sigh of exasperation when a plan goes gloriously off-script.

Gear and presentation

The Shadows of the Past arrives in a compact box that feels like it was designed by someone who thinks every RPG accessory should double as a fashion accessory. The art direction leans into the moody monochromes and salt-spray blues that City of Mist is known for, with occasional pops of color to highlight the mythic runes and icons. Component quality is solid: cards are sturdy, counters have a satisfying heft, and the GM screen (if your table uses one) has the right balance of opacity and skulls-on-fire menace. The real star, though, is the new memory track system introduced by this expansion, which includes deck-like memory prompts and a handful of “what-if” triggers that can derail or accelerate a session with surgical precision.

What is City of Mist: The Shadows of the Past?

At its core, City of Mist asks players to inhabit legends: the hero who is also a citizen, the detective who is also a mythic avatar. The Shadows of the Past doubles down on the personal histories we carry into every scene. The expansion introduces a handful of new mechanics and narrative hooks centered around memory, guilt, and the way past choices shape present outcomes. Think of it as a forensic examination of your character’s life, taken with the dramatic flair of a graphic novel and the procedural rigor of a noirist’s notebook.

For players who love the core engine—the Incarnations (your mythic identities), fieldwork driven by clues, and the interplay between private truth and public deception—this expansion feels like a very natural extension. For those who found the base game too talky or too dependent on the GM’s ability to thread subplots, there are moments where the pacing can drag. It’s not a fail; it’s a design choice that rewards groups who can tolerate, or even crave, slower, more intricate storytelling.

If you want to compare, this expansion slots nicely next to the core game’s narrative engine. It doesn’t replace the core rules; it deepens them. If you’re familiar with the way City of Mist handles powers and masks—how a character’s “mythic” power can clash with practical limitations—The Shadows of the Past gives you more arrows to fire from that quiver.

Mechanics: what’s new and how it feels

The heart of City of Mist is that players don’t have simple powers; they wear myths that describe who they are under pressure, with a GM-driven city as the stage. The Shadows of the Past adds several layers to that: new memory cards (or prompts), new milestones tied to your character’s history, and a structured but flexible approach to handling secrets, betrayals, and the consequences of old choices.

New memory prompts come in two flavors: evocative prompts that push players to reveal a clue or a piece of their legend, and mechanical prompts that nudge the GM toward a specific outcome in the scene. The idea is to keep the session anchored in character memory while providing a reliable engine to generate conflict and momentum. When it works, it’s a cascade of “aha!” moments that feel earned rather than manufactured; when it doesn’t, you get a few too many “remember this memory” cards lying flat on the table, like a deck of tampered-with-stationery that forgot to light up the noir properly.

The expansion also introduces a more explicit penalty/reward structure tied to past actions. It’s not punitive; it’s just that the game now has more levers to pull when a player tries to cheat fate with sheer willpower. This can be exhilarating for players who enjoy the sense of a living, breathing memory-space; it can also feel a bit drawn-out for groups who prefer lean, high-velocity sessions. The designers clearly wanted to give the GM more narrative leverage without stepping into the dreaded “railroad” territory—and for the most part, they succeed.

Narrative design: memory, secrets, and the past as character

City of Mist has always thrived on secrets. The Shadows of the Past treats secrets not just as plot devices but as instruments the players must actively manage. Secrets have cost; they demand upkeep, and they sometimes require characters to confront not just what they did, but why they did it. In practical terms, that means more intimate scenes: flashbacks, introspective monologues, and the occasional break-in at the character’s own psyche to retrieve a memory key that unlocks a new inference or ability.

This is where the expansion shines for groups who like to foreground character arcs. Scenes become micro-dramas about who a character is when no longer wearing the hero’s mask. The downside is that this can slow down sessions if players aren’t ready to trade action for introspection. It’s a brave, sometimes uncomfortable design choice, and I appreciate that willingness to push players into uncomfortable emotional spaces—especially at the table where a session can easily drift into “we all get to be super cool for a while.” The Shadows of the Past says, “No—let’s interrogate why you want to be cool.” And that interrogation, when done with consent and good-natured banter, yields some of the most memorable moments this game has produced.

Art direction and components: beyond the words on the page

The art direction remains the game’s strongest non-mechanical asset. The Shadows of the Past uses moody linework, splashes of color in important moments, and a typography that reads like a detective’s notebook from a crime scene in a cyber-noir city. The cards—the primary physical prop here—feel tactile and sturdy, with a satisfying shuffle. The memory prompts, while numerous, are well-curated and thematically consistent; you won’t find a “random memory” card that feels out of place unless your table ignores tone entirely.

One caveat: because the expansion leans into personal history, some prompts can be deeply personal and, well, heavy. If your group doesn’t vibe with emotionally charged scenes or you’re playing in a casual, lighthearted style, you might find this expansion pressing rather than playful. It’s not a flaw in the design so much as a reminder that City of Mist wears emotion on its sleeve, and this expansion twists the sleeve into a lace cuff for added drama.

Play sessions: pacing, balance, and table dynamics

If you’re bringing The Shadows of the Past to your table, consider a few pacing tactics. First, establish a memory theme for the session early. Is this going to be a memory-anchored chapter about guilt, or a memory-unlocking caper that requires solving a mystery about a past betrayal? Second, set expectations: how much time are you willing to dedicate to introspection per session? Third, prepare a couple of “memory keys” that can unlock new scenes quickly in case players stall. The expansion rewards preparation; it punishes laziness by turning memory prompts into cold, clinical interrogations of motive.

For GMs who love to improvise, these prompts are a gold mine. For players who prefer a more dice-driven approach, you’ll want to inject some momentum mechanics—short, sharp beats that push toward action before the memory devours the entire pacing cycle.

Comparisons to base City of Mist and other expansions

If you own the base game, The Shadows of the Past feels like a natural next step rather than a radical rewrite. It leans into the components you already love (the masks, the noir atmosphere, the collaborative storytelling) and adds a more explicit emotional and psychological dimension. If you’ve explored other City of Mist expansions, you’ll recognize a familiar pattern: more tools for the GM to shape the city, more hooks for players to reveal their legends, and a few riskier moves to blend memory with action.

Where does it fit in a collection? If you savor mystery-driven campaigns and character-centric arcs, this is a must-have. If you’re building a campaign for players who want to punch through walls with power rather than peel back the layers of a person’s past, you’ll still enjoy the setting, but you may not feel the expansion’s full impact until you lean into the memory-focused sessions. It’s not a universal good; it’s a very targeted improvement that shines brightest when you let memory drive the plot forward.

Design notes and caveats

A few design notes that might matter at your table:

- Memory prompts are best used in session openings or mid-campaign “reveal” moments. They can derail a plan, but they can also unlock a thread you forgot to plant in earlier sessions.
- The penalties tied to old actions are clever, but they can pile up. If you’re not careful, the party can get stuck in guilt loops that slow the game. Use short timers for how long a memory loop can run before it yields something actionable.
- The expansion assumes a certain level of group maturity for handling heavy themes. Consent check-ins are recommended, especially in longer campaigns.

What I’d like to see in a future update

If there’s a wish list here, it’s modest: more modular memory keys that can be slotted into existing campaigns without forcing a full rewrite, plus a streamlined quick-start version for first-time players who want the mood but not the maze. A digital companion that helps track memory prompts and consequence tracking would be a welcome addition for online sessions, where paper cards can sometimes vanish into a dark corner of a virtual table.

Final thoughts on value and playability

The Shadows of the Past is not the cheapest expansion in City of Mist’s catalog, but it offers something that the base game can only hint at: a tangible, working mechanism to elevate memory into a core driver of plot. If you already love the mood and the meta-play of City of Mist, you’ll likely find this expansion a satisfying addition that deepens the experience. If you’re still deciding whether you enjoy a game that asks you to wrestle with memory as a narrative currency, this expansion might be a gentle nudge in the direction of “yes.” For groups that crave a more introspective, character-driven noir, Shadows of the Past is a buffet: take a little, savor a lot, and prepare to discuss your character’s life as if it were a case file marked confidential.
