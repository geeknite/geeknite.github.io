## What is Herd Mentality?
At its core, Herd Mentality is about collective guesswork, but with a democratic twist. A prompt card is drawn, and every player writes down an answer on a personal sheet. The twist is that you score for matching the most popular answers of the group, not for originality. The majority wins points, and anyone who bucks the crowd can still score if their answer happens to be among the majority’s top options. The catch is that you never want to be the lone holdout who misreads the room—unless being an outlier is your brand of chaos, in which case you may become the breakout star of the night.

The base game scales from four to about eight players comfortably, and with the 20 Extra Questions Inc deck, it claims to scale further. The scaling challenges include staying engaged when the group is large and avoiding piles of repetitive prompts that fizzle out the energy. We tested both ends of the spectrum: low player count with intimate humor, and high player count with an unpredictable social chorus. The results were consistently entertaining, though sometimes the biggest laughs came from the wrong answers that almost everyone guessed would be popular but weren’t.

This is a game about reading social signals more than about clever phrasing. It rewards folks who are good at predicting friend group consensus, and it punishes those who insist on a niche answer that no one else wants to defend. It is also a surprisingly forgiving party game in that you do not need to be a “gamer” to enjoy it. In a family setting, that texture matters: it invites participation without performance anxiety.

## Setup and Flow: How to Play
The general flow of a Herd Mentality session is straightforward and forgiving for beginners:

- Each round starts with a prompt card drawn and revealed to all players.
- A short time window is given for players to think and write their answers privately.
- Once the timer ends, all players reveal their answers simultaneously.
- The group discusses briefly and then votes on which answer they think will be the majority. The actual scoring follows a simple rule: you gain points if your answer matches the majority choice.

This setup is designed to be rapid and social rather than methodical. You can squeeze in a couple of rounds between a main course and dessert just as easily as you can run a full tournament with a dozen players. The presence of a large number of players can feel chaotic in the best possible way, as the room’s volume rises and the energy becomes contagious. The more players, the more surprising the majority can be. And that surprise is the fuel of the night more often than not.

A few practical tips for setup:

- Establish a clear turn order and communicate how the majority will be decided on that round (the simplest path is a show of hands or a private tally). In the digital era, you can also adopt a lightweight app to expedite counting, but nothing beats good old paper slips for the real room vibe.
- Set a sensible time limit. If you have ten players, a 60 second decision window per prompt can produce a terrific rhythm; if you have twenty, a shorter window prevents stagnation.
- Decide how to handle ties. Do you redeploy the tie round with a fresh prompt, or declare that the tie breaks in favor of a specific strategy (for example, the player with most recent correct guess wins a bonus point)? The deck gives you flexibility to set your own rules.

For players who demand deeper structure, you can weave in family-friendly rules that encourage discussion or storytelling. The prompt cards can double as conversation starters, prompting the table to reminisce about childhood, travel mishaps, or favorite movie quotes. The 20 Extra Questions Inc deck often contains prompts that yield stories, which is a big part of the game’s charm.

## Strategy and Play Style Tips
If you want to tilt the odds in your favor without becoming a powermonger, consider these strategies:

- Read the room before you write. A good gauge of the crowd’s tendencies will help you align or disalign your answer without appearing desperate.
- Scripting helps, but only to a point. A clever mashup of plausible yet bold answers can catch players off-guard and earn you points when the majority chooses a different path.
- Use your social leverage. In a dense group, you can partially influence the majority by planting a shared frame of reference or an inside joke that nudges others toward a particular response.
- Avoid the extreme. The best answers are not the most ridiculous, but those that feel like the near-certain choice but with a twist that reveals your understanding of the crowd.
- In the 20 Extra Questions Inc deck, lean into prompts that allow for storytelling. Short, memorable anecdotes tend to travel across the table much faster than longwinded explanations and can shape the majority more effectively.

If you find yourself in a lull, switch to a couple of “warm-up” prompts that almost everyone can answer quickly. This will re-energize the table and set up the room for the more thoughtful or ridiculous prompts that follow.
