### Playing the Market Standard Edition: A Quick Tour

To start, players gather around the table and split the deck into a face-down pile. The aim is simple: run through your hand by trading with other players and try to “go Pit” by shedding your commodity cards, sometimes until one player shouts victory and rings the bell with triumphant gusto. The excitement is less about strategy and more about improvisational social theater. You learn to read micro-expressions, decipher who looks away when a certain commodity comes up, and decide if you want to push for a multi-card trade or hold back for a riskier single-card swing.

The Market Standard Edition plays like this in spirit, though your mileage will vary by the people around the table. The core mechanic—trading cards representing commodities—remains. You propose a trade, someone else counters, and the pit roars in response. If you have ever played a game where you can feel the adrenaline in your fingertips as you shout the name of a product while your friends swing to accept or reject, you know what Pit is about. The modernization of components in 98 sealed packaging is not about adding new features so much as preserving the feel of a party game from a time when the internet was still a rumor and your only scoreboard was a bell and somebody shouting the price.

The rulebook in the 98 edition stays loyal to the original rhythm: there is no price mechanism, no monetary value to cards beyond the excitement. The secret sauce is the group dynamics. Your success hinges on your ability to read the table, to time your declarations, and to resist the urge to scream a random commodity at the wrong moment, which is how you accidentally insult the market and ruin a lengthy handshake deal. It is the social equivalent of a flash mob built around a table of snacks and a memory of pop culture commercials. It is chaos, in a good way.
