## What is Era? A quick legend, in case you’re new to the table

Era is a card-driven civilization builder where players draft historical developments, combine them into era-specific synergies, and score points by building a narrative of civilization through cards. The base game (as re-released in this Kickstarter edition) focuses on four eras: Antiquity, Classical Age, Medieval, and Modern. Each era introduces unique card types, action windows, and scoring opportunities. The theme is obvious but lovingly rendered: the board you imagine in your head is the one you actually play on when you’re not arguing about which empire did the best job of not getting invaded.

Visuals stay playful rather than dour—this is not a wargame with a blood splatter on every card. It’s a historian’s sandbox with a sense of humor, a little tabletop theater, and enough “aha!” moments to justify giggling at a strategic reveal. The game rewards careful planning and a willingness to pivot when someone sneaks in a clever synergy you didn’t expect. If you like games where you feel smart for about five turns and then realize you’ve been completely outmaneuvered by the guy who studied the art in school, Era will feel right at home.

## The Kickstarter bundle: 4 expansions and a bonus card

The NEW edition’s big selling point isn’t just “more cards” but “more ways to chain eras into glorious combos.” The four expansions act as accelerators for different playstyles, while the bonus card acts as a quirky wild card that can tilt the table in unexpected ways. Let’s break them down one by one, with a practical note on how they affect pacing, interaction, and the old friend called ‘counterplay.’

### Expansion 1: Chronicles of Civilization
What it adds: A set of “great achievement” cards tied to major civilizations: Rome, Han, Gupta, Mali, and beyond. Each civilization contributes a unique mechanic, such as a temporary production boost, a special scoring track, or a one-time double-dip synergy when you time the development card with a neighboring player’s move.
Gameplay impact: Expect more long-term planning; you’ll be juggling in-era synergy with potential counter-moves by others who want to block your big score. It introduces a richer asymmetry without tipping the game into the “you’re playing a puzzle” territory.
Playstyle fit: Great for players who enjoy empire-building and planning to emerge as the last civilizations standing.

### Expansion 2: Time Travelers Pack
What it adds: Time travel is represented by event cards that alter the order of actions or grant a one-turn rewind option for a single pick. Think “footnotes becoming footnotes with practical effects.”
Gameplay impact: It raises the “what if” factor—players can twist the current turn order, steal a crucial action, or force someone else’s plan to adapt mid-stream. It’s deliciously chaotic in a controlled way.
Playstyle fit: For folks who enjoy spicy meta-games and want to feel the tinge of chaos without collapsing into random luck.

### Expansion 3: Dynasties and Revolutions
What it adds: Power-shift mechanics where the “leader board” can be overtaken by a late surge of influence from underdog players. It also adds new modern-era cards that reflect industrial revolutions and major societal shifts.

### Expansion 4: Global Exchange & Exploration
What it adds: A trading and exploration sub-system. You can trade cards, scout new eras, and unlock “accent” resources that give temporary bonuses for a round or two.
Gameplay impact: Trading introduces negotiation elements that are seldom mandatory but always entertaining. The exploration track gives board-state rewards and a sense of exploration that dovetails nicely with the historical theme.
Playstyle fit: For social players who like interactions, diplomacy, and occasional back-and-forth bargaining that doesn’t devolve into “who has the biggest stealth alliance.”

### The Bonus Card: The Archivist
What it does: A single, clever card that can be played to reveal a hidden history fact from the current era, granting a small but meaningful point boost or a protective shield against one negative effect.
Gameplay impact: It’s a nice modular piece—not game-defining, but mood-lifting and micro-satisfying. It’s the kind of card you tuck into a sleeve to surprise your opponent and your own strategy alike.
Playstyle fit: A delight for power-users who enjoy tempo plays and “one more little thing” moments that keep you smiling as you score the final points.

## How to play: a quick refresher for veterans and a friendly nudge for newcomers

This section provides a practical sense of the core loop, focusing on the base game dynamics and how expansions nudge that loop without turning the table into a math lab.

### Setup and core mechanics
- Each player starts with a small deck of era-appropriate cards and a personal board that tracks resources, influence, and a scoring track.
- The game unfolds in rounds; each round features a set of actions that players can perform using cards from their hands.
- Cards come in several types: Development, Event, Resource, and special action cards. Each card has a cost, a potential era-appropriate effect, and sometimes a trigger that modifies other cards in play.
- Players evoke era-specific bonuses by meeting certain conditions on their boards or by playing synergy cards that align with an era’s themes.
- Scoring occurs at the end of each round or when a particular objective is met. The point system rewards both long-term planning and timely risk-taking.

### Turn structure and decision points
A typical turn looks like this: draw, plan, play, and resolve. You’ll balance between expanding your own civilization and curbing others’ momentum. The expansions add layers, but they don’t force a single “best” path; multiple viable routes exist depending on who you’re playing with and which cards you draw.

One of the clever design choices is the way era transitions are handled. You don’t simply convert from one set of cards to another; you unlock new abilities when you pass into a new era. That keeps the tension high, especially when someone is sitting at a comfortable lead and you’re eyeing a late-game swing with the Time Travelers Pack or the Archivist bonus card.

### Card types and their roles
- Development cards push your civilization forward, unlocking new abilities or score multipliers.
- Event cards trigger once per round in interesting ways, sometimes giving you a temporary edge or nudging a neighbor toward a less desirable outcome.
- Resource cards supply the raw materials you need to perform big moves, so you’ll want a steady rhythm of draws and plays to avoid droughts.
- Special action cards (including some from the expansions) offer once-per-game shuffles, protection from negative events, or the chance to steal a small victory from a rival.

### Ongoing strategies you’ll actually enjoy
- Tempo vs. tempo: If you pace your era transitions to misalign an opponent’s best moves, you buy yourself several precious turns of advantage.
- Neighborhood synergy: Many expansions rely on neighboring players’ choices. You’ll look to align your cards to maximize combined effects or to block a dominant combo before it spirals out of control.
- Late-game swing: The Archivist card introduces a delightful endgame push that makes it feel like a race to set up a final scoring flourish rather than a long, quiet stroll toward victory point paradise.
