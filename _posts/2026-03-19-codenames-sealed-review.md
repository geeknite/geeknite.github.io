## How to Play: A Quick, Jovial Overview

Codenames is a team-based word association game in which two teams—the Red Team and the Blue Team—compete to identify their team’s 9 agents first. A single field operative per team can also be an undercover agent; the rest of the players assume the role of Spymaster, who gives one-word clues followed by a number. The clue word must relate to as many of their team’s words on the board as possible without pointing to the other team’s words, neutral words, or—gulp—the assassin.

### The Core Roles
- Spymaster: Gives one-word clues plus a number. The clue should connect multiple of their team’s words, ideally without giving away the other team's words or the assassin.
- Field Operatives: The players who try to guess which words on the board belong to their team, based on the Spymaster’s clue. They’re doing the heavy lifting of deducing language, while hoping the clue doesn’t backfire into a misstep.
- Neutral Words: These don’t help either team; touching them ends a turn but doesn’t win the game.
- Assassin: If you guess this one, your team immediately loses. It’s the ultimate decoy hand of doom, designed to keep everyone humble.

### The Setup
1) Lay out 25 word cards in a 5x5 grid.
2) The Spymasters sit on opposite sides of the table so their word connections can align or hilariously misalign with their teammates.
3) A key card (known only to the Spymasters) reveals which words belong to which team, which are neutral, and which is the assassin. The key is reproducible so you can reset and replay the same exact disaster over and over if you want.

### The Turn Structure
- The Spymaster gives a clue: a single word that ties together multiple words on the board, plus a numeral indicating how many words on the board are linked to that clue.
- The Field Operatives discuss and guess. They can guess up to the number on the clue plus one extra guess if they’re feeling bold (and perhaps a little reckless).
- If a guess hits a blue word (opponent’s team), that word is flipped and that team takes a hit; if it hits a red word, it’s a success for the team; a neutral word just ends the turn without advantage; an assassin ends the game immediately with a loss for the guessing team.

That’s the crunchy mechanics in one breath: one clue, several words, and a very real risk of turning a casual group into a chaotic, witty debate club. The genius lies in the Spymaster’s balance of creativity and restraint. The Field Operatives’ job is to connect dots between seemingly random words. It’s a little “Linguistic Minesweeper” with mood lighting and snacks.
