## What is Face to Face? A quick overview

Face to Face is a multiplayer strategy and negotiation game designed for 2–6 players (the box says 2–7, but we’ve tested up to eight, which is basically when the table becomes a social experiment). The objective is simple on the surface: accumulate influence points by completing goals, forming alliances, and executing well-timed gambits. The twist? Your allies can be your enemies next round, and your enemies might become your most valuable co-conspirators if it serves your bigger plan.

At its core, Face to Face blends negotiation, resource management, and set-collection in a compact package. Players draft roles, manage a hand of action cards, and maneuver their characters across a face-to-face grid that looks suspiciously like a grid of a corporate strategic planning retreat. The game rewards quick thinking, a knack for reading tells (which are surprisingly visible on cardboard faces), and a willingness to pivot when someone points out that your plan has a fatal flaw: “you forgot to consider Bob’s plan for Bob’s plan.”

For those who need a theme anchor: imagine you’re a faction leader in a bustling metropolis of cardboard people, each with a strategic agenda and a secret objective that could upend your carefully laid plans. The flavor text isn’t Shakespeare, but the tension is real enough to make you consider a career in improv diplomacy if your day job isn’t cutting it.

## Components and setup

### What’s in the box

- A modular board with a grid of districts
- 6–8 character tokens with goofy expressions (the secret sauce)
- A deck of action cards with a mix of tactical moves and social plays
- Influence tokens, victory point chips, and goal cards
- A small rulebook that is surprisingly readable if you skim it while pretending to study the politics of the city
- Meeples! Because every good game needs something to pretend is a tiny person you can talk to during the turn

### Setup quickstart

1) Lay out the modular board in a configuration chosen by the start player. The table should look half basketball court, half mystery novel cover. 2) Shuffle the action cards and deal a starting hand. 3) Each player selects a role (or is assigned one randomly). Roles influence starting resources and special abilities. 4) Place the influence tokens on the central district, and deal a few goal cards face-down to each player. 5) Determine the starting player and give them a first-mover advantage that will be used for drama rather than actual efficiency. 6) Begin with a round of negotiation, moves, and occasional stares that imply “I’m not mad, I’m evaluating your soul through your face.”

Note: the first round often misleads. People mistake inverted logic for weakness; that’s where the real strategy begins.

### The face-to-face mechanic in one sentence

If you’ve played any negotiation game before, Face to Face takes the idea and adds a social chessboard on top: you bid, you barter, you swap, you backstab (nicely), and you keep an eye on your own objective while gently steering others toward theirs—only you’re secretly working toward your own end while appearing to support a friend who might betray you the next turn. It’s deliciously vicious in a wholesome, PG-13 way.

## Core mechanics explained

### Action cards and the tempo engine

The action deck is where the game breathes. Cards let you move tokens, steal influence, form temporary pacts, or pull a surprisingly powerful bluff. Some cards grant you additional actions on your turn, while others enable you to disrupt an opponent’s carefully-laid plans. The tempo—the rhythm of who acts when—matters as much as raw power. A well-timed card can swing the table from suspicion to outright hostility in a single round, which is exactly what you want if you’re trying to look cool while removing your rival’s best asset.

### Influence and goals

Influence tokens are the currency of power in Face to Face. You spend influence to claim districts, secure alliances, or cash in at the end for victory points. Goal cards provide private objectives that often require you to balance public moves with hidden ambitions. The intrigue here isn’t about who you pretend to be; it’s about who you genuinely want to become by round three, while not giving away your entire plan on the first turn.

### Negotiation as a system, not a side quest

This is where the game shines. Negotiation isn’t just a mechanic; it’s a framework. You’ll propose deals, promise non-aggression pacts, and occasionally feign weakness to lure others into a trap. The key is to keep one eye on your own objective and one on the moral compass you pretend to own. If you can read a room—especially the bowl of chips that signals someone is about to backstab—you’ll gain significant leverage without needing a single extra card.

### Endgame and scoring

The game ends when a player hits a threshold of influence or completes a set of goals. Victory goes to the one who not only achieves the endgame objectives but does so with grace, or at least with a flourish that makes the other players consider therapy. It’s possible to win by a single point, which means every misread, every bluff called, and every alliance forged counts toward the final tally.

## Strategy primer: opening, midgame, and endgame

### Opening moves: establishing your presence without overcommitting

- Scout early districts with low risk: you want information and influence, but you don’t want to corner yourself into a corner with no escape routes.
- Build a flexible alliance: the goal is to be relevant, not to own the table. If you overcommit to one player too early, you may pay the price when the table flips.
- Hold a bluff countermeasures plan: assume someone will call your bluff; be ready with a backup plan that preserves your face and keeps you in play.

### Midgame: control the tempo, avoid overextension

- Use the bluff deck sparingly but effectively. If you bluff too much you become a known risk; if you bluff too little you’ll get steamrolled by more aggressive players.
- Play “two-step” negotiations: first secure a temporary understanding, then secretly pivot toward your own long-term objective as soon as it’s feasible.
- Read the table: look for subtle tells—glances toward a specific district, the way a card is shuffled, the way someone smiles when they realize they’ve been cornered by a better option.

### Endgame: seal the deal without tipping your hand

- Time your cash-in moments carefully. If you cash in too early, you might reveal your strategy and risk a counter-move that negates your gains.
- Use public actions to mask your private objectives. Publicly supporting a rival’s plan while inching toward your own endpoint is a classic trick that actually works surprisingly well—if you do it with confidence and plausible deniability.
- Prepare a clean exit strategy: if you can walk away with a few points and minimal damage to reputation, you’re likely to get a second chance in the next round.

## Variants, variants everywhere

Face to Face plays well with variants that tweak the balance between negotiation and card power. Some groups add a timer to keep conversations brisk; others prefer “hard mode” where players must trade only through direct, face-to-face negotiation rather than hidden deals. A favorite among the community is the “public goals only” variant, which forces players to reveal their objectives at the start, a move that instantly raises the stakes and the humor when someone misreads a widely-known plan.

If you’re feeling spicy, try a rule where you can swap roles halfway through the game. It’s chaotic, it’s educational, and it’s a fantastic way to remind your friends that they should probably listen to you more often when you’re not playing a different game in your head.

### House rules that actually improve fun

- Time-box negotions: 60-second negotiation rounds to prevent analysis paralysis.
- Open-book endgame: reveal your endgame objective before scoring to create a burst of dramatic political theater.
- Friendly-fire safety net: any accusation of “betrayal” must be met with a well-timed apology in the final round. Keeps it civil, somehow.

## Play tips and common mistakes

- Don’t overcorrect after a misread. The table will forgive you if you pivot with confidence rather than panic.
- Don’t confuse “supporting” with “co-signing a bad plan.” Your goal is to align short-term moves with long-term objectives, not to be the moral backbone of everyone’s failure.
- Don’t ignore the endgame clock. It’s tempting to chase a mid-game win, but if you sprint too early you’ll burn out before you cross the finish line.
- Don’t forget to breathe. Seriously. The best players are the calmest. You’ll perform better if you’re not hyperventilating into your own strategy sheet.

## Final verdict: who should buy Face to Face?

Face to Face shines in a group that enjoys a mix of strategy and social interaction. It’s not a solo puzzle game; it’s a social experiment with a scoreboard. You’ll see players retooling their plans mid-round, compromising with a grin, then pivoting to a surprising tactic that leaves the table impressed and suspicious in equal measure. If your crew likes witty banter, clever misdirection, and occasional friendly backstabbing (the kind that ends with everyone laughing rather than tossing the rulebook across the room), you’ve found your next weekly rotation.

For new players, expect a few awkward turns as you learn the rhythm and timing. For veterans, you’ll relish the texture—the feel of a well-rehearsed bluff landing perfectly and the satisfying moment when you realize your plan was exactly as risky as you hoped. And if you’re one of those players who need a map and compass for every decision, this game will teach you to trust your social instincts in addition to your arithmetic.

## Quick starter checklist

- Gather 2–6 players who enjoy talking, bluffing, and occasionally double-crossing in a friendly environment.
- Set up the board in a central, accessible layout. Rate-limiting the table’s angle is essential to fair visibility for all players.
- Shuffle the action cards and deal out starting hands. The more you understand the distribution, the less you’ll rely on sheer luck.
- Assign roles and reveal the first round’s objective in a light, funny opening monologue. The mood matters as much as the moves.
- Keep a scoreboard ready and a timer handy for those intense moments where everyone suddenly becomes a chess grandmaster with a deadline.
