### What is Phase 10, again? A quick refresher for the uninitiated
Phase 10 is a rummy-tinged card game originally designed for 2–6 players (though we’ve seen it hosted with seven adults and one very patient child). The object is to complete all ten phases in order. Each phase has a specific combination, such as two sets of two, a run of four, or a run of seven with a set of fours attached. Players must complete a phase, lay down their cards, and then draw to continue chasing the ever-elusive best hand. The catch? If you fail to complete your current phase, you must try again on your next turn, which leads to plenty of dramatic “No, not my Phase 3!” moment—perfect for any family who loves a good melodrama.

### Quick rules refresher (with UK-flavoured twists)
- Each player starts with a hand of ten cards. The rest of the deck sits in the middle as the draw pile; a discard pile forms on top of it.
- Phases are completed in order. The phase list is printed on the card backs; there’s no need to memorize a twelve-step program unless you want to be that person who reads the rules aloud every time.
- On your turn, you draw one card (from the draw pile or discard pile) and then discard one. If you’ve completed your current phase, you lay down your hand (or just the relevant cards) in front of you and move to the next phase on your next turn.
- The first player to complete Phase 10 wins. If you’re playing with cheeky family members, you’ll discover new rules like “no showing your hand” or “sudden phase swap” that appear as the game goes on.
- Optional house rules abound: time-box rounds, newbie penalties, or a “soft play” option for the younger ones so they don’t feel left out when the rest of the table is feverishly counting phases.

### House rules for UK players: keeping peace in a lively household

- The “London Fog” rule (optional): if a player finishes a phase but then discards a card that could have helped someone else finish, they must announce it loud and proud, along with a witty rhyme about Phase 2. 
- Shuffle etiquette: the person who shuffles must perform a tiny victory dance (the “Phase Shuffle”) before handing the deck back. It’s optional but recommended for morale.

### Strategy and tips: how to tilt your way to victory without tilting the table
- Plan ahead, but not too far ahead. Phase 4 might require a specific set that’s currently out of reach; missing that card can lead to a night-long chase. Balance short-term gains with long-term opportunities.
- Card counting is optional, but paying attention to what’s been discarded and drawn can inform your decisions. If you’ve seen a sequence of runs that frequently appears in your circle, you know which phases to target next.
- Don’t ignore the power of the discard pile. It’s more than a graveyard for mistakes—it’s a potential treasure map if you read it correctly. If you can shape the draw somehow via clever discards, you’re basically a phase-ologist.
- Bluffing? It exists, but in a gentle, family-friendly way. When Dad pretends to be stuck on Phase 2 while secretly hoarding Phase 3 cards, it’s not cheating—it’s character development.
- Adaptability beats rigid strategy. If someone’s going for a specific run, pivot to a different phase or disrupt the flow by forcing a quick draw with a risky discard. It’s a great way to remind everyone that the deck has a surprising sense of humor.
