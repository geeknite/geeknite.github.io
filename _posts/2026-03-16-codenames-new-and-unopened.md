## What Is Codenames, Anyway?
Codenames is a word game with a secret-agent vibe and a casual party-game soul. Two teams compete to contact all their agents first, using one-word clues from their spymasters. The twist? Your clue must bridge multiple words on the table without accidentally guiding the opposing team to a booby-trapped assassin word. It’s a balancing act between cleverness and chaos, between I can tie this clue to five words and still avoid the danger-word and who left the blue alien card in the box again?

In the traditional setup, you’ll have a grid of 25 word cards laid out on the table. Each card hides a word that could be a person, place, thing, or concept. The spymaster gives a one-word clue plus a number — for example, Animal 3 could hint at three cards that share a theme with animals. The team then debates, argues, and occasionally bullies the table into consensus, picking cards one by one. The goal is to reveal all of your team’s agents before the other team reveals theirs, while avoiding the assassin card that ends the round immediately.

## Deep Dive: Mechanics, Strategy, and Player Roles

### How the Core Gameplay Works (In a Nutshell)
Two teams — red and blue — each with a spymaster. The spymaster knows which words belong to their team (via the agent cards) and which word cards are neutral or assassin. On a turn, a spymaster gives a one-word clue and a number indicating how many cards on the table relate to that clue. Everyone else tries to deduce which cards the spymaster is hinting at. If they pick a word on their own side, that word is revealed; if they pick a word on the opposing team’s side, that word is revealed to the other team; if they pick a neutral word, the turn ends; if they pick the assassin word, the game ends for that team immediately with a loss.

The brilliance of the clue is in the ambiguity. A good clue will point to multiple connected words without crossing into danger territory. A weak clue might be so specific that only one card is revealed, leaving the table to silently contemplate why a clue like the sushi of ideas was chosen. The best clues feel like inside jokes that only your group gets to enjoy—one that your friendly neighborhood meta-narrative might dare to repeat on a podcast without getting canceled by the word police.

### Strategy: From Newbie to Sly Whisperer
For beginners, the early days are about learning to manage risk and translate quick mental maps into safe phrases. Start with smaller numbers in your clues, because the first turns are about mapping players’ receptors to the board vocabulary. As you gain confidence, you’ll experiment with multi-word hints that tie several cards together. The trick is to maintain a balance between scope and safety: a clue like Animals 3 might connect to Tiger, Panda, and Whale — but you’ll also have to consider that Whale could collide with a more dangerous word, or that Tiger could spark a cascade of related cues that leads your team toward a dead-end labyrinth of possibilities.

Veterans often exploit misdirection. They use clues that pull in culturally loaded references or inside jokes that only a few players understand. This can be hilarious and a touch ruthless. It also means you should be careful about fairness and inclusivity; if you are playing with people who aren’t in on the joke, you might want to scale back those we all know this moments and default to more universal associations.
