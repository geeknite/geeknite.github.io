### The tech behind HDBaseT

HDBaseT squeezes audio, video, power, Ethernet, and control signals into a single shielded channel that travels over CAT cables. The result is a tidier rack, less cable clutter, and a signal path that travels hundreds of feet without the need for a mirror universe of distribution amplifiers. In practice, you connect the transmitter to the HDMI source, run a CAT cable to the receiver, connect the HDMI output to the display, and you are basically done. Of course there are knobs to tweak like EDID mode, possibly IR routing, and a little bit of witchcraft in the firmware, but the core idea is simple: one cable, one power, one heroic signal journey.

### Setup and installation — setting the stage for signal nirvana

Getting this kit working is not a ritual sacrifice to the AV gods. It is mostly plug and play with a dash of EDID tuning and a respectable amount of cable management. Here is the typical workflow you will follow:

1) Place the transmitter near the HDMI source. This can be a Blu ray player, a game console, or a PC GPU. Avoid placing it on top of a warm power supply unless you enjoy heat shimmer effects in your video. 
2) Run a CAT6a cable from the transmitter to the receiver. The better the cable, the more likely you are to enjoy clean HDR without shimmering artifacts. If you are running in a ceiling space or behind a wall, use staples and proper cable protection to keep the signal clean and your hands from becoming a tangle of frustration on a lazy Sunday. 
3) Connect the HDMI output from the transmitter to the TV or projector via the receiver. Make sure you are using a high quality HDMI 2.0 or 2.1 cable on the output side to preserve the best quality. 
4) Power up both ends. The kit usually comes with dual power bricks feeding the transmitter and receiver. If you can, label the power bricks so you do not perform a zero gravity juggling act when you upgrade your setup. 
5) If EDID mode selection is offered, decide whether you want the device to simply pass through the EDID from the display or to override to a fixed profile that suits your content lineup. This can help with compatibility in tricky setups where the source gets shy about HDR and color spaces. 
6) If you plan to use IR passback, configure the IR blaster and receiver in the same room as your remote controlled device. Then test a few IR commands to confirm they reach the source without a dramatic delay. 

Beyond setup, you can expect the device to hold hands with your HDR workflow, delivering color and brightness with that cinema vibe. If you have a projector or a very large TV, you may want to test multiple sources to confirm the EDID does not throw a tantrum and hand you a washed out picture. If you encounter issues, double check the CAT cable quality and try a slightly shorter run to measure if the distance is the culprit. Backup your EDID settings so you can restore quickly if something goes off the rails.
