## What Phase 10 actually is (and why this tin matters)
### The quick refresher
Phase 10 is a rousing, competitive card game that has you completing ten different phases to win. Each phase requires you to collect specific sets or runs, and the first player to finish all ten wins. It’s approachable for younger players but has enough wiggle room to keep adults entertained as well. The core pieces are a shuffled deck, a draw pile, a discard pile, and a set of ten different phases that drive the entire night. The standard setup is simple: deal out cards, figure out which phase you’re aiming for, and keep track of who’s keeping score as the night ages gracefully into chaos.

Color coding is a big part of the game, and that’s where the color blind accessible tin aims to flex its muscles. A lot of families rely on color to separate sets, distinguish phases, and keep track of who owes who what for the next snack break. For color blind players, color-coded cards can feel more like a puzzle with missing puzzle pieces. The Phase 10 Color Blind Tin seeks to reduce that friction by introducing high-contrast visuals, symbols, and an organization system that doesn’t depend solely on color perception.

## Color blind accessibility: what this tin actually does
### Visual cues with purpose
The core ambition of a color blind accessible design is to reduce the reliance on color alone for important game actions. In this Phase 10 tin, you’ll typically see:
- High-contrast card backs and fronts where allowed, ensuring players can distinguish between sets and phases without requiring perfect color perception.
- Symbols and patterns on phase cards and essential markers that offer a reliable second cue to color. Think shapes like circles, triangles, squares, and stars present in a visually distinct way.
- An indexing scheme that uses both color and symbol for quick recognition. Practically, this means you can at a glance tell which phase you’re aiming for and where you are in the current hand, even if you struggle to separate certain hues.
