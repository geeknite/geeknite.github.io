## Mechanics, balance, and table dynamics
City of Mist is built on a dance between logos (powers and identities) and myths (narrative hooks). Fortune Row’s objective is to add a tactile layer to that dance: a handful of physical representations of luck, risk, and consequence that can be manipulated by players and the GM alike. Here’s how that translates into real table impact.

### Luck as a tangible resource
The fortune chits and luck-adjacent prompts invite players to treat luck as a coin to spend in the scene. You can trade a fortune chit for a modest advantage, or save it for a climactic moment that will change the night. This creates a micro-economy of risk reminiscent of noir economies where every break comes with a price tag and a story hook. The risk, of course, is token hoarding: cautious players may accumulate resources and hesitate to use them, which can sap momentum if the GM doesn’t design moments that reward daring spending. The bundle’s recommended approach is to seed micro-mud moments where a spat of tokens can flip a scene from okay to iconic without breaking the dramatic balance. The tokens themselves scale across multiple sessions, which is a major plus for a Fortune Row arc that wants to feel ongoing rather than one-shot.

### Clues that propel scenes forward
The rumor and clue prompts are designed as movable hinges rather than rigid plot rails. They’re meant to spur improvisation and multiple route options, aligning well with City of Mist’s “city is a tangle” philosophy. When a rumor card lands in a player’s hand, you’re not locking them into a single path; you’re presenting a doorway that can be opened in many directions depending on the group’s choices. This is where Fortune Row shines for groups that love quick world-building: a single clue card can pivot a whole night toward a new lead, a fresh misdirection, or a dramatic reversal of a plan that players believed was airtight.

### The table rhythm and pacing
Fortune Row’s design nudges a cinematic rhythm: quick cuts, reveals, and group leaning in (the kind of moment where the room almost stops breathing to hear a whispered rumor). The quick references on inserts help you maintain momentum without rifling through the core rulebook mid-scene. The risk, as with any prop-heavy toolkit, is sensory overload. If you go too far, you can turn a noir night into a shrine to props, which is not the vibe most groups want when they’re chasing a mythic twist. My recommended practice is to pull in only a few key assets per session, rotate the rest, and allow players to earn the privilege to see the next card flipped. Fortune Row’s true magic lies in turning a small, tactile set into a shared ritual—an everyday magic that makes the city feel alive.

## Practical GM tips and party considerations
Here are practical guidelines to help you squeeze the most mood and momentum from the Fortune Row Accessories Bundle without letting the spell break.

### Use prompts as anchors, not shackles
Think of prompts as sparks, not scripts. When you hit a lull, pull a rumor card, location cue, or fate twist and let it steer the session toward a character moment. You want players to own how they interpret a clue, not feel commanded by it. A reliable tactic is to present two or three possible outcomes and let players vote with their actions on which path becomes the scene’s next star. This keeps the table agile, dynamic, and invested in the moment rather than following a prewritten beat sheet.

### Manage pacing with token economy
Tokens are a pacing tool as much as a resource. Use them to mark major transitions, or to escalate risk. When intensity spikes, a quick payoff using a fortune chit can launch a reveal or a combat encounter with the city’s own tension acting as a referee. Just be mindful of the total token pool; you don’t want to exhaust resources before you reach a satisfying climax. The rules aren’t about hoarding; they’re about paying for stories with tangible weight.

### Elevate the room with props, not a prop parade
Props are best when used as mood anchors rather than dazzle devices. A single street sign or a poster can catalyze a scene with a glow of legitimacy. If you overdo it, you risk turning your table into a stage production rather than a game. The key is to reserve the props for moments when they punch up a scene, and describe the surrounding space in language that makes the artifact feel alive rather than static.

### Integrate Fortune Row with existing mythos and logos
Fortune Row thrives when players sense the city’s mythic fabric pulling at their choices. Tie a location cue to a player motif and let that motif surface as a recurring thread. The result can feel cohesive, with the Fortune Row elements acting as connective seams that hold disparate scenes together rather than loose scraps that vanish after a single night.
