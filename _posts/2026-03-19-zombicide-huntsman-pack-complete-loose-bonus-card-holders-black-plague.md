## Introduction
If you thought your zombie apocalypse needs were satisfied by swarms of bitey plastics, think again. The Zombicide line keeps rolling out expansions faster than a zombie shuffles toward a plate of brains, and today we dive into the Huntsman Pack for Black Plague. This is the kind of add-on that can either turn a cozy game night into a heroic endurance test or, if mismanaged, become a chaotic scavenger hunt for your tiny plastic survivors. The pack I am reviewing here is described as complete but loose—meaning all the goodies are present, just without the protective box. Bonus card holders are in the mix, because apparently no one enjoys rifling through a pile of tiles and cards that sulk in a recommended state of chaos.

To set the stage for the uninitiated: Zombicide Black Plague is a cooperative miniatures board game where you control a band of survivors, hack at undead blurs with swords and axes, and try not to die in spectacularly gruesome fashion. The Huntsman Pack expands the standard zombie roster with new menace types and a few tricks up its sleeve that make you rethink every dice roll and door open. The added bonus card holders, meanwhile, are there to save you from the brain-muncha of card misplacement. In a world where a single missed card can ruin the turn order, these holders are either a genius lifesaver or a small reminder that you own too many components for your own good.

Before we dive into the nitty gritty, here are the quick takeaways you’ll care about if you’re eyeing this as a purchase:

- The Huntsman Pack adds new enemy types and one or two new mission cards that alter how you approach the board.
- The miniatures, as with most Zombicide sats, are on point for detail and scale; you’ll want to either paint them or admire them from a distance while your survivors heroically fail a few times first.
- Card holders are a surprisingly nice ergonomic upgrade for long campaigns and heavy card shuffles.
- It’s not the cheapest expansion, but if you’re all-in on Black Plague or you love hunting packs with thematic variety, it delivers on ambiance and challenge.

## What’s in the box (the complete, loose edition)
I’m not going to pretend there’s a magical inventory that fits onto a single page, because there isn’t. The Huntsman Pack (complete, loose) delivers a handful of components that rub elbows with your existing Black Plague setup and quietly says, Hey, we’ve got a bit more bite now.

- Huntsman monster miniature and related stat card: a new big nasty with a particular knack for ranged pressure and mid-range skirmish prowess. The name implies patience, but the gameplay screams move-fast-or-perish. He’s the kind of foe that makes you rethink if you should chase that last zombie biker through a doorway because, yes, the doorway may be a trap. 
- A couple of zombie variants and reinforcements: more bodies on the map means more decisions to juggle. It’s not just quantity; these variants bring new attack patterns that can punish lazy zoning and reward careful planning.
- New event or mission cards: these cards shake up the expected rhythm. You’ll be juggling objectives, upgrading your survivors in different ways, and suddenly a simple door corner becomes a puzzle that demands teamwork and precise timing.
- Bonus card holders: a practical add-on designed to corral those usually loose initiative or loot cards. The holders aren’t flashy, but they’re sturdy and sized to fit standard Zombicide card stock. You’ll notice fewer last-minute card flittering and a lot less frantic card-shuffling while you beg for one more action before the apocalypse arrives.
- A couple of tokens and reference sheets: nothing too fancy, but helpful for quick access during tense turns when you’re trying to remember who can do what and when.

The big question is how all these bits fit into your current Black Plague table presence. If you already own a few expansions with similar content, the Huntsman Pack will feel familiar, albeit with a tighter focus on the Huntsman mechanic. If this is your first foray into Black Plague extensions, you’ll want to keep a notebook handy, because the rules layer can feel like a mini game of its own—one that rewards cautious planning and punishes reckless bravado.

### The huntsman’s cameo: what to expect on the board
The Huntsman is the star turn of this pack, and his addition changes two essential things: how you approach the map and how you coordinate your squad. He’s designed to force players to consider routes, line of sight, and an estimation of how many zombie turns lie ahead. The opposing force is not simply a tanky brute; it’s the kind of antagonist that punishes bad information and rewards careful reconnaissance. If you enjoy a run-and-gun style session, you’ll still have your moments to break the board apart, but you’ll also be reminded that the board is a place of calculated risk as opposed to a carnival of bravado.

The other miniatures and variants are there for density and variety. They don’t reinvent the wheel, but they do offer a few new angles on classic zombies. Expect new attack patterns and some fresh symbolism for how your survivors break away from the hordes. It’s a reminder that in Zombicide, as in life, a little extra variety tends to keep the adrenaline humming rather than turning into a dull campaign grind.

## Assembly, painting, and overall quality
This is where the rubber meets the road for many Zombicide buyers: how good do the minis look on the table, and do they fit the game’s vibe? The Huntsman Pack continues the high-contrast sculpts you’ve come to expect from Black Plague era products. The miniatures are crisp, scale consistent with the base game and other expansions, and the poses are dynamic enough to avoid that “standing statue” look that plagues too many zombie sets.

A practical note: if you’re into painting, these minis are a bit more forgiving than some of the ultra-fiddly sculpts from other lines. They offer enough surface features to pick out with contrast paints and wash without becoming a time sink. If you’re a painter who wants to hobby your way through the apocalypse, this pack gives you a satisfying palette to work with without demanding hours you’ll likely never reclaim from real life.

The card holders are a no-frills addition, but they land where it counts. They’re sturdy enough to survive a campaign pile-up and small enough not to crowd your play area. If you game on the go, you’ll appreciate not having to dump the deck onto your table and blindly hunt for the correct card while you pretend you’ve got a strategic mind. They won’t convince you that you’re saving the world, but they’ll convince you that you’re saving your sanity.

## Gameplay impact: does it lift or drag the experience?
Let’s get practical. The Huntsman Pack is not a must-have for every table. If you’re someone who loves a clean, simple zombie slasher experience where the core set gives you all you need, this expansion adds a nice tinge of spice rather than a recipe for total rework. If, however, your group thrives on custom challenges and you like to mix up enemy behavior, the Huntsman Pack injects a new leash into the doghouse. It forces you to think ahead about where your survivors will end their turns, how you’ll shepherd the weaker characters to safety, and when to press the advantage or cut your losses.

One of the standout changes is the Huntsman’s counterplay potential. In many Zombicide sessions, you can punish a pile of attackers with a clever line of attacks and a successful block. The Huntsman complicates that by creating liability zones where you can’t pin him down as easily as other foes. It’s a subtle shift, but once you’ve learned the rhythm, you’ll start to appreciate how a single monster can alter the table’s tempo. This is where the expansion earns some respect: it doesn’t just add more minis; it revises how you approach the map, how you allocate actions, and how you plan your turn sequencing.

If you play with a sizeable group, you’ll notice the added challenge scales nicely. If your table is small or you’re frequently short-handed due to sick cats or real-world emergencies, the Huntsman might feel a touch punishing. The beauty of Zombicide is that you can tailor your experience with the right house rules or by selectively using the new cards. The Huntsman Pack fits well into that philosophy—enough new stuff to feel fresh, but not so much that it breaks the base game’s rhythm.

## Card holders: a small upgrade with a big impact
The bonus card holders deserve a separate spotlight. They serve a practical purpose without turning the entire game into a card management DJ set. What matters here is improved usability. You get rid of the card-slapping, the “where did that extra card go?” moment, and the occasional accidental discard of a critical modifier during a tense round. If you’re someone who nasally keeps a lot of modifiers and tokens handy, these holders are going to be your new best friend while you juggle multiple missions.

Visually, they’re unobtrusive, which is a compliment. They don’t scream look at me as you crush a Roman-novel villain with a glowing sword. They just exist, quietly doing their job. And in a game where tempo can swing on a single die roll, a small ergonomic upgrade may be the difference between a crushing victory and a leg-sweeping wipeout.

## Setup, playtime, and table presence
Setup time for the Huntsman Pack is basically: unpack, separate, count, and you’re ready to roll. It’s not a long process, but it’s definitely longer than a vanilla game night. If you’re prepping for a session after work and you want to minimize the fiddly bits, you’ll probably want to automate some parts of your setup with trays and organizers. The loose nature of this edition means you’ll be packing and repacking, so be prepared to keep the files in a way that won’t drive you insane between games.

Playtime can stretch a bit longer than base Black Plague scenarios due to the expanded enemy pool and additional cards. If you’re a veteran and you already know the scenarios well, you’ll be able to speed through the turn economy and still deliver satisfying outcomes. If you’re relatively new to Zombicide, you’ll want to build in extra time for learning curves and the occasional heroic misplay that becomes a glorious meme you carry home with you.

Table presence improves with these added miniatures. The Huntsman and friends loom over the board with a bit more presence than the average zombie, which makes table dynamics a little more cinematic. You’ll find that you’re spending more time describing terrain and line of sight than you did before—this is where the game really shines as a storytelling experience rather than a pure optimization puzzle.

## Value, pricing, and what you’re paying for
Value is always a subjective metric, especially in hobby board games. The Huntsman Pack isn’t the cheapest expansion by any stretch, but it offers a noticeable uplift in mood, tension, and narrative weight. If you’re a collector who wants a complete Black Plague ecosystem with a bit more tactical pressure, the price is justified by the density of extra content and the quality of the new minis.

If you’re budgeting for a year of gaming, you’ll want to consider whether the Huntsman Pack aligns with your playgroup’s preferred cadence. For a casual weekly crew, it can be a fantastic way to keep things fresh without investing in a new standalone game. For die-hard fans who already own a shelf full of expansions, this pack is a solid addition that deepens the tactical palette without requiring you to relearn the entire system.

## Comparisons to other expansions
A quick family reunion with other expansions helps frame the Huntsman Pack. Compared to smaller add-ons that provide a handful of cards or a couple of zombie variants, the Huntsman Pack sits in the middle—enough new content to justify the purchase, but not so much that it becomes its own mini-sandbox.

When stacked against major expansions that bring brand-new core rules or massive roster categories, Huntsman feels more like a mid-season enhancement than a full reboot. It’s the spice rather than the main course. If you like variety but don’t want to retool your entire strategy, this is the sweet spot. If you crave heavy rebalancing and new core mechanics, you might find yourself wishing for a bit more bite in a single purchase.
