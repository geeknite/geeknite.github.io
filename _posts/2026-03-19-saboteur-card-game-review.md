### What is Saboteur?
Saboteur is a card game designed around hidden roles and path-building. The core objective is simple and elegant: be the first team to reach the treasure by placing a valid path from the start to the treasure card. There’s one catch: not everyone is on your team. Some players are Saboteurs, set on steering the path away from treasure or otherwise tangling the route so that no one can win. The game thrives on people pretending they’re on your side, then pulling a fast one when you least expect it.

The design is light-hearted and colorful, with cartoonish dwarves and gleaming gold that looks suspiciously like it belongs in a pirate’s treasure chest. The art communicates rules cleanly and invites everyone to jump in without needing a degree in Mineshaft Etiquette. It’s the sort of package that makes you want to lay out a mat, grab a bag of chips, and declare a mining expedition worthy of a sitcom spin-off.

### Why Saboteur shines on a game night
- Easy to learn, hard to master: The rules are straightforward, but the social dynamics keep evolving as players switch from builders to saboteurs and back again.
- Fast rounds, big laughs: Each round is short, letting the group jump into a new scheme before the last scheme finishes collapsing. You’ll get more heartbeats per minute than a rollercoaster of emotion.
- Flexible player counts: Whether you’re playing with a trio or a full house, Saboteur plays well. Higher counts increase the suspicion, which is basically free entertainment.
- Accessible for non-gamers: It scratches the social-deduction itch without requiring you to memorize a gazillion card effects. The vibe is party-friendly, not card-nerd-athon.

### Components and Setup
Saboteur doesn’t rely on fancy components to shine; it uses a compact deck of cards that carry most of the drama. Here’s what you typically get and how it goes down:
- Path cards: These are the main workhorses. They come in varying lengths and directions, and your job is to lay out a path from the start to the treasure card.
- Action cards: Things that change the course of the tunnel—rotating cards, blocking cards, or a little bit of “take that” with a mischievous grin.
- Role cards: Each player is secretly a Builder (the dwarves who want treasure) or a Saboteur (the folks turning the wheel). A few neutral-ish twists may appear depending on the edition.
- Gold nugget tokens: The currency of victory. Grab the treasure, collect the gold, and bask in your own glory or blame-shifting.
- Start and treasure tiles: The physical endpoints of the tunnel that anchor the round and provide the dramatic “we did it!” moment when a path actually connects.

Setup is mercifully simple:
1) Shuffle the role cards and deal one to each player, keeping the roles secret. If you’ve got a larger group, you’ll have a couple of Saboteurs at least to stir the pot.
2) Place the start card and the treasure card face down at the opposite ends of the table. The treasure becomes the target that you’re all trying to connect to, or in some cases, a decoy you pretend to treasure while the Saboteurs smirk.
3) Deal a starting hand to each player. The deck comprises path and action cards, and your dexterity with these cards is what makes the rounds sing.
4) Decide how many rounds you’ll play (typical is best of three or best of five). The arena is set; your band of dwarves is assembled; the pickaxe is shining with potential mischief.

The first round begins when the first player lays down a path card adjacent to the start. If you’re a Builder, you’re trying to create an unbroken trail from start to treasure. If you’re a Saboteur, you’re trying to block, distort, or redirect that path so no clear route to treasure exists by the time the round ends. The tension hits early when someone tosses a rotating or double-backed card, and suddenly the path you hoped would lead to glory becomes a higgledy-piggledy maze of “what just happened?”

### Gameplay Walkthrough: The Rhythm of Saboteur
Saboteur rewards adaptability. Here’s a deeper dive into how a typical round unfolds, with a focus on flow rather than just rules:
- Turn structure: On your turn, you may lay a path card or play an action card. If you’re a Saboteur, you might use a blocker to halt a promising corridor; if you’re a Builder, you might extend the tunnel towards the treasure with a well-placed path card.
- Path-building strategy: Path cards often have directional cues. You’ll be looking for a sequence that can chain from Start to Treasure without hitting a dead end. The longer and straighter your tunnel, the more confidence you’ll feel in your own plan—until the last-minute twist reveals the Saboteur’s handiwork.
- The role reveal moment: At the end of a round, the roles of the players are typically revealed, and the outcome becomes clear: were you the mastermind builder who just barely squeaked out a victory, or did the Saboteur plant a last-second trap that snatched away the glory?
- Endgame and scoring: If the treasure is successfully reached, Builders score. If not, Saboteurs score or split the blame and celebrate the sabotage they pulled off. The exact scoring can vary by edition, but the sense of “we almost made it” is universal.

### Strategy Across Roles
- Builders: The best Builders quietly coordinate, signaling intent through card placement rather than loud declarations. It’s a delicate dance of reading the table and deciding when to push a risky path or pull back. You’ll find that the most successful Builders aren’t strictly loud or quiet; they’re adaptable and suspicious of over-eager path extensions that feel fishy.
- Saboteurs: Saboteurs are the wildcards. Their power comes from misdirection and well-timed block cards. The optimal Saboteur inserts doubt just enough to fracture a plan without giving themselves away too early. The best Saboteurs feel like traitors at a tea party: calm, collected, and terrifyingly precise in the moment of reveal.

### Variants, Expansions, and House Rules
Saboteur has inspired a few expansions and variants over the years. The core experience remains intact: you’re drafting a chaotic path with suspicious companions, which is already a heavy lift without extra bells and whistles. Some groups enjoy optional rules such as:
- Additional Roles: Introduce new secret roles to spice up the suspicion. A third faction can tilt the balance between builders and saboteurs.
- Timed rounds: Add a timer for each turn to boost pressure and hilarity as players frantically decide whether to play that crucial card before time runs out.
- Thematic twists: Swap in alternate treasure cards or set a different goal beyond treasure, like locating a mystical gemstone or escaping a cave in under a set number of moves.

As with many party-style games, some players love fiddling with rules; others prefer a clean, strict experience. Our recommendation? Start with the base rules to learn the rhythm, then experiment with light expansions if your group wants to lean into the drama.
