## Authenticity, preservation, and how to tell if this NOS gem is the real deal

Yes, NOS is a good thing. But NOS does not guarantee flawless beauty. Here are some practical checks and common sense guidelines you can use when assessing a 1991 International Games UNO deck:

- Packaging integrity: Look for a crisp seal and a cardboard insert that is still snug around the deck. If the seal shows significant crimping or signs of rewrap, you should consider a closer inspection for potential reseals or replacements.
- Card condition: The cards should show minimal to no signs of use. Expect the edges to be sharp, with perhaps a trace of aging on the card backs or minor color shift due to long-term storage. Faint scuffing on the backs is not uncommon, but heavy signs of handling may indicate use rather than storage.
- Card stock and finish: Vintage UNO decks from this period typically feature a durable stock; you might notice a slightly different texture and a less reflective finish than contemporary decks. If the stock feels unusually flimsy or the print looks misaligned, these could be red flags worth investigating.
- Print and branding: This edition will showcase the International Games branding rather than the more modern Mattel branding. If you flip the deck over and observe a logo layout that screams vintage, that can be a good sign—emphasis on the word vintage, not vintage as a euphemism for questionable quality.
- UPC and edition markers: Many NOS decks have a UPC or a location marker that ties the product to a specific distribution run. If you find a barcode that looks contemporaneous with the era, that’s a nice indicator. Conversely, a barcode that looks anachronistic could warrant a closer look or a professional opinion.

## Practical care and preservation: how to keep the nostalgia fresh

Preserving this vintage UNO deck is less about fancy gear and more about environmental control and gentle handling:

- Temperature and humidity: Keep it in a location with stable temperature and humidity. A closet cabinet away from direct sunlight is a good bet. Excess heat or moisture can warp the box or degrade the card stock.
- Handling: When you do inspect the deck, handle it with clean hands. Oils and residues can transfer to the cards and cause gloss changes over time.
- Display: If you plan to display it, consider a display case with UV-protective glass. A simple acrylic case can also do the trick, as long as it blocks sunlight and keeps dust at bay.
- Storage: For long-term storage, avoid stacking heavy objects on top of the box. A gentle, upright stand or a shelf with a soft backing can help maintain the integrity of the packaging.
- Cleaning: Do not scrub the cards to remove dust. If you must refresh the look, a dry, soft brush can lift surface dust without introducing moisture. Remember, you are preserving a time capsule, not performing a spa treatment for a modern deck.

## Authenticity checks: a tiny inspector gadget moment

Before you commit to buying a sealed deck, run a quick authenticity check:

- Compare branding to known examples from the era. If the box art, fonts, or color balance looks off, it might be a sign to investigate further.
- Inspect the seal’s edge. A perfectly even edge is rare but a strong indicator of original packaging. Odd irregularities can be a sign that the seal was replaced or rewrapped.
- Look for internal markings on the components. The presence of an original rule sheet and insert, aligned with the 1991 design language, is a positive sign. Missing pieces or unusual replacements should raise flags.
- If you can, physically examine the card stock. A genuine 1991 deck has a certain heft and spring to the cards when you feel the surface. If they feel notably flimsy or the print shows unusual wear for a sealed item, consider caution.

As with any collectible, there is a healthy balance between enthusiasm and due diligence. Enjoy the thrill, but do your homework to avoid seller pitfalls and misrepresented items.
