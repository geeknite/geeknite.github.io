## What is Block Block Burrito Expansion?
Block Block Burrito is an expansion that intends to turn your living room into a small-scale carnival of mayhem. The core idea is simple in theory but deviously clever in practice: add a modular board made of chunky Block Blocks, introduce new burrito projectiles with their own rules, and provide players with fresh power-ups that trigger in unexpected ways. The expansion leans into two high-impact design goals:

- Increase interaction and strategic counterplay without turning every game into a chalkboard of amendments.
- Preserve the core feel of Throw Throw Burrito—where chaos is a feature, not a bug—while offering new ways to bluff, misdirect, and accidentally fling a burrito at your own foot.

If you’ve ever asked yourself, “What if the burrito had a personality and preferred geometry?” Block Block Burrito answers with a set of modular blocks that can be arranged in dozens of ways, creating new paths, traps, and shelves for your enemy’s carefully curated self-destruction.

### Components and what’s in the box
The box (as usual with Exploding Kittens products) arrives with a compact, colorful aesthetic that straddles cute and chaotic. Here’s the typical inventory you’ll encounter:

## How to play with Block Block Burrito
The expansion threads itself into theThrow Throw Burrito core rules like a burrito thread through a tortilla. If you know how to throw burritos, you’ll recognize many of the core beats—but there are important new wrinkles:

- Setup: You arrange a grid of Block Blocks on the table. The number of blocks and their arrangement depend on player count and the difficulty level you want to attempt. The idea is that every game feels a little different, like a chaotic blueprint for a carnival ride.
- Turn flow: On your turn, you perform two actions (draw a card, move a burrito, or push a Block Block to trigger a chain reaction). The Block Blocks introduce new movement rules and line-of-sight considerations; you’ll be surprised how often geometry becomes a factor in whether you get splatted by a flying burrito or emerge victorious by muttering a clever pun.
- Burrito projectiles: The new projectiles behave differently than the standard plush burritos. Some bounce, some flatten, some explode into a harmless shower of foam confetti. The key is to time your throws to maximize pressure on your opponents without accidentally hitting yourself. Yes, you will hit yourself. This is a game about embracing your own incompetence and pretending that it’s strategy.
- Event cards: These cards can tilt the table in seconds. They might force all players to pass their burritos to the left, or reverse the direction of gravity for a round, or create a “fast-forward burrito mode” where everything moves twice as fast but you gain an extra life if you land a perfect toss.

The rules are still humane enough for casual players while providing enough nuance for power players who want to optimize every toss. The rookies will fumble with grace; the veterans will deploy a cunning mix of misdirection and block-chain reactions that would make a physics professor blush.

### A sample turn in Block Block Burrito land
1) Draw Phase: You reveal a Block Block synergy card that gives you a temporary bonus if you manage to knock out two or more burritos in a single move.
2) Action Phase: You either slide a Block Block to open a new line of attack or pivot a burrito throw toward an opponent whose defenses are vulnerable.
3) End Phase: The board recalculates, and a couple of blocks wobble dangerously. If you’re unlucky (or maybe very unlucky in a perfectly unscripted way), a chain reaction buries your strategy in a stack of foam and giggles.

The important part is to read the room: Block Block Burrito punishes arrogance and rewards improvisation. It’s not a chess match; it’s a carnival where every move could send your own team tumbling into the snack bar of doom.

## Gameplay pacing and table experience
Block Block Burrito tends to slow down the most chaotic moments of Throw Throw Burrito, but it does so in a way that feels organic rather than punitive. You get more bite-sized decisions per round, and the modular board creates new choke points and escape routes. The pacing shifts from “everyone is flinging like caffeinated mayhem scientists” to “we’re orchestrating a bongos-and-tax-cut rhythm”—still chaotic, but with a rhythm you can learn and enjoy.

One of the surprising strengths is how this expansion scales with different groups. New players can enjoy a gentler introduction with simpler Block Block layouts, while seasoned players can crank up the difficulty to test memory, spacial reasoning, and a surprising degree of cooperative sabotage. The game’s humor remains a constant booster seat for the night—quirks line up, the banter flows, and you’ll find yourself laughing in the moment and re-living it later with a carefully edited montage of clumsy throws.

## Strategy and takeaways
If you want to extract maximum value from Block Block Burrito, consider these tips:

- Master the space, not just the target: The Block Blocks create new lines of play. Learn how to funnel opponents into bad angles where your burrito toss becomes an unavoidable cluster of chaos.
- Fusion plays beat pure force: Combine an event card with a Block Block trigger to force multiple reactions on a single turn. It’s like multi-tasking, but the tasks involve throwing foam burritos and saying clever things.
- Bluff and misdirect: Maintain a careful cadence that keeps others guessing. If your opponents suspect you’ll aim for a high-score move, throw a low-impact toss and set up a larger trick next turn.
- Protect your assets, but don’t hoard: The risk of blocking your own team’s lines is high; balance aggressive plays with defense to keep your squad from being buried under a mountain of foam.
- Keep track of the “gravity” rule sweeps: Some events flip gravity for a moment. Position your pieces to exploit these moments rather than fight against them. The moment a burrito bows to gravity is the moment you either seal a win or break a lamp—kudos to theater of the absurd.

For newcomers, the key is to stay flexible. The Block Block burritos are not about perfect precision; they are about clever improvisation under pressure. For veterans, the game rewards you for planning a few moves ahead, but never forget to keep a little chaos in reserve. The best strategies in Block Block Burrito feel like dance moves: you lead with a step you didn’t plan, and your partner—your fellow players—responds with a wild twirl that ends in spontaneous laughter and a slight sense of accomplishment.

## Comparisons to base Throw Throw Burrito
If you’re asking whether Block Block Burrito replaces the core experience, the answer is no. It expands and adds a new layer. Here’s how it stacks up:

- Complexity: Block Block Burrito adds a layer of spatial reasoning and card synergy, but it remains accessible. It won’t turn away casual players; it just tempts them to experiment with new setups.
- Interaction: The expansion amplifies interaction. Block Blocks create more blocking opportunities and force players to think two steps ahead—not just one-throw ahead.
- Replayability: The modular board means no two sessions look the same. You can shuffle the Block Blocks in countless ways, producing new tactical relationships and slightly catty banter at the table.
- Thematic coherence: The burrito motif stays central; the new elements feel like natural extensions rather than forced add-ons. This is crucial for maintaining the game’s identity while expanding its horizons.

If you loved the core concept and want a more dynamic battlefield, Block Block Burrito is a no-brainer. If you’re on the fence about Throw Throw Burrito in general, this expansion won’t magically fix every issue; it will, however, give you more chances to misplace a burrito and pretend it’s all part of the plan.

Block Block Burrito remains friendly to families and groups that enjoy silly chaos. It’s not a serious strategy title; it’s a party game that encourages playfully malicious behavior. The added complexity can be charming for older players or hobbyists who enjoy puzzle-like elements while happening to have an abundance of energy to spare.

For accessibility, the game’s components are color-coded and clearly labeled. The rules are readable, and the layout of the board is designed to minimize confusion during the fastest moments of play. If you have players with quick reaction times, you’ll appreciate the fast-paced nature of the new rules. If you have players who are colorblind, the design team did a decent job of using distinct shapes in addition to color, which helps identify blocks and burrito types. In all, it’s a solid step toward a more inclusive chaotic party game pivot.
