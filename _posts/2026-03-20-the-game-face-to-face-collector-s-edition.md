## How It Plays: A Quick Tour of the Face-to-Face World

Face to Face is a social strategy game about reading cues, managing reputations, and coordinating your moves to maximize face time—literally. The objective is to accumulate influence by successfully predicting others’ choices, all while managing your own public image. In a world where you have been told to multitask, the game becomes a laboratory for one essential skill: yes, you guessed it, reading your friends’ faces.

### Setup and Turn Structure
Setup takes about 5–10 minutes, depending on how well you know your players and how thoroughly you lined up the elaborate tokens. Each player receives a board, a handful of face tokens, and a set of action cards. The central area on the board hosts a rotating “face phase,” where players reveal a face token and commit to a course of action that may affect everyone else’s plans.

Turns proceed in rounds. Each round features a face-off phase where players reveal a token or pair of tokens to determine who gets to set the tone for the next set of decisions. The challenge isn’t simply choosing the action; it’s timing it so that your choice aligns with others’ behavior patterns in a way that looks like a well-choreographed improv scene rather than a chaotic dumpster fire.

### Core Decisions and Bluffing
The heart of Face to Face lies in the balance between transparency and deception. You want to seem honest enough to gain trust, but not so honest that your rivals steal your strategies. Bluffing is subtle here: you’re not bluffing about a card you hold, you’re bluffing about your true intentions. The Collector’s Edition’s improved components make this feel more tactile—the click of a marker, the weight of a token as it slides into a zone—giving you a slight, delightful dopamine hit every time someone buys your read.

### Endgame and Scoring
Scoring comes together in a crescendo of public reveals, a few surprise bonuses, and a last-second twist that makes you reevaluate the entire session. The final tally rewards those who managed to keep a consistent narrative about their alliances, while punishing those who kept switching sides like a daytime talk-show host with a switchboard obsession. It’s a satisfying wrap, especially if you’re the kind of player who enjoys the drama of a well-executed timing gambit and the catharsis of hearing the room groan when a bluff is finally busted.
