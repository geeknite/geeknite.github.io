## How to Game: Setup and Playthrough for 2–5 Players

Sushi Go Party shines when you can set up quickly and slide into the action. Here’s a practical walkthrough for a typical 40-minute session with two to five players.

### Setup

- Place the tin in the center of the table. If you’ve got Velveteen Tokens in your bag of tricks, line them up on the side for easy reach. 
- Shuffle the current menu cards and deal a hand to each player, depending on the number of players. The drafting method is identical to the base game: you choose one card, then pass the rest to your neighbor, repeat until all cards are taken.
- Prepare the scoring cards and place them within easy view. The party edition keeps the scoring threshold clear, so nobody has to squint at a screenshot of the rulebook while the group yells “Blue Plate! Blue Plate!” across the living room.
- If you’re using the velveteen pouches, you can place them at the edges of the table as a tactile reminder that you own the pouches and you’re not afraid to use them for organizational glory.

### The Drafting Phase

Drafting is the real heart of the game. You pick one card from your hand, then pass the rest to the player on your left. This continues until no cards remain. The tension in the room builds as you see your neighbors assembling the sets you fear you’ll have to skip. The fun comes from predicting what your opponent will take and trying to foresee how the rest of the table will respond in the next round.

### Scoring Phase

After a round of drafting, you tally points based on the sets you collected. Sushi Go Party isn’t about brute force; it’s about the elegant symmetry of sets. A few points here, a few there, and suddenly you’re the sushi savant of the living room. The inclusion of a rotating menu means you’ll be chasing different patterns from game night to game night. The total score is the sum of all rounds, plus any end-of-game bonuses if your table uses the dessert or special plate cards. 

### The 2–Player Experience

Playing Sushi Go Party with two players can feel surprisingly tense in a delightful way. The drafting becomes a tighter tug-of-war, and due to the smaller pool of opponents, each card you pass has a slightly higher chance of being the exact card your rival desperately needs. The game remains light and upbeat, which makes it an ideal break during a long session of heavy strategy games or as a palate cleanser after a marathon of dungeon crawls.

## Buttoned-Down Play and House Rules

Even though Sushi Go Party prescribes a fairly straightforward flow, there are a few house rules that can turn a good game into a great one. Some players like to experiment with: 
- Adjusted draft rounds for a quicker finish during busy nights. 
- Optional “unknown card” drafting where players reveal their chosen card after passing, adding a small bluffing element. 
- A “fast round” variant where you shave a couple of minutes off the timer to keep the energy high. 

The velveteen pouches can be repurposed for these variants as a tactile reminder that some rounds are quick, while others require deeper tactical consideration. The beauty of a tin game is that if you decide to start experimenting, you won’t feel guilty about making a mess — you can just toss the cards back into the tin and be ready for the next misadventure.
