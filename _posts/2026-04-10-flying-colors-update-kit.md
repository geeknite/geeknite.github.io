## Palette in Action: A Miniature Demo

### Step 1: Priming and Base
Start with a clean model and a reliable primer. The Update Kit shines here because you can pick a base shade that matches the overall mood you want for the figure. The trick is to keep your base thin so that you can layer without losing detail.

### Step 2: Shade and Layer with Update Colors
Apply a midtone base color from the kit. Add shadows with a deeper hue, then highlight with lighter tones from the same family. The included glaze options help you push the shadows toward the lighter end without sacrificing contrast. You’ll want to keep your glaze layers very thin and controlled to avoid pooling in the recessed areas.

### Step 3: Glaze for Cohesion
Glazing is where you unify the piece. Try a pale glaze over the whole model to bring subtle warmth or coolness to the surface. This is especially effective on faces, armor edges, and fabric folds where you want a consistent tone rather than a patchwork quilt of colors.

### Step 4: Final Highlights and Touch-Ups
Finish with precise edge highlights on raised ridges and pick out the reflective points on metallic surfaces. The kit’s metallics, while not the star of every show, add a nice punch to armor and weapon details without screaming for attention.

### A Quick Note on Lighting
Lighting will either make or ruin your glaze work. If you’re painting under strong LED daylight, you’ll see more pigment saturation; in a warmer lamp, colors may shift a touch. This is not a bug; it’s reality. Having a consistent lighting setup will help you judge your progress accurately and avoid over-darkening or over-brightening your highlights.

## The Geek's Tidbits: Lighting, Varnish, and Display
Lighting matters, varnish matters, and display cases matter more than you think. A matte varnish will settle colors into a realistic, grounded appearance, while gloss can enhance metallics and saturated colors for display purposes. Satin varnish is a nice middle ground for those who want a balance between realism and pop. If you plan to showcase your work, consider a simple lightbox or a daylight lamp. It’s not cheating; it’s science with better lighting.
