## What is Codenames: Top Secret?
Codenames: Top Secret is Vlaada Chvatil’s espionage-flavored spin on the classic party word game. It retains the core mechanic of your two teams racing to identify their agents on a grid of words using single-word clues from their spymaster. The twist? The box brings a spy-gadget aesthetic, a set of mission-style missions, and a few house rules that push the game toward a more thematic and, frankly, more chaotic experience. It’s designed for groups and venues that enjoy humor, strategy, and the occasional overconfident leap of deduction.

For players who adored regular Codenames but wanted a dash of noir—Top Secret tries to deliver that with a wink. The result is a game that feels like a spy caper you’d play during a puzzle-filled weekend rather than a dry corporate training exercise. If you’re the type who loves code names, cryptic hints, and the thrill of a perfectly chained clue, you’ll likely appreciate the way Top Secret leans into that vibe.

The box nails the theme with a production quality that matches its ambitious premise. Here’s what you’ll typically find inside:

- Spymaster screens or brief cards that guide the clue-giving process. Depending on the edition, these might be more elaborate than your standard hints, nudging players toward riskier connections or spicier combinations.

### Setup
Setup time is similar to classic Codenames—roughly 2 to 4 minutes, depending on how meticulously you arrange your grid and how many players are at the table. The steps usually look like this:

1) Lay out the 25 word cards in a 5x5 grid.
2) Assign teams (red and blue) and hand out the spymasters’ cues.
3) Decide who starts first (this affects which color cards are the “first team” and gives a slight advantage, which is a small but real factor in any competitive setup).
4) Shuffle the mission/secret objective deck and place it within easy reach of both teams.
5) Place the starting player’s assassin or other “do-not-choose-this” icon in a visible spot so the players know which words to fear.

If you’re the type who loves a tidy setup, Top Secret will test your organizational skills but won’t demand a full tactical operations research degree to play. It’s not a chore; it’s a vibe. ### How to Play (The Quick Rundown)
Codenames is a team-based word-guessing game where a spymaster gives a one-word clue followed by a number. The clue word should relate to multiple words on the board that belong to their team. The number tells the team how many words on the board are connected to that clue. The catch: you must avoid words that belong to the opposing team, the neutral words, or the killer word (the assassin). If you select the assassin word, you instantly lose that round.

Top Secret adds a few flavorful twists to this core mechanic:
- The spymaster’s clue words may be encouraged to lean into espionage imagery (think “Espionage, 3”) rather than purely literal connections.
- Mission cards introduce temporary constraints or alternative victory conditions, nudging players toward different risk/reward calculations.
- Some rounds feature “secret info” that only the spymaster has access to, adding a layer of secrecy that mirrors the spy theme.

On a typical turn, the team that is guessing will debate and deliberate until they either strike a correct word, hit a wrong word, or exceed the number of words indicated by the clue. The tension is delicious: a good clue can pull in multiple words at once, creating a cascade of swoons and groans as the table pressurizes to declare victory or doom.

### The Top Secret Twist: What Changes Compared to Regular Codenames?
The biggest changes you’ll notice are thematic and rule-adjacent rather than a full reimagining of the core gameplay. In Top Secret you’ll see:
- A more cinematic or spy-focused feel in both artwork and flavor text. It’s less “just guessing words” and more “solving a mission under cover of darkness.”
- The presence of mission cards that sometimes enforce narrower clues (limit to one word that describes exactly two or three board words, for instance) or slightly different scoring quirks. These keep the game brisk and replayable without wrecking the base tactics.
- A few new tokens or markers that behave like “intel points” or “cover stories,” which can subtly affect risk calculation. They’re not game-breakers, but they do nudge players toward different paths than your standard Codenames session.

If you’re already hooked on Codenames and crave a themed upgrade with minimal learning overhead, Top Secret scratches that itch without forcing you to re-learn the wheel. The learning curve remains friendly, and for new players, the spy-flavored world is a warm fan that makes it easier to explain the basic goal: identify your agents while avoiding the assassin.

### Strategy and Tips (From a Friendly Geek’s Desk)
Here are practical ideas you can actually use without needing a degree in cryptography:
- For Spymasters:
  - Build clues that touch multiple board words but avoid overloading a single clue with too many options. The sweet spot is three or four words, sometimes five if you’re feeling bold and the grid is forgiving.
  - Use thematic clues sparingly. A good spy clue like “Containment, 3” could connect to words like “lock,” “safe,” and “cover.” It’s about a mental map rather than a literal dictionary match.
  - Watch for the assassin word. If you’re eyeing a risky connection, weigh the odds: is one misstep worth snagging two critical teammates’ words? Often not.
- For Guessers:
  - Listen for the bonds your spymaster is trying to create, but don’t overcommit. In Top Secret, slight misalignment can bite you twice because the mission cards can introduce penalties.
  - If you’re uncertain, go with the safety card: guess one word at a time. It’s tempting to unveil three words at once when you’re feeling confident, but the risk is real and the tension can derail the round.
  - Communicate clearly; the guessing phase is as much about the team’s shared understanding as it is about raw inference. If someone’s hint seems too cryptic, ask clarifying questions and avoid derailing the entire game for one bold guess.
