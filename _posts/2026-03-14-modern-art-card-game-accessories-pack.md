## What’s inside the pack

The box contains a handful of components that look and feel premium, but don’t overshadow the core game. Here is a breakdown of what you will find, with notes on how each piece changes the mood of the table.

### 1) Money tokens upgrade and money tray

The pack replaces some of the generic cardboard bills with color-coded, slightly larger plastic tokens. The color palette is modern-art friendly — a blend of warm earth tones and bold primary accents that feel purposefully imperfect, like a study on balance by a frustrated sculptor. The money tray is a tidy, shallow plastic insert with labeled slots for hundreds, tens, and fives. It makes counting during a tense auction feel a little more cinematic, which is to say you can pretend you are a curator who is about to funding the next avant-garde installation rather than just paying for pizza.

In practice, the bigger tokens are easier to handle and less likely to slide off the table during a heated bid. If you have players who fidget, the tactile satisfaction of moving chunky coins can speed up memory recall on who currently has the most art-charm power in the room. The design cue here is not to make the coins more valuable, but to give you a satisfying physicality for the money that makes the game feel more consequential.

### 2) Card sleeves and protectors for art cards

The Accessories Pack includes sleeves designed to fit the art cards snugly. They protect investment cards (the ones that hold the bidders' choices) from fingerprints and accidental drips of soda, which is a real hazard in game night. The sleeves are clear and slightly frosted, which reduces glare under your kitchen-lamp sunset and keeps the art colors resonant. A welcome detail is the soft, non-glare finish that minimizes reflections when someone with a strong overhead light tries to pre-immerse themselves in the card art.

Sleevability improves long-term play. Cards will survive more sessions and still look sharp on a crowded table. The downside is a minor increase in shuffling friction when you are not used to working with sleeves, but that is a small price for preserved art and less sticky vinyl on the fingers.

### 3) Art cards display stands

The pack adds small stands that let players prop their favorite art cards during the bidding or after a round ends. The stands are designed as a simple, low-profile acrylic piece that can hold cards upright at a slight tilt. It gives a charming studio-gallery vibe to the table and helps everyone keep track of which art cards have been placed on display, which are in the bidding pool, and which are still tucked away in the deck.

The stands are not essential, but they are delightfully ergonomic. They reduce the need for players to keep leaning and squinting at the same corners of the table to remember the last placed card. If you have a table that invites dramatic lighting or a streaming setup, the stands can make your shots look more intentional and less chaotic.

### 4) Organizer tray and storage solution

One of the nicest touches is the tray that helps separate auction layers, art cards, and money tokens into clearly labeled zones. It is not a full-blown insert, but it is a smart, compact organizer that fits inside the box or in a drawer. The idea is that when you finish a round, you can reset quickly without digging through piles of cardboard tokens that somehow accumulate overnight.

The storage solution is a small but satisfying upgrade that shines when you play multiple sessions in a row. It reduces the chance of misplacing pieces and gives you a crisp, presentable setup for your next game night photo.

### 5) Rule clarifications and mini inserts

The pack includes an expanded, glossy mini insert that reprints a handful of clarifications from the core rulebook. These are not transformative changes, but they address a few common ambiguities that show up in multiplayer chaos. The mini insert has a couple of diagrams and quick-reference tips, designed to be handy during the first few plays after the pack arrives.

This is a thoughtful addition. It reduces table friction and helps new players acclimate to the auction cadence without constant page-flipping.

### 6) A small piece of style: display mat and care card

To tie the look together, a slim display mat is included. It doubles as a wrap-around art board on the table and serves as a decorative centerpiece during a round. The care card explains basic cleaning and storage recommendations, ensuring everything stays in good shape after the first art-festival chaos of the night.

The display mat may feel a touch premium, but it is not purely aesthetic. It gives you a consistent, stylish surface that harmonizes with the art cards and tokens, which matters when you want your photos to pop on social.

- It enhances readability. The display stands and upgraded tokens make it easier for players to identify the status of each card, which is especially valuable for stubborn late-game rounds where memory fog can creep in.

You will catch people glancing at the table with a slightly more reverent look, as if they are about to bid on a rare print rather than a set of card decks.

It also reduces the chance of misplacing art cards between rounds, which means fewer frantic rummages and more playtime. For players who love the flow of Modern Art as a bidding game, this feels like a natural improvement rather than a distraction.
