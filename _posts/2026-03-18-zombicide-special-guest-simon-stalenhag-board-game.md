## Introduction
In the land of cardboard and plastic, where the zombies are as predictable as a Tuesday and the art looks like it was painted by someone who once found a vending machine that dispensed hope, Zombicide Special Guest lands with a guest star quality that could fill a stadium but instead fills a kitchen table. Yes, dear reader, today we’re chewing over a box that promises to merge the moody mysticism of Simon Stålenhag with the frantic, friendly chaos of Zombicide. If you ever wanted a zombie apocalypse where the monsters stand in the rain and stare at old tractors while you try to rescue a cat, congratulations: you are about to get your wish.

### The Concept
Zombicide Special Guest is not just a skin swap. It is a full mood upgrade. Stålenhag’s art style—rusted machinery, retro future, lonely roads—bleeds into every card, board texture, and plastic survivor miniature. The game invites you to step into a world where the undead are less about brain munching and more about existential dread accompanied by a phantasmic barn with a neon glow. The Special Guest edition is built around a campaign that threads through a small town, where you must piece together clues about what happened, while fending off waves of the undead and the occasional perplexing encounter with a robot or a lamb that looks oddly suspicious.

### Aesthetics
The production quality is strong. The board art is tinted in melancholy teal and orange. The miniatures’ sculpts capture the 80s sci-fi vibe: bulky exosuits, weathered coats, and a vibe of hope slowly eroding under a range of blackwash and dry brushing. The tokens and cards carry a consistent aesthetic. If you enjoy staring at art that looks like a painting printed on dozens of cardboard chits, you will be in heaven. The game uses the same base system as other Zombicide games, so fans will feel at home with the general flow. The difference is in the mood, and mood is something you can actually measure with a mood meter: you flip a scene card and the board suddenly looks like a still from a moody sci-fi film.

### Components
The box includes:
- A modular board with decay textures
- A handful of survivors with diverse silhouettes
- A deck of event cards that twist the world into new shapes
- A stack of zombie tokens in different varieties
- A special guest character card featuring a unique ability
- A handful of environment cards and terrain tokens

Build quality is solid. The card stock is sturdy, the insert holds everything neat and tidy, and the imagery looks sharp with a nice matte finish. The art direction is the star here; the mechanical bits are serviceable.

### Gameplay core
Here we get to the actual game. Zombicide uses a cooperative system: you and your crew must work together to survive the night, complete objectives, and avoid getting eaten by undead and the occasional Roomba of doom. The Special Guest edition keeps the core system but adds new twists: environment cards that alter the terrain mid-might, special guest mission cards that reference Stålenhag’s world, and a campaign track that encourages replayability.

Turn structure is familiar for Zombicide fans: players take actions on their turn, then the zombies activate, and the board changes. The twist in this edition is that environment cards spawn new hazards that feel thematically aligned with the art: rusted machines waking up, tractor silhouettes humbling the party, and shadows that bite. The new guest-themed events do not simply bolt new rules onto the game; they reframe how you approach each objective.

A few specifics:
- Actions: Each survivor typically has two actions per turn. Actions include move, search, close combat, ranged attack, or use an item. In this edition there are also unique actions tied to the special guest card, such as calling in a friendly AI drone or humming to coax a vehicle to start.
- Objectives: The mission pack includes short scenarios and a longer campaign arc. Some scenarios require you to uncover a hidden facility, others require you to stage a daring extraction under a time pressure. The pacing is strong: you get small wins that build to a meaningful finale.
- Enemies and encounters: The zombies advance in waves and sometimes come with special abilities. The environment cards can add hazards like acid rain or power outages that complicate the hero's path.

Tip: If you know the base game, you know the rhythm. If you are new, expect an action-packed co-op experience with a little pull-your-hair-out moment when a horde spawns on your last move.

### Coop and Solo
The cooperative play remains the central thrill. The presence of a campaign with a narrative thread gives players motivation beyond simply “clear rooms.” The campaign also includes a few optional solo modes that let you test the mission design without a full group. The solo mode uses AI rules for the zombies and the environment cards; you can still feel the tension and the sense that you are being watched by a robot in the distance.

### Thematic synergy
The magic of Simon Stålenhag’s art is not only in the visuals but in the sense of place. The town, the fields, the distant silhouettes of machines—these elements translate into tactile gameplay: scenario cards that depict a gloomy farmhouse with a robot in the back pastures; a cul-de-sac that becomes a trap; a salvage yard that becomes a puzzle. This edition nails the mood. The soundtrack of the game, if you use one of the many fan-made playlists, will add to your experience. The game becomes a living, breathing melancholic adventure rather than a mere survival horde.

### Comparison with the base game
Zombicide Special Guest shares the core engine with the base Zombicide line: the action economy, the threat of undead, the need to manage resources, and the satisfaction of a successful mission that involves teamwork. The difference here is not just a few new cards; it is the tonal shift. The base game has bright colors and sometimes cartoonish violence; Special Guest leans into palette choices that feel cinematic: desaturated blues, warm oranges, and a sense that every room could be your last. If you enjoyed the base game but wanted something with more story, more atmosphere, and more of a painterly vibe, this edition scratches that itch.

### Campaign progression and replayability
In Zombicide Special Guest, there is a campaign map that tracks your party’s progress across the town. Each completed mission unlocks new areas to explore, new enemies to face, and new narrative beats to reveal. The replayability is fairly strong. The variety of environment cards means you can experience different challenges on a second or third playthrough, and the guest card ensures that your party approach can change depending on the character you pick.

### Artwork deep-dive
One of the most delightful aspects of this edition is how the art direction informs the board and the encounter cards. It is not enough that the survivors wear heavy jackets and the zombies look like something out of a roadside museum; the game uses the imagery to foreshadow encounters. A field that looks serene on the surface may hide a machine-driven menace underneath. The cards often include a little flourish of language that references a specific landscape, a weather pattern, or a piece of tech that belongs in a 1980s sci-fi novel. The result is that each scenario feels like stepping into a painting rather than simply flipping a coin to see if you succeed.

### Strategy and tips
- Establish a control zone near a critical objective early on. The environment cards may close off routes later; having a safe space to retreat is invaluable.
- Balance action economy with scouting: you want to search for resources but avoid leaving your squad pinned in place when the horde approaches.
- Use the unique guest card to your advantage. Some survivors excel at controlling machines or coaxing derelict vehicles back to life. Use those moments to leap ahead on the board.
- Don’t forget the environment cards. They are not optional: they can alter the terrain dramatically and create opportunities to flip the game in your favor if you time it right.

### The good and the bad
- The mood and aesthetics are the standout. If you love Stålenhag’s kind of world, you will be delighted by the visuals and the tonal feel.
- The game remains a bit punishing in solo mode, but the AI system is balanced enough to keep things tense without being unreasonable.
- The rules, while familiar to Zombicide players, can feel dense in early play. It is worth dedicating a session to learning the rules and then diving into the campaign.

## Verdict
Zombicide Special Guest is not a casual pick. It is a love letter to a specific mood, wrapped in a robust co-op system. If you want more than just a zombie deck-building experience and you crave a sense of place and narrative, this is a must-have for your shelf. For newcomers to Zombicide, this edition can be an ambitious entry point, but be prepared for a learning curve and a longer playtime. For hardcore fans who own the entire line, Special Guest offers a fresh lens through which to view the core engine and a new reason to bring friends around for a game night.

### Final recommendation
- If your group prioritizes atmosphere, mood, and narrative, pick this up.
- If you want a quick pick-up-and-play session with glossy art and light rules, this may feel heavy but still rewarding.
- For solo players who like a challenging but fair AI opponent and a campaign, this edition shines.
