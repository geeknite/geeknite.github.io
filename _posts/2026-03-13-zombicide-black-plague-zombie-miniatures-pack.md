### Painting Considerations
If you’re planning to paint these minis, you’ll want to treat them like the little theater troupe they are: give them a base coat, then a wash down to bring out the shading, and finally a dry brush on the raised areas to emphasize texture. A simple palette consisting of bone, rusty copper, dark greens, and purples can deliver a convincing undead presence without turning your hobby time into a color-matching nightmare.

One trick worth trying is painting in batches. Separate the “slower” details (armor crevices, teeth, and eye sockets) from the general skin tones, so you can keep your momentum going without getting bogged down in minute highlights. The plastic takes paint well, and if you use a proper primer (gray or black works fine), you’ll get good coverage with fewer coats. If you’re feeling extra fancy, you can experiment with edge highlighting on armor plates to create a dramatic silhouette on the board in low light—a setting not uncommon in our gaming rooms where the lights dip just enough for dramatic gloom.

## Painting and Model Tips for Maximum Impact
For painting enthusiasts, this pack is a playground. The details reward careful work, and the variety invites experimentation. Here are some practical tips to get the most out of your minis:

- Prime in a neutral gray or black to increase contrast. White primer can work, but it often requires extra coats to reach full coverage on shadowed areas.
- Start with a base coat of skin tones for the living threats, then move to darker washes for the undead: sepia, brown, or a black wash to bring out recesses.
- Dry brush metallics and bone colors to bring edges forward. A light silver on armor plates can catch the light as your survivors step into a narrow alley.
- Consider color-coding factions or zombie variants by applying a consistent accent color (e.g., red for bloodied variants, blue for frost-bitten undead) to help players quickly identify threats at a glance.
- Use a matte varnish to reduce unwanted shine that can distract from the miniature details during gameplay.

If you’re not into painting or you prefer a quick-turn approach, you can still benefit greatly from this pack by keeping the minis on the board as “on-model” blurs; even unpainted, the shapes and silhouettes communicate enough to elevate the tension and storytelling of your session.
