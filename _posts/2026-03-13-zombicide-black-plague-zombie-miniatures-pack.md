### Painting Considerations

If you’re planning to paint these minis, treat them like a troupe of stage performers: base coats, washes to pull out the recesses, and dry brushing to emphasize raised texture. A palette built on bone, rust, dark greens, and purples yields a convincing undead presence without devolving into a color-mest of chaos.

Batch painting helps: separate slower details (armor crevices, teeth, eyes) from broader skin tones to maintain momentum. The plastic accepts paint well, and a solid primer (gray or black) yields good coverage in fewer coats. Edge highlighting on armor plates can create dramatic silhouettes that read even in dim lighting—the kind of mood you want when the lights dip in your gaming room.

If painting isn’t your jam, you can still deploy these minis as on-model silhouettes to maintain tension and storytelling even in a bare-bones setup.

## Painting and Model Tips for Maximum Impact

This pack is a playground for painting enthusiasts and a practical boost for players who prefer minimal prep. Here are practical tips to maximize the impact:

- Prime in neutral gray or black for best contrast in shaded recesses.
- Base skin tones on living threats, then apply darker washes for undead—sepia, brown, or black wash to deepen the brows and sockets.
- Dry brush metallics and bone tones to brighten edges; silver on armor catches the light as survivors advance into a narrow alley.
- Color-code variants with a consistent accent color to aid quick threat identification on the board (red for bloodied, blue for frost-bitten, etc.).
- Seal with a matte varnish to tame shine during table light.

If painting isn’t your jam, you can still deploy these minis as on-model silhouettes to maintain tension and storytelling even in a bare-bones setup.
