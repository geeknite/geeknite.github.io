### Condition, Grading, and LP What It Means for Value
Grading is a language, and in Pokemon vintage circles, the language is spoken with an accent that could melt the plastic. The common lexicon uses terms like LP (Lightly Played), EX (Excellent), NM (Near Mint), and occasionally “shadowless” or “first edition” as if you’re listing a vintage fountain pen rather than a trading card. For LP cards, expect minor scuffing or edge wear, minimal creasing, and a flat surface that doesn’t threaten light reflection. The value calculus becomes a blend of science and folklore: how perfect is the front, what about the back, do you see printer flaws, and did this card survive the fairgrounds of your childhood with its corners intact?

## Care and Display: Protecting the Queen's Crown
Care is the simple luxury of card collecting. Here are practical tips:
- Use sleeves compatible with Japanese print sizes to avoid border abrasion.
- Place the LP card in a rigid top-load or screw-down holder if you plan to display it. It protects against accidental bendage from coffee spillages and enthusiastic cat pawing.
- Store away from direct sunlight to prevent color fading and ink migration. It sounds dramatic until you see the price of a sunburned No. 044.
- Avoid excessive handling. Oils from hands can degrade the surface over time, so use clean, dry hands or gloves when handling your prize.
- Document your ownership with a cheap scan and keep it in a digital archive. You never know when your binder will become a family treasure, and you want to prevent any “lost card, found at garage sale” drama from turning your sleep into a mystery novel.
