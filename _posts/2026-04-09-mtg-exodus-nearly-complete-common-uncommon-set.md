## What does “Nearly Complete Common & Uncommon” actually mean for Exodus?
Let’s start with a practical definition before diving into the glue that holds this subset together. In the Magic universe, a set’s commons and uncommons are the bread-and-butter tier—the cards you see most often in drafts, the cards you flip through in binder pages without needing a microscope, and often the ones you first learn to love (or hate) about a given color bias or archetype.

The phrase “nearly complete” signals two things: first, that you’re unlikely to ever achieve 100% completeness in the real world due to misprints, distribution quirks, or reprint cycles; and second, that the subset is still rich enough to meaningfully study the color pie and the draft experience, even if a few stubborn common or uncommon cards remain elusive or nonessential.

In Exodus, the common/uncommon pool acts like a skeleton key: it unlocks theme decks, casual queues, and budget brews that still feel thematically consistent with the era. You can craft a two-color aggro deck, a midrange thicket, or a tempo control shell using mostly genera of this subset without needing to splurge on rarer power—though certain uncommons definitely carry heft, either through efficiency, flexible mana costs, or the occasional surprise effect that bends how you approach the game. The near-complete aspect is a fun reminder that for many players, the game’s heart isn’t always about the rare chase—it’s about opening a booster and discovering a card that speaks to your playstyle in a loud, slightly ridiculous way.

For collectors, the Common/Uncommon pool is a gold mine of information about how Exodus aged. It tells a story about reprint cycles (or lack thereof), how the card art across the subset resonates with the set’s broader flavor, and how values drift with supply and demand without turning into a dragon you can’t ride. It also teaches the important lesson that a complete set doesn’t always equate to a pile of flashy rares; sometimes it’s about savoring the little things—the two-mana accelerator that’s actually playable in a casual build, or the common creature with a subtle synergy that shines in a draft pod on a humid Saturday.

## The Commons: everyday power and the art of “the more you see it, the more you like it”
Commons are the bread, butter, and occasional questionable cheese of a set. They are the first cards you’ll hand to a new player and the ones you’ll reach for when you want to tell a story about a draft night that spiraled into a color-morted chaos of timeless humor. Exodus’ commons, in this nearly complete pool, tend to emphasize a few recurring themes:

- Efficient two-drop bodies in red and green that push early pressure and set the tempo of a game. These are the kind of cards that teach you how to measure the pace of a match and why a 2/2 on turn two can feel like a win when your opponent whiffs a removal spell.
- Creatures with simple, flavorful keywords that don’t require a glossary to understand. The charm of a clean, direct creature for two mana is timeless, and Exodus’ common pool leans into that. You’ll find creatures that hit the board, threaten to hit again, or enable a simple ‘play and attack’ line that feels absolutely core to the set’s cadence.
- Slightly above-average removal or disruption for the mana cost, enough to keep you honest but not so oppressive that a casual player feels like the game is decided before you draw lands. In the Exodous commons, these pieces often show up as efficient two-for-one options that don’t require you to twist your deck into a pretzel to use them well.

If we were to name-check a few exemplar commons from Exodus without getting into specific card-by-card naming (and risking misremembering a real card name), we’d describe them like this: “Two-drops that trade efficiently, a couple of pingers that poke for 1 damage, a small but relevant card advantage engine that costs just enough to feel fair, and a creature with an ability that punishes over-aggression without breaking the bank.” The end result is a core set that feels robust, which means you can realistically draft with a handful of friends and walk away with a playable, cohesive deck.

## The Uncommons: nuance, power, and the “I didn’t realize I needed this until I did” moment
Uncommons tend to carry the deck-building weight when you start moving beyond the low-cost beaters. In a near-complete Exodus CU set, uncommons often provide the following roles:

- Card-advantage or card-selection tools that don’t require you to pay a premium in mana.
- Removal or targeted disruption that is efficient enough to matter in a draft environment but not so aggressive that it dominates the format.
- Symmetry-breaking effects that enable you to find your colors, fix your mana, or unlock a specific synergy you’re chasing.
- Flavor-intense creatures or artifacts that push the set’s theme but don’t require constant heavy investment to be valuable.

What makes uncommons special in this subset is their ability to support a coherent game plan without turning your deck into a one-trick pony. The “nearly complete” pool means you’re likely to encounter a handful of uncommons that do the exact job you needed in a given draft, and you’ll remember them with the fondness that only a well-timed two-for-one can impart. If commons are the drums, uncommons are the melody with occasional guitar riffs that remind you why you started playing in the first place.

A practical tip for collectors diving into Exodus’ uncommons: build a small, color-coded wishlist. Don’t chase every unknown card—chase the uncommons that either enable your favorite archetype or cover a glaring gap in your color pairings. This approach helps you avoid blinking into a pile of uncommons you’ll never play and instead focus on the ones that actually improve your deck-building odds in real games.

## The hunt for the nearly complete CU set: practical strategies
Completing a near-complete Common & Uncommon set is less about hunting one last legendary and more about constructing a process—one that respects your time and your wallet. Here are some practical strategies to approach this noble quest:

- Start with a checklist. Create a color-centric or archetype-centric checklist of the commons and uncommons you actually want. This helps you avoid impulse buys that you’ll never use. The Exodus CU pool has a lot of variety, and a plan helps you separate the signal from the noise.
- Sort by color and rarity, then by cycle. Sorting helps you spot gaps in your collection that you otherwise might miss. A typical cycle approach is to sort by color, then by mana costs, then by whether a card is a creature or not. It’s not glamorous, but it’s efficient.
- Don’t overlook misprints and variant printings. The wrong printer or a misprint can be charming, not just valuable—which is a fancy way of saying you might end up with a “rare in disguise” in your common stack. If you’re a binder collector, pay attention to the foil vs. non-foil dynamic, because the same common can look radically different depending on the printing.
- Play the long game. Exodus’ common/uncommon pool shines when you use it as a motivation to play more casually or with friends. You’ll learn which cards you consistently reach for in draft, and that knowledge translates well into other sets, especially when you’re trying to teach a new player the joy of a well-timed removal spell.
- Use digital tools for cross-referencing. There are plenty of community-maintained lists that help you keep track of which CU cards exist, what their printings are, and which ones you’re missing. While we won’t cite external data in this post, a quick search can unlock a world of organization hacks and printable checklists.

## The art, flavor, and the vibe of Exodus CU: a nostalgia trip with a laser pointer
Exodus was a product of its time: art that leaned into the chaotic, characterful flavor of the late 90s, with a dash of sci-fi inspiration and a lot of fantasy that felt hand-drawn and unapologetically imperfect in the best way. The common and uncommon cards mirror this vibe—designs that look approachable when you skim them, but reveal tiny details if you stare long enough. The lines between common and uncommon art sometimes blur in a delightful way: a simple creature on a field of color can carry a lighting-level mood that makes you grin at how much personality is packed into a two-drop.

In practical terms, the Exodus CU pool often reminds you of the era’s drafting experience—the way a set’s early picks shape the rest of the game, the way certain color pairs dominate the draft spectrum, and the way a single unusual common can suddenly unlock a whole deck archetype you hadn’t considered. If you’re chasing “the perfect nostalgic game night,” the CU subset can provide the backbone for a few nights of memorable plays without requiring a mortgage or legal advice from a GP.

For those who crave cross-media nods, the Exodus period is rich with flavor that’s easy to translate into modern formats. The art direction and thematic choices invite easy comparisons to fantasy literature and pulp sci-fi, which makes a lot of people smile when they pull a card that looks like it belongs in a different planet’s tavern. If you want a deeper dive into the flavor notes and how they intersect with the commons and uncommons, this is a great set to revisit with a friend who’s never seen a cardboard dragon before.

## Where to find and how to acquire Exodus CU cards today
- Local game stores (LGS): The LGS environment is the best place to hunt—especially for a nearly complete CU set. You’ll find folks trading in bin piles, or you’ll discover a friend who’s finally willing to part with cards you’ve been pestering them about for years.
- Online marketplaces: If you’re into shipping cards across continents and negotiating with strangers who may or may not ship the same day, online marketplaces are your jam. You’ll encounter a broader pool of copies and print runs, but you’ll also face the usual caution: condition, misprints, and the occasional red flag.
- Trade nights and collector communities: These are the places where you’ll find someone who has exactly the missing set you want and is willing to negotiate in the most friendly, conspiratorial way possible. Trading is part of MTG’s social glue, and the common/uncommon pool is often the easiest to trade to complete because the risk-to-reward ratio feels fair for both sides.

If you’re serious about completion, consider setting up a search alert or a simple spreadsheet to track your progress. It saves you from that sinking feeling when you realize you’ve traded away the one common you need—yes, the one that was sitting in your binder with a tiny sticker on it that says “DO NOT TRADE.” The binder is a social artifact as much as it is a storage solution.
