## CGE Codenames Pictures Board Game 2nd Edition The Top Secret

Welcome to a deeper dive into CGEs Codenames Pictures 2nd edition, a party game that swaps wordplay for image scavenger hunts and somehow still ends up with the same chaotic energy as a tabletop version of a reality show audition. The Top Secret vibe is strong here, with spies and image clues turning your living room into a quickie art museum where you are constantly betrayed by a toaster that looks like a clue card. If you know the original Codenames, you expect speed, misdirection, and the occasional awkward pun. If you know Codenames Pictures, you expect a gallery of photos that may or may not resemble your aunt s vacation album. The 2nd edition amplifies both the art and the tempo, and its micro improvements are enough to justify a new box on the shelf and a fresh debate about what counts as a clue.

### The top secret difference in 2nd Edition

In the 2nd edition, the card art has been refreshed for clarity and humor. The images pop with more color and the card backs have a slightly less busy pattern, making it easier to distinguish at a glance which cards belong to your team. The improved iconography on the board helps prevent the classic who did i tap which card confusion after a couple of rounds, which is a polite way to say the table will start arguing about whether a card is red or blue. There are a few new special cards that add a touch of spice to the standard red blue neutral format. The assassination card remains a deadly but essential mechanic that keeps players on their toes. If you enjoy the original s vibe, you will likely enjoy the 2nd edition s refinements without losing the spirit.

## Components and setup

### What s in the box

The box is a parade of color and curiosity. You get a deck of large image cards, multiple agent markers in red and blue, neutral tokens, assassin tokens, and a double sided board that marks off clues and scoring. The images range from candid snapshots to stylized collages that feel almost like a memory game for your childhood road trips. The components feel sturdy, with thick cards that survive being shuffled, rifled through, and occasionally dropped in a chaotic coffee spill moment. The 2nd edition has improved card stock and crisper artwork compared to the first run, which means less edge wear and fewer complaints about frayed corners after week two.

### Setup time

Setting up for a party of four to six players takes about five to seven minutes. Shuffle the image cards, lay out the board, assign red and blue spymasters, and randomly choose who goes first. The rest of the players gather around the table like scouts awaiting a signal flare while the spymaster arranges a few starter clues. The first few rounds are a gentle boot camp to get everyone used to the rule quirks and to calibrate your ability to interpret an image as a multi layer clue rather than a direct reference. If you rewatch a couple of rounds, you will see patterns emerge: some players are good at spotting abstract associations, others are more literal and best off with obvious clues that point to a specific card on the table.

### Components quality and design

Art direction in Pictures is its secret weapon. The second edition leans into bright color palettes and a mixture of photographic and illustrated images that feel modern and accessible. The cards are large enough to be read at a casual distance and the clues on the extremes of the table do not blur into a color blob when two players lean in. The spymaster board is minimal but functional, and the token system is intuitive: red for one team s agents, blue for the other, neutral tokens for safe gates, and the assassin for the big red button of doom. The quality is consistent with CGEs reputation for sturdy components and a product that holds up to frequent play sessions.

## How to play a round

### Setup your team and pick a spymaster

Two teams, red and blue, each with a spymaster. The spymaster s job is to give one word clues that tie several of their team s pictures together while avoiding the opponent s cards and the assassin. The other players try to interpret the clue and pick cards that fit. The tension rises quickly as the clock of camaraderie ticks away. A good spymaster uses a clever blend of associative thinking and common sense while avoiding references to the last season of a niche TV show that only your cousin watched.

### The clue and the turn

On your turn, the spymaster can give a single word clue followed by a number that indicates how many cards on the table are connected to that clue. The number sets expectations: more cards can be guessed, but the risk goes up with each guess because the assassin card lurks in the mix. The teammates discuss and point at image cards they think align with the clue. Then the smart players mark off cards with red or blue tokens depending on their team s color. If a team guesses all its cards, they win the round and keep playing until someone claims victory. If they hit the assassin, their turn ends immediately and the other team gets a chance to catch up.

### The top secret catch: image interpretation

The core skill in Pictures is translating visual elements into a cohesive clue. This is where the Top Secret energy shows up: players need to find a line between a visible object in a photo and a broader concept that ties together other targets. Some teams like to cluster around a shared cultural reference, others rely on color, shapes, or mood. The images can be silly, surreal, or surprisingly domestic; you may find yourself connecting to a card that depicts a sandwich and realize your clue also binds a chef, a kitchen, and a rainy afternoon. The challenge is not only to be clever but to be precise enough that your teammates do not accidentally pick up the wrong cards especially the assassin which is the universal buzzkill.

### Example playthrough snippet

A round begins with the red spymaster dropping a clue like playful hurricane and a number two. The team s brains light up: a cartoon cat scratching a post, a beach scene with a sunset, and a bicycle leaning against a wall could become those two targets. The players discuss whether the clue is too ambiguous, whether the team is ready to take a risk, and whether the coffee on the table has suddenly become a clue in this round. After a burst of debate, they place two tokens on two cards. The blue team counters with a cautious two card guess, hoping to lock in a safer path to victory. The assassin card hides in a corner, and someone gasps as the turn ends with a misstep that turns the tide of the game. This is where the fun lies: you celebrate the small wins and tolerate the near misses while everyone tries to read the room and the imagery better.

## Strategy tips and play styles

### For spymasters

- Build clues around one clear cluster of images, but avoid overloading with too many cards. A two or three card clue is often better than a risky four or five card clue.
- Use broadly connected themes that your teammates can latch onto quickly. A well targeted clue saves time and reduces confusion.
- Keep track of your own team s guesses and how they react to types of clues. You may notice patterns that help you tailor future hints.

### For guessers

- Start with the cards that match the most obvious elements first. If a card depicts a recognizable object, it is usually worth trying before moving on to more subtle associations.
- Communicate clearly and avoid over analyzing. The game is built to reward rapid, confident decisions that feel right to the majority.
- Watch for the assassin and be mindful of near misses. A cautious guess sometimes yields a guaranteed next turn instead of an immediate loss.
