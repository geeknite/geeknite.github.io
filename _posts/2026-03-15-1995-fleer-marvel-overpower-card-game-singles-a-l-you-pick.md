H2: What is OverPower? A quick refresher for the curious

OverPower was Fleer’s ambitious foray into the Marvel Universe in a card-game format that combined collectible collecting with combat strategy. Each card represented a hero, villain, or supporting character with a defined power profile, a cost to play, and a few special abilities that could alter the flow of battle. The goal was straightforward in theory: assemble a stable of heroes, use their powers to outwit your opponent, and outlast their team by exploiting synergies and timing. The tactile joy—thick cardstock, glossy artwork, and the satisfying click of a well-timed power—made it feel more like a miniature fantasy battle than a mere card exchange.

The “Singles A–L You Pick” concept is a compact slice of that larger ecosystem. It’s the chance to curate a small but potent roster from the alphabet’s first dozen letters, each chosen for distinct flavor, power curve, and nostalgia factor. Whether you’re trying to recreate a favorite cinematic moment, or you’re chasing a long-lost collector’s dream (that rare foil! that misprint! that one with the different cape hue!), the A–L lineup offers a microcosm of the game’s design philosophy: bold ideas, bold colors, bold names.

H2: Singles A–L: Card snapshots and how they feel on the table
Below are the A–L picks, each with a quick snapshot of why they matter, how they play, and what you might be hoping for when you draw them in a match. We’ll keep the tone fun, but also practical: these aren’t just pretty pictures; they’re strategies waiting to happen.

H3: A — Ant-Man

- Card snapshot: Ant-Man (core power user, size-shifting antics, nimble dodges)
- Why he matters: Ant-Man gives you micro-scaling disruption—think of him as the optimist who believes every problem is a tiny atom you can rearrange with enough confidence. In play terms, he’s great for tempo if you like prodding the enemy with clever positioning and low-cost tricks.
- When to play him: Early-game tempo setter or mid-game disruption specialist.
- Aesthetic note: The art has that mid-90s gloss, with a tiny hero looking heroic even when dwarfed by a giant villain’s silhouette. It’s goofy, delightful, and somehow still cool.

H3: B — Black Panther

- Card snapshot: Black Panther (steely prestige, martial prowess, tech-leaning bite)
- Why he matters: Panther embodies a balanced striker with a hint of tech-savvy support. He loves to sprint into fights with a calculated strike and then anchor a corner of the board with defensive options.
- When to play him: Mid-game anchor, especially if your opponent relies on heavy single-power plays.
- Aesthetic note: The suit design and art direction feel regal, a nod to the character’s royalty and the comics’ sense of sleek, ceremonial tech.

H3: C — Captain America

- Card snapshot: Captain America (shield bash, leadership aura, resilient hero)
- Why he matters: Cap is quintessential: a reliable frontline with synergy potential. He can soak hits, buff allies, and occasionally break through with a disciplined move that punishes overextensions.
- When to play him: As a dependable core for your early-mid game where you want to survive and stall for better combos.
- Aesthetic note: The iconic shield, the star, the vigor—classic Americana as a card. It’s comforting in the most nostalgic way.

H3: D — Daredevil

- Card snapshot: Daredevil (stealthy, agile, sometimes punchy in unexpected ways)
- Why he matters: Daredevil slots into a hit-and-run playstyle. He loves misdirection and speed, dazzling opponents with quick hits that disrupt their rhythm.
- When to play him: Useful when you want to trigger chain effects or punish a slower opponent by forcing them to react rather than plan.
- Aesthetic note: The red and black motif pops; Daredevil’s art conjures a nighttime, city-hush vibe that feels perfect for a tactical skirmish.

H3: E — Elektra

- Card snapshot: Elektra (assassin flair, precision strikes, mobility)

H3: F — Mister Fantastic (Fantastic Four)

- Card snapshot: Mister Fantastic (stretchy control, field manipulation, inventive power use)

H3: H — Human Torch

- Aesthetic note: The light pastel tones and sparkly effects capture the character’s youthful, energetic vibe.

H3: K — Kitty Pryde

- Card snapshot: Kitty Pryde (phasing, tactical repositioning, escape routes)
- Why she matters: Kitty Pryde is a movement prerogative: if your meta tilts toward predictable positioning, Kitty reshuffles the board and your plan with it.
- When to play her: When you need to save a key unit from a strong counter, or set up a surprising follow-through that catches the opponent off-guard.
- Aesthetic note: The “phasing” idea is visually represented with translucent layers that emphasize speed and evasiveness.

H3: L — Loki

- Card snapshot: Loki (trickery, misdirection, control over fate)
- Why he matters: Loki plays the mind games card. Not the largest attacker, but he can manipulate the battlefield to tilt outcomes in your favor by forcing suboptimal plays from your foe.
- When to play him: Mid-to-late game when you want to flip a situation or force a nasty dilemma on your opponent.
- Aesthetic note: Loki’s silhouette sits in a theatrical pose—clever, slightly smug, and perfect for a card that loves a good bluff.

H2: Deck-building tips with A–L singles

Putting together a coherent deck from A–L isn’t about simply throwing power onto the table. It’s about sequencing, resource management, and exploiting moments when your opponent has left a window open. Here are some practical tips you can try at home:

- Start with a core duo: pick two cards that complement each other’s abilities. For example, a frontline brawler with a secondary control card can help you weather the early game while you set up your late-game engine.
- Balance speed and power: you want some fast, low-cost options to pressure your opponent early, but you’ll also need reliably strong finishers to close games once the tempo shifts in your favor.
- Build around synergies: if you have a card that buffs allies or triggers on successful blocks, ensure you pair it with attackers who can capitalize on that advantage in the next turn.
- Resource management matters: figure out how many cards you can play per turn and plan your turns so you don’t overcommit and get punished for it.
