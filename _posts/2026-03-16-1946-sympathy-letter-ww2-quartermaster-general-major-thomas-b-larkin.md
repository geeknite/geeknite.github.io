## Who was Major Thomas B Larkin
To understand the letter, we should know who wrote it. Major Thomas B Larkin served in the Quartermaster General’s office during World War II. The Quartermaster Corps was one of the oldest branches of the U S Army and handled the lifelines of war: rations, clothing, medical supplies, construction materiel, and even transportation support. They had to keep millions of men and women supplied across multiple theaters of operation, often in the most inhospitable conditions. The job demanded a blend of logistics mastery and human sensitivity because the people who read the war’s casualty lists or the last shipment of a damaged plume of rain gear were real families waiting for news.

Larkin, as a Major who rose through the ranks, would have known that good supply chains become emotional lifelines. When a unit faced a setback, a delayed shipment or a misrouted crate could add strain beyond the physical. The letter writers in such positions understood the need to acknowledge loss, express sympathy, and communicate practical support. The human element was never optional for a quartermaster officer; it was the core of their mission as seen through the letterhead and the closing signature.
