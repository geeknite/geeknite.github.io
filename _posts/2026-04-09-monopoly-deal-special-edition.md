## How to play (quick refresher)
Monopoly Deal is a fast-paced card game where your goal is to collect three complete property sets by playing cards from your hand. You’ll do that by playing property cards to your personal “world” area in front of you, paying rent to others with money cards, and utilizing action cards to disrupt opponents and defend your own empire.

The Special Edition doesn’t reinvent the wheel; it sprinkles a few fresh cards that add new little wrinkles to the strategy. You’ll still draw five cards per turn, discard down to seven, and keep a few secret tools up your sleeve. The main difference is the inclusion of a few new action and property cards that can shift the tempo of a late-game sprint. Expect more tension around auctions and trades, as well as more art that begs for a social media photo with your friends.

> For readers who learned with the original, the rules remain approachable. The action cards are easy to grasp, but timing is everything—like a good punchline.

## Special Edition cards and new mechanics (expanded discussion)
The heart of any Special Edition is the new content. In this edition, you’ll find:
- Additional action cards that add utility: swap sets, steal cards, copy effects, draw extra cards.
- Property cards with new color codes and rarer property sets that require more planning to complete.
- A handful of “twist” cards that hinge on timing; you’ll use them to disrupt other players’ progress or to protect your own path to victory.

Mechanically, these new cards don’t break the game; they simply tilt the board slightly toward aggressive play. You’ll find yourself racing to complete a property set faster, then defending that set with a borrowed rent card or a Forced Deal that mutates your opponent’s layout. It’s nothing revolutionary, but it creates a more dynamic late game, which is exactly what we want in a party card game that’s designed to be played in under 60 minutes.

In terms of balance, the new cards are not overpowered. They require texture and timing; they’re not capable of singlehandedly ending a game unless you’re already cruising. The risk is that new players will default to the same few safe moves, so if you’re introducing someone to Monopoly Deal, you might want to pair the Special Edition with a brief primer on how to value property sets and how to use action cards with intent.

## Strategy and play tips (expanded)
- Build a flexible property plan: don’t lock into a single color. The Special Edition’s extra property cards give you more color options, so you can pivot if someone’s paying attention.
- Use action cards to protect your path: if you see someone closing in on a three-set moment, a timely Swap or Forced Deal can buy you the breathing room you need.
- Manage your money cards: the Rent cards require payment, and you’ll need cash to block opponents who want to snatch a set out from under you. A common strategy is to hold a small reserve of money cards so you’re not caught with a big economic mismatch in the event of a big steal.
- Timing matters: don’t waste a powerful action card in the first round. Save a few for the late game where the pressure is on and a single move can swing the entire board.
- Reading the room: Monopoly Deal is as much social psychology as it is card play. Watch your opponents’ tells—if someone glances at your hand, they’re probably analyzing whether you’ll drop a big forced deal or a sly block.

Our recommended approach with the Special Edition is to mix offensive and defensive strategies. In practice, you’ll need to gauge your opponents’ hand sizes and risk tolerance. If a player is aggressively building, you should focus on disrupting them with forced card deals or swap actions. If someone is slow and steady, you can lean into property collection and rent pressure.
