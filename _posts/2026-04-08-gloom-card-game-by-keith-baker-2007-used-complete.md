Gloom, the mood ring of hobby games, is one of those titles that sounds like a sad pun and ends up being the most outrageous party game you’ve ever played with a straight face. Designed by Keith Baker and first published in 2007, this is the game where you try to make your family as miserable as possible while you simultaneously keep the other players’ families from looking too cheery. It’s dark humor wrapped in translucent plastic and wrapped in more cardboard than a NERF factory. Today we review a used, complete copy of the base game—yes, the full enchilada, including all the little moods, misfortunes, and the infamous gloom deck that makes your life a living, breathing tragedy painted in pastel colors.

Introduction: The Bruised Couch of Temerity
Gloom is not a game you play; it’s a mood you carry around like a cursed amulet you can wave at your friends to induce sympathetic groans and riotous laughter. The premise is simple: each player controls a family, and your objective is to die the most spectacularly miserable death for your own characters while your opponents’ families live long, happy, and slightly judgmental lives. The deck of transparent cards allows you to overlay misfortune onto family members in delightfully dramatic ways, and the translucent cards feel a bit like peeking into a magical, cursed diary from the 1930s.

For a used copy, you’re not just buying a game; you’re buying a story—every bend in the box, every slightly bent corner, every card that has survived a few embarrassing parties with friends who should never be trusted with a Sharpie. The complete edition means you’re not chasing down those tiny expansion cards later; you’ve got the whole melodrama, ready to be staged in your living room theater of the grotesque.

What is Gloom, Exactly? A Short Case for Maximal Melodrama
Gloom is a card game about storytelling with a framework that conveniently punishes you for, say, letting Grandpa’s ponytail live too long, or for having the family keep a secret where the sun never shines. Each player is dealt a starting hand of “unhappiness” cards and a family card, and then you begin flipping over cards—your own cards, your opponents’ cards, or neutral cards—that attach to family members to add gloom. The grim irony is that you want your own family to suffer the most at the end, because gloom points are negative points; lower totals are better when you’re aiming to win the game with the most wretched family story arc.

In practice, this means you’ll be stacking misfortunes like a stack of pancakes at a freakishly gloomy breakfast joint. You’ll pair curses with wretchedness, attach gravestones to step-parents, and watch as your mother-in-law finally gets what she deserves—an appropriately dramatic death in the annals of your family saga. The humor comes not from cheap gags but from the absurdity of the cards themselves: titles like “A Pint-Sized Parlor Nightmare” and “The Infernal Inheritance” feel like dark humor written by a playwright who’s had one too many cups of coffee with a grim reaper in a wine bar.

Setup, Play, and the Core Loop: How a Round Goes Down
The base mechanics are simple enough for a party game and deep enough for a story night that drags on longer than the family saga you’re about to ruin. Each turn you’ll attach a card to one of your family members, or to somebody else’s, to impose a scheme of gloom. Cards may move a gloom marker, add a new trait, or create a dramatic twist that affects scoring. As gloom piles up, your goal is to ensure that when the final tally is done, your own characters are as miserable as possible while your opponents’ families remain relatively cheerful. The catch: you have to pass cards and play in a way that protects your own characters as you sprinkle misery on others. This leads to delightful negotiation and, in some circles, petty sabotage—things that would make a tax auditor cry, but in a good-natured, giggly way.

There’s a surprisingly brisk tempo to the base game. Even with a full table of players, the rounds move along because the mechanics are simple, the cards visually communicate their effects, and the variable nature of each game (which cards are in play, which families you’re targeting, and which gloom counters you’re racing to) keeps things fresh. The narrative weight is in the overlay of story cards: each card title reads like a mini-arc, and the art, while often charmingly grim, is stylized in a way that doesn’t feel too monstrous for a casual gaming night. The result is a balance: light enough to bring people into the game, dark enough to give you that delicious bite of “this is going to end badly for somebody,” which is exactly what the doctor ordered for a tabletop goth party game.

The Narrative Fabric: Why Gloom Works as a Storytelling Vehicle
What makes Gloom a standout is not the sheer cleverness of the card effects (though they are clever) but how the game invites storytelling. The suits of misfortune are not just points; they’re plot devices. You’re not just racing to accumulate gloom; you’re weaving a narrative. The cards themselves often contain short snippets of dialogue or caption-like text. When you attach a card to a character, you’re not just changing your points; you’re changing the character’s story, and by extension, the stories you’ll tell at the table about that character. The humor is derived from incongruity—sweet-looking illustrations that tell the opposite of what their visuals suggest—and from the dramatic irony of seeing all the bad things happen to someone else’s family while your own is left to suffer just a little more.

If you’re a fan of thematic card games that reward clever card placement and a penchant for melodrama, Gloom scratches that itch with a smile. The art direction leans into a vintage, almost Victorian illustration style, which pairs well with the retro vibe of “stories told around a lamp while the rain drums on the window.” It’s not grimdark; it’s more like a campfire story told with a chintzy glow and a dash of sarcasm.

Used Copy Specifics: Condition, Value, and the Joy of the Treasure Hunt
A used, complete Gloom set can be an excellent bargain for players who want a copy with character without paying boutique prices for a mint-condition box. Here are a few pointers to consider when evaluating a used copy:
- Card wear: Look for edge wear, small creases, or slight fading. The cardboard stock on Gloom cards is sturdy, and minor wear doesn’t affect playability. If you’re picky about aesthetics, you may want to sleeve the cards or use a card protector for the story cards.
- Box wear: A dented lid or a scuffed spine doesn’t ruin the mechanics; in fact, it can add to the “ lived-in ” ambiance. If the box is heavily crushed, you’ll want to price it accordingly or invest in a replacement later.
- Completeness: Verify you have all the necessary components—the gloom overlay, family cards, story cards, and the rulebook. Some used copies are missing a card here or there, which can be annoying but is usually easy to substitute with a duplicate card from a different edition or a printable replacement from the internet. The good news is that the core gameplay remains intact even if a few extras are missing.
- Non-game surfaces: The rulebook, if a little browned or creased, can still be perfectly legible. The rule language is fairly concise, and even if you have to reference a clarifying FAQ online (or your friend’s questionable memory), you’ll still be able to play the game without too many “we did this wrong” moments.

The Theme, the Tone, and the Gloomy Aesthetic: Aesthetic vs. Mechanics
Gloom shines not because it reinvented the wheel but because it wrapped a simple mechanic in a witty, theatrical shell. The theme—family misfortune told in a melodramatic, tongue-in-cheek manner—gives players permission to lean into the archetypes: the brooding eldest, the oblivious aunt, the cursed pet, and the mysterious relative who never quite explains themselves. This is not a game that wants to be “serious”; it wants to be memorable. The complete experience—box, cards, and little gloom counters—works together to produce a product that’s more than the sum of its parts. When you add in the humor of misfortune and the cunning of card placement, you have a game that rewards both storytelling chops and strategic planning, without becoming a heavy math puzzle.

Gameplay Pacing and Table Talk: When It Works and When It Goes Off the Rails
One of Gloom’s strengths is that it thrives in a casual environment—living room, kitchen table, or a cramped apartment with a loud game-night gang. Either way, the social element—the way players attempt to one-up each other with “and then her aunt’s ghost appeared at the Christmas party…” lines—is what makes Gloom a standout in the party-game landscape.

Artwork and Card Design: A Gentle, Vintage Whimsy
The art style is a deliberate nod to early 20th-century illustration with a playful edge. It’s not grim, even when the “gloom” theme is heavy—the colors are soft; the shapes are rounded; the characters look like they belong in a children’s book that has learned some darker lessons. That aesthetic ensures the game remains accessible to a wide audience. It’s a clever balance: you get the dark humor without the visual intensity that could turn off casual players. This is especially important for a used copy that has seen a few gatherings; the art continues to charm, and the cards retain their readability even as the corners tell a few stories of their own.

Compared to Other Narrative Card Games: Where Gloom Stands
If you’ve played other narrative card games like Dixit or Mysterium, you’ll notice that Gloom trades abstract storytelling for a sharper, more direct storytelling approach. It rewards clever card chaining and the ability to set up dramatic reveals. Unlike some heavier themed games that require long explanations and heavy setup, Gloom remains surprisingly approachable. The “play cards to other players’ families” mechanic fosters interaction and mild strategic manipulation, which can be a blast with a rowdy group. It’s not the same kind of brain-burn you’ll find in a true competitive game, but it isn’t trying to be; Gloom is about laughs, storytelling, and the occasional gasp as a character meets a particularly ridiculous fate.
