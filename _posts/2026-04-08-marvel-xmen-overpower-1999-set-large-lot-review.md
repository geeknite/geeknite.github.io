Sorting: Sorting is Caring (for Your Wallet and Sanity)
----------------------------------------------------------------
Sorting a large lot into a usable collection is half science, half archaeology, and half storytelling. Here’s a practical plan that won’t bore you into the next millennium:

1) Separate condition bands. Create piles for Mint/near-mint, Lightly played, Heavily played, and Poor. You’ll want to identify border scuffs, surface scratches, corner dings, and any moisture rings. 2) Separate by rarity. If you’re lucky enough to see a print sheet with rare or ultra-rare variants, set those aside in a safe, labeled area. If the lot lacks explicit rarity markers, you can still separate by character power level and card text complexity—these often correlate with rarity in 90s CCGs.
3) Group by character archetypes. It’s surprisingly satisfying to group Wolverine family cards into a “Wolverine + friends” stack or to slot Magneto and Brotherhood cards into “Bad Guys Assemble” piles. It makes deck-building later much more approachable.
4) Note misprints and fun anomalies. The 1999 OverPower era produced a few misprints and odd card frames. These aren’t always super valuable, but they are absolutely delightful to see when you’re flipping through a large pile. Keep a micro-notepad handy to jot down anything weird you encounter for later research.
5) Create a “two-button deck” binder. This little binder will help you test decks quickly when you’re short on time. You’ll sort cards by synergy potential and then assemble a handful of ready-to-play decks for a spontaneous game evening.

Handling Card Condition Without Losing Your Mind
----------------------------------------------------------------
A lot of collectors fear that a large lot means you’ll get nothing but frustration. But with the right approach, it can be a treasure chest of playable cards and a few potential upgrades. Here are some practical tips:

- Use soft sleeves on fragile cards, especially those with foil finishes. 90s foils can curl or crack if not stored properly.
- Store in a cool, dry place away from sunlight. Color-shifted art or fading is no joke when you’re trying to sell or trade.
- If you’re considering grading, be honest with yourself about the condition. Grading can be expensive, and a bulk lot rarely provides enough truly high-grade cards to justify the cost unless you’re chasing a handful of popular rares or foils.
- Consider a monthly sorting ritual: two hours per month to re-check, re-bundle, and update your deck-building intentions. You’ll stay engaged and avoid the pile-of-chaos syndrome.
