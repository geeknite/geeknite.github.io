## Gameplay Mechanics: How the On-the-Go Edition Plays
### Setup
- Shuffle the small deck and deal out a hand according to the edition’s rules.
- Place the first phase card face-up for all players to see.
- Prepare the scoring track or pad if you’re using scoring rules.

### Turn Structure
On your turn, you draw a card from the draw pile or the discard pile, then decide which cards to put toward your current phase and which cards to hold in your hand for future phases. The objective remains the same: complete ten phases as efficiently as possible, with the possibility to finish early if you’re extraordinarily lucky or you’ve developed a cunning plan to misdirect your opponents.

### Phases and Scoring
Each phase is a mini objective like a set of three, a straight of four, or some combination you’ll learn as you play. Completing a phase typically grants you points; you move on to the next phase card for the next round. In the end, the person with the lowest total of penalties wins if you’re playing with a scoring system.

### Endgame and Puzzle Moments
The tension in micro play is to finish before your neighbor does, all while not letting your own hand devolve into a chaotic jumble of mismatched cards. Sometimes you will be forced to redraw or swap phases mid-round, which can lead to spectacular, humorous outcomes as you watch your carefully curated strategy collapse into a glorious shuffle of chaos.

## Strategy Tips and Common Pitfalls
If you want to climb the ranks of airline lounge gaming, you’ll want to blend planning with opportunistic play. Here are some tactical guidelines:
- Early-phase focus: If a first-phase layout is easy for you, lock it in. A strong early phase reduces the risk of late-round panic where you’re forced to hoard unhelpful cards.
- Discard pile mastery: The top discard card can be a lifeline or a trap; read your opponents, but never reveal all your short-term plans.
- Flexibility is king: The micro edition rewards players who can pivot quickly when a phase becomes unworkable. Don’t be shy about dropping current goals to pursue a more favorable path.
- Card counting as storytelling: In a short game, counting can still be a valuable skill. Track what phases your opponents completed and which cards have dominated the deck.

Common mistakes:
- Overcommitting to a phase that isn’t going to come together within a couple of turns. It’s better to pivot early than to watch a promising plan turn into regret.
- Not managing downtime: In a travel group, the difference between a smooth game and a messy affair is how well you fill gaps between turns. A quiet, quick narration of your thought process can keep the energy high.
