## Token Design and Altered Edition Features
Tokens are not just about color and shape; they are about the reading experience on the table. The design team behind Altered Edition took this to heart and created tokens with several practical upgrades. Here is a breakdown of what you get and why it matters:

### Token Morphology
- Shapes: The tokens move beyond circles and squares into shapes that feel tactile and distinct. The geometry is not just a visual joke; it helps players quickly scan the board and locate resources by shape recognition, which reduces the cognitive load in the late game when you have three different resources on four different tracks.
- Thickness and weight: The tokens have a satisfying heft that makes clacking sounds in a way that signals progress rather than chaos. The goal is to give you a little audio cue that says you are doing well, even if the coordinates on your map say otherwise.
- Edges: Subtle bevels on the tokens help prevent printing wear and misalignment. This matters if you like to shuffle tokens like a blackjack dealer shuffles a deck, giving your table a sense of organized chaos.

### Readability in the Heat of Gameplay
- Color contrast: Color choices prioritize readability under typical table lighting. If your gaming nook resembles a dim dungeon, these tokens are designed to pop less to avoid eye strain and more to avoid miscounting a pile of six as seven.
- Iconography: Each token type carries an icon that is easy to parse at a glance. The icons were designed to be friendly to color-blind players, with distinct shapes that can be recognized even if color interpretation is not ideal.
- Size variation: Tokens come in varying sizes to indicate hierarchy or value. This is helpful for cutthroat players who like to quickly identify the biggest resource from the smallest, reducing the need for constant counting debates at the table.

### Material Quality and Longevity
Durability was a primary design goal. The tokens use a coated finish that resists sticky fingerprints and accidental coffee dives. The coating also provides a very satisfying tactile response when you slide a token across the table during a tense moment. Yes, it is the small things that keep a game night from becoming a memory of the night you shorted yourself on snacks and forgot the rules mid-turn.

## How It Changes the Gameplay Experience
Tokens can be more than just pretty decorations; they affect how players perceive the game state. The Altered Edition adds a few small but important shifts that can change the late game tempo and how players allocate attention:

- Faster counting: The larger but standardized token shapes make it easier to group tokens and count a pile at a glance. This translates into more turns spent in action and less time spent in table math.
- Visual pacing: The color and iconography prevent the dreaded moment when players realize they forgot to track a resource across multiple turns. With clearly distinguished tokens, your brain can offload some memory work to the tokens themselves.
- Event resonance: Special event tokens provide urgent cues when a critical moment hits. You get a tactile and visual sign that something pivotal is happening, which improves the sense of drama without requiring a rulebook raid in the middle of a match.

In practical terms, expect slightly faster setup and a more fluent flow during mid to late game. You will still argue about strategy and greed, but you will argue with a little more swagger and a lot less squinting at tiny holo stickers.
