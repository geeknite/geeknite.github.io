Fortune Row takes that vibe and hands you a player friendly guide plus a set of character sheets that feel like they were drawn by a street artist who moonlights as a tarot reader. ## What Fortune Row is and why it matters
Fortune Row is a district add-on for City of Mist that brings a gritty, urban edge to your legends and a fresh crop of labels and environments for your players. The vibe leans hard into cinematic crime, tall neon, and the kind of street-level mythology that makes you lean in and whisper I know a guy who knows a guy. The guide is designed to help players align their drifter heroes with a local power structure, weave personal legends into a social fabric, and outfit their characters with sheets that fit the Fortune Row aesthetic without forcing you into a thousand micro-deck decisions per scene.

From a mechanic standpoint, Fortune Row keeps the City of Mist core rules intact but augments them with a few district-specific moves, icon sets, and a pool of Ambitions that feel both personal and social. If the core game is your big city map, Fortune Row gives you a neon-lit alley with secret doors and a clue that only glows when the right attitude is present. It is not a full game overhaul; think of it as a stylish, punchy supplement that slides into the same vein with ease.

What you will get in the Player’s Guide is a blend of narrative prompts, mechanical options, and a practical layout that is designed for on-table use. The guide presents a curated set of Arcana, a handful of Major and Minor Powers for the new icons introduced by Fortune Row, and a compact set of starter character sheets that look good enough to print and hand to a player before the first about-to-sink dice roll. ## Inside the Player's Guide: what to expect
### A compact but mighty sheet set
The character sheets in Fortune Row are designed to be usable at a glance. They balance the need for quick reference during scenes with the desire for players to track their evolving legend. You get: a full sheet, a half sheet for quick sessions, and a simplified vignette card for in-game prompts. The art direction is bold but functional, with labels and icons that echo the Fortune Row vibe without clashing with the core palette of neon noir that players already love.

Key feature: a dedicated section on the sheet for a player to record their city connections. In a game built on myths and misdirection, who you know can be as important as what you know. Fortune Row recognizes this by giving players a clearly delineated space to note who in the district owes them a favor, who thinks they are trouble, and which gang or guild tolerates their presence for a price. The result is a sheet that reads like a street ledger rather than a tax form, which is exactly the mood you want when you are trying to keep your legends coherent across scenes.

### Icons, labels, and new mechanics
Fortune Row introduces a fresh set of Icons in addition to the City of Mist core icons. Players can choose from a variety of district aligned Icons that reflect the Row’s mythology and lawless charm. These icons come with minor mechanical twists that feel like breadcrumb trails rather than full on rules revamps. The intent is to give players meaningful choices without bogging them down in new edge-case calculations mid-session. If you are running a one shot, these icons serve as a handy mythic shorthand for character motivations in a single night of mayhem.

There is also a curated list of Ambitions, a concept in City of Mist that tracks personal goals and how the player intends to influence the city. Fortune Row adds a handful of Ambitions that tie to Fortune Row’s neighborhoods, rival factions, and city-wide legends. Ambitions feel like micro-quests that can push the plot forward while giving players a consistent reason to pursue particular threads. If you like long-term storytelling, you will appreciate how these Ambitions can connect with arc-based GM notes and scene prompts.

### Character sheets you can actually hand out
The design emphasis here is on readability and tactile satisfaction. The sheets use clean typefaces, high-contrast sections, and visual motifs borrowed from Fortune Row signage, graffiti, and payphones. You will find dedicated zones for:
- Core attributes and license to practice your mythic craft
- City ties and faction affiliations
- Personal connections and street cred
- The Arcana and their associated powers, with quick modifiers for in game checks
- A compact sheet for keeping track of Scene Tags and Fortune Row style prompts
This is the glue that holds a group night together. Players can glance at their sheet and know exactly what to roll, what to narrate, and what to trade with the GM in the next scene. There is also a printable vibe to the sheets; they look nice enough to be laminated for reusable session kits, and the art style makes the sheets feel like a collectible, not a chore.

### A sample character layout that works on day one
Fortune Row includes a couple of starter templates that illustrate how an intended vibe translates into mechanical flavor. A typical sample character may be an Arclight who uses urban myth and a mentor from a forgotten sparrow font bar, or a Whisper who gathers secrets like postcards. The point here is that you can drop into a Fortune Row game with a ready to play character concept and then customize as sessions unfold. The sheets provide a structured route for this customization, ensuring that the process remains fun rather than fiddly.

### Print-ready and digital-friendly
If your group uses a virtual table as often as a real table, Fortune Row plays nicely with virtual sheet sharing and digital plan sheets. The layout scales well for digital printouts, and the design retains legibility on different screen sizes. The image quality still reads well on a laptop, which means you can run a Fortune Row session without needing a dedicated print copy per player. The convenience factor here is real, not just a marketing line.

## How to use Fortune Row in your sessions
### Session prep tips
Fortune Row shines when you prep with the players, not for them. Before your next session, gather the district map notes and the Ambitions list. Ask players to choose an Icon or two that ties their legend to Fortune Row. This pre session alignment helps you avoid the last minute myth ping pong that can derail pacing.

Pro tip: reserve a couple of minutes at the start of play for the group to declare their city ties. A quick round of who owes whom a favor can unlock several plot lanes and provide natural hooks for conflict. The Fortune Row guide expects you to seed the city with small, personal obstacles that escalate into bigger stories. It is the old school storytelling trick dressed up in neon rain jackets.

### Scene flow and sheet choreography
When you run a Fortune Row arc, use the sheets as the narrative spine. The Arcana, the Ambitions, and the city ties should drive scene selection. If a scene neglects these elements, you might as well roll dice for the sake of dice. The sheets give you a reliable framework: who the character is, what myth they embody, who they owe, and what they want in the next scene. That clarity reduces table friction and increases player agency.

### GM tips for managing District politics
Fortune Row is as much about local power dynamics as it is about individual legends. A great GM trick is to angle for the consequences of a single action in the neighborhood. If your Arcana acts in a way that affects a local faction, describe the ripple effects in subsequent scenes. The players will recognize that their choices carry city-changing weight, which in turn elevates engagement and player investment.

You should also lean into the theme of masks. Fortune Row can promote a scenario where multiple NPCs look like friends while scheming behind backs. Let the players pick who holds the mask of truth in any given moment, and reward them with narrative payoffs that feel earned rather than handed to them by a GM fiat. The result is a session that feels like a living city rather than a scripted string of events.

### A quick example of using a character sheet in play
Player A is the Arclight, a street level mystic who glows when a crime is about to unfold. The player marks an Arcana action: glows when danger is near, and an Ambition that relates to a missing painting from Fortune Row. In a scene where a crime boss is using a counterfeit art ring to launder fortunes, the Arclight can leverage the icon to reveal a clue and then trade a favor for a key piece of information. The sheet tracks this with a simple check and a short narrative prompt on the side. The DM can then adapt the scene to reflect the new clue, weaving it into the big city myth that Fortune Row is building.

### Roleplay prompts on each sheet
Fortune Row sheets include small prompts that nudge players toward roleplay choices. Prompts like the following appear on the edges of the sheet and act as ready-made hooks for players who want a little nudge:
- Who owes you a favor and is likely to help in exchange for future tokens?
- What rumor do you know that could turn the tide of a stakeout scene?
- What is the cost of your power in the neighborhood right now?

These prompts help players stay in character while the GM keeps the pacing brisk and cinematic. If you are short on time, the prompts can serve as quick scene seeds that you can drop into a campaign on a single page.

## Art direction, layout, and practicalities
Fortune Row adheres to the same visual language as the core game, with a graffiti-meets-neon aesthetic that feels at once classy and street level. The packaging is sturdy, the typography legible, and the overall motif fits the mood of late nights, rainy sidewalks, and pockets full of secrets. If you are a pinboard GM, you will love the way the character sheets lay out information in color-coded zones. If you are a digital GM, you will appreciate the clean lines that render well on screen and print out crisp on letter size paper.

The balance between art and function is well judged. There is enough whimsy to keep the myth alive, but not so much that the sheets become a visual distraction during a tense scene. The designers appear to understand that a good sheet is a silent partner at the table: it tells you what is possible and what is likely, with a quick glance rather than a full read-through. That is the key to great table flow in a game like City of Mist where narration carries a heavy load and dice rolls sprinkle in the drama.

## How Fortune Row compares to other City of Mist supplements
If you have explored other City of Mist expansions, you know the pattern: core system, a few new mechanics, and some region-specific color. Fortune Row excels at the second pass: it gives you a district kit with clear narrative hooks and a tight sheet design without inflating the rulebook. In terms of practical play, it is easier to drop into an ongoing campaign than some other expansions that feel like reseeding the entire city. The emphasis on connection maps and faction dynamic is a strong point, because in a game about legends, the city is the ultimate boss and your social network is your damage dealer.

The character sheets are a highlight here. They feel crafted by players for players, not by designers who forgot what it is like to juggle a few character sheets while describing a battle in a crowded room. In a group where quick on-table decisions matter, Fortune Row shines by giving you legible, printable sheets, a few ready-to-run vibes, and a neat, cohesive aesthetic that helps players stay in the moment rather than stuck in the logistics.

## Final verdict and recommendation
Fortune Row is a strong addition to the City of Mist toolkit. It does not try to reinvent the wheel; instead, it sharpens the wheel, adds stylish new spokes, and gives you a better way to carry the car keys. If you love neon-drenched mythic crime, if you want your players to have tangible ties to a district and a city-wide mythology, and if you want ready to hand character sheets that look and feel great, Fortune Row is worth your time and your table space.

For players who enjoy a modular approach to mythic storytelling, Fortune Row offers a compact, flavorful package that can support long campaigns or quick one shots. For GMs, there are enough hooks, prompts, and faction dynamics to keep you plotting for weeks without the need for heavy prep. And for casual groups, this pack is a way to jump into City of Mist magic without waiting for a full campaign arc to unfold.
