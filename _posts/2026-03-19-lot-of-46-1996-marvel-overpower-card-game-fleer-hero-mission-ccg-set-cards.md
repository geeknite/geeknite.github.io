## What Is OVERPOWER, and How Does This Hero Mission Subset Fit In?

OverPOWER, produced by Fleer in the mid-1990s, was one of the many Marvel-themed CCGs that hit shelves during a boom period for superhero card games. The core idea: build a deck around a hero, assemble allies and actions that support your plan, and try to outplay your opponent with clever card interactions. The mechanics mixed attack, defense, and power values with special abilities that could be invoked in combat or for mission objectives.

The Hero Mission subset—your 46-card haul here—revolves around cards themed with hero-centric missions. You’ll typically encounter a handful of hero cards, a few villain cards, and a series of mission or action cards that modify the battlefield or grant special effects. The 1996 print run is known for its distinctive art style, bold typography, and the late-90s flavor of “maximize the shine” rather than “maximize the clarity” when it came to card text.

For the uninitiated, the vibe is: you’re collecting character fantasies, but with a deck-building skeleton that sometimes feels like a board game wearing a glossy cape. If you remember a Marvel movie from this era (or at least the marketing posters that came with them), you’ll recognize the aesthetic: punchy one-liners, dynamic action poses, and enough heroism to hallucinate the sense that you could, too, save the world… if you draw the right card that lets you do it without breaking your budget.

## Card Types You’ll Encounter in the Lot

Within a 46-card Hero Mission bundle, you’ll typically see a mix that supports two core play styles: deck-building around a chosen hero and exploiting mission-based tactics that hinge on timing and resource management. Here are typical card archetypes you might find, with notes on how they were designed to function back in the day:

- Hero Cards: These are the centerpiece of any OverPOWER deck. Each hero card carries a power value, a set of abilities, and sometimes keywords that interact with other cards. Expect classic heroes in dynamic poses: capes, dramatic stances, and a facial expression that says, “I’ve got this—somehow.”
- Villain Cards: A mirror universe to the heroes, villains bring counters and counters to counters. They provide the antagonistic tension necessary for a real game, and in 1996 art, they often wore a scowl that felt almost cinematic in a card-printed form.
- Mission/Event Cards: The heart of the Hero Mission subset. These cards create mini-quests, modifiers, and sometimes sudden shifts in the battlefield. They’re designed to shake up the standard turn-by-turn rhythm and force players to adapt on the fly.
- Equipment/Support Cards: A handful of items or support abilities that augment your hero’s capabilities for a short window or for a long-term fight. In the 90s, these often came with creative names and bold, readable text that invited you to imagine the in-game application.

## Mechanics 101: How the OverPOWER System Worked (in the Hero Mission Era)

While modern card games lean heavily into long, complex rulebooks and ever-evolving rule sets, OverPOWER kept things relatively approachable, with a few key knobs that players could twist to enact their strategy:

- Power and Defense: Every card had a numeric value representing attack strength and/or defense. Combat often came down to who had the better numbers, with modifiers coming from mission cards or situational bonuses.
- Abilities and Keywords: Cards carried keywords that interacted in predictable ways but with enough variety to feel interesting. Keywords could modify how damage is dealt, how you draw cards, or how you move on the board (conceptually, since this is a card game and not a true board game).
- Mission Cards: A core differentiator for Hero Mission sets. These cards often created side objectives—things you had to achieve to unlock a win condition or to apply a large effect at the right moment.
- Resource Management: Some cards improved your draw rate, some provided direct effects, and others drained your opportunity to use them. Players needed to balance risk and reward—choosing when to deploy a powerful effect versus waiting for a bigger payoff.

The synergy between hero cards and mission cards was where the tactical depth could emerge. You could build a deck around a single hero and try to overlay mission effects to reach a creative win condition. Or you could mix and match a few heroes, leaning into the synergy between their abilities and the mission cards that supported them. The result was a flavor of “heroic chaos” that many players found irresistible in the mid-90s.
