## Specs and tech talk
### Power delivery and display support
The star of the show is the 120W power delivery—an ambitious power profile that should, in most cases, keep a high-end laptop charging while you push data across TB4 channels to external displays. In practice, you will likely see some systems sipping power while the dock also runs peripherals, monitors, and perhaps a cooling fan for your laptop if that particular model runs hot when connected with a sensor-obsessed energy drink in hand. The 120W figure is the headline, but the reality depends on your host machine and what else you plug in. If you have a laptop that likes to sip or gulp power, the G4 will play nice, as long as your laptop is within the dock’s negotiated capabilities. If you are chasing a surplus of extra 10-20W bleed for a gaming laptop in throttle mode, don’t expect miracles on a used unit—these things have a finite appetite and a habit of negotiating with your device politely but firmly.

Displays-wise, the G4 handles multiple displays with Thunderbolt 3/4 braille-like clarity, supporting a mix of HDMI and DisplayPort connections. If you pair it with a capable TB4 host, you can push 4K displays with a sane color profile. If you try to run triple 4K panels at once, you are entering the realm of heroic engineering or a cave painting of a desk setup—your mileage will vary, but the dock is not shy about stepping into the arena. We tested a dual 4K monitor setup and a single 4K with a high-refresh-rate monitor on the other side, and the results were steady enough that we stopped muttering about the reality of cables and started celebrating like grown adults who found spare charger cords in a desk drawer.

The port selection on the HP G4 is a pragmatic suite: a mix of USB-C/Thunderbolt, USB-A, HDMI, DisplayPort, Ethernet, and a few audio options depending on the model. The audio pass-through and a network jack are tidy additions for those who are particular about routing audio to external speakers or a conference-room mic array. The bottom line: the port layout is practical, not indulgent. It’s built for work, not a fashion show in a co-working space.

## Real-world performance

Setting up the dock is straightforward for TB4-capable laptops and desktops. If your host supports Thunderbolt 4, you will likely get a “we got you” moment on first boot. The docking station is compatible with Windows and macOS ecosystems, though the user experience may vary depending on driver availability for particular macOS versions and firmware.
