Expect a level of irregular charm that proudly signals handmade origins without veering into the chunky texture you might find in lower-cost, mass-produced pieces. ## Care, maintenance, and longevity
### Daily and weekly care
A quick vacuum once or twice a week is enough to keep debris at bay. Use a suction height setting that avoids aggressive agitation, which can compress the pile over time. For spills, blot rather than rub. Blotting prevents staining from seeping deeper into the fibers. For pet hair, a brush attachment can be helpful. The goal is to keep the wool fibers unburdened by loose hairs and surface dirt that dulls the pattern.

### Stain management and spot removal
If a spill happens, act quickly. Blot with a clean, dry cloth, then lightly dab with a mild solution of water and a gentle detergent. Always test a small area first. Avoid harsh chemical cleaners that could fade the pattern or degrade the wool fibers. For wine or coffee, you may want to use a dedicated wool-safe stain remover and follow with a gentle rinse. After cleaning, allow the rug to dry completely before resting yourself on it again; damp wool can lead to odor growth or mildew in poorly ventilated spaces.

### Rotation and long-term care
In rooms with strong sunlight, rotating the rug every few months can even out sun exposure and prevent color fade in hotspots. Flipping or rotating also helps to distribute wear more evenly, which is especially important in high-traffic zones. If you have a pet that enjoys digging into the fibers or scratching the surface, you might consider a protective underlay to reduce friction and extend the rug life. The goal is to keep the pattern crisp, the colors vibrant, and the backing stable through years of use.
