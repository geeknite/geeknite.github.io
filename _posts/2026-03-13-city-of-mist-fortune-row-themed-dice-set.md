## Unboxing and First Impressions

Fortune Row arrives in a sturdy, glossy box that looks suspiciously like it was designed by a fortune teller on commission from a board game publisher who saw Casablanca once and decided to remix it. The packaging leans into a noir aesthetic with gold-foil accents and a die-cut that reveals a glimpse of the dice inside. Opening the box feels a little like pulling back a velvet curtain and peeking at a stage where fate is about to perform a slapstick routine. Inside, you’ll find a compact dice tray (fold-out) and a set of five themed dice, each one bearing symbols that feel like they belong in a back alley divination business rather than a standard role-playing table.

The dice themselves sit in a molded foam cradle, each a little different from the others in a way that says, We know exactly which myth you’re chasing and we will tease it into existence. If you are the kind of gamer who needs tactile proof of quality, Fortune Row passes the vibe check before you even roll. A quick note on build: these dice are well-balanced for a story-driven game rather than a precise math slog. That said, most players will find the balance perfectly acceptable for narrative dice rolling, and they look gorgeous on a game night spread.

## Usability at the Table: Readability, Weight, and Roll Feel

Dice are mostly about feel and clarity. Fortune Row doesn’t skimp on either. The dice have a comfortable weight without feeling brickish, which makes rolling satisfying without punishing your wrists. The sizes are standard, so dice towers and rolling trays you already own will accommodate them with no fuss. The matte finish helps prevent glare on camera, which is nice for streaming sessions or if you just want to post your epic roll to social media without the die flashing back at you like a disco ball.

One potential usability caveat for some players is that the symbols can be dense in certain light conditions. If you are a GM who likes to describe every icon in vivid detail, you will love the flavor; if you prefer quick, surgical familiarity, you might need a moment to orient after rolling. A quick trick is to assign a quick mental mapping before the game starts: identify the five dice by color and their dominant icon, then let the narrative catch up to the visual cue. This approach keeps the pace up without sacrificing the immersion that Fortune Row is engineered to boost.

Aside from readability, there is the question of compatibility with other City of Mist expansions. Fortune Row fits neatly with the broader aesthetic, but you won’t find any economic or mechanical cross-compatibility that would threaten the core balance of a typical scenario. It’s a thematic add-on that encourages storytelling rather than shoves a new mechanic into your face. For players who like to mix and match, you’ll feel rewarded by the variety, not bogged down by an additional rule set.

## How to Use Fortune Row in Your Sessions

- Start with a mood map: before you start, lay out a quick mood map for the scene. Decide which dice will symbolize the primary mood and which ones might trigger rising action.
- Use icons as prompts: position the dice where players can see the icons clearly. Let the symbols serve as prompts for dialogue and action rather than a strict resolution framework.
