## How to Play at a Glance
Herd Mentality uses a simple turn structure that any adult with a pulse can memorize in under a minute (we tested it on a sleepy cat, and it woke right back up). Here is the gist:

1) Each round has a question card that everyone sees. The lead player reads it aloud and then each player secretly chooses an answer card that they think best matches what the majority would say. If you are playing with 4 players, each person chooses one answer and places it facedown.

2) After everyone submits, the answers are revealed. The goal is not to choose your own favorite answer but to pick the one that you think will align with the herd. It is a social guessing game in the purest sense, where your social radar is mainly your sense of humor and your fear of being judged for a silly choice.

3) After all answers are revealed, players vote on which answer they believe was chosen by the herd. The twist is that you cannot vote for your own card. If your card matches the majority pick, you score points. If your card is in the majority but not the top pick, you still get a small consolation based on the house rules. This is where the bragging rights come into play, because some players will insist they predicted the crowd with the confidence of a fortune teller who moonlights as a meme lord.

4) The round ends, the score is tallied, and a new question card is drawn. Rinse and repeat until someone hits the winning threshold or the table erupts in laughter first. In our sessions, the laughter often outpaced the actual scoring, which is precisely the point.

### The 4-player sweet spot
Herd Mentality shines most in a tight four-player orbit. You have enough different personalities to yield a believable herd and enough predictability to make anticipation part of the strategy. If you stretch to 5 or 6 players, the herd becomes more of a loose flock, and the dynamics shift toward more wild, less predictable outcomes. You will still have a blast, but the game tempo changes and set-up time per round increases a bit as more people debate which prompts are best suited for the group. If your occasional game nights include cousins you have not seen since last Thanksgiving, the upplifting nature of Herd Mentality can be exactly what you need to bring everyone into the same comedic orbit without requiring a physics degree.

## Practicalities: Setup, Pace, and Playtime
Herd Mentality is fast to learn and faster to play. The rule book clocks in at a few pages and the rest of the play is improvisation with rules of social engagement. Setup takes about 5 minutes with four players, including time for shuffling cards, placing the backing tokens, and letting everyone rehearse their best smug face when their answer hits the mark.

- Playing time per session: 20 to 40 minutes. If your group is ruthless with time management, you can sprint through a handful of rounds in record time. If you are in it for the long haul and want to savor the goosebumps of the herd, you can stretch it to an hour or more by letting prompts simmer and encouraging extended banter.
- Difficulty: Easy. The only thing you might stumble on is resisting the urge to scoff at a wildly perfect prediction. This is not the game you play when you need a heavy strategic brain workout; this is the social warm bath that warms your cheeks and your funny bone.

## How It Scales: From Cozy Night to Family Marathon
The four-player arrangement is the sweet spot, as mentioned. With four players, you can rely on a reliable herd dynamic without too many voices clashing at once. It also ensures that the top voting choice is not always the loudest person at the table, which keeps things fairer and funnier. When you add more players, the herd becomes a larger, noisier spectacle. The top pick is easier to achieve, but defining a clear majority becomes trickier, which can either heighten suspense or elongate the round into a parody of a family council meeting.

Here are some practical tips for different group sizes:

- Four players: lean into the social guessing game. A robust sense of humor and a willingness to lean into the ridiculous is all you need. The pacing is comfortable, and the rounds feel quick enough to keep momentum without rushing the laughs.
- Five to six players: designate a rotation to manage decision making. Consider playing with a slightly reduced set of prompts to prevent the table from turning into a mini United Nations of opinions. You still get plenty of laughs; the pace is a touch more chaotic, which some groups adore.
- Three players: this is still doable, but you might feel the herd is a tad too small for strong predictive dynamics. You can adjust by letting the third player nudge others toward a shared direction or by using a variant that gives players a little more leeway for predicting outcomes.
- Larger groups: use multiple herd tokens and a rotating host to keep the flow intact. The social energy can be incredible when everyone contributes and the room erupts in a chorus of mock judgment and good-natured teasing.
