## How to play: the classic duet loop
If you’ve played Codenames, you’ll feel right at home. If you haven’t, relax: Duet is still a gateway drug to word games and cooperative strategy. The game centers on two roles per duo, cycling through turns until you win by identifying all 15 agents (in the standard goals) or you lose by misreading too many clues or hitting the assassin.

- Setup: lay out 25 word cards in a 5x5 grid. One player is the Spymaster; the other is the Field Operative. You both share a single clue track and must coordinate as if your collective brain has learned to whisper through a vestibule of fear.
- Turn structure: on a turn, the Spymaster gives a one-word clue plus a number. The number indicates how many agents on the grid relate to the clue. Your partner then flips over that many cards, one by one, trying to identify agents while avoiding the others. If you flip a neutral or an opposing agent card, it still counts as a turn but adds risk. If you reveal the assassin, you both lose immediately. Yes, even if your partner is “close enough.” The doulbe-edged thrill lives here.
- Clue discipline: you’re allowed one-word clues; compound words are a major no-no. The 2nd Edition tightens this with some gentle reminders—the kind of reminders you’d give to a clever but sleepy spy who keeps using “amazing” as a clue for everything. The spirit remains: concise, relevant, and fearless.
- Alternating roles: you’ll swap roles and re-enter the strategic labyrinth. The two-player dynamic means you can test hypotheses quickly, admit fault with grace, and celebrate tiny breakthroughs (like when you finally clue your partner into the fact that “apple” can hint at a word connected to both “fruit” and “teacher”).

A few subtle changes in the 2nd Edition groove keep the flow smooth. The rule clarifications reduce the “wait, what does this mean again?” moments. The board and card design support faster recognition so you can get to the fun and the chaos without a long preamble.

## Strategy and tips for two players
Here are some practical guidelines to help your duet perform better than your coffee-fueled enthusiasm:

- Build a shared vocabulary map. Early in the session, take stock of your most reliable clue-building zones. If you’ve discovered a cluster of words that share a theme (animals, food, tech terms), you can anchor a few early clues around those clusters.
- Favor safe clues early. In a two-player game, you don’t have a crew cheering you on; you have the looming possibility of doom. Consider starting with shorter, safer clues to build momentum before you try the long-shot clues that might trigger a cascade of incorrect picks.
- Use the number strategically. The edition’s hints emphasize how the number can tip the risk-reward balance. A larger number can push you into risky territory or help a stutter-step by giving your partner confidence to pick multiple cards—assuming they’re all in the same mental neighborhood.
- Watch the assassin with your partner’s blind spots. In Duet, you’ll have to coordinate without seeing all of your partner’s reasoning. So you’ll learn to trust a well-chosen clue more than a perfect run-through. If your partner’s track record with certain words is shaky, avoid calling out those words in the same clue. Better to adjust your clue approach than to push your partner into a misstep.
- Embrace near-misses as learning opportunities. A near-miss can be a goldmine for sharpening future clues. When you stumble, discuss why the connection felt valid at the moment and what you’d adjust next time. The game rewards reflective practice almost as much as quick thinking.

### Word-key insights
If you’re stuck, speed-dial your brain to a few reliable word-link strategies:
- Semantic neighborhoods: think about synonyms or related concepts that could plausibly fit multiple target cards but not the assassins.
- Shared contexts: if a cluster of words relates to a specific domain (for example, sports or foods), a clue anchored to that domain can unlock several cards together.
- Phonetic and structural cues: similarity in sounds or spelling can provide crunchy but workable hints, especially when you’re trying to coax your partner to a cluster of words.
