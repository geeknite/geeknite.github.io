## Space Planning for 10 Desks

The most intimidating part of deploying a 10 person bench setup is space planning. It is one thing to say we can fit ten desks in a room; it is another to ensure that the space allows for correct posture, proper line of sight for screens, and enough space to walk around without practicing a door dash around a chair every time someone takes a step. WTE Black is designed to be adaptable, and that adaptability is the secret sauce for optimizing workflow.

Layout options typically include:

- Straight line rows for a classic software development floor, perfect for high density and quick hallway sprints between stand up meetings
- Cube farm like clusters with shared peripheral zones, suitable for collaborative sessions while still preserving personal space
- L shaped corners for teams with specialized hardware or high resolution monitors that need dedicated attention

A practical tip is to consider the visual axis of monitors when selecting a layout. Avoid the classic mistake of colleagues facing a wall and others facing a blank slat ceiling. It is not just a matter of aesthetics; it reduces neck strain and helps with focus. Also think about power access. The design of WTE Black allows for centralized power hubs at the core of the bench, which minimizes long power runs and reduces the risk of tripping over a chaotic power strip party.

For room lighting, consider a balanced approach. Too much glare on screens makes color work uncomfortable and saps morale quickly. Where possible, install indirect lighting or adjustable task lamps that can be aimed away from monitors. Light temperature matters too; in a high intensity coding session, a slightly cooler light helps focus, while for design or content work a warmer tone can reduce eye fatigue during long sessions.

## Cable Management and Electrical Safety

The safety and neatness of cables matters. A ten desk bench can become a hazard if cables are left to roam like a unruly octopus. The WTE Black architecture includes an integrated cable tray that runs the length of the bench. In practice, this tray handles multiple power and data lines, helping to keep the floor clean and the desk tops clear of tangles.

We conducted a stress test by running high throughput devices in a few workstations and then refilling the tray with an assortment of HDMI, USB-C, Ethernet, and power cables. The system performed well, with cable separation that minimizes interference and reduces the chance that one device yanks a monitor off its arm during a late night debugging session. The ability to route cables cleanly also simplifies future maintenance and reduces the time required to swap components when the team expands or retools.

Safety is also about power distribution. If you plan to support ten rigs, consider a robust power strategy with a central UPS for critical stations and surge protection along with ground fault circuit interrupters where necessary. It is not the most exciting part of a setup, but it is one of the most important if you want a reliable operation that doesn t fail during a sprint week or an annual hardware refresh.
