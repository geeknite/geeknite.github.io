## What is Gloom?

Gloom is not a game you play to win. It is a game you play to make your opponents lose. That is the essence of the mood: a Victorian mortality cabaret where every action drains sorrow away from one family only to drench another in misfortune. The core mechanic is delightfully simple and cleverly brutal: players play cards to alter the state of each family, adding or removing happy and unhappy facets. The objective? Have the gloomiest (i.e., most unhappy) family by the time all cards are revealed. And yes, the gloom can and will propagate like a well-timed chain of unfortunate events, thanks to play sequencing and damage transfer between families.

The design emphasizes storytelling as much as strategy. Each card has a small illustration that nudges you toward a narrative, turning the game into a sort of cooperative storytelling exercise with a cutthroat auction of who gets to suffer the most. It is delightful, dark, and a little cheeky—exactly the vibe Geeknite loves to celebrate without tipping into horror-nightmare territory. If you like games that reward you for carefully orchestrating misfortune while cracking wry smiles at the sheer audacity of the premise, Gloom will feel like a comfortable cardigan you resented wearing for a long time but now adore.

For fans of the original, the two expansions we will talk about—Unhappy Homes and Unwelcome Guests—offer more dastardly misfortune to stitch into your family trees. They push the system in new directions while keeping the core one-card-per-turn mechanic intact. The result is a package that scales well from casual mess-ups to heavy, long-form storytelling sessions where you owe your friends a sincere apology for the plot twists you inflicted on their hapless families.

If you want to peek at the official lore and visuals, you can swing by the publisher’s page at Atlas Games, which hosts the original images and product notes. ### Quick note on tone and audience

Gloom lives in a twilight zone where macabre humor meets family-friendly mechanics. It is not grim dark; it is a lopsided, satirical take on family portraits gone wrong. The game rewards narration and playful cattiness almost as much as it rewards clever card play. It is suitable for groups of 2–4 players, though the expansions sometimes encourage looser social structures, as you will see below. If your gaming table enjoys jokes about misfortune, you probably already know you are the villain of the group—and that is precisely where the fun hides.

## Components and setup

### What you get in the base game

- A deck of gloom cards featuring distressed families and unhappy contexts
- A set of mood tokens (happiness, sadness, etc.)
- Transparent life cards that let you peek into the narrative of each family
- A plastic-ish playing mat and a few tokens to track status

The art is a lighthearted pastiche of Victorian morbidity, with lampshades that double as meta-jokes. It is cheerful enough that even your skeptical partner will admit the illustration is charming, which makes the whole package easier to sell to non-gamers at the table. The components are sturdy, but the tactile joy comes from the act of stacking and sliding gloom cards in the correct order to perpetuate misery across families.

### Setup rhythm

Setting up Gloom is almost as ritual as the gameplay. You deal out a starting family for each player? Yes, each player creates a family with your color-coded gloom deck. The aim is to accumulate as much misery as possible on your own family while spreading cautionary tales to others. The decks get layered with life cards that show the baseline happiness and unhappiness, creating a grim baseline. With the expansions, you can add extra families, or you can mutate existing families with new cards that alter the tone and available actions.

If you are planning to run a long session, a quick house rule to speed things up is to pre-build a few sample families that are moderately gloomy. This balances the game for newbies and ensures the first few rounds do not feel like an endless scavenger hunt for the first miserable outcome. The base game is already a puzzle, but expansions can turn it into a sprawling, melodramatic saga with rotating alliances, backstabbing, and the occasional moment of empathy that lasts about five seconds before the next catastrophe arrives.

### The unspoken star: card play order

Gloom rewards careful sequencing. In practice, you typically play a card to turn a happy to unhappy or to apply a negative modifier to a family. The trick is to combine cards in a way that triggers other effects or to delay a particularly nasty card until you need to mitigate an opponent’s favorable run. It is all about timing, which is the lifeblood of any good misfortune drama. As you become more comfortable, you will start to see patterns: certain cards are best played late, others early, and a handful are incredibly versatile and can be used to pivot the narrative on a dime.

## The expansions in focus: Unhappy Homes and Unwelcome Guests

Now we come to the two expansions bundled in this lot. The first is Unhappy Homes. The name alone raises eyebrows and invites a chorus of lurid, comedic murmurs from the table. The second, Unwelcome Guests, introduces a new layer of social anxiety to the gloom—guests who complicate the gloom calculation and force you to rethink your family’s misfortune portfolio. Together they create a more dynamic puzzle while sticking to the same basic grammar of the base game: cards move from happiness to misery, and misery compounds when you chain events.

### Unhappy Homes

Unhappy Homes expands the play area with new home cards that introduce structural quirks. These cards often have global effects on all families, or they create conditions that force you to revisit earlier plays. The expansion adds a level of meta-puzzle solving: you not only manage your own family’s misfortune but you must also anticipate how shared cards will interact across players. Thematic flavor remains intact—the heart of the expansion is the idea that a home can be a vessel for misfortune as well as a sanctuary from it. The novelty here is that you must juggle two or more home contexts at once, which elevates the mental load in a satisfying way.

From a gameplay vantage, Unhappy Homes introduces a handful of cards that reward risk-taking. For example, some home cards grant temporary protection for one turn, creating a window to deploy a killer mood card without waking up the table’s collective judgment. Others enforce chain reactions where a misfortune in one home echoes into others, which is exactly the flavor you want when you crave a dramatic crescendo in your gloom story.

### Unwelcome Guests

Unwelcome Guests flips the social dynamic. In this expansion, you invite guests to the gloom party, but they come with their own agendas and quirky interactions. Guests can complicate scoring, alter the sequence of cards you draw, or modify how happiness interacts with misery. The concept of guests is a playful nod to the idea that a party is not just a static snapshot of sadness but a social event with its own weather and mood shifts.

Mechanically, Unwelcome Guests adds guest cards that can be played or targeted on an opponent. Guests may leave lingering effects that require players to adjust their plans mid-stream, which is perfect for players who love improvisation. It also introduces new win conditions and alternate scoring methods for those who want to push toward a longer, more episodic gloom arc. The net effect is a richer, more chaotic tapestry that makes every round feel like a mini-drama with escalating stakes.

### How the two expansions interact with base Gloom

The expansions were designed to complement the core game rather than replace it. They offer more opportunities for storytelling, more ways to influence the table’s mood, and more chances to punish your friends in stylish, humorous ways. They also pair nicely with house rules that encourage role-play or more explicit narrative threads. If your group enjoys collaborative storytelling with a dash of cutthroat competition, you will relish the synergy. If you prefer clean numbers and tightly constrained strategy, these expansions still give you bountiful opportunities to sculpt the tone of the session while keeping the game approachable.

## Two-player viability and house rules

Gloom scales well with more players, but the duo format has a special charm. Two players become the ultimate rival playwrights, each trying to outdo the other with a more tragic backstory for their families. The scoring, however, remains a shared river: you both contribute to the gloom level and the one who curates the most over-the-top tragedy by the end wins. This dynamic can maximize drama and lead to some delightfully petty finish lines.

House rules to consider for two players with Unhappy Homes and Unwelcome Guests:

- Shared misery commits: Allow both players to contribute to the chain effects of a card in certain rounds to speed up the tragedy; this keeps the pace brisk and ensures that neither player can lag behind.
- Narrative focus: After a few rounds, pause to craft a short paragraph describing the most miserable family. It is a fun way to anchor the storytelling aspect and get laughs as the plot grows darker.
- Tempo control: Use a timer for turns to prevent analysis paralysis. The aim is to keep the gloom moving and the table entertained rather than turned into a calculus lab.

If you are playing with more than two players, the same rules apply but with more opponents to torment. The base game shines in party configurations, and the expansions widen the field of possible misfortune and dramatics. The key is to embrace the chaos and enjoy the narrative value more than flawless optimization.

## Strategy tips and common pitfalls

- Start with a plan, but not a rigid one: The beauty of Gloom lies in its flexibility. It rewards players who can adapt to the way cards fall. It is often wise to keep a handful of cards that can be played quickly to create immediate misery while you set up a longer-term strategy for your main family.
- Balance risk and reward: Some cards offer significant benefits but at the cost of exposing your own family to more unhappy events. Decide early whether you want to gamble on a dramatic payoff or take a safer route that yields slower, steadier misery.
- Read the room: The social dynamics matter as much as the cards. A good gloom master can read the table and tailor their plays to maximize drama without alienating fellow players. Remember that the joke is on the characters, not the participants at the table.
- Manage the timing of reveal: Since the game ends when all cards are revealed, learning when to reveal the most damaging cards is key. You want the climax to land near the end, when the scorecards are closest and tensions are highest.
- Leverage expansions deliberately: Unhappy Homes teaches patience and chain reactions, while Unwelcome Guests emphasizes disruption and social interaction. Use them in tandem to escalate tension and create memorable moments rather than just more numbers on a sheet.

## Theme, humor, and art direction

Gloom is about gloom, but it is not a grim experience. The art direction uses playful caricatures of misfortune that land just on the right side of silly. The Victorian ghoulish vibe plays nicely with modern humor; you get a sense of old-world melodrama without it becoming a dreary slog. The humor is clever and self-aware, which keeps the mood light and invites players to lean into the storytelling rather than grinding through a dry mechanic.

Across the base game and expansions, the art remains consistent in its tone. The cards read clearly, the icons are intuitive, and the layout makes it easy to track who is miserable and by how much. The aesthetic is a big part of why the experience feels cohesive. If you are showing this to non-gamers, the card illustrations provide immediate talking points and even casual players can jump into the fun after a quick explanation.

## Final verdict and recommendation

Gloom with the Unhappy Homes and Unwelcome Guests expansions is a confident, cheeky take on the storytelling card game format. It shines when players lean into the groove of misfortune, embrace the performance aspect of the narrative, and laugh at the melodramatic fates they curate for their families. If you value social interaction, quick rounds, and a sense of theater as much as you value clever card play, this package is a strong pick for game nights that want more than a dry math puzzle. The base game remains approachable for newcomers, while the expansions add enough complexity to satisfy veteran gloom-mechanics fans without sacrificing the core accessibility that keeps the game inviting.

On balance, I recommend the Gloom set with Unhappy Homes and Unwelcome Guests for groups who enjoy storytelling, light strategy, and a healthy dose of macabre humor. If you love the aesthetic and want a platform for regular gloom-themed evenings, this is one of the most reliable and entertaining choices in the space. For strictly competitive players who dislike taking jabs at friends for personal amusement, you might want to skip the expansion-heavy route and stick to the base game with a few light house rules to keep the pace brisk.
