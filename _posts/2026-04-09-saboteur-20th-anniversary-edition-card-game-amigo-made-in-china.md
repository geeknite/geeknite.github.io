## Gameplay Overview
Saboteur is simple at first glance but has depth in the interactions between players’ hidden roles. Here’s a concise look at how the game plays in practice.

### Setup
- Each player is dealt a role card, either Miner or Saboteur. Most players are Miners, with a few Saboteurs depending on the player count.
- The miners aim to construct a path from the start to at least one of several treasure cards. The Saboteurs aim to derail that objective and make the path unusable or the treasure unreachable.
- The treasure cards are placed at the path’s far end, and the deck is shuffled for draws.
- The game uses a grid-like structure of path cards and rock cards played to create a winding tunnel that players collectively build. The catch: you might be laying down a path that helps your own victory or hinders someone else, all while not letting others know your true allegiance.

### Roles and Objectives
- Miners work together to complete a valid tunnel from the start to a treasure. They can share information, bluff, and aid each other—until someone reveals who they truly are.
- Saboteurs can block progress, steer the tunnel in the wrong direction, and weaponize rocks or dead-ends that stall the team. They enjoy sowing distrust and watching the chaos unfold.
- Communication is key. Players can discuss strategy, but withhold or misdirect about their own loyalties. The art of cohort manipulation is as much a skill as card-drawing in this game.

### The Heist and End Conditions
- The round ends when either a tunnel reaches a treasure card or the path becomes irreparably blocked. When a treasure is reached, miners win; when the tunnel collapses or blocks threaten to block everyone, saboteurs party hard—declaring victory in their own sneaky way.
- Depending on the edition, there might be multiple treasure cards at stake or a single “end of round” condition that triggers when a single path completes. The chaos scales with player count and your willingness to pretend you’re not the saboteur while actively sabotaging the rail lines.

### Player Interaction and Strategy
- The thrill is in the micro-interactions: the “I’m not sabotaging, I’m defending” moment that instantly outs someone else as the saboteur, the dramatic reveal that someone has been quietly hindering the tunnel while maintaining a helpful public persona, and the meme-level chirping that accompanies every misplay. If you love social deduction, Saboteur is a doozy; if you hate social deduction, you’ll still love beating your friends at a game where you pretend to be helpful while actually hoarding the lanterns.

## How It Plays at Your Table
- Groups of 3–10 players typically have a blast. The more people you have, the more dramatic the misdirections and chalk lines of betrayal become.
- Play time tends to hover around 20–45 minutes per round, depending on the number of players and how stubborn the miners can be when faced with a nice, clean tunnel route.
- The balance between teamwork and betrayal means you’ll often do the classic table dynamic: a helpful teammate appears, then suddenly you realize they were quietly aligning with the saboteur behind your back. It’s deliciously chaotic, especially when a plan you thought would succeed collapses in a spectacularly funny way.

Tips for maximizing fun and minimizing table-drift chaos:
- Start with a quick teaching round. Don’t be afraid to pause and summarize roles and objectives before you start playing.
- Embrace misdirection. If you’re honest about your intentions, you’ll lose trust quickly. A little bluffing goes a long way.
- Rotate the mine map layout to change the path each round. Fresh paths keep the game feeling new and prevent repetitive patterns.
- Use a timer or a strict round-end to keep momentum high. Saboteur is at its best when the tempo stays punchy rather than dragging toward a decision fatigue valley.
