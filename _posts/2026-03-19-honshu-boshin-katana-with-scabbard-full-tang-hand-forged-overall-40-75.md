## Design and Build: What you get

### Full tang and hand-forged construction

The claim that this katana is full tang is not just marketing puffery. A true full tang blade uses a single piece of steel that extends through the handle, providing the feel of a single, continuous instrument rather than a blade that is glued into a separate handle. The abstract math of a full tang is simple: more metal in contact with your grip usually means better resonance, stiffness, and a sense of durability that can be surprisingly comforting after you accidentally dull a knife in the kitchen (not recommended, but we have all been there). Hand forging adds a romance layer to the narrative. It suggests a craftsperson worked the metal by hand, shaping edge geometry, curvature, and the temper line in a way that mass production cannot replicate. In practice, hand-forged blades can show subtle inconsistencies that collectors call “character,” and casual users may call “a nice reminder that humans still make things.”

The blade length sits in the typical long katana territory, with a slender silhouette designed for a clean draw and a crisp line through the cut. The tang, we are told, runs fully through the handle, which should help with balance and resilience if you decide to test the edge against a stubborn tatami mat or a stubborn pine board we do not recommend you try at home. ## Maintenance and care: keeping the shine without the burn

### Basic care routine

- Wipe the blade after handling to remove oils and sweat. You want to leave it clean to resist corrosion and patina.
- Lightly oil the blade with a food-safe mineral oil or camellia oil depending on your preference. The aim is to keep a thin protective layer that prevents moisture from invading the steel. Do not drown it in oil; we are not treating this as a deep-sea expedition.
- Store the sword in a dry place. Humidity is your worst enemy for a midrange high carbon blade. If you live in a humid climate, consider a dehumidifier or an inline humidity regulator for your display case.
- Periodically check the mekugi (the pegs that anchor the handle). If you notice looseness, a quick tightening and realignment will help preserve safe handling and the structural integrity of the tang.

### Cleaning and sharpening notes

You should only sharpen the blade with a method you understand. If you are new to sharpening, consider enrolling in a local class or seeking guidance from a reputable bladesmith who understands traditional katana geometry. A poor sharpening job can ruin a blade more quickly than rough handling. If you are a beginner, a light burr removal and a few passes on a stones set at fine grits can bring back a serviceable edge.
