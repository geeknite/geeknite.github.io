## Overview: what is Board Royale: The Island 2nd Ed plus eight expansions?

The Island 2nd Edition is a thematic, modular board game that crams competitive and cooperative elements into a tropical-sounding package. The core box ships with a surprisingly robust set of components: a double-sided island map, modular terrain tiles, plastic minis for champions, event cards, resource tokens, fate dice, and a timer that absolutely refuses to be precise, because drama is the point. The eight expansions—the kind that collectors whisper about in the same hushed tones you reserve for limited-edition metal dice—add more tiles, more heroes, more cursed artifacts, and more ways to cheese your friends out of victory.

Arvis Games designed this product to be both approachable for newcomers and rewarding for long-term players who enjoy chasing victory through ever-more-intricate synergy. The packaging calls it a “SUPERPRICE,” a marketing gloss that implies extraordinary value. The truth, as always with board games, resides somewhere between “you get your money’s worth” and “you could have bought a smaller, saner game for the same price.” We’ll return to that category by the end of this review.

A note on tone: The Island is chaotic, loud, and often non-euro in feel. If you’re a tight-lacing engine-builder who prefers a quiet farm about resource management, this game will feel like putting a cat in a kayak: delightfully ridiculous, occasionally triumphant, and always suspicious of the weather. If you want to know how it actually plays, keep reading. If you want to know how it feels to be outplayed by a cardboard cutout of a parrot, we’ve got you covered as well.

## The box contents and component quality

The Core Box
- A double-sided island board that snaps into a few dozen configurations. The variety is real, though some setups feel more balanced than others, which is exactly what the game wants you to debate for the next hour.
- Hero minis: chunky, well-detailed, and occasionally wobbly on their plastic bases. Expect some paint-splatter enthusiasm from hobbyists, and some minis that resemble a potato with armor from the factory, which is okay because the game’s personality is the real star here.
- Resource tokens, loot tiles, and event decks: sturdy enough to survive a dozen playtest sessions, and the art direction is vibrant enough to make you squint at the map to figure out where you are.
- A deck of character cards with unique abilities. Some powers feel overpowered out of the box; others require building a tiny strategy around a later-expansion card to become truly powerful. This is deliberate: asymmetry is part of the design language.
- The countdown timer is a nice thematic touch, but you’ll rarely rely on it for precise timing. It’s more of a narrative cue that your island is about to erupt in chaos than a precise mechanism you’ll consult every round.

The Eight Expansions
The expansions run the gamut from “just a few extra tiles” to “the sea itself gets a new module and a storm.” Here’s a quick snapshot:
- Expansion 1: More tiles and alternate victory conditions. Balance is a moving target here, so expect some games to favor one strategy while others swing wildly in the opposite direction.
- Expansion 2: New hero abilities that tilt the meta toward trickery and bluffing. These are the good ones if you love mind games.
- Expansion 3: Additional artifacts that can break the game in hilarious ways when combined with Exploration actions.
- Expansion 4: A new event deck with world-altering catastrophes. This is where you remember why you invited your friends over in the first place.
- Expansion 5: Environmental hazards that restructure the map on the fly. The island literally rearranges itself, which is chef’s kiss for theme.
- Expansion 6: A co-op mode that ironically reduces cooperative tension, because the island wants you to work together… to stab each other in the back more efficiently.
- Expansion 7: A revised scoring system to keep things tight until the final counter. Expect dramatic comebacks and heartbreaking wipeouts.
- Expansion 8: A season-pass tier that unlocks a rotating set of “special event” rounds; it’s like a theme park ride, but the line is always long and your dice are getting beaten by the park’s own marketing department.

In terms of quality, the expansions match the core’s tone: they are sturdy, colorful, and a little overstuffed. The art direction ties into the tropical vibe while keeping readability high on the board. If you’re a completionist, the eight expansions feel like a treasure chest you’ll want to open not once, but repeatedly—though your friends may beg you to stop once the board becomes a hyperactive beehive of rules interactions.

## How the game actually plays: setup, flow, and decisions

Setup time is not zero, but it’s not unreasonable either. The island is board, tiles, and a few deck shuffles away from chaos. A typical game sees 3-5 players, depending on your tolerance for friend-flare and existential risk. Each turn, you’ll perform a handful of actions: explore tiles, pick up loot, engage rivals in combat, and weigh the risk of triggering a catastrophe you can’t control. The core loop is fast enough to keep the energy high, but deep enough to reward careful planning if you avoid tunnel-vision sprees.

Combat uses dice that are fairly forgiving, with enough modifiers to make each roll feel meaningful. The outcomes are not deterministic—luck does influence the game, which is essential for a “royale in a box” experience—but there’s always some hedge you can build with items, allies, and a few well-timed bluff plays.

The eight expansions come into play in two respects: they either tilt the core game toward a particular playstyle (for example, more aggressive play via new artifacts) or they introduce modular subsystems that can be added or removed between sessions. The modularity is a strong feature because it means you can tailor the chaos to your group’s temperament. If you’re hosting a slightly competitive but friendly table, you can disable the most punitive expansions. If your group wants to treat it like an annual chaotic tradition, you roll all eight expansions and let the dice decide who pays for the therapy bill later.

Keep in mind that the game’s length scales with player count and with how aggressively people chase the final objective. A 5-player blowout can last longer than a cozier 2-3 player session, and that’s by design: the island thrives on pressure, negotiation, and occasional backstabbing that would make a salad knife blush.

## The island’s mechanics in depth: what works, what doesn’t

- Asymmetrical characters: The different heroes each have signature abilities that reward varied playstyles. This is a strength, because it cultivates table talk and a little chess match energy as players compare who has the skill to pull off a late-game gambit.
- Tile modularity: The board’s modular layout means no two games are identical. This is a huge plus for replayability; you’ll savor discovering a new island layout that creates a fresh tactical problem.
- Event deck and catastrophe mechanics: These introduce dramatic turns. They feel thematic and unpredictable in a way that makes your eyes widen and your tablemates groan in equal measure.
- Resource management: You’ll manage a mix of tokens, loot, and usable artifacts. The tension here is well-constructed: you’ll want to pick up loot but you’ll worry about carrying too much at once and becoming a target for your rivals.

Where the design sometimes falters is balance across expansions. Some combos create power spikes that can feel unfair in apples-to-apples comparisons. The designers clearly want you to experiment, but if you’re not careful, a single artifact chain can overshadow the intended tension of hunting opponents. The fix is to agree on a house rule: Grand Prize Artifacts are only eligible for endgame scoring if they’re earned via a non-contrived sequence of actions, which feels fair and thematically resonant.

## Visuals, theme, and overall vibe

The Island’s art direction nails the tropical, slightly campy vibe. It’s not realistic; it’s the sort of fantastical map you’d expect to see on a poster in a sci-fi comic shop. The character designs lean into archetypes—the fearless hunter, the cunning trickster, the healer who carries a backpack full of questionable remedies—and the expansions push even more wild card options into the mix. If you enjoy a game that looks as chaotic as it plays, you’ll love flipping through the expansion tiles and spotting callbacks to classic adventure tropes.

The physical product feels robust enough for a couple of years of regular play. The miniatures are chunky enough to stand on a table without fear of toppling during a particularly dramatic moment, and the boards lay flat enough to avoid annoying warping problems in most play spaces. If you’re into customizing your setup, you’ll have a field day swapping out tiles and hero cards to tailor the game’s look and feel to your taste.

## Solo play, team play, and table dynamics

If you want a break from the full table, there’s a light solo mode that uses a pared-down event deck and simple AI-ish rules for an opponent. The solo mode works well as a filler when your gaming group is late, or when you want to test a particular build before subjecting your friends to it. It’s not the core experience, but it scratches the itch for “what would this look like if I played alone and talked to myself about strategy in a normal voice?”

Team play is more about negotiation than pure synergy. You’ll help allies sometimes, betray others at strategic moments, and use the expansions to create believable (and not-too-discreet) loyalty tests. It’s a social game at heart, which is exactly what the best party-box experiences strive to be.

## Value for money and price positioning

The SUPERPRICE label intends to signal exceptional bang-for-buck. The box set, base game plus eight expansions, does deliver a broad array of content, but it’s not cheap. If you only want the core mechanics, you can indeed save money by starting with the base edition and testing the waters before investing in expansions. If you crave the full chaotic tapestry and you know your group will commit to long-term campaigns, the price can feel justified as a once-a-year treat or a monthly ritual to bring everyone back to your table.

From a value perspective, the eight expansions are where the equation gets interesting. They aren't all essential by any stretch; some add a lot of flavor and variety, while others are more akin to spice racks that exist because the designers wanted to prove their own limitless imagination. The decision to purchase all eight should come down to whether your group enjoys constant variation, or whether you’d prefer a tighter, more predictable play experience.

If you’re hunting for a quick mental anchor: the core box + 2 expansions is a solid, repeatable baseline. The full eight-expansion bundle is for players who adore the spectacle, not for those who want a lean, strictly balanced strategy game. And yes, you’ve probably guessed this: the more expansions you add, the more table talk and the more “let’s just roll the dice and see what happens” energy you’ll cultivate. That’s not a flaw; it’s a feature—assessing how comfortable your group is with exhilarating chaos is part of the game’s ritual.

## Replayability, balance concerns, and house rules

Replayability is the crown jewel concept here. With a modular board and eight expansions, you’ll rarely encounter the exact same game twice—though you may encounter the same dynamic: someone draws a ridiculously lucky artifact, someone else counters with a perfect counter, and a third player sneaks to victory on the last turn. The narrative arc is strong, which is crucial for a game built on a “battle royale” theme: victory should feel earned, not simply luck-dominant.

Balance-wise, expect swings. Some players will discover “the one combo that wins,” especially when multiple expansions interact. The cure is to mix and match with care or introduce a house rule set that caps certain artifacts or limits the number of wildly overpowered combinations per game. Our recommendation: start with a smaller configured set and scale up as your group gains familiarity. This keeps the table from devolving into a rules-nerd brawl and instead fosters playful experimentation.

## How it compares to other games in the genre

There are many games in the tropical, battle-royale-adjacent space, but few hit this exact blend of open-world exploration and cutthroat competition with such a generous dash of chaos. If you’ve played a game like Blood on the Clocktower, you’ll recognize the social maneuvering and bluffing elements; if you’ve played a heavy euro with resource compression, you’ll notice how the chaos acts like a release valve. Board Royale sits in a niche that’s simultaneously chaotic and character-driven, which is rare and valuable for a table that wants to talk more than they want to optimize.

Compared to other multi-expansion titles, this one excels at providing a coherent thematic throughline—your island’s fate matters, not just your card draw. Some rivals push for optimization to the point of mechanical deja vu; The Island resists that by encouraging players to improvise and adapt to changing terrain and event conditions. If you like feeling clever about your choices, you’ll savor this approach.

## Final thoughts and recommendations

The Island 2nd Edition with eight expansions is not a game for people who want a quiet, methodical engine-building session. It’s a loud, exuberant party that occasionally requires you to pause and explain to your friend why a perfectly reasonable move just triggered a chaotic cascade you didn’t anticipate. The experience scales up wonderfully with player count, and the expansions provide enough variety to turn a single purchase into a multi-year shelf-of-hobbies without ever feeling stale.

If your group enjoys the art of negotiation, dramatic swings, and the occasional ridiculous victory dance after snagging a last-turn artifact, this package is a strong pick. If you’re more into tight resource optimization with a single, elegant path to victory, you’ll want to partition your group’s appetite or treat The Island as a seasonal spectacle rather than a regular staple.

The core box is a solid foundation; the full eight-expansion bundle is an experience in excess. The price is frankly not small, but the value comes from the sheer number of playthroughs and the kinds of stories you’ll tell long after the chips are down. In geeky terms: this is the kind of game that earns its keep through both laughter and occasional fierce debates about macro-level strategy that you’ll still be arguing about years later.

If you’re ready to add some tropical chaos to your game night, you’ve got two paths: start with the core game and add expansions gradually, or dive headlong into the eight-expansion chaos and embrace the mayhem. Either way, you’ll end up with more table stories than you can shake a palm tree at.

## Practical tips for your first dozen games

- Establish a house rule for artifact saturation. Too many artifacts in the early game can derail balance; stagger their introduction.
- Try two different island layouts in your first two sessions to understand how terrain choices shape aggression and retreat routes.
- Rotate the role of “island governor” (the player who manages event timing) to keep the pace lively and give everyone a stake in the turn order.
- Use the co-op mode as a warm-up for new players to catch up with the rules and the rhythm of chaos before diving into full competitive play.
- Save a specific night for expansions-only play, where you purposely ignore base game rules to feel the full expansion’s impact. It’s a ritual; celebrate it.
