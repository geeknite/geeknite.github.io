## What is Codenames Pictures (Vlaada Chvátil edition)?
Codenames Pictures is a sibling to the classic Codenames, but instead of word cards, you’re deciphering a board of pictures. Picture clues often require a broader leap of associative thinking: a cat in a hat might evoke “Madness,” “Capers,” or “Pet Shop” depending on the team. Vlaada Chvátil—master of witty, sometimes devious game design—takes the core espionage vibe of the original and drapes it in a visually rich, sometimes wry, sometimes obtuse picture-language world.

The Czech Games Edition release, featuring the art direction and production values CGE is known for, brings together a 5x5 grid of picture cards, a handful of agents for each team, one assassin card, and a few operation tokens to keep track of who’s winning the war of wits. It’s a lean, focused design surface that rewards clever associations and punishing misreads in the same breath.

In this edition, you’ll notice that the pictures lean into storytelling instead of wordplay. Some are cheeky, some are surreal, some are cleanly abstract. The art direction is a wink to the imagination: a tiny window into a larger world where a single image can carry multiple meanings depending on who’s looking and how fast they’re looking.

## How to play: the basics in a caffeine-fueled paragraph
Codenames Pictures follows the same two-team relay logic as its word-based cousin. Spymasters see all the picture cards on the table and give clues using one or two words (plus a number indicating how many cards the clue relates to). The guessers on their respective teams then try to pick the cards that match the clue. Some cards are gold stars for your team; others point to the opposing team, and there’s always the assassin—the existential gut punch that ends the round immediately if you pick it.

In Pictures, though, you’re chasing a narrative through pictures. A clue like “Adventure, 3” might send the team toward a troupe of explorers, a treasure map, and a ship in a bottle—whatever threads the spymaster can weave into a single mental thread. The catch: the same picture can be read in wildly different ways by different people. That’s the beauty, the chaos, and the occasional headache of this edition. It’s less about exact decoding and more about shared imagination and the social dance of “do we all agree this picture belongs here?”

A typical game plays to about 30 minutes, depending on how aggressively your table tastes risk. It’s perfect for a game night that wants a rapid tempo, lots of laughter, and the occasional heated debate about whether a picture of a cat wearing sunglasses is a clue to “cool” or to “thief in the night.”

### Spymaster phase
The Spymaster looks at all the cards and crafts a clue. The best clues tie into a theme the table is comfortable with: pop culture, literature, or a shared experience that doesn’t require a Wikipedia dive to interpret. The risk is misinterpretation: a good clue can open doors, but a bad clue can close them forever in the eyes of your teammates. And yes, the assassin looms—one wrong card, and the round ends in a dramatic, cinematic fashion that would make even a Bond villain pause mid-plan.

### Field Operatives phase
The field operatives discuss, debate, and finally pick cards to reveal. If the team reveals only their own agents, the round continues; if they reveal the opposing team’s agents, you’re granting the other side a victory with a well-placed misstep; and if you hit the assassin, you may as well surrender to the melodrama and start planning your next party game around the couch-destroying hue of defeat.

## House rules, variants, and optional chaos
No two tables run the same game of Codenames Pictures. Here are a few ideas we’ve seen work well (and some that make the table sigh with equal parts glee and horror):
- Blind Clue: Spymaster offers clues to both teams but with a “safe word” that prevents accidental cross-over thinking. It’s silly, but it makes for surprising strategies.
- Time Crunch: A timer that forces the Spymaster to deliver clues faster; the clock becomes part of the game’s tension and adds a dash of arcade energy.
- Story Mode: After each round, the table creates a micro-narrative based on the images revealed. It’s a collaborative storytelling exercise that rewards creativity and pun-strong teamwork.
- Solo Variant: If you’re sneaking a micro-night with a friend who’s got a logic-nerd heart, try a two-player co-op where both players attempt to solve the board together—sharing clues, balancing risk, and debating the best path forward.

## Strategic tips for the brave and the curious
- Start broad, then narrow: The best early clues are ones that tie to multiple images on the board without leaning too heavily on a single interpretation.
- Read your table: If your teammates are the punny kind, lean into humor and pop-culture associations; if they’re more literal, go toward concrete imagery that can be argued with data (ahem, a picture of a lighthouse clearly implying “guidance”).
- Don’t fear the assassin: It’s both an exit ramp and a drill for risk assessment. If you’re tentatively certain, someone else will be too. Sometimes the safest play is a cautious reveal that avoids the trap.
- Balance is everything: A good spymaster knows when to push for a bold, risky clue and when to pull back to preserve momentum.
