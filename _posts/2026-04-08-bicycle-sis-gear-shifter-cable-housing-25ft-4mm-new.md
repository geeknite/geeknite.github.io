## Installation tips and tricks
### Tools you’ll need
- A good pair of cable cutters or sharp scissors (don’t ruin the roll by trying to tear it with your bare hands)
- A set of needle-nose pliers (to fish the cut housing into ferrules or stops)
- A derailleur cable, properly measured and pre-lubed (if you don’t have a pre-lubed cable, you’re going to learn the word friction in a new, personal way)
- A small bottle of light lubricant for the inner cable and some spray contact cleaner for the outside of the housing
- An Allen wrench or two for the bottom bracket or cable stops, depending on your bike
- Patience; if you have too little patience, take a deep breath and re-check your setup before you cut anything

### Step-by-step installation
1) Measure and plan: Determine which sections need replacing and measure the distance along the frame. A 25 ft roll is generous, but you still want to avoid waste. Plan to use shorter segments for the straight runs and a little extra for the tight bends near the derailleur.

2) Prepare the cable: Cut the housing slightly longer than needed. It’s easier to trim again than to discover you cut too short after you’ve threaded the cable through a ferrule. Debur the ends with a small file or what you’d use to fix a tiny edge on a new coin.

3) Thread the inner cable: Slide the cable through the housing with minimal resistance. If you hear a squeak, you’ve probably got a bend that’s too tight. Gently adjust to maintain a smooth curve. If the inner wire catches on something, back out and re-thread; you don’t want to create a kink that will come back to haunt you on the steepest climb.

4) Fit into the stops: Attach the housing ends to ferrules or stops with the correct end caps. A secure fit reduces slop and ensures the shifting is crisp and predictable.

5) Adjust and test: Use the barrel adjuster to fine-tune tension and the limit screws to keep the derailleur in check. Shift through the gears on a flat stretch, then try a few climbs. If you hear ghost squeaks, you may need to reseat the housing ends.

6) Clean up: Wipe down excess grease from the cable and housing exterior. Cleanliness is part of the joy of the ride, and it helps you pretend to be a professional mechanic during your next home session.

### Common issues and fixes
- Stiff shifts: This can be caused by a too-tight housing bend, a dirty cable, or improper lubrication. Re-route with a slightly longer, smoother bend and recheck for smooth operation.
- Slippage: A result of worn inner cables or misadjusted barrel tension. Replace or adjust accordingly.
- Noise: Could be from misaligned stops or a kinked housing end. Re-check alignment and ensure the ends are capped correctly.

## Maintenance and care
Cables and housing aren’t a one-and-done item; they’re part of a cycle of care and replacement. Here’s how to keep your 25 ft roll in top form.

### Cleaning and lubrication
Keep the exterior clean to avoid grime acting as an abrasive on the housing. Lightly lubricate the inner cable so it slides through the housing with minimal resistance, but avoid over-lubricating the housing itself as it can attract dirt and wear down the internal surface over time. Periodic checks are wise, especially if you ride in rain, snow, or a city where road salt is the new fashion accessory.

### When to replace
If you notice kinking, severe cracking, or significant friction that cannot be resolved with a simple re-routing or slight tension adjustment, it’s time to replace. A 25 ft roll means you have the luxury to replace a few sections without a grocery store trip for hardware or parts. Replacement timing depends on use, environment, and your personal tolerance for squeaks in the night.
