Gameplay: The core loop
Zombie Kittens relies on a fast, chaotic loop: draw, play, resolve, and move on to the next player. The core mechanic is to keep pressure on your neighbors while trying not to become the purely unlucky person who ends up with the doom card. The orange glow of the zombie eye on some cards signals danger, but don’t worry—the humor keeps things light.

## How to Play: Setup and Turn Flow

Setup
- Each player draws a starting hand (typically five cards).
- The deck sits in the center, and you flip the top card to create the first discard.
- Decide the first player, or use a die if you’re feeling theatrical.

Turn structure
- On your turn, draw one card and then play as many cards as you want from your hand as long as you’re following the rules on each card.
- Some cards require you to target other players; others affect the deck or the state of play.
- If you play a card that ends the round for you, you’re out of the running for that round, but the table may still enjoy the chaos around you.

Card types and sample effects
- See-the-Undeath: Peek at another player's hand (be careful—this can spark chaos or anger).
- Attack: Force the next player to take two turns (kicking the door down on a calamity chain).
- Zombie Shuffle: Swap hands with another player (good luck with that one).
- Skip/Reverse: Change the flow of the round (great for shaking up decisions).
- Resurrection: Bring back a previously discarded card or reset certain effects.
- Brain Freeze: A pun-laden snarl that makes players reveal something silly about their strategy.

Interaction and table talk
The heart of the game is interaction. You’re constantly watching the table, counting who has what type of cards, and figuring out whether you should attack first or hold your cards for a more dramatic moment. The social aspect is heightened by the comedic tone; even when you’re losing, there is a joy in watching someone else stumble under a cascade of misplays and mischief.

The theme and humor
The zombie kitten theme is a silly, lighthearted approach to undead and felines. It’s not horror; it’s a party game with a wink and a nudge. The humor comes from puns on brain snacks, cute undead cat art, and the over-the-top dramatic phrases that cards often display. It’s the kind of game where you yell something ridiculous, and the table erupts in laughter rather than hostile shouting. The tone is inclusive of players who prefer lighthearted chaos rather than grim scares.

House rules and optional twists
If you want to push the chaos even further, you can try a few house rules:
- One More Round Rule: After someone hits the doom at the end of a round, add one extra final round to decide the winner—this avoids sudden doom endings that feel anti-climactic.
- All-In Attack: Allow all players to play an Attack card on a single turn; the last player standing wins the round (or loses, depending on your group’s vibe).
- Secret Totals: Have a score sheet where you secretly track points earned per round based on how many pills you took or how many zany cards you played.
