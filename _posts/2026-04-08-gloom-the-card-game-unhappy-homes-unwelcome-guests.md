## Overview: what you’re actually buying here

Gloom is a competitive storytelling card game where you want to make your own Family cards as miserable as possible while making your opponents’ families as cheerful as possible—or, more precisely, as sad as possible for them to endure. The goal is to have the lowest total “Plot Value” in your family by the end of the round. That means you want your family to suffer, but not too early; you want to inflict sorrow on others to push down their scores sooner than you worsen yours.

Unhappy Homes and Unwelcome Guests expand the gloom by introducing new thematic cards that push misfortune into different corners of the house and invite more “guests” who misbehave in how they contribute to the story. We’re talking haunted wallpaper, obsolete teapots with a secret, unhelpful relatives, and the neighbor who always brings a cat with a face that clearly belongs to a dramatic monologue. The expansion cards add more faces, more places, and more opportunities to craft truly melodramatic tableaux of doom.

If you’re a fan of narrative-driven games with a dexterous sense of humor, this duo-pack is basically a pair of stagehands who dump extra fog onto your stage. The fog is optional, but it makes the drama a lot more—well—dramatic.

## The mechanics: how Unhappy Homes & Unwelcome Guests actually change the game

Gloom operates with two main elements: transparent plastic family cards (which you’re trying to torch in the most spectacularly sad way) and an assortment of shadier card types that modify those families or the “story” you’re telling about them.

Unhappy Homes adds new location cards and character cards that affect the home itself. Think: a bedroom with a draft that steals your memories, or a kitchen that chronically forgets your birthday. These new cards alter the playing field by giving you more opportunities to assign misfortune to specific characters or to create interconnected stories that make the audience (the other players) groan in unison.

Unwelcome Guests brings in more guests to spice up the tragedy. Some guests arrive with negative utility (they pile on the gloom) and some with quirky attributes that tilt your storytelling options just enough to make you pause and mutter, “Do I want to make my own guy suffer more, or sabotage my neighbor’s narrative with a devastating guest cameo?” It’s the tabletop equivalent of a party where every guest insists on telling their own sad anecdote at the exact moment you were about to reveal your own twist.

Key tactical ideas you’ll encounter in this Lot:

- Stacking misfortune: you’ll chain effects so that one bad event triggers another, creating cascading tragedy. It’s basically DOM for despair, and the audience never quite believes the punchline until the last card is drawn.
- Story control: you’ll be juggling “story” cards that affect how much your characters suffer and when. Timing is everything; delaying a reveal can mean you sabotage someone else’s tale just as their drama hits its peak.
- House-wide effects: the home-themed cards touch multiple characters, so you can craft a single narrative thread that ties several sad homeowners into a single, glorious tragedy.
- Guest leverage: Unwelcome Guests provides opportunities for strategic interruption. Do you invite a guest who increases your own sorrow while lowering someone else’s, or do you invite chaos in a way that benefits your broader scheme? It’s like a high-stakes drama where every new attendee changes the set design.

For players who enjoy “boss level” misfortune with a sprinkle of wry humor, this expansion duo is a treat. The rules remain accessible; the new cards simply give you more levers to pull, which is exactly what a good party (game) should do when it’s ready to turn into a melodrama you can hold in your hands.

If you’re new to the game, don’t worry: the learning curve stays gentle. Gloom’s core mechanic—make your own family sad while others’ families feel less sad than they do—remains intuitive. The expansions add complexity, but they’re not cryptic. They’re more like “Now with more soap operas, more tragedy, and more reason to sigh dramatically.”

## Setup, pace, and how well the expansions fit into a game night

A Gloom game with the Unhappy Homes and Unwelcome Guests expansions typically follows the same setup rhythm as the base game with a few extra piles to shuffle in. You’ll still deal out family cards to each player, lay down the gloom-laden and the doom-laden effect cards, and begin the routine of weaving a story around your characters. The new cards simply add more options to the storytelling script and more places for misfortune to land.

Pacing with the expansions is a touch more dynamic. You’ll often experience slightly longer rounds because there are more cards in play and more potential interactions. This can be a boon for groups that like to savor the narrative, as you’ll have longer “build-up” segments and more opportunities for dramatic timing. If you’re in a rush, you can easily trim by discarding or not using a subset of the expansion cards, but that’s always an option you’ll wish you could explain with a dramatic sigh.

One potential caveat: if your group has a strong preference for quick, light games, the extra layers of storytelling in Unhappy Homes & Unwelcome Guests may push the rounds toward the upper end of a typical session. It’s still a “light-medium” weight game, but with expansions, you’re trading speed for storytelling depth.

A well-run session often looks like this:
- Turn-based reveals as players add to their fellow players’ misfortune
- A couple of “guest” interruptions that shift how you score and how you tell stories
- A final moment where one player realizes their last card has tipped the scale toward maximum melodrama for all involved

If you want a quick, repeatable cadence, you can implement a “five-minute warm-up” hack: set up with a handful of pre-selected cards, and require a quick 60-second storytelling snippet per player before passing the turn. It’s silly, but it’s the kind of silly that makes the mood for the rest of the game flourish.

## Narrative craft: stories, endings, and that sweet dramatic payoff

The heart of Gloom lies in storytelling. It’s not just about cramming as many doom icons as possible onto your family; it’s about crafting a coherent and entertaining tragedy that makes the other players grin, groan, and occasionally mutter, “I’ve made a terrible life choice.”

Unhappy Homes adds narrative threads about places to doom your family—rooms in the house with their own misfortunes—while Unwelcome Guests introduces characters who influence the arc of your stories in fresh ways. The result is not a random pile of bad things; it’s a collaborative improvisation where every card flip nudges the tale toward an inevitable, hilariously tragic conclusion.

Pro-tips for storytelling with these expansions:
- Build a recurring motif: a family member with a recurring unfortunate trait (e.g., a shadowy aunt who keeps misplacing happiness) creates a satisfying throughline.
- Use guest timing to pivot: drop a guest at just the right moment to steal a turn and alter the emotional weight of the scene.
- End with a twist: save a final card that flips the table with a punchline—an unexpected cause of gloom that makes the room howl with laughter and sorrow in equal measure.

These moments are the reason people come back to Gloom. It’s not just the cards; it’s the shared storytelling that turns a routine game into a small theatrical event with a cheerful cup of tea that’s probably plotting something dark and delicious.
