## Deck Building and Meta Considerations
Post‑draft, you’ll want to translate your seeded experience into a coherent deck or two that can stand up to casual, local tournament, or a mixed session with friends of varying skill levels. Draft Box 06 leans toward flexible archetypes rather than strict, one‑note decks. That means you’ll likely walk away with a few strong three‑card combos, a few solid midrange lines, and some pressure options that require careful sequencing to maximize. Here are practical tips to build from this box:

- Prioritize synergy clusters: Group cards that interact with each other to create a coherent plan. Single cards with splashy effects are fine, but the real value tends to show up when you can string together three or four pieces.
- Diversify your threats: In draft, you often face opponents who can answer a linear plan. Mix in cards that scale well with different board states and that can win checks in multiple scenarios.
- Manage your energy curve: The DBS game system rewards careful energy management. Don’t overcommit early; instead, aim to hit a point where you have options every turn and the opponent struggles to stabilize.
- Sideboard ideas: If you’re playing a more structured draft night with sideboarding, keep a small pool of interaction cards for common opposing strategies, but don’t overload the deck with too many countermeasures. You want a deck that still feels agile and capable of improvisation.

If you’re new to deck building after a draft, the quick-start sheet inside the box can become your best friend. It’s not a substitute for personal experimentation, but it’s a strong baseline that helps you identify immediate opportunities to implement a plan and test how your choices behave against a variety of opponents.

## Practical Play Tips for Tonight's Session
To help you walk away with a successful and entertaining night, here are some practical takeaways you can apply to your first few drafts with Draft Box 06:

- Don’t force a color you’re not comfortable with: Drafts where players force a color with weak removal and little acceleration tend to stall. If you’re not confident to pilot a secondary color, pivot to a safer, more flexible plan.
- Aim for late‑game inevitability: Even if you’re not winning early, you can look to a few precise turns where your cards line up to force a board state that your opponent can’t fully answer. Those turns feel incredibly satisfying and aren’t as punishing as you might fear.
- Control the tempo: Light, timely removal can tilt the game without burning your best threats. Try to craft turns where your opponent is forced to react rather than execute their own perfect plan.
- Communicate clearly at the table: Drafts are social experiments. Describe your intended plan briefly, read the room for sentiment, and adjust. A good plan plus good table talk can be half the fun of a night out with friends.
