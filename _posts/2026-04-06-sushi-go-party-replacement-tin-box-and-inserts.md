## Inserts: materials, design, and practical fit
Inserts are the heart and soul of keeping a command center for your Sushi Go Party. They come in various materials: foam, EVA foam with felt, dense cardboard, or thin plastics. The best inserts for replacement purposes tend to be foam-based or EVA with cutouts that snug the cards and tokens without bending them. Here is what to look for:

- Slot density: There should be enough slots for the number of cards in the Party expansion plus the base set tokens. If you are packing tokens like little soy sauce bottles, you will want a dedicated space that reduces movement and noise. A cramped insert can cause everything to rattle and become a mini percussion section during a bumpy ride.
- Card-specific cutouts: Some inserts offer shallow card slots that hold a single deck securely. Others provide deeper compartments for multiple decks. If you have misaligned cards in your storage, you know the pain of cards curling under pressure and grumbling about life choices.
- Tokens and chips: A solid insert will include well-fitting pockets or slots for the tokens and counters that come with Sushi Go Party. If your inserts only hold cards, you might as well be transporting a deck of playing cards with a handful of loose chips floating around like tiny pirates.
- Protection level: Foam materials provide padding but can compress over time. EVA foam tends to hold its shape longer, but you should confirm that the inserts are not so rigid that they gouge your cards when you force them into place. A little give is nice, a little bite is not.

For the general case, the inserts should be modular or at least have a couple of adjustable slots so you can reorganize as needed. The goal is to minimize shifting during transport while also maximizing the usable space. If the inserts are so rigid that you need a crowbar to insert a card, you are doing it wrong.

Sushi Go Party expands the baseline with more cards and additional tokens, which means your replacement tin and inserts must be flexible enough to accommodate a larger stack. In practice, compatibility hinges on few factors:

## Practical tips for restoration and reuse
Even if you purchased a bare-bones insert and tin, you can get creative with your storage to maximize lifespan and minimize wear on your cards:

- Labeling: Use small label stickers to mark compartments. It saves precious seconds when you are riffling through the tin in the middle of a heated game night. Quick labels also help new players find their way without turning your dining room into a detective agency.
- Card sleeves: If your cards are not sleeved, you might want to sleeve them before placing them in the insert. Sleeved cards slide more predictably and resist corner wear. If you sleeve them, ensure the insert slots can handle the added thickness.
- Cleaning routine: A quick wipe-down before storing cards can keep your tin box fresh. A mild soap solution or a microfiber cloth will wipe away the inevitable smudges of soy sauce and accidental marker ink left by your younger self. Do not use harsh solvents that could degrade the card faces or ruin the insert material.
- Travel considerations: If you plan to move from game night to game night via bus, car, or cramped spaceship, consider tapping a bit of flexible foam around the edges to dull vibration. A little padding makes a big difference when the tin becomes a tiny drum during potholes.

## Alternatives to replacement inserts
If you are not convinced by a dedicated replacement tin and inserts, there are a few strategies you can attempt:

- DIY inserts: Some hobbyists cut foam or cardboard into card-sized slots and glue them into a plastic tray. This is a weekend project that may result in a custom-fit solution, albeit with a higher risk of misalignment. It is the MacGyver approach for people who know their way around a ruler and a hot glue gun.
- Card organizers from other brands: Look for universal card organizers that advertise compatibility with small deck games. These can sometimes be adapted to Sushi Go Party with a bit of trimming and flexible inner dividers. The downside is aesthetics and a potential mismatch between card thickness and divider height.
- External storage solutions: If the base tin is beyond salvage or you simply want to keep components pristine, consider separate sleeves and a dedicated pouch for tokens. It may mean a different travel experience, but it can also reduce wear on the actual tin interior.

The most important thing is to maintain the integrity of your cards. A good insert is not about fancy branding; it is about the practical reality that you should be able to reach for the exact card you want without auditioning half of your deck.
