### Assembly and maintenance
Packing and assembly are the least glamorous parts of trampoline ownership, but they’re the most consequential for long-term satisfaction. ORCC trampolines ship with a hardware kit, instruction manual, and a lot of small bolts that require a calm playlist and a patient friend or two. A typical assembly flow:
- Unpack and inventory parts against the manual. Lay out the frame rails and legs so you’re not fumbling with a million small pieces later.
- Assemble the base frame, ensuring corners are square and legs are evenly tightened. A square frame translates to safer lateral motion and less wobble.
- Attach the springs to the frame and to the mat. This is the moment where you realize counting springs matters. A spring tool helps avoid pinched fingers and dramatic grimacing.
- Erect the enclosure net: align poles, secure top rails, and affix the net with taut tension. The zipper should function smoothly.
- Final safety check: walk around the frame, bounce gently to confirm stability, and remove any tools or packaging that could become projectiles during weekend chaos.

Maintenance is straightforward: inspect springs for corrosion or cracks, re-tighten loose frame bolts with a good wrench, keep the mat clean, and inspect the enclosure net for wear and tear. If the weather is rough, you may want to cover the unit or relocate it to a safer under-cover area to extend its life. 

## Step-by-step assembly guide (quick reference)
- Start with the frame in a level, open space. Attach legs with the provided hardware; ensure all legs are secure and evenly tightened. 
- Attach the frame segments using the included connectors. A helper can make this easier. 
- Install the protective pads around the frame to prevent injuries from contact with sharp metal corners. 
- Mount the enclosure posts and connect the net to the frame. Make sure the net is taut and the zipper aligns with the frame so you don’t have to perform a late-night zipper-tango. 
- Attach the jumping mat with springs. Use the spring tool to secure each spring to both the mat and the frame. Avoid over-stretching; you want consistent tension, not a trampoline that looks like an accordion. 
- Double-check the entire structure for wobble. If it wobbles, tighten the bolts and re-check the ground anchors if you’re on soft soil. 
- Perform a slow test bounce with light use before inviting the kids to join in. 

### Performance, safety, and user experience

### Myths, physics, and the bounce reality
- Myth: Bigger is always better. Reality: Bigger frames give more room to wobble and misjudge landings; smaller frames can be surprisingly stable if the jump mat and net are properly tensioned. 
- Myth: A stiffer mat means better performance. Reality: You want a balance of elasticity and support. Too stiff, and you’ll feel every micro-jump around your ankles; too soft, and you’ll sink into the mat and risk unrealistic bounces. 
- Myth: The enclosure net is optional. Reality: It’s a safety feature that reduces accidental exits from the bounce zone. It’s not optional in the eyes of your neighbor when Aunt Edna’s lawn chair becomes a high-velocity obstacle.

### Safety-first tips and zone planning
- Always supervise children during use. The trampoline should be a supervised play area and not a free-for-all. 
- Create a safety perimeter around the trampoline to keep dogs, sprinklers, and curious siblings away from the takeoff area. 
- Enforce one-at-a-time or supervised multi-kid play depending on weight distribution and experience. 
- Weather is a major factor; postpone jumping in rain, snow, or high winds. 
- Regularly inspect safety features: net tautness, zipper alignment, frame bolts, and mat wear.
