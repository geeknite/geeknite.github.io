## How to play and what makes it tick
### Setup and flow
Card Sharks unfolds in a handful of rounds, each compact enough to fit on a coffee break but with enough tension to feel like a tiny tournament. Setup is straightforward: shuffle, deal, assign a starting pot, and designate a first bettor. Turns progress as players bet, call, or bluff, and the round ends when someone correctly reads or misreads a bluff, shifting chips or points accordingly. The rulebook emphasizes quick rounds and light strategy, designed to keep the table moving rather than stalling while someone unveils a decades-long repertoire of tells.

### Core mechanics in plain language
In plain terms: each round, a player reveals information about their hand or opponents, and others decide whether to accept risk, double down, or fold. The bets are the currency of tension; the cards themselves introduce a handful of surprises to keep gameplay fresh. Bluffing is the main spice, and a well-timed read of an opponent can turn an underdog position into a table-wide moment of triumph. The mechanics are approachable for casual players but reward social strategy for those who want to tilt odds with clever misdirection and timing.

### The pace and table presence
One of Card Sharks’ standout traits is its pace. Rounds are short but not bite-sized to the point of feeling undercooked; the tension builds with each reveal, and a perfect bluff can flip the table dynamics in a single breath. The design invites talking, joking, and the occasional dramatic misread, which is exactly the social atmosphere many geeks covet when trying out a new title. If you enjoy party games with a competitive edge that doesn’t demand endless rules-lawyering, Card Sharks scratches that itch without becoming an instruction manual masquerading as fun.

## Strategy tips and reader-friendly nudges
- Read the room: social bluffing shines when you’re attuned to seating dynamics, pacing, and the obvious tell you can’t quite place yet.
- Mix bets with banter: keep the energy high but avoid tipping your hand with overly obvious tells.
- Use small, strategic bets to probe: you don’t always need to go big to test a bluff—sometimes a conservative bet exposes more about others’ confidence than a bold move.
- Create micro-narratives: turn a sequence of bets into a story the table can follow; it makes misreads more entertaining and less punitive.
- Establish house rules up front: a few agreed tweaks can keep the game fresh but coherent across multiple sessions.
