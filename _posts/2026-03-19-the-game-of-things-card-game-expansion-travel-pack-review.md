The Game of Things is a shape shifter in the world of party games. The base game wants you to write absurd or surprisingly honest responses to prompts, then give someone else the citation for the best or worst response. The Travel Pack expansion is the compact little buddy that slides into your carry-on like a TSA dream weapon for humor. It promises new prompts, travel friendly cards, and a justification for you to beg your friends for one more round in the back of a taxi or at a layover gate.

In this review we will cover what the Travel Pack actually contains, how it changes the core experience, and whether the humor lands when you are crammed into a vehicle with someone who says they only run on coffee and sarcasm.

## Overview

The Travel Pack is designed to be the smallest possible version of a party game expansion while retaining the vibe that you expect from The Game of Things. It features a new prompt deck, a streamlined rules section, and a compact card holder that looks suspiciously like a passport wallet. It is not a replacement for the full expansion but a deliciously bite-sized companion that you can toss into a backpack, glove compartment, or a suit jacket pocket that you keep for snacks anyway.

### What's in the Travel Pack

The exact contents vary by edition and retailer, but in general you get a handful of card slices that carry prompts like a caffeinated prompt-manda. The prompts are designed to tease out players responses with minimal setup. The set also includes a small rule booklet that spells out how this pack interacts with the base game. For players who know the base game inside and out, this expansion is less a redefinition than a reminder of why this game works so well in short bursts.

### Card quality and design

The cards are typically durable stock with rounded corners and a gloss that can survive a bit of airport lounge neglect. The art is humorous but not overbearing; you will not confuse a travel prompt with a luggage tag on the outside of your suitcase. The fonts are readable in dim lights, which is a blessing when your host insists on a midnight game under fluorescent hallway bulbs.

## Gameplay and player interaction

If you know how to play the base game, the Travel Pack requires almost no extra mental overhead. The prompts lean into the same mix of silly, snarky, and surprisingly insightful responses. The travel edition often features prompts designed for quick rounds, which means you can squeeze a couple of games into a layover if you are willing to risk a few annoyed airport security guards from the laughter you cause.

### Prompts and humor

If you love silly prompts that require players to channel their inner stand-up comedian, you will not be disappointed. The expansion adds prompts that revolve around travel mishaps, awkward moments on planes, and the universal experience of trying to explain how the TSA works to your friend who refuses to check a bag. The humor lands best when at least three players participate and two are willing to lean into ridiculous.

### Quality of components

The travel pack emphasizes portability above all else. The box is slim, the cards fit, and the rule book fits in your back pocket like a spare battery. The only real complaint is that the compact size can lead to misplacement if you are not careful — which is exactly the kind of hazard a travel game should anticipate.

## How it changes the base game

The base game asks players to write down prompts and then have others guess which response goes with whom. The Travel Pack doesn't alter the core scoring or the timing; it adds new prompts and a handful of variant setups that you can mix and match with the standard deck. It is the kind of expansion that respects the original design while offering enough new flavors to keep things fresh.

### House rules and variants

If you want to mix up your play sessions, consider a variant that emphasizes speed rounds. In this mode, players have 60 seconds per round to draft and read their prompts. Another variant is to rotate the judge so that all players get equal opportunities to be in charge of the discourse. These small tweaks help maintain energy and prevent the game from stalling in a long social night.

### Sample round walkthrough

Setup: four players around a coffee-fueled table. You shuffle the prompts, deal three cards to each player as potential prompts, and one prompts card to the judge. The judge reads the prompt aloud and the rest of players write a response on their sheet (or the included answer pad). When the timer starts, you scrawl your best quick quip and pass your slip to the judge. The judge reads all responses aloud, and votes by secret show of hands or a digital poll if you are playing in a modern lounge with a questionable wifi signal.

Round example: the prompt is travel related a la, what is the most ridiculous thing you could bring on a plane that would still be allowed by TSA. Players throw out their responses; talk about the reasons their choices are better than others. Laughter erupts at a ridiculous pick like a life-size inflatable dolphin or a snorkel disguised as a marshmallow roasting fork. The judge awards the point to the best response and the next round begins.

### Accessibility and inclusivity

We found a few prompts that could be sensitive or culturally loaded. The best practice is to pre-screen prompts for your group, and to set guidelines for what to skip or reframe. Not every prompt will land with every group, but a respectful approach and a sense of humor goes a long way. If you want to lean into inclusivity, you can create a set of safe prompts and swap them in, using the Travel Pack as a flexible shell.

## Value and longevity

If you own the base game and perhaps a larger expansion or two, the Travel Pack slots in as a reliable add-on that you can pull out whenever you want to dispel fatigue without blowing a Saturday afternoon. The portability means it is always as ready as your phone’s battery minus the risk of a tweet that aliens are invading. It also means you can reuse prompts, adjust the number of rounds, and tailor the experience to the group size. If your gaming group thrives on quick, sharp humor rather than long, sprawling campaigns, the Travel Pack may earn a permanent seat in your carry-on.

## Storage, care, and sustainability

A lot of travel editions lean on plastic sleeves and sketchy cardboard. The Travel Pack is typically better than that. The card stock is sturdy enough to survive a few jams in a suitcase, and the packaging is slim enough to fit wherever you keep your flight snacks. If you care about sustainability, you might consider reusing a small, eco-friendly pouch to avoid contributing to the airport's plastic pollution. The Travel Pack is not a luxury edition, but it is friendly to the planet and your luggage.

## How to host a travel night like a pro

- Pick a flight-friendly playlist that does not overpower conversation but sets the mood.
- Label a few prompts as safe or spicy depending on your group's vibe.
- Invite a guest juror to keep things fair and to judge with a sense of whimsy.
- Have a light snack ready to go; laughter pairs well with chips and trail mix.
