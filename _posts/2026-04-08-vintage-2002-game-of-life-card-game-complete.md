## How to play: a compact walkthrough
This edition is designed to be approachable, but not dumbed-down. The aim is to get through life’s big moments by leveraging the cards in your hand, your career outcomes, and the luck of the draw. Here’s a practical walkthrough that will help you set up and play without needing to consult a lawyer, a life coach, or a tearful aunt who is always happy to remind you about all the things you didn’t accomplish in your twenties.

### Setup
- Shuffle the Life, Career, Event, and Money decks separately.
- Each player draws a starting hand from the Life deck to reflect their first big decision. You can decide whether this is the classic married-with-kids start or a more modern, single-ship budget scenario.
- Decide the order of play. Some groups let the oldest player go first; others flip for initiative. Either way, the goal is to get everyone into the game’s rhythm quickly.
- Place the currency tokens within reach. In a nutshell, you’ll use these to pay salaries, taxes, and the occasional mortgage payment.

### Turn structure
- Draw phase: Draw one Life card and possibly one Event card depending on the variant you’re playing.
- Play phase: Use a Career card to determine your base salary for the round. Play any Life or Event cards that apply immediately. You may be required to pay taxes or fees, or you may unexpectedly hit a windfall.
- Resolution phase: Adjust your cash on hand. Some cards may force you to take penalties or to restructure your assets (the more dramatic the better).
- End-of-turn: If you’ve achieved a milestone—like owning a house or hitting retirement—you’ll collect corresponding tokens. Your goal is to stack wealth and status in a way that keeps you in the running for the final victory.

The actual rules can vary slightly depending on your group’s house rules or the exact versions you own, but the spirit remains: balance risk and reward, manage cash flow, and enjoy the drama as life events shower you with both absurd perks and ridiculous expenses.

### A quick example of a typical turn
You draw a Life card that says, You win a tax refund, plus a debt collection notice for your student loans you forgot about. Your Career card gives you a salary, a small discount on a mortgage, and a special benefit that you can apply to future turns. You might decide to invest in a bigger house token, spending cash now in exchange for a potential future payoff. Your Event card might quiz you with an unexpected expense due to a family vacation. The turn ends with you reevaluating your assets and deciding whether to cut back on non-essentials or to keep riding the wave of life’s unpredictable carnival.

It’s fast, it’s messy, and it’s a surprisingly good test of your bluffing skills when your friends start negotiating for more favorable loan terms or suggesting you should just “buy the retirement condo now and enjoy the ride.” You may laugh, groan, and perhaps rethink your own life choices as you watch your pretend-take-home pay propagate through the game’s economy.

## Strategy and tips for the modern retro gamer
- Play to the end, not merely to win this round. A lot of players get laser-focused on the final tally and forget to enjoy the journey. A great strategy is to pick a long-term asset plan early and steer your choices to support that stack of house tokens and salary cards.
- Manage risk with flexibility. The Event deck is your friend and your nemesis. Use events to maximize cash flow when times are good, but don’t neglect emergency funds for when a sudden expense hits.
- Don’t be shy about bargaining. The card game’s social dynamics thrive on light negotiation. If your friends offer a better deal in exchange for a favor later, take it—just make sure the payoff is worth the risk.
- Deck order matters. Shuffling with intention—like keeping high-value Life cards toward the top for later bursts—can give you an edge in longer games. It’s not cheating; it’s strategy with a wink.
- Don’t underestimate the power of a well-timed retirement. In a lot of games, retirement is not just a finish line; it’s a gateway to points that can swing a close match. If you can plan for retirement with your earnings, you’ll often see the biggest payoff.

For families, this game offers a surprisingly good teachable moment: money management, decision making under uncertainty, and the folly of “more is better” when you’re paying for life events.
