Version 1 vs Version 2: A Collector’s Perspective
--------------------------------------------------
Version 2, if issued, would likely aim to fix any minor aesthetic discrepancies or durability concerns observed in Version 1. Think of Version 2 as the “polygon fix” patch for a beloved video game: not a reboot, but a refinement. Collectors often seek Version 1 specifically because it represents the item closer to its original release state. In some markets, Version 1 decks are valued slightly higher for their presumed scarcity and for the nostalgia of holding a first wave product.

Material Quality and Card Stock
================================
Card stock quality is the backbone of any set’s longevity. The 1984 LA Olympics cards aren’t modern 310gsm magic, but they aren’t flimsy either. They strike a middle ground: resilient enough for regular game play, yet delicate enough to reward careful handling. The finish is notboard-flat; it’s a tactile, slightly powdery texture that tells you there’s an artistic decision behind the manufacturing process. If you’re used to modern plastic or 1.5-millimeter thick poker cards, you might notice a modest difference in stiffness, bend, and spring. Yet that difference is part of the charm: they feel “authentic.” You can perform a few deck-sleights, maybe a rifle or two, without fear of immediate edge wear. Still, being sealed, the true test of longevity is what’s left beneath the shrink wrap: a deck that has stayed faithful to its original geometry.

Preservation: How to Store and Care
=====================================
If you’re lucky enough to own a sealed two-deck set, you’re the custodian of potential future value. The best preservation strategy is minimal handling and stable storage. Temperature control matters; humidity matters; direct sunlight is a villain with a grin. Keep the decks in their original protective casing if possible. If you’ve opened the wrap for inspection (we won’t judge you—we’ve all done it, at least once in the name of research), consider storing the unsealed decks in protective sleeves, possibly in a binder. For the truly obsessive, a climate-controlled display case with UV protection is the gold standard; or at least a polycarbonate card holder with an inert spacer that prevents flexing.

Authenticity and Value Tips
===========================
- Look for the Version 1 marker on the packaging (if it’s present in the labeling) and compare print characteristics with known reference images.
- Examine the edge wear pattern; sealed decks should not show signs if they’re truly factory-sealed.
- Compare the back design alignment. Any misalignment in the modern reproduction will be a tell-tale sign.
- Confirm the seal integrity. A broken seal generally reduces value a good bit, especially for buyers who want mint condition.
- Seek provenance when possible: a receipt, a photo of the packaging in storage, or a catalog reference from the era adds credibility.
