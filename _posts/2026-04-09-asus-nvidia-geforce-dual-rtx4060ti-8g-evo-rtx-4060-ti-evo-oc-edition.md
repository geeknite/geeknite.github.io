## Specifications at a glance (why this matters)

The RTX 4060 Ti is a mid-to-upper mid-range performer in Nvidia’s current stack, and the ASUS Dual EVO OC Edition wears it with a measured confidence. Here are the high-level specs that matter for most builds:

- GPU architecture: Ada Lovelace (yes, the fancy one) 
- CUDA cores: around the mid-5k region (exact counts vary by SKU, but expect ~4352 cores for the 8GB variant)
- Memory: 8GB GDDR6, 128-bit bus
- Memory speed: typically around 18 Gbps (effective), which translates to solid memory bandwidth for 1080p/1440p textures
- Boost clock: factory OC, generally in the sub-2.6 GHz range depending on cooling headroom
- TGP: around 170-190W typical, with some OC scenarios nudging higher
- Outputs: 1x HDMI 2.1, 3x DisplayPort 1.4a (varies by model)

What that means in practice: you’re looking at a card that can hit 1440p with respectable frame rates in a majority of modern titles, deliver decent ray tracing with DLSS 3 uplift, and do all of this without making your PC sound like a jet engine when you launch a heavy title.

For those who love real numbers, we ran this card on a test rig with a modern CPU, 32GB of fast RAM, and a PCIe 4.0 NVMe drive. Our testing included several modern AAA titles at 1440p, a handful of esports titles at 1080p, and a couple of ray-traced engines with DLSS 3 enabled. Some numbers will look familiar to you if you’ve been following mid-range GPUs since the dawn of the 30-series, but the crucial point is that ASUS’s implementation keeps noise down, power consumption predictable, and performance up where it counts.

## Design and build quality: form meets function

### PCB and cooling architecture

ASUS uses a compact PCB paired with a two-fan cooling solution on the EVO OC Edition. The PCB is designed to keep VRMs cool enough for daily use and light overclocking without needing a massive cooler block. The two fans are not the loudest on the market, but they’re not the quietest either—think pleasant hum rather than a guillotine of air. The card’s heat spreader and backplate are robust enough to feel premium without making you wonder if you bought a partially assembled sculpture. The heat sink is designed to manage the 170-190W TGP range gracefully, with sufficient copper in critical hotspots and a well-placed sump of aluminum fins to spread the heat across the chassis.

### Dimensions and compatibility

Length is a consideration with any dual-fan GPU, and the Dual EVO OC Edition is compact enough to fit in most standard mid-tower builds. It’s not a featherweight, but it’s not a behemoth either. Height and width are within typical single-GPU dimensions for a dual-slot card, which means you won’t have to rearrange PCIe lanes to accommodate it. The shroud design avoids protrusions that would otherwise collide with cable management or other PCIe devices.

### Aesthetics and RGB

Let’s be honest: the crowd that cares about subtle RGB is a fan of subtlety. The EVO OC Edition is clean, with a restrained lighting profile that you can enable or disable in the driver. It’s enough to satisfy your inner SJW (no, not that one) who demands a little glow, but not so much that your PC looks like a Las Vegas showroom. If you’re building a professional-looking rig that can still game like a beast, this card slides right into the vibe.

## Cooling and thermals: how quiet is quiet?

### Acoustic performance

During light workloads, you’ll hear almost nothing from the Dual EVO OC Edition—great for streaming, editing, or pretending to work while gaming. Under heavy gaming loads, the fans ramp up to a steady, not-so-annoying whirr. It never crosses the line into “the GPU is going to wake the neighbors,” which is always nice. In our tests, the idle-to-load transition was smooth, and the GPU hovered around mid-60s Celsius under sustained 1440p gaming in moderate ambient room temperatures. You won’t see the chassis turning into a small jet engine, and that matters when your desk is in a small apartment or a quiet office.

### Thermal headroom and overclocking potential

The EVO OC Edition leaves a little headroom for overclocking—enough to push a bit more FPS in a few titles, but not so aggressive that you’re chasing a white whale of stability. The factory OC profile provides a mild uplift in clocks, and with modest manual tuning (＋50 to 100 MHz on the core, a little tweaking on memory), you can squeeze a few extra frames in demanding titles. Just remember: every GPU is unique. Some chips love the OC, some are meh about it; the only way to know for sure is to test on your specific rig and watch temperatures closely. If you prefer a completely hands-off approach, leave it on the OC profile and enjoy the ride, knowing ASUS’s cooler does a good job keeping things civil.

## Performance: how does it actually run?

We tested the ASUS Dual RTX 4060 Ti EVO OC Edition across a matrix that mirrors a typical mid-range gamer’s library: 1440p, some 1080p, a sprinkle of ray tracing with DLSS, and a few titles that tilt toward VRAM-friendly textures and stable frame pacing. Here are the highlights from our hands-on run:

### 1080p testing: esports and shooters

- Esports titles (CS:GO, Valorant, Apex Legends): consistently stable north of 240 FPS, with the EVO OC Edition well within single-digit frame dips under peak maps. The card trades micro-stutters for your nodding head and keeps your aim steady.
- AAA shooters (control-level titles) at 1080p: you’ll see low-to-mid 150s FPS depending on texture settings and RT load. Not the target resolution for this card, but it’s comforting to know it can punch above its weight if your display’s sync-friendly.

### 1440p testing: the sweet spot

- AAA open-world games (e.g., a recent blockbuster with crowd density and weather effects): around 110-140 FPS on high settings with DLSS 3 active. Without DLSS, you’re still in the respectable territory of 70-110 FPS, which is perfectly playable for most players who don’t chase 4K fantasies.
- Light ray tracing: moderate enabling of RT with DLSS 3 tends to keep frame rates in the 70-110 FPS range depending on the scene. It’s not a ray-tracing champion, but it’s a very solid mid-ranger’s approach to RT that doesn’t turn every title into a tech showcase that punishes your hardware.

### Ray tracing and DLSS—the modern reality

DLSS 3 is a godsend for those chasing higher frame counts without a data-cruncher CPU. On the RTX 4060 Ti EVO OC Edition, enabling DLSS 3 tends to yield pretty compelling results in supported titles, especially when you’re playing at 1440p. Ray tracing, when toggled on, is a bit more demanding, but the combination with DLSS provides a usable experience in many titles that would otherwise be rough at native settings. If you want maximum FPS for competitive play, DLSS 3 with a light RT load is a practical sweet spot on this card.

For creative workloads like video editing or 3D rendering, the 8GB of VRAM is typically sufficient for many mid-range projects. It won’t beat a high-end 12GB or 16GB card in VRAM-heavy scenes, but it’s perfectly fine for hobbyists and content creators who want a solid all-rounder that also looks nice in-game.
