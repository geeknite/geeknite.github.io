## Setup and How to Play
- Build the tower: The blocks are stacked in threes, alternating orientation. If you’ve ever built a tower with LEGO, you’ve basically got this down.
- The first player pulls a block from anywhere below the highest two tiers.
- The block is placed on the top of the tower, making the stack taller and more unstable if you’re not careful.
- On your turn, you pull a block and hope the tower doesn’t crash. The air gets thick with dodgy jokes and suspicious glances.
- The game ends when the tower collapses; the last person to successfully remove a block without toppling the tower wins… or perhaps you all cry a little and realize you’ve spent a Friday night fighting a cardboard box.

## The Geeky Angle: Physics, Psychology, and Strategy
Let’s nerd out for a moment. Jenga is a kinetic sculpture, a physics puzzle, and a social experiment all rolled into one. The blocks’ friction, the alignment of blocks across layers, and the distribution of weight all contribute to the moment of truth: will the tower sag gracefully, or will you witness a dramatic tumble that sounds like a tiny forest fire?

- Friction and grain direction: The smooth texture of the wood matters. Found a block that drags a little? That could be your downfall. The exact wood grain alignment can mean the difference between a graceful slide and a catastrophic tilt.
- Center of gravity and leverage: The block you choose changes the tower’s balance. The more blocks you remove from the inside, the more you’re relying on the outer edge to hold the structure together.
- Human psychology: The moment when the next move will likely topple the tower creates a pressure different from any other game: the social stage is set, and every player is playing the most intense round of simultaneous decision-making you’ve seen since Mission Control watched Apollo 13 breathe.

## Variants and House Rules
Traditional Jenga is a clean canvas; the real fun begins when you blend in your own flavor. Here are a few house rules that have worked for us (and sometimes exploded in our faces, which is part of the charm):

- Speed Jenga: Each player has a 15-second timer. If you take longer than 15 seconds, you lose your next turn. This accelerates the anxiety and dramatically increases the number of “do you really want to pull that one?” decisions.
- Block swap: After a successful removal, swap a random block from the tower with a back-of-the-box block from a different game. Chaos, but in a good way.
- Timed crescendo: After every three successful moves, the tower height increases by a single block and the difficulty scales accordingly.
- Themed rounds: Use blocks with clues or stickers that suggest a category (movies, sci-fi, sports). Each time you pull a themed block, you must name something that fits, entertaining a roomful of fans while you resist the urge to drop the whole stack.
- Cooperative variant: All players work together to keep the tower upright for a full minute. It’s oddly satisfying to all win together or lose together, like a nerdy team-building exercise.
- Scoring twists: Keep track of blocks removed per player and award bonus points for blocks removed from the center vs. the edge. It adds a layer of strategy that can spice up a routine game night.

## Pros and Cons
Pros:
- Simple rules; accessible to all ages.
- Portable and reliable; you can bring this to a party without needing a power cord or a manual.
- The tactile wood and satisfying click as blocks are removed make it a sensory delight.
- Great for social bonding and harmless competition.

Cons:
- It can be frustrating if the group is too cooperative (nobody wants to topple the tower if a teammate is at risk).

- The rhythm can drag on if players take too long between turns, causing the tension to sag.
- Replacement blocks are not included in the base box; you might break the streak if you lose the last piece.
