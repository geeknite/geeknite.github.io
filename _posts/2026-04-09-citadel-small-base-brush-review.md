## What is the Citadel Small Base Brush?
The Citadel Small Base Brush is designed as a base-coating tool. In theory, base coats are the first real color you lay down on a model after primer and blue-tac, and they should be applied in smooth, even layers. The base brush is typically sized to handle slightly larger surface areas than a standard detail brush, but not so large that it makes control a nightmare. The Small Base Brush is meant to fill that niche: a compact ferrule, a modest-sized synthetic head, and a handle that won’t feel like you’re wielding a medieval weapon every time you reach for it.

In practical terms, you’re looking at a brush that can lay down a base color over irregular surfaces without leaving obvious brush marks—assuming you don’t push too hard or flood the area with paint. It’s not a miracle wand; it’s the kind of tool you reach for to get the first layer down cleanly, then switch to something else for shading and highlighting.

## Performance: Base Coating and Coverage
Now the big test: how does it actually perform when you lay down base coats? The short version: it’s reliable enough to be your go-to for initial layers, especially on flat or moderately textured surfaces. The long version involves a little more nuance.

- Load management: The brush holds a reasonable amount of paint for its size. You don’t have to reload every 2 seconds, which is the dream when you’re trying to cover a 28mm trooper’s armor in under the Game Commander’s clock turn. You do need to reload periodically, as with any brush of this size, but the key is not to flood the bristles. Flooding leads to running paint and pooling in recesses, which then demands a rescue mission with a fine detail brush or a wash to rescue the day.
- Flow and control: The bristles release paint smoothly. There’s a pleasant balance between stiffness and flexibility. It’s not a scalpel, but it’s not a paint gun either. You get a consistent line width, which is essential for base coats on simple shapes.
- Surface adaptation: On flat panels, you’ll get even coverage in a single pass if you have a decently thinned paint and use light, even pressure. On slightly irregular surfaces (think space marine armor with sculpted panels or a vehicle with rivets), you’ll still be able to apply a clean base, but you might find yourself needing lighter passes and a careful second coat to keep the texture from showing too aggressively.
- Drying behavior: Base coats dry fairly quickly, especially with a thin layer. The brush’s performance is improved when you don’t overwork the paint. A few quick passes, a brief pause, and you’re good to go for the next color area. In short, the Small Base Brush is capable of delivering reliable base coats with a reasonable margin for error. It’s not the plushest lipstick brush for the most nuanced shading, but for base work it hits the sweet spot of reliability and speed.

## Maintenance and Longevity
A tool is only as good as its care. Here’s a practical maintenance checklist for your Citadel Small Base Brush:

- Cleaning: Rinse with lukewarm water immediately after painting. For synthetic bristles, you can use mild dish soap if needed, but avoid harsh solvents that may degrade bristle integrity.
- Reshaping: After cleaning, gently reshape the bristles into a clean, tapered tip. This helps preserve the brush’s designed shape and prevents misalignment over time.
- Drying: Let the brush air-dry with the bristles pointing downward to prevent water from running into the ferrule. A small brush rack is ideal; if you don’t have one, rest the brush on its side on a clean cloth.
- Storage: Store with the cap or a brush cover if you’ve got it; this protects the bristles from dust and accidental bending.

With proper care, the Small Base Brush should stay useful for a long while. The synthetic bristles tend to hold their springiness well and resist the common problem of bristle fraying that plagues cheaper brushes.

## Tips and Tricks for Using the Small Base Brush
A few practical tips to help you maximize base coat quality:

- Thin your paints a bit more than you think you should for base coats. A slightly thinner paint will lay down evenly without heavy pooling.
- Use long, smooth strokes. Short, jagged motions create texture you’ll then need to smooth later with a second pass, which defeats the purpose of base coating being quick.
- Clean up quickly after you paint. A quick rinse between color changes helps prevent color bleeds and keeps your base coat looking clean.
- Practice on a test model before you commit to a large order. It’s not cheating; it’s strategy. You want to know how the brush behaves with your chosen paints across different surfaces.
- Pair this brush with a well-primed model. A smooth primer gives the brush a lot less trouble and helps your base coat look more even from the start.

## Common Mistakes and Debunked Myths
- Myth: More pressure yields better coverage. Reality: more pressure often means bloats, bristle splay, and base coats that look like they’ve been spray-painted through a cloud of pepper. Light pressure and a steady hand win the day.
- Myth: Bigger brushes are always faster. Not true for base coats on detailed miniatures; the Small Base Brush hits a sweet spot for maneuverability and control.
- Mistake: Not thinning paints. Base coats need a little thinning; failing to do so leads to brush drag and uneven coverage.
