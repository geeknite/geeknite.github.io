## Card types and sample prompts
The expansion introduces three categories that create variety and help you navigate the delicate art of self-exposure while keeping the energy funny:

- Deep Dive Prompts: Examples include prompts about long-term life goals, personal growth moments, and surprising fears. A sample prompt might be: “Describe a moment when you felt truly seen by someone, and why that changed how you think about yourself.” These prompts demand time, vulnerability, and a willingness to be emotionally present—temper with care, and your group will go from friends to a mini support group that still knows how to roast the heck out of each other.
- What If Cards: These prompt scenarios let you weigh silly vs. serious decisions. A typical prompt could be: “If you could swap lives with any character from a movie for 24 hours, who would it be and why?” The outcomes range from insightful to absolutely ridiculous, and the group’s creative muscles get a cardio workout.
- Reality Check Prompts: These are the spicy ones, designed to surface opinions that you might usually keep to yourself. Example: “Would you rather reveal one embarrassing habit publicly or pretend to be someone you’re not for a day? Explain.” You’ll see friendships tested, but you’ll also get the kind of laughs that only come from irreverent truth-telling.

The prompts are written with a care for consent and boundaries, including quick reminders to keep conversations respectful and inclusive. If someone isn’t comfortable, you can skip or pass—Dizzy Date remains a party game, not a passport to therapy. The expansion respects that boundaries message while leaning into the chaotic joy that adult parties crave.

## Hosting tips for a great game night
Dizzy Date is at its best when the vibe is right. Here are practical suggestions to maximize fun and minimize awkward pauses:

- Set expectations up front: a quick, playful safety check where everyone lists a topic they’d prefer to avoid can save you from a pent-up conversation that ends with a dramatic misinterpretation of a prompt.
- Create a comfortable space: lighting that’s not too dim but not stage-level bright helps participants feel relaxed. A couple of comfortable seats with enough distance between players creates a friendly arena rather than a courtroom.
- Snacks matter: a handful of salty snacks and a few sweet bites keep everyone from turning into philosophical philosophers at the table and instead stay in the realm of good vibes.
- Drinking game balance: if you’re including beverages, moderate sipping and a few non-alcoholic options help maintain energy and prevent the “we’ve peaked emotionally” moment too early in the night.
- Timeboxing prompts: consider assigning a timer for each prompt to maintain momentum. Deep prompts deserve time, but nobody wants a 30-minute monologue about their aunt’s summer vacation photos during a fun party game.
