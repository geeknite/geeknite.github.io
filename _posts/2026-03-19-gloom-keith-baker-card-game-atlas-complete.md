## Gloom by Keith Baker: A Card Game That Lets Your Inner Dark God Take the Night Off

Gloom is not just a card game; it is a mood lighting setup with cardboard cutouts. From the moment you crack open the slim, moody box, you know you are in for a ride that blends dark humor, storytelling, and the occasional misfortune card that makes Aunt Edna’s favorite mug melt into a puddle of emotionally devastating acrylic. Created by Keith Baker and published by Atlas Games, Gloom is a clever little engine that encourages you to pile misfortune onto your own characters while quietly scuttling the same stuff toward your opponents. The irony is delicious, the rules are refreshingly simple, and the art is delightfully grim in a way that makes you want to hug a fog machine while you cry a little about your fictional family.

## What is Gloom, Anyway?

Gloom is a card-drafting game with a storytelling layer shoved into it like a tiny, well-dressed goblin. Each player controls a family of characters, usually preferring to see them suffer more than the other families on the table. The goal is to end the game with your own family as miserable as possible while ensuring the other players’ families are even more miserable. The winner is the one who exits with the lowest total gloom on their family cards. Yes, the lowest gloom wins, which means you want to keep your own characters’ gloom value in check while inflating everyone else’s. It’s like being a frat of benevolent horror writers, except you’re actually playing cards instead of shouting into megaphones at a kegger.

Gloom’s twist is magic: you can attach gloom cards to any character to boost their misery, then you can attach other cards to affect not just gloom numbers but who gets affected when the family eventually dies. The board is simply a handful of character cards on the table, each with their own gloom rating and a banner that tells you whether their fate is good or bad. The gloom cards, the modifiers, and the transparent overlays come together to create a layered, narrative-friendly experience. The game invites you to tell a story as you play. It rewards cleverness, cruelty, and a bit of theatricality.

## Components, Setup, and the Thin Line Between Comic and Cruel

What you’ll find in a typical Gloom box varies slightly by edition, but a classic starter set usually includes:

- A stack of character cards for each player's family
- A matching set of gloom front cards and modifier/backstages that interact with those characters
- Transparent overlay tokens or plastic sleeves that let you display gloom without permanently altering the status of a card
- A handful of token chips for easy gloom tracking
- A few “event” or “quirk” cards that introduce extra twists to a round

Setup is intentionally quick. You deal out a small starting family for each player, you give each player a color-coded gloom overlay, and you shuffle the gloom and modifier decks. The first round introduces a few quirks—like a character who is “loved by a ghost” or a gloom card that mutates when you attach it to a specific type of character. The rules purposefully stay lean; you can get to playing in under five minutes, and you can spend the rest of the evening debating whether a fictional aunt’s taste for antique teacups qualifies as “gloomy enough” for the purposes of the current round.

If you’re a visual person, Gloom plays like a miniature theatre piece. The translucent overlays give you an old-school theater-prop vibe, where every card can be placed to reveal a new layer of misery as you go. It’s not just about numbers; it’s about staging a story that makes sense within the rules you’re using. The result is a game that feels surprisingly cinematic, even when you’re making your grandma’s ghost wear a top hat and a monocle because why not?

## How to Play: The Core Loop and the Philosophy of Misfortune

At its heart, Gloom is a turn-based engine that revolves around three core actions per round: draw, play, and attach. On your turn you draw cards to your hand, you play cards to your family to create or increase gloom, and you attach additional cards to either your own or opponents’ characters to influence gloom totals. The rules are simple enough that a first-timer can learn in a single sitting, but the strategic depths emerge as players learn the best ways to chain misfortunes and to conceal their own poor luck behind a veil of misfortune that belongs to someone else.

Key ideas include:
- Target selection: who should receive gloom right now? Attaching misfortune to an opponent’s character is often a better long-term move than crippling your own team, since the game ends when all families are dead and the final gloom total is tallied.
- Timing and chaining: certain cards trigger when they’re attached, or when a character dies, or when a family card is discarded. The timing of these triggers can dramatically alter the outcome of the round.
- The free-flow storytelling mechanic: since you reveal character backstories and misfortunes as the cards are added, you can weave a narrative that might swing a player's feelings from competitive to warmly sympathetic to hilariously cruel.

The balance between luck and strategy in Gloom is not the barking-mad chaos of a dexterity game, nor is it the sterile crunch of a typical Euro. It sits somewhere in the middle, leaning toward theatre, whimsy, and narrative insurance that nobody has to play perfectly to have a good time. It’s a game that invites you to be dramatic, and the best players are the ones who can be dramatic without turning the table into a courtroom drama.

## Theme, Mood, and Artwork: The Mood Ring of Card Games

Gloom leans into melancholic humor in a way that feels sincere but never grim to the point of damage. The art direction favors moody, Victorian-esque gloom with soft color palettes that still pop against the gloom overlays. The characters have the sort of retro whimsy you’d expect from a fantasy Dickensian vibe, with names that are equal parts punny and charming. The witty flavor text on misfortune cards adds layers to the storytelling and often serves as a gentle reminder that you are, in fact, playing a game while wearing a cape made of cardboard.

The emotional stakes are built into the mechanics. You want to maximize pain on your own family and minimize pain on your opponents', all while narrating a story that makes the other players smile, cringe, or groan in delighted frustration. The humor lands well when you’re in a room with friends who enjoy theater and a good pun. If you’re in a mood that appreciates dark comedy over grim grit, Gloom will feel like a wicked little playground rather than a dungeon crawl.

## Components and Expansions: When Slightly Melancholy Isn’t Enough

What makes Gloom particularly appealing is how the components support the storytelling. Transparent sleeves or overlays let you add gloom without permanently altering cards, which in practical terms means you can rearrange a family for another round without sacrificing clarity. The card stock is sturdy, the art is consistent, and the overall package feels well thought out for a game that sits on the lighter end of the hobby spectrum. The optional expansions, such as Unhappy Homes or other supplementary sets, add new families, new misfortunes, and new tricks to the deck, expanding the storytelling potential without turning the game into a refrain of rulebook checklists.

A word on the counting: because the goal is the lowest gloom total, you get to practice a very satisfying kind of arithmetic where you’re constantly juggling numbers while pretending to be a magical realist author. The math is approachable, and the pity party you throw for your own characters when you’re pulling off an elaborate chain of misfortunes is, frankly, the best kind of personal payoff in a social game.

## The Experience: Social Play, Laughs, and the Occasional Indignant Sigh

Gloom shines as a social experience. It is a game that asks for table talk, story beats, and a willingness to let your opponents in on what you’re planning next. The interplay between players creates a dynamic where you’re not just calculating gloom values in your head; you are crafting a narrative pitch as to why Aunt Mabel deserved a little more misfortune than Uncle Gideon this time around. The humor tends to pop up in the best moments: when a chain of misfortunes collapses into a perfectly timed reveal, or when a character’s “happy ending” finally gets crushed by a ironically titled gloom card.

One pleasant surprise is how accessible the game remains to newcomers. The rule set is light, the iconography is approachable, and the pace stays breezy even as a table grows more competitive. It’s not a game that demands a long preamble; it rewards curiosity and a willingness to lean into the storytelling aspects. If you’re the type who enjoys a quick, witty parable with a dash of melodrama, Gloom scratches that itch with abnormal effectiveness.

That said, the game does have a few caveats. For players who are extremely sensitive about themes of misfortune, death, or family turmoil in a lighthearted context, Gloom may feel a tad macabre. It’s all done with a wink, but if you’re playing with younger audiences or folks who prefer a gentler vibe, you’ll want to steer toward lighter themes or keep the table strictly PG. The humor is sharp but not mean-spirited; still, tone is important when you’re trying to create a shared story rather than a private roast.

## Variants, Expansions, and Longevity

As the baseline, Gloom offers a compact experience that scales nicely with new players. Its expansions add more families and more misfortune cards, which in turn increases the moral calculus of each decision. Expansions tend to provide more replayability by introducing unfamiliar family dynamics and new endgame conditions. The core trick remains the same: you want your own characters to be miserable, while ensuring your opponents’ families endure even greater misfortune before the final tally.

A note on replayability: the modular nature of the deck means you can tailor the experience to a party size or a preferred mood. If you want a tighter, more cutthroat experience, you can lean into aggressive misfortune cards; if you want a more storytelling-forward game, you can slow down the pace and let the humor cruise in as your family members wade into odd, cartoonish fates. The tone you choose will shape your experiences, but the core engine stays delightfully sound and flexible.

## Comparisons: Gloom in the World of Light-Hearted Card Games

If you enjoy lighter card games that encourage light storytelling, Gloom stacks up well against other house-rule friendly titles. Compared to something like Love Letter, Gloom offers more narrative control and a longer arc per round. It’s not as fast or as purely luck-driven as some micro-games, but it rewards strategic attachment and clever storytelling more than a simple draw and hope approach.

When placed alongside heavier storytelling games, Gloom remains approachable. It isn’t trying to replace the experience of an RPG session or a sprawling campaign; instead, it provides a compact, social experience that scratches a similar itch—shared humor, collaborative world-building, and a touch of the unnatural. It’s the kind of game you can pull out between larger games, and it will still get a room to laugh, groan, and cheer for a cleverly mailed misfortune.

## Value, Longevity, and Practical Takeaways

For the price, Gloom delivers a strong value proposition for casual and semi-serious game groups. It’s not a colossal investment, and the replayability is decent due to expansions and the storytelling element. If you’re somebody who loves the aesthetic of a Victorian parlor game but wants it with modern pacing and minimal setup, you’ll probably end up pleasantly surprised by how often you reach for this title.

In terms of longevity, the game holds up well. The core mechanic is simple enough to re-learn after long stretches away, and the expansions provide enough new content to keep veteran players engaged. The hidden misfortune trick remains entertaining, and the storytelling prompts keep returning to the table with new jokes and voices, which is exactly the kind of dynamic a good party game should offer.

## Final Verdict: Should You Gloom It Up?

If you’re looking for a game that blends storytelling with light strategy and teaches you how to ruthlessly and creatively ruin fictional lives, Gloom is a strong choice. It’s playful, quick to teach, and ready to adapt to your table’s vibe—whether you want a high-spirited chaos session or a more intimate, narrative-driven round. The art direction and the concept of transparent gloom overlays give the game a distinctive mood that sticks with you long after the last misfortune card is tucked away. It’s not the heaviest game on the shelf, but it has one of the sharpest moods in its category, and it proudly wears its punny, morbid heart on its sleeve.

If you have a group that enjoys collaborative storytelling with a side of competitive mischief, Gloom is almost mandatory. It’s the kind of game that invites you to be a character in a story you’re playing together, rather than simply a host of numbers on a page. And if you want to bring a little more theatre into your game nights, this is a fantastic anchor title to build around.
