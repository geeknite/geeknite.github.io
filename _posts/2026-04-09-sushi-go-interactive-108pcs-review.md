## How to Play in 10 Minutes or Less
Sushi Go! 108pcs follows a crisp, bite-sized loop that is both intuitive and insanely replay-friendly. Here’s the fast-forward version you can teach in less than a minute:

- Each player starts with a hand of cards (usually 7 in the standard base setup, but it scales with player count and box rules).
- On each turn, you pick one card from your hand and pass the remaining cards to the left. Everyone does this simultaneously in a pass cycle.
- After all players choose their card, you reveal the chosen card and add it to your personal Sushi Plate (your scoring area).
- Rounds continue until all cards are drafted. Then you score the plates according to the specific dish combinations and special rules.
- The player with the most points after all rounds is the winner.

What makes the pick-and-pass mechanic sparkle is that it creates a rhythm that rewards reading the table almost as much as reading your own hand. You can’t control everything, but you can control enough to set up your next move while preventing others from getting the exact card they need. It’s a little chess game but with more sizzle and fewer bishops.

In practice, a typical round might go like this: you spot that a left-neighbor is eyeing a high-value nigiri, which means you want to grab that dumpling or a miso soup to secure points without tipping the board in someone else’s favor. You weigh the risk that the card you want will be passed to someone who’s been watching your strategies all night. The joy and tension come from those micro-decisions, the table chatter about what to keep, what to pass, and whether to aim for a sushi-combo or niche point tokens like pudding that pay off in the long run.

To help you get started, here are three quick strategies that work at casual family tables and in more competitive playgroups:

- Strategy A: Build early sets on quick, high-value dishes (like nigiri and sashimi) before the board becomes packed with competition. This gives you a fast early lead and prevents opponents from stealing your most valuable combos.
- Strategy B: Diversify your plate. Avoid over-committing to one type of dish too soon; variety can pay off later when pudding tokens come into play for end-game bonuses.
- Strategy C: Pacing matters. In a 6- or 7-player game, you aren’t chasing every single point. It’s often smarter to secure a comfortable mid-range score and deny your neighbor a big swing than to chase an impossible last-round hero card.

These are starting points, not universal laws. The beauty of Sushi Go! 108pcs is that it rewards adaptation. Your table will throw curveballs—someone might unexpectedly pivot to a sushi-goal you hadn’t accounted for, and you’ll have to recalibrate on the fly.

## Strategy Tips for Maximum Mayhem (and Points)
A lot of the joy in Sushi Go! 108pcs comes from the micro-decisions you make on each hand. Here are some concrete tips to help you maximize your score without turning the game into a math seminar:

- Scan early, plan mid-game: On turn one, you don’t need to settle for a single choice. Look for two or three potential paths to score in the long run and hold a flexible plan as you pass cards around.
- Prioritize sets with high return: Nigiri and sashimi offer big points, but you need the right cards to complete sets. It’s often worth grabbing a high-value piece even if another low-valued card seems appealing.
- Don’t underestimate dumplings and tempura: These dishes can swing scores significantly in the right hands. If you can lock a few of them into your plate, you’ll often outrun players who chase flashier options.
- Watch the pudding for late-game bonuses: Puddings are often overlooked, but if the endgame scoring includes pudding bonuses, those late-game grabs can push you over the top when everyone’s head is turned toward the neon of Nigiri Town.
- Read the neighbors, not just your cards: In a multi-round game, the way others draft tells you a lot about what they might be chasing next. If someone is hoarding maki rolls, you may want to pivot to a different path and let them chase the same cards they’ll eventually regret missing.

Remember, these tips aren’t a guaranteed win; they’re about increasing your odds in the chaotic candy shop that is a family card game night. The real magic is observing human behavior around a table and adapting your strategy on the fly.
