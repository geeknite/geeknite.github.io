## How to Play at a Glance
Sushi Go! is known for its speed and its drafting mechanic. The Collectors Tin preserves the base gameplay, so if you’ve played Sushi Go! before, you’ll feel right at home. If you’re new, here’s a concise primer to get you going in about five minutes:

- Setup: Shuffle the deck, deal a hand to each player (usually 7 cards for 2–5 players). Place the remainder in a draw pile within easy reach.
- Turn structure: Each player selects one card from their hand to keep (face down), then they simultaneously reveal their chosen cards. All players pass their remaining cards to the left.
- Card effects and scoring: You draft to form sets and combos that score points at the end of each round. Common goals include collecting sets of Nigiri, accumulating Maki rolls for a temporary advantage, and balancing Wasabi boosts with Tempura/Sashimi pairings.
- End of round: When the deck is exhausted and all cards are scored, the round ends. A fresh hand is dealt, and the next round begins. After three rounds (typical for the base game), the player with the highest total wins.
- Endgame: If a player reaches a predetermined score threshold, you may decide to call the game early, but usually players finish three rounds to savor the drama of perfect Pudding trades and last-minute Wasabi steals.

In short: you will draft, you will smile at the cute art, and you will pleasantly forget about real-world responsibilities for approximately 15–20 minutes per game. ## Mechanics and Strategy Deep Dive
If you’ve played Sushi Go! before, you’ll notice the Collectors Tin doesn’t rewire the rules; it reframes the experience with a glossy shell. For newcomers, here’s a more granular breakdown of what makes the drafting system tick and how to leverage it for decent scores.

### Card Types and Scoring Motifs
- Nigiri: The simple but efficient core. Some Nigiri cards come with Wasabi, which multiplies the value if you sequence them correctly. The trick is to time your Wasabi when you can place a high-value Nigiri on it.
- Maki: The longer you can hold a lead on Maki, the better. It’s a race element that rewards forward-thinking and your ability to anticipate other players’ drafting patterns.
- Sashimi: This one’s a binary joke: either you collect enough to score full points or you don’t score at all. It’s the risk-reward dynamic of the game distilled into a few cards.
- Tempura: It rewards pairs, so you’re always thinking about whether you’ll be able to grab the second Tempura to seal the round’s points.
- Wasabi: It’s a multiplier tool. Play Wasabi with a high-valued Nigiri to maximize points, but don’t overcommit if you won’t be able to play the higher value card under Wasabi later.
- Pudding: A longer-term scoring element that often rewards players who survive through all rounds. It’s a buffer against the game’s shorter-term swinginess and encourages consistent drafting.

### Strategy Tips for New and Returning Players
- Track what’s been drafted: Pay attention to what your neighbors took and what seems to be missing. A good memory of previous rounds helps avoid overpaying for a card that will be immediately passed to you with a suboptimal value.
- Don’t overcommit to a single set: If you chase Maki too aggressively, you risk leaving a big gap in your end-game points. Balance is key: a few Maki stacks with consistent Nigiri returns usually beats a single big Maki lead with no other points.
- Plan for the endgame: If you’re trailing in Pud- ding points, you might opt for a safe late-round pick that nets you reliable last-round points instead of swinging for a high-risk card that might never get you the second half you need.
- Adapt to table dynamics: If you’re playing with a table of fast readers and skimmers, you’ll want to edge toward smaller, more flexible sets that allow you to keep pace with the passing sequence without getting stuck on a specific card.
