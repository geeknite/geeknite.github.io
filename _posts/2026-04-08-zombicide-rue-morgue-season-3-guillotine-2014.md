## Introduction

Zombicide Rue Morgue (Season 3), commonly nicknamed Guillotine by some fans for reasons you will understand by the end of this article, slides onto the table with the fanfare of a street performer and the existential dread of a late-night bus ride. If you’re here, you probably picked up a copy that is somewhere between “near complete” and “this box has seen more battle trauma than my first car.” We’re going to treat this as a loving scavenger hunt through cardboard, plastic, and questionable puzzle design decisions.

## What is Rue Morgue Season 3?

Rue Morgue is the Parisian nightmare CMON cooked up to torture friends into cooperating… and then blaming you when the finishing blow lands on your own survivor. In Season 3, the Guillotine module is the star attraction: a mechanically quirky, sometimes merciful, sometimes merciless set of rules that makes your group question whether you’d rather fight more zombies or the math behind the scoring. The core ideas stay familiar if you’ve played early Zombicide boxes: co-op play, puzzle-ish objectives, and a rotating cast of survivors who all think they’re the one who will carry the team to victory.

This box supposedly aims to be “near complete,” which is gamer code for: some pieces might be hiding under a misprint, and one injury card might be missing, but you can still play if you pretend your coffee is a prop weapon and your strategy is pure improvisation. The Guillotine module adds a new layer of risk management: you aren’t just routing zombies; you’re navigating a theater of events where a misdraw can send your squad tumbling into the abyss with dramatic flair.

## Components and Setup

### What’s in the box?
- Survivors: a rotating roster of unlikely heroes who somehow remember the last five minutes of their lives after being bitten.
- Zombies: a mix of walkers, fatties, and new special enemies—because if you’re going to run a horde, you might as well diversify the facial expressions on your opponent’s miniatures.
- Map tiles: a Paris-inspired grid that looks suspiciously like a temporary map from a high school theatre department.
- Guillotine module: a mechanical gimmick that adds a doom-swinging risk to the board. The aesthetic is 19th-century parsimonious villainy, and you’ll either love it or fear it.
- Cards, tokens, and dice: enough components to cover your dining table in cardboard confetti.

### Setup tips for the near complete box
1. Unpack with care, because you’ll want to avoid the cardboard shreds of shame that come with misfit components.
2. Sort cards into keeping piles (for mission objectives) and fear piles (for the Guillotine’s “surprise” events).
3. Assemble the map tiles following the included scenario sheet. If you’re color-blind, you’ll want a buddy to help decipher icons and arrows that look suspiciously like a modern art piece.
4. Place survivors on starting tiles. Pro-tip: coordinate your team to avoid the sorcery of “one bad dice roll” ruining the entire afternoon.

## How the Guillotine Module Changes the Core Rules

The Guillotine module is the lovechild of dramatic storytelling and “dice for breakfast.” It introduces new timing windows, a risk mechanic, and a few house rules that force players to talk out loud about their intentions while performing a last-minute card discard.

- Your survivors gain a token pool that can be spent to reroll dice or mitigate certain attacks. But every token spent raises the consequences when the Guillotine triggers.
- The fragility of a plan becomes a feature, not a bug. You will watch your carefully laid mission go sideways via a single misdraw, which is either hilariously unfair or a glorious demonstration of tabletop chaos.
- There are special event cards that reference the “guillotine” motif—where the blade doesn’t actually cut people, but it does cut your plan into tiny, snappy pieces.

### Why players tend to love and hate this module simultaneously
The Guillotine module embodies the essence of older CMON productions: it’s ambitious, a little overcomplicated, and deeply satisfying when it finally clicks. The moment you set up a chain reaction that accumulates into a winning turn, you’ll feel like you just pulled off a tiny miracle in a zombie apocalypse that requires more math than you remember from your high school days.

## Gameplay Experience: Punches, Zombs, and Puns

In this version, you’ll be juggling multiple goals: keep your survivors alive, complete mission objectives, and outpace the ever-advancing zombie tide. The Guillotine adds an extra clock to beat—the game isn’t just a race against undead; it’s a race against time, cards, and the blade’s theatrical menace.

I played this with a group of seasoned players who think “table talk” is a sport. We used the new tokens to reroll critical dice and found that the synergy between cooperative planning and wild randomness is where Zombicide shines: you get a few predictable turns, then a glorious, chaotic burst of luck that changes the tide.

The components feel sturdy, with thick cardboard and nicely sculpted miniatures. The 2014 production values still hold up decently; you’ll notice the plastic is not as glossy as the today, but that rough charm is part of the nostalgia. If you’re a completionist, you’ll enjoy hunting for the near-complete collector’s item vibe—missing tokens be damned, you can improvise the replacements with spare coins or chocolate chips; it’s the spirit of the game that counts.

## Strategy and Tactics: Getting the Most from a Near Complete Box
- Coordination is king: line up your survivors to maximize cover and minimize exposure to the horde. The Guillotine’s timing demands good timing from your team.
- Resource management: your tokens are scarce but powerful when spent at the right moment. Don’t be shy about spending one to save a fellow survivor … especially when the alternative is a dramatic “slice” of your plans.
- Objective prioritization: in some missions, killing zombies is a secondary concern; the real goal is to reach a target tile or reveal a hidden objective. The Guillotine module forces you to re-evaluate what counts as success.

### Balancing quirks and flaws
No box is perfect, especially a box that’s as old as the dinosaurs—or at least as old as the original iPhone. The near complete designation means you might find misprints, missing tokens, or flavor text that contradicts later rule clarifications. It’s part of the charm: you become a master of improvisational governance, patching holes in your own rules with a smile and a shave-and-a-haircut level of patience.

## Quality, Components, and the Box Itself

The physical quality of Rue Morgue Season 3 is a product of its era. The miniatures are solid, the boards hold up to repeated play, and the card stock has a satisfying heft. If you’ve ever opened a CMON box and felt a little guilty about the price, you’ll still appreciate the tactile joy of shuffling a deck of shiny cards and lining up a well-designed play area.

A few quibbles come with the territory of an “incomplete” or “near complete” packaging. Some of the larger tokens might be missing in a box that looks complete from the outside, and a couple of stickers may peel off if you over-experience the excitement of a napkin-dedicated gaming session. The key is: you can still assemble a terrific game experience with a pinch of ingenuity and a dash of gamer stubbornness. And if you’re into homebrew solutions, you can craft your own emergency replacements from bits of cardboard and the leftover tokens from similar Zombicide sets.

We’ve heard collectors debate whether this qualifies as a “collectible” or a “curated museum piece.” The answer is delightfully ambiguous: it depends on whether you want to memorize your misprints or simply marvel at how far a hobby can stretch a person’s sense of commitment.

## Design Philosophy: The 2014 Era and Its Aftertaste
The Rue Morgue box sits at an interesting crossroads: it embodies both the ambition of early CMON campaigns and the budget-conscious realities of mid-2010s manufacturing. If you adore the tactile heft of cardboard tokens and the drama of oversized die faces, you’ll likely forgive the occasional remnant misprint and copyright-friendly iconography. The Guillotine module is a wink to fans who enjoy the theatricality of doom: it’s not just about surviving the horde; it’s about surviving the moment when the theater curtain drops and you realize your strategy is now officially a tragedy in 28mm scale.

## House Rules and Variants
If you want to turbocharge your near complete box, here are a few house rules that can keep the table lively without breaking the game:
- Token Trading: allow players to trade a limited number of survivor tokens per round to alter outcomes (with a small penalty).
- Mixed Difficulty: introduce a “grimace” deck that introduces harsher events every third turn to simulate a higher-zombie density.
- Solo Play: swap survivors for a single “guide” with expanded abilities; treat the rest as AI-controlled zombies with a simplified command scheme.

## Solo Play Considerations
If you’re into solo table role-playing, Rue Morgue Season 3 can be surprisingly satisfying. You’ll be juggling objective decks, managing a handful of survivors, and choreographing the Guillotine’s triggers all at once. It’s not the most efficient solo experience, but it’s a glorious test of patience, planning, and the occasional napkin fix for missing tokens. If lone-wolf gaming is your thing, you might want to prototype a few “auto-resolve” rules so you aren’t forced into a dozen tiny micro-decisions every turn.

## Accessibility and Learning Curve
New players can be overwhelmed by the volume of cards and tokens. The Guillotine module adds a lot of terminology that can feel like a vocabulary test at first. My recommendation: play a few practice rounds with a veteran group, then gradually introduce the more complex objectives. The scenic theme helps — it’s hard to stay bored when the rules are being narrated with the gravitas of a courtroom drama starring zombies.

## Maintenance and Storage Tips for a Near Complete Box
- Keep a running inventory: list what’s present, what’s missing, and what you’re substituting. When you store the box, stack cards in protective sleeves to avoid edge wear.
- Use small labeled bags for tokens and extra dice. A little organization goes a long way toward reducing setup fatigue.
- If you’re missing a crucial token, improvise with generic cardboard cutouts. A little DIY can salvage a lot of fun without costing an arm and a leg.

## Quick Start Guide (One-Page-ish)
- Set up the map and place survivors.
- Shuffle the Guillotine event deck and draw the first three events.
- Each round: take a standard Zombicide turn, then resolve the Guillotine event (if its trigger condition is met).
- Complete mission objectives to win; if you fail, you’ll at least have a story about why everything went wrong in the most dramatic way possible.
- Track wounded survivors and resource spending; don’t forget to celebrate the moments of genius when everything goes right.

## The Final Verdict: Is It Worth It?
If you’re a Zombicide aficionado who likes a bit of mischief, the Rue Morgue Season 3 Guillotine edition offers a bountiful blend of chaos, strategy, and thematic bite. The “near complete” label is a gentle reminder that the real value lies in the memories you make around the table and the improvised fixes you conjure when a piece is missing. For newcomers, it’s a bit of a lottery: you may end up with a fantastic, fully realized experience, or you may find yourself stitching together a game from a handful of almost-right parts. Either way, you’ll get a memorable session that sticks with you in the same way a well-placed zombie silhouette on a box lid sticks with you after a long drive home.

### Final Recommendation Summary
- Fun factor: high, especially during chaotic Guillotine-triggered turns.
- Replayability: excellent with multiple survivor rosters and mission setups.
- Thematic flavor: strong, with a historical gothic vibe that pairs surprisingly well with zombie mayhem.
- Value: decent for a 2014 release if you love the aesthetic and are willing to improvise missing pieces.

If you want a serendipitous mix of strategic planning and tabletop theatre, Rue Morgue Season 3 Guillotine is worth your table time. If you crave a pristine, issue-free box with no frayed edges, you might want to look for a more recent reprint or a complete used copy.
