## Quality control: how to assess what you got
When your 2014 replacement lot arrives, a little QA goes a long way. Here are practical steps that have served Geeknite reviewers well. Remember, this is about getting your deck playable and visually cohesive, not about scoring scores of fashion points for the card stock alone.

### Inspecting print consistency
- Compare the sashimi cards against one or two known good originals if you have any. Note color consistency, symbol clarity, and font legibility.
- Check the backs for uniformity. Backs should be consistent in tone and pattern. A bright mismatch can be charming in a quirky way, but it might also hint at unlicensed production.

### Checking dimensions and weight
- Most Sushi Go cards are standard poker-sized. If your batch feels noticeably smaller or larger, that’s a red flag. Hold up a sleeve to confirm comfortable handling.
- If you sleeve cards, confirm that they still fit in your sleeves without binding. A slightly thicker stack can cause occasional shuffling hiccups, but not necessarily game-ending faults.

### Condition grading and return policy
- Expect a mix of near-mint to good condition cards. Some edges may show minor wear, which is entirely plausible after years in circulation. If you’re chasing pristine, this may not be the right purchase.
- Before you commit, check if the seller offers a return window or a damage-based refund. A used market item is a risk, and you deserve the option to swap or bail if the batch doesn’t meet expectations.

## Care, sleeves, and long-term upkeep
A replacement card lot is only as good as its longevity. If you’re invested in keeping your Sushi Go ensemble intact for years, you need a simple care routine.

### Sleeve strategy
- Sleeve all replacement cards to prevent further edge wear and to keep color fidelity consistent. If you sleeve everything, you’ll also reduce the risk of scuffing during fidgety player hands.
- Choose sleeves with a snug fit that still allows easy shuffling. A tight fit can be a nuisance during quick rounds; a loose fit invites bending and curling. Balance is key.

### Handling and storage
- Store your deck in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight. Fading is real, and sashimi greens like to go on a mini vacation when exposed to high heat.
- Use a deck box or a sturdy binder with clear sleeves to protect the entire set. Treat this like a little museum case for your culinary diplomacy cards.

### Cleaning practice
- Gentle bump-free cleaning is enough. Wipe with a soft microfiber if needed and avoid harsh solvents that can erase print details. There’s a delicate balance between preserving the look and protecting the surface.
