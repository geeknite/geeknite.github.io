Cables, connectors, and modularity
-----------------------------------
If you’re eyeing this PSU for a modern mid-range build, you’ll be glad to know the S12III-620W provides a solid mix of a few essential cables without confusion:

- 1× 24-pin ATX
- 1× 4+4 CPU power connector
- 2× PCIe 6+2 (older non-modular units often arrive with 2 connectors; check your GPU needs before committing)

Because it isn’t modular, cable management is a tad more “traditional PC build” than “space-age cable choreography.” You’ll route tubes and tubes of cable behind the motherboard tray and tuck the rest in the PSU shroud if you’ve got one. For most mid-range builds, the stock cable length and arrangement are perfectly adequate, especially if you’re not rocking massive GPUs or a mega reservoir of SSDs. If you’re chasing a ultra-clean look, you might want to budget a sleeve kit or a case with generous cable combs, but the S12III’s non-modular nature should not be seen as a deal-breaker in this segment.

Energy efficiency and electrical performance
---------------------------------------------
This is where the S12III-620W tries to do the boring-but-important thing really well: be reliable while meeting modest efficiency standards. The official spec classifies it as 80 PLUS Bronze, which places it in the realm of typical efficiency you’d expect from a budget unit. In practical terms, that means you’re looking at decent efficiency around typical home voltages with a high likelihood of hitting low to mid-80s under real-world loads. It’s not pretending to be a high-efficiency, peak-60-amp runner; it’s a workhorse for a mid-range gaming PC or a compact workstation.

In our lab, we measure efficiency across a few loads: 20%, 50%, and 100% of rated output. The scorecards show the device performing within Bronze-class expectations, with ripple and voltage regulation within safe margins. The +12V rail is the workhorse here—stable enough for a modern CPU and GPU pairing that doesn’t demand extreme headroom. If you’re stacking performance with overclocking fantasies, you’ll want something with more headroom and tighter regulation, but for a standard build, the S12III-620W is comfortable.

Noise, cooling, and acoustics
------------------------------
The 120 mm fan isn’t silent, but it isn’t loud enough to become the star of your build’s soundscape either. In idle and light gaming scenarios, you’ll likely enjoy quiet operation that blends into the background. Under heavier load or while pushing a power-hungry GPU, expect the fan to spin up, but the acoustic profile remains more tolerable than some budget units that sound suspiciously like a hairdryer auditioning for a metal band.

The cooling solution is straightforward: a conventional fan, a well-spaced internal layout, and no extreme ripple on heat. The result is a PSU that stays relatively cool under normal workloads, with heat being effectively managed by the chassis. If you’re building a compact or hot environment PC, you’ll still want to keep the intake and exhaust paths clear, because the last thing you want is thermal throttling or unexpected reboots due to thermal issues.
