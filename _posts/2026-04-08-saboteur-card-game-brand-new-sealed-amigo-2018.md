### What Saboteur is, in a few lines
Saboteur is a social deduction game wrapped in a dwarven mining theme. Players take turns placing path cards to build a tunnel toward a treasure card, all while playing hazard and sabotage cards to disrupt others. Some players are trying to help the team reach the treasure, while others secretly work to prevent the treasure from ever being claimed, either by stalling, undermining, or outright wrecking the tunnel. The twist? Only the players know who’s who—until the end, when the roles are revealed and glory (or humiliation) is doled out accordingly.

This 2018 edition sticks to that core but polishes some of the rough edges that early prints had. The layout is clearer, the iconography more intuitive, and the balance between construction and sabotage generally tightened. If you’re joining a friend’s board-game night and want something with easy rules and big personality, Saboteur does a surprisingly good job at delivering both.

### How to set up and start playing
Saboteur is known for its simplicity in setup and execution. Here’s a rough blueprint of how you’ll get from box to table:
- Shuffle the treasure and path cards separately. Shuffle the role cards and deal them out to players according to the player count.
- Each player receives a role card face-down; you’re keeping a secret whether you’re a miner or a saboteur.
- The first player lays down a path card or draws a card to begin constructing the tunnel toward the treasure. The lead is not strictly linear; players can contribute to any part of the tunnel, which is where the chaos begins.
- On your turn, you can place a path card, or play an action card to disrupt someone else’s tunnel progress, or to protect your own path. The goal is to reach the treasure card at the end of a tunnel before the saboteurs derail everything.
- The round ends when a treasure card is revealed at the end of a path, and scoring happens. Then, depending on who’s your ally and who’s your saboteur, points are awarded.

The 2018 edition’s rules are simple enough to teach in about 5 minutes. The challenge is not in understanding the rules; it’s in managing the social dynamics that emerge when everyone is trying to figure out who’s the honest miner and who’s the shady saboteur. If you’ve played party games like Werewolf or Secret Hitler, you’ll recognize the thrill of deduction without the political baggage. Saboteur’s gentler theme—miners digging for gold—keeps things light, even when someone’s railroading the tunnel under your nose.

### Crafting a better-than-average night: tips and tricks
- Use the “read the room” rule: a successful round hinges less on the exact path cards you draw and more on how well you read the table’s vibe. Note who’s claiming to know the “best path” and who’s quietly shuffling their feet with a suspicious shrug.
