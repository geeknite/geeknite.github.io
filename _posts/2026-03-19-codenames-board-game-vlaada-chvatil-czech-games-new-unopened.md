## What is Codenames?
Codenames is a duel of wits in a 5x5 grid of words where two teams race to uncover their agents before the other team finishes. The twist is in the clue: the Spymaster gives a single-word clue that relates to multiple words on the grid, plus a number that indicates how many of those words are tied to the clue. The catch is that there are also bystanders and a lone assassin on the board; guess wrong, and you can hand the advantage to your opponents—or you could even trigger a devastating chain of events by hitting the assassin. It’s a game about language, perception, risk management, and a dash of theatrical flair as players lean in, whisper, or bellow a clue that may or may not make sense to the person across the table.

Codenames is a game that looks simple on the surface, but the real challenge emerges in the negotiation of meaning. The Spymaster must choose a clue that connects several words without steering the team toward the assassin or bystander words. The Field Operatives then interpret that clue, deciding which cards to flip, which requires a blend of pattern recognition, cultural literacy, and a willingness to take a risk on a word that could either be a slam dunk or a spectacular misread. It is, in short, a linguistic game that invites both cleverness and chaos in equal measure.

## How to Play (In Sane, Playful Quickness)
Codenames is deceptively simple to learn, but it rewards players who lean into the wordplay rather than muscle memory. The game moves in turns, with two primary roles: the Spymaster and the Field Operatives. The Spymaster gives a one-word clue that connects multiple words on the table. The clue must be a single word and must be a word that can reasonably relate to the target words on the grid. The Spymaster also includes a number that indicates how many words on the grid are connected to the clue.

### Spymaster Turn
- The Spymaster studies the grid and the key card, then chooses a one-word clue with a number. The trick is nuance: a good clue may connect two or three or even four words on the grid, but every extra word you connect increases the risk of your team guessing an enemy’s word, a bystander, or—worst-case—the assassin.
- The Spymaster speaks the clue and the number aloud. Example: “Ocean, 2.” The field operatives must then work as a unit to identify two words on the grid related to oceans.

### Field Operatives Turn
- The chosen team must deliberate and pick words one by one, in order, hoping to match the clue. They can discuss freely, banter, and propose hypotheses about which word connects to the clue—and why. This is where the game becomes a social experiment: the best clues often emerge from shared experiences and inside jokes that somehow align with the word list.
- If a Field Operative guesses a word that belongs to their team (a red agent or blue agent, depending on which team they are on), that word is covered with the corresponding color and play continues.
- If they guess a bystander word, a penalty happens: you turn that word over as a bystander and the turn ends immediately. If they hit the assassin, the game ends immediately with a loss for the guessing team.
- The turn ends either when the team can no longer justify guessing more words (perhaps the mental map of clues has begun to double back on itself) or they decide to stop early to preserve momentum. The team that completes all of its words on the grid first wins the round.

### Strategy Snacks (For the Serious but Friendly Nerd Inside You)
- Start with a safe, inclusive clue: try to connect as many words as possible without snagging an enemy or assassin. This often means prioritizing more common, general terms rather than hyper-specific references unless you’re confident.
- Think cross-linguistically and cross-culturally. The words chosen on the grid can map to a surprising variety of cultural contexts. A clue that seems obvious to you might be cryptic to someone else, and that’s part of what makes the game entertaining.
- Use thematic clues sparingly if your group has a strong sense of group inside jokes. The joke can be powerful, but it can also derail the team’s ability to generalize a clue to other players.
- Protect the assassin: if you’re the Spymaster, you’ll often want to avoid clues that would easily converge on the assassin unless you’re the risk-taking type. If you’re the Field Operatives, you’ll want to talk yourselves through the possibility that the clue could lead to the assassin, and decide whether the risk is acceptable.
- Practice improves pattern recognition. The more you play, the better you’ll get at spotting the implied connections in a single word. And the more you play with a group, the more you’ll discover the memorable phrases that become your team’s signature cues.
