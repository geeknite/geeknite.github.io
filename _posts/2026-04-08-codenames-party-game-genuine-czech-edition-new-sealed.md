## How to play: a quick refresher for the new sealed edition

Codenames, at its core, is a team-based word association game. One player from each team acts as the spymaster, trying to guide their teammates toward their team’s cards on the grid without landing on the opposing team’s agents or the assassin. The rest of the players become guessers. The spymaster gives a one-word clue that relates to multiple codename words on the grid, followed by a number indicating how many cards on the grid relate to that clue. Guessers then decide which word cards to reveal. The goal: identify all of your team’s agents first. Simple in idea, chaotic in execution, especially when a perfectly good clue could snowball into a cascade of guesses.

The Genuine Czech Games Edition keeps the pace brisk by offering clear typography and a grid that’s easy to scan, even when the room’s energy is high and someone’s attempted to start a spontaneous karaoke session between rounds. Setup is fast: lay out the 25 codename cards in a 5x5 square, place the red/blue agent cards for both teams somewhere accessible, choose a spymaster per team, and you’re off to the first clue.

A typical round unfolds like this:
- The spymaster studies the key card that aligns the grid to their team colors.
- They select a one-word clue and a number, such as “Galaxy 3.” The number tells the team how many cards are tied to that clue.
- Guessers discuss, debate, and point at the words they think match the clue. They can stop early if they feel confident, but guessing more cards can backfire if you hit the assassin or an opponent’s card.
- Once a card is revealed, the spymaster confirms whether it’s theirs, theirs, neutral, or assassin. If they hit an opponent’s card, the round continues but those cards are now under the other team’s control. If they hit the assassin, the round ends immediately and that team loses.
- The round ends when all of one team’s agents are revealed, or the assassin ends the round prematurely. Then a new round begins with the other team as the spymaster.

The game’s charm comes from the tension of crafting clues that are broad enough to connect multiple words but specific enough to avoid the other side’s domain. The new sealed edition feels particularly satisfying because you’re not worrying about condition issues with the components; the box is fresh, the cards lay flat, and the words are crisp enough to avoid eye-rolling during tense moments.

## Game-night dynamics: tips and tricks for this edition

- Start with a quick read of the rulebook before your first round. It’s short and designed for rapid onboarding, which is perfect for a party scenario where people show up with different levels of board game literacy.
- Decline the first two clues if you’re new to the game. The first round is a feeler round—let players learn what a good clue feels like without going overboard.
- Use a timekeeping cue if your group needs it. The game isn’t inherently time-bound, but a light timer can keep momentum going when your group transitions from “we’re just chatting” to “we’re trying to win.”
- When in doubt, use clues that are broadly associated with multiple words, but stay away from ambiguous terms that could link to the other team. The more you play, the better you’ll be at judging risk-reward.
- Mix up the spymaster role so more players get to experience guiding and guessing. It’s fun to see different people’s thinking styles in action, and it’s also a great way to keep the energy up on longer game nights.
