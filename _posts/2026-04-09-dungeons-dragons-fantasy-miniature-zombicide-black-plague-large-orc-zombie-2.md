## The Sculpt: Design and Detail
Let’s talk about the crunch: the design. The Large Orc Zombie #2 is, frankly, a show-off. The sculpt gives you a squat, hulking frame with plates of battered armor clinking like a chainmail percussion solo in a bad fantasy orchestra. The head is a helmeted slouch, jaw slightly ajar as if it’s muttering a curse to a wayward adventurer, and the spear/polearm option (depending on release) is presented with a weight that suggests this isn’t a simple decoration for your shelf; this thing wants to reach out and, you know, crit you in the next round.

Texture work is where this mini shines. You’ll notice dented pauldrons, torn leather straps, cracks in the shield, and a riotous amount of bone peeking through the armor in tasteful places. It’s the kind of model that benefits from a thin wash to bring out the crevices, followed by a dry brush to highlight edges on the armor. The result is a miniature that looks like it has survived a dozen skirmishes and is one bad RNG away from giving up the ghost… again.

Scale-wise, this is where you’ll likely compare to your familiar D&D minis. Zombicide uses a standard “tabletop height” that often sits just a hair larger than a typical 28-30mm D&D figure. The Orc Zombie sits in that sweet spot: imposing enough to be a boss in a dungeon, yet not so colossal that it looks like it wandered in from a space opera. If you use it in a party of standard PCs, expect some extra side-eye from your players as they parse the minis on the map and ask, “Is that a dragon or a very beefy orc who learned a curse-word in orcish and picked up a sword?”

For hobbyists who love conversion opportunities, this model is gold. The base could be swapped for a ruined cobblestone effect, the armor static could be enhanced with metallics and rust effects, and the undead flesh can be tuned with a greenish corpse wash to emphasize the rot without making the figure look like a rotten Christmas hamper. It’s a perfect candidate for a quick two to three-layer paint scheme with a wash, a dry brush on raised details, and a final gloss or matte seal to preserve the undead sheen for tabletop use.

## Painting and Hobby Notes
If you’re thinking of painting this bad boy, you’re in for a relaxing evening and possibly a second coffee. The sculpt’s texture provides natural contrast for your first layers: a darker undercoat, a mid-tone for leather and armor, and a highlight to bring out the edges. The bones and exposed joints respond beautifully to a bone-color wash, while the armor can be treated with metallics and a corrosion wash to suggest ancient, battle-worn steel.

Pro tip: a thin glaze of green or blue over the exposed flesh helps sell the undead vibe without turning him into a swamp monster. For a more cinematic approach, apply a subtle purple glaze to the shadows in the armor crevices to give it that otherworldly mojo that makes undead enemies so memorable.

If you’re painting for D&D compatibility, you’ll want to pick a color scheme that reads clearly on the gaming table. High-contrast colors for the armor and a muted, off-white skin tone will ensure the figure remains legible from a tabletop distance. You’ll also want to think about the “action pose” in your mind’s eye: if it’s a charging zombie, consider dynamic limb placement and a slightly angled torso to imply movement. If it’s a guard in a ruined city, a static stance with a heavy, planted foot will communicate weight and menace.
