[h2] How to Grade and Evaluate a Mint Copy [/h2]

Grading a Japanese Carddas card is a bit different from Western card grading, but the core principles hold: corners, edges, surface, and color fidelity. Here’s a practical quick-start guide to evaluating No. 135:

- Corners: Look for crisp, sharp corners with minimal chipping. Any visible fraying is a red flag for mint status.
- Edges: Inspect for whitening if you tilt the card gently. A clean edge with consistent gloss suggests better condition.
- Surface: Check for scratches, scuffs, or print defects that pop under light. High-gloss finishes can hide minor marks, so use a bright light and tilt the card to catch any imperfections.
- Centering: A well-centered card looks balanced visually; misalignment affects the overall aesthetic and can influence value in fragile markets.
- Print batch hints: Some collectors prefer earlier print runs for their slightly different hues or slight shifts in color saturation. If you’re chasing a precise batch, tracking the print year and batch code matters.

If you’re buying online, request high-resolution scans. If you’re buying in person, a simple cheek-to-cheek flick under a strong light may reveal whitening and edge wear you’d otherwise miss.
