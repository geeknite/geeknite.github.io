## Overview

Season 2 leans into bigger, bolder, and arguably squishier undead. The sculpt team apparently attended a after-hours session with a zombie whisperer, because the variants feel more distinct and a touch spookier than Season 1. The box promises a blend of classic walkers, runners, and a handful of special variants that mix up the challenge without turning the core game into a full-blown RPG. The goal here is simple yet devious: give you more variety without making you feel like you’ve allergic-reacted to plastic glue.

## The Minis: Design and Sculpt Quality

### Aesthetic virtues and the sculptors' notes
Zombicide Season 2 pulls off a surprising trick: the minis actually look like they belong on a modern tabletop, not a dusty shelf in a museum of failed paint jobs. Sculpts are chonkier in some areas, with more pronounced facial features and ragged clothing that tells a story without needing a legend card. The variety feels intentional rather than slapdash; you can identify a jogger zombie, a chef zombie, and a zombie in a lab coat without consulting the rulebook every five minutes. The detailing is crisp, though not hyper-fine, which is a relief for hobbyists who fear their paintbrush might rebel at the sight of a fine line.

### Scale and base ergonomics
The scale is consistent with Season 1, which means you can mix and match without subway-turf confusion. The bases are standardized; they click into place with a satisfying snap that tells you the model has accepted your project as their new reality. If you have a preference for basing with texture or scenic elements, the Season 2 plastics are forgiving enough to support it without a heroic amount of glue and sand. If you’re a purist who wants to magnetize everything, you’ll find the metal-friendly magnets still work, but the minis resurface quickly enough that you’ll want to repaint them anyway.

### Painting prospects
Paint schemes go from “zombie apocalypse drab” to “zombie couture” with surprising ease. The textures are accessible for beginners yet offer enough depth for seasoned painters to show off shading, dry brushing, and a few subtle highlights. A warning: some variants have slightly more sculpted details in the face and hands, which means you’ll want a steady hand or a very patient heart. If you’re collecting for display rather than gameplay, Season 2 provides a delightful canvas for gradient skin tones and decayed fabrics. If you’re pinning your hopes on a single color to rule them all, you’ll find that the gray-green palette reads well under table lights and not-so-well under fluorescent mall lighting—where a lot of us do most of our painting.

## Assembly and Basework: Practicalities

### Glue and go or pin and plan?
Season 2 minis are largely snap-fit, with occasional tiny connective points that prefer a dab of glue for longevity. If you’ve ever glued a miniature to a base and watched it wobble like a wobble-wobble duck, you’ll enjoy the improved stability in these figures. They don’t require a full conversion process unless you’re attempting a diorama that would put a diorama to shame. For players who enjoy customizing basing, this season provides enough surface area for basing kits, basing compounds, and a few dead things to create a story on the tabletop.

### Assembly time estimates
If your table time is precious, you’ll be pleased to learn that Season 2 minis are relatively quick to assemble compared to some obsessively posed kits from other franchises. You’ll still want to dry-fit everything, especially with the variants that feature extra limbs or unique torsos. The good news is that even your average hobbyist with a coffee-stained glue bottle can assemble a reasonable squad without needing a mechanical engineer on hand.

### Storage considerations
Box inserts are decently sized, and the mini trays nestle in nicely. If you’ve got a small army, you’ll want to plan for a couple of extra organizers to keep track of the different zombie variants. The Season 2 set doesn’t create a new storage headache unless you start collecting multiple expansions and paint sets. If you’re filing away a whole shelf of Zombicide, consider labeling the trays by faction type to save time during setup—the last thing you want is a sudden cascade of walkers tumbling toward you like a poorly managed DIY project.

## Painting Tips and Hobby Notes

### A quick palette guide
- Flesh tones with a touch of gray and green wash for realism.
- Clothes in muted, earthy tones; you’ll want a few brighter accents to distinguish factions on the table.
- Blood and weathering: dry brushing with a bit of rust and a dark red washes brings life to tattered fabrics.

### Techniques for best results
- Basecoat with a mid-tone, then shade in cracks with a darker shade to emphasize the texture.
- Dry brush lightly on fur or ragged edges to create the illusion of wear without overloading the model with paint.
- Use a fine-tipped brush for teeth and eyes; they’re tiny but deliver big character.

### Nail your basing for table presence
Consider basing with dirt, sand, or tiny rubble bits to simulate a ruined environment. A little basing grit goes a long way toward selling the postapocalyptic vibe without requiring a full diorama kit. And yes, moss on a zombie is a thing you can try, but maybe save it for a Sunday afternoon when you’re feeling unusually bold.
