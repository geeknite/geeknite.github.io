# The Game: The 2017 Edition - Expansion Pack Review

![Expansion Art]( /assets/images/the-game-2017-expansion.jpg )

If you’ve spent a Friday night staring at a deck of cards like it’s a treasure map to a long-lost level of sanity, you’re probably one of those people who owns a stubborn copy of The Game. Yes, the one where your goal is to discard every card in ascending or descending order without ever being the person who busts the table by misplacing a card with the grace of a caffeinated hedgehog. Now imagine a mysterious parcel arrives. It’s the 2017 Edition Expansion Pack, a box that promises more chaos, more cooperation, and more existential panic than a group chat when someone says, “Let’s do a quick game night.” Spoiler: it delivers. In a good way. Mostly.

This is not a review for newcomers who hear “expansion” and think it’s a fancy way to say “we added more cards so you can fail harder.” This is a love letter to the obsessive, the braggy, and the stubborn: the people who treat a perfectly good cooperative puzzle like a high-stakes heist where the getaway car is a standard playing card. If you’re reading this, you’ve likely dragged your friends to the brink of ridiculousness at least once. The Expansion Pack is here to give you more excuses to squint at the table and proclaim: we did this together—and somehow it worked (sometimes).

But enough preamble. Let’s dive deeper into the 2017 Edition and decide whether this is a must-own for your shelf or a slightly tilted elevator pitch that one day you'll pretend you never bought.

## What’s in the Box and How It Plays With The Game

First things first: the Expansion Pack is portable—like a small, aggressive safe that you can slip into your backpack without triggering airport security. Inside you’ll find a new deck of cards—think of them as the “bad vibes” cousins—plus a handful of player aids, a couple of rule clarifications, and a few quirky tokens that feel almost magical when you realize they’re just cardboard with fancy printing. The art direction remains on-brand: minimalistic, a touch ominous, and loaded with tiny icons that look innocent until you realize they’re batteries for your brain.

The core mechanic of The Game remains intact: players collaborate (read: attempt to save their collective sanity) by discarding cards in a prescribed order before a clock of doom—okay, a clock of doom is a hyperbolic metaphor; it’s really just a timer or a deck of burn cards, but I like drama. The Expansion introduces three major additions that change the rhythm without uprooting the fundamental tension:

- New “Chaos Cards” that up the stakes, change the discard order, or temporarily invert the rules in deliciously cruel fashion.
- A handful of “Event Cards” that trigger at inopportune moments, forcing you to adjust plans on the fly.
- Optional rules that scale up from casual co-op to “we’re on a professional board game support group now.”

Let’s break these down, because the joy of The Game is in the details, and the Expansion Pack doesn’t waste a single one.

### The Chaos Cards: Embrace the Randomness (or Fight It)

Chaos Cards are the heart of the expansion’s personality overhaul. Each card is a tiny nudge toward chaos, and not the charming, wink-wink chaos you can control with a spreadsheet. These cards can:

- Adjust the discard order by forcing players to pick a new pile order mid-round.
- Clone a specific condition (e.g., “two cards in the same color must be played consecutively”) that makes you curse the color wheel.
- Impose dual discard turns for one player, doubling the stress while granting that player a window of questionable triumph when they nail a tough sequence.

The beauty is that these aren’t random in the sense of “rolling a dice and praying.” They are thematic, consistent, and most importantly, they’re “soft” enough to be reversible with teamwork—but only if you all decide to work together instead of pretending you’re in a spy movie and silently sabotaging each other. If you’ve ever role-played a heist in a coffee shop, this will feel like easing into a familiar rhythm—except rather than a smooth, well-synchronised plan, you’re all improvising a jazz solo with your cards.

### Event Cards: The Heartbeat of the Session

Event Cards are the adrenaline bursts in the engine. They can be triggered at the start of a round, when a certain condition is met, or when a player makes a heroic misstep. Events bring flavor and consequence: you might lose a few cards from the deck, redraw from a secondary stack, or impose a penalty that requires you to cooperate in ways that make your group chat crash with laughter and despair simultaneously.

Some events test memory (Remember the rule we decided was optional? It’s now mandatory for one round), some test luck (draw from the “worse-case scenario” card pile, which is exactly as comforting as it sounds), and some simply punish one “lucky star” player who refused to stretch their brain past its comfort zone. The effect is a rollercoaster that’s mostly about cohesion: you’ll either become a better team or you’ll mutter about the designer’s cousin who clearly never had to deal with family night chaos.

### Player Aids and a Little Bit of Psychology

TheExpansion adds new player aids—compact cheat sheets and little tokens that let you (and your seriously judgmental friends) track what the current discard color rules are, what the global penalty is, and how many Chaos Cards are currently in circulation. The best feature here is that they’re small but significant: you can glance at a card, glance at your teammates, and decide whether to go for broke or to play safe—an option that doesn’t exist in the base game unless your risk assessment involves a lucky lottery ticket and a lot of caffeine.

These aids feel like the designer looked at you, the over-enthusiastic table captain, and said: “Yes, you’ll narrate this. Yes, we’ll give you tools for the narration.” It’s a win for cooperative storytelling that doesn’t force you into the role of a lecturer at a board game club—though some players will still stand up and explain the correct sequence with the dramatic flourish of a TED Talk on minimalism.

## Modes: Solo, Duo, and a Couple of Quirky Variants

The Expansion Pack shines when you start mixing modes. It’s designed to be friendly to loyal players who want an extra challenge and to newcomers who want to taste the “hard mode” without diving into a sea of doom. Here are the modes you’ll probably actually use:

- Solo Mode: The most adoringly cruel way to play The Game. You control multiple “colors” of cards and must juggle them across different discard piles. It’s basically a puzzle with your own brain as both the puzzle and the antagonist. It’s perfect for when you want to test your mental endurance without dragging a friend who enjoys sorrow into the process.
- Co-op Advanced: A stricter version of the original, where the rules tighten on every round. It rewards thorough planning and punishes last-minute improvisation—a rare condition in any tabletop environment outside of a family group chat during a power outage.
- Casual Co-op with House Rules: The expansion doesn’t force you into “grimacing intensity.” There’s a friendly, optional set of house rules that allows players to soften the blow a bit, pretending it’s a team-building exercise rather than a test that could end friendships. If you’re hosting a mixed crowd (gamers and non-gamers), this mode is your lifeline.

If you’re a regular at Geeknite-style gatherings, you’ll appreciate how these modes let you calibrate the room’s energy without resorting to distractions like a screensaver with a blender sound effect. You can casually dial up difficulty when the crowd is tired of laughing at their mistakes, or dial it down when a new player joins and the group looks at you as if you’ve invented a sport that abuses cutlery.

## Theme, Artwork, and the Vibe You’d Expect

The 2017 Edition is careful not to pretend it’s a totally different game. It’s more like a stylish extension that knows what fans want: more tension, more unpredictability, and a sense that you truly have entering a team sport where the scoreboard is a pile of discarded cards and the prize is not-quite-senility-diluting satisfaction.

Artwork remains minimalist and clean, with the color palette leaning toward moody teals and grays. It’s not going to win any art awards by itself, but that’s not the point: the art is legible, the icons are intuitive, and the design communicates the mood without shouting. The quality of the cards themselves is noticeably better than the base game, which is essential when you’re shoving a lot of them back and forth in tense, judgment-filled moments.

If you’re the type who enjoys a thematic tie-in, you’ll appreciate the little flourishes: the events feel like mini-narratives, and the Chaos Cards each come with a one-line flavor description that makes you imagine the game’s world as if it existed in a coffee shop where two baristas are on a tiny crusade against the orderliness of life. It’s the kind of humor that lands when you’re already deep in the weeds of a long session.

## A Walkthrough: What a Session Feels Like With the Expansion

To give you a tangible sense, here’s a run-through of a typical session with the Expansion Pack plugged in. Imagine you’re with three friends who all believe they’re secretly a game designer in the basement of a sci-fi bookstore.

- Round 1: The table is optimistic. Someone shuffles the chaos part of the deck and announces, with brave confidence, that “we’ll be fine.” The first Chaos Card lands: “Reverse Red and Blue piles.” Suddenly everyone is refolding their mental map. The Event Card is “One player draws two extra cards.” This is where you learn if your team has synergy or if you’re going to witness a spectacular table-slide into chaos.
- Round 2: The Event Card makes someone lose a turn. The team pivots, reassigning roles. A different person misreads a symbol and reminds others that you’re not playing a rhythm game; you’re playing a strategy puzzle with timer anxiety.
- Round 3: The Chaos Cards escalate. A “mirror opposite” card swaps your color rules, causing a hilarious moment where two players are arguing about whether the color blue should be played on the red pile. You settle it with a quick, gentle consensus: the color wheel doesn’t care about your feelings; it only cares about your ability to cooperate.
- Finale: You crash across the finish line, not with a triumphant cheer but with a tired, grateful exhale. The deck’s last few cards land in a way that makes you feel like you’ve earned a tiny victory over chaos itself. You laugh. You almost forget that you started this night with the intention of “just a quick game.” The room now feels like a small victory party for human adaptability and the inexplicable human talent for collective problem-solving, even when the problem is shuffling a deck of 60 cards without breaking a sweat.

That cadence—the mix of tension, laughter, and false bravado—feels tailor-made for a group that’s been playing together long enough to know better but still stubborn enough to want one more round.

## Strategy Notes: How to Use the Expansion Without Turning Your Living Room Into a War Zone

- Communicate early and often. The expansion rewards lines of dialogue that don’t rely on a silent telepathy you pretend you have with your friends.
- Assign a “focus captain.” This is the player who tracks the current rule changes and yells “new round!” when necessary. It’s like a tiny, benevolent referee who’s not afraid to remind you to use your brain.
- Embrace the chaos—but not at the cost of the whole session. If a Chaos Card starts to derail you, pivot to a simpler strategy for one round and rebuild your confidence.
- Remember: you’re playing as a team. Even when someone makes a heartbreaking blunder, your win condition is the group’s success, not the glory of one star player who insists on heroic sacrifice as a life philosophy.

If you follow these guidelines, the Expansion Pack becomes less a weapon of mass confusion and more a curated stress test that yields a surprising amount of satisfaction when you finally “beat the system” together. And yes, you’ll probably talk about this night for years—the way you might reminisce about a very silly, slightly dangerous, but ultimately meaningful road trip you took with friends who still remember the map you drew on a napkin.

## Where It Hits or Misses: The Honest Take

Like any expansion, this one isn’t universally adored. A few caveats worth noting:

- It adds complexity. If you’re new to The Game, the base rules are still a lot to absorb. The Expansion compounds that. It’s a great second purchase, not a first timer’s best friend.
- It can eclipse the base game’s simplicity. If you’re in the mood for quick, breezy sessions, you may find the expansion too dense. That’s not a flaw; it’s a design choice. The expansion asks you to bring your team cap and your best “we’re going to solve this” attitude.
- Not all groups will want to chase the chaos all night. Some players want a relaxed vibe; the Expansion can be a little relentless in the right hands. In those moments, revert to the House Rules for a gentler tempo.

On the flip side, if you’re a fan of the base game and you’ve spent enough nights learning each other’s tells—the nonverbal cues that indicate “we’re about to fail spectacularly”—then you’ll likely find the Expansion a worthy purchase. It adds both variety and narrative momentum, letting your group feel like a crew of slightly overconfident puzzle-solvers who somehow manage to save the day, in a very loud, very caffeinated way.

## Quick Post-Play Sign Off: Who Should Buy This Expansion?

- Players who adore The Game and want more variety without switching to a completely new title.
- Groups that thrive on cooperative puzzles, friendly rivalry, and bragging rights that come with solving a near-impossible brain teaser.
- Parties that include a mix of veteran players and curious newcomers who want an accessible entry point into the world of tabletop strategy with a dash of drama.
- People who enjoy a good joke at their own expense—a little chaos can be a wonderful teacher if you’re laughing together instead of at one person’s misplay.

If you fit into any of these categories, you’re likely to love what the 2017 Edition Expansion Pack brings to your table. It’s not a “must-have” for every collector, but for the right crew, it can elevate a standard game night into something you’ll be recounting with a smile and a few remorseful chuckles weeks later.

## Final Recommendation: Is It Worth It?

- If you love The Game and want more texture to your sessions, YES, this expansion is a strong addition. It preserves the core challenge while injecting new flavor, making rounds feel fresh without requiring you to relearn the wheel.
- If you’re mostly playing with new players who aren’t sure if they’ll enjoy cooperative puzzles, approach with care. Use the Casual Co-op mode at first to get everyone comfortable and then scale up as confidence grows.
- If you merely tolerated The Game the first time around, this expansion won’t magically convert you into a fan overnight. It’s a thoughtful upgrade for the right vibe and the right crew—but not a universal cure for a tepid reception.

In short: the 2017 Edition Expansion Pack is a careful but generous improvement. It respects the original design while acknowledging that players want a little extra spice—something to stir the pot, not boil it over. If you’re ready for more chaos with a smile, this is your ticket.
