## Zombicide Season 3: Rue Morgue with 2 Expansions — CMON Complete Game (GUG0048)

![Zombicide Rue Morgue box art]({{ '/assets/images/zombicide-rue-morgue-box.jpg' | relative_url }})

Zombicide Season 3 enters the arena with a bang and a scream, delivering a boxed set that tastes like cinnamon donuts and gore at the same time. Rue Morgue is the official name drop for a zombie apocalypse in a comic book style urban nightmare, and this complete package from CMON packs the base game along with two expansions that promise more of everything: more zombies, more survivors, more ways to blunder into hazard pay and heroism alike. If you thought Season 2 was a long walk through a haunted mall armed only with a stale baguette, get ready for Rue Morgue where the mall has a mind of its own and the zombies bring their A-game. In Geeknite style, we break down the components, the rules, the chaos, and the sheer madness that makes Zombicide a cornerstone of modern cooperative gaming.

### In this review in the Rue Morgue, what you get and what you actually do with it

The base game of Zombicide has always been about turning your friends into walking budgets for bad decisions. Rue Morgue doubles down on that premise with two expansions that alter the balance, the variety of zombies, the pool of special abilities, and the overall feel of the game. It is not just a larger pile of plastic; it is a more suspective puzzle, a more chaotic orchestra, and a more intense sprint into the heart of the undead chorus. If you are new to Zombicide, Rue Morgue is a meaty introduction to the franchise that still polishes the essential experience: cooperative problem solving under pressure while one player keeps shouting about morale like a theatre director with a flamethrower.

### What's in the box and what it feels like to unbox it without losing your sleeves

In this complete edition you are greeted by a substantial box that feels as heavy as a necromancer's mood. The contents include the core game, plus two expansions, each adding new miniatures, new mission cards, new boss encounters, and new equipment. The artwork remains a triumph of goth-tinged color schemes that pops on the table and sometimes makes you fear the color palette more than the zombies themselves. The zombie sculpts are chunky, tactical, and recognizable, with enough variation to justify the price tag. If you are into collecting miniatures, Rue Morgue feels like an early Christmas for hobbyists who like their eyes to water when they count the number of unique zombie types in the box.

The two expansions included in this CMON Complete Game are not just add-ons; they reframe the entire threat evaluation. They bring new zombie families with specialized abilities, a handful of new characters with distinct playstyles, and fresh mission objectives that stretch the cooperative concept. One adds more environmental hazards, turning a calm street into a gauntlet of environmental traps. The other adds new supply caches and a different set of objectives that rewards aggressive play instead of cautious camping. In other words, you can go from playing the game like a methodical heist to playing it like a chaotic scavenger run with a bunch of roommates who forgot to buy rent insurance.

### Setup: how long until you realize you forgot to shuffle the zombies correctly

Set-up remains relatively straightforward for seasoned Zombicide players. You separate survivors and zombies, assemble the map board, distribute starting weapons and tools according to the mission card, and then unleash the first wave. The rules maintain that you should not overthink the stacking order or the location of the apocalypse, but that does not stop you from debating whether the survivors should start with a crowbar or a stun baton when the clock is ticking and the zombie density is rising faster than your blood pressure after the third cup of coffee. The two expansions impact setup by adding more boards, more mission cards, and more starter conditions. In practical terms, if you thought the base game required a reasonable chunk of time to explain, Rue Morgue with expansions is a minor event with a lot of moving parts that will remind you of setting up a new console: you will have to read the manual, consult clipboard-accurate notes, and then still forget an item you needed in the first round.

### How Rue Morgue changes the core gameplay loop

The core loop in Zombicide is simple on paper: you activate one hero, explore, gather gear, fight zombies, and survive. The expansions push on this with new zombie types that require new tactics and new objectives that reward risk rather than reservation. Some of the bigger changes include more dynamic environment rules (fires, traps, alleyways that collapse), and a villain-ish set of weekly boss encounters that turn the game into a sort of boss rush with a crowd of the living dead cheering you on. The balance is delicate: you want the new mechanics to feel like meaningful upgrades rather than a tax on your time and memory. Rue Morgue accomplishes this by staggering the new pieces so that you learn the new interactions gradually, rather than being smashed with them all at once.

### Surprises, dilemmas, and moments when the dice decide your fate

Let us be honest: part of the joy of Zombicide is watching the dice derail your carefully laid plans. Rue Morgue does not shy away from this. It leans into it by adding new threat levels and cunning zombie blocks that create moments where you realize the best plan is to improvise and bluff your way through. There will be rounds where a critical weapon fails to appear and the team is forced to pivot to a melee-focused strategy that you did not think you would be forced to execute. The expansions shine when the table laughs at your attempts to coordinate a squad of survivors who are mostly good with duct tape and optimism. In Geeknite fashion, it is the kind of chaos you cannot fully predict but you would not want to miss for the world.

### Thematic resonance: does Rue Morgue feel like a goth romance with a pulse?

Theme matters in a co-op game, and Zombicide typically nails this with a combination of gore, humor, and melodrama. Rue Morgue amplifies the atmosphere with art direction that nods to classic monster cinema and some narrative flavor that makes you forget you are counting the number of times you have failed to pull a weapon. The setting feels like a living, breathing urban ruin that would be equally at home as a stage for a cyberpunk opera or a late-night indie horror screening. The expansions deepen the mood by adding characters and events that feel tailored to this place rather than generic zombie clichés. The result is a game that is not just about surviving a zombie outbreak but about enjoying a particular fantasy of dark, comic decadence while you do so.

### Breadth of components: why two expansions matter beyond more plastic

A common complaint in big-box board games is redundancy. Rue Morgue avoids this by ensuring that the expansions are not merely extra bodies for a crowd control exercise but meaningful reinforcements to your board state, objectives, and character options. The two expansions are balanced in a way that encourages players to mix and match, creating variable game states that feel fresh even after multiple sessions. The addition of early-access style upgrades gives a sense of progression that is missing in some other co-op titles. You can curate a campaign mode by selecting missions that ladder up in difficulty and complexity, letting you tailor a session to your group’s mood. And if you like to customize your own little apocalypse, there is plenty of space in Rue Morgue to experiment with different character builds and environment combos.

### Player count, play time, and a practical reality check

Zombicide is often at its best when you have a group of 4 players; the core experience scales well, offering a lively dynamic with enough miscommunication to be hilarious but not crippling. Rue Morgue with expansions remains in the sweet spot: you will usually get a 60- to 120-minute session depending on the number of players and how quickly you decide to politely ignore the zombies while you negotiate the best path through a hazard-laden city block. The rulebook is not tiny, but it is approachable, especially for groups who have played at least one other edition of Zombicide. If you are a solo player, there are rules for solo play that can feel like a brutal puzzle, but it is still enjoyable when you want to work the brain in a zombie-themed escape room without fear of losing friends to a wrong dice roll.

### Visual production and tactile joy

If there is one thing Zombicide has always done well, it is the tactile joy of manhandling tiny plastic characters to a safe haven or a blood-spattered corner. Rue Morgue is no exception. The weapon cards have a satisfyingly chunky feel, the backs of the mission cards have art that invites you to lean in, and the insert organization is decent enough to justify the investment. The production quality is a reminder that this is a premium product from CMON: not the cheapest, but the presentation makes the chaos feel worthwhile.

### A touch of humor in the face of apocalypse

The designers know that a zombie game benefits from humor, and Rue Morgue provides this in the flavor text on card backs, the occasional rumor card, and the overall tone of the mission briefs. It is not a stand-up routine but a polished sense of fun that keeps you smiling during the moments when the dice decide your fate. It is easy to get caught up in the gravity of a city-wide ruin, and Rue Morgue gives you a playful wink to keep things from becoming too grim. In Geeknite style, we celebrate the small joys: a survivor’s heroic bluff, a luck-based weapon activation that somehow pays off, and the moment you realize your team has gelled into a well-oiled slightly-dysfunctional machine.

### How it stacks up against Season 1 and Season 2

Comparisons are inevitable. Season 3, Rue Morgue, brings a new energy by introducing two expansions that broaden the tactical repertoire without making the base gameplay feel like a separate game. Season 1 had the original charm with its clear design and accessible rules, but Season 3 benefits from the iterative improvement across the series. If you value a heavier co-op with more decision points and a deeper puzzle, Rue Morgue is a natural upgrade. It is a balance between approachable enough for casual folks who want to fight zombies with a friend, and crunchy enough for hobbyists who crave table-time optimization and plan a weekend that is less about chores and more about outsmarting a horde. The expansions add layers that are not optional flavor; they are essential to a richer table experience without alienating new players who want to pick up the game mid-discussion and still jump in.

### Final verdict: should you buy this edition

If you are a fan of the Zombicide line, Rue Morgue with the two expansions is a strong recommendation. It expands the game in meaningful ways, increases the variety of strategic options, and preserves the cooperative thrill that makes the series so engaging. The price is not sunk-cost cheap, but the value live on the table is undeniable when you consider the expanded content, the quality of components, and the longevity of scenarios you can run with friends. For players who like hard choices, emergent storytelling, and a breakneck pace with occasional moments of crippling luck, Rue Morgue delivers. If you are new to Zombicide, this edition can still be a compelling entry point, but be prepared for the added complexity that comes with expansions. You will want to host a walkthrough session with experienced players, but the payoff is a memorable gaming night that feels like a small festival of chaos with a well-tuned engine under the hood.

### Where to buy and who should buy this edition

- Best for: groups of 4 who enjoy a longer co-op session with a rich environment, a mix of tactical decisions, and a healthy dose of misfortune that keeps everyone honest.
- Secondary buyers: collectors who appreciate top-tier production and the aesthetic of Gothic zombie-apocalypse art.
- Side note: this edition shines in campaign-friendly groups who like to weave a narrative across multiple missions while unlocking gear and survivors. If you fall into that camp, you will likely find Rue Morgue to be one of the more satisfying entries in the Zombicide family.
