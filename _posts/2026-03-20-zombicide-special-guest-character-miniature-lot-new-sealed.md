## Assembly, Bases, and Painting Potential
Let’s assume the lot requires a little assembly—most guest miniatures arrive with pre-mitted heads or weapons that need to be glued in place and mounted on a base. Here are some general tips to turn your sealed plastics into tabletop-ready stars without turning your hobby space into a disaster zone:
- Dry-fit first: Before applying glue, dry-fit the pieces to ensure you know where they go and to prevent the misalignment that leads to a heroic-yet-bustard-looking pose.
- Clean the parts: A quick file or a careful sand along mold lines is worth its weight in gold. It improves paint adhesion and reduces the risk of paint chipping during play.
- Prime with purpose: A good primer is essential. A neutral gray or black primer will reveal all the details and help you decide how much weathering you want to apply to the guest’s armor or clothing.
- Base design: Consider the base as part of the storytelling. A guest character’s base can reflect their origin—an icy platform for a frosty hero, a rocky crater for a post-apocalypse merc, or a drab urban tile for a city-dweller turned survivor. A good base makes your character pop without stealing the spotlight from the rest of your squad.
- Paint scheme: Start with a mid-tone base and then add shading to pull details out. The guest character’s signature items deserve special attention—metallics for weapons, bright color accents to reflect their personality, and perhaps a touch of weathering to suggest wear from their experiences.

## Assembly Tips and Maintenance
The moment you pull a guest miniature out of a sealed bag, you’ll want to secure it to a base and prepare it for painting. Here are some concise steps to help you maintain a smooth workflow:
- Inspect before you glue: Look for any mold lines or mis-cast areas. A quick shave with a hobby knife can save you hours of frustration later.
- Glue with precision: Apply a small amount of plastic cement or super glue to the base contact points. If you’re worried about glue on the miniature, use a sprue clipper to keep your pieces clean and avoid accidental glue smears on the sculpt.
- Prime and paint in stages: A thin primer layer helps you see the details while you paint. Build up with light layers of color rather than a single thick coat, so your mini retains its crisp sculpt lines.
- Sealing: A matte or satin varnish can help protect paint from chipping during play and handling. If you’re planning a heavy weathering style, a gloss or satin layer in specific areas (like metallics and armor) can give you the look you want without making the whole figure too shiny.
