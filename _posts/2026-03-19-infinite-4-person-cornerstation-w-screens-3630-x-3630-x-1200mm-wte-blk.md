## Ergonomics and Comfort: Sit Right, Code Right, Snack Right

### Seating and Posture: Four People, Four Healthy Spines?
Ergonomically, the corners offer a nice balance. Each station typically supports a height-adjustable chair with seat depth that accommodates a range of leg lengths. In practice, you’ll want to encourage team members to set chairs to roughly elbow-height on the work surface and to pivot to the main screens with minimal neck rotation. The risk here is the dreaded “monitor world tilt,” where someone’s display becomes a dragon that requires a neck crane to read the bottom row of code. The remedy is a good monitor arm setup and the occasional reminder to stretch between sprints.

### Personal Space vs. Team Synergy
The tactile separation created by privacy screens gives each person a sense of control: you can block out the group chat about the latest office trend and pretend you’re in a private den. Yet, there’s a flip side: the same panels that shield you from the outside world can also muffle collaboration. If you’re in a creative team that thrives on spontaneous whiteboard sessions, you’ll want to ensure the screens can be easily retracted or repositioned to reopen the shared space when the sprint demands it. It’s a balancing act, but a manageable one with the right planning and the occasional “sprint standup in the open air” moment.

### Lighting and Screen Glare: A Teasing Memory of BPMs
With four people in one corner, you’ll quickly learn that lighting becomes a two-edged sword. Overhead lighting creates glare and reflexive screen-glancing, while tinted screens can help. The WTE Black variant tends to pair well with softer, indirect lighting to minimize glare on the screens—you know, the things you actually care about when you’re hunting for that last line of code before the client yawns. If your office has a lot of natural light, you’ll want to consider blinds or window films to keep reflections from turning your coffee into a laser show during a critical debugging run.
