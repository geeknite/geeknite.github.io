## How to play: a quick-start guide for families
If you have played Codenames before, this section is mostly familiar with a few gentle nudges toward the 2.0 variants. If this is your first rodeo, you are about to discover a party game that feels like cheering for your kids and their ability to chain clues together rather than just greedily grabbing all the cards that look like a cartoon dog.

### The goal in a nutshell
Two teams compete to identify their hidden agents on the board using one-word clues from their spymaster. The twist in 2.0 is the family mode and the slightly broadened clue scope, giving you more room to be clever without tipping into cryptic territory that would require a vocabulary lesson.

### The turn structure
- Spymaster gives a one-word clue and a number indicating how many words on the board relate to that clue.
- Team members discuss and select cards they believe match the clue.
- If a team hits their own agent, the turn ends and play passes to the other team. If a bystander card is revealed, it ends the turn with no penalty. If an assassin card is revealed, the game ends in defeat for that team.

The pace is fast, the laughter is plentiful, and the tension is perfectly balanced by the timer option. For families with young kids, you can dial back the number of words in play, which makes it easier for the little ones to parse the board without needing a translator and a sign-language interpreter.

### Tips for success as a family
- Start with simple clues and avoid overly abstract words; you want wins that feel earned, not impossible to connect.
- Use the cooperative mode to warm up the room before diving into the competitive mode.
- Let younger players be the voice of the board: their perspective on word associations can be unexpectedly brilliant and entertaining.

## Variants and house rules: expanding the table without breaking it
CGE includes several variants that stretch the game without taking away the core appeal. Here are a couple you can try, depending on your crowd.

### The speed round variant
This is for the family that wants a little more adrenaline than a Sunday brunch. In speed rounds, teams have a strict time limit to find as many agents as possible. The time pressure adds a playful urgency and reduces the chance for analysis paralysis, which is a common hazard when families overthink every clue.

### The cooperative family mode
In this variant, both teams work together against a shared deck of clues. The idea is to emphasize teamwork and discussion over competition, which can be a huge plus for mixed-age gatherings. It also gives grandma a chance to contribute without fear of being overshadowed by the kids’ brain power.

### The memory twist (optional rules)
A light memory variant means players must remember one clarifying question that came up in a previous round and consider it when formulating future clues. This keeps players engaged and adds a mini memory challenge that is surprisingly satisfying when you actually recall something you discussed 15 minutes earlier.
