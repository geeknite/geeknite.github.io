## Zombicide Black Plague: Adrian Smith Box — Art That Slaps You With a Skeleton Pointer

In the world of board games, some expansions are mere add-ons; others are full-on art heists staged by undead pitmasters. The Adrian Smith Box for Zombicide Black Plague sits squarely in the latter category. It promises more chaos, more color, and more reasons to politely refuse your friends' invitations to game night, because once you open this box, the only thing you’ll be playing is “find the cover art you’ll stare at for an hour.” Let’s dive into what this box actually is, what it adds to your table, and whether it’s worth your hard-earned coffee money.

For the uninitiated, Zombicide Black Plague is a cooperative zombie-slaying game where you and your crew try to survive waves of the risen dead while completing quests. The Adrian Smith Box is a special, art-forward edition that Leans hard into the aesthetic of a fantasy-infused apocalypse. Adrian Smith is a legendary fantasy artist known for his work on Warhammer and other high-fantasy epics. In this box, his art graces more than just the cover; it spills onto miniatures, player aids, and propaganda-style cards that make the undead look like something out of a glossy coffee-table tome—if your coffee table also had a faint, deliciously awful odor of rotting cedar and antiseptic.

## What’s in the Adrian Smith Box?

If you’re buying this for the contents alone (and let’s be honest, we all are), you’ll want a tidy checklist. Here’s what typically ships with the Adrian Smith Box, along with notes about how those components behave at the table.

### Art and Minis
The marketing copy will tell you that the art is “gorgeously rendered” and “worthy of framing.” In practice, it means you’ll have:
- A large, glossy art print or booklet featuring Adrian Smith’s work
- A softcover guide with new lore flavor text and scenario ideas
- An expanded set of zombie miniatures with new sculpts that echo Smith’s style
- Hero survivors with alternate art that makes your squad feel suddenly cooler

Let’s be real: those miniatures aren’t just toys; they’re tokens that demand you say “Look at me!” every time you wipe out a horde. The sculpts may or may not be tournament-legal depending on your local playgroup, but they’re certainly table-dominating.

### Box Content and Quality
Beyond the pretty pictures, you’re looking at:
- A sturdy, premium insert designed to keep everything cradled, not jostled in a transit horror show
- Color-perfect tokens for wound markers, action points, and special abilities
- A curated set of scenarios that lean more into narrative storytelling than pure tactical skirmishing
- A glossy rulebook supplement with easy-to-skim sections and narrative prompts

Quality-wise, the packaging feels weighty, the cards are thick enough to survive a clumsy coffee spill, and the miniatures feel sturdy enough to survive a casual game night without someone “accidentally” dropping them into a glass of water to see if they float.

## Design, Theme, and Flavor

Zombicide Black Plague already nails the mood with its undead horde and medieval fantasy setting, but this Adrian Smith edition cranks the dial up to 11. Thematic cohesion is the star here: the art, the aesthetics, the color palette—all designed to evoke a “fantasy apocalypse” vibe rather than a gritty street-level zombie outbreak.

From an aesthetic perspective, the box and its contents feel like a coffee table book that forgot to disable the power button and decided to play a campaign while you’re not looking. The color scheme leans heavily into rich blues, purples, and golds, giving the undead a regal-cracking vibe. The survivors look heroic, the zombies look horrifying, and the narrative cards push your game into a “this is now a legend” mood rather than a simple objective-based mission.

Gameplay-wise, the art is mostly flavor, but flavor matters. It sets expectations: you’re not just fighting mindless zombies; you’re navigating a stylized, high-fantasy plague critter-ballet. The box’s design nudges players toward taking more cinematic photos of their miniatures, which in turn makes your social media feed a waterfront cruise for horsemen of the apocalypse.

## Gameplay Impact

Let’s talk about what this box does for your actual tabletop experience. Is it a game-changer? Not in the way that a new game engine or a new rule set is a game-changer, but it does something still valuable: it elevates the feel of your campaign.

- Narrative weight: The new scenarios and art prompts add a little theater to your sessions. It’s not just “go kill 10 zombies,” it’s “go brave the crypts to recover artwork worthy of a god-king’s vanity.” The prompts encourage players to role-play more and to narrate their actions with a touch of melodrama.
- Visual impact: On the table, the figure designs and the cards with Adrian Smith’s art give you a splendor of atmosphere. Even if you’re losing, you can still look stylish while your survivors are getting devoured by a particularly dramatic cadre of rotters.
- Accessibility: New players can be overwhelmed by the number of miniatures and rules with any box set, but the special edition tends to include clearer visual cues and a more intuitive color palette. If someone in your group is overwhelmed by the standard box, this edition can help them feel like they’re stepping into a well-curated museum of the undead.

## Production Quality and Value

There’s something to be said for packaging that feels like an investment rather than a temporary amuse-bouche. In this box, CMON and the art team have clearly spent money on materials, packaging, and the tactile experience.

- Card stock: Thick, with satin coating that resists creases after a few plays
- Miniatures: Durable plastic or metal mix, depending on the batch; posing is dynamic and lends itself to diorama building
- Packaging: A compact yet sturdy case that can survive a moving day without the pilot light of anxious kittens
- Artwork: Adrian Smith’s signature style—bold line work, dramatic lighting, and a sense of mythic scale

Value-wise, the price point for a premium edition is heavier on the wallet than the base game. If you’re the kind of gamer who keeps a dedicated shelf for “Art Editions,” this is very much your jam. If you’re price-sensitive, you’ll want to compare what you’re getting (extra minis, art, and scenarios) against the cost of the base game plus separate expansions.

## Final Verdict

If you’re a fan of Zombicide Black Plague and you’ve got a soft spot for high fantasy artwork that threatens to steal your attention away from tactical planning, the Adrian Smith Box is a worthy purchase. It’s not the most practical upgrade—though the scenarios do add some flavor—it’s undeniably the most visually arresting take on the game. It’s the sort of box you pull out and suddenly you’re heroically lining up photos for social media while your group mutters, “We’ll never beat the last wave,” as your mat glows with the painterly glow of Smith’s artistry.

Pros:
- Stunning art and figure design that elevates the game’s theme
- Durable components with a premium feel
- Narrative-focused scenarios that add flavor to campaigns

Cons:
- Pricey for players who care primarily about mechanics
- May contribute to an art-collector’s obsession rather than a pure gamer’s objective

Bottom line: If you’re building a Zombicide collection around aesthetics and story, this box is a slam dunk. If you’re playing for the simplest, fastest route to victory every night, you may want to pass.
