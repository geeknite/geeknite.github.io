## The five-pack lineup: what you’re likely getting
Zombicide has exploded into a family of stand-alone games, each with a distinct flavor and a shared DNA of cooperative zombie-slaying. In a typical used five-pack, you’ll usually encounter these five titles:

- Zombicide (the original base game): a gridded, miniature-thrashing exercise in dice-fortune, cooperative problem-solving, and heroics gone hilariously wrong.
- Zombicide: Black Plague: the medieval/fantasy twist with dragons, sorcery, and knights who forgot to bring enough healing. The box is heavier, and the miniatures look like they spent a semester at art school—carefully primed and possibly a little dusty.
- Zombicide: Green Horde: the green team’s counterpart to Black Plague—still fantasy, but with more forest vibes and orcs who are probably tired of marching. This set is a favorite for players who enjoy trying to set up a tactical blockade while a goblin breaks the rhythm of your plans.
- Zombicide: Invader: space zombies. If Black Plague is a banquet in a castle hall, Invader is a dice-fueled sprint through a starship corridor with glowing panels and suspicious oxygen levels. As you can imagine, the mix gives you a broad spectrum of gameplay styles, from dungeon crawls to space pistols to gun-slinging showdowns. ## Inspecting a used set: what to look for and what to fear
When you’re hunting used Zombicide, you’re not buying a brand-new car; you’re buying a used car that has likely seen the occasional eldritch explosion. Here are the main checkpoints to ensure you’re not signing up for a box full of broken rules and missing minis.

### Box condition and insert integrity
- Look for water damage or warping on the external box. Zombicide boxes are durable but not invincible; a wavy lid can indicate humidity exposure, which is bad news for cards and tiles.
- Check the inner trays and plastic inserts. The inserts often crack or split with age. If you need to replace them, thrift-store boxes can become a DIY project, which is a fun way to spend your weekend—but not if you’re trying to play next Friday.
- Rulebook presence is non-negotiable. If the rulebook is missing or torn, ask yourself if you’re buying a rulebook or a scavenger hunt. If you’re a rulebook hoarder, you’re in luck; if not, consider a digital copy or a scanned PDF from fellow players.

### Components: minis, cards, tiles, and dice
- Miniatures: look for chipped plastics, bent weapons, or bases that won’t stand. Inspect a few minis under bright light; if you see nub marks from poor casting, it’s not a fatal flaw, but it does affect play and collection value.
- Cards: check for bent corners, creases, and warping. Cards in good health are essential since you’ll be shuffling them a lot. A worn card does not fail the game, but it does fail your aesthetic desire to have a pristine play surface.
- Tiles: tiles should be flat, not warped. Warped tiles can mess up movement and line of sight, which in a zombie apocalypse translates into many more wasted rounds thanks to mis-measured distances.

### Condition across the five games
- Base Zombicide: the original run has iconic components; expect sturdy chits and a few paint chips on minis if the set has been loved by painters. If you’re sensitive to minor paint scrapes, brace yourself for a few stories about “how the goblin got its green tint.”
- Black Plague and Green Horde: expect more metallic or ornate minis. They can be more delicate. Watch for gaps in sculpts where sprues were clipped too aggressively; it won’t ruin play, but it’s a quality note.
- Invader: fewer miniature casualties in transit, given that space is a premium. However, you might find some glow-in-the-dark or metallic finishes that have aged oddly under bright lights. Test a couple of cards to make sure they still read clearly under normal table lighting.
- Undead or Alive: Western miniatures sometimes enjoy a weathered paint job that can look charming or vandalized, depending on your taste. If you’re a purist about “clean” minis, consider a light repaint or wash to bring them back to a cohesive look.

### The rulebooks and play aids
- In a five-pack, you’ll get five separate rulebooks (one per game), plus various other player aids and reference sheets. Make sure these are present; sometimes players misplace rule cards or reference sheets. If missing, you can usually source PDFs from the internet or use fan-made cheat sheets, which can actually speed up learning for new players.
- If you see a handful of reference sheets that look overly worn or taped, that’s a sign of heavy use. Not a deal-breaker, but you might want to print fresh sheets if you’re planning to run heavy sessions.

## Play experience: what the set actually feels like on your table
A used five-pack offers a broad spectrum of experiences, and that diversity is the magic sauce that drew me to this kind of purchase in the first place. Let’s break down how each title tends to feel in play, especially when it’s used and possibly a little battle-scarred from previous campaigns.

### Zombicide (base): classic, approachable chaos
The original Zombicide is where many players cut their teeth. It leans into straightforward cooperative goals: work together to clear tiles, rescue survivors, and survive the zombie onslaught. In a used copy, you might notice minor scuffing on the tiles, but that’s part of the charm. The core mechanic—yellow dice for copses of survivors and red dice for the big bads—remains the heartbeat of the product. You’ll see players leaning into classic tactical decisions: which corridor to hold, which door to breach, and which abomination to trigger in a tactical puzzle that only makes sense when you’re two hours deep and caffeine is your best friend.

### Black Plague and Green Horde: fantasy, but with the same panic trigger
These two share many mechanics, with a twist; Black Plague introduces swords, magic, and fantasy mobs; Green Horde turns it into verdant goblin mayhem. Both are deeper and more tactical than the base game, thanks to wider action spaces and more elaborate enemy waves. In used condition, you’ll still get the same satisfying sense of accomplishment when you corner a zombie necromancer or draft a clever two-turn trap that buys your team three extra rounds of shooting.

### Invader: sci-fi sprint through a starship maze
Invader ramps up the pace and the range of enemies. The aliens don’t just roam; they flank with purpose, and the terrain cards create dynamic corridors that force players to adapt. In a used set, the most important thing is that the space die is legible, and that the card art hasn’t faded into a blur of glow and weird punctuation. You’ll still experience frantic sequences where your crew tries to obtain reactor cores, repair hull integrity, and fight off a wave that seems to come from every direction at once. It’s the kind of chaos that makes you swear eternal loyalty to your co-op partners, and then immediately blame them when the “Ambush!” card drops on a critical turn.

### Undead or Alive: a dusty Western front with skeletons in the dust
The Western edition is a theme shift more than a mechanical revolution. It’s skirmish-y, with a little more emphasis on positioning and range. In used form, you might find the maps showing a bit of foxing around the edges, but the core experience—shooting, slicing, and shouting “Draw!” when the timer hits zero—feels surprisingly timeless. The Western vibe gives a unique flavor to multiplayer chaos and makes for fun storytelling around a campfire table.

## How to price and what you’re actually paying for
Used prices vary by region, seller, and how much a local gamer community loves Zombicide. In many places, a base Zombicide in good condition lands around 20–40 USD. Black Plague and Green Horde can fetch 25–60 USD depending on the rarity of a particular edition and whether the minis are still neatly painted or look like they’ve been re-wielded by a party of enthusiastic players who used a paintbrush like a weapon.
Invader tends to land around 25–60 USD as well, driven by sci-fi collectors and fans of modular space combat. Undead or Alive can swing between 20–50 USD, again depending on the quality of the miniatures and whether you’re lucky enough to catch a box with a crisp, mint-looking insert.

If you’re buying as a bundle, you should expect some discount relative to buying each game separately. A five-pack can feel like a steal if you’re a player who enjoys switching between themes, but you’ll want to account for potential extra costs: shipping, taxes, and possibly replacements for lost or damaged manual pages. A fair rule of thumb is to compare the total price with the price of buying each title new or in new-ish condition. If the bundle saves you 30–40 percent, you’ve found a good deal. If it’s only 5–10 percent, you might be better off cherry-picking titles you actually want.

## How to maximize the value of a used Zombicide collection
Here are practical steps to get the most out of your purchase, plus a few strategies you can adopt to ensure you’re playing the longest possible version of these games instead of letting them gather dust on a shelf.

### Inspect thoroughly before you hand over cash
- Bring a bright light and a friend with a keen eye for mini details. Two heads are better than one when you’re hunting for hairline cracks in the plastic bases.
- Ask about the box contents and confirm the expected components. A five-pack should have ten to twelve dice packs, five rulebooks, five core decks, a handful of tokens, and the board tiles for each game.
- Make sure you can find the essential add-ons: survivors, zombies, weapon cards, and mission sheets. If there are big gaps, you can negotiate a price that reflects the missing items and possibly purchase replacements from the same seller or a local shop.

### Prepare for some cleaning and restoration right away
- A light wipe-down with a microfiber cloth is a good start. No aggressive cleaning—these minis are tiny works of art; you don’t want to rub away the paint entirely, especially on small details that give the set its character.
- If you’re comfortable with minor edge touch-ups, you can touch up chips or paint loss. It’s optional but can improve the visual cohesion across the five titles.
- Replace loose or creaky wax seals on the box lids with proper tape to ensure the set can travel safely to your gaming table in future gatherings.

### Do you want to expand or scale down your play sessions?
- If your group tends to have six players who rotate turns around the table, base Zombicide can become a learning experience for newbies and veterans alike. Black Plague and Green Horde scale the player count differently, so think about your typical group size before committing to a fixed plan.
- Invader and Undead or Alive offer different paces of play. If you’re more into one-and-done sessions, you’ll still get hours of narrative chaos from a single night without burning out.

## Cross-compatibility and expansions: what to know
One of the joys of Zombicide is that many expansions add or adjust bits of the base game rather than reinventing it. Towards a used five-pack, you’ll find that the expansions are designed to be retrofitted with the base system. While you can mix and match a lot of components across the different games, there are caveats:

- Thematically, the zombie figures and tokens are not universal across editions. A Black Plague zombie package won’t perfectly match an Invader board’s figure count and card interactions. If you want clean, uniform gameplay, you may want to standardize around a single edition and add expansions within the same “family” (e.g., Black Plague with its own expansions).
- The rulebooks share language, but not always terms. If you’re a rules purist, you’ll want to keep a copy of each game's reference sheet handy while you learn. If you’re more of a “learn-as-you-play” person, you can rely on the main rules and the mission sheets to guide you through the chaos.
- The post purchase upgrades are often more of a quality-of-life issue than a game-changing mechanic. You’ll find improved dice sets, better board components, and more robust storage solutions. If you’re planning to resell, consider whether you’re going to throw these upgrades into the price or keep them for yourself.
