## Build and materials
### Blade geometry and steel
The Honshu Sub Hilt’s blade is the centerpiece—long, straight-edged, and with a silhouette that begs for a hero shot. The 11.38-inch overall length means you’ll get a long blade without needing a forklift for storage. The actual blade length is slightly shorter than the full length, which helps with balance and reach during use. The steel specified is 7Cr13MoV, a stainless alloy widely used in budget knives. It’s not the stainless equivalent of premium carbon steel; it’s closer to the entry-level tier that prioritizes corrosion resistance, ease of sharpening, and affordability. Edge retention isn’t spectacular, but you can dial in a usable edge with a few minutes at the stones—assuming you’re patient and not hoping for a 1,000-year shaving edge.

In practice, 7Cr13MoV behaves like a reliable workhorse steel for everyday tasks: box opening, rope slicing, kitchen prep, and the occasional out-of-window-sash cutting session. It won’t hold an edge like a premium tool steel, but for a budget blade or a training piece, it does the job well enough that you won’t feel ridiculous using it on standard chores. If you’re hoping for stainless nirvana with a razor-edge lasting for months on end, you’ll be disappointed; if you’re hoping for a budget blade that can still cut, you’ll be pleasantly surprised.

### Handle and grip

The Sub Hilt naming implies a partial finger guard that protects your digits in a defensive stance or, more realistically, helps you avoid knuckle contact while performing a rapid cut with a heavy blade. The ergonomics aren’t theater-quality—no full contoured grip here—but the combination of a long handle and TPR texture gives you a solid hold for most everyday tasks. It’s not a high-speed, pro-user grip; it’s a practical, budget-friendly grip that gets the job done.

### Edge maintenance and care tips
The edge retention of 7Cr13MoV in budget knives is a trade-off. Expect to refresh the edge more often than premium knives, but you’ll discover how simple the process can be with common sharpening tools. A few minutes on a whetstone (1000–6000 grit) or a basic ceramic rod can bring back a sharp, serviceable edge. Pro tip: keep a dedicated sharpening stone in your gear bag for quick touch-ups in the field.

Moisture and corrosion resistance will improve with regular cleaning and oiling after use. Wipe the blade dry, apply a light coat of oil to prevent rust on areas that might trap moisture, and store in a dry environment. The TPR handle is relatively forgiving to humidity, but you don’t want to leave the knife soaking in moisture overnight; the goal is to keep the blade dry and happy.

## Maintenance routine: a practical guide
- After use: wipe clean and dry thoroughly. Avoid leaving water droplets on the blade surface.
- Cleaning: a mild soap and water wash is enough for routine maintenance; avoid harsh chemicals that can degrade the handle.
- Drying: ensure the blade and handle are completely dry before storage.
- Lubrication: apply a thin layer of mineral oil or knife-specific oil on the blade’s edge to prevent rust.
- Storage: store in a dry, ventilated area; if possible, keep the knife inside the sheath to minimize exposure to dust and moisture.
