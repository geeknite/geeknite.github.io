## How to Play: The Quickstart
If you know Skip-Bo, you’ll recognize the spirit: you’re trying to get rid of your stock or hand cards by playing them into a sequence. The Dice Game version changes the tool from cards to dice, but the vibe remains the same: create valid sequences, use the right dice to break ties, and outlast your family members’ patience.

### Basic turn flow
1. Roll a subset of your dice at the start of your turn.
2. Look at the dice results and assemble them into one or more valid sequences or discard opportunities.
3. Place completed sequences onto your build area (or “table piles,” if you want to sound classy at the airport lounge).
4. End your turn and pass the dice to the next player.

The rules come with variants that tweak how many dice you roll, how long a game lasts, and what happens in tie situations. The core thrill is that your fate rests in the ambivalent whim of the dice, which is exactly how every family board game secretly wants to feel.

### Strategy basics
- Reading the board state matters more than you’d expect. If someone is sitting on a mountain of small numbers, you might want to go for mid-range sequences to prevent them from powering ahead.
- Timing is everything. The moment you can drop a big run, you should do it—ideally when it creates the biggest chain reaction for your opponents.
- Don’t hoard dice like a dragon. You’ll regret it when you can’t complete a necessary sequence because you’re holding onto a single critical die.

## Comparisons: Skip-Bo Dice vs. Skip-Bo Card Game
If you’re coming from the Skip-Bo card game, you’ll notice several shifts:
- Components: cards vs. dice. The tactile feel changes the pacing and the sense of control.
- Pace: dice games tend to be quicker on average, but a well-tuned variant can drag out longer for dramatic effect.
- Accessibility: dice are straightforward for most ages, while Skip-Bo cards demand more strategic planning and reading of complex card stacks.

That said, the core spirit remains intact: you’re always racing to minimize your own piles while leveraging your dice to disrupt others. For families who want the Skip-Bo energy without the card shuffling, this is a strong substitute.
