## Card Drafting, Scoring, and Strategy: The Tasty Bits

### Core mechanic: Draft, pass, reveal, repeat
If you’ve played Sushi Go Party before, the core flow will feel familiar: draft one card from your hand, pass the rest to the left, and reveal all at once. You then score for your collected combinations, which are determined by the card types (nigiri, maki, sashimi, tempura, etc.). The goal is to assemble the best combination of cards across rounds for maximum points. The variety in Sushi Go Party elevates the tension; you’re not just chasing a single set but weaving a tapestry of potential scoring lines that can change with each round.

### Strategy: Short-term gain vs. long-term joy
Two recurring themes emerge in strategy discussions:
- Short-term: Take cards that block your opponent or deny them a high-scoring combo, even if it’s not ideal for you. Denial is a legitimate tactic in party games, and sometimes it’s more valuable than a tiny point swing.
- Long-term: Build toward flexible scoring opportunities that can adapt to opponents’ choices. For example, securing a mix that scores well even if a round gets undercut by someone else can keep you in the running when luck tilts away from you.

In practice, you’ll find yourself plotting two or three rounds ahead, then adjusting as the table dynamic shifts. The best players aren’t always the ones who snag the biggest single curry roll; they’re the ones who accumulate multiple smaller gains that stack up by the end of the game.

### The gentle art of risk and card-spotting
Sushi Go Party rewards card-spotting—recognizing what your table is likely to chase and pivoting to counter. When you see a frantic rush for sashimi or tempura, you might pivot toward a complementary set that you can complete with fewer competition. The density of choices in the Tin edition means there’s always a path to at least a respectable score if you read the table well. That’s the charm: it remains approachable (and often laugh-out-loud fun) while still rewarding attention to detail.
