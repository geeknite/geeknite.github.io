## What is Codenames Duet?
Codenames Duet is the two-player cooperative variant of the famous word-grid guessing game. Instead of competing teams, you and your partner shoulder a joint mission: contact all of your agents before you run out of clues or accidentally step on the assassin. The game uses a shared 5x5 grid of codename cards—the familiar assortment of words that will either be your best friends or the cause of a dramatic, dramatic sigh. A special double-sided key card acts as your map: it marks which words are agents for you and which are for your partner, and it indicates the deadly assassin slot you must avoid.

What makes Duet distinctive is the shift from competitive pressure to cooperative strategy. You aren’t trying to guess more words than your opponents; you’re trying to coordinate with your teammate to strike a balance between revealing your own agents and not stepping on the assassin. It’s a social experiment wrapped in cardboard: you have to communicate clearly, infer your partner’s thought process, and accept that sometimes a clue will land like a rubber duck in your coffee mug—unexpected, slightly silly, and somehow not entirely unwelcome.

The duo nature of the game means you’ll be negotiating with your own cognitive biases rather than the other players’ egos. You’ll discover whether your partner is more literal or more metaphorical, whether you should lean into specificity or chase the rope of a broad association. And yes, there’s a little bit of mind reading involved—though in most cases, you’ll learn to accept that your partner’s interpretation might be wilder than your own.

## How to play: flow, turns, and clues
Codenames Duet follows a collaborative rhythm of turns that feels almost like a dance. The key sheet marks the two players’ agent goals, and both of you share responsibility for contacting all the agents. The core loop is simple in concept but rich in nuance:

1) The clue-giver selects a one-word clue that relates to one or more of the target words on the board. The clue should be a single word, not a phrase, and it must be logically connected to the target words you’re trying to hint at. You’ll also decide on a number to accompany the clue — this is how many words you think your partner can correctly identify in that round.
2) The clue-giver reads the clue aloud, then your partner looks at the grid and makes guesses by touching the cards. Each guessed card reveals immediately whether it’s a correct agent, a neutral card, an opponent’s agent, or the dreaded assassin.
3) If your partner hits a red/blue agent corresponding to your color (or any agent listed on the key), your team gradually makes progress toward the goal. If you reveal a neutral card, you’ve still learned something, but progress is slower. If you reveal the assassin, the game ends immediately with a loss. The adrenaline is real, and the nerves are optional but encouraged.
4) After a guess, the turn continues until the number is exhausted (or until your partner decides to stop guessing early). Then, you switch roles: the partner becomes the clue-giver on the next round.
5) You repeat this dance, trying to cover all required agents before you exhaust a limited number of guesses or reveal the assassin. The game typically ends when all agents are contacted or when you’ve made enough wrong guesses to trigger a failure state.

An important nuance: in Duet, the number you choose with your clue doesn’t guarantee you’ll hit exactly that many words. It simply guides your partner’s confidence. A cautious pair might say a clue of 2 with a small number to reduce risk, while a bold duo might push a higher number to accelerate the pace. The balance between risk and reward is the heartbeat of the game.

## Strategy: how to build better clues and read your partner
If you want to survive more than a handful of rounds, you’ll need a toolkit of clue-building techniques. Here are a few that tend to work well in practice:

- Start with the obvious, then pivot to the obscure. If you’ve got a couple of obvious word clusters on the board, your first clue might cover those, but avoid locking you into an overly narrow path that your partner can misinterpret.
- Consider category-based clues. If several target words share a common theme (animals, travel, tech terms, etc.), a single clue can unlock multiple cards — but be mindful of the risk that you’ll inadvertently point toward an assassin or a non-target card.
- Use cross-word bridges. One word clue can connect words that you previously revealed or that you expect your partner to notice as connected. It’s a risk, but if your partner reads your mind correctly, it can pay off in spades.
- Think about the assassin last. The moment you’re close to finishing, it can be wise to avoid a high-risk clue and instead opt for a steadier, more measured approach that preserves the possibility of winning without triggering catastrophe.

Reading your partner is half the game. If you are the type who turns every clue into a tiny TED Talk, you’ll either become a legendary word whisperer or a conversational hazard. Conversely, if you’re the minimalist who uses a single word and a platinum glare, your partner may feel like you’re handing them a scavenger hunt with the secret code missing. The best teams blend your styles until the clues start echoing in both of your heads like a well-tuned synth duo.

## Going beyond the basics: variations and house rules
If you’ve played Duet a few times and want to shake things up, consider these light variations that can keep the game fresh without breaking the core rules:
- Time-boxed challenge: Use a timer to keep sessions tight. This forces quicker clue construction and can add a fun rush to your decision-making process.
- Thematic clues: Pick a theme (movies, space, tech) for a game night and encourage clue words that tie to that theme. It’s a playful way to stretch your associative muscles and see how your partner’s mind maps the connections.
- “Two clues, one turn”: Allow the clue-giver to deliver two separate clues within a single turn, provided the total number of guessed words stays under a safe limit. This variant can boost pace and add a tactical layer to clue ordering.

Keep in mind that these variants are fine as long as both players agree on the house rules. The beauty of Duet lies in your team’s shared creativity, and a little experimentation with constraints can often yield unforgettable moments and story-worthy misreads.
