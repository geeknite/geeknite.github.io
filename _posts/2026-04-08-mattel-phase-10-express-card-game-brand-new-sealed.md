## Strategy and Pro-tips for New Players
### Getting started with confidence
New players should focus on learning the phase progression rather than chasing the highest score in the first two rounds. The express cadence rewards players who stabilize a single strategy early and adapt as the table changes. A good starting approach is to identify which phases appear most frequently in the deck and plan your hand to support those. If you can quietly assemble a couple of safe sets while your opponents chase their own glory, you will begin to accumulate momentum that pays off in later rounds.

### When to push and when to hold back
In a fast sprint, there is a delicate balance between pushing for a quick finish and holding back to avoid a sudden hand clog. If you are near the end of a round and you have a chance to complete a phase with a low risk discard, take the risk. If your hand is fragile with several blockers, you may want to pivot and attempt a different phase that minimizes exposure to high value discards. Observing opponent patterns is helpful; if someone tends to hoard a particular suit or number, using a discard to block their progress can be a small, satisfying victory that pays off in a later round.

### Common missteps to avoid
A frequent mistake is chasing an ambitious phase too early when your hand lacks the supporting pieces. The Express format punishes this impulse by forcing you to redraw and reorganize, which can lead to a cascade of lost momentum. Another pitfall is underestimating the power of the wild card. The wild can save a round or completely flip the table if used at the right moment. Remember that timing matters more in Express than in the long slog of a classic Phase 10 session. Stay sharp, stay flexible, and keep your trash talk friendly and limited to jesting about how your opponent miscounted a run.
