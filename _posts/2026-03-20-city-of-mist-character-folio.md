## What is in the Character Folio?
The folio’s core promise is clarity without stripping away the chaotic charm that makes City of Mist feel like a living comic book. It isn’t merely a stack of printable pages; it’s a narrative instrument that helps players pin down who their character is across multiple masks and how those masks interact with the world’s rules. You will see sections for:

- Masks and Mythos: the dual identity slots that drive the game’s conflict
- Logos: the thematic alignment that shapes how your character interacts with the city’s underbelly and its legends
- Icons, Abilities, and Stunts: what you can actually do when the story calls for a dramatic reveal
- Relationships and Contacts: the social web that makes the city feel alive
- Arcs and Progression: how your character evolves in response to the story’s beats
- Tragedies, Turning Points, and Tags: what makes your character’s journey feel like a myth in progress

The folio also offers a few “quick-start” sheets to help groups begin play with a character already sketched, with the caveat that this game thrives on storytellers who give space to the emergent legends rather than a rigid, preordained arc.

For those of you who want the aesthetic of the folio before you buy, here’s a quick image hint: the design leans into moody, neon-noir typography with a slightly weathered, retro-futurist vibe. It’s the sort of thing you’d expect to see on a poster for a film festival that happens to be set in a city full of people who secretly wear mythic mantles. The folio captures this vibe through a combination of high-contrast black-and-white line art, a color palette that nods to early 80s sci-fi romance, and mild grain textures that make each page feel tactile rather than digital.

As a companion piece, it succeeds when you want a tactile, ritualistic approach to character creation. It’s not merely “write down your scores” but “stage your identity in panels,” and that matters for a game where identity is both the weapon and the battleground.

## Design and Layout: Form that Follows Function
Let’s talk typography, margins, and the surprising joy of on-page hierarchy. The folio isn’t a high-gloss coffee-table accessory; it’s a working tool that you’ll handle during sessions, back-and-forth with another player, or last-minute during a post-session debrief. The design team has strived to keep the pages legible under store lighting and the glow of a laptop screen late at night when you’re “just checking a rule real quick.”

- Typographic balance: Clear headings, readable font sizes, and a consistent typographic rhythm that makes scanning the page feel like a micro-pace-reading experience rather than a sprint across a blank sheet. The headers do a good job of signaling where to write your real notes, which is important when you’re scribbling in the margins between gunshots and moral quagmires.
- Color usage: The folio uses color as a navigational aid more than a mere aesthetic flourish. Masks, Logos, and Mythos have colored accents so you can locate them at a glance. It’s not garish; it’s practical neon—a subtle nod to the City’s visual language.
- Page layout: The sheets avoid shrinking small print into a corner. There’s enough margin for your own doodles and side-notes. A few pages use tab-like dividers that help you flip back to the section you need without sacrificing a sense of continuity.
- Graphic elements: The line art echoes the city’s mythic street-level energy. You’ll see silhouettes of masks, silhouettes of city skylines, and modular icons that you can reuse across sessions. It’s the visual equivalent of a detective’s mood board, but with a more elegant restraint.

If you’re a designer-minded gamer who loves good borders and the alignment of a page that feels “crafted,” you’ll appreciate what the folio offers on a visual level. It’s not merely decorative; it’s a design system designed to keep the storytelling engine well-lubricated.


## The Folio in Play: How It Affects the Session
A character folio in a noir-tinged game should not be only a place to store numbers; it should be a player’s mnemonic device — a tool that makes the mythical identities pop in real time. The folio succeeds here by providing:

- Quick-reference corners: Typical City of Mist sessions rely on quick checks—what is your Mask’s ability? What is your current Link with a Contact? The folio makes those references fast, reducing table friction while you’re chasing a ticking clock or a cunning villain.
- Narrative prompts: Each major section (Mythos, Logos, Mask) comes with prompts that can nudge a player toward a storytelling fork. You won’t be railroaded; you’ll be invited to choose a path that feels like comic-book suspense rather than traditional RPG tropes.
- Relationship weaving: The “Contacts” and “Relationships” areas are incredibly handy for the GM who wants to know how every NPC ties into a player’s life. It’s not just about who owes a favor; it’s about how those debts ripple through the story’s social graph.

In actual tabletop sessions, the folio tends to speed up the process of getting characters onto the stage. You’ll see players finishing their character sheets and starting the actual game with a level of confidence that wasn’t always present before. The folio reduces the cognitive load of “remembering all the rules” and increases the cognitive load of “remembering the story.” The balance is delightful when you’re chasing a scene’s emotional beat rather than wrestling with a stat block.

As with any dual-identity system, the folio’s value is higher if you lean into the game’s thematic drift. If your players treat Masks and Mythos as purely mechanical slots, you’ll miss the point. If you treat them as living narrative devices, you’ll get a lot more mileage out of the folio—and out of City of Mist itself.
