# Saboteur (Xbox 360, 2009) - Complete Review

![Saboteur cover]({{ '/assets/images/saboteur-360-cover.jpg' | relative_url }})

If you ever wanted a World War II open-world caper where the city itself is a character you can punch in the face, Saboteur is your weird little gem. Developed by Pandemic Studios and published by Electronic Arts in 2009, the game flirts with a high-contrast art style, a city that glows brighter as you chip away at the Nazi regime, and a protagonist who talks like a faster-talking 1940s pilot who learned his sarcasm from a pint and a dare. The Xbox 360 version—our focus here—delivers a hybrid of driving-shooter and stealth-skirmish action, all set against a version of Paris that’s as much a stage for color as it is a battlefield for boots and bravado. And yes, we’re calling it a “Complete Edition” for this review because the packaging that shipped back in the day didn’t come with a DLC splash page that felt essential—yet the game’s world begs to be explored in full.

In Geeknite fashion, we’ll wander through the city, paint by paint, and critique the good, the bad, and the “how in the world did you pull that off” moments. Let’s dive into what Saboteur does well, what it stumbles over, and whether the Complete Edition (as we imagine it) actually lives up to the promise of liberating a city one color bloom at a time.

## Overview: A Colorful Wartime Playground

Saboteur is best understood as a confident attempt to blend two big ideas: an open world where you’re rewarded for liberating districts from Nazi control, and a stylized visual language that uses color as a storytelling mechanic. The game starts in a Paris that’s swaddled in sepia-toned dreariness; each district you free splashes back into color, a cinematic reminder that resistance is not only righteous but also pretty to look at. You control Sean Devlin, a rough-around-the-edges anti-hero who’s more likely to crack a line about a German’s shoes than to compliment a pastry—and that sarcasm is as much a weapon as the pistols in his belt.

This particular entry in the 2009-2010 era of AAA action-adventure games wants to be your big, loud fantasy of wartime heroism: street-level sabotage, car chases, rooftops, and tradecraft with a dash of noir. It isn’t afraid to lean into spectacle—the bellowed takedowns, the sweeping chase sequences, the dramatic lighting that makes you feel like you’re walking through a black-and-white film that forgot to color inside the lines. And yet the heartbeat of the game is simple and sincere: you want to see Paris brighten up, you want to outsmart Nazis, and you want to feel like your choices in the moment matter to the city you’re liberating.

This review is written with the premise of a hypothetical “Complete Edition”—all patches and updates included, and all released content bundled into one big late-2009/early-2010 package. In reality, Saboteur didn’t receive a formal, widely advertised “Complete Edition” in the same way that some shooters did later; instead, players got the core game plus patches and a handful of DLC packs over time. For the purposes of this review, we’re treating the Complete Edition as an idealized construct: every improvement, every tweak, every additional mission integrated into the main arc. If you’re hunting for a one-stop Saboteur experience on the Xbox 360, this is the experience you’d hope for—whether you’re replaying it to chase a better score or you’re discovering the city’s color for the first time.

## Gameplay: Driving, Shooting, Stealth, Repeat

### Core Loop: Paint, Sabotage, and Lift the Veil

Saboteur’s core loop is deceptively simple: complete missions to sabotage the Nazi regime’s grip on a district, liberate that area to reveal its true colors, and use the momentum to unlock more challenging encounters. The map is big enough to matter, but not so big that you forget what you’re here to do. The open world design leans into the “one-more-district” mentality, which can feel addictive in a way that’s both good and dangerous: good for momentum, dangerous for your sleep schedule.

Color returns to the city as you reclaim districts, but that color is more than aesthetic. It’s a light moral compass: the more you liberate, the more you see people living, marching, and taking back their own streets. The color mechanic also doubles as visual feedback for your progress and as a performance cue in the lighting engine. It’s not just pretty; it’s a narrative device that rewards exploration and direct action rather than sitting behind a stealthy throne of patience.

### Combat and Firefights: Guns, Punches, and Explosions

Combat rarely tries to be elaborate; it’s a practical, punchy mix of gunplay and brawling that suits Sean’s character and the game’s pace. You’ll find yourself in street skirmishes, rooftop melees, and chase sequences where a good set of quick-time events (QTEs) or timed dodges can turn the tide in your favor. The game doesn’t demand that you play to the most meticulous stealth every time; it acknowledges that Sean’s style is kinetic and a little reckless, and the design supports that with enough variety to feel like you’re not locked into one trick.

That said, Saboteur is not going to win awards for depth in its gunplay. The weapons feel practical and serviceable, not spectacular. If you’re seeking the kind of precision and weight you might find in a dedicated shooter, you’ll probably wish for a bit more impact in your shots and a little more nuance in enemy AI. The hit reactions are satisfying enough to keep you engaged, but there are moments where you’ll realize the game’s combat is more functional than flashy—an honest compromise for a title that wants to rap in the same rhythm as a heist movie rather than a tactical shooter.

### Stealth and Movement: Shadow Games in a Colorful City

Stealth in Saboteur is functional, not fancy. You can creep along rooftops, hide in alleys, and use the environment to your advantage, but the game never turns into a pure stealth sandbox. It’s more of a spice than the main course: stealth sections are tense and well-timed, but they’re not the entire flavor profile. Your success depends on reading patrol patterns, using distractions, and picking the right moment to strike from the shadows or to escape into the next colorfully liberated neighborhood.

Movement feels mostly fluid on the Xbox 360. Sean controls with a certain swagger that suits his roguish persona. The driving segments earned more love and more groans than any other sub-system; gliding through tight Paris streets with the neon glow reflecting off rain-slick cobbles can be a mood that lingers—buried under a few stumbles like occasional camera quirks and some less-than-perfect handling when you’re pushing for a high-speed pursuit.

### Driving: Wheels, Wires, and the Wind Across the Seine

Driving in Saboteur is always a little theatrical. The city’s layout encourages you to weave through plazas and paralelled boulevards while avoiding checkpoints and snipers. The car handling offers a sense of momentum and risk, but it’s not Without its rough edges: occasionally, turning can feel a tad imprecise, and some vehicle physics can make you yaw into a lamppost in a way that makes you wonder about your reflexes more than your driving prowess. Still, driving remains one of Saboteur’s most memorable threads because it couples well with the game’s mission structure—get to a location, stir up trouble, and watch a neighborhood wake up in color.

## Visuals and Audio: A City That Breathes in Colour

### Art Direction: A Noir-Infused Paris That Pops

Saboteur is unapologetically stylized. Its art direction treats Paris like a living painting with a world-class mood ring. When the city is in grayscale, every shadow feels heavier and every silhouette sharper. As you liberate districts, you see color bloom in the environment: red storefronts, blue skies, green parks, and yellow signs. The effect is akin to flipping a switch on a film projector and letting the color wash over the frame. It’s a clever design choice that underlines the game’s core theme: resistance is not just about defeating enemies; it’s about bringing life back to a city that’s been asked to forget what it feels like to be alive.

The level design works hand-in-hand with this palette. Alleyways feel narrow and claustrophobic, open squares feel expansive, and landmark buildings punctuate the skyline with a sense of history. Some districts are decorative moirai; others are more utilitarian battlefields. The net effect is a city that feels lived-in and reactive, even if some of the textures and pop-in aren’t quite as polished as the era’s most expensive open worlds.

### Soundscape: The City’s Pulse and Its Peril

The audio in Saboteur carries the game’s swagger with a soundtrack that leans into brass, percussion, and a sense of urgency. It’s not a symphony of shock-and-awe; it’s more akin to a score you hum while sprinting through a street chase. Voice acting embraces grit and charm in equal measure—Sean’s charisma is part swagger, part streetwise grit, and the supporting cast does enough to keep you invested in the city’s drama without ever slipping into cliché.

The sound design excels at reinforcing the color mechanic: you’ll hear shifts in ambiance and music as you reclaim districts, and every gunshot or explosion adds a tactile layer to the scene that makes you feel the stakes without needing a constant narration about “what’s at stake.” It’s a soundtrack that complements the visuals in a way that makes you notice, then forget you’re noticing because you’re having too much fun.

## Narrative, Setting, and World-Building: A War-Torn Paris with Personality

Saboteur’s story isn’t a sprawling epic; it’s a tight, street-level arc about taking back a city block by block. The setting gives you a sense of place—Paris as a living, breathing character with its own rhythm, its own people, and its own set of vendettas. Sean Devlin, as a protagonist, doesn’t strive for the moral high ground; he marches through danger with a grin and a quip, which can feel exhilarating and a little reckless in equal measure. The writing toe-taps the line between serious wartime drama and pulpy escapism, a balance that may not be for everyone but suits the game’s tone well enough to earn sneers and cheers in equal measure.

The mission variety helps this aspect shine. You’ll sabotage supply lines, plant explosives, escort NPCs, and disrupt enemy operations in ways that tie directly into the city’s liberation arc. The more you complete, the more the streets brighten, the more people stroll the boulevards, and the more you feel you’re contributing to a sustainable, if imperfect, liberation. It’s not the most nuanced narrative you’ll encounter in an open-world game, but it’s sincere and affectionate toward its own ridiculous, joyous premise.

## Technical Performance on Xbox 360: A Mixed Bag That Still Sings in Color

On the Xbox 360, Saboteur runs with a performance profile that’s as varied as the color palette itself. In quieter moments, you get stable frame rates, crisp textures for the era, and a sense that the hardware is doing something thoughtful with lighting. In more explosive sequences, you’ll notice frame dips and occasional texture pop-ins. It’s not a disaster; it’s a reminder that the era’s hardware was flexible, not infallible. Load times are noticeable, especially when moving between districts or loading into new missions, but they never cross into “unplayable” territory. The save system is forgiving enough to keep you from losing precious progress, though you’ll occasionally get caught in a checkpoint loop if you’re not careful about your route choices.

The Complete Edition concept helps in this regard: patches and content updates would ideally smooth out some of the rough edges—the balancing of enemy spawns, the unpredictability of chase sequences, and the occasional camera wobble that makes you question your life choices when you’re mid-roof-dash. If you’re chasing longevity, this is the kind of game that benefits from a curated package that irons out the quirks and preserves the things that work—fast transport, punchy confrontations, and the city’s radiant, liberating color.

## Content, Pacing, and Longevity: How Much of a Meal Is This?

Saboteur’s pacing is best described as brisk and snackable. The city invites you to roam, but you’ll find yourself chasing a manageable stack of main missions interspersed with side activities. The side tasks aren’t revolutionary: they vary from car races to rescue operations to tag-and-taint missions that force you to act with immediate decisions under pressure. The variety helps prevent mission fatigue early on, but there’s a natural ceiling to how much you can experience before the game starts recycling set-pieces and textures. That’s not a fatal flaw; it’s a reminder that Saboteur is a game that thrives on momentum rather than grandiose world-building.

A Complete Edition would, in theory, house every patch and DLC, thereby smoothing out pacing and expanding late-game options. In practice, you’ll likely appreciate the clean arc of the main campaign and the way color returns in waves as you liberate more of the city. After the mid-game, the map opens up enough to feel like you’ve earned the privilege of truly exploring; after the late-game, you’ll feel that you’ve seen most of what the experience wants to offer, and the rest is a variable-sum of your own curiosity.

## Comparisons: Where Saboteur Stands in Its Company

If you’ve played other action-adventure titles from the late 2000s, Saboteur sits somewhere between the bold color experiments of a game like Prince of Persia: The Two Thrones and the chaotic, mission-driven open worlds of GTA clones of the era. It doesn’t attempt to out-GTA GTA; instead, it offers a distinct blend: a stealth-action pulse mixed with driving sequences, all under a narrative voice that’s a touch more noir than neon. The result is a game that doesn’t outrun its own ambition—it's better when it sticks to its strengths and avoids trying to be five games at once.

In the end, Saboteur’s strongest competitor isn’t another title; it’s real-life expectations for what a wartime action-adventure could be. It’s a stylish, ambitious exercise in mood, color, and kinetic sequences. If you’re in the mood for something that’s more cinematic than sim, more daring than procedural, it’s worth your time. If you’re seeking a flawless shooter with a deep stealth system and sprawling branching narratives, you may want to temper your expectations.

## Verdict: A Bold, Imperfect Gem That Glows When It Gives Its All

Saboteur on Xbox 360 is a game with a voice and a vision. It’s not perfect: the combat doesn’t always land with the weight it promises, the open-world structure can lean on repetition, and the technical edges show their ages in places. But its ambition shines through in the best moments—when the city blooms with color, when Sean fires a quip with perfect timing, and when you realize you’ve driven through a Nazi blockade and found a café where life continues as if a war hadn’t disrupted it. The Complete Edition idea feels earned in spirit because the game rewards you for sticking with the city’s arc long enough to see the color fill the map and the streets breathe again.

If you love stylish action-adventure games with a rebellious streak and a penchant for visual storytelling, Saboteur is a strong recommendation on Xbox 360. It’s not the best of its era—far from it—but it’s a game that invites you to lean into its personality and enjoy the ride, even if the wheels occasionally squeak. Do not expect a flawless open world; do expect a city that responds to your actions with color and a bravado that makes you grin as you swerve past a line of guards with a triumphant one-liner.

## Final Recommendation

- If you’re chasing a compact, stylish action-adventure with a distinctive visual fingerprint and a driving loop that keeps you on your toes, Saboteur is worth a first or second pass on the Xbox 360.
- If you demand near-perfect pacing and flawless stealth mechanics, you may find the game’s rough edges more distracting than endearing.
- If you’re specifically hunting for a “Complete Edition” experience, treat this review as a blueprint: the ideal edition bundles all patches and DLC in a way that smooths pacing, tightens combat, and preserves the city’s luminous color story without sacrificing the swagger that makes Saboteur distinct.
