## Battery health and runtime expectations

Runtime is the big question with any UPS review, especially in an open-box scenario. When you’re looking at a 3 kVA unit like the SRT3000RMXLT, you’re in the territory where a few minutes of battery life can be the difference between a graceful shutdown and a frantic scramble for a generator that is not laughing at your cables.

In practice, you should expect a few distinct regimes:

- Light load (~20-30%): roughly 10-15 minutes of runtime, maybe more if you optimize ECO mode and power factor within your load profile.
- Moderate load (~50%): 5-8 minutes of runtime is a sensible expectation for older batteries or units that have not recently had a battery health check.
- Heavy load (~80-100%): 2-4 minutes at best; this is where you want the UPS to do its job and keep your OS from screaming in the middle of a software update.

Battery health is the wild card in open-box purchases. If you can, start with a battery health test from the LCD or via the APC software. Look for the battery age, whether there are any alarms reported, and the expected runtime numbers. If you find a battery health score that’s questionable, consider replacing the battery module or negotiating a discount with the seller. In many cases, you’ll find the open-box price offsets the need to immediately replace the battery, which is perfect for a budget-minded lab.

## Open box caveats and best practices

Let’s be real for a moment. Open box means the unit could be cosmetically pristine or a little roughed up. Here are concrete checks to perform before you declare the deal delicious:

- Battery health check: Run the battery test from the LCD or APC software. If the test reports “Replace battery,” you’ve got a choice to make this week rather than a crisis next quarter.
- Visual inspection: Look for dents, loose screws, or obvious wear on the rack ears. Cosmetic marks are okay; structural issues aren’t.
- Firmware version: Check the latest firmware and update if possible. Sometimes open-box units ship with older firmware that can cause compatibility quirks with modern servers.
- Cable sanity: Ensure you have the correct 208V cabling and that there are no missing connectors from the kit. Missing screws are not a sign that you’re a pirate, but they do reduce the unit’s rack integrity.
- Warranty and return policy: Confirm the store’s return policy on open-box units and what warranty you’re getting with the hardware. It can be a nice safety net if something goes sideways after the initial boot.

If all the checks come back clean, you’ve probably landed a strong deal that pays for itself in reliability and a fraction of the price of a shiny, brand-new SKU. If you find a red flag—battery age well above typical life expectancy, a dinged chassis that won’t clean up, or a firmware that refuses to update—you still might be able to negotiate a discount or a service option that suits your risk tolerance.
