## Phase 10 1992: The New Condition Edition at a Glance
New Condition is a fun label that dealers and collectors use to signal that a deck is unused or barely used, with pristine corners and a header card that isn’t yellowed by time. ### What’s in the box (in broad strokes)
- A full deck of Phase 10 cards, typically including multiple copies of 1–12, along with a handful of special cards (passes, wilds, and skips depending on the print).
- A rulebook that pretends to be concise but is actually a tiny novella about phase strategy, with examples that require a pencil and a napkin to track.
- A fold-out guide to the ten phases, which is basically a mini-tuzzle you’ll refer back to while you argue about solid color runs.

## Rules: A Brisk Rehash with a 1992 Flavor
If you already know Phase 10, skim this section to catch the quips; if you’re new, here’s the long and short of it. The vertex of Phase 10 is a phase sheet: players must complete ten specific patterns to advance. The 1992 rules edition doesn’t change this core, but it does lean into the clarity of presentation. Expect the following from the New Condition deck:

- A hand is typically drawn and players discard, building towards the objective.
- Phases might include sets (e.g., two triplets of the same rank), runs (a sequence of five consecutive numbers, usually in one color), and mixed variations.
- Some cards alter turns (e.g., Skip cards) or wild-card-like behaviors depending on how your group interprets them.
- The first player to complete all ten phases wins.

### The ten phases (in 1992 order, for nostalgia’s sake)
1. Two sets of 3
2. One set of 3 + One run of 4
3. One set of 4 + One run of 4
4. One run of 7
5. One run of 8
6. One run of 9
7. Two sets of 4
8. One set of 5 + One set of 2
9. One set of 2 + One run of 6
10. One run of 5 + One run of 5

If you’re thinking, “That sounds like a lot of math to spin while cards slide around the table like slippery donuts,” you’re absolutely right. That’s the charm: you’re constantly balancing immediate hand value against the looming pressure of the current phase. You’ll edge toward the finish line with a flourish, miss the finish line with dramatic flair, and have a friend whispering “just one more card” as if you’re a casino armchair gambler. The New Condition edition gives you crisp card visibility—an important factor when the table lights get low and the chatter grows louder than your attempted plan.

### Turn structure and strategy tips
- Draw: Take the top card from the deck or the top card of the discard pile. Smart players weigh the risk of breaking a current run for a potential future gain.
- Play: Lay down your completed phase cards when you actually achieve the phase, not the moment you think you’ve nearly got it. The glory is in the timing, not in the brag before you claim the phase.
- End turn: Discard a card and pass the turn. Pay attention to the set distributions on the table; if someone is in a position to nudge toward a particular phase, you might want to derail your own plans to prevent them from exploiting your discard.

In 1992 fashion, the New Condition print makes it easier to read the board without tilting your head toward a light source. That light-to-dark transition is your friend when someone shuffles a face-down stack into a full-blown block party of cards on the table. If you’re a purist, you’ll argue that the 1992 deck doesn’t need fancy rules clarifications—it just needs enough confidence in a player’s competence to avoid that one, infuriating moment where the discard pile is your destiny and your fate simultaneously.

## Practical tips for picking up a 1992 New Condition Phase 10 today
- Inspect the box: Check corners and edges for wear. A pristine exterior is a good sign that the deck was stored carefully, but more importantly, confirm there are no major creases that might reveal a bent card under the spectacle of a long game.
- Check the cards: Look for glare-free faces, ensure colors are vibrant, and verify there aren’t any duplicate misprints that could throw off your phase display.
- Check for complete contents: Confirm that all the phase reference sheets and any rule inserts you expect are present. Sometimes a historical edition may omit a page or two; it’s part of the collector’s journey to verify how complete your copy is.
- Consider sleeves: If you plan to play a lot, a light set of protective sleeves can prevent wear and make shuffling easier, especially for the 1992 stock.
- Storage: A simple box insert or pouch will help prevent the cards from warping in a damp basement. Phase 10 cards, like many classic games, survive best when you keep them dry and flat.
