## What’s in the box

Saboteur 20th Anniversary includes a deck of treasure cards, role cards, tunnel cards, action cards, and a fresh set of illustrated dwarf tokens. The art has been facelifted for a modern light, embracing bolder colors and clearer icons that spare you the suspense of misreading a map in the dark—though you’ll still want to squint at 3 a.m. when you’re negotiating with a Replicant-Rock Gnome who swears he’s just painting the shaft. The components are sturdy and well-suited to boisterous table play: thick card stock, glossy gold tokens that clack satisfyingly when someone quietly pockets a treasure, and a compact rulebook that manages the rare feat of being concise and funny at the same time. 

The edition is designed for 2–12 players and about 30 minutes per game, which means you can either squeeze in a few rounds of head-to-head mine warfare or use it as the “quick chaotic party game” between longer campaigns. The 2-player mode exists, but Saboteur shines when the majority of the table is alternately grumbling about a traitor and cheering at a perfectly timed gold haul. If you’ve played the original Saboteur, you’ll recognize the core bones: hidden roles, copper-colored coins, and a race to claim treasure while someone in the group is secretly sabotaging the team. If you haven’t, congratulations—welcome to a family-friendly PvP-lite where trust evaporates faster than a pickaxe in warm lava. 

## How to play at a glance

### Setup
In a typical 20th Anniversary session, players draw role cards that determine who is a Miner (team player) and who is a Saboteur (the wolf in dwarven beard gel). The number of Saboteurs depends on the player count, ensuring at least one traitor in most games and perhaps a second one if you’re feeling spicy. Each player receives a starting hand of action cards and a few tunnel cards to start their underground adventure. The box includes a short cheat sheet with key terms and action card effects—great for teaching. A single round revolves around constructing a path of tunnels to reach treasure goals while opponents try to derail the tunnel’s progress with treachery and cunning. 

### Turn structure
On your turn you will typically do one of the following: play a tunnel card to extend the mine, place a role-based action card to influence the line-up, or draw more cards to replenish your hand. The objective is simple in concept: guide the group toward treasure while obscuring the actual path from the others. However, the mechanics reward clever deception and careful timing. A Saboteur can block, misdirect, or convert a path with a well-timed action card course-correcting the route. A Miner, on the other hand, must cooperate (kind of) to lay down a viable tunnel while watching for telltale signs of sabotage and glitzy gold temptations. 

### Endgame
The round ends when a treasure is reached or when the mine collapses under the weight of too many misguided decisions. Then players score points based on who claims treasure without getting sabotaged too badly. The scorekeeping is straightforward, and the game’s pace encourages quick rounds that feel like a rapid-fire duel of wits and might.

## Why Saboteur works as a social experience

### Hidden roles and trust dynamics
Saboteur thrives on the tension of hidden information. In every game, someone is secretly plotting against the team, and the fun comes from watching players read micro-expressions, deduce who’s got the “I want to win but also want to be loved” vibe, and misdirect with a perfectly timed Wall or Sabotage action card. The 20th Anniversary edition preserves this core experience while presenting it with crisp art and intuitive mechanics. Expect heated debates about whether that path should go left or right, punctuated by a chorus of “how did you know I did that?” moments. 

### Short but satisfying rounds
30-minute rounds are a sweet spot for party nights. You get enough tension for a story to emerge without the night spiraling into a multi-arc campaign you can’t back out of before midnight. The pacing supports a good volume of rounds in a single session, which means a well-meaning gaming night can morph from a “we’ll just try a few” to a “we’ve been at the table for six hours and somehow still have half the treasures left.” The 20th Anniversary edition doesn’t overstay its welcome, and that’s a critical factor for a game designed to be played in bursts rather than marathon sessions. 

## Strategy and tips for victory (even if you’re the Saboteur)

### For Miners: how to survive the chaos
- Communicate carefully, but not too much. Share information about safe tunnels, but never reveal all your cards or you’ll end up with a table full of conspirators.
- Watch for over-eagerness. If someone is rapidly placing tunnel cards in a straight line toward treasure, they might be a Saboteur attempting to lock you in.
- Use your action cards to protect weak points and keep the treasure’s path open long enough to reach the endpoint, but don’t forget that some “protection” cards only apply to your own path, inadvertently helping the saboteur in disguise.
- Remember: the path is the goal, not the glory. Focus on building a route that makes sense and force others to reveal their alignments under pressure.

### For Saboteurs: tricks of the trade
- Mix timing with uncertainty. Your best moves often happen just when players believe they’re on the right track. A well-placed sabotage can derail a perfect route and trigger a counterreaction that reveals loyalties.
- Play for misdirection. If you can cast doubt on a specific tunnel or route, you’ll buy valuable rounds of confusion that benefit you and your team’s enemies inadvertently.
- Use decline-and-reveal moments. If your cover’s about to be blown, you can pivot toward a counterfeit treasure path that delays the moment of truth and buys you critical seconds.
- Be mindful of group size. In larger games, misdirection can be more effective because more players will misinterpret the signals, leading to glorious chaos that’s academically delightful to observe.

### Group dynamics and teaching moments
The beauty of Saboteur 20th Anniversary is that new players can jump in quickly, but experienced players still feel the thrill of a carefully orchestrated plan unspooling. As a moderator or host, you’ll be juggling instruction, suspicions, and delightfully poor puns about picking up the “miner’s pick” as if it were a award-winning prop. The 20th Anniversary edition is especially forgiving to newcomers, with a helpful rulebook and clear boards that reduce pre-game confusion. Expect lots of energetic table talk, friendly rivalry, and occasional shouts of “I knew it!” that are more entertaining than any tournament drama you’d find on a reality show.
