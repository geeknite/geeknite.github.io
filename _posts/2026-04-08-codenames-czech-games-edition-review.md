## Introduction: What is Codenames, and why does it work as a party game?
Codenames is a word liaison game that pitches two teams against each other in a linguistic tug-of-war. Each team has a spymaster who gives one-word clues that point to several words on the table. The goal? Identify all your team’s words before the other team does. The catch is that the table is a grid of words; some belong to your team, some belong to the opposing team, some are harmless innocents, and one is the dreaded assassin. A good clue can crash through a wall of neutral words and land right on your team’s remaining targets. A bad clue can turn your win into a disaster in the blink of an eye, especially in a crowded living room with a clock ticking and a dog wondering why there’s so much shouting in his house.

From a design perspective, Codenames nails the magic formula: simple setup, quick rounds, and a clever constraint that makes every turn feel meaningful. It scales from party-night chaos to tense, strategic scrums in a way that few games manage—this is what has kept Codenames popular for more than a decade. ## Setup and flow: getting from box to table in under 5 minutes
Codenames is a ritual in itself. The entrance barrier is minimal, but the payoff is maximal for a board game night. Here’s a typical flow:
1) Shuffle the word cards and lay out 25 cards in a 5x5 grid. The layout is the stage, the words are actors, and the performance begins as soon as a team is chosen.
2) Randomly select a key card to determine which words belong to red or blue, and which words are neutral or assassin. The spies know their own codes; the other team’s knowledge is intentionally foggy, which is where the fun begins.
3) The red or blue spymaster (or the faction you’re playing as) looks at their key card and, without revealing it to the other players, gives a one-word clue plus a number. The clue should connect as many of their own words as possible without hitting the other team’s words or the assassin.
4) The team then guesses up to the clue’s number plus one extra guess if they think they’ve cracked it—taking risks is part of the appeal. If they identify a word belonging to their color, it’s covered with that team’s marker. If they hit an opponent’s word, that word is snared by the other team, moving the game along in a way that can feel brutally fair.
5) The round continues with alternating spymasters attempting to outmaneuver one another until one team hits all their words or the assassin is revealed.

Setup is refreshingly quick, which means you can keep the energy high without spending half the evening on logistics. The real skill is in the clue-giving—learning to balance breadth and precision, and to sense when a safe-but-slightly-risky clue could pay off in a big way.

## Strategy: tips for spymasters and guessers alike
If you’re aiming to elevate your game beyond “randomly shout a word,” consider these practical strategies:
- For spymasters:
  - Start with broad connections but beware of bordering on the other team’s words. If you can hint at two of your words while avoiding any chance at the other team’s targets, you’re ahead of the curve.
  - Use color-coded or thematically linked clues sparingly. A one-word clue that nails three or four words is a jackpot, but it’s also a red flag if it could point to the opponent’s words or the assassin.
  - If you feel the table’s energy sliding toward chaos, switch to smaller clues. A safer, more precise clue helps regain control and keeps the game moving.
- For guessers:
  - Focus on the shared story your team is telling. The best clues often tie together two or three words you wouldn’t expect to be closely related.
  - Don’t over-guess. You only have a finite number of attempts; the risk of hitting the assassin or your opponents’ words increases with every extra guess.
  - Communicate with your team. A great group can use a hint that seems awkward at first glance but proves to be perfectly aligned after a moment of discussion.

The CGE edition’s clarity aids this process. The box design and card layout minimize confusion, allowing groups to jump into a flow where clever connections carry the game rather than stumbling over misinterpreted rules.
