## What’s in the box and how it works

Two core ideas drive Throw Throw Burrito: set collection and dodgeball chaos. You’ll be chasing sets of cards that earn you points, while the threat of a flying burrito (or two) becomes a recurring hazard that can instantly flip the board game into a comedy reel.

- Card deck: A rotating mix of sets, threats, and action cards. The cards encourage you to form matching pairs or triplets that score points, and they carry instructions that can force you to take a risk by drawing from the center or initiating a dodgeball moment. 
- Plush burritos: The soft projectiles that fuel the chaos. You will throw them, dodge them, and occasionally catch the burrito as a dramatic save-your-sanity move. They are designed to be gentle enough for a friendly bounce yet robust enough to survive the occasional dramatic tumble to the floor.
- Score tokens and tracking: Small chips or token discs help you count your points and identify who is in the lead as the rounds roll on. The scoring system is intentionally straightforward, but the chaos around it makes maintaining control feel like juggling burritos while wearing oven mitts.
- Quick start guide: The rule sheet gets you up to speed quickly. Don’t worry if you have never played a fusion of dodgeball and card collecting; this thing wants you to play, not study.

## How to play: the core loop

The core loop of Throw Throw Burrito is simple on a first read and brilliantly chaotic in practice. On your turn, you draw a card. If it’s a set card, you begin working toward forming a matching collection. If it’s a dodgeball/throw command card, a burrito throw event will occur, triggering a moment of chaos that often becomes the highlight of the session.

- Draw and resolve: On your turn, draw a card and resolve its effect. Many cards push you toward forming a set; others push you toward initiating a throw event with a burrito duel.
- The burrito phase: When a throw event card is played, players stop the strategic planning for a moment and engage in burrito tossing. The goal is to tag opponents by throwing one of the plush burritos. A successful tag often shakes up the scoring, sometimes awarding bonus points for the hit. The safety mechanics are built in; you only throw under controlled conditions and in a way that preserves social contracts around friendly play.
- End of round: The round ends when a certain score threshold is reached or when players decide to call it a night. The endgame is a race toward a target score, but in reality, the end is often determined by how many times you can hit your friends with a burrito in-between roaring laughter.

The rules are intentionally light. The idea is to lower friction so people can jump in and start playing in minutes. If you want to dive deeper into a more formal breakdown, you could look at standards elsewhere, but the real joy lies in how quickly the chaos unfolds and how the group evolves its own micro-rules on the fly.

## Strategy and tips: playing to your strengths

While the core loop is simple, there are several ways to optimize your experience without turning it into a math test. Here are some practical tips that can help you tilt the odds in your favor while keeping the chaos intact:

- Read the room before you throw: The best burrito shots come from a moment of calm observation. If you time your throw when someone is distracted by a particularly ridiculous card, you’ll maximize your chances of hitting a big target and getting a points payoff.
- Use misdirection: You don’t always want to be the person who throws. If your buddy is about to secure a second of a kind, you can opt to hold your throw for a moment to disrupt their momentum. This creates reactive play that’s not purely about accuracy but about timing and psychology.
- Balance aggression with care: The game is designed to be chaotic and fun, but it’s also messy. Keep safety as a priority. If someone is not comfortable with being hit, pivot to a non-contact action or a gentler throw. The goal is shared laughter, not real frustration.
- Team moments: In larger groups, pair up to share insights on which sets are easiest to complete. While it’s a competitive game, there’s often room for light negotiation or lighthearted alliances that dissolve as quickly as they form.
- Shorten or extend play: If you have a shorter night, you can speed things up by setting a lower target score. For longer sessions, you can stretch it out by allowing more rounds and weaving in side challenges that involve the burritos in new ways.
