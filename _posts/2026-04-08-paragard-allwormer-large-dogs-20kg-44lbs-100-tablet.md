- Frequency: Some all-wormers are monthly, others quarterly or per a veterinarian’s schedule. The information on the package and the leaflet tells you precisely how often to re-administer.

## Safety, Side Effects, and Warnings
Dewormers are generally safe when used as directed, but side effects can occur. In dogs, common mild side effects include:
- Temporary digestive upset (vomiting, diarrhea, or soft stools)
- Lethargy or decreased appetite in rare cases
- Scratching or mild skin irritation around the mouth if a pill is chewed and causes a reaction

If you notice more severe symptoms—persistent vomiting, severe diarrhea, tremors, ataxia, pale gums, or any signs of an allergic reaction—seek veterinary care immediately. Remember: this is a medication, and with meds comes the potential for adverse reactions, especially if your dog has underlying health issues or is taking other drugs.

Always store tablets in a cool, dry place away from children and pets who might want to join the party. And keep your receipt; you never know when you’ll need to price-check your quest against an alternate route of purchase.

## Customer Questions and Myths Debunked
- Myth: Dewormers instantly eradicate every worm type you’ll ever meet.
  - Reality: They’re effective against many common species when used correctly, but reinfection and exposure risk mean ongoing monitoring and proper sanitation are part of the fight.
- Myth: A single dose lasts forever.

- Myth: If the dog is healthy now, there’s no need to deworm.
  - Reality: Worm infections can be asymptomatic for a long time. Prevention equals better long-term health, especially in multi-pet households or dog parks where greeting a lot of furry friends is a daily event.
