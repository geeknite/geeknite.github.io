### How to play in a hurry: setup and core loop
As with the traditional Clue experience, the goal is to determine the murderer, the weapon, and the location. The 1949 edition preserves the core flow:

1) Setup: each player receives a detective sheet, takes a corresponding token, and the deck is shuffled. The suspect, weapon, and room cards are drawn to create the secret envelope contents. The rest of the cards are distributed according to the rules in the manual.
2) Movement: players roll dice to move their tokens along the board, passing through rooms where they can collect hints. The movement system introduces a little tension as you navigate the mansion’s floor plan to gather clues without giving away your own thinking to the other investigators.
3) Clue gathering: you pick up clue cards that hint at which cards are in the envelope. These clues are meant to steer conversation and discovery without outright spoiling the ending for others.
4) Accusation: if you think you have solved the mystery, you may make a formal accusation. If you are correct, you win. If you are wrong, you are out of the detective game for the rest of the round, which can be hilariously embarrassing or downright dramatic depending on your group dynamic.

The pace is a rhythm: slow build, a few dramatic revelations, a race against time as players chase down a final, satisfying reveal. In the modern gaming world where time is a currency and attention spans are a commodity, the 1949 cadence feels almost quaint, but it is also wonderfully social. You will hear dramatic gasps, mock denials, and an optimistic chorus of the table claiming they were just about to deduce the truth before someone else shouted the answer first.
