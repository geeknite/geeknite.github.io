## Card Basics: What Makes This Variant Tick
### Card Number, Set, and Rarity
Gloom 109/108 AR sits in SV3’s exclusive AR subset, a designation that instantly adds a few extra commas to your thought process when you see the price tag. In a world where PSA 10s can fetch the GDP of a small country, an AR is the gleaming rumor you chase after your third espresso. The SV3 set launched in Japan with a promise: “If you didn’t gasp at the art, we’ll repaint the gasp for you.” 109/108 places this Gloom just about the right side of the chase curve, balancing rarity with the chance you’ll actually use it in a deck or display it behind glass like a tiny, moody sun.

### Alternate Art: What AR Brings to the Table
AR stands for Alternate Art, which means this Gloom doesn’t just tell you it’s a gloom monster; it yells, “I am the gloom, but I glow. And yes, I have a crown.” The Japanese SV3 AR treatment often emphasizes rich contrasts, a darker palette, and frame alterations that set this card apart from its rainbow-foiled cousins. The black flame effect (a nod to the card’s title) runs along the border, weaving into Gloom’s figure with a sinister elegance. If you’re into the kissing cousin of fancy—it’s the art style equivalent of wearing velvet in a thunderstorm.

### Artwork and Text: The Japanese Flair
The artwork leans heavily into a regal, almost tyrannical vibe. Gloom crouches among black flame wisps, with neon reds and a smoky purple haze that makes you feel you’ve stepped into a samurai fantasy while waiting for a latte. The Japanese text on the card means fewer global players will claim it’s “easy to read,” but that’s part of the charm: the language barrier becomes another cosmetic layer, a reminder that this is a piece of culture as much as a card.

The flavor text on SV3 ARs often hints at deeper lore, and this card is no exception. The Ruler of the Black Flame suggests a backstory where Gloom inherits a throne of embers, with Vileplume and Bellossom as its bureaucrats, all while a chorus of Sneasel cycled through the gallery like a night market DJ. It’s silly, it’s dramatic, and it’s exactly the kind of lore that card nerds will argue about during a midnight stream.

## Artwork: A Deep Dive into the Design Choices
### Color Theory and Composition
The color palette leans into the noir spectrum—deep blacks, charcoal shadows, and flame accents that pop against the otherwise muted background. The artist uses a cool blue-violet light quality on Gloom’s face to create a contrast with the warm flame—an intentional juxtaposition that mirrors the tension between gloom and glory. The border is slim but metallic, with a subtle gradient that catches the light just enough to feel expensive without looking gaudy.

### Texturing and Effects
If you look closely, the black flame threads weave around Gloom’s silhouette like a cauldron of secrets. The glow is not just decorative; it interacts with the card’s edges in a way that makes the whole card look as if it’s humming. Some collectors have used the term “living card” in jest, but it’s not far from the truth: the AR treatment makes the imagery feel dynamic, even in static form. The texture on the flames is a tactile illusion—your brain tells you there’s heat where there isn’t, which is a mild safety hazard for those who enjoy leaning their sleeves on the card while they playtest.

### Japanese Typography and Aesthetics
The kanji and kana choices on the card add a layer of elegance. It’s not just translation; it’s calligraphy with swords. The text layout guides your eye along the flame’s arc, emphasizing the sense of hierarchy: the Gloom figure dominates the foreground, the flame roosts behind, and the flavor text settles in like a whisper of prophecy. For the collector who loves a narrative, this is where the card stops being a mere object and becomes a compact myth.
