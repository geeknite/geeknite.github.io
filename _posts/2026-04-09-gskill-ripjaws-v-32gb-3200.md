### Installation notes
If you’re assembling a fresh PC, slap these into the motherboard slots labeled DDR4_1 and DDR4_2 (or the two primary slots your motherboard manual recommends for dual-channel operation). On most boards, enabling XMP in the BIOS will automatically set the rated 3200 CL16 timings. If you’re on a stricter manual tweak path, you’ll want to set: 3200 MT/s, CL16, tRCD 18, tRP 18, tRAS 38, and keep DRAM voltage at 1.35V as a baseline. Your mileage may vary depending on the silicon lottery and your motherboard’s IMC quality.

Here’s a quick install checklist:
- Ensure your CPU cooler isn’t touching the DIMMs.
- Check for BIOS updates to improve memory compatibility.
- Enable XMP once installed, then run stability tests.
- If instability arises, try reseating the modules or adjusting the voltage within safe ranges.

## Compatibility and caveats

### Intel platforms
With modern Intel boards (600- and 700-series), 3200 CL16 is a sweet spot for gaming and productivity. It usually picks up the XMP profile without a fuss, and a lot of motherboards have strong IMCs that handle 32GB in two 16GB sticks gracefully. If you’re pairing with a high-end CPU, you’ll likely see the biggest gains in multitasking and memory-heavy workloads.

### AMD platforms
On AMD builds with Zen 2/3/4 architecture, 32GB DDR4-3200 CL16 is a very sane choice for a balance of speed and capacity. As long as your motherboard supports 3200+, you’ll be fine. For those chasing every last FPS in rasterized games or who want to maximize memory-heavy workloads, this kit will be a reliable backbone.

### Things to watch out for
- Some budget boards or extremely tight BIOS slots can require manual tweaking to stabilize 32GB in dual-channel. If you encounter POST or boot issues, step down to a compatible voltage and loosen timings slightly.
- If you’re running on a very old platform, ensure your BIOS is up to date; some early DDR4 boards have quirks with larger memory kits.

## INSTALLATION GEEK-OUT: tips and tricks

- Always back up your BIOS before making changes. It sounds dramatic, but it’s the responsible thing to do.
- When enabling XMP, run at least a quick stability test with your favorite benchmarks or a few rounds of Prime95/IntelBurnTest (or your go-to stress tester). If it crashes, back off a notch and test again.
- If you’re upgrading from a smaller kit, run a clean install for best results. If you’re reusing an older motherboard, clearing CMOS can help avoid odd boot issues.
- Don’t forget to monitor RAM temperatures after long gaming sessions. The RipJaws V sticks stay relatively cool, but a good airflow path always helps.
