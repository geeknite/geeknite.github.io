## What is the Express 10-Person Workstation with Screen?
The Express 10P with Screen is not just a bigger desk. It is a modular, plug-and-play ecosystem designed for teams that need shared data visibility without leaning into a wall of monitors. Each station has its own 24 to 27 inch display depending on configuration, an adjustable monitor arm, and a personal work surface that includes integrated power and data ports. Central to the system is a power/data backbone that runs beneath the benches, with modular drop-downs for network switches, conference call hubs, and the occasional coffee machine how-to-teleportation module (okay not an actual coffee machine, but the dream lingers).

The configuration is what sets this apart. Rather than a single oversized, ungainly desk, you get a grid of pods connected by a shared trough for cables and airflow. Most teams will have ten dedicated workstations aligned along a central spine, but you can reconfigure into 5 x 2, 4 x 3 with one spare, or a long bench for cross-disciplinary sprints. The screens at each station are not just decorative; they function as a personal window to cloud desktops, dashboards, and collaboration apps, all while maintaining the ability to mirror one station for pair programming sessions or to present a live demo to the whole room.

If you are used to the traditional open-plan office, you might wonder how this affects collaboration. The Express 10P with Screen leans into a hybrid approach: you can glance at someone’s screen with minimal neck strain, but you’re not forced into constant side-by-side eyeballing. It is a delicate balance between shared awareness and personal space, a little like pairing a robot vacuum with a Roomba and asking them to share a living room without tripping over the charging dock.

## Features and Technical Deep Dive
### Display configuration and ergonomics
Each workstation comes with a monitor that can be adjusted for height, tilt, and swivel. The goal is to achieve a neutral neck posture for a majority of users. In practice, this means the top of the screen sits roughly at eye level when the user is seated with feet flat on the floor. For taller users, the arms are adjustable, allowing for comfortable line-of-sight without the standard desk neck ache. The built-in displays also support daisy-chaining or remote display mirroring for collaborative sessions where two researchers need to share what’s on their screen without crowding around a single desk.

A feature that deserves praise is the integrated USB-C hub and power distribution. This means fewer wall outlets required across the floor and fewer collapsed power strips on the desk. USB-C pass-through can deliver both power and data up to 100W per station in some configurations, which is enough to keep laptops charged during a long sprint. For teams requiring more, you can always plug in a docking station or a wireless keyboard/mouse payload to a shared USB hub so no one fights for peripheral space at the end of a demo.

### Processing power and network backbones
The Express 10P system is designed as a hub-and-spoke network. The backbone includes a switch cluster, a central NAS or cloud docking device, and optional collaboration tools like a dedicated conferencing unit with echo cancellation. This isn’t just a display wall. It’s a distributed computing environment that allows each user to connect to their preferred cloud workspace while still sharing a common screen for demonstrations or reviews. In some configurations, teams run light local apps on edge devices, while the heavier computations live in the cloud or on a central server. The result is a hybrid approach that reduces latency for critical tasks and simplifies the IT footprint—one footprint, many possibilities.

### Cable management and acoustics
The cable troughs keep cables out of sight and out of the way, but they also act as a guide to reduce tangling and tangled thoughts. Detachable panels enable quick changes when teams pivot from software development to data analytics or to a teaching mode for training sessions. Acoustic dividers, when installed, add a surprising amount of quiet to the space. They aren’t soundproof walls, but they are enough to reduce the loudest chatter and the clatter of keyboards at action-packed standups.

### Docking, peripherals, and compatibility
The system is designed to be hardware-agnostic to a reasonable degree. For example, if your team uses a particular brand of keyboard or a specific mouse setup, you can attach your favorite peripherals without the fear of docking compatibility issues. The central hub supports standard interfaces (USB-C, HDMI, DisplayPort) and many enterprise configurations offer a secure boot and device management profile to keep the workspace compliant with IT policies. For teams who prefer paperless demos or live coding, there is a built-in screen sharing workflow that can be activated with a single button press on the conference hub.

### Software and collaboration workflow
What makes the 10P really compelling is how it integrates with collaboration tools. You can mirror a presenter’s screen onto all workstations for a consistent demonstration, or you can route specific screens to dedicated “focus zones” when someone needs undisturbed time. The management software allows administrators to assign display layouts, configure power profiles, and push software updates to all screens with minimal disruption. It’s not magic, but it is pretty close when you’re coordinating a team of ten people who sometimes forget to save their work.

## Setup, Installation, and Day One Realities
Setting up the Express 10P is a three-act play: unpackagerays, anchor the backbone, and align the stations. The initial unboxing is straightforward; the parts are well-labeled and there’s a simple instruction guide that won’t require a doctorate in mechanical engineering to decipher. The backbone is mounted along the central spine, with the displays and desks slotted into place. The most time-consuming part is routing cables and ensuring the height adjustments line up with user seating. Expect a dedicated installation window, especially if you are working in a space with strict floor plans or unusual ceiling heights.

Power distribution is a highlight here. With a robust power backbone, you’ll be less likely to find the dreaded under-desk outlet crawl. You’ll still have to do some cable planning if your space lacks accessible power sources, but the system is designed to minimize the number of outlets needed while still providing adequate power to all workstations, plus a spare for occasional demos or charging anchors.

The software setup takes a little longer than the hardware. You’ll want to configure the central hub, pair the displays, and ensure the network policies align with your IT standards. It’s a one-time-heavy phase; once configured, the system behaves consistently across days and weeks. If you’re migrating from a more traditional setup, there is a slight learning curve as users adapt to the new workflow patterns. Training sessions and quick-start guides help, and the modular nature of the system means you can stage new features gradually instead of flipping a switch and hoping for the best.

For teams that value speed, the fastest path is to start with a smaller configuration and expand as you confirm needs and budget. If you have a 10-seat team but only 6 desks in your current space, you can deploy the Express 6P variant as an incremental step toward the full 10P. That gradual approach helps reduce risks and keeps the IT department in the loop for long-term capacity planning.
