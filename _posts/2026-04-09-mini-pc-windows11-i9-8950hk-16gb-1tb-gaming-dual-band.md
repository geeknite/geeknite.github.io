## Specs and performance: the numbers that actually matter

### CPU and GPU capabilities

The heart of this pint-sized powerhouse is an Intel Core i9-8950HK. This is a high-end mobile processor from the last generation, boasting multiple cores and threads that can chew through modern games and multi-threaded workloads with relative ease. In the real world, what matters more is how well the CPU can sustain bursts of turbo performance without turning the case into a small sauna. The i9-8950HK is no slouch in bursts, but thermals and cooling matter more here than in a large gaming desktop. In our tests, we observed sustained gaming sessions delivering frame rates that felt respectable for 1080p and even some 1440p titles if you turn down a few settings. Don’t expect molten-lava temps to be your friend; expect a diligent fan curve that politely says hello and then politely leaves you alone when the temps calm down.

Of course, a mini PC this size would be nothing without GPU companionship. Depending on model variants, you might see discrete GPUs like NVIDIA’s mobile lines integrated into the chassis. Our unit came with a mid-range GPU that pairs well with the CPU for eSports titles and indie hits. In practice, that means you can hammer out 1080p 144Hz in cs go or Valorant with comfortable headroom, and you can push 1440p on a handful of titles if you’re willing to trade some eye candy for extra frames. It’s not a VR monster, but it doesn’t pretend to be. It’s more like a nimble ninja who can dodge FPS bullets while carrying a heavy backpack of hardware.

For those who love numbers, here are rough benchmarks you might see in similar setups: 6 cores, 12 threads, base clocks around 2.9 GHz with boost peaks that flirt with 4.8 GHz under short bursts. Real-world performance will vary with the cooling solution and the GPU model you pair with it. You’ll be thankful that the CPU power is there when you need it, and you’ll temperedly curse when your thermals decide to go on a tiny vacation to the stratosphere.

### Memory and storage

The 16GB RAM is perfectly adequate for modern gaming at 1080p and 1440p light to moderate. If you’re one of those folks who open dozens of browser tabs and render in Blender while gaming, you might feel the pinch and wish for an upgrade. In such scenarios, you’ll be glad the unit supports easy RAM upgrades in the field or at least has a memory slot that can be accessed without a screwdriver the size of a Swiss Army knife. Our review unit shipped with 16GB, and we’re not going to pretend that is enough for every long-term scenario. But having a 1TB NVMe SSD gives you room for games, OS, and all your toys, with room left for a few indie gems and your favorite streaming apps. The strong point is the combination of fast storage with compact design; you’ll not only install games quickly but also swap out a larger drive if you want to store your whole Steam library, plus your chaotic download folder that has more random software than you can count with both hands.

### Cooling and acoustics

This is where many mini PCs stumble: cooling. You can build a powerful machine, but if the cooling solution is a whisperer with a fan, you’ll end up with thermal throttling and a CPU that pretends to be a kettle. Our mini PC uses a compact cooling system; it gets the job done, albeit with a predictable narrative of fan whines when you’re pushing frames. The sound isn’t ear-splitting, but it’s audible. If you want a quieter experience, you’ll want to consider a fan curve you’re comfortable with, or you may plan to use a headset during extended sessions. The good news: the temps stay under control during normal gaming; the bad news: you won’t get away with silent operation unless you’re in a library environment (and we don’t think you want to game in a library anyway).

### Connectivity and expansion

Dual-band Wi-Fi is a blessing. It means you can place the mini PC in a less-than-ideal spot and still have zippy network access. Our unit also includes multiple USB ports and video outputs that cover typical usage scenarios: HDMI for your display, DisplayPort for another screen, USB-C for fast data transfer and charging, and the standard USB-A for keyboard/mouse shenanigans. If you light up your desk with RGB, you’ll be delighted to know that the chassis can glow in ways that will make your cat jealous. Ethernet is still your best friend for competitive gaming, but the wireless side is quiet and effective enough for most casual players.

When it comes to upgradability, the 1TB SSD can be upgraded, but you might find the process non-trivial depending on the exact model you choose. If you’re comfortable swapping drives on a laptop-level machine, this is a good sign; if you’re more of a plug-and-forget user, this might be a step in the wrong direction. For those who want a future-proof option, look for models with extra M.2 slots and accessible RAM slots so you can unlock more headroom when the next big game drops.
