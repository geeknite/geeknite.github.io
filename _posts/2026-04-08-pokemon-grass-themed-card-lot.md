## Condition, grading, and how to sort like a sane person

Bulk lots are a test of patience as much as they are a test of collecting instincts. The realistic expectation here is a spectrum of condition: some cards near mint, some showing beginner’s wear, and a few corners bent from homeroom trading sessions gone sideways. Here’s how I recommend you approach sorting and assessment without losing your mind:

- Create three stacks: Near-Mint / Excellent, Good, and Play-Worn. If you want to go deeper, add a rare-foil vs non-foil sub-stack.
- Inspect corners and edges carefully. A light bend at the corner may not ruin playability, but it can affect resale value. If you’re purely collecting, minor edge whitening is often acceptable if the card has sentimental value.
- Check for print blemishes and flatness. Some older prints come with minor misalignment or surface curvature; a good light source can help you spot this. If it’s crisp enough to pass under a binder sleeve, you’re likely fine to keep it in the “Excellent” pile.
- Watch for creases, water damage, or heavy bends. Those are deal-breakers for many collectors. If you find one, set that card aside for a later decision: trade-in, donation, or a creative art project using the card’s back design.
- Holos and foils are always a bit more delicate. If you find one in the lot, separate it early to avoid it getting scratched or nicked during sorting. The goal is to protect the value and beauty of any shiny piece you pull.
- Energy cards can be clutter; decide early if you want to keep a stack for purposes of deck-building, or recycle them into a craft project for the kids’ classroom (yes, I went there with the craft idea).

## Display, care, and long-term preservation

A good display is part science project, part hobbyist art. Here are a few practical tips to help your green stack stand the test of time:

- Use sleeves with solid protection to guard against corner wear and surface scratches. A standard 2.5 mil to 4 mil sleeve is a good baseline.
- For holos or highly valuable cards, consider top loaders or magnetic snap cases for extra protection against bending.
- Keep cards away from direct sunlight to preserve color integrity and prevent fading on older prints.
- Maintain a stable temperature and humidity level to avoid warping or border whitening. A basic climate-friendly room can keep the binders happy.
- Organize by set, then by rarity and condition. It makes future trades and acquisitions easier and far less chaotic than a scavenger hunt through a couch cushion filled with spare chrome dice.
