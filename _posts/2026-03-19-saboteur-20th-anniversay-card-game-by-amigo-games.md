## How to Play in 30 Minutes or Less
Saboteur thrives on short, punchy rounds where decisions must be made quickly and with imperfect information. The 30-minute target is not a guarantee in every setup, but the game is designed to wrap up a satisfying set of rounds before the table grows bored or impatient. Here’s a compact walkthrough to get you from box to bragging rights with minimal drama.

### Setup
1. Shuffle the path cards and form a draw pile. Place the treasure path card face-down at the center to indicate the goal of the tunnel. Depending on player count, a subset of map cards is used to shape the board. The setup encourages improvisation, which is where the game’s charm lives.
2. Shuffle the role cards and deal one to each player. The exact mix varies by player count, but you usually end up with a handful of saboteurs and gold-seekers in the mix. Each player looks at their own card secretly.
3. Shuffle action cards into a separate deck. These are the tools you’ll use to influence the tunnel and your fellow diggers.
4. Distribute tokens and set up the play area. The cards form a little mosaic of tunnels as players place cards. Your goal is simple: guide a path to the treasure while ensuring the route remains as treacherous as possible for others.

### The Turn Structure
- Each round, players take turns placing a path card to extend the tunnel or playing an action card on another player’s piece of the tunnel. The tension grows as the tunnels twist and turn, sometimes opening opportunities that you didn’t realize you were giving away.
- Path building happens with a simple rule: once you lay down a path card, you cannot move it. You can, however, place action cards to alter the state of another player’s section, block a crucial route, or reveal a hidden aspect of someone’s role. The flow of turns can swing quickly as the board tightens around a possible treasure route.
- If a player completes a path to the treasure, the round ends and you tally points. If the path is sabotaged or blocked, the round ends differently, and the table decides who gets rewarded for being cunning or lucky.

### Endgame and Scoring
After several rounds, the tally adds up. The gold-seekers who managed to deliver a clean route to the treasure are rewarded. The saboteurs, on the other hand, win by expertly fracturing the tunnel’s integrity and causing misdirection. In some variations, players may switch roles between rounds to keep the strategy fresh and ensure that no single faction seizes control for too long.

## Strategy and Tactics: How to Win Without Losing Your Friends
Saboteur is less about brute force and more about social dynamics. It rewards a careful blend of deception, timing, and reading your peers. Here are some practical tips for players who want to improve without turning their table into a courtroom.

### For Gold Seekers
- Early information is valuable: try to glean who might be a saboteur by watching where people place their cards and how quickly they react to certain plays.
- Don’t telegraph your goals: even if you’re confident you’re on the path to treasure, avoid shouting it from the tunnels. Reducing obvious tells buys you time to react to others’ moves.
- Build with a plan, but stay flexible: a path that looks perfect on paper may be sabotaged by a single well-timed action card. Keep your options open and be ready to pivot.

### For Saboteurs
- Fake confidence is your ally: act like you’re helping build a safe path while inserting small detours that only reveal themselves later.
- Control the tempo: you don’t always have to block the path immediately. Subtle misdirection, like nudging a fellow digger toward a dead end that you already controlled, can be more effective than a blunt obstruction.
- Watch for collusion cues: when players make quiet side conversations or hesitate on a critical decision, there’s a good chance something is going on behind the scenes. Use these moments to your advantage.

### Group Dynamics Tips
- Rotate roles if you can: a little role-switching between rounds lets you explore different strategies and keeps players guessing.
- Keep banter light: the humor is part of the glue that holds the game together. Don’t let accusations get personal; the best nagging is ridiculous and over the top.
- Adjust the pace: if your group tends to overthink, set a timer for rounds or prune the number of map cards to quick-fire scenarios. If your group craves depth, add small house rules to extend decision points without turning the game unplayable.
