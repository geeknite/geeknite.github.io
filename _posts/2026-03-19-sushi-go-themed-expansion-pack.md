## What's in the Box
The expansion lands with four theme decks, each roughly 15 cards plus a handful of tokens and a compact rules sheet. The cards are color-coded and sport a cohesive art style that nods to Sushi Go!’s original silhouettes while leaning into a house-cart aesthetic. The tokens are chunky enough to survive a table tumble and the rule sheet keeps the new scoring lines readable without turning the experience into a trampolined math problem. In short: components feel sturdy, production values respect the base game’s fast-and-fun ethos, and nothing screams “overlay” so much as “we meticulously avoided a burden.”

### The Four Themed Decks
- Nori Night: Focus on seaweed wraps with chain combos and a few small penalties to keep players honest. This deck rewards careful timing and the artful reuse of one or two seaweed cards to maximize flow.
- Tempura Takedown: A twist on the classic tempura pair rule with a scoring flourish that rewards longer-term planning and not just the biggest pair total. Think patience, not panic.
- Maki Mayhem: A deck built around sushi rolls, offering multi-card combos that reward color-matching and set-building. It’s where you’ll hear the classic “just one more roll” refrain while your brain does a tiny victory jig.
- Nigiri Nation: A deck that leans into quick scoring bursts and riskier plays, with a few payoff tokens for those who sprint to the finish. This one is the “ICU for the impatient” in deck form.

Each deck brings its own micro-goals and thematic flair, yet the overall engine remains familiar enough to teach in under ten minutes. The new scoring lines slot in alongside base Sushi Go!’s scoring, so you’re not learning a new language—just a new accent.

### Thematic Scoring and Tie-Breakers
Beyond the standard Sushi Go! scoring, the expansion adds flavor-forward tracks specific to each deck. Nori Night might award extra points for chaining seaweed-wrapped cards, Tempura Takedown introduces multipliers when you hit certain combinations in a single round, Maki Mayhem emphasizes rolling sets and color coordination, and Nigiri Nation leans into timing and last-minute grabs for a bonus boost. The scoring rules are laid out clearly on the new sheet, and the math stays friendly enough that you can teach a first-time player in a single turn. Playful bonuses help close gaps or tilt a tight finish, but they never make you feel like you missed a crucial step in the core game.

### How It Plays Across Player Counts
Like the base game, the expansion scales smoothly from 2 to 4 players and clocks in around 15 to 20 minutes per game, depending on how briskly the table passes cards and how many jokes are told about mis-timed chopsticks. For 5–6 players, you can still play with house rules or by using Sushi Go Party components, but the expansion shines best in tighter groups where interaction remains high and the pace stays zippy. In practical playtests, the table tends to laugh more than argue, which aligns with the designers’ goal of a light, social experience rather than a brutal optimization sprint.

A note on table presence: the four decks don’t all hit the table in the same hand; you’ll often see someone sprint for a specific deck, while another player tries to block or bait the others into a suboptimal track. The result is a lively dance rather than a single high-stakes chase—the kind of round where you’re certain someone shouted “Wasabi!” at precisely the wrong moment and somehow it was perfect.

### Card Examples and Flavor Text
Here are a few sample cards to illustrate how the expansion injects flavor without derailing core decisions:
- Seaweed Symphony (Nori Night): When you take this card, if you already have a Nori Night card in your collection, you may draw an extra card this round. Flavor text: seaweed with jazz hands.
- Tempura Trail (Tempura Takedown): If you have two Tempura cards by end of round, you gain additional points equal to the number of Tempura pairs you collected this round.
- Maki Marvel (Maki Mayhem): When you collect a Maki card, you may add an extra card to your hand for the next round.
- Nigiri Nudge (Nigiri Nation): If you end with three Nigiri cards, you gain a small bonus token that increases your final point total.
These examples are representative rather than exhaustive, and the actual deck contains a few more variants that push players to experiment with timing and blocking.

### Strategy and Tips
- Synergy over brute force: Each deck rewards crafting multi-round combos instead of chasing a single card’s power. Build a flexible plan that can adapt when new cards appear.
- Read the room: Passing cards creates telltale signals. If a neighbor is chasing a Maki stack, you can pivot to block or accelerate other decks for your own payoff.
- Tempo and timers: The tokens and scoring lines reward well-timed plays that align with the round cadence. Don’t chase every card; decisive action can yield bigger returns.
- Keep it light: The pacing is designed for sociability. If a round spirals into chaos, embrace the chaos as part of the charm; humor should stay in the foreground and the win in the middle ground.

### Teaching Notes for New Players
- Start with base Sushi Go! without expansion rules to ensure everyone is confident with drafting and passing.
- Introduce one deck at a time in a short practice round, then gradually bring in the rest as players become comfortable with the rhythm.
- Emphasize the social aspect: encourage quick banter about food puns and timing, not endless analysis paralysis.
- Use scoring sheets from the included rules to show how the new tracks offset or augment base scoring.

## House Rules and Variants
If you want to mix things up, try these quick variants:
- Twist of Fate: At the start of each round, reveal one random card from each deck. These cards gain a minor bonus effect for that round only. It shakes up early-game planning and creates forked paths.
- Festival Night: Each deck gets one additional scoring token that can be earned by achieving a specific milestone in that round. This keeps players attentive without adding complexity.
- Rapid Fire Round: Shorten rounds by one pass, forcing players to adapt to more abrupt card streams. Great for faster after-dinner sessions or family game nights with younger players.
- Theme Curator: Before the game starts, each player chooses one deck they feel most connected to and receives a minor starting bonus that nudges them toward that theme. It’s a gentle nudge for players who want a bit of flavor with minimal extra math.
