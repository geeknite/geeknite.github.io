## Unpacking the Rope: Synthetic vs Steel
One of the major talking points with any modern winch is the rope material. Synthetic rope has become the de facto standard for good reason. It’s lighter, easier to handle, and generally safer to work with than steel under moderate loads. The rope used on the Stealth is designed to resist kinking, which matters when you’re paying out and retracting in tight spaces or around obstacles. It’s also easier on your hands; you don’t have to wear as heavy gloves for protection when coiling or inspecting the line.

Pros of synthetic rope in this setup:
- Lighter overall weight: easier to manage as you spool and unspool.
- Safer on hands and body: reduces the risk of lacerations if the rope snaps or part of it shows wear.
- Strong against abrasion: the rope’s outer sheath helps it survive rocky surfaces and rough bark when you’re drag-checking around a fallen tree.

Cons (to be aware of):
- More care required for sharp edges and heat: synthetic rope can be susceptible to cuts if you’re dragging it across a jagged surface, so a tree saver strap is recommended for winches placed near sharp obstacles.
- Can hold moisture and degrade if stored damp for long periods: ensure proper storage, dry out after use.

If you’re choosing between synthetic rope and steel, synthetic offers safety and ease for most recreational recoveries. Steel remains a strong option for heavy, industrial-grade recoveries, where extreme pulls and abrasion resistance are primary concerns. For the typical car, trailer, ATV, or UTV recovery job, synthetic rope in the Stealth kit is a well-balanced choice that keeps you safer and more nimble in the field.

## Installation and Fitment
A good install is half the battle won. The Stealth winch arrives with a mounting pattern that lines up with many bumper and bumper-plate configurations, but there are a few caveats to ensure a clean, reliable fit from the moment you tighten the last bolt.

- Measure the bolt pattern on your bumper or winch mount plate before ordering. If you’re using a product that’s a non-standard platform, you might need a compatible mounting plate adapter to ensure the winch sits flush and stable.

Electrical considerations are not to be ignored. You’ll need an adequate power supply—ideally a deep-cycle battery or a high-cranking amp battery in a vehicle kit. If you’re wiring to a switch or relay control, double-check the power path to ensure the motor draws with minimal resistance and that the switch is rated for the current your winch will demand.

## Safety Considerations
Winching is not a hobby for the faint of heart or the shallowly prepared. Here are a few non-negotiables for safe operation:
- Wear gloves: synthetic rope under tension can cut if it frays. Gloves protect your hands and improve grip for paying out or re-spooling.
- Keep bystanders clear: the rope and line can snap or whip during a failure. Establish a safe perimeter and assign a lookout.
- Use a tree saver strap around fixed objects: this reduces line wear and prevents rope damage when you’re hooking to natural anchors.
- Never stand in the direct line of pull: the line can snap or slip unexpectedly, risking serious injury.
- Inspect before each use: look for wear, frays, or signs of heat damage on the rope. If you see any, retire the rope and replace as needed.
- Have a plan for power: ensure a solid battery or power source, proper fusing, and an easily accessible kill switch.

The Stealth kit’s design factors help with safety: a robust fairlead, a stable housing, and a rope that’s easier to manage under load. But the onus remains on you, the operator, to respect the tool and its limits. It’s a mechanical system with energy behind it—treat it with the care it deserves.

## Maintenance and Longevity
Like any good gadget, your winch rewards regular maintenance. Here are a few rules of thumb:
- Clean and lubricate moving parts after exposure to dirt or water. A light spray with a silicone-based lubricant helps reduce friction and wear on the rope drum and fairlead.
- Dry the rope after wet deployments. Moisture can lead to mold or mildew in the spool area if stored damp for long periods.
- Inspect fittings and connectors for corrosion. A little rust is a signal to replace parts before they fail under load.
- Replace the rope if you notice any fraying or deep kinks. Synthetic rope is forgiving but not indestructible; a small cut can propagate later into a bigger issue.
- Keep the remote and wiring dry. Water in control mechanisms disrupts function and increases failure risk.

If you adopt a regular maintenance routine, you’ll maximize the Stealth winch’s life and performance, ensuring it remains a reliable part of your recovery toolkit for seasons to come.
