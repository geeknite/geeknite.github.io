## How to play: a brisk refresher that won’t ruin your vibe

If you’re new to Codenames, here’s the compact gist: you split into two teams. Each team has a spymaster and field operatives. The goal is to guess your team’s hidden words on the table using one-word clues given by the spymaster. Clues must be a single word (plus optional one-number hint) that relates to as many of your team’s words as possible without leading to the opposing team’s words or the assassin word. It’s a game of risk assessment, vocabulary gymnastics, and the occasional explosive guess that leaves you both triumphant and slightly embarrassed.

On your turn, the spymaster gives a clue like “Ocean 2,” pointing your team toward two relevant word cards on the table. Your team then discusses and votes on which cards might match. If you pick a red agent word, great—your team advances. If you pick a blue agent word, the other team moves forward. If you pick a bystander word, nothing happens but it might cause a moment of mild panic. The assassin word? That’s a sudden, dramatic end to your turn, and ideally you avoid it at all costs unless you’re into dramatic finales.

The core gameplay loop is fast, dynamic, and often hilariously off-target. The first good clue that snaps multiple words together—like “Underwater 3” and your team shouts “Submarine!” and actually gets three words—feels like a small victory for the language gods. The game shines when your group embraces the chaos rather than fights it. It’s not a test of your general knowledge or your deep-lore trivia; it’s a test of how well you can steer a group of humans toward a shared mental map with a single, well-placed word.

For our sealed copy, the fresh edges of the card stock likely provide crisp sleights-of-hand and snappy shuffles. The ritual of shuffling new cards, stringing the key cards to the proper grid, and lining up the teams is part of the experience. You’ll feel the game’s tempo settle into a pattern: rounds of intense clue-giving alternating with moments of communal laughter as players misinterpret a perfectly good clue and end up shouting the wrong card. The party energy is infectious, and you’ll hear a chorus of “No, that’s not what I meant” or “I was aiming for number two!” in quick succession.

## Strategies for success: tips from the field operatives and spymasters alike

No matter how sharp you are at wordplay, the beauty of Codenames lies in teamwork and strategy that doesn’t overcomplicate things. Here are a few practical pointers for using this sealed 265-card edition to its fullest:

- Craft clues that maximize coverage without inviting disaster: If your team is rolling on a set of words that share a common theme, try a broader clue that ties them together. But beware the assassin word—if that word exists in your pool, a single risky clue can derail an entire round. The key is to balance breadth with precision.
- Use neutral words sparingly: By default, you want a clue that steers your team toward several of your own words but avoids the other team’s words or the assassin. If you’re stuck, a one-word clue with a supporting discussion about each candidate card is often better than a risky two-word gambit.
- Read your teammates: Part of the fun is reading the social cues. Some groups respond to playful hints, others enjoy dry, clever wordplay. If your team leans into humor, lean into witty clues; if they’re more serious, tailor your approach accordingly.
- Don’t fear bad rounds: A few misfires are part of the game’s charm. The best players recover by reframing the next clue in a way that nudges the table toward safer picks, while still maintaining momentum. The sealed copy’s fresh deck gives you clear feedback on what works and what doesn’t because you’ll be mapping your clues to a still-pristine set of words.
- Leverage the spymaster’s perspective: The spymaster has to see the clearest path to their team’s words while avoiding the other team’s. If you’re the spymaster, practice quick mental mapping: which words share a common association? Which associations feel obvious to players but risky due to bystanders or the assassin? The more you refine this mental map, the fewer rounds end with a frantic, “I can’t believe we chose that card.”
