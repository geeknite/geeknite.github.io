## Unboxing Troy and Evil Troy: a tale of plastic chaos and medieval menace

Welcome, brave contender of the snack table and sworn enemy of the tidy living room. Today we dive into a sealed chunk of tabletop chaos: Zombicide Black Plague featuring Troy and Evil Troy by Guillotine Games. This is not just a box; it is a miniature armada of doom encased in glossy cardboard with enough plastic to fill a small dragon’s fear cabinet. If you thought your desk was a war zone, wait until you disperse the contents of this box onto a table and pretend you know how to manage a horde of undead peasants while your friends pretend they know what a siege engine does. The goal is simple in theory: survive, rescue your teammates, and ensure that no one forgets to loot the last tavern before the last boss arrives. ## Inside the box: what you’re actually buying when you press that seal button

Guillotine Games has a way with presentation that makes you think the undead deserve a designer label. Inside the box you’ll typically find:

- A modular, gothic board that looks like it was carved from a fallen cathedral floor. 
- A stack of zombie miniatures of various grotesque flavors: walkers, runners, fatties, and the occasional abomination that makes your eye twitch in a way that indicates both awe and impending despair.
- Hero cards for Troy and Evil Troy, plus a handful of generic survivors to mix with the chaos.
- Tiles for dungeon-like rooms, ladders, doors, and barricades, all of which are perfect conversation starters about “which tile goes where” and “which zombie will ignore cover this round.”
- A rulebook that is surprisingly readable given the topic and a quick-start guide that you’ll try to shortcut and then promptly regret when you realize you forgot the basics.
- Dice crafted to feel like you’re rolling the fate of a village against a tide of undead maulers. The dice aren’t just numeric; they are tiny, adorable harbingers of doom.
- Cards for weapons, gear, and event effects that will make your pizza dinner smell vaguely medieval and terrifyingly heroic all at once.

The components aren’t just thrown together; they’re married with a sense of theme that makes you feel like you’re sliding into a tavern brawl where the baddies have more plans than your life insurance policy. The miniatures have a good heft, the board clicks into place with satisfying magnet-like precision, and everything feels sturdy enough to survive the occasional accidental table flip that tabletop gamers secretly practice for.

## Troy and Evil Troy: two opposing forces in a single heroic duo

In this expansion, you don’t just get more bodies to throw at the problem; you get two named heroes—Troy and his more questionable counterpart, Evil Troy. Troy is the classic noble survivor with solid combat chops and a knack for leadership. Evil Troy, on the other hand, brings a more reckless, high-risk/high-reward attitude to the table. It’s like having a buddy who insists on charging the boss while you’re still figuring out which leg of the map is safe to step on. The two T roy variants come with unique abilities that synergize in amusing, sometimes infuriating, ways:

- Troy often acts as the moral compass in the group, offering bonuses to ally survivability and a helping hand that feels like you’re playing support in a fantasy-RPG while your friend rolls the dice for glory.
- Evil Troy skews toward aggressive tactics: higher damage potential, riskier moves, and the occasional moment where you question your life choices as you watch a hero leap into a room full of zombies with a grin and a plan that involves “just roll a 6.”
- The dynamic between them creates memorable moments: dramatic saves, near-misses, and the inevitable debate about whether it’s better to push through a corridor of walkers or retreat to regroup and re-roll the deck of misfortune.

If you’re chasing that “dynamic duo” vibe, Troy and Evil Troy don’t disappoint. They create a micro-drama on the tabletop: one hero fiercely protecting the party, the other heroically ignoring the obvious warning signs in favor of a dash toward the glory that comes with the last stand. It’s silly, it’s cinematic, and it’s a reliable source of inside-joke banter for your gaming group.

Zombicide Black Plague leans into a dark, medieval aesthetic, and Troy and Evil Troy fuse into that vibe with a sense of myth and chaos. The knights aren’t wearing gleaming armor for looks; they’re survivals in a plague-ridden world where even the creaking floorboards seem to hiss with the scent of undead doom. The theme is complemented by bright card art, suitably gruesome miniatures, and a game flow that makes you feel like you’re constantly balancing on the edge of a siege, where any misstep can turn your well-laid plans into a cartoonish sprint through a horde of undead with nothing but a sharpened spoon and a prayer.

## Setup and play flow: how long until you’re knee-deep in Zombicide chaos?

Setup is relatively quick if you’ve done this before with any Zombicide base game or expansion. The board sections snap into place with ease, the zombie spawns are set according to the scenario, and you lay out the hero boards for Troy and Evil Troy. The more you play, the faster you’ll be able to set up without having to check every table edge twice. The initial rounds usually involve a tense shuffle of prioritizing mission objectives, securing weapons, and blocking doorways just enough to feel clever without becoming a traffic jam. Expect first plays to take around 60-90 minutes once both sides have the hang of the mechanics; subsequent sessions drift toward 45-70 minutes as you optimize your route planning, resource management, and the inevitable “one more turn” compulsion.

The core gameplay loop remains faithful to Zombicide’s signature style: you alternate turns with your crew, draw and play cards, and try to keep as many heroes alive as possible while slaughtering the helpless, shambling masses. The tension ramps as you add more zombies, spawn new tiles, and realize that your heroic rescue plan has just become a module for improvisation.

## Mechanics and balance: what works and what makes you double-check the rulebook

- Action economy: Each hero has a limited number of actions per turn, and you’ll need to juggle movement, combat, and loot. The balance feels tight: you’re never overwhelmed by random chance alone; you’re often the architect of your own doom as you chain in a few risky actions that either pay off gloriously or explode in your face.
- Combat: Dice are the deciding factor, with excess luck and bad rolls often determining whether your group survives a roomful of the undead or ends up sprinting away with a single hero dragging three corpses behind. The good news is that even when you fail spectacularly, there’s a humor in it that keeps the mood buoyant rather than grim.
- Hero abilities: Troy and Evil Troy give you interesting tactical options. The “lead by example” vibe of Troy pairs nicely with the more aggressive, risk-hungry paths of Evil Troy. They feel distinct enough to justify their inclusion beyond simple flavor.
- Scaling: The Black Plague box set is already a story-driven experience, and the Troy and Evil Troy duo adds a new layer of strategic choices. You can scale the difficulty by selecting different scenarios or mixing in other expansions. The result is a game that remains approachable for newcomers while offering depth for veterans who want to squeeze every last ounce of planning out of their turn sequence.

## How Troy and Evil Troy compare to standard Black Plague experiences

Zombicide Black Plague is a fan favorite for a reason: it blends classic fantasy with the chaotic energy of a zombie horde. The complexity is still approachable, but Troy and Evil Troy add a layer of decision-making that can feel heavy for first-timers. If you’re patient and excited about the lore, though, the payoff is entertaining, and you’ll find yourself cheering when Evil Troy pulls off a reckless but effective moment that saves the day.

## Characters’ voices and how they influence the table dynamic

Troy’s leadership vibe encourages coordination and safe plays. You’ll often see him carving a path for the ailing survivors, pointing out cover options, and reminding everyone not to split the party. Evil Troy, meanwhile, has the “go big or go home” mentality that fans of dramatic plays often love. You’ll hear the table debate whether a risky surge is worth the potential payoff, and in many rounds, someone will boldly declare, “This is how legends are born,” right before a clumsy door-rumble turns the corridor into an improvised obstacle course.

This duality doesn’t just create entertainment; it fosters memorable storytelling. Your group will reminisce about the time Evil Troy sprinted into a room full of zombies, drew a dozen crits, and somehow managed to come out with two survivors and a story about how fate decided to throw a chair at his plans. It’s the kind of hijinks that makes a campaign feel alive rather than a linear grind, and it’s precisely what many players crave in a boxed expansion: narrative flavor with tangible game impact.
