## What makes a good hallway runner rug?

## Size and fit: finding your corridor sweet spot
Hallways vary wildly in width and length. A rug that is too narrow can look like a misfit, while one that stretches too long can become a tripping hazard or make the corridor feel like a runway with a speed limit.

### How to measure your space
- Measure the width: The rug should be wide enough to cover most of the walkway without blocking door clearance. If you’re dealing with a heavy traffic zone, aim for at least 2 inches of non-carpet floor visible on either side for a crisp edge.
- Measure the length: Typically, a hallway runner should leave about 6–12 inches of bare floor at both ends. This looks balanced and allows you to step on the rug without the sole of your shoe stepping off onto hard floor mid-journey.
- Consider door clearance: If you have doors that swing inward, ensure the rug won’t get snagged. Some folks opt for a shorter length near doors and a longer run in the middle to preserve function and aesthetic.

## Cleaning, care, and maintenance
Let’s be real: you’re not buying a rug to stare at it with admiration from a distance—you want it to survive, and perhaps be easy to clean after the occasional muddy paw print or coffee mishap.

### Routine care
- Vacuum weekly with a low-suction setting. Avoid aggressive beater bars that can snag shag fibers.
- Shake or beat the rug to release loose dirt if you have a scuffed area. A gentle tug and a few shakes can do wonders.
- Spot clean spills immediately with a mild detergent and cold water. Blot rather than rub to prevent the pile from frizzing and becoming a science experiment in lint production.

### Washing instructions (when needed)
- Most shag rugs in this category are not fully machine washable, and the LNSHRR is no exception. If you must, check the manufacturer’s care label. In general, hand washing with mild soap and air drying is safer for the plush fibers than a crowded washing machine spin cycle. If machine washing is supported, use a gentle cycle with cold water and a mild detergent, and avoid high heat in the dryer. Air drying flat is best to preserve pile structure.
- Rotate occasionally to keep wear even, especially in high-traffic corridors. This ensures you don’t end up with a bald spot that makes your cat or dog question your fashion choices.

### Dealing with shedding and pilling
Shaggy fibers shed a bit at first. Regular vacuuming helps normalize the pile. If pills appear, a gentle fabric shaver on a low setting can restore the surface. Don’t overdo it—these fibers aren’t designed to be shaved like a sweater; you’re aiming for a neat, fluffy look, not a bald runway.

## Practical buying tips: what to look for when shopping
- Backing quality: Look for a dense, grippy backing that matches your floor type. A cheap backing will peel up or slip in a month.
- Fiber and construction: A plush pile with good density supports longer foot comfort and better wear resistance. Avoid extremely lightweight options if you have pets or kids.
- Edge finishing: Some cheaper rugs coil at the edges after a year. Clip looks and finishing matter for longevity.
- Care label readability: A clear care label with washing instructions saves headaches later.
- Return policy: A lenient return policy helps if you discover the rug’s color is not quite what you expected in daylight.

## Measuring your space like a pro
If you’re assembling a hallway makeover, you should have a plan. Bring a tape measure, determine the width from wall to wall, and map out a few inches of bare floor near the ends for balance. Then, choose the rug size that leaves you with comfortable margins and not a rug that threatens to trip you every time you reach for the hallway light switch. If in doubt, err on the side of slightly longer rather than too short; you can always tuck the ends under a rug grip or extend with a second rug if needed.
