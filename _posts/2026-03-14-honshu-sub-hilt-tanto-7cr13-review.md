## What is the Honshu Sub-Hilt Tanto?
The Sub-Hilt Tanto is a design that borrows the dramatic silhouette of a traditional tanto and marries it to a modern, high-impact size. It’s not a pocket knife, and it’s not a kitchen knife. It’s the kind of blade that could plausibly cut through a stubborn log, then slice a stubborn confidence in half. The “sub-hilt” feature refers to a raised guard-like area that sits just beneath the handle, offering a finger stop and a hint of reverse-grip protection. This is not a fashion flourish; it’s a deliberate ergonomic choice meant to enhance grip security during heavy use or when you need to pull off a controlled thrust with confidence.

From a distance, the knife looks like a long, sleek, black steel spear with a dramatic tip and a leather sheath that whispers, “I’m ready for outdoorsy adventure or a theater prop gig.” Up close, you’ll notice you’re dealing with a blade that plays nicely with both brute force and careful, precise work when you decide to put it through a centrifuge of tasks—wood processing, campcraft, or that questionable chore of prying a stubborn jar lid off a stubborn something that stubbornly resists ordinary tools.

For curious readers who like the rabbit hole: 7CR13 stainless steel is the budget-friendly cousin of more expensive stainless varieties. It’s not a high-end steel by any stretch, but it’s known for decent corrosion resistance, ease of sharpening, and affordability. ## Maintenance and Care
No blade is perfect out of the box; all blades need a little love. For the Honshu Sub-Hilt Tanto, maintenance is straightforward:
- Clean after use to prevent rust and residue build-up, especially if you’ve used it in damp environments.
- Dry thoroughly before storing in the sheath to minimize moisture-related issues in the leather.
- Light oil the blade to maintain corrosion resistance and ease of future sharpening. If you want a quick DIY routine, we’ve got a beginner-friendly guide that walks you through the basics: {% post_url 2024-07-10-beginner-sharpening-guide %}.

The 7CR13 stainless steel takes a fair edge with basic sharpening tools. It’s not a rock-hard steel, but it’s forgiving for hobbyists who want to keep a sharp edge without needing a full-blown blacksmith setup. Remember: a proper sharpening angle and consistent technique matter far more than premium steel names when you’re trying to keep a working edge under field conditions.
