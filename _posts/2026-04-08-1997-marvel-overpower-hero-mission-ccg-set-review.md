First, the context

OverPower is not a modern, streamlined game. The card faces are bold, the typography chunky, and the art style very 90s in the best possible way. The base set focuses on hero teams, power ratings, and a battleground that is more concept than a precise chessboard. The Hero Mission expansion introduces mission cards that modify how victory is achieved, add new encounter types, and sometimes shuffle in fresh mechanics to increase the level of chaos. For collectors, a 1997 Hero Mission lot signals both the print run reality and the quirky design choices that define late 90s licensing-based card games. If you care about chase cards or promo variants, a lot like this might include a surprise or two; more often, the real value is in the sheer scale and the nostalgic glow you get from flipping through a stack of glossy faces.

Contents of the Lot: what you’ll likely see

A 75+ card Hero Mission lot is a snapshot of an era. Expect a mix of content that can be roughly categorized as follows:

- Hero cards: Expect a mix of marquee heroes like Spider-Man, Wolverine, Iron Man, Captain America, Hulk, Storm, and occasional favorites such as Black Panther or Daredevil. Not all are equally rare; some are common, others mid-tier, and a few rare or promo-oriented prints can pop up depending on the batch.

- Mission cards: The heart of the Hero Mission expansion. These cards set objectives, inject special rules, or tilt the action toward a narrative beat rather than a simple beat-down contest.

- Villain and henchmen cards: A typical lot includes a handful of major threats and a slew of disposable antagonists to stand in the way of the heroes.

- Equipment and support cards: Gear, armor, and situational modifiers that can swing a battle on a well-timed play.

- Rare and promo cards: Depending on the print run and the batch, you may encounter promotional cards or glossy variants. These can significantly alter the perceived value of the lot for collectors.

- Duplicates and wear: A bulk lot almost always contains duplicates, sometimes of the same hero or mission card. Card condition will vary from minty-fresh to lightly to moderately played, and the back sides may show different degrees of wear depending on storage and handling.

Condition and grading: how to approach it

In a 75+ card bundle, you’ll encounter a spectrum of wear. Here are practical guidelines to evaluate the lot without losing your mind:

- Mint to near mint: Sharp corners, clean surfaces, and colors that pop. The back of the card should be intact with no notable fade.

- Light wear: Edge wear or minor surface marks; front imagery remains vivid.

- Moderate wear: Corner creases, visible scuffs, and some surface imperfections; still playable and displayable with a bit of patience.

- Heavy wear: Large creases, water damage, or substantial fading. These are more about sentiment than value.

- Poor: Readability issues or severe damage that makes it hard to identify the card.

How to inspect a lot quickly

- Flip through visually first to get a sense of the hero distribution and the presence of rare cards.
- Check for any foil or promo variants separately; these can be a nice bump to value.
- Look at the mission cards: readable text equals better usability for play scenarios.
- Inspect the condition of both sides, especially if the lot has been stored in a damp environment or with other swappable materials.
- Photograph the lot if you plan to resell; clear shots of a few representative rares can help buyers gauge value.

Playing with a lot: best practices

- Separate the hero cards from the mission and villain cards to speed set-up. A small binder or three-ring system helps here.
- Use sleeves for protection, particularly for the hero cards that have the best art or the most play-value.
- Create a simple reference sheet with the common mission keywords and their effects so you don’t have to flip through the entire deck during play.
- If you are playing with new hands, consider a draft-style approach to avoid heavy one-sideds more often than not.
- Use a timer to keep the game moving; older rules can drag, especially when you’re all reliving a memory rather than playing a match.
