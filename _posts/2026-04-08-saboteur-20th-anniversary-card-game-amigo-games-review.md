## How to set up Saboteur 20th Anniversary Edition

Setting up Saboteur is a ritual that signals the start of a strategic arc and a few light-hearted squabbles. Here s the quick-teach version that should take about five minutes:

1. Separate the role cards into miners and saboteurs, shuffle them, and deal a number to each player according to player count. In a 2 player game you re likely to end up with a lot of strategic tension and perhaps two saboteurs arguing over the best route; with 12 players you get a dwarf city built on suspicion and a rumor mill that would make an influencer blush.
2. Shuffle the tunnel and treasure cards into their respective decks. Place the treasure card deck face down and flip the top treasure card to reveal the first target.
3. Deal a small number of path cards to each player. These cards form a network that eventually yields a tunnel to the treasure. The trick is you do not know who is on your side and which path will sabotage your hopes.
4. Set the five target rewards face up near the treasure. These are what you re racing toward.
5. Decide who will go first and let the digging begin. Pro tip: keeping a straight face while discussing suspicious choices is a game-changer.

The quick-teach emphasises the core of Saboteur: visibility in suspicion, choice in action, and luck in the first draw. The trick is to think like a network engineer in a fantasy setting. You want to build a path while preventing others from sabotaging your plan, all with a little misdirection and a dash of friendly treachery.


## How to play: core mechanics explained

Saboteur works in rounds. In each round, players take turns playing tunnel cards and action cards to either reveal a path toward the treasure or sabotage someone else s path. The round ends when the treasure is found or everyone runs out of cards. The twist is that you do not know who is friendly and who is not until the end of the round, and even then you may be wrong.

Key mechanics include:
- Role assignment: miners vs saboteurs. Your role informs your objectives, but your actions are visible to the table so you must balance deception with a plausible construct of honesty.
- Tunnel building: players lay down tunnel cards to connect a path from the start to the treasure. Each tunnel card has a specific orientation and you must connect with the previous card to extend the route.
- Action cards: these allow you to break, block, ruin or repair tunnels, or swap or rotate them. This is where players have the most agency to alter the course of the route, and where suspicion tends to spike like a mushroom in a dark cave.
- Treasure cards: once a path reaches the treasure, players reveal the treasure card and the round ends. If the miners reach the treasure before the saboteurs derail the route, the miners win; if the saboteurs derail it first, the saboteurs win.

The core cycle of play is fast: you draw a card, you place a tunnel or an action, you pass the turn. The energy comes from social dynamics—every time someone plays a bridge card that blocks a crucial corridor, or uses a tool to fix a fake tunnel, the table bursts with a chorus of groans and friendly accusations.


## Strategies for miners and saboteurs

Saboteur shines on the social engineering front. If you are a miner, your job is to cooperate with fellow miners to connect a valid route to the treasure while trying to root out saboteurs who are tossing in dead ends and red herrings. If you are a saboteur, your aim is to muddle the path and create confusion without making your own allegiance obvious.

- Miners: focus on clear communication, but avoid predictable collaboration. Use information provided by map cards to triangulate a plausible route. Watch for players who consistently block certain tunnel sections or who seem overly eager to flip a card that ruins someone else s path.
- Saboteurs: you want to sow doubt and confusion. Do not reveal your true color too soon; use misdirection and subtle blockages. The best saboteur moves are those that force others to question whether a director-of-operations is acting in good faith or simply trying to avert suspicion away from themselves.
- Mixed groups: with a diverse mix of players, Saboteur becomes a dynamic social puzzle. The more people you have in the room, the bigger the opportunities for bluffing, misdirection, and dramatic reveals at the end of the round.

In practice, a round may begin with innocent tile placement and quickly pivot into a tense standoff as players suspect each other. The fun comes from the way information leaks through the choices that players make and how those choices ripple across the table. You will often find yourself muttering the classic line that started this entire tournament: I m not sabotaging anything—yet.

## Player count, playtime, and table dynamics

Saboteur is famous for scaling from 2 to 12 players. In practice, the way you experience the game changes with the number of players:

- 2 players: The tension becomes a chess match with cryptic halts and tiny misdirections. It can feel intense and strategic, but the pace slows a bit as you try to coax information out of each other without tipping your hand.
- 3 to 4 players: A sweet spot where balance remains robust and the social dynamics start to shine. It is easy to track who did what, but enough confusion remains to keep everyone engaged and guessing.
- 5 to 8 players: A bustling, chaotic experience that highlights the social deduction aspect. People talk, bluff, and sometimes forget which tunnel card they just placed, leading to hilarious misplays and delightful revelations.

In terms of time, Saboteur clocks in around 30 minutes per round, which is a sweet spot for a social deduction game. ## Quick teaching guide for a smooth session

Teaching Saboteur quickly is as important as not spoiling the plot twist. Here are steps to get a game going in five minutes:

- Step 1: Explain player roles. Emphasize that one group is trying to construct the tunnel while the other is trying to derail it. Do not reveal too much; let players infer and guess as play proceeds.
- Step 2: Demonstrate a simple tunnel placement. Show how two consecutive tunnel cards connect to form a line toward the treasure. Emphasize the direction and orientation aspects.
- Step 3: Describe action cards. Provide a few examples of how you can block, rotate, or repair tunnels. Clarify when a tool is used and what happens to the route at that moment.
- Step 4: Do a mock round with a few cards to illustrate the flow. The goal here is to show how quickly a round can evolve into clever misdirection and friendly banter.
- Step 5: Establish the end condition. When the treasure card is revealed, the round ends and you tally who s on which side. This is where the drama peaks and the group decides what to tweak for the next round.

The teaching approach should be short and practical. The rest of the game is about playing with the group, reading tells, and letting chaos unfold. The more comfortable players are with the mechanics, the more they will enjoy the narrative of the game.
