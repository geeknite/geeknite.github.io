## What is 32P and why steel?
The RC world is littered with numbers like 32P and 5mm bore. If you're not a math wizard, these are not secret codes for a space game, they are physical specs that determine how well the gear meshes with a spur gear and how tough it will survive the inevitable brick-wall-to-axle moment during a bumpy track day. 32P refers to the standard pitch of the gear teeth: the number of teeth per inch of pitch line. A higher pitch means finer teeth and usually smoother meshing, but at the cost of increased wear and lower transverse strength. A 32P gear is a sweet spot for many 1/10 and 1/8 scale builds, balancing smooth engagement with robust tooth profile. The 5mm bore indicates the central hole diameter that slides onto the motor shaft and is tied to the set screw or grub screw arrangement used to lock the pinion in place.
The Hot Racing's steel pinions are not the toy-grade aluminum variants you might find for a quick flip mod. Steel is heavier, yes, and it conducts heat like a little blacksmith's forge, but it also stands up to torque and heat much better, which matters when your gear mesh is loaded with hills of torque on high-power brushless systems. The result is less tooth wear, less slippage, and the kind of reliability that makes you feel like you might actually win that weekend RT race, if only you could remember the turns.

## Specifications and what they mean
- Pitch: 32P. This is the standard on many popular spur gears; if your spur is 64P you might be able to mesh anyway but expect contact patch changes. A 32P pinion fits many 64P spurs; you should check the professor-level gear mesh math in your head or on a whiteboard before you spin up the motor.
- Bore: 5mm. This is the inner diameter that fits onto a 5mm motor shaft and is typically held in place with a grub screw. Ensure your motor uses a 5mm bore or adapters exist to adapt from 3.0mm or 5.0mm; some pinions come with multiple sets of grub screws or a clamp-style bore, but this one is the classic fixed 5mm.
- Material: Steel. Heat-treated steel with a polished finish. The machining tolerances are what you’d expect from competitive RC components: tight enough for consistent meshing, not so tight that you need dental-grade micrometers to measure the clearance.
- Teeth count: The exact tooth count will depend on the product variant; however, 32P pinions in the size with 18-22 teeth are common for mid-range speed and torque. The exact tooth count will influence your final drive ratio, reaching into the realm of rocket speed or wall-of-torque.

Note: If you’re new to this, you might be thinking, Why steel? Why not plastic? The answer is: weight, durability, and the kind of clunk you can take on an off-road course or on-engineered terrain. The steel choice means when you're banging through the rocks or the curb, the pinion won't fail with the same frequency as delicate aluminum or plastic equivalents. On the flip side, it’s heavier and can cause more heat generation in a small build. For a basher or a track day where you want longer life and less replacement, steel is a strong candidate.

## Fitment and compatibility
- Spur compatibility: The 32P pinion will mesh with many 64P or 72P spur gears, provided the tooth count allows proper mesh and center-to-center distance. It’s essential to verify the gear mesh by listening for a clean, chuff-free engagement rather than a noisy, grinding chorus. The pinion’s 32P pitch should match your spur’s tooth size to avoid binding.
- Motor shaft compatibility: The 5mm bore is compatible with motors that have a 5mm shaft. Most brushless motors and some brushed motors come in 5mm shaft options, but the bore must have space for the grub screw or a clamping mechanism. If your motor uses a different shaft diameter, you’ll want an adapter or a different pinion with the correct bore.
- Set screw arrangement: Most 5mm bore pinions use one or more grub screws. The typical arrangement is two grub screws opposing each other for better locking force and to reduce the risk of slip under heavy acceleration or a hard corner. It’s worth checking if the screws come with the needed metric threads (usually M3) and if thread lock is recommended by the manufacturer.

## Installation and tuning tips
- Tools you’ll need: 2.5mm or 3mm hex driver for grub screws, a torque wrench or torque-limiting driver, thread-locking compound, calipers for quick measurement, and a spin-down gauge for checking pinion alignment.
- Alignment: Set the gear mesh by adjusting the position of the motor plate or using adjustable motor mount plates. The mesh should be snug but not binding, with a slight bit of play that is typical of geared systems. If you hear a high-pitched squeal, you’ve probably got too tight a mesh; back off just a touch.
- Blade test: Once installed, gently spin the spur and pinion for a minute or two at low throttle to seat the gear teeth and to ensure the grub screws have properly engaged the shaft. Re-torque the grub screws after the initial run as the heat may cause slight loosening.
- Preventing slip: Use a drop of thread-lock (blue Loctite recommended) on the grub screws. Let it cure for a few minutes before running at speed.
- Break-in period: A light break-in of 3-5 minutes at low to mid throttle can help seating; after that, perform a confident test run to ensure the mesh doesn’t shift. Do not push the motor into full throttle during break-in. A gentle warm-up will do.

## Durability and maintenance
- Cleaning: After a run, wipe down the gear and shaft to remove dust and debris. If you ride on wet surfaces, dry it quickly to prevent corrosion on exposed steel surfaces.
- Lubrication: Pinions generally do not require heavy lubrication; a light coat of dry graphite or a thin film of light oil on the gear teeth can help reduce wear and noise. Avoid heavy oils as they can attract dust and gunk.
- Replacement: If you start seeing chips on the teeth or if the gear becomes badly worn, it’s time to replace. A worn pinion can degrade mesh with your spur and cause more issues than a fresh pair.
- Bearing health: Gear wear can also put extra load on the motor bearings due to misalignment or slippage. Check bearings during maintenance and replace if needed, especially if you notice unusual sounds or vibration.

In our durability testing, steel pinions showed excellent wear resistance, especially on rough track days, and held up well under high-torque, short-duration runs.

## Comparisons
- Versus aluminum pinions: Steel pinions resist wear far better, especially in high-torque scenarios. Aluminum can deform or wear quickly under heavy loads, causing mesh changes and inconsistent gearing. The trade-off: steel is heavier and can transmit more heat to the rest of the drivetrain and the motor. On a well-ventilated, temperature-controlled track, steel is typically the more durable choice.
- Versus plastic or nylon: Plastic gears are cheaper and lighter but wear out far quicker under high power or aggressive use. For a sprint race or bashing on uneven surfaces, steel is the safer option for reliability and longevity.
- Versus other 32P steels: Not all steels are created equal; some brands use higher-grade steel or improved heat treatment, which can yield better performance. The key is to look for comments about manufacturing quality, tooth finish, and how quickly the gear seats into mesh.

## Troubleshooting and tips
- If you hear grinding: you likely have misalignment or too tight a mesh. Loosen screws, re-seat, and re-tighten with a small amount of thread lock.
- If you see tooth chipping early: check your spur alignment; a misaligned pinion can cause uneven wear that leads to early teeth chipping.
- If you experience heat: consider increasing the mesh gap slightly or switching to a larger spur if your motor is overloading. Better still, consider airflow improvements around your motor and gear case.
- If noise is loud: check the gear teeth for burrs and ensure the bore is properly seated on the shaft.
