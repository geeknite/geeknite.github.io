## Introduction
If you thought the Green Horde had enough greenskins to keep you busy, think again. Zombicide Green Horde Friends and Foes Expansion lands like a vegetarian burrito packed with spicy survivors, greener-than-green foes, and a bunch of small rules that somehow feel vital rather than fiddly. It is, in short, the sort of expansion that makes you grin at the prospect of exploring a new corner of the same old sandbox. And yes, there are new survivors who can talk to plants, new foes who have more personality than your last date, and enough tokens to start a small city’s worth of paperwork.

In this review we unpack what the expansion actually adds, how it affects the balance and the vibe, and whether it makes sense for your playgroup as a standalone purchase or as a companion to the core game. We will also compare this to other Zombicide expansions and drop a few battle-tested strategies you can use in your next session.


## What is in the box
The Friends and Foes expansion arrives with a carefully curated stack of additions that feel purposeful rather than filler. Here is the quick rundown of what you should expect in the tray:

- A new Survivor deck featuring four to six survivors who each bring a distinct flavor to the game
- A fresh set of Foes that lean into the Friends and Foes theme, often with strange alliances and new mechanics
- A handful of new Bosses and Boss traits that ramp up the late-game tension
- Additional Terrain and Objective cards to refresh mission variety
- New tokens, dice, and a small rulebook that clarifies the interactions between the core game and the expansion
- A foldable reference sheet to help you avoid flipping to the base rulebook every other turn

If you’re the sort who loves a nicely curated loot box, you’ll appreciate the packaging and the quality of the components. The tokens are thick enough to survive a coffee-and-snack-laden game night, and the plastic miniatures have a little more charm than your average cardboard standee. The art direction continues the earthy, slightly grim vibe of the Green Horde, with a touch more whimsy in the Friends cards.

## New Survivors: who are these green palm readers?
The expansion’s Survivors are written with a light touch of humor and a surprising amount of heart. They feel like characters who would exist in a tavern world where goblins politely argue about who cut the bread. Here are the four standout survivors you’ll want to try first:

### The Gleaner and the Grasp
- Role: Support/Control
- Abilities: This duo can gather supplies from the map and turn them into temporary advantages for the team. Think of them as the party’s logistics nerds who still punch zombies with gusto.
- Playstyle: Good for beginners who want to dip a toe into the survivorship pool without feeling overwhelmed by combat optimizations.

### Fern Caller Lio
- Role: Ranged scout with nature themed buffs
- Abilities: Lio can call on plant allies to create temporary barricades or disrupt enemy lines. It’s the closest we get to Druidic magic in a zombie town, which is delightfully silly and surprisingly effective.
- Playstyle: Excellent for players who like to set up zones and control the board from a distance, while keeping pressure on walkers from behind hedges.

### Bristleback Toma
- Role: Tank with a soft spot for friendly faces
- Abilities: Bristleback can soak a lot of damage, protect weaker allies, and get a few extra actions under pressure. He is your classic meat shield with a bit more personality.
- Playstyle: For players who enjoy frontline brawls and creating space for the rest of the crew to operate.

### Willow the Snaremaker
- Role: Trap creator and ambush specialist
- Abilities: Willow lays small traps and can manipulate zombie routes, turning the horde into a feeding ground for clever players.
- Playstyle: Great for players who love mind games and tactical planning. Willow rewards careful map reading rather than pure brute force.

In short, the new survivors bring a bit of thematic flair and a lot of practical utility. Each one can shift how your squad handles crowd control, loot sharing, and mission pacing. If you enjoy synergy between survivorship and board control, you’ll likely keep the Fern Caller and Willow in heavy rotation.


## New Foes: friends and foes, in a more literal sense
The Friends and Foes expansion introduces antagonists who sometimes ally with your survivors for a moment, or at least offer an interesting moral dilemma. The design philosophy here is to complicate the standard zombie meta without turning the game into a puzzle you solve by shouting numbers. The new foes bring some refreshing twists:

- Blight Witches who can heal or corrupt zombie waves depending on the card draw
- Grove Brutes that demand a chivalry of sorts, forcing you to coordinate team actions to survive their charges
- Whispering Hardshells that lure survivors into missteps by playing with line of sight and distance

The net effect is a more dynamic encounter table. You won’t be staring at the same handful of zombie types every game; the new foes require your group to adapt on the fly and to coordinate utility and positioning more deliberately. This is a welcome change for late-night sessions that risk growing repetitive.

The balance between survivors and foes remains the heart of Zombicide, and this expansion nudges it in a direction that feels playful but not gimmicky. You still rely on your team’s collective wits, but the new cards give you enough leverage to tilt the odds when things go sideways.


## Mechanics and balance changes
If you’ve played Zombicide before, you know the core loop: you activate your survivors, you kill zombies, you complete objectives, you go home knowing you could have done better. The Friends and Foes expansion nudges that loop in several directions:

- Card-driven synergy: The new Survivor and Foe cards create micro-synergies that reward clever pairings. Sometimes you’ll want to pair a Fern Caller with a Willow trap to corral zombies into a kill-zone and maximize XP.
- Tactical variety: The new tokens and a few terrain cards open up different line-of-sight plays and trap layouts. It’s a nice nudge away from the board always looking the same by mid-game.
- Difficulty curve: The expansion doesn’t slam you into the cauldron of despair; instead, it adds complexity in a measured way, which is ideal for groups that enjoy a bit more strategic planning without a steep learning cliff.

A notable design note is that the expansion remains friendly to players who are new to the franchise. The new survivors come with straightforward abilities, and the foes, while trickier, don’t feel opaque. You still rely on the core tactics you know — blocking, funneling, and picking fights you can win — but with a few extra levers to pull.


## Thematic and aesthetic notes
The Green Horde has always balanced a grounded, nature-forward aesthetic with a pinch of whimsy. This expansion nudges that balance in a good way. The art remains serviceable and expressive, especially on the new survivor cards where each character has a tiny personality vignette that shows up in the flavor text and ability icons. The new terrain cards look cohesive with the base game, and the additional tokens feel like they belong on the same table rather than a mismatched add-on.

If you’re playing with a mixed group of players who enjoy roleplay flavor, the new survivor cards deliver moments you can retell later. A Willows trap turning a droning zombie wave into a dramatic block move, or a Bristleback’s taunt giving a comically inept zombie a chance to stumble into a prepared spike, are all the little joys that make Zombicide nights memorable.


## Play sessions and pacing
Let’s get practical. How does this expansion actually feel at the table? In our test games, we found that:

- The pace is pleasantly variable. You get spiced-up turns with the new abilities that feel meaningful without bogging you down with reams of text.
- Group composition matters more than ever. The expansion rewards players who adopt a diversified team: a trap creator, a tank, a support, and a ranged specialist can work wonders when you coordinate.
- Mission variety scales well. The new objectives offer a handful of fresh routes to success, which keeps sessions from grinding to a halt in the late game.

If you’ve played the base game enough to have your own preferred rhythms, you’ll appreciate the expansion’s ability to add new rhythms without destroying your familiar tempo. If you’re a completionist who wants every possible combo, you’ll be delighted by the number of scenario variations this pack unlocks.


## Solo and co-op viability
Zombicide shines as a cooperative experience, and this expansion does not derail that. Solo play remains viable with the right set of survivors and a bit of patience. The new survivors tend to scale well because their abilities are not overly reliant on teammates to activate their strengths. Co-op synergy becomes a bit more critical here, as you’ll often want one or two players who can set up traps or draw fire while others execute the plan.

If you primarily play solo, you may want to lean toward Willow and Fern Caller Lio for their board-control and ranged utility. In a full group, Willow and Bristleback combine to create a frontline that can still operate with a couple of open lanes for ranged or trap-based strategies.


## How it compares to other Zombicide expansions
The Zombicide ecosystem has a healthy rotation of expansions, each with its own flavor. Where this Friends and Foes pack stands out is in its balance between thematic flavor and practical board-room utility. It doesn’t come off as purely cosmetic or as something that requires an entirely new playstyle to enjoy. If you loved the Green Horde baseline, you will recognize the DNA here and still find fresh ideas to play with.

Compared to large box expansions that overhaul mechanics, this one sits in a middle ground: it offers meaningful new content that integrates smoothly with existing systems and lets the group decide how deep they want to go into new tactics.


## Strategy tips for surviving the expansion
- Prioritize map control early. Use Fern Caller Lio to put up plant-based barriers that slow down zombie advances and buy your team precious turns to complete objectives.
- Pair Willow with Bristleback for a reliable frontline. Willow sets traps, Bristleback soaks hits, and your team uses the momentary gap to strike back.
- Don’t ignore scavenging. The new Survivor cards reward efficient loot generation. A well-timed Gleaner action can swing the resource economy in your favor for the rest of the mission.
- Plan for the long game. Some Foes scale with the number of objectives completed, so you’ll want to manage zombie waves while still chasing the mission goals.
- Stay flexible. The best-laid plan often goes sideways in Zombicide. The expansion rewards players who adjust on the fly and keep the team’s cards in circulation rather than hoarding them.

## Component quality and production value
Zombicide expansions generally win on production quality, and Friends and Foes is no exception. The tokens are sturdy, the board-ready components are legible, and the new cards fit cleanly into the core deck management system. The new miniatures feel appropriately sized for a typical table and hold up well in playtesting photos if you like to document your campaigns for social media.

One small gripe you may notice is that the rulebook occasionally borrows heavily from the base game’s language. While not a dealbreaker, it may require a quick skim to refresh your memory on certain edge cases. The quick-start rules sheet that comes with the expansion helps mitigate this, but if you’re a rules-lawyer by temperament, you’ll still want to reference the base game occasionally.


## Narrative vibe and replayability
The Friends and Foes expansion leans into a more character-driven vibe than some of the other mechanical overhauls in the Zombicide line. The survivors you recruit have personality arcs that you can tease out over multiple sessions, and the enemy design makes you rethink how you approach a map entirely. Replayability is good here, in part because of the way the new objectives mix with the terrain cards to create fresh mission setups.

If you enjoy community talk about campaign nights, you’ll appreciate the way this expansion seems to invite storytelling. Certain moments — Willow outsmarting a horde with a trap while Lio calls a plant shield into place — practically beg to be turned into a little vignette for the tabletop diary.


## Final verdict and who should buy this
If you own Zombicide Green Horde and want to add a healthy dose of variety without reinventing your entire play style, this expansion is a solid purchase. It offers new survivors with distinct personalities, fresh foes that mix in interesting ways, and mission variety that keeps sessions from sagging into repetition.

- Best for groups that enjoy roleplay vibes and tactical positioning
- Great for players who want to extend campaign nights without forcing a whole new system onto the table
- A good match for families and friendly gaming groups who want a cooperative but challenging experience

If you’re already maxed out on expansions and are looking for something that reconfigures the game rather than expanding it, you might want to hold off for a future release. That said, if you love the Green Horde aesthetic and you crave new ways to outthink the horde, the Friends and Foes expansion is worth a serious look.
