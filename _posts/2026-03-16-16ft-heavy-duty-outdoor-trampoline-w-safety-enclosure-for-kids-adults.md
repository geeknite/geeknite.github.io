## Safety features and best practices
Safety is the backbone of this product. The enclosure is designed to keep jumpers inside the safe zone while the padding reduces the risk of hard impact with the springs or frame. Still, there are a few best practices to maximize safety:

- Supervision is non-negotiable for kids under 12, and highly recommended for older kids who love to attempt backflips and mid-air stunts. Even with a net, a little adult oversight can prevent tiny splashes of chaos from becoming headlines.
- Respect weight limits and avoid overcrowding the mat. Multiple jumpers are fun, but too many bodies in one bounce can lead to collisions that are loud, chaotic, and occasionally spectacular in a not-so-good way.
- Enforce proper footwear and clothing. Avoid loose items that could catch on the net or frame. The unicorn horn hat you wore to be funny? Probably not a good idea on trampoline day.
- Secure the enclosure zipper when jumping. It’s tempting to lean toward the door when the net is in the wrong position, but that is not how you maximize safety. The zipper must remain fully closed during use to prevent accidental egress from the net.

## Maintenance and long-term care
Long-term care for a trampoline means routine checks and a little bit of preventive care. Here are some practical tips:

- Inspect springs and mat for signs of wear every season. Replace any stretched springs or damaged mesh promptly.
- Clean the mat and frame periodically to remove dirt and moisture that can cause corrosion or mold growth.
- Keep the enclosure zipper in good shape. A stuck zipper is not only frustrating; it’s dangerous during high-energy sessions.
- Store the enclosure and pads properly if you plan to leave the unit unused for extended periods. A dry, shaded spot helps extend the life of foam padding and fabric components.

By keeping maintenance manageable, you’ll make sure the trampoline keeps a high bounce factor, safe environment, and family-friendly charm for years to come.
