### What you get in the Wulfsburg box
The Wulfsburg expansion is a large box by any standard. It ships with a handful of new hero characters, a stack of double sided player boards, a hefty pile of zombie miniatures, and a swath of terrain tiles designed to extend the map and introduce new environmental hazards. The components feel familiar if you’ve done a lot of Zombicide already; if you haven’t, the sheer volume can be a bit intimidating at first glance. The plastic quality is typically competitive for a board game of this scale: sturdy, with clean injection points and little visible flash on the miniatures—unless you go hunting for it, which we did, because we’re basically product testers in our spare time.

The Wulfsburg map pieces tend to show a slight variance in color tone compared to the base set, which is a nice aesthetic touch. The new figures—vicious werewolves, hulking brutes, and cunning necromancers—are all in the standard Zombicide scale, which means they fit nicely with the rest of your collection. The quality of the sculpts varies a bit; some are detailed enough to be showcased on a shelf, others are more functional than flamboyant. ## The Dice Tower: history, hype, and hardware
The dice tower is one of those accessories that can be either a magical speed boost or a decorative paperweight. In the Wulfsburg pack, the Dice Tower is promised to centralize dice rolls, keep the board tidy, and spare your friends from the dreaded dice-throwing mishaps that result in someone misplacing a critical 6 while everyone scrambles for a new rule interpretation. If you have played enough Zombicide, you’ll know there is a rhythm to rolling dice: you jostle for space, you roll, you look at the result, you either celebrate or groan. A good dice tower can speed that up, cut down on rearranging, and keep the table from turning into an accidental accidental dice tornado. A bad dice tower requires you to relearn how to stack, tilt, and release dice without them rolling across the table like confetti in a hurricane.

The Wulfsburg Dice Tower in particular feels like a midrange product: not a premium, die-cast dream, but sturdy enough to justify the price. You can set it on the table, hear that satisfying click as the dice bounce down the chute, and then watch as the druidic necromancer of chaos manifests a double six followed by a spate of failed saves. The engineering is straightforward, with clean slots for standard D6s and a decent height to prevent a lot of misthrows. It’s not the kind of piece you’ll regret buying for the next two decades, but it’s the sort that might become a go-to accessory for your next ten campaigns at least.

From the perspective of someone who has used dozens of plastic components, the Dice Tower is at least a helpful sanity machine during long sessions. It reduces the number of times you have to reach for the dice cup, which is a blessing if you’re playing in a city that frowns on coffee-stained wooden tables. It’s also a minor performance boost at the table; you can move quickly from round to round as your party takes on the next wave of werewolves, and the dice landing in the trapdoor feel somehow less random and more cinematic—a small psychological win when you’re wrestling with the despair of a failed heroic strike.

## Hardly used plastic: truth, myth, and practical tips
The phrase hardly used plastic is a little cheeky, and yet it captures a genuine phenomenon. Some boxes arrive with pristine sprues, straight from the workshop floor, untouched by human hands except for the occasional factory QA review. Other boxes arrive with sprues that look like they’ve weathered a long voyage through the rough seas of a thousand hobbyists. The Wulfsburg set sits somewhere in the middle—sculpted well enough that you won’t mistake it for a rescue mission, and with plastic that is forgiving if you decide to paint the pieces later. The real magic happens when you assemble and decide what to do about the abundance of parts. A lot of Zombicide players will tell you that the first thing you should do when you crack open a big box is to inventory the sprues, identify any flash you can quickly trim, and plan out the color palette before you start to separate parts from the runners.

In our test copy, the plastic was surprisingly well-organized. The runners separated cleanly and did not force you into a PhD-level degree in plastic anatomy to identify which limb belongs to which body. The colors on the miniatures, while not wildly vibrant, do hold well under light painting. If you’re going to assemble a horde of undead in a single session, the last thing you want is to be hunting the correct head for a mounted goblin while a crowd of zombies is encroaching on your living space. The Wulfsburg components are not the easiest to assemble in a single go, but they are manageable with standard hobby tools and a bit of patience. If you’re worried about the “hardly used” phenomenon affecting your box, you’ll be pleased to know that in most cases the plastics will tolerate a bit of rough handling during punching and trimming without fracture.

For folks who like to keep everything pristine on their shelves, the Wulfsburg box benefits from a little maintenance after punching. A light sanding of mold lines and a quick wipe with a microfiber cloth can prevent a dusty haze from blurring the details under the table lamps. If you’re a color enthusiast, you might consider priming the models before painting, which will also give you a clearer baseline to work from when you want to highlight the veins on a drawn sword or the fur on a werewolf model. The bottom line is that the plastics in this set are robust enough to withstand regular play, not so fragile that you’ll be worrying about micro-scratches in your first session, and not so brittle that a sneeze would cause a casualty count among your archers.

## Gameplay impressions: does the box actually change the game?
Zombicide Black Plague is a teamwork-and-chaos exercise, and Wulfsburg adds more tiles, more monsters, and more objectives. The gameplay loop remains familiar: you move your hero across the board, you roll to attack, you search for loot, you avoid or engage the undead, and you try to rescue civilians or complete an objective before the world ends in a cacophony of bones and the blare of triumphant victory music selected from 8 bit memory. The expansion adds new mission types and some fresh tactical wrinkles that can alter your approach. In practice, that means more planning around the next wave, a few new strategy decisions when you choose your heroic class, and a few moments where you realize you’re chasing a table full of werewolves with a plan that falls apart after three rounds.

From a co op standpoint, the expansion shines when you have the time to set up properly. The map tiles are larger than the base game, which allows you to run multiple crews and set piece events that feel cinematic rather than random. It also means your rounds can stretch a little longer, since you’re coordinating with more teammates and trying to optimize the route through a den of wolves and necromancers. If your group tends to bicker about turn order, you’ll appreciate that Wulfsburg’s layout tends to create natural hotspots of action, which can help your team stay engaged instead of drifting into a yawning stalemate.

On the dice tower front, the added precision helps when you are trying to resolve contested results, particularly for critical hits or saves against a particularly nasty pack of werewolves. The dice tower does not rewrite the laws of combat, but it does create a psychological edge: you’re more likely to feel in control of your fate when you see the dice fall down a chute rather than land on the table and vanish under a chair. It’s not a magic bullet, but it’s a nice little morale boost for players who sometimes feel their rolls are conspiring against them.

If you’re curious about how this plays with the base set, our recommended approach is to integrate one or two of the new tiles in a standard mission. This gives you a taste of the Wulfsburg flavor without overwhelming the group with too many moving parts. For players who like a bigger challenge, you can push the new map to its limits and try the more difficult scenarios. ## Components breakdown: minies, maps, and more
### Miniatures
The zombie minions in the Wulfsburg add a nice visual variety to the field. The werewolves, in particular, are visually striking, with dynamic poses and a sense of motion that helps them read well on the table during a flurry of activity. The bigger monsters provide a nice contrast to the standard zombies, allowing you to stage epic boss-like moments without needing a separate boss box. The sculpts on human heroes feel consistent with the rest of the line, with enough variation to help players identify their favorites quickly during a high pressure moment.

### Terrain and tiles
The Wulfsburg map set expands the playable area significantly. It introduces new terrains that can affect line of sight, movement, and the pacing of the game. If you have a preferred play style, you might find you lean into the new terrain features to create more dynamic engagements. The tiles themselves are sturdy and stand up to repeated play, which is essential since a board full of adventurers and undead can create a fair bit of shoving and sliding during the heat of battle. The color palette remains faithful to the Black Plague look, with muted earth tones that suit the medieval fantasy theme without clashing with the color schemes of your other Zombicide sets.

### Cards and dice
New cards and dice accompany the expansion, offering fresh weapons, abilities, and event triggers. The balance of these new options is reasonable; you won’t find yourself locked into a forced meta, but you will have to adapt to how the new gear interacts with the existing pool. The dice themselves are well-formed and comfortable to roll; they have the tactile feel you expect from a modern board game, with enough weight to feel satisfying without being a clangy noise-maker that disrupts a quiet living room strategy session.

## Aesthetic and assembly notes
Aesthetics matter, even in a game you intend to beat to death with a strategy guide and a heaping spoonful of sarcasm. The Wulfsburg content holds up well visually. The character models have crisp details that pop once you apply a basic coat of primer. If you enjoy painting, you can lean into the theming and give your crew armor a metallic sheen or add a wash to deepen the creases in the wolves’ pelts. If you’re a stickler for color harmony across your game collection, it’s worth noting that you may want to pull a palette that blends nicely with your current figurines. The last thing you want is a table that looks like a color chart in a children’s art class, with one model bright as a cherry and the next one a desaturated emerald.

In terms of assembly, the Wulfsburg miniatures are not extremely fiddly, though there are certain pieces that require a patient hand. A trick that our team uses is to separate the pieces by type before you start painting or assembling. This reduces the chance of mixing up limbs with torsos and speeds up the process when you’ve got a coffee-free window for hobby time. And if you’re planning to use the Dice Tower at the table, you can set it up early and let it do its ceremonial roll duties while you discuss strategy with your teammates. The result is a calmer, more organized session that still feels like a chaotic, heroic dungeon crawl—the very essence of what Zombicide aims to deliver.

## Final verdict and recommendations
Summary in one breath: Zombicide Black Plague Wulfsburg with the Dice Tower is a solid package for players who crave bigger maps, more monsters, and a few quality-of-life improvements at the table. It’s not the most revolutionary expansion in the history of table top co ops, but it nails the core experience—cooperative chaos, strategic planning under pressure, and that satisfying moment when your dice finally cooperate after two rounds of rolling snake eyes on critical hits. The components are robust enough for regular play, the dice tower adds a tangible sense of control, and the overall presentation remains faithful to the beloved Black Plague aesthetic. If you’re on the fence about sinking money into a sizeable add-on, ask yourself: do you want more werewolves chasing your heroes, or do you want your decisions to feel a little more theatrical? If the answer is yes to both, this box will likely become a staple in your collection.

If you’re a completionist who wants the entire Zombicide line in one shelf, you’ll likely grab Wulfsburg and not look back. If you’re more of a casual player who loves the meme of painting brave heroes in dragon-slaying glory but hates the idea of extra setup time, you might want to lean into a smaller expansion and mix in the Wulfsburg missions only occasionally. It’s a fine balance, and your mileage will vary depending on your group size, your painting ambition, and how much you enjoy the occasional campaign arc that ties into your world-building vibe.

### Recommendation grid
- For fans of heavy co op play with a story driven feeling: High recommendation
- For players who dislike large box expansions and extra setup: Moderate recommendation
- For painters and display hobbyists: Solid to high recommendation if you enjoy a few extra minis to paint and show off
- For players who want maximum table-time efficiency: Strong recommendation if you embrace the Dice Tower and the new terrain layouts
