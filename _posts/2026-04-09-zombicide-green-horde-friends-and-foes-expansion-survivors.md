# Zombicide Green Horde: Friends and Foes Expansion — Survivors &

If you cracked open Zombicide Green Horde and thought, “Boy, these orcs really need more friends and more foes to fight,” congratulations, you were listening to the right tabletop deity. The Friends and Foes expansion, part of the Green Horde family, drops into the base game like a brass-knuckled grenade wrapped in a cuddle, blending new survivors, new foes, and new motives for mischief. And yes, it is as chaotic and flavorful as a goblin at a loot-filled bazaar. Here at Geeknite, we strapped on our favorite ghillie cloak, sharpened our joke knives, and ran this through the wringer to tell you whether this expansion makes the orc-infested swamp feel bigger, badder, and somehow more ridiculous.

## What is this expansion, exactly?

Zombicide Green Horde: Friends and Foes is not a standalone game; it is a carefully angled additional flavor shot for the base Green Horde experience. It introduces new survivors who bring oddball abilities and quirky backstories, plus a set of foes who add variety to the undead lineup. The core aim remains the same: survive until the end of the hour-long hellscape while clear-cutting your way through waves of generic undead who now have some personality and even some shade of strategy beyond “move toward the loud noise.” If you enjoy the Green Horde vibe—mud, magic, improvised weapons, and a slightly chaotic approach to resource management—then the expansion leans into those strengths while nudging you to think in new directions.

### What’s new at a glance

- New survivors with distinct powers that interact with the Horde’s nature and the characters you’ve brought from the base game.
- New foe types that aren’t just stronger zombies but also smarter and a touch more annoying when you’re in a pinch.
- Fresh encounter cards and tactical choices that force you to balance friendship with the Horde’s fickle ethics. Yes, this is the expansion that makes you ask, “Do we ally with the greenskin or kick them in the shins?”
- Compatibility with the core game and existing expansions, so you don’t need a PhD in confounding rulebooks to integrate this into a night of gaming with friends.

## Inside the box: components and quality

The box contents cheerfully embrace the Green Horde aesthetic—earthy greens, rusty metals, and a palpable sense of damp dungeon. The survivors come with full-color character boards, miniature tokens, and card decks that look sturdy enough to survive a small apocalypse. The foes—orc raiders and a few undead permutations—have miniatures with more personality than your average board-game figure, sporting punny names and creepily confident poses. The production quality feels in line with Zombicide’s recent offerings: good plastic, clear sculpting, and punchy color-coding to keep track of threats even when your brain is half-zombie anyway.

The board itself holds up under the strain of extra units and extra rules. Card stock for encounter and equipment cards is solid, not flimsy, and the art continues to reflect the grim humor that makes Zombicide feel like a dark fantasy carnival rather than a grim slog through doom. If you’re a stickler for table presence, the Friends and Foes expansion adds a lot of personality to the table without making the aesthetic feel alien to the base game’s vibe.

## Survivors and foes: who you’ll be playing with (and against)

In Friends and Foes, you’ll meet a cadre of new survivors who each bring a flavor to your run. Some are mercenary-minded, chasing glory with a blade that’s seen better days; others are more resourceful, turning scraps into practical solutions when every weapon is a risk. Each survivor has a unique ability that can shift the tempo of a round—from boosting a friend’s survivability to enabling a risky gambit that could pay off big if you pull it off correctly.

New foes are not simply “more damage.” They come with distinctive traits that influence how you approach a given corridor, doorway, or courtyard. Some foes appear in waves, requiring you to reassess your plan mid-session. Others have special “morale” or “ally” tokens that enable temporary alliances with the greenish host—yes, rule of cool takes the wheel here, and it’s glorious when it clicks.

If you’re a fan of tactical play and asymmetric threats, you’ll enjoy the juxtaposition of survivors with unusual abilities against foes who demand you to rethink positioning, line of sight, and resource allocation. The design language leans into “play smarter, not just harder,” and for longtime Zombicide players, that’s a refreshing shake-up rather than a gimmick.

### A few notes on balance

The balance between new survivors and new foes is thoughtful, not gimmicky. The designers didn’t jam in a handful of power cards that break the game; instead, you’ll notice synergy between the survivors’ abilities and the foes’ taunts, creating moments where a single decision can swing a round. It’s satisfying when you pull off a clever trap, bait a zombie horde into a chokepoint, and use a new survivor’s ability to turn a near-disaster into a triumphant exit.

However, like all expansions, it does raise the ceiling of difficulty. If your table tends to grind through rounds with a casual approach, the Friends and Foes content can feel a touch punishing—in a good way, if you enjoy crunch. If you’re a die-hard cooperative purist who hates any element of risk, you’ll want to set expectations and perhaps adjust the number of foes to taste. The good news: you can scale this fairly easily with the base game’s standard difficulty levels and a couple of house-rules.

## Mechanisms and how they affect your playstyle

### Surviving with new powers

The new survivors introduce a spectrum of tactical options. Some abilities reward cooperative play—like buffing a neighbor’s shield or sharing equipment in a pinch—while others are more self-contained, encouraging a “trust-but-verify” approach with your teammates. You’ll want to map out who benefits from what and plan several rounds ahead. The glory of Zombicide lies in planning for failures—because you will fail, hilariously and often—and then using that failure to pivot into something better next turn.

### Foes that force you to move

New foes aren’t just “shoot more and cry later.” They’re designed to push you toward smarter positioning, better resource management, and more careful consideration of line of sight. Some of them push you to split up your team, which can be a trap if you’re not careful, but also a route to escape if you’re feeling clever. The expansion therefore teases out a different rhythm: you won’t always be able to rely on a single meat shield; instead, you’ll be juggling multiple threats while trying to coordinate a safe extraction or a calculated sprint for a critical objective.

### Encounters and randomness

As with many Zombicide modules, randomness plays a big role. The friends and foes you encounter are shuffled into new encounter decks, so even if you’ve played the base game to death, you’ll still face fresh combos that require quick adaptation. If you enjoy the unpredictability of a well-timed card draw and a perfect mix of luck and skill, you’ll be delighted. If randomness makes you grumpy, you can use the base game’s common-sense approach—tweak encounter decks a notch or two, or designate a single “boss wave” for a more structured finale.

## Theme, flavor, and narrative drive

The expansion leans into a punchy, slightly chaotic vibe that suits the Green Horde’s spirit. You’ll hear lines of dialogue that feel lifted from a tavern skirmish rather than a pristine war room. The survivors bicker, negotiate, and occasionally trade a few precious shreds of loot, which may or may not be worth the risk. The antagonists’ designs embrace a gobsmacking mix of menace and humor, so the game remains approachable even when the stakes feel existential—because someone has to survive this swamp, and we’d rather it be us.

If you’re into lore, this expansion offers some nice world-building breadcrumbs that developers tucked into the card flavor text and mini bios. It’s not a full narrative campaign, but it rewards players who enjoy roleplaying moments and in-game storytelling. You’ll find yourself creating little backstories for two-sentence survivor cards and weaving them into the way you treat each encounter. That, dear reader, is where Zombicide shines: you’re not just a pair of dice and a blade; you’re the captain of a ragtag crew that’s learning to survive in a world that actively wants you dead.

## The setup and the run: how long does it take, really?

Setup for the expansion is a touch longer than the base game because you’re adding new components and might be juggling more tokens. Don’t panic; it’s not a forensic audit. The rulebook lays out a clear order: separate new survivors, prepare new foe decks, set up encounter markers, and then blend the new cards into the existing piles. In terms of time, you’re looking at an extra 5–15 minutes for setup depending on how you organize your table. The actual playtime remains roughly in the same neighborhood as the base game—roughly 60 minutes for a standard conflict, though desperate players might stretch it longer with ambitious plans and a chorus of dramatic groans.

Gameplay flow remains familiar: you choose survivors, you move through zones, you fight zombies, you complete objectives, you survive the chaos. The difference is that the “minutes to glory” might be spread across more moving parts and a few more decision points. The suspense of choosing to push your luck on a risky encounter or to retreat and regroup grows more pronounced, which is exactly what you want when you’re craving a more dynamic, bite-sized campaign experience.

## Strategies, tips, and house rules

- Start with your strongest synergy pairings. If a survivor’s ability improves a nearby ally, place them close enough to take advantage of a shield wall, and keep an eye on where the zombies cluster. The Boss Horde you’ll encounter later in the expansion loves a close-quarters party, so don’t let your team become a buffet line.
- Use the new foe dynamics to set up “bait and switch” moves. Lure a few enemies into an advantageous chokepoint and then swing in with a well-timed burst from a survivor who can handle a couple of foes at once. It’s not just about raw firepower; it’s about intelligent crowd control.
- Rotate your focus. The new cards sometimes reward split objectives. Don’t be afraid to split your team to conquer two fronts; just be sure you’ve got a plan to reconverge before the orcish reinforcements become overwhelming.
- Don’t be afraid to tweak. If your group is near-legendary at speedrunning the base game, consider slightly reducing the number of foes in the crucial early waves, or designate a “soft difficulty” mode for your first playthrough. The point of expansion content is to expand your joy, not to ruin your friendship over a dice roll.

## How it compares to other expansions

If you’ve dabbled in other Zombicide expansions, Friends and Foes slots in as a thoughtful mid-range addition rather than a giant leap in complexity. It respects the base game’s learning curve while giving players a handful of new tools to experiment with. In that sense, it resembles the best of the post-release content: it doesn’t overwrite the core experience; it augments it in ways that feel additive rather than disruptive.

Compared to other Green Horde expansions, this one leans more toward roleplaying opportunities and strategic positioning than pure punch-through power. If you prefer simple “more zombies, more trouble” gameplay, you might find some moments a bit more than you bargained for. Conversely, if you enjoy a little more puzzle-solving within your zombie carnage, you’ll likely savor the balance and theme here.

## The verdict: should you buy it?

Short version: yes, if you already love Zombicide Green Horde and you crave a fresh layer of tactical interaction with a dash of humor. The expansion succeeds by weaving new survivor personalities into the Horde’s world, and by giving the new foes a distinct rhythm that keeps you guessing. It’s not a must-have for casual groups who only play once every full moon; it’s a strong enhancement for regular players who want to push their table toward more cunning decisions, sharper teamwork, and a few more moments of “I can’t believe that just happened.”

If you sit on the fence, here’s a quick heuristic: do you enjoy the base game’s crunchy decisions and chaotic energy? Do you want more storytelling possibilities and a richer board dynamic? If the answer is yes to both, then this expansion is a safe bet. If you’re purely after “more zombies = more chaos” without new tactics to master, you’ll still enjoy the novel tempo shifts, but the payoff might feel smaller. Either way, you’re not likely to regret adding this to your shelf unless you’re actively trying to cut down your gaming budget by 80% and pretend the zombie apocalypse is a staff meeting.

## How to incorporate this into your night (and keep it fresh)

- Alternate between “Survivor Showcase” games where you deliberately test a new survivor’s full kit and “Foes Feast” games where you lean into the new enemy design with a slightly tighter objective. This helps you savor both the roleplay and the tactical aspects without feeling overwhelmed.
- Mix and match with other expansions from the Green Horde line. The rules are designed to interoperate with other content, allowing you to craft a persistent campaign feel, even in one-off sessions.
- Record your party’s performance. A quick note-taking habit (which survivors performed best, which foe type caused the largest headache) can help you tailor future sessions and keep your table talking long after the board is cleared.

If you’re curious to see how others approach this expansion, you can explore community-driven playthroughs on external platforms and compare your style to theirs. For those who appreciate curated insights, a few players have documented their strategic evolutions as they tested the expansion across multiple sessions, noting which survivor combos consistently outperformed others under particular conditions. It’s the kind of meta that makes you feel smart about your dice, which is exactly what we deserve after the zombie apocalypse arrives in your living room.

## Final thoughts and recommendation

Zombicide Green Horde: Friends and Foes is a well-composed expansion that respects the base game’s DNA while seasoning it with fresh personality and threats. It rewards players who enjoy teamwork, careful positioning, and a willingness to embrace a little chaos in service of a more dynamic and flavorful session. It’s not a radical overhaul; it’s a thoughtful enhancement that makes the swamp feel a touch more alive, a touch more dangerous, and a whole lot more entertaining.

If you’re a fan of the Green Horde and you’re looking for a reasonable way to extend the late-game tension with new survivor voices and foes that actually require you to think, this expansion is a solid pick. It doesn’t fix what wasn’t broken, and it doesn’t pretend to; instead, it adds a new layer of strategy and storytelling that fits the tone of Zombicide while inviting you to experiment with different team compositions and encounter flows.

For players who crave a light campaign feel without the commitment of a separate campaign box, Friends and Foes serves as a wonderful bridge. You’ll end sessions with a sense of accomplishment, a few new battle stories to tell, and an itch to run it again with a different combo of survivors and foes. And if you’re into the idea of more humor mixed into your grim horror, you’ll get plenty of that here—there are moments where the silliness clashes with the grim stakes in the best possible way.
