## What’s in the Starter Set?

The box is compact but surprisingly dense with character and atmosphere. You get everything you need to play a first campaign or a handful of one-shot mysteries that feel cinematic rather than statistical.

### The core contents

- A polished, art-forward quick-start rulebook that emphasizes narrative resolution over crunchy math. It teaches you how to approach scenes with mood and the “mist” mechanic without turning the table into a whiteboard of charts.
- A handful of ready-to-run adventures that are compact enough to be DM-friendly but expansive enough to spark a few nights of gaming before you realize you’ve chained the party to a conspiracy that’s barely holding itself together.
- Character sheets and templates. City of Mist uses a motif-based approach (your character’s “Guy, the Mist,” is less about a rigid class and more about who they are when the lights go out).
- A selection of tokens, maps, and clues that help ground the narrative in physical space. The set textures the table with noir vibes and gives players something tangible to grip while they argue about truth and myth.
- A starter deck of “tag” cards that players and the GM can leverage to modify outcomes. If you’ve ever wanted a game where you can literally say, “My tag is tea-shop owner who knows your name and your mom’s name,” City of Mist provides the mechanics to pull that off without turning the table into a long debate about balance.

The components feel well-made and purpose-built. The art direction leans into neon-laced noir with enough mythic flourishes to remind you that you’re not just rolling dice—you’re constructing a myth around a city that is tired, glamorous, and suspicious of its own reflection. The physical book is glossy without feeling fragile, and the cards are sturdy enough to survive a few table-flipping moments (which will inevitably happen if your squad discovers a clue that changes everything in the first scene).

### The starter set versus the core game

The Starter Set is a curated sampler rather than a full cookbook. It gives you the vibe, the rules-lite foundation, and a few ready-made stories to run. If you’re new to City of Mist, this is exactly what you want: a low-friction doorway into the larger world. If you already own the core rulebook, the Starter Set acts as a quick-start run-through, a discardable experiment to break in newcomers, and a stylish “cold open” to test the waters before you dive into a heavier campaign.

## System and Mechanics (in a nutshell)

City of Mist uses a narrative, tag-driven resolution system. The idea is simple: you propose what you’re trying to do, you declare tags (your character’s attributes, your gear, and your situational advantages), you roll a resolution die, and you interpret the results through the lens of your Monster, Mentor, or Myth motif. The Starter Set trims the fat and leans into storytelling moment-to-moment rather than long, complex formulas.

### The role of tags and motifs

Tags are the highlights of your character: what’s true about them, what they have, and what the city knows about them. A tag could be something like “Detective Tie,” “Street-Smart,” or “Dark Past.” In play, you lean on tags to justify your actions, and the GM uses tags to frame complications. The synergy is organic: you narrate your approach, the GM adds a twist, and the dice attempt to catch the line between what you intend and what actually happens.

The Starter Set doesn’t bury you in a sea of subsystems. It emphasizes the way you react and improvise—exactly the kind of play that leads to memorable moments like, “We expose the conspiracy by revealing the city’s favorite pastry shop as a front.” You laugh, you cheer, you realize you’ve just defeated a villain with a croissant and a confession.

### Dice, difficulty, and the art of not overcomplicating it

The resolution is designed to be quick and narrative-forward. The difficulty is abstract and determined by the story context. The GM uses a simple set of prompts to escalate or ease tension, and the players describe the outcomes they want to trigger via tags and motifs. The whole thing is more about telling a story together than crunching through a spreadsheet. If you’ve ever wanted a game where you can justify a rooftop chase by saying, “The city wants us to survive this,” City of Mist gives you that platform.

The Starter Set keeps the dice work approachable: you’ll probably be rolling a handful of dice and comparing outcomes to simple success criteria. There’s enough structure to keep the story from wandering into chaos, but enough flexibility to let players improvise without tripping over a rulebook highway of footnotes.

## Theme, Tone, and Immersion

If you crave a game that merges superhero myth with noir detective fiction, City of Mist is likely already your jam. The Starter Set nails that mood with visuals, tone, and a meta-horror beat that says, “You are what the city believes you are.” It’s not a grimdark apocalypse; it’s a city that believes in myths, even when those myths are used to excuse ordinary crime and the occasional heroic daredevil moment.

### Character creation that feels like choosing a disguise

Within the Starter Set, character creation is less about piling on attributes and more about choosing a believable identity that can change the story in new directions. You don’t pick a perfectly balanced superhero kit; you pick a persona, a set of myths you’re harnessing, and a couple of “tags” that can shift a scene from tense to cinematic. The joy of this approach is that you can get a hero-not-quite-perfectly-formed in the moment and still have a character that feels real by the end of night one.

### The vibe on the table

The art direction and design language of City of Mist celebrate mood: neon glow, rain-slicked streets, posters peeling off brick, and a city that feels alive and suspicious. The Starter Set captures this vibe with a compact aesthetic. Players lean in, not just to read mechanics, but to lean into the cinematic beat—the moment when a clue aligns with a motif and a twist lands with a satisfying thud.

## Gameplay Experience: Flow and Feel

If you’re looking for bite-sized sessions that still feel cinematic, the Starter Set hits a sweet spot. It’s not a one-shot in the sense of a published module you’ll forget; it’s more like a pilot episode for a longer series. You’ll walk away with a clear sense of your role in the city’s mythic ecosystem and enough hooks to spark a longer campaign if you want to keep the vibe alive.

### Pacing and session structure
The quick-start nature of the set makes it easy to run a session from character creation through a complete mystery in a few hours. You’ll likely run two or three short adventures to build your party’s relationships, test the tones of your table, and figure out what players love most—investigations, chases, or dramatic reveals. The pacing is controlled by you: you can zoom in on a single clue and dwell on its narrative implications, or you can sprint through a case in a single long night.

### Player agency and GM guidance
City of Mist’s design honors player creativity. The Starter Set gives just enough guidance—the core concepts and a few ready-to-run scenarios—so the new GM isn’t left to noodle in the dark. Veteran GMs will appreciate the succinctness: you can tailor the tone, amp up the noir weather, and weave in personal stakes without re-learning a mana-heavy rule tree.

### The social contract
One of the hidden strengths of the Starter Set is how it frames social play. It invites players to own their motifs and to respect each other’s narrative beats. If one player wants to lean into a melodrama, the set’s design supports that; if another wants a hard-nosed procedural, the set still accommodates. It’s a game about stories people tell together, so the social contract matters more than a perfectly optimized build.

## Comparisons: City of Mist versus the Field

If you’ve dabbled in other narrative RPGs like Masks, FATE, or Blades in the Dark, City of Mist sits in a friendly middle ground. It shares Masks’ character-driven focus, the flexible world-building of a Fate-style system, and the procedural clarity that Blades in the Dark fans often crave—without feeling like you’re stuck in a single genre. The Starter Set leans into this blend by giving you a defined mood while keeping the rules approachable. If you want to sample something that’s both mythic and noir with minimal fuss, you’ll likely reach for City of Mist ahead of a heavier epic or a more strict procedural.
