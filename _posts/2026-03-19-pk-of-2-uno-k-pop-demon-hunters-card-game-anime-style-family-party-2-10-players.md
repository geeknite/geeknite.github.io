## How to Play: The Core Mechanics, with a K-Pop Twist
If you know UNO, you know most of the basics: matching color or number, last-card shouts, and the occasional draw-two disaster. The Demon Hunters edition isn’t rewriting what UNO does; it’s remixing it with two major bake-it-in features:

1) Demon Hunter Cards: These are special action cards that trigger unique abilities tied to the theme. They might skip a player, force a draw, or unleash a character power that can reverse the turn order with a dramatic pose. These cards add strategic depth without turning the game into a math test on how to optimize hand filtering. The effect cadence makes the game feel like you’re performing on a stage rather than calmly lining up for a snack.

2) K-Pop Energy Meter: Somewhere between a mood ring and a hype train, an energy meter tracks the table’s vibe. High-energy moments (big plays, perfectly timed demon card plays) build momentum; too much moaning about wilds can burn the meter and trigger a safety glaze that makes everyone giggle. This metric keeps the pace snappy and the table engaged, and it’s surprisingly effective at curbing the classic UNO slumps where people stare at their hand for five minutes while the cat naps on the sofa.

Playing with two decks feels like you’re piloting a small raid team. The two packs can be functionally combined in a handful of house rules or played separately for shorter sessions. The balance shifts depending on how aggressively you treat the demon hunter cards. If you hoard them, you’ll crash the energy meter; if you deploy them like an overconfident pop idol mid-song, you’ll probably end up cheering for your own misplays.

The base rules remain approachable: match color or number, draw when you’re out of moves, and call UNO when you’re down to one card. The demon hunter additions stack on top, so you’ll be juggling more options but never feel like you should have taken that left turn at Albuquerque. The learning curve is gentle, especially for players already fluent in UNO slang and “Uno, dos, tres” choruses after a few rounds.
