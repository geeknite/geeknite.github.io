## How the game plays: quick refresher with a retro twist

### Core rules
UNO, at its core, is built on quick rounds and fast decisions. If you’ve played UNO before, you’ll pick this up quickly: players take turns matching the top card in color or number, or playing a Wild card to switch colors. When you’re down to one card, you shout “UNO!” or risk the penalty for failure to declare—depending on how salty your friends are feeling that night.

The Iconic 2010s Retro Version adheres to these basics but throws in a few tweaks that capture the era’s vibe:

- A revised draw rule: certain retro cards may force the next player to draw an extra card, but you’ll only know this after you draw a card and realize you’ve just been “punked” by a deck that loves nostalgia more than your savings account.
- Wild draw rules: Wild cards in this edition are color-rich and feature retro imagery. They function as wilds but also provide a small flourish of style, letting you declare a color with extra flair.
- Skip and Reverse: these classic action cards remain, but their artwork and icons are drawn in that playful 2010s style that makes you go “Aww, retro bloodsport.”

### The special edition twists
The Retro Version doesn’t reinvent UNO; it reinterprets it. The 2010s vibe adds a storytelling layer to your typical card toss: you’ll narrate your “strategy” like you’re casting a spell in a retro RPG, largely because you want to entertain (or annoy) your group. In practical terms, the twists may affect strategy more than result: you’ll be balancing between card-counting logic and the confidence you have to push the “Wild” button to switch colors exactly when your opponent looks too confident.
