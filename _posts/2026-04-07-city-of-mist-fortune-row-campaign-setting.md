## Fortune Row at a Glance

Fortune Row is the city’s most glamorous, most dangerous horizontal corridor—think Times Square-meets-Twilight Zone, with a dash of casino floor and a whisper of street-urchin poetry. It’s a district that feels mid-loliday, as if every storefront is playing host to a secret carnival where the prize is always a little more than you can take home. The mythology here is relentlessly civic; the masks worn by its denizens reflect a community story about luck, leverage, and the price of both.

The tone in Fortune Row is deliberately synthetic and beautiful—neon reflections across rain-slick pavement, champagne bubbles in a barrel-chested bartender’s glass, and the soft clack of high-heeled shoes on marble while rumors slide along like coins in a slot. Players who crave a heist of fate, a caper with heavy mood, and a city that feels almost alive will find Fortune Row irresistible. If a usual urban fantasy version of New York or Tokyo was too predictable, Fortune Row bows its head and says, in a velvet whisper, “Try me again, darling.”

In terms of mechanics, Fortune Row leans into the core City of Mist feel—the mythic overlay on a modern city—while introducing a tighter set of micro-geographies and recurring, high-stakes intrigues that can anchor a campaign from a one-shot to a long arc. The district provides narrative pressure points: a casino-tower with a tragedy at its core, a warehouse-turned-research-lab where fortunes are harvested, and a social club where deals are made not with dollars but with reputations and promises. The GM can use these levers to escalate tension and draw players toward a central mystery—the question of who profits from the city’s dreams and who pays the price to maintain that profit.

For readers familiar with City of Mist’s signature framework, Fortune Row emphasizes three things: identity, risk, and reciprocity. Characters will have a personal stake in the district’s fortunes, whether as a client, a caretaker, or a would-be controller. The risk isn’t just physical; it’s reputational. In Fortune Row, the line between “friend” and “fate” tends to blur, and a single social misstep can cascade into a mythic-scale revelation.

If you’re planning a Fortune Row campaign, you’ll want to gather a few essential notes before your players arrive: what are the district’s dominant myths? which factions hold sway? what are the recurring motifs (cards, coins, mirrors, and clocks)? And perhaps most important, what is your crew masking to protect, and what myth do they fear the most?

## What Fortune Row Brings to City of Mist

Fortune Row expands the City of Mist toolkit with a robust regional lens that lets players explore the city’s underbelly while never quite leaving the glow of glamour. It adds a compact, highly playable palette:

- A concentrated setting: a district with a handful of iconic locales that can be mapped on a single city board or a stylized map. The physical space is manageable, which makes it ideal for GM prep and for players who love chasing a coherent arc rather than chasing flashpoint locations across a sprawling map.
- A web of factions: you’ll meet the Wagering Guild, the Velvet Salon, the Thread of Fate, the Ledger Collective, and other groups who each have something to win or lose from Fortune Row’s fortunes. Conflicts can be personal, financial, or mythic—there’s room for intrigue without losing track of the city-wide orbit.
- Thematic anchors: cues about luck, risk, and performance—the everyday magic that makes a rumor feel like a prophecy and a story feel like a score in progress. Fortune Row makes these themes tactile, giving GMs ready hooks to push players toward meaningful choices.

Gameplay-wise, Fortune Row leans into a street-level epic: characters can leverage their Masks not just to blend into crowds but to unlock micro-magas of power, influence, and fear. The district creates recurring cycles—seasonal festivals, quarterly shipments of rare Glamour, and a clockwork mechanism that governs the city’s mood. Fortune Row invites players to interrogate luck: what does luck require? what does it forgive? and what does it punish?

## The Neighborhood: Fortune Row’s Core Locations

Fortune Row isn’t a nameless labyrinth; it’s a curated walk through a handful of signature sites, each with its own mood, clientele, and secret. We’ll cover the big five and a couple of honorable mentions that you’ll want in your GM notebook.

### The Velvet Box
A nightclub carved from velvet and ribbed chrome, The Velvet Box is where late-night deals go down and early-morning regrets wake up. The Box isn’t just a place to drink; it’s a stage—each night features a curated show where performers reflect the current mood of the city. The box’s owner believes in two things: profit and spectacle. If your players ever need a public scene to catalyze a conflict, this is the place.

### Luck's Dock
A waterfront promenade where fortune-tellers, hustlers, and shipping crews mingle. The Dock is home to a ring-side bar that serves cocktails named after myths (The Kraken Sour, The Siren’s Note, The Fortuna Flip). It’s also a listening post for information—the kind of whispers that become plot threads if you’re willing to cash in a favor or two. The docks are sticky with intrigue, and the water carries the city’s secrets like cheap whiskey on a windy night.

### The Neon Altar
A storefront-turned-temple to public performance and ritualized luck. Neon signage flickers with inconsistent devotion; patrons participate in micro-rituals that feel almost ceremonial: toss a coin, hum a line from a prophecy, and wait for a sign. Some patrons leave with a story worth bragging about; others walk away with a debt incurred by a promise that wasn’t fully uttered.

### The Oracular Pier
A coastline promenade punctuated by a series of weather-beaten kiosks where fortune-tellers wax prophetic about love, power, and the city’s next disaster. The Oracular Pier is where most players discover their first major hook: a vision that ties their personal myth to Fortune Row’s fate. It’s a perfect location for a one-shot opener or a mid-campaign revelation that reframes a player’s motivations.

### The Gate of Coins
A towering casino-hotel hybrid that acts as the district’s primary power hub. The Gate of Coins isn’t merely a location; it’s a narrative engine. The casino’s security system—an enchanted ledger that records every decision the house makes on a guest’s fate—can be exploited or guarded against. The Gate is where many long-term plots coalesce, and it’s also a place where players can optionally cash out on their luck.

Each of these sites has a distinct tone, set of NPCs, and set of hooks. The GM can structure arcs that loop through two or three locations, letting players develop relationships with custodians, enemies, and patrons who become part of the city’s mythic tapestry.

## Factions, NPCs, and the Web of Power

Fortune Row’s factions aren’t pure villains and heroes; they’re complex organizations with motives that intersect in clever ways. Here are a few core players you’ll meet:

- The Wagering Guild: An alliance of risk-takers and gamblers who view life as a bet and death as a long shot. They’re always playing for the next big payoff, but their bookkeepers fear the day the city’s luck runs dry.
- The Velvet Salon: An aristocracy of performers and social climbers who curate appearances as if they were sacred rituals. Their power is cultural; their leverage is influence. They’re charming until you cross them, after which they’ll write your fate in a line of glittering ink.
- The Thread of Fate: A shadowy network of informants whose currency is information and whose rules are unwritten but understood. They know everyone’s secrets and use that knowledge to nudge events toward outcomes that look like coincidence but feel like inevitability.
- The Ledger Collective: Masters of numbers and probabilities. They’re the city’s accountants of doom, turning luck into a predictable equation—or trying when possible to convert a chaotic event into a solvable problem.
- The Archivists: A guild of librarians and memory-keepers who guard the city’s past. They aren’t glamorous, but they’re patient and dangerous; their knowledge can shape present-day events if used with care.

NPCs from Fortune Row are designed to be memorable force multipliers for both PCs and the GM. You’ll find characters with strong masks (public identities that people in the city recognize instantly) and others who exist in the gray between masks—the kind of people who know enough to tell you what you want to hear but not enough to tell you the truth.

GM tips: mix masks with personal stakes. When a PC’s secret intersects a faction’s objective, you get the best dramatic payoffs. If your players love social intrigue, lean into the threads that connect the factions. If they love high-stakes action, schedule a sequence where the whole district must perform a dangerous ritual to avert a city-wide calamity—and your players are the only ones who can step into the roles required by myth.

## Campaign Hooks and Story Seeds

Fortune Row shines when you give players the sense that the city is both a playground and a conspiracy. Here are five hooks tailored to the district:

1) The Night it Rained Fortune: A storm of coins floods Fortune Row, and every coin found carries a whisper that predicts the holder’s future misfortune. The PCs must identify the source of the rain, stop a ritual, and decide who deserves to know the truth about their destinies.
2) A Debt Owed to Time: A former patron of The Velvet Box resurfaces, claiming the city’s time has been overdrawn. The PCs must restore balance by returning a stolen hour to the city’s clock tower, or watch fortune itself unravel.
3) The Gate’s Secret Key: The Gate of Coins experiences a security breach: the enchanted ledger has a backdoor that lets someone rewrite outcomes. The PCs must discover who tampered with it and what bargain they struck with fate.
4) The Oracular Heist: A fortune-teller predicts a “last show” at The Velvet Box. If a player helps stage the show, the district’s mythic balance shifts—and a new power emerges from the audience’s own masks.
5) The Quiet Rebellion: A faction inside the Ledger Collective seeks to democratize luck, distributing “truthful probabilities” that disrupt the city’s gilded order. The PCs can join or resist, with consequences that ripple through Fortune Row and beyond.

You can adapt these seeds to a short arc or weave them into a longer campaign with evolving stakes. Fortune Row wants ongoing relationships, not one-and-done set-pieces. The best arcs tend to hinge on a single, emotionally charged choice: should a PC sacrifice their own mask to save someone else’s, or let fate take its course and watch the city burn with the glow of a million speculations?

## Mechanics and GM Guidance: Getting Fortune Row Right

Fortune Row is designed to be a narrative engine that sits on top of City of Mist’s core mechanics. The Masks and Mythos are alive here, but the district also nudges players toward social stratagems—bluffing, bargaining, and bluff-bargaining under the neon glare. Here are some concrete tips to maximize the Fortune Row experience:

- Embrace the mood with stagecraft: treat key scenes like performances. Lighting, sound cues, and a curator’s commentary from a non-player character can make the moment feel like a living theater piece rather than a dungeon crawl.
- Tie glamour to choices: Glamour, the city’s currency of awe and power, should feel earned through risk and meaningful decision-making. Don’t hand Glamour out with a spreadsheet; let it be the result of a character choosing a path with consequences that ripple through the district.
- Use recurring motifs: coins, mirrors, clocks, and cards are not just props; they’re memory devices. Use them to trigger flashbacks or reveal a character’s hidden truth. If a player’s mask is that they “always bet on the wrong horse,” you can reflect that in how often they’re offered a risky proposition.
- Leverage factions’ incentives: each faction should want something specific from the PCs, and the PCs should want something from them. The best meetings occur at a location where a prop matters—The Gate of Coins is perfect because a ledger-in-the-wall confession can be the centerpiece of a scene.
- Balance visibility and mystery: Fortune Row thrives on rumors, but you don’t want it to degenerate into a maze of red herrings. Structure your sessions to deliver a few tangible clues and a larger-than-life revelation that makes the players feel the city’s myths were listening all along.

## Quick Start Adventure: The Coined Fate Opener

Premise: Fortune Row hosts a festival night featuring a “Coined Fate” game where a parade of coins is cast into the air by the crowd, each coin landing with a silent whisper about a possible future. The PCs must navigate a web of competing interests to recover a coin that grants a dangerous vision—the kind of vision that could tilt Fortune Row’s balance and reveal the city’s next big event.

Setup: The PCs are drawn to Fortune Row by a rumor of a coin that reveals a person’s true mask when held under a certain light. They discover that a rival faction intends to weaponize the coin to force a major political move. The set-piece ends with a choice: use the microphone in The Velvet Box to reveal a truth that hurts someone they care about, or preserve the lie to protect a friend.

Outcome: The opener ends with a taste of what Fortune Row can do when a mask slips and a rumor becomes prophecy. It gives players a sense of scale, a reason to care about the district, and a couple of lasting consequences that you can reference in later sessions.

If you want a more narrative-driven launch, try the “Night It Rains Fortune” hook and then loop it into a longer arc using the Oracular Pier as a recurring meeting point. The setup is simple, but it yields rich storytelling. For players who crave more tactical play, swap in a heist scenario at The Gate of Coins where the ledger’s backdoor is the objective, and time is a factor—the more you work against the clock, the more the city’s luck shifts in your favor or against you.

## Visual and Tone Guide for GMs

Fortune Row’s aesthetic is a vivid blend of noir, neon, and carnival. To capture this in play:

- Use lighting to cue mood. Dim red for danger, electric blue for revelation, amber for nostalgia. Let the room feel like a stage and the city feel like a living costume shop.
- Encourage players to lean into their Masks. Masks aren’t costumes; they’re coping mechanisms and liabilities. The more a PC leans into their Mask in a scene, the more you should reward with narrative payoff—unless the Mask bites back.
- Treat Glamour as a resource you can earn and spend, not hoard. A hot streak at Fortune Row should feel kinetic and gold-laden, not a passive score. Glamour could unlock a temporary boon or reveal a hidden truth at a cost.
- Fold in props that matter. A coin with a date that’s wrong on the future’s calendar, a mirror that shows a possible past, a clock that ticks in reverse for a few moments—these props become story engines if used consistently.
