## What is Dixit, Exactly?
Dixit is not a to-the-point tactical game. It’s a storytelling party game. Players take turns being the storyteller, selecting one of the beautifully bizarre Dixit cards and muttering a clue—one that is poetic, cryptic, or hilariously off-base. The catch? You don’t tell your audience exactly what the card is. You let the story be a bridge between your clue and the image on the card. Then everyone else picks a card they think matches your clue, and the group votes on which card was the storyteller’s. If your turn as a storyteller is the only one to match the clue exactly, you score a penalty for being too obvious. If enough players pick your card but not all, you score points. It’s a delicate balance of misdirection, shared imagination, and the occasional “how on earth did you see that in that card?” insult that’s all in good fun.

Dixit mixes a core of the familiar party game vibe—play quickly, banter loudly, points accumulate—with a surprising depth of interpretation. The cards are not literal. They are surreal, impressionistic scenes, dreamy landscapes, and little moments that seem to exist just outside your memory of a dream you had in a foreign language. The new copy that came sealed in a box from Libellud is a reminder that art can be both a centerpiece and a catalyst for social chemistry. If you’re someone who enjoyed Pictionary but found it stiflingly literal, Dixit invites you to inhabit a realm where your clue could be interpreted in a dozen different ways by a dozen different brains—and that’s the whole point.

## How to Play (A Quick, Friendly Primer)
- Each round, one player becomes the storyteller. They look at one card in their hand and give a clue that is at once specific enough to guide their teammates but broad enough to misdirect opponents.
- Everyone (including the storyteller) selects a card from their own hand that best matches the clue and places it facedown.
- The facedown cards are revealed, and players vote on which card they think is the storyteller’s. You can’t vote for your own card.
- Points are awarded: the storyteller earns points if at least some but not all players select their card, while players who guessed the storyteller’s card also earn points. If nobody or everyone guesses the storyteller’s card, the storyteller earns fewer points, and the round continues.

The rules encourage storytelling as a social art form. It’s less about “winning” and more about creating shared moments of “wait, what was that card even?” The sealed box is a reminder that you’re beginning a ritual where you and your friends become co-authors of a living story. The rules are light enough to pick up during a caffeine-fueled breakfast, but the implications (interpretation, fantasy, and misdirection) float around the table like moths to a dream lamp—beautiful, a little dangerous, and utterly charming when your grandma lols at your clue about “the teacup that tasted like a memory.”

## Storytelling Mechanics: Why It Feels Like Magic Without the Tricks
Dixit is fundamentally about storytelling as a social act. The storyteller’s clue is a spark that must ignite the imagination in a way that aligns, imperfectly, with other players’ interpretations. The art direction—dreamy, abstract, and slightly off-kilter—gives you a vocabulary you didn’t know you possessed. You’ll describe a card like you’re composing a line of a poem in a language your audience barely understands, and that is the point. The laughter often comes from the misreadings—the clue that seemed straightforward to the storyteller but becomes an inside joke to the table because someone noticed a tiny detail in the card that’s only obvious if you’ve spent a dozen other nights with this deck.

Consider the family-friendly angle: kids learn how to craft clues that are intriguing but not too explicit. They experience the thrill of “got it!” when someone picks their card, and the mild fuel of friendly competition when their own card is the one chosen by most. It’s a game about language, about metaphor, and about letting your mind wander into shapes and colors and moods. Adults benefit too: it’s a gentle reminder that not every story needs a plot twist with a dramatic reveal; sometimes the joy is in the private joke you share with the person across the table.

In a sealed copy, you also get the opportunity to discuss authorship of stories. Whose imagination led to which interpretation? Who has the most evocative vocabulary when describing the same card? It’s not about who is best at clueing—it’s about how well you can listen and how gracefully you can accept being surprised by someone else’s interpretation. That dynamic (listening, laughing, and then swapping roles) is what keeps Dixit fresh, even after dozens of plays.
