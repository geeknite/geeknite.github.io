## Introduction
If your game night banners read like a gothic novel and your friends' idea of light reading is a cryptic scavenger hunt through a haunted house, then you and Gloom are destined to be neighbors. Gloom, by Atlas Games, is a 2–4 player card game that trades sunny afternoons for a family tree that looks like a Rorschach test after a kindergartner found a black marker. The aim is simple and deliciously mean: you want your family to die first, while making everyone else’s family suffer longer. It’s not just a game; it’s a therapy session written in translucent cards and wry smiles. Geeknite is not immune to its charms, and after multiple playthroughs, we’re here to tell you why this little coffin of a box continues to haunt our shelves in the most delightful way.

First off, the art direction. Gloom wears its mood on its sleeve and then shouts it through a hollowed-out melodrama. The cards themselves are translucent plastic, so you can layer misfortune onto one character while affixing a shred of happiness to another. It’s a clever gimmick that isn’t gimmicky at all once you see the light shine through it—quite literally, because the clarity of the imagery makes every tragedy feel like a stage prop chosen with exquisite cruelty.

## What is Gloom Anyway?
Gloom is a card game about misery, but its misery is cooperative in the sense that you’re all writing your own tragedy. Each player starts with a tiny family of one or two characters, each with its own life story (that you don’t reveal to others yet, because secrets make the best kind of drama). The twist is that you can see your opponents’ cards, but not their intentions, and you can apply a variety of “story cards” and “gloom” cards to bless or curse the characters in play.

The core loop is simple: draw cards, play cards, and aim your misfortunes onto opponents’ families while shielding your own. When a character dies, their card flips to reveal its negative score; happier endings reduce your misery, gloom cards pile up negative points, and the player with the bleakest family (i.e., the lowest total score) wins. It’s a grim carnival ride with the safety rails well within reach of your rational brain, but your inner ghoul will cheer for the chaos anyway.

## Components and Quality
The physical goods are a treat. Gloom uses transparent plastic character cards layered over a standard card deck of a few dozen cards per player. The translucency is not just a gimmick; it makes the feed of doom come alive as you stack stories onto characters and watch the gloom pile up in elegant, ghostly layers. The deck quality is solid, the punch lines land, and the cards shuffle with a satisfying rustle that tells you you’re in for a properly theatrical experience.

There are two helpful pieces of advice we’d pass along for new players:

- Keep your character layers organized. The translucent cards are beautiful, but they can become a tangled web if you’re not mindful of what’s overlapping what. A small insert or tray to separate the layers between rounds goes a long way.
- Don’t be afraid to reveal the “gloom” cards late in the game. Part of the fun is seeing the audience—the other players—realize how badly you’ve schemed for this moment. It’s a subtle form of social theatre that Gloom handles with surprising grace.

The aesthetics align very well with the tone. Gloom’s graphic style is playful yet macabre, which makes it approachable for casual gamers while still offering enough bite for more veteran players who enjoy clever sabotage.

## Setup: Quickstart Guide for Your First Run
The setup is forgiving, which is essential in a game about misfortune and melodrama. Here’s the gist:

- Each player picks a color or a family archetype and receives one or two starting character cards with neutral life events.
- Shuffle the gloom deck (the bad stuff) and the story deck (the situational events). Place both within reach.
- Draw a starting hand. You’ll typically hold a mix of character cards and a handful of story/gloom cards to get the narrative rolling.
- Begin with a simple, not-too-heavy goal: place a few cards to set the tone, then start the slow burn of accruing gloom on others’ characters while you try not to doom your own family too early.

That’s it. It’s light on overhead, which is a blessing when your group’s banter is half improvised, half sarcastic prophecy.

## How to Play: The Core Loop
Gloom is built on a few straightforward moves that create an endless flow of decisions and moral calculus. The core loop tends to cycle through these steps:

1) Draw two cards from either the character deck or the story/gloom deck. The card draw injects new options, new disasters, and possibly a glimmer of light.
2) Play one card. You can either add a new character to your family, attach a story to a character, or drop a gloom card on someone else’s character to deepen their misery. The rules encourage you to both expand your own family and attack the others, which is where the game earns its reputation for delicious misanthropy.
3) Discard one card. The discard pile is your ally in long-form mischief; you’ll want to keep a few cards in reserve for those late-game gambits when the room turns into a theater audience.
4) End your turn by drawing back to a standard hand size if you’ve altered the deck dynamics through special abilities or events.

What makes this loop delightful is the tactical layering. Because each character can hold multiple effects, the same card could have radically different outcomes depending on where you place it and what’s already attached to the target. It’s a game of partial information and total performance—do you tilt toward the risk of a strong, devastating move now, or do you hedge your bets with a series of lighter, slower burns?

A typical round unfolds in a way that keeps everyone involved. There’s almost always a moment where someone says, “Oh, you did not just do that,” followed by a chorus of grumbles and roars of laughter. You’ll often see a chain reaction where a gloom card placed on one player spirals into a cascade of misfortune as others respond with their own counterplay. The drama is not accidental; it’s designed to feel rewarding in the moment and later when you recount the night’s inevitable misfortunes.

## Strategy and Tactics: Reading the Room and Your Deck
If you’re serious about winning at Gloom, you’ll quickly realize there are two axes of strategy: controlling your own happiness versus maximizing others’ misery. The best players learn to balance the two and time their worst cards to land at the right moment.

Here are a few tactical tips that consistently help in a first couple of games:

- Target a rival who has already begun stacking good events on themselves. It’s more satisfying to see a well-laid plan go up in smoke than to sink a weak target that’s already trending downward.
- Use story cards to influence the tone of a character’s fate without tipping your hand too early. Your audience (the other players) will read your moves as a signal of your long-game intent.
- Keep a defensive plan. A single well-placed gloom card on your own character is still a risk, but if you’ve stacked a few protective story cards or beneficial events, you can endure longer and crank up the tension in later rounds.
- Endgame threats are real. If you can force another player to chase a last-minute victory by targeting their crucial characters, you’ll reap the benefit of their desperation as the game closes.

The balance is what makes Gloom re-playable. It’s not enough to simply dump gloom left and right; you need to read the table, anticipate responses, and pivot when your plans get outmaneuvered. The deck’s design rewards cunning, but it always keeps room for chaos—a hallmark of a good party game and a necessary trait for a title that celebrates misfortune as entertainment.

## Theme, Mood, and Accessibility
Theme wise, Gloom excels in pairing a somber premise with a breezy, almost cartoonish execution. The taboo humor lands in the realm of “grim but not vicious,” which makes it friendlier for groups with varying thresholds for dark humor. The accessibility is high: turn structure is straightforward, and with a few rounds under your belt you’ll be executing card combos with the confidence of a seasoned stage manager.

That said, the translucent cards (while gorgeous) do require some care. If you’re a table with rambunctious players, a mat or a dedicated card tray helps keep the layers from sliding into a tangled knot. The humor remains strong even after multiple plays, which is a testament to the design’s durability and its clever wordplay on the cards themselves.

## Variants, Expansions, and Longevity
Gloom is a game with a few expansions that expand both the family legacies and the mood cards. The base game is robust and satisfying; expansions typically add new character cards and a broader range of gloom and story options. That means the same table can stay engaged for many sessions without feeling stale.

What you gain with expansions is more malleable play patterns. More cards mean more possible combinations, which translates to more opportunities for dramatic reveals and surprising turns of fate. ## Social Experience and Accessibility
One of Gloom’s strongest suits is social interaction. It invites storytelling as a core mechanic; you’re not just playing cards—you’re producing a tiny, melodramatic play each round. The tension builds naturally as players react to unexpected reveals and cunning plays. It’s ideal for a group that enjoys witty banter, side conversations, and the occasional theatrical flourish.

As far as accessibility goes, the rules are not overly burdensome. They’re straightforward enough for casual players, and they scale well with a mixed-ability group. The transparent cards provide a visual cue that’s easy to grasp; even non-gamers can jump in quickly once they see the first couple of rounds. The humor and storytelling help bridge the gap for players who might otherwise feel overwhelmed by more mechanically dense games.

If you’re planning a game night with folks who like to riff on the action, you’ll quickly discover that Gloom acts as a catalyst for conversation. It can be a gentle gateway to heavier strategy games or a comforting, silly detour when your regular picks feel a bit too intense.

## Art, Components, and Design Notes
The design is playful and intentionally theatrical. The character cards—though simple in silhouette—carry personality through the life events and portrait choices. The overlay mechanism (the transparent cards) is not merely a gimmick; it’s the signature feature that defines the entire experience. You’ll find that the layering allows for visually striking combos that feel clever rather than gimmicky.

From a design perspective, the rulebook does a fine job of balancing humor with clarity. It’s light on exposition and heavy on practical examples. That matters, because a lot of the joy in Gloom comes from translating a silly sentence into a meaningful in-game consequence. The writing itself earns points for flavor; the game’s mood is never overshadowed by heavy mechanics or arcane jargon.

## How It Compares to Similar Games
If you’re scanning the shelf for something that scratches a similar itch, you’re likely to compare Gloom with a handful of other social, storytelling, or light strategy games. Here are a few quick contrasts:

- Versus a traditional deduction game: Gloom relies less on hidden information and more on storytelling and social manipulation. You’re encouraging others to read your intent through the irony of the cards you play, not by bluffing with a secret hand.
- Versus a party storytelling game: The difference is the degree of structure. Gloom provides a narrative spine that you don’t always get in pure improv games, which makes it easier for players who enjoy a storyline but want a game with defined end conditions.
- Versus cooperative card games: Gloom is not cooperative; it thrives on competition, but the interplay is still built on a shared universe with a common tone. If you want a heavy cooperative experience, you may gravitate toward other titles; if you want a social, cheeky, doom-spiced session, Gloom is your jam.

## Pros and Cons (TL;DR)
- Pros:
  - Unique translucent card mechanic creates a striking aesthetic and clever play interactions.
  - Thematic humor lands well for groups that enjoy dark comedy without crossing lines.
  - Easy to learn, quick to teach, and scalable for casual and semi-serious players.

## Final Verdict
Gloom is a design triumph that packs a lot of personality into a compact, accessible package. It invites you to tell a micro-drama, to interact with your table in interesting ways, and to do so without heavy rules baggage. It’s a game that rewards cunning, storytelling flair, and a willingness to embrace a little darkness in service of laughter and shared misfortune. If your weekly game nights crave something stylish, witty, and a touch wicked, Gloom is a top pick.

The experience scales well from 2 players to a full 4-player roster, and the expansions promise continued expansion of narrative possibilities. It’s not the longest or most serious game in your library, but it may become the most replayable for the right crowd. In our book, that’s a win you can’t sneeze at, especially when the air is thick with imaginary gloom and the room erupts in approving, wry laughter.
