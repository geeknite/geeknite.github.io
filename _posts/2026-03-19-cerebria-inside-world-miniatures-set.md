## Build, assembly, and basing
Assembly is straightforward, but there are some decisions to make that can affect the final look and space on your board.

### Prep and tools
A basic hobby kit suffices: nippers, a light file or hobby knife to clean flash, primer, and your chosen paints. If you want to go the extra mile, magnetized bases provide swap-out flexibility so you can swap different emotions between turns without sacrificing display charm. If you’re new to mini painting, this is a forgiving set to practice on—the bigger faces stay readable even with modest brushwork.

### Magnet bases vs fixed bases
The temptation to magnetize is strong here. The larger centerpiece pieces look heroic perched on their bases and benefit from a magnet approach to enable quick transitions between poses. Smaller figures can also benefit from a magnetized setup, especially if you rotate them for different players or scenarios. If you’re not into magnets, the standard fixed bases still look solid and stable; you’ll just miss the modular showmanship a little bit.

### Assembly tips
- Start with good lighting and a clean workspace; the details on some of the inner-world figures reward careful attention.
- Use thin layers; thick coats tend to obscure fine sculpts. Build color gradually, preserving depth in recessed areas.
- When attaching, consider pinning larger pieces for extra stability if your table sees frequent bumping or enthusiastic table-slap moments.
- Match the tone of your color palette to the emotions you want to emphasize. A brighter palette can heighten Joy, while cooler tones can accentuate Fear or Sorrow.

## Painting and color theory
If you enjoy painting, this set is a dream with a few caveats. The lines are bold enough to give you a clean canvas, but the high-contrast faces and robes invite careful shading to reveal their character without turning them into mud pies.

### Color palettes and narrative cues
The packaging and included paint guide lean into a storytelling approach. For quick-start schemes, one option is to assign a color triad to each emotion: a base color, a mid-tone for folds, and a highlight. For more dramatic scenes, you can push toward luminous edges—think a subtle glow along the outer rims to imply inner radiance. The joy characters benefit from warm, bright accents; fear can get subtle teals and cool blues; sorrow and melancholy lean into purples and deep blues. This is not just painting minis; it’s painting a mood.

### Techniques worth trying
- Layering for depth: thin layers of color, building up to peak contrast on raised areas.
- Wash lightly into recesses to heighten the negative space that defines the emotions' silhouettes.
- Dry brushing on textured fabrics and cloaks to pick up edge highlights without over-emphasizing the sculpt’s lines.
- Glowing edges or aura effects around certain specks of light can give a feel of inner power without overpowering the miniature's silhouette.

## Gameplay implications and compatibility
A key question for any expansion is: does it actually change how the game plays? In this case, the Miniatures Set preserves the base game’s mechanics while upgrading visual clarity and immersion. The rules themselves remain unchanged, so this is not a power-boosting add-on. What it does offer is a tangible signal to players: the inner-world characters aren’t just abstract tokens; they are real enough to trigger the same emotional gravity the game tries to simulate. Some groups report faster tabletop readability—players can reference a pose to understand which emotion is anchored where more quickly than they could by looking at a card or marker. If you care about narrative clarity as much as mechanical depth, you’ll appreciate the minor but meaningful calibration this set provides.

### Table layout and space considerations
With roughly 20-something minis, you’re looking at a modest increase in footprint on a crowded table. - Painting the existing tokens: If you’re price-sensitive or you enjoy the challenge, painting the base tokens to a higher standard can recapture some of the drama without buying new figures. It’s a different kind of project, but it scratches a similar itch.

## Painting tips from the bench
- Start with quick-blocks to lock in the emotion identities (Joy in warm tones, Fear in cooler hues, Sorrow in deep purples).
- Build up color gradually; avoid heavy washing on sharp edges to preserve sculpt readability.
- Use a mist of glaze to unify colors and add a soft glow for inner-light effects on the highlights.
- Practice on a few test minis before committing to the whole set; you’ll thank yourself when you realize you like the finished look more than the store-bought baseline.

## Maintenance and storage
High-detail minis deserve care. After the sessions, consider storing them in a padded tray with individual compartments to prevent rubbing of the delicate joins and to keep the more dramatic poses from turning into unintentional abstract art pieces. Keeping them dust-free and upright also helps preserve the paint job for longer, which is essential if you plan to show off your table at conventions or friends’ houses.
