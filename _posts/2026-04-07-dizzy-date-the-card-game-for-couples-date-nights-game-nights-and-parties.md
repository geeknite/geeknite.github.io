## How to play: a quick-start guide
If you’ve played party games before, Dizzy Date follows a familiar rhythm with a dash of chaos. Here’s a quick-start guide to get you from “what is this” to “we just decided to adopt a new family tradition.”

1) Gather players: ideally 2–6, but a few extra guests can join as audience members for the dares. The more, the merrier, the louder, and the more likely you are to witness a spontaneous interpretive dance routine.
2) Set the base rules: decide on a few ground rules for the night (no cards that threaten to end the relationship as you know it, a no-shame zone for embarrassing stories, etc.).
3) Shuffle and deal: each player gets a hand of prompts. The game selects a starting player, who draws the first card from the deck and reveals it.
4) Resolve: follow the card’s instruction. If it’s a prompt, answer or discuss; if it’s a dare, complete it; if it’s a mini-challenge, perform a quick task. The tone should stay friendly and funny—if it starts to feel mean, pause and recalibrate.
5) Pass or score: some cards offer you the chance to award points or pass on a prompt. If you want a cheesier vibe, keep score. If you want to focus on connection, skip the numbers and treat it as a memory-generating activity.
6) Endgame: after a pre-agreed number of rounds or a time cap, tally points (if you’re doing the scoring route) and celebrate with a finale card or a debrief about what you learned about each other during the game.

The pace is fast, the energy is high, and the room’s temperature seems to rise whenever a particularly cheesy prompt appears. It’s the perfect tempo for date nights: you get enough structure to guide conversation, but you’re still free to riff on the prompt with your own inside jokes.

## Card types and how they influence your night
Dizzy Date categorizes cards into three broad classes: prompts, dares, and mini-challenges. Here’s how each category tends to shape the evening:

- Prompts: These are your conversation catalysts. They range from “Tell a story about your first impression of your partner” to “Pitch a bizarre date idea you’d actually hate to try.” They’re designed to reveal quirks, preferences, and shared memories without getting too heavy too fast.
- Dares: The dare cards inject physical comedy or silly tasks. Think “Do the water bottle flip while giving a 60-second monologue about your most embarrassing moment” or “Trade outfits with your partner for one round.” The fun lies in the execution; the sillier, the better.
- Mini-challenges: These are the quick execution tasks—one-minute sketches, speed storytelling, or rapid-fire questions. They keep momentum high and prevent the game from turning into a mealtime discussion about the best ice cream flavor.

The balance of prompts, dares, and mini-challenges makes a playable arc that feels like a night out with a friend who got into improv. It’s silly, occasionally ridiculous, and simultaneously endearing because it nudges you to share small pieces of yourself in a non-threatening, humorous way.
