## Ergonomics and Comfort
Let’s talk about the elephant in the room: length and ergonomics. A 5.4-meter desk sounds like a runway for productivity, but it also introduces challenges you don’t encounter with a traditional desk. The key to making this work is how you allocate the zones and how you position the screens relative to each user. Ergonomics in such a space isn’t just about chair height or keyboard placement; it’s about sightlines, reach zones, and the emotional comfort of knowing your coworkers aren’t stealing your airspace with their elbows.

- Sightlines: The central screen(s) should be at eye level for the primary line of sight. If the display sits too high or too low, users will constantly tilt their necks, leading to a chorus of neck aches that could qualify as a new scale for pain. The Eternal design often includes adjustable mounts or a modular rail that allows you to fine-tune height and tilt. In practice, you want a setup where remote participants can see the center of the display without needing a neck brace.
- Reach Zones: Keyboard trays, monitor arms, and document stands should form comfortable reach zones for each user. The modular nature helps here: you can configure the zones so that everyone has a comfortable arm extension without bumping elbows. The goal is to create calm micro-environments within a grand macro-space. Translation: no one should feel like they are doing battle with their adjacent coworker just to reach the stationary pencil cup.
- Posture and Movement: The desk benefits from a sit-stand or height-adjustable feature, if available. If the model ships with electric height adjustment across the frame, you can cycle between standing and sitting during long sessions without creating a humanitarian crisis over a tilted neck or the physics of gravity. If not, plan for a stationary desk with good chair support and a variation of movement during the day to prevent rest-for-mobility syndrome.

Another ergonomic perk is the cable management system. The reality of a long, shared desk is cables. They multiply like digital rabbits. A well-thought cable channel is your friend. It reduces the risk of someone tripping on a stray power cord while simultaneously keeping the workspace visually tidy—at least until the team’s clutter becomes the official aesthetic. The Eternal model typically features integrated cable channels, zip ties, and grommets at sensible intervals, making it easier to route power, data, and the occasional USB-C tether for a laptop or monitor dongle. If there is a caveat, it is that with great cable management comes great responsibility; someone on the team will need to be the designated cable whisperer, ensuring that the channels don’t fill with scrawled notes and old brads from a forgotten project.

## Screen Setup and Display Considerations
The central screen strategy is what gives this desk its identity. Some configurations opt for a single widescreen panel, others for a pair of mid-size panels. The value proposition hinges on how teams want to share content. A two-monitor layout in the middle can serve as a shared briefing board while the six end-user stations maintain personal display real estate for coding, word processing, and the occasional spreadsheet that requires more color-coded cells than a rainbow has. It’s a design decision that prioritizes collaboration without forcing every person to crowd around a single monitor like a group of monks on a pilgrimage.

When you place the screens, you want to consider lighting. In a white finish environment, reflections can become your worst enemy. The best practice is to position screens to minimize glare and ensure that the primary audience is the participants rather than the fluorescent tubes overhead. If the desk supports it, ambient lighting with dimmable controls is a subtle win; it makes technical presentations more readable and prevents the room from feeling like an operating theater during late-night sprints.

## Assembly and Installation Realities
Ok, let’s get practical. A 5.4-meter long bench is not a one-person job. It’s a small-scale project management exercise with the potential for heroic feats and a few curse words at the helper table. Expect a multi-stage assembly process that includes: frame assembly, rail installation for screens, top panel alignment, cable channel setup, and the actual as-assembled reveal—six workstations waiting, as if you’ve just dropped a futuristic vending machine into your office and asked it to vend ideas instead of snacks.

- Pre-assembly planning: Before any bolt touches metal, map the floor plan and mark where outlets live. The last thing you want is a cable cluster drama in the middle of a Tuesday meeting.
- Tools and time: A standard toolkit (Allen keys, screwdriver, maybe a drill) will be necessary. Your installation timeline should account for at least a few hours with a buddy who doesn’t mind a steady stream of phrases like we got this, champ and measure twice, cut once, even if neither phrase actually helps with the level.
- Modularity in action: The real magic—if you’re into it—happens when you configure the zones. Place the end-user stations with comfortable armrests and keyboard trays, then mount the screen rail where the central viewers can see it without crane-worries. Once assembled, you’ll likely discover new micro-adjustments that need fine-tuning, such as the height of the desk in the standing mode or the angle of the central screen rail. You’ll tweak, you’ll swear softly, and eventually you’ll realize the space is not a static object but a living, breathing collaboration platform.
