## What is Saboteur and how does it play?
Saboteur is a social deduction card game that uses role assignment and path-building to determine who reaches the gold first. The deck consists of path cards that connect to make a tunnel, obstacle cards that block or hinder progress, and role cards that assign a secret allegiance to each player: miners (who want to reach the gold) and saboteurs (who want the tunnels to fail). The twist is that even among miners, some are saboteurs, and among saboteurs, there might be a secret plan that helps the group—if you can figure out who is who before the tunnel collapses and everyone loses their momentum.

This 2018 Amigo edition remains faithful to the core mechanics while polishing the components for more consistent card quality and readability. You’ll still flip cards, lay down path segments, and carry the weight of possibly failing a dig because one clever saboteur sneaked in a rock obstacle when no one was looking. The humor of Saboteur is in the tension; you want the tunnel to succeed, but the misdirection from players is almost always the most entertaining part.

## Setup and basic flow
Setup is straightforward: shuffle the deck, deal each player a few role cards (one or two are hidden from others’ view), distribute miner tokens as needed, and lay down the first few path cards to start the tunnel. Each turn, you either play a path card to extend the mine, place an obstacle to hinder a rival, or draw a card to refresh your options. The goal is simple on paper—be the first to reach the gold card with a continuous path that also satisfies the secret goals of your role—but in practice, you’ll be negotiating, bluffing, and occasionally yelling with the kind of theatrical disappointment you reserve for drywall projects that go slightly askew.

One thing Amigo nails here is the clarity of turns. The rules are light, the confusion is entirely optional, and you can choose to lean into heavy strategy or lean into social shenanigans. Saboteur rewards confident pacing and punishes indecision with fewer honest options on the table. It’s a game that scales nicely from a quick 20-minute blast to a more involved 30- to 45-minute affair when the blow-by-blow narrative of your table becomes part of the fun.

## Mechanics and strategy: the heart of the game
At its core, Saboteur blends hand management with set-collection path-building. The path cards have connectors that click together like a tiny railroad; if you manage to connect four or five cards in a row without breaking the chain, you may reach the gold—provided there’s not a rock obstacle somewhere along the way that blocks your tunnel from being complete. The saboteurs add spice by forcing you to question every action: did that miner place a path card to help him or to hinder me? Is that obstacle meant to stall the group or to eliminate a potential threat?

Key strategic considerations:
- Role deduction: If you’re a saboteur, your success hinges on your ability to blend with the group while quietly steering the tunnel toward a dead end or a rock fall. If you’re a miner, you must weigh each fork in the tunnel with a mind for risk and potential betrayal.
- Path planning: You need to anticipate the path’s viability several steps ahead. A tunnel that looks promising on the left might be a dead end on the right, and you’ll have to pivot quickly when the board doesn’t cooperate.
- Resource management: Some decks include cards that let you redraw or switch paths. Use these sparingly; conservation is the name of the game when the table starts to suspect your every move.
- Bluffing and social play: Saboteur thrives on misdirection. A well-timed wink, a casual statement, or a conspiratorial whisper can set the tone for the entire round. Remember: your words are as much a tool as your cards.

The 2018 Amigo edition doesn’t drastically rewrite these mechanics; instead, it refines readability, pile management, and card art to make table talk and bluffing a little less chaotic and a lot more entertaining. The game rewards a balanced approach: you can go all-in strategic if your table is composed of cerebral strategists, or you can lean into the social engine if you’re sitting with a crew that enjoys drama, jokes, and team-based shenanigans.

## Path cards, obstacles, and the art of misdirection
Path cards come in all shapes: straight corridors, short bends, long arcs, and the occasional scenic detour. Obstacles disrupt rather than end the game; they force players to adapt and re-evaluate the tunnel’s viability. The humor arises when the table collectively realizes that a path looks perfect on paper but collapses under the weight of a poorly timed obstacle. The art for these cards leans into the dwarven fantasy vibe—gems glitter, pickaxes gleam, and the little gold token glimmers with sufficient promise to make you forget the conspiracy around you for a moment.

Pro-tip: keep track of potential dead ends and remember that a saboteur can leverage an obstacle to push the game in a less favorable direction for others. You might think you’re about to claim a glorious tunnel to gold, but the table might discover a sneaky backdoor to victory via a hidden card chain that only a few players remember. It’s exactly this kind of memory test that makes Saboteur so delightful at larger player counts.

## Tips and tricks for players
- For miners: communicate without revealing too much. Subtle hints can guide your team toward a better tunnel, but you don’t want to reveal your own plan too early, or the saboteurs will lock you out.
- For saboteurs: blend in, observe patterns, and plant obstacles at moments when they will cause the most friction. A well-timed rock card can derail a promising run and create a narrative moment that the table will remember long after the game ends.
- Watch the table’s emotion: Saboteur thrives on the social pulse. If someone starts laughing a little too mischievously, you might have a saboteur among you—or a mastermind who just loves dramatic reveals.
- Don’t overlook short rounds: even a 20-minute game can feel epic if the table leans into the story. Short rounds are perfect for nights when you’re exhausted but still craving a little chaos.

## Unofficial strategies for tough tables
If you’re dealing with hyper-competitive players, you can lean into a bluffing game within a game. Pretend to be a cooperative miner while you subtly guide others into a false sense of security. If you’re dealing with social butterflies, lean into the humor and use misdirection as a social lubricant, turning accusations into laughter and a cascade of witty banter.

On the other hand, if your group enjoys deep puzzle-solving, you can treat Saboteur as a collaborative puzzle with a few sabotages sprinkled in. Build a path you know will be tricky to navigate but will reward careful coordination and sharp reading of fellow players. The beauty of Saboteur is that you can tune the intensity to fit your mood and the room’s energy level.
