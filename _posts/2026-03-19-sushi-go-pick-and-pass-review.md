## What is Sushi Go
Sushi Go is a pick and pass card drafting game with a focus on set collection and simple scoring patterns. The card types range from nigiri and maki to pudding and sashimi. Your goal is to assemble the best possible combination of sushi cards across multiple rounds, with different scoring rules that reward different combinations. The end result is a light strategy cake layered with cute art, bright colors, and a timer in your head that tells you when the round will end—usually about the time it takes to decide whether you want that extra maki roll or that tempting pudding token that might be worth more at the end than your total point haul.

### How to play in under a minute (well almost)
In a typical game you will play 3 to 4 rounds depending on player count and house rules, but the gist is always the same:
- Each player starts with a hand of cards. The hand size equals the number of players plus one minus something depending on the number of rounds. Don t worry about the exact numbers; the rule book lays it out plainly and you will learn by doing.
- Everyone picks one card from their hand and places it face down in front of them.
- All players reveal their chosen card at the same time, then pass the remaining cards to the left.
- You add your picked card to your personal score area for the current round. After all cards are exhausted, you tally up points according to the patterns on the cards and set aside your total for the round.
- The next round begins with a fresh hand, and the passing continues until the deck is exhausted.
- Final scores are tallied after all rounds, with pudding cards providing endgame fun and potential tiebreakers as the final boss fight in your table round.

The genius of Sushi Go lies in the speed, the tension, and the almost inevitable moment when you misread your neighbor s intention and find yourself with a card you wish you had passed a second earlier. It is a game that rewards short-term memory and quick pattern recognition without dragging you into a long planning phase. This is the sweet spot of micro game design: high replayability with minimal mental overhead.

### Card types and scoring patterns
Nigiri cards: a set of tiny rice topped with tuna, egg, or squid. Each Nigiri has a point value, but the bigger story is how you pair nigiri with wasabi, which adds a little extra flavor to the mix. 
Maki cards: these award points for having the most or second most maki in a round, so you may end up drafting a card simply to deny a rival the lead. Think of it as culinary chess, but with more wasabi and fewer bishops.
Pudding cards: these mention the end of the game scoring. They don t score during the round but give you a nice chunk of points if you end the game with the most puddings, or possibly if your pudding count is on the generous side relative to others. This often becomes the deciding factor in close games, and the players who keep a mental tally of puddings can sometimes swing outcomes with a single well-timed pick.
Chopsticks and other special cards: depending on the version you own or the variant you include, there may be extra mechanics that spice up the strategy. These are optional, but they can dramatically shift decisions without turning the game into a math marathon.

To give you a feel for the flow, imagine a table where you are juggling a handful of tiny sushi cards while the rest of the players snipe at their own sets. You will be balancing the desire to collect a perfect sashimi run, the necessity to snag enough nigiri to outscore, and the subtle, almost subconscious urge to throw a renge-like tactic into a puddling protection plan.

## Strategy and house rules
While Sushi Go is simple to teach, there is nuance enough to reward thoughtful play. Here are a few tips to up your game without turning the table into a math lab:
- Watch your neighbor s likely patterns. If you notice a person hungrily chasing a particular card type, you may want to adjust your pass to either block or bait them with a different option.
- Balance short term gains with endgame goals. It s tempting to chase a single high value card, but the broader board often rewards a well rounded hand that captures a mix of cards across several categories.
- Count the cards mentally where possible. If you know the likelihood of encountering a given sushi type later in the round, you can plan your passes more precisely and avoid ending up with mediocre leftovers.
- Use pudding as a late game lever. Pudding cards can swing an entire game, so if you sense you can outpace others in pudding count, you might pivot to preserve that advantage rather than chasing a risky big card that won t deliver.
- If playing with kids, embrace the chaos. The rules are light enough that a few silly decisions can lead to big laughs, and that is often the point of a family game night.
